# APPENDIXBLECTURE BYEMERY WALKER ![](images/b30cc853c297a8aa9d0337f8cf728077e523ffed56e3d5e89f8a3c4937d08735.jpg) HE lecture by Emery Walker on Letterpress Printing and Illustration', delivered on 15 November 1888 to the Arts and Crafts Society, was a turning-point in the history of modern printing, for it inspired William Morris to design his own typeface and to establish the Kelmscott Press. Since Walker spoke (rather nervously, according to Morris) from notes, the lecture was never published in the form in which it was delivered; yet, because it had such far-reaching consequences, I have made an attempt here to reproduce the substance of the lecture. There are three important sources of information: Walker's notes (in the Humanities Research Center, University of Texas); Oscar Wilde's (unsigned) article, Printing and Printers', Pall Mall Gazette, 16 November 1888, p. 5; and ‘The Art of Printing. . . A Lecture by Emery Walker', British and Colonial Printer and Stationer, 6 February 189o, p. 94, which reports a slightly different version of the talk, delivered at the City of London College in 189o. Of these, the notes and Wilde's article are more significant, and I have therefore transcribed them in their entirety, but I have also made use of the article in the British and Colonial Printer and Stationer (which is more detailed but filled with inaccuracies) to clarify certain doubtful allusions. Two other reports of essentially the same lecture by Walker were published in the British and Colonial Printer and Stationer, 2 January 1896, pp. 10−11, and 31 March 1898, pp. 205−6, both accompanied by illustrations based upon Walker's slides. In the later variants of the lecture Walker concluded by showing pages from the Kelmscott Press books. An important recent study of the lecture is John Dreyfus, Emery Walker's 1888 Lecture on “Letterpress Printing": A Reconstruction and a Reconsideration', Craft History, 1(1988), 118-30. Since Walker used these lecture notes on more than one occasion, it is not always possible to distinguish precisely between the original text and later additions to it. The inconsistencies in numbering the slides reflect these revisions: only Walker's final renumberings have been transcribed here. Any necessary annotations of the two texts have been placed within square brackets; Walker's own square brackets in his notes are reproduced as arrow brackets. ## ## Walker's lecture notes Although, I suppose one may not unreasonably think that every person here, has at least some idea, of the methods by w[hi]ch books are made, I hope I may be pardoned, if for the sake of impressing the essentials of the various processes on your minds, I, with the assistance of my friend here, very briefly demonstrate the rudiments of the art. A series of most interesting specimens of old printed books and manuscripts were displayed on the screen by means of the magiclantern, and Mr. Walker's explanations were as clear and simple as his suggestions were admirable. He began by explaining the different kinds of type, and how they are made, and showed specimens of the old block-printing which preceded the moveable type and which is still used in China. He pointed out the intimate connection between printing and handwriting - as long as the latter was good the printers had a living model to go by, but when it decayed printing decayed also. He showed in succession on the screen a page from Gutenberg's Bible, (the first printed book – date about 145o-5) and a manuscript of Columella; a printed Livy of 1469, with the abbreviations of handwriting, and a manuscript of the History of Pompeius by Justin of 1451. The latter he regarded as an example of the beginning of Roman type. The resemblance between the manuscripts and the printed books was most curious and suggestive. He then showed a page out ofJohn of Spier's edition of Cicero's Letters, the first book printed at Venice, an edition of the same book by Nicholas Jansen [Jenson] 147o, and a wonderful manuscript Petrarch of the 16th century. He told the audience about Aldus who was the first publisher to start cheap books, who dropped abbreviations, and had his type cut by Francia pictor et aurifex, who was said to have taken it from Petrarch's handwriting. He exhibited a page of the copy-book of Vicentino the great Venetian writing master which was greeted with a spontaneous round of applause, and made some excellent suggestions about improving modern copy-books and avoiding slanting writing. A superb Plautus printed at Florence in 1514 for Lorenzo di Medici, Polydore Virgil's History with the fine Holbein designs, printed at Basle in 1556, and other interesting books were also exhibited on the screen, the size of course being very much enlarged. He spoke of Elzevir in the seventeenth century, when handwriting began to fall off, and of the English printer Caslam [Caslon], and of Baskerville, whose type was possibly designed by Hogarth, but is not very good. Latin, he remarked, was a better language to print than English, as the tails of the letters did not so often fall below the line. The wide-spacing between lines occasioned by the use of a lead, he pointed out, left the page in stripes, and made the blanks as important as the lines. Margins should of course be made, except the inner margins, and head lines often robbed the page of its beauty of design. The type used by the Pall Mall [Old Style] was, we are glad to say, rightly approved of. With regard to illustration, the essential thing, Mr. Walker said, is to have harmony between the type and the decoration. He pleaded for true book-ornament as opposed to the silly habit of putting pictures where they are not wanted, and pointed out that mechanical harmony and artistic harmony went hand in hand. No ornament or illustration should be used in a book that cannot be printed in the same way as the type. For his warnings he produced Roger's [sic] Italy' with a steelplate engraving, and a page from an American magazine which being florid, pictorial, and bad, was greeted with some laughter. For examples we had a lovely Boccaccio printed at Ulm, and a page out ofLa Mer des Histoires' printed in 1488. Blake and Bewick were also shown, and a page of music designed by Mr. [Herbert P.] Horne. The lecture was listened to with great attention by a large audience, and was certainly most attractive. Mr. Walker has the keen artistic instinct that comes out of actually working in the art of which he spoke. His remarks about the pictorial character of modern illustration were most well-timed, and we hope that some of the publishers in the audience will take them to heart. Next Thursday Mr. Cobden Saunderson [Sanderson] lectures on book-binding, a subject on which few men in England have higher qualifications for speaking.
