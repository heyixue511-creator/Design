Since we have already (in Chapter Three) traced the process by which Morris designed his types, it is not necessary to linger very long over his views on that subject; but it may be useful to touch on a few points briefly. First, Morris's decision to restrict himself to the kind of fount available to fifteenth-century printers imposed limitations on the Kelmscott Press that probably became obvious only after several books had been printed. In particular, the lack of italics made it difficult, if not impossible, for Morris to include any scholarly apparatus with medieval texts, even had he wanted to do so. This may not have troubled Morris, but it was a source of constant frustration to F. S. Ellis. Second, Morris's habit ofjamming paragraphs together, separated only by the paragraph symbol or by one of his smaller leaf ornaments, was an unfortunate decision. It did, needless to say, shape the text into the black rectangle which Morris desired, but it also disfigured the page with measles-like spots and made impossible that uniformity of colour which he himself sought. Third, the generous breadth of Morris's individual letters, which made them unsuitable for ordinary commercial purposes, likewise necessitated a broad page that strikes some readers as clumsy: the tall but narrow page which became the trademark of the Nonesuch Press book is unquestionably more gracefully proportioned. Finally, something needs to be said about one aspect of the Kelnscott books that has aroused much controversy: the considerable depth to which the type is impressed into the paper. Accustomed as we are now to offset printing, which makes no indentation whatever in the page, we may be tempted to conclude that Morris has taken an accidental characteristic of the hand-printed book and carried it to excessive lengths. Certainly some critics of the Kelmscott Press have accused Morris of obsessive archaism in this matter, and in support of their charge they have been able to cite a note signed by him and inserted in The Golden Legend: If this book be bound the edges of the leaves should only be T RIM M ED, not cut. In no case should the book be pressed, as that would destroy the “impression" of the type and thus injure the appearance of the printing.’ Yet the true explanation of Morris's attitude probably lies at a deeper level. The deliberate removal of the type-impression by pressing would have been a dishonest act in his eyes, because it represents an attempt to give an artificial smoothness to a product by concealing the real nature of its material. Furthermore, we need to remember that the unsurpassed presswork of the Kelmscott Press, which has been universally praised, was dependent upon a deep, slow impression of the forme into the fabric of the dampened paper. In short, the ‘embossing' of the Kelmscott page, far from being an affectation, was an integral part of a superior system of inking. Examined minutely, Morris's typographical theories and practices do not display the sort of monolithic uniformity that a merely casual acquaintance with his books might suggest. There are ragged edges to his ideas, and there is some evidence in the later Kelmscott books — and still more in the trial pages of those books - that his views continued to develop until the time of his death. But, even when we have acknowledged the deficiencies and inconsistencies of Morris's work as a book-designer, we are in the end left with a large, impressive body of theoretical writings and fifty-two books which have had a considerable effect upon the books we buy and read today. Exasperating and obstreperous, Morris is none the less still a force to be reckoned with in the world of bookmaking. What was perhaps a faint misgiving in Morris's mind has become, in many other minds, the most serious objection to the Kelmscott Press books: that they are (according to this view) so overburdened with ornament that they suggest heavily laden ships likely to be capsized by the gentlest breeze. Another, more jocular figure of speech was often invoked after the turn of the century. The volumes of the Kelmscott Press, it was said, bore an unhappy resemblance to his wallpapers. In fact, the debate about the Kelmscott ornaments has been carried on to a remarkable extent in metaphoric language. Holbrook Jackson, who was outspoken in his reservations about Morris's achievement as a printer, offered this vivid (and, one assumes, admiring) characterization in 1913: ‘The Kelmscott books look not only as if letter and decoration had grown one out of the other; they look as if they could go on growing." Beatrice Warde, on the other hand, suggested in a well-known essay that good printing should be invisible: like a crystal goblet, it is a transparent medium which encloses the wine of literature.³ Such argument by analogy has had a powerfully damaging effect upon the reputation of the Kelmscott Press in the twentieth century. Of course the crystal goblet' figure, though undoubtedly a brilliant image, evades some important questions about the proper relationship between text and ornament in the printed book. Behind it lies the assumption - which by Beatrice Warde's time no longer had even to be expressed - that elaborate ornamentation of any kind is not only ill-advised but also immoral. In the 192os the new doctrine of functionalism had persuaded many that the lingering influence of Victorianism could be eradicated only by declaring war on all historicist, non-functional ornament, whether in buildings, paintings, household furnishings, or books. The volumes of the Kelmscott Press, so thickly encrusted with initials, borders, and ornamental illustrations, inevitably found themselves caught in the crossfire ofthis aesthetic battle. Attitudes towards book-ornament had been very different when the Kelmscott Press was still taking shape in Morris's head. During the final decades of the last century there were influential voices urging a revival of ornament and illustration (wood-engraved, if possible) that would blend harmoniously with the typefaces used on the same pages. Gilbert R. Redgrave concluded an historical survey published in the periodical the British Bookmaker (October 189o) with these words: It is, we think, a matter of regret that the glory of the early days of woodcut initial letters has been lost to us, and that in the place of the graceful designs which once headed every chapter we have now too often to rely upon the meaningless scrolls and flourishes which the typefounder supplies us. Because he was unwilling to use process engraving, Morris himself turned to sympathetic craftsmen who could translate the decorative designs for the Kelmscott books into wood-engravings that would not clash with his typefaces. His solution was different but the aim was identical: the artistic integration of word and picture. ‘This means, in other words,'said Morris, [that there must be] the designer of the picture-blocks, the designer of the ornamental blocks, the woodengraver, and the printer, all of them thoughtful, painstaking artists, and all working in harmonious cooperation for the production of a work of art. Borders, by their very nature, are used more sparingly than ornamented initials: the same simple border appeared in the first five books of the Kelmscott Press (always on the first text page), and only later did Morris introduce a profusion of full, three-quarter, half, quarter, and corner borders into his books with splendid but sometimes confusing effect. The History of Godefrey of Boloyne (1893) must be the most over-decorated of the Kelmscott books, surpassing in this respect even the Chaucer; the other end of the spectrum is represented by the earliest books (when Morris did not yet have the resources of a ‘bloomer book'’ at hand) and the latest ones (after Morris's death). Like the initials, the borders consist mainly of stylized floral and vine patterns in white line against a black ground, though Morris also did a few in outline style. Their obvious resemblance to some of Morris's wallpaper designs has struck nearly all readers, and the analogy is not as unflattering to the Kelmscott volumes as some wits have supposed. On rare occasions Morris employs a border that reflects the text in some fashion, as when a rose border surrounds the opening of Chaucer's Romaunt of the Rose; but for the most part he believed that borders should be ornamental rather than illustrative. (Hence there is not one historiated initial or border in any Kelmscott book, even though Morris could have found many examples of them by going no further than his own library.) Various sources have been suggested for his borders, including the Windmill Psalter (which he owned), sculpture in the porch of Beauvais Cathedral, and of course Ratdolt's books. In fairness to Morris, it must be said that Ratdolt's borders, while vaguely sinilar, are much less successful and more static than those in the Kelmscott books. This appears to be yet another instance of Morris improving upon, rather than directly imitating, a medieval model. Even the most severe critics of the Kelmscott Press have acknowledged that Morris's borders are things of extraordinary beauty. The borders have inspired prolonged debate, however, about whether they distract and dazzle the eye and thus call too much attention to themselves. Morris's full borders, like Ratdolt's, reflect the classic proportions of the book page — that is to say, the border progressively increases in breadth from the inner margin to the lower margin. R. B. McKerrow justly complained that such a pattern produces an unpleasantly heavy concentration of black ink at the bottom of the page. It is a pattern that ultimately derives from the medieval scribes and illuminators, but they had the advantage of being able to produce borders of lighter texture.9 These broad borders also produced technical problems for Morris: they were in part responsible for the discoloration of the Chaucer sheets, and in fact the press on which the Chaucer was printed had to be reinforced with steel bands because of the unusual size of the ornaments. We have already seen that one of the chief decorative elements in the Kelmscott books, the engraved title facing the first page of text, was apparently Morris's own invention, since the first two generations of printers used, at most, label titles that allowed no scope for ornamentation. Morris's distinctive engraved title evolved slowly, perhaps accidentally. The first five books from the Press had only simple titles set in the Golden type, but the sixth Kelmscott volume, A Dream of John Ball (1892), included a frontispiece by Burne-Jones (the first illustration in the Press's books) across from the opening text page, with large Roman lettering underneath designed by Morris. Probably this gave Morris the idea of placing an engraved title (within a border, like the John Ball illustration) in this position, because the first such title appears in the next Kelmscott book, The Golden Legend.2° Of the fifty-two books printed at the Kelmscott Press, twenty had woodengraved title-pages, though naturally there were no more after the Chaucer because of Morris's death in the autumn of 1896. Morris took pains with the lettering, even consulting Arrighi, one of the sixteenth-century writing masters represented in his library,2 when he was designing the John Ball frontispiece caption; yet the Roman titles which he created leave much to be desired. Rather than resembling any of the graceful hands recommended by Arrighi, Morris's hand-lettered titles suggest a crude version of Caslon or, at worst, the wood-types used on Victorian posters. They are nevertheless redeemed by Morris's consistently fine floral and vine backgrounds and by the borders that surround then. The Gothic titles, not surprisingly, are much more satisfying. Morris first revived the paragraph symbol, which he employed frequently in the early years of the Press, as a substitute for a paragraph indentation. After News from Nowhere he abandoned it, no doubt wisely, though Charles Fairfax Murray persuaded him to revive it briefly in Epistola de contemptu mundi (1894). Morris in effect replaced the paragraph symbol with three other small ornaments, which produced the same unfortunate effect of destroying the even colour of the page. Curiously, Morris never made use of the simple ornament that is the favourite of most printers, the rule — either plain or swelled — though in the proofs of The Nature of Gothic (1892)²⁴ there is a rule between a border and the enclosed text that was eliminated before the book was printed. ## Book-ornamentation and illustration may seem to be two separate subjects, but Morris believed that illustrations — except in the most baldly functional books such as reference works and textbooks - should be primarily ornamental and must, like ornaments, be designed in reference to the book as a whole and to the typeface in particular. This was a subject that much exercised the minds of Morris's contemporaries, because the advent of zincos and halftones, which were printed most effectively on glossy paper, posed a new threat to the structural unity of books in the late-nineteenth century. Alfred Dawson, whose firm had introduced photomechanical engraving to England, offered a radical but plausible-sounding solution: in a paper entitled ‘The Most Artistic Book', read at the Annual Meeting of the Library Association in 1891, he suggested that the illustrations and text should be produced on separate presses and brought together only at the binding stage. The illustrations, under this scheme, would be printed on a copperplate press with dry paper; the text, set in old-face type, would be printed on dampened paper, if possible handmade.² But this was an admission of defeat in the struggle for unity, and Morris would have none of it. His answer was to bring harmony to a book by returning to the older method - printing wood-engraved illustrations locked in the same frame with the type, and thereby using the same paper and ink. Morris, in a lecture given a year after Dawson's, summarized his conception of the ideal book with pictures: An illustrated book, where the illustrations are more than mere illustrations of the printed text, should be a harmonious work of art. The type, the spacing of the type, the position of the pages of print on the paper, should be considered from the artistic point of view. The illustrations should not have a mere accidental connection with the other ornament and the type, but an essential and artistic connection. They should be designed as a part of the whole, so that they would seem obviously imperfect without their surroundings. The designs must be suitable to the material and method of production, and not offer to the executant artist a mere thicket of unnatural difficulties, producing no result when finished, save the exhibition of a tour de force. The executant, on his side, whether he be the original designer or someone else, must understand that his business is sympathetic translation, and not mechanical reproduction of the original drawing.'6 It was perfectly natural for Morris, given his passion for the medieval book, to prefer wood-engravings to photomechanical reproductions; as early as the 186os he had made an ambitious effort in The Earthly Paradise to restore a broad, simplified style of engraving to its former pre-eminence. Both he and Walker were convinced that an engraved line was somehow ‘sweeter' than one reproduced by a camera. And of course Morris's insistence upon the artistic freedom of the wood-engraver, who is not to obey the dictates of the designer servilely, is wonderfully Ruskinian and consistent with Morris's own view of the craftsman's role. It is a seductively attractive theory of how book illustrations should be produced. In practice at the Kelmscott Press, alas, all was not so simple. Part of the difficulty lay in the temperament and training of William Harcourt Hooper, who engraved most of the Kelmscott illustrations. Described by Walter Crane (in 1897) as ‘almost the only first-rate fac-simile engraver on wood left — and a good artist and craftsman besides',27 Hooper had engraved the drawings of artists like Fred Walker, Millais, du Maurier, Tenniel, and Leighton for the Illustrated London News and Punch. A neighbour of Walker's and a committed socialist (and, in earlier days, a pupil of Rossetti at the Working Men's College), he was persuaded by. Morris to come out of retirement to work for the Kelmscott Press; but almost immediately there were disagreements between Hooper and his new employer. Morris, like Ruskin before him, believed firmly that the craftsman ought not to be merely the tool of an artist-designer. In practice this meant that the engraver should participate in the creative process by adapting and modifying the artist's design as he transfers it to the surface of the woodblock. Hooper's entire career, however, had been devoted to producing exact facsiniles of the drawings given to him. This fidelity to the design was in fact a matter of pride to engravers like Hooper: he was delighted on one occasion when Ruskin was unable to distinguish one of his proofs from the drawing on which it was based.2⁸ During the first year of the Kelmscott Press's existence, Hooper engraved only initials and borders, but there were even then occasional outbursts of indignation from Morris about his work, as in this letter to Walker of4 February 1892: ‘Thanks for note and proof of Hooper's cuts. . . . In all the designing that Burne-Jones had done for Morris and Company he exhibited a reluctance to provide finished drawings to scale, and in an interview published in the Studio (1894) Morris explained how his firm had got round this problem when producing textiles. Morris's remarks are worth quoting at length, because they shed considerable light on both Burne-Jones's technique as a draughtsman and Morris's use of photography: ‘The original studies are not above 15 inches high. The figures are grouped and drawn from carefully prepared studies: for the rest there is but little minuteness of detail; and they are only slightly tinted. That is the form in which they come into our hands. We have to have them enlarged by photography, in squares varying in size and number according to the full dimensions required. The enlarged sections are then fitted together, and the whole, now of the proper size, submitted, together with a small coloured study, to the artist for his revision and approval; and on these enlargements he does a great deal of work, especially to the heads and hands. The ornamental accessories, the patterns of brocades in the draperies, the flowers and the foliage, are left to us, and are drawn, for the most part, by Mr. H. Dearle. . . .' We know that from the mid-187os the same procedure was used at Morris and Company for translating Burne-Jones's small sketches into suitable designs for stained-glass windows; and in the end something not unlike this had to be done to his Kelmscott Press illustrations as well. The most distinctive characteristic of a wood-engraving is that it can render only blacks and whites; intermediate tones must be suggested by parallel lines (which Morris believed should be used sparingly) or cross-hatching (which he thought should not be used at all). But the designs which Burne-Jones provided for the Kelmscott illustrations were pencil sketches filled with delicate nuances of shading. Obviously a great deal of translation' had to be done before they could be made into wood-engravings. According to Morris's understanding of craftsmanship, Hooper should have been the person to adapt the sketch to the limitations of the engraving process, yet in practice a veteran ‘facsimile engraver’ like Hooper was incapable of bridging the gap. Burne-Jones, adopting a similar but less flattering metaphor, described Catterson-Smith as an efficient tool in his hands.4 That it was an extraordinarily successful collaboration there can be no doubt; yet at the same time Morris must have been uncomfortably aware that the use of a craftsman as a mere ‘tool’ of an artist undernined the ideological basis of the Kelmscott Press. It illustrates once again the chasm that sometimes appeared between Morris's theories of bookmaking and what actually took place at the Press. The wood-engravings created by the joint endeavours of Burne-Jones, Catterson-Smith, and Hooper are a curious hybrid of the characteristics of engravings and woodcuts. They are unquestionably engravings, because they are cut on the end grain of boxwood with gravers' tools; yet they display little of the delicacy of effect usually associated with an engraving. The lines are broad, the wood is engraved deeply, and there is no hint of the white-line technique used by most engravers after Bewick. The methods of shading are similar to those of a woodcut. What Morris and Burne-Jones produced, through their craftsmen, was an engraved version of the woodcut as it appeared in the fine illustrated books of the fifteenth-century printers of Ulm and Augsburg. Once they were completed, Morris found that wood-engraved illustrations are technically difficult to print. They require exceptionally careful presswork, with overlays and underlays, and, because they bite deeply into dampened paper, problems arise when large engravings appear on the verso of a page of text. In fact, not until the Chaucer was published in 1896 did Morris succeed in fully integrating words and pictures. In the illustrated Kelmscott Press books published before the Chaucer the wood-engravings always appeared alone on leaves that were blank on the verso; some were printed in an entirely separate operation and the leaves merely inserted when the book was bound, a procedure perilously close to the formal dichotomy of text and illustration that Morris always condemned in the books of others. (Another reason for Morris's adoption of this unhappy expedient is that Burne-Jones was often so slow in producing his designs that they were engraved only after the rest of the book had been printed.) There were occasional proposals that the Kelmscott Press illustrations might be issued by themselves, in a form suitable for framing, but here Morris drew the line. Writing to the bookseller Frederick H. Evans in November 1894, Cockerell declared: 'I have spoken to Mr. Morris about the proposal in your letter . . . & I am sorry to say that he does not see his way to printing the pictures [of the Chaucer] apart from the book. He thinks that if this were done it might injure the sale of future issues, as some people would abstain from buying the books themselves in the hope of being able to secure the illustrations separately.'47 Cockerell's letter was expressed in appropriately commercial terms, yet Morris was no doubt more troubled by the theoretical implications of Evans's proposal. He might be willing to compromise in his search for unity of word and picture, but to publish the Chaucer illustrations separately would have made a mockery of his ideal of integration. As Burne-Jones's words suggest, he and Morris hardly thought alike on all subjects. There were in fact two great sources of tension between them. The first was that, whereas Morris was drawn to the art, literature, and values of the Gothic North, Burne-Jones (for all his medievalism) felt an equally strong attraction to the Mediterranean world. Morris's commitment to socialism also threatened at times to divide him from his closest friend. In 1854 he was asked to provide pictures and ornaments for Archibald Maclaren's book, The Fairy Family, which was to be published by Longmans. One reason Burne-Jones was able to produce so many illustrations for the Kelmscott Press of such consistently high quality within a short time span is that he borrowed heavily from his own earlier work. A large proportion of the Kelmscott pictures are derived from paintings on similar or identical themes: this ought not to surprise us, for Burne-Jones was the sort of obsessive artist who returned constantly to certain recurring motifs, which Penelope Fitzgerald has ably summarized as the enchantment of the willing victim, sleep, waiting, imprisonment, loneliness, guiding, rescue, the quest, losing and finding, tending the helpless, flying, sea-crossing, clinging together, the ritual procession and dance, love dominant and without pity, the haunting angel, the entry into life'.7° Yet he did not offer Morris mere mechanical adaptations of earlier designs. The Kelmscott illustrations based on specific paintings by Burne-Jones are always transposed into a new, more haunting key. Human figures are shown in graceful but static poses against sometimes disturbing, surrealistic backgrounds, gazing past each other (and past the reader) with strangely hypnotic expressions. It may be significant that most of Burne-Jones's Kelmscott Press illustrations, except for the Chaucer and The Well at the World's End, are upright rectangles, often quite narrow, filled with emphatically vertical and slender figures (usually female), for the pattern reminds one immediately of the scores of stained-glass windows that he had designed for Morris and Company. As in the windows, the human beings seem divided from each other, though here it is an invisible barrier rather than a mullion that separates them. The darker side of Burne-Jones's imagination is much in evidence in these illustrations. His distrust of deep perspective, in the paintings as well as these designs, has often been commented on, and of course the comparative flatness of Burne-Jones's drawings goes well with the markedly shallow depth of Morris's ornaments. What is perhaps less commonly noticed about the Kelmscott illustrations is that Burne-Jones, in accordance with the theories that he and Morris had evolved, made certain that his pictures were above all ornamental. Whether they told a story was beside the point; he confessed that he always evaluated his illustrations by looking at them upside down." The real tests, in the end, were the decorative quality of the pictures and the extent to which they contributed to the mise-en-scène of the printed page. In his foreword to the Kelmscott edition of The Poems of William Shakespeare (1893), Ellis called attention to the question: After a lapse of about three hundred years Shakespeare's Poems are in this edition printed with the original spelling. This was a point of view long advocated by Frederick J. Like Ellis, Morris believed that to alter the spelling of a word was to conceal its history: he no doubt saw modern-spelling editions as the literary equivalent of insensitive Victorian restoration of medieval churches. Bowden soon became Morris's foreman; Mrs Pine, who was paid the same wages as the men (contrary to the usual practice at the time), achieved the distinction of becoming the first woman to join the London Society of Compositors.³⁴ Mrs Pine, her father, and a pressman named Giles soon retired from the Kelmscott Press, but Morris's staff grew as the output of the Press increased. A photograph taken in September 1895 shows eleven printers working for Morris at that time; the size of the establishment obviously fluctuated according to how many books were in production, and it seems probable that Morris's staff reached its maximum size in 1895 and early 1896 when work on the Chaucer was approaching completion. By the time the final sheet of the last book was printed in March 1898, there was only one pressman, Stephen Mowlem, still employed at Sussex Cottage. Though W. H. Bowden himself was not a socialist, several of Morris's printers — John Tippett, Stephen Mowlem, H. Howes, and Thomas Binning — had been associated with either (or, in Binning's case, both) the Hammersmith Socialist League or C. R. Ashbee's Guild of Handicraft.3 Binning, who had been the foreman printer of the Commonweal and became father of the chapel (the printers' equivalent of shop steward) at the Kelmscott Press, was a stern, unbending old trade-unionist who intinidatcd even Ashbee, for whon he later worked at the Essex House Press. It was probably Binning who, through the London Society of Compositors, arranged for the unionization of the Kelmscott Press. Though the presence of Mrs Pine at first created obstacles, in time all were enrolled as members of the LSC. When initially approached by the union authorities, Morris was able to reply truthfully that his printers were already well treated. Precisely how much they were paid is not known: Emery Walker implied that their wages were high, and H. Gage-Cole, who was an apprentice at the Kelmscott Press before working at the Doves Press, claimed that the Kelmscott pressman were paid five shillings a week above the standard rate.37 W. H. Bowden testified that ‘Mr. Morris never tied his men down to any certain number ofhours. He was most generous in the matter of holidays. On every Bank Holiday they had two days instead of the usual one day. When any of his men were ill, if it was only a short time, they always received full pay; if it was for a long time they had full pay for a month and half pay afterwards.'⁸ The Kelmscott printers must also have enjoyed the odd sensation of being the centre of attention in the world of English book-lovers. On the day of the University boat-race in 1893 Morris invited his printers to tea at Kelmscott House (Cockerell diary, 22 March 1893), while once a year he and May joined them on the outing known in the trade as a ‘Wayzegoose'. After a substantial dinner at a hotel or inn near London (the Dumb Bell Hotel at Taplow, Berkshire, in 1 894, for example), the printers celebrated with a lengthy programme of toasts, songs, and recitations. In 1894 they heard, among others, Binning toast the Kelmscott Press, F. Collins sing ‘At Trinity Church I Met My Doom', and Binning (once more) recite ‘Cassius instigating Brutus'.
