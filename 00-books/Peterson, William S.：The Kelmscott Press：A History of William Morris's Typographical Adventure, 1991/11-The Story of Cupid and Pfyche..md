Using the original wood-engravings (owned by the Society of Antiquaries) and Morris's Troy type, Will and Sebastian Carter, at the Rampant Lions Press in Cambridge, produced an illustrated edition of The Story of Cupid and Psyche which represents the kind of book Morris wanted to print in 1868 but was unable to achieve because of the typographical limitations of the day.³⁴ ## [Morris] makes a poem these days,' wrote Burne-Jones in 1871, — in dismal Queen Square in black old filthy London in dull end of October he makes a pretty poem that is to be wondrously happy; and it has four sets of lovers in it, and THEY ARE ALL HAPPY and it ends well, and will come out some time next summer and I shall make little ornaments to it. . . .'3 What Morris had in mind for Love is Enough was more ambitious than the phrase little ornaments' might suggest, though he certainly was now thinking on a smaller scale than in the case of The Earthly Paradise. In addition to wood-engraved ornaments and illustrations, Morris intended that the pages should be decorated with much hand-illumination. (One can see, very imperfectly, what Morris had in mind in Harry Buxton Forman's copy – now in the Berg Collection of the New York Public Library — which has an illuminated title executed by Coventry Patmore's daughter in 1889. Was Morris ever shown this copy?) Morris designed a series of ornamental initial ‘L's (which are much more delicate than the initials that were to appear in the Kelnscott books) and several borders closer in style to his calligraphic work than to the Kelmscott Press borders; Burne-Jones produced one or more borders and at least one illustration. The ornaments were all engraved by Morris. Trial pages, probably struck off in 1872, betray the same weakness that beset the Earthly Paradise trials, perhaps even more acutely in this instance: the pages are set in a conventional modern-face type that seems weak and enervated compared with Morris's vigorous ornaments.36 Like The Earthly Paradise, the book was published without decoration in 1873, yet Morris had a second opportunity to realize the right typographical form for it when he issued Love is Enough at the Kelmscott Press.
