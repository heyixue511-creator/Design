The Chinese Hero (Chung-wah Ying-hung), 1983 Art/Script: Ma Wing-shing

When The Chinese Hero first appeared——as a supplement to Wong Yuk-long's Drunken Master, then as its own comic book—it made the artist, Ma Wing-shing, something of an instant legend, in part because of his rags-to-riches life story, but mainly because of the skill and style of the work. Ma's comic, which was considered a breakthrough at the time, had a realistic style, clear and detailed action scenes, engrossing plots, and skillful use of color. Ma's style was influenced by the Japanese manga Crying Killer, by the artist Ryoichi Ikegami.

The Chinese Hero tells the story of Wah Yinghung, a young man living happily with his parents and fiancée until one tragic night: a man murders his parents and tries to kill him, too. Both of his parents were experts in martial arts, and he inherited their skills. He escapes, marries his fiancée, and they have twins; eventually the story shifts its focus to Wah Yinghung and his children's fight against evil.

When The Chinese Hero appeared as a standalone comic, it was an immediate hit, selling some 45,000 copies. Over the next several years, the title's circulation averaged 60,000 and hit peaks of 200,000. Ma became the hottest manhua artist in Hong Kong, and the English version, The Blood Sword (later renamed Blood Sword Dynasty) was published and distributed in North America. In 1989, Ma left Wong's Jademan Comics; however, the company owned the title and continued to publish it under the direction of another artist, Yuan Ga-bo. This comic became a movie in 1999, called A Man Called Hero.

Sea Tiger I–III, 1994

Tang and Wan's Sea Tiger, considered one of the classic manhua of the 1990s, combines two genres: fighting and science fiction. ![](images/51b4098be492f0fdab0b3c12170f2d10709462d8338d6c7fa7a0425217d4b60b.jpg)

At the beginning of the twentieth century, children in Hong Kong could choose to read two kinds of things outside of school: illustrated stories that appeared in the local newspapers, either as columns or supplements, or illustrated story books imported from Shanghai. Before the Second World War, Shanghai was much more progressive than Hong Kong; in fact, it is considered the first modern city in China. Many of the cultural advances that came to China in the twentieth century appeared in Shanghai first. The first Chinese-language kids' magazine, Enlightenment Pictorial, for example, was published there in 1908. Compared to that of Shanghai, Hong Kong's media suffered from a lack of talent; the first locally produced children's magazine, New Children's Bimonthly Magazine, didn't appear until 1941. It was the most influential children's periodical in Hong Kong until the early 1950s, when the influx of refugees from mainland China increased demand for all kinds of reading materials. Unfortunately, the shortage of talent continued to affect the quality of local magazines.

Hong Kong's daily newspapers, including Wah Kiu Daily and Sing Tao Daily, began to produce original material geared toward young readers. Some small publishers also published picture books for kids, although most were reprinted prewar magazines or pirated Chinese-language versions of Western books and Disney cartoons. Palm-size picture books produced before the war—known as lianhuanhua shu or kungchai shu—were also available for rent from street kiosks. Until the 1950s, these were essentially the only sources for children's comics.

The appearance of Children's Paradise in 1953, edited by Law Guan-tsiu, changed all that and marked the beginning of the children's manhua industry in Hong Kong. The magazine's success not only inspired imitators, but it also helped nurture local manhua talent, which fueled the comics boom of the 1950s and 1960s. Television was not broadcast in Hong Kong, so reading was one of the main forms of entertainment for the city’s children. In the 1950s and 1960s, too, many public and private libraries were founded, along with public housing projects equipped with reading rooms, making children's reading materials more widely available.

After television was introduced in 1967, the production of local children's comics began to drop off. Japanese manga became more popular—a by-product in part of the Chinese-dubbed Japanese animation that appeared on TV. Pirated editions of manga soon appeared; Fujio Fujiko's Doraemon, published in Japan in 1970, was redrawn and printed without permission as Ding Dong in Children’'s Paradise in the mid-1970s. Television also inspired several non-manga comics, including Children's TV (1975), Happy Together Children’s Pictorial (1975), and Big Brother Sunny (1978).

In the 1980s and 1990s, Hong Kong's children's magazines struggled to stay afloat. The two longest-lived titles, Children's Paradise and Little Friends Pictorial, both folded in the 1990s. Several new magazines did emerge, such as Sing Tao Good Children’s Pictorial (1982), as did a short-lived children's newspaper, Children's Daily (1989), and some authorized Disney magazines. But kids’ comics have never again been as popular as they were in the 1950s and 1960s. In order to survive, publishers had to find ways of making money. Many of the original children's comics from this era, such as Little Ming Pao Weekly (1989), Whiz-Kids Express Weekly (1993), and Yellow Bus (1990), appeared as supplements to weekly publications. Of these, Yellow Bus was the most successful, although at the cost of almost constant adaptation to appeal to young readers’ shifting tastes.

Today, the market for children's comics in Hong Kong is dominated by Japanese manga; very little material is produced locally. Taking a wider view of the genre, one sees that kids’ reading materials were constantly influenced by sources outside Hong Kong: first by Shanghai manhua, then by American comics, and more recently by Japanese manga and anime. The brief era when original creations such as Children’s Paradise and Little Friends Pictorial dominated the market is now considered the golden age of Hong Kong children's manhua.

## 1.

Children's Pictorials Bimonthly Magazine, 1923 This was one of the first children's magazines in China. Although it was published in Shanghai, it circulated in Hong Kong as well. Its content was mainly illustrations, but jokes, stories, activities, and games were also regular features.

## 2.

## Children's Magazine, 1932

Many early children's magazines published in Shanghai were circulated all over China in the 1930s. This type of magazine was the first example in the modern history of China of a magazine aimed at providing a well-rounded education outside the classroom.

## 3-6.

## Children's World Bimonthly, 1932

During the Japanese occupation and the Second World War, this long-running magazine moved from Shanghai to Changsha, and its content reflected wartime priorities. General information about the conflict was printed, as well as features on preserving country and family. Over a long career, Children's World Bimonthly included a more diverse array of content than previous children's magazines.

## 7-14.

## Young Children's Literature Collection, 1934

This Shanghai-based series included both text and illustrations covering health, society, and natural science, as well as arts and crafts projects and quizzes. Also featured were stories that included historical Chinese tales, Western fairy tales, and stories of everyday life. It lasted for more than 200 issues.

## 15.

## Good Children's Weekly: Children's Reading Materials, 1938

This magazine, begun at the outbreak of the Sino-Japanese War, was filled with anti-Japanese propaganda. Its purpose was to promote nationalism, and it urged children to support the war and eventually fight it, with cover stories such as “Children of China, Stand Up” and “I Want to Go Too, Papa.”

## 16-18.

## New Children's Bimonthly Magazine, 1941

This was Hong Kong's first literary variety magazine for children. Èdited by Wong Hing-wan and supported by university professors, it included work by such contemporary writers as Xu Di-shan, Fung Zikai, Yan Wen-jing, and Hu Bing-chun. After Hong Kong was occupied by Japan, the editorial staff moved to southern China, where they continued producing the magazine until 1944. The magazine returned to Hong Kong in 1946, where it published fifty more issues. In 1951, it moved back to China; it was renamed Young Pioneers in 1956.
