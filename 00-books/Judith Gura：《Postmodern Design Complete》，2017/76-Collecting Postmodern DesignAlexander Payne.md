# Collecting Postmodern Design Alexander Payne Postmodern design has been getting more attention in the past few years as a new category in collecting It has actually been collected since the mid-i99os and has been selling consistently at auction since the beginning of this century. In fact museums were interested in Postmodernism from the get-go. Some curators bought the originals from the early design exhibitions, and when the secondary market developed, auction houses were selling to museums before they were selling to collectors. The first collectors were Europeans who were in Italy when postmodern design began; they visited designers in their studios or saw the work at early exhibitions in Milan galleries. The market now is much more international. What does this mean for the individual who wants to start collecting postmodern design? Is there much left, and is it affordable? Fortunately, there is now an abudance of design coming into the market, and most of which is not unduly costly, especially when compared to styles like French Art Deco. Where should you begin? It is perhaps best to start by educating yourself on the subject. Radical Design came first, and Postmodernism per se dates from the late i97os. Find out what interests you, then do some serious research. Look at the main protagonists in this story; learn which Italian design movements came out of the period. Read about the principal designers to understand how their work has gone on to influence design today. Much of the experimental design of the i98os, for example, has informed textile or silverware design today, and many connections can be made. It is worth looking up the key works that were presented in the seminal exhibitions and events that put this design language on the map, including those associated with Archizoom\*, Superstudio\*, Alchimia\*, and other Radical Design groups. Identify the most important pieces by designers like ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), ALESSANDRO MENDINI (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), ANDREA BRANZI, MICHELE DE LUCCHI, and SHIRO KURAMATA (see all under "Desięn"); also check out NATHALIE DU PASQUIER (see under "Design"), PETER SHIRE (see under “Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design"), and GEORGE SOWDEN (see under “Design"). Discover old issues of Domus, Casabella, and other publications that showcased the period (some of these are collectible as well). Look for interesting concepts and experimental design to see which themes and types of objects appeal. Most seasoned collectors of postmodern design are not collecting objects by category; the specialist collector is more the exception than the rule. Most people will buy a design object, whether it is furniture, ceramics or glass, metalwork, lighting, or jewelry, because they are particularly drawn to it. Serious collectors will make a purchase in order either to live with the work, to incorporate it into another collection, or perhaps to connect it with the architecture of their home. They're motivated by a feeling for the object itself, which is the only way to collect. If you're inspired by a particular piece and feel a passion to own it, you're not likely to make a mistake. One important caution: you will need to find out how many editions were made of the object you like—the most collectible pieces are major works that were produced in limited editions, or of which few examples are known to exist. This is more difficult than it might sound: information such as this was not always recorded, and some pieces were produced only for a salone or triennial, or were one-offs for avant-garde happenings. When looking specifically at MEMPHIS designs (see under “Edited Design") , dates are important. Many are still being produced, decades after they were originally designed. The newer ones are not as valuable and serious collectors will only consider works that can be categorized as coming from the original postmodern period. This is why provenance—a record of where the piece came from and/or who owned it—makes an enormous difference. While this is important for all collectors, it is especially so for collectors of postmodern design. If you cannot date the piece to its conception/presentation to the world at large, then you can't be sure that you are collecting a “genuine" work. You could be buying something that was made much later: even if the design looks the same the value won't be. How do you tell an item's provenance? Invoices are best, if they are available, but there are other possible clues: the piece may have been illustrated in a catalogue, written about in a newspaper or magazine, or otherwise documented. It may have been created for a specific performance, an exhibition, or a display, sometimes even for a special client. Fortunately for the novice collector, postmodern design is not unduly costly. Even with a significant piece, prices do not compare to categories like, for example, Art Deco design. There are exceptions, of course. Proust chairs, by Alessandro Mendini, have been bringing in strong prices from collectors looking for good early examples. And Shiro Kuramata's Miss Blanche chair has set auction records. But there is much more to look for, and it can be found at fairly reasonable prices. The market for postmodern pieces is still in its infancy and has a long way to go as more people discover the period. And while investment is never a sure thing, if you collect in a category that is still developing, there is a good chance your purchase will increase in vaiue. If you're interested in collecting Postmodernism, now is a good time to start.
