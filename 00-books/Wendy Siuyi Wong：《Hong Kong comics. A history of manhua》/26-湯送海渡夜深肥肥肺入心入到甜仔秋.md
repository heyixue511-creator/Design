102–103.

Sang Po, 1975

Publisher: Wong Chun-lung (Wong Yuk-long)

Sang Po was Wong's first daily publication. At first, the tabloid-size newspaper tried to imitate regular papers, covering entertainment, health, and news and featuring columns by famous writers such as James Wong, Sha Yung, Tong Mo-lee, and Fung Ka. Comics filled only three-and-a-half pages each day, with Wong's Oriental Heroes appearing in full color on the back cover. After a month, Sang Po switched to pure manhua.

## 104–119.

Oriental Heroes, 1975

Art/Script: Wong Yuk-long

When Wong gave the Little Rascals series its new title, he made a few other changes: the setting was moved to Japan; he printed a note saying that the story was fictional; and he explored ways to depict violence without explicit bloodshed. He relied on fire, water, poison, and other methods of violence. Wong's drawing technique also improved, especially in his depiction of muscles and the lines denoting action. Oriental Heroes was published every day in Sang Po and collected each week in book form.

In the mid-1980s, Wong modified his drawing style again, making it more serious and less comical in response to the success of Ma Wing-shing's realistic The Chinese Hero. After 1991, Oriental Heroes was drawn by other artists; it is still published today, with a total run of more than 1,000 issues.

## 120-134.

Lee Siu-lung (Bruce Lee), 1971

Art/Script: Seung-gun Siu-bo

This comic, which used Bruce Lee's Chinese name as its title, emphasizes the use of martial arts to fight against evil and for justice. The main character, Lee Siu-lung, was drawn with physical features similar to those of the real Bruce Lee. Other characters included Lee's follower, Sa-dam Chai, a young rebel with strong principles. Violent acts such as eye gouging were depicted graphically, although less explicitly than in Wong's Little Rascals. Still, after the 1975 restrictions were imposed, the artist was forced to publish the comic daily in the newspaper Hei Bo to maintain its audience.

135–136.

Hei Bo, 1975

Publisher: Seung-gun Siu-bo

Entertainment news covered the front page of this newspaper; the remaining pages were filled with manhua. The manhua features were Lee Siu-lung, on the inside front page, and Sheng-gun Siu-wai's Brother of Dragon and Tiger, printed in full color on the back page. The newspaper lasted only two years, folding in early 1977.
