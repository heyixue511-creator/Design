We can never know the answer to that question; but it is evident that Morris had numerous schemes in mind at the time of his death, the most important of which were, according to Ellis, an edition of Shakespeare's plays, the English Bible, and Froissart's Chronicles.' Morris on his deathbed made one last, feeble effort to prolong the life of the Press, as recorded in Cockerell's diary on 30 August 1896: He asked whether I should be prepared to carry on the Press after his death, with Walker, & I said that I was in favour of its ceasing - as otherwise it would fizzle out by degrees, & the books already issued would suffer by inferior ones following them. He said he thought I was perhaps right.'Nevertheless, there was much unfinished business which Cockerell and Walker had to supervise after Morris was gone. The renaining five volumes of The Earthly Paradise were issued, and ten more titles (as well as the Froissart trial pages) were published during the last phase of the Press's history. Not until the spring of 1898 — more than a year and a half after Morris's funeral — was Cockerell able to bring the Kelmscott Press to a definitive close. For the most part the books produced during this period had been planned or even begun while Morris was still alive, but it is enlightening to discover that fully a fifth of the Kelmscott volumes were produced without his direct supervision by associates and employees who had learned to emulate his principles of typography. Morris showed signs of deteriorating health in 1895. Although one medical diagnosis suggested that he merely suffered from the disease ofbeing William Morris, and working 18 hours a day',2 it was obvious to his family and friends that — as Burne-Jones put it — his enormous vitality [was] diminishing'.3 By February 1896 the symptoms were alarming, and Morris was seeing doctors regularly, though Cockerell still reassured himself that all would be well in the end. ‘W.M. very unwell & weak,' he wrote in his diary on 2o February. . . . Surely it is influenza.' Throughout the spring of 1896 there were further signs of decline, but of Morris's intimate circle only Burne-Jones and Blunt seemed to recognize the gravity of his illness. Blunt's diary describes a visit on 14 May to Hammersmith, where I found my poor Morris looking very ill and aged, doddling feebly in front of his house. We went in together however and he brightened up and told me of his maladies in a cheerful and not too desponding way. . . .’' When a fortnight later the Morrises visited Blunt at his country house, he observed in his diary (29 May) that Morris's health had worsened: he was now‘very feeble & aged. I fear from the luck of things that it is some form of consumption and that he will not recover, but his spirits are fairly good and he talks at times as brilliantly as ever. . . . his illness does not make him gloomy, only it troubles him in his work.' (Blunt's amateur diagnosis of consumption was correct.) These developments plunged Burne-Jones into misery: now I am frightened — & can think oflittle else — steadily he seems to be consuming away - without pain — but growing weaker & weaker and I can't think about anything else,' he declared in a letter to Blunt on 6 June.⁴ A few days later, on his doctor's recommendation, the Morrises left London for Folkestone in search of the beneficial effects of sea air. In July Morris, again on professional advice, took a voyage to Norway, but when he returned the following month to Hammersmith, his health displayed no sign of improvement. He now withdrew to his bedroom at Kelmscott House, which he never thereafter left for more than a few hours, though during the last month of his life there was a steady flow of visitors — the Burne-Joneses, Ellis, Mary De Morgan, Charles Fairfax Murray, Blunt, and others - to the room. On 9 September 1896 Morris made a new will, naming his wife, Ellis, and Cockerell as executors. When Quaritch called at Kelmscott House on 17 September, he discovered Morris‘worn out and feeble', emaciated, and staring out of the window at the river. ‘I fear a catastrophe is impending,'Quaritch wrote later the same day. To raise Morris's spirits, Burne-Jones came on Sundays and sat in the bedroom drawing illustrations for the Kelmscott Press edition of Sigurd the Volsung, and Ellis, elsewhere in the house, transcribed traditional Border ballads for another Kelmscott book that Morris hoped to publish. Morris himself, at intervals, struggled with the design for an initial word (‘Empty’) that was to appear in The Water of the Wondrous Isles (1897). 'He sat up & was going to design “Empty". . . but only just managed to black in the spaces, & just rough in the word,' Cockerell wrote in his diary on 4 September. And two days later: W.M. rather tired, but decidedly no worse. He had done a little more to “Empty".' But Morris never completed the design, and after his death Catterson-Smith had to put the finishing touches on it. On 28 Septenber Blunt witnessed Morris entering the diningroom at Kelmscott House like a man risen from the grave'. The following day Morris was in better spirits and insisted upon being taken outdoors in a bath chair and then walking about within the house; a short time later Cockerell, returning from an errand, found him upstairs with blood streaming from his mouth. Morris died on the morning of 3 October. The effect on his friends was devastating. Burne-Jones fell on his knees next to the body and burst into tears; Cockerell retreated to Morris's study opposite the bedroom and wept in private; Crane paid tribute to him by placing a sonnet and a wreath next to the Kelmscott Chaucer.that went on display the following Monday at the Arts and Crafts Exhibition. But it is entirely characteristic of Cockerell that he did not indulge his personal grief for more than a few minutes. When his sobbing abated, as he testified many years later, I set to work on all there was to be done."Jane Morris had little experience in handling money, Ellis was in poor health himself and lived in Torquay, and therefore almost the entire responsibility for managing the Morris family's financial affairs fell on Cockerell's shoulders. During the following months he engaged in ceaseless, almost frenzied activity. A buyer had to be found for Kelmscott House; arrangements were made for Morris's library to be sold; Jane Morris and her daughters required constant attention and sympathetic understanding. Above all, Cockerell had to avert the immediate collapse of the Kelmscott Press by pushing through to completion the books already in production and by devising a rational scheme for gradually closing down the Press. To the discerning eye there were soon signs of Morris's departure from the Kelmscott Press. The largest book then passing through the press was The Earthly Paradise, three volumes of which had appeared before Morris's death. Two projected folios were abandoned, however, with greater reluctance. In 1893 Furnivall had persuaded Morris to undertake an edition of Shakespeare's plays, with either P. A. The Froissart specimen reveals a stunning new departure in Morrisian ornamentation: though the two-column text is arranged in the same pattern as that of the Chaucer, the borders, incorporating coats of arms, are extraordinarily vigorous and spiky in appearance, an initial word is connected to one of the borders, and the initials extend into the margins. (Morris also designed some similar initials, with vines and tendrils trailing below, for the unfinished Sigurd."²) Such innovations will startle us only if we have mistakenly assumed that Morris's principles of book-design and ornamentation were static and wholly incapable of development. The Froissart specimen pages leave no doubt that Morris's comparatively early death robbed the world of a work which would have rivalled the Chaucer as one of the finest printed books of the post-medieval era. Swinburne's astute comment was, Why did not Morris take up & finish Froissart earlier — instead of the less appropriate work (Shelley, for one instance) on which the Kelmscott Press was occasionally wasted."3 Of the final group of books published at the Press, the one that caused Cockerell the greatest anxiety was Morris's The Story of Sigurd the Volsung, which was originally intended to be a folio adorned by some twenty-five Burne-Jones illustrations. The book had been planned as early as 1891, but Burne-Jones was not able to turn his attention to it until January 1896 after he completed the Chaucer designs. ‘To pacify and please Morris I will do what I can to adorn the Volsung Tale,' he wrote to Lady Leighton at about this time.' But to his assistant he confessed, 'I shan't like doing it so much as Chaucer. The scenery for one thing isn't quite a painter's scenery – it's more a poet's." By May, Burne-Jones was still working fitfully on the Sigurd designs and still complaining that he found the assignment uncongenial. ‘It's quite wonderful how distasteful the task of making drawings of it is to me,' he said. . . . Terrible the tragedy of it is, and the fine things in it are so much fitter for literature than for my art, and I've no turn for the dramatic." As Morris lay dying at Kelmscott House during September, Burne-Jones kept his promise by bringing the designs to his friend's bedroom each Sunday, thus extending to the last moment the intense intellectual and artistic relationship that had existed between them since their Oxford days. Burne-Jones was plainly relieved when the folio Sigurd was abandoned, but on the evening of 26 January 1897 Cockerell convinced him to furnish two illustrations for a scaled-down quarto version of the book, which was eventually published in February 1898. It was his last piece of work for the Kelmscott Press, and both he and Cockerell were conscious that it was the end of an epoch in Burne-Jones's career. Cockerell had lunch with him at the Grange, his home in South Kensington, on 7 October 1897, and made this terse record in his diary: EB-J alone & very nice. Those were the words he entered in his diary that Christmas Eve; earlier in the day, as the workmen waited to carry off his desk, Cockerell wrote a final letter to Jenny Morris from his office in Sunderland Cottage, regretting that the fog prevented a last nostalgic view across the Thames from his window. Builders at that moment were already beginning to turn the cottage into a granary.21 The last few sheets were printed in Sussex Cottage during the early months of 1898. Following two springlike days, Cockerell reported to Jenny on 7 January: Our pressman, there is only one left now, says that we need a frost for the ink rollers, which get soft & difficult to print with in damp weather. I hope it will come, as we have the second Sigurd picture to do next week, & the map for the Sundering Flood — and perhaps the first picture for Love is Enough, which Mr Hooper is now engraving — all of these will need his best skill —" The sole surviving pressman was Stephen Mowlem, who on 19 March printed the last copies of the last signature of the last Kelmscott Press book, A Note by William Morris. The final sheet, appropriately, was given to Walker; the penultimate sheet, signed by Mowlem and W.H. Bowden, the only other printer still on the premises, went into Cockerell's collection. The official publication date of the book was 24 March. The time had come, as Cockerell remarked to a correspondent the previous month, ‘to write EXPLICIT over the door of the Kelmscott Press'.²3 On 31 March Cockerell sent a benedictory letter to J. and J. Leighton (thanking them for all your care & attention to the binding of the Kelmscott Press during the seven years of its existence'4), said goodbye to Jane Morris, had lunch with Georgiana Burne-Jones at the Grange, and sailed the next day to Siena, leaving behind Mrs Peddie to deal with the Press correspondence until he returned in early May. The demise of the Kelmscott Press did not go unnoticed by the book trade or the press. ‘There has been a great rush on the German woodcuts & they were all sold out last week,'Cockerell observed on 30 November 1897.. . . A good many people have been left out in the cold, as orders keep coming in! I suppose it is the announcement that the Press is closing that has made people so eager."s Frank Hollings, the London dealer, trumpeted to his customers in December that there is no doubt that the dissolution of the “Kelmscott Press", . . . and the strictly limited number printed of each work, will cause them to rise in value by leaps and bounds', and he added with becoming modesty, 'I have a remarkable stock of these desirable volumes’.26 The Academy, in a long article entitled ‘The End of the Kelmscott Press' (12 December 1896), offered a balanced appraisal of Morris's achievement as a printer, concluding that ‘on one thing, at any rate, we are all agreed, that for better or for worse, the folio edition ofChaucer which appeared this summer forms a great landmark in the history of printing, and were sufficiently monumental in itself, had he produced no other book, to render the names of the Kelmscott Press and of William Morris memorable for all times'. ## Yet, when Cockerell walked out of the door of Sussex Cottage on the last day of March 1898, he did not succeed in putting the problems of the Kelmscott Press behind him. He had agreed to sell the presses and other equipment to C. R. Ashbee's Essex House Press (which will figure again in the next chapter), and several of Morris's printers, including Binning, decided to join forces with Ashbee as well. Eventually Hooper, Catterson-Smith, and Douglas Cockerell also contributed their services to the Essex House Press; Ashbee's decorated initials were freely adapted from Morris's, and in one of his books he even invoked the name of the Kelmscott Press in the colophon. Morris's old friends and associates were outraged by what they considered to be a shameless exploitation of a minor connection. To Walker it seemed futile to try to imitate the Kelmscott Press. ‘The main-spring is broken,' he remarked with his usual moderation to Ashbee, ‘and when the watch stops there is nothing further to be done."7 Others used less temperate language. The Press was brought to a definite end, and no one has the right to pose as successor to William Morris,' W. H. Bowden declared in a letter to a newspaper.28 Cockerell breathed threats oflawsuits, but Ellis counselled restraint. I certainly do not think Ashbee worth taking any heed of,' he wrote to Cockerell in June 1899. It is only the inmates or candidates for Earlswood [an Asylum for Idiots in Sussex], who are likely to confuse Ashbee with Morris, & I don't think they are worth troubling about. . . . Ashbee's initial difficulty I take to be entire ignorance of [printing] whether from the literary or artistic point of view.'9 Ellis was slightly embarrassed about the uproar, because he had earlier agreed to edit Shakespeare's poems for Âshbee (just as he had done for Morris), and he added jovially in his next letter, Perhaps you will learn in his next circular that he has bought me as a freehold. But I still think him quite unworthy of powder and shot.'º Cockerell devoted a good deal of time and energy during the closing years of the century to matters of this sort. In general he was much more disturbed than Ellis and Walker were by supposed threats to the reputation of Morris or the Kelmscott Press. When a copy of the Chaucer in unbound sheets appeared in a London auction room in the summer of 1899, Cockerell became agitated because he was convinced that it must have been stolen from the pressroom; a lengthy correspondence between him and Ellis ensued, with Ellis again advocating a calm response. (The mystery was never solved, though another set of Chaucer sheets was offered for sale by Sotheby's the following year; in both instances May Morris's friend Robert Steele and Sparling appear to have been implicated.) Walker favoured a proposal to print Morris's collected works in the Golden type at the Chiswick Press, but Cockerell vetoed it, and only five slender volumes of lectures by Morris were printed thus. The question of the ultimate disposition of the Kelmscott types continued to vex Cockerell for several decades. When May Morris was revising her will in 1919, Cockerell suggested that the types ‘might perhaps go to Oxford University, if Walker has nothing better to propose’.3' Seven years later he wrote to her that, with Mr Hornby's approval [St John Hornby was now one of the trustees of the Morris estate], I am transferring the Kelmscott Press type from the Chiswick Press (which has now passed into hands that we know not of) to the Cambridge University Press, which is now doing very good work. Apart from the fact that I am in Cambridge for a while the Press here is a permanent concern, whereas the Chiswick Press was recently in difficulties — so I hope you will also think it a wise change.'² Cockerell recommended to May in 1933 that she give the types to the Cambridge University Press, but it was not until 194o that they became the property of the Press by purchase.³ Morris's founts have recently been deposited in the Cambridge University Library. Today the flotsam and jetsam of the Kelmscott Press are scattered round the world. The types are in Cambridge; the woodblocks are in the British Museum; the presses are in Hammersmith, New York, and California; Morris's ‘bloomer book' is in Oxford; the address book and the ledger of the Press are in New York; a smaller account book is in Japan; and huge mounds of paper — all those precious scraps of proofs and trial pages so assiduously collected by Cockerell — have floated away to several continents. Confronted with these sad reminders of the impermanence of all human endeavours, we may perhaps find some consolation in the thought that the more durable monument to the memory of William Morris and the Kelmscott Press is, as the Academy suggested, the fifty-two wonderful books, particularly the Chaucer, printed in Hammersmith between 1891 and 1898.
