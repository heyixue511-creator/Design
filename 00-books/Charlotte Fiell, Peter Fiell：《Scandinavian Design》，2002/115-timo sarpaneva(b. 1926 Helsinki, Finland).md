![](images/3592fdb478c51d11943ae88d43fc96f35354be6ceb579fc0311063ff4478af5b.jpg) Timo Sarpaneva, Festivo candleholders for littala, 1966 # timo sarpaneva (b. 1926 Helsinki, Finland) ![](images/3755ecdddff6ca08f8f670072ed49addabbef0a80d836aa308f08e4a62fcb9c7.jpg) One of Finland's most prominent designers, Timo Sarpaneva studied at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu (University of Art and Design), Helsinki, from 1941 to 1948. He won secondprize in a glass competition held by the Riihimäkiglassworks in 1949 and the following year became a product designer and head of the exhibition section at the renowned Karhula-littala glassworks. During the early 1950s, Sarpaneva also worked extensively as a textile designer, receiving a silver medal for his textile designs at the 1951 (IX) Milan Triennale. He also taught textile printing and design at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu from 1953 to 1957, and briefly worked as art director for the Porin Puuvilla cotton mill from 1955 to 1956. It was, however, his ground-breaking glassware designs that brought him the greatest international acclaim. One of his first technical innovations at littala was a steam blowing method, which was used for his early work, such as the Kajakki (1953), Maailmankaunein (1954) and Linnunsilmä (1953) vessels, as well as for his delicately colour tinted thin glass plates known as Aquarelles. These remarkable art glass pieces reflected a new sculptural confidence and creative vibrancy in Finnish design. During the mid- 1950s, Sarpaneva introduced his i-Glass range, which attempted to bridge the gap between expensive art glass and utility glassware. This industrially produced product line comprised seventeen items that were available in several colours, including lilac-grey, blue-grey, smoke-grey and green-grey, which could be used together to produce different effects. The 1950s and 1960s saw a great dynamic synergy at the littala glassworks, with Sarpaneva and Tapio Wirkkala vying good-naturedly with each other to create new forms and techniques. One of Sarpaneva's most interesting and influential glass designs from this period was his Finlandia series of vases (c. 1964), which incorporated a technique using moulds made from slabs of roughly hewn and carved alder wood, which were charred by the red-hot glass. The patterns resulting from this technique bore the close imprint of the wood's structure and powerfully evoked a sense of icy rawness. The nature of this process also ensured that each design was unique, as each time a mould was used it was burnt further, meaning that the patterns would be changed. The charred wood technique was also used to produce Sarpaneva's well-known Festivo candleholders(1966), but because of the expense of continually replacing the wooden moulds, metal moulds were eventually used instead. Both the Finlandia and Festivo ranges were highly influential and heralded the fashion for industrially mass-produced “frosted” glassware in the 1970s. Sarpaneva later developed other glassmaking techniques like that used for his Archipelago range (1978), in which different sized “trapped” bubbles produced a solid ice-like effect. For his Claritas range (1984), he used a similarly innovative technique that trapped and suspended large bubbles within a clear glass casing, while his elegant Marce/ series (1990) incorporated a new blow-moulding process that pushed the material to its very limits. Many of Sarpaneva's designs used closed “round-square” forms that relate to the smooth pebbles of the Indian Tantric cult, most notably his Claritas vases for littala and his cast-iron cooking pot with wooden handle (1960) for W. Rosenlew & Co. Other designs, such as his large Lasiaika glass sculptures, incorporated more open and expressive forms. In 1963, he was made an Honorary Royal Designer for Industry in London and in 1967 received an honorary doctorate from the Royal College of Art, London. In tribute to his achievements he also received a State Award for Industrial Art from the Finnish Government in 1985. Beyond his exquisite glassware for littala, Sarpaneva has also designed ceramics for Rosenthal, metalware for W. Rosenlew & Co. and Opa, textiles for Kinnasand, Finlayson Forssa and Tampella, plastic wares for Ensto, and other glassware for Corning and Venini, as well as books and theatre sets. Like so many other Finnish designers, Sarpaneva has worked with equal skill in the realms of both exclusive one-off studio production and industrial mass-production. Through his supreme mastery of materials and artistic sensibility, Sarpaneva's work evokes the spiritual essence and raw beauty of nature, and in so doing has had a crucial influence on the development of Finnish glass design for over fifty years. ![](images/64349be879942b58d4b5424a7069e21a14c7e88716db30c4e8c0281b1fc9d3be.jpg) ![](images/cbb9990613d38e8cc2f7a6b81ce25e2c73679d8c33ea3f1011e2d9530aeb0c97.jpg) Δ4 Timo Sarpaneva, Finlandia vase for littala, 1964 ![](images/73e69cbb50b6decd491bd6658b9eee434a26a8ddc492b16d6f0549ac97242e7a.jpg) Δ> Timo Sarpaneva, Finlandia vases for littala, 1964 – These remarkable vases were made using wooden moulds that were charred by the moulten glass. InSeptember of the same year the “Dan Viking”(the first DC-4 tobe painted in the distinctive SAS colours) made its maiden transatlantic flight from Stockholm to New York via Copenhagen. Later in the 1950s SASbecame a high-profile ambassador of “Scandinavianess”with its eye-catching blue and white logo and the chic uniforms of its aircrew. The company helped promote its image even further by commissioning Arne Jacobsen to design a new landmarkterminal-cum-hotel adjacent to the central railway station in Copenhagen. The resulting Royal Hotel and SAS Terminal (1956-1961)was a flagship International Style building, which incorporated state-of-the-art communications systems and interiors that exemplified the cool understated elegance of post-war Scandinavian Modernism. Jacobsen conceived the project as a fully integrated Gesamtkunstwerk (a total work of art), which included numerous site-specific furnishings, from his well-known Egg and Swan chairs (1957–1958) to specially designed door handles and cutlery. In the late 1950s SAS restated its commitment to progressive design by commissioning Sigurd Persson to develop a forward-looking grey plastic in-flight service. One of the greatest pioneers of Scandinavian industrial design, Sigvard Bernadotte was also responsible for the design of an in-flight tray and cutlery for the company. Later in 1988 Ergonomi Design Gruppen designed an innovative and stylish plastic in-flight coffee jug for SAS that minimized the possibility of spillage. The following decade, SAS began a comprehensive programme of change with the intention of raising its levels of competitiveness and profitability, and a new updated corporate identity programme was instigated in order to strengthen the airline's brand recognition. Introduced in 1998, this corporate image makeover included new-look aircraft (both inside and out), new Euroclass lounges designed by Thomas Eriksson, a modernized logo, and new typography, graphics, colours and uniforms. All of this was intended to convey “the basics of Scandinavian design: simplicity, functionality, informal elegance and a respect for materials and resources” and to accentuate the airline's profile. In 2001, the new SAS corporate identity won the main category of the Core Design-Corporate Design Awards, testifying to the airline's continuing patronage of contemporary Scandinavian design and its commitment to “developing new products and concepts to increase the focus on customers in the areas of quality, service and comfort”. ![](images/0cd88322b408bf299535eb9e29526a8c771fa1b64ee177d24c320a3da4234f01.jpg) Δ Arne Jacobsen, entrance lobby of the SAS Royal Hotel, Copenhagen ∇ Ergonomi Design Gruppen, SAS in-flight service coffee jug for SAS, 1988 ![](images/1383c4f3ad3da8cf8d36c43cfda51555394fa05e81eee6b42144bf9be60a49c8.jpg)
