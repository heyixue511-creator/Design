# JOHN OUTRAM b.1934 BRITISH ARCHITECT OF EXUBERANT BUILDINGS THAT BOTH REFLECT AND MOCK HISTORICISM. The man who, according to the London Sunday Times, became "Britain's most eye-catching architect" didn't originally intend to become one. The third generation of a military family, John Outram lived in British India until he was twelve years old, and when he was sixteen wrote to his father, "I want to be an engineer and design things that work."3' He changed his mind, however, and did his national service as a pilot, intending to pursue a career in aeronautics. Though the service gave him the opportunity to visit America and Canada, he found flying insufficiently stimulating, and changed course to study architecture. He trained in London, at Regent Street Polytechnic (now Westminster University), qualified as an architect in i96o, and worked for architectural offices first in Cyprus where he became attracted by classical forms, and then for public sector and private firms, including classical architect Louis de Soissons Outram opened his London practice in 1973, when his plan to emigrate to Cyprus was cut short by political tensions that ended in Turkey's invasion of the island. As John Outram Architects became a going concern, he progressed from designing kitchens and bathrooms for relatives to modest commercial buildings and then to more ambitious projects. The Pumping Station (i988), an exuberant public structure on the Isle of Dogs, London, brought considerable publicity—and awards—for its originality. The design is a tongue-in-cheek mix of styles that pokes fun at both historicism and the machine aesthetic, with a classical pediment of corrugated iron, columns with polychrome capitals, and a non-functioning turbine as ornament. Outram went on to win competitions for major projects, including overseas commissions in the United States and the Netherlands. Outram's work is as much a reflection of his outsize personality and outspoken style as of his allegiance to postmodern principles. His virtuoso use of color and deft application of classical motifs and ornament make his buildings altogether different from those of his contemporaries; immediately recognizable, they are guaranteed to lift spirits. ![](images/5e4377c76b4beff4fc9aedb6c98d916c7aba4f2fdaca5262ea442c5fa237feb4.jpg) In Outram's hands, classical forms are ingeniously manipulated to serve as spacedefiners as well as ornamental motifs. In one case, he conceived an altogether new capital that he (tongue firmly in cheek) designated the Sixth Order. In another, for the Judge Institute of Management Studies (i995, renamed Judge Business School) at Cambridge University, he used clusters of columns to construct the Basilican Core and a series of convivial social spaces in what would otherwise have been a cavernous and unwelcoming interior. Believing that anything he might want to design for his buildings had already been invented, he came up with variations on existing forms that look altogether new. Handcrafted details are part of any Outram project—he considers the British RIGHT Four-story central space in Duncan Hall, 1997. to be natural craftspeople whose pleasure in making things has been hamstrung by contemporary culture. His buildings give free expression to such elements, and have a charm that reflects the amiable nature of the architect himself. Though engaging in practice, Outram was always interested in concept, committing himself to devising a “universal theory of architecture." Over a period of seventeen years he documented his ideas in “44 Lectures' that dissected every aspect of the craft. His writings, discursive and opinionated, are filled with personality. Rejecting a label for his architecture, he asserts that Postmodernism was a “spineless title"3² whose undoing was its political unacceptability in seeking discipline and pursuing the past, particularly in the context of the Cold War.
