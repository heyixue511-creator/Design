Febvre's objection to the flattening of local detail is telling, as he was a pioneer of a historical approach that took geographical context into account. Such matters also interested Neurath and his colleagues but it was impossible in the Vienna Method to show local details when indicating quantities on a map of the whole world, at least at the scale of the charts in Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft.2⁵ Neurath stated in his introduction: 'In order to make the essentials stick in the memory, many details must be omitted.' The decision about what is ‘essential' was naturally open to question; Neurath believed that selection and simplification were legitimate educational strategies for providing encyclopedic information that could help individuals to find their voice.

Both Woytinsky and Febvre suggested that Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft could be more suitable for children than for grown-ups. Undoubtedly it was not aimed primarily at sophisticated readers such as them, as Neurath made clear:

Pictorial education provides tools that make it possible for the less educated to gain a surer orientation in a short time than the highly educated usually ever reach, in spite of more exact study. The fully educated person, precisely through his word-education, is less suited to learn by pictorial education, so that the Vienna Method is more difficult to access for him than for thé worker who predominantly soaks things up with his eyes. In practical life, however, a reliable, rough orientation, such as pictorial education provides, is often much more important than more detailed knowledge that is not mastered with complete assurance. This mastery only comes to a small number of specialists.

He added that the Vienna Method could be a'bridge' between illiterate people and the rest of the world, and asserted that ‘all these problems of social education are also problems of school education'.²⁶

Since 1927 the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum had cultivated links with schoolchildren and teachers. In its regular supplement to the educational journal Das Bild, the Museum stated that its work in pictorial statistics was a part of Viennese school reform'.²7 The subtitle of Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft – bildstatistisches Elementarwerk (elementary work in pictorial statistics) – implied that children were intended to be at least part of its audience. It made a conscious link to the earlier Elementarwerk of 1774, a nine-volume work by Johann Bernhard Basedow, featuring numerous copper-engraved illustrations by Daniel Chodowiecki.2⁸ Neurath connected Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft explicitly with another standard schoolbook by commenting that the atlas was intended as the beginning of a new Orbis sensualium pictus in the spirit of Comenius. He was critical of the images in both of these publications, and he stated how Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft was different in his introduction to it:

Unity of conceptual representation requires unity of pictorial reproduction. Each chart can be compared with the others, and by comparison new connections become apparent that could not be shown on a single chart. The design of each individual chart always takes the following principle into account: where the same object is represented, the same symbol is used, essential divergences in form mean divergences in content.

Neurath also stressed that it gave a 'colourful picture of human civilization and its development'.²º

The nature of the atlas as a collection of unbound, loose leaves enabled it to be used as a kind of portable exhibition: multiple charts could be displayed simultaneously. There are several mini-series within the 10o charts that suggest such a strategy (see pp. 207–15 below).

Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft was used in this way as part of trials made with pictorial statistics in schools in Austria and Germany. Reports of these trials provide valuable information about the use and reception of the publication. Vera Stenzel-Deutsch, a teacher at a Viennese secondary school, reported the following:

My experiences were with a form of 11–12 year old girls of low intelligence. Though officially the curriculum is the same as for the grammar schools, I have to restrict myself to a bare minimum if I want to have any lasting effect at all. The difficulty with such children is first to arouse their attention and comprehension, then to impress anything into their memory.

In geography the curriculum for this age group is Africa and Asia: for history – scenes from Greek, Roman and medieval life.

I used charts from Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft for the first time when I had dealt with Africa and wanted to summarize. I used chart 33, 'Vegetation zones of the earth', and started with the colour explanations concealed. With the help of the children I enlarged part of this chart to the scale of our wall map of Africa, and now it gave great pleasure to recognize the various areas and to compare them with each other. The next chart was no. 22, ‘States and population 193o' [see p. 207 below]. The children themselves related density of population with the types of vegetation and wondered why the Gulf of Guinea was more densely populated than other areas. So we were led to talk about cocoa and

palm oil and of the position of Africa in world trade. Up to then the children had looked with pleasure at the economic map included in their atlas and had derived many erroneous ideas. But when numbers were added to words, the whole book knowledge came alive.

As soon as things are put before children in simple symbols and numbers they stimulate imitation. The rather laborious and inadequate copies of maps are replaced by diagrams, and this active participation furthered the grasping and remembering of facts greatly. In the course of time we have made our own independent table summarizing things referring to Africa and have tried the same approach for Austria with data we got from the Vienna Fair.

According to the children's view learning has become easier and more attractive. 'Why did we not learn like this always? It is so much easier to remember.' They asked for the same method when dealing with Asia. So we have at the side of the usual map of Asia a sketch of the outlines in the same scale, on which vegetation and production are drawn. I cannot imagine any longer how to teach geography without such diagrams. They are my greatest support not only in the summary but also at the initial and all later stages of teaching the subject.3º

Such an experience encouraged Neurath's later view that Isotype 'may be used only in addition to the usual educational technique, but there is also a possibility of putting the Isotype technique in the centre and building up the educational process around this backbone, without giving the visual material too much space'.³1

Other teachers, while generally positive about the value of pictorial statistics in school, had some reservations: a teacher of arithmetic and geometry shared Woytinsky's view about excessive rounding off of numerical data; a teacher of geography and history considered Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft not specifically suitable for the classroom because some charts were very complex, and suggested that the charts should also be larger to enable group viewing. (The publication was already expensive and printing in a larger format would have made it more so, and also unwieldy.) Hans Pemmer, a school principal involved in the Viennese school experiments, concluded that not all charts from Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft were suitable for 1o-14 year olds. He recommended it for less gifted' pupils, and especially for teaching geography, history and science. He concluded:

If pictorial statistics are well chosen and not too often used in education, they will render a useful service particularly for the visually gifted ones to make things clearer and to remember them better. ... Let us not forget that pictorial statistics has an educational value regarding taste because of its modern style.ª

In addition to these informal, observational reports, some psychotechnical' tests were made on Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft at a technical college in Braunschweig. Psychologist Dr Helmut von Bracken and his colleague W. Meyer tested two charts from the atlas alongside two examples depicting the same data with the usual, naturalistic representation.'³³ Twenty-one students were asked to interpret the sense of the charts on seeing them, and then to do the same a week later without seeing the charts again, in order to test what they had retained from each style of statistical representation. Von Bracken reported that charts in the Vienna Method already gave better results on immediate interpretation, but that the really significant differences emerged after one week: the more precise figures in the naturalistic' examples were rounded off in the memory of the test subjects, with many errors resulting, whereas the figures contained in the schematic representations of Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft were retained much better and more accurately. He concluded that 'representations according to the Vienna Method are remembered about two and a half times better than the traditional representations'. 34

The design of this experiment would no doubt be considered primitive by cognitive psychologists today, but Neurath's willing submission of material to such a test reflects his ambitions to make Isotype a method of ‘turning the statements of science into pictures'.³⁵ Neurath was not shy of publicizing the advantages of the Vienna Method but he did not seize on these test results as an opportunity to lend it a stamp of scientific authority; he was aware that ‘the importance of the whole Isotype technique cannot be analysed by means of such single investigations'.³⁶

## Further plans for an ‘Isotype encyclopedia'

Before Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft was printed Neurath had already approached Friedrich Adler (son of Viktor Adler, founder of the Austrian Social Democrat Party) at the Labour and Socialist International in Zurich to request his collaboration on a book titled Arbeit und Wirtschaft which Neurath implied would develop material from the atlas then in preparation.³7 Nothing came of this, nor of various other proposals Neurath made towards realizing the ‘visual thesaurus/Isotype encyclopedia'. Strangely, it seems that he did not regard the atlas of 3o charts produced by the Vienna Museum and published in 1932, Technik und Menschheit, as a partial realization of the larger project along with Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft.38

From 1936 Neurath aligned his plan for a pictorial encylopedia with plans for the International Encyclopedia of Unified Science, of which he was editor-in-chief. He proposed to supplement the projected 26 volumes of this encyclopedia (only a fraction of which were ever published) with a ten-volume visual thesaurus. 'It is evident that this Encyclopedia will tend towards the unification of not only the scientific language, but also graphic representation', Neurath stated; 'All pictures that the Encyclopedia will present will be made of standardised elements, if technical, biological, sociological or other subjects are to be represented. ... The Mundaneum Institute in The Hague will adapt the picture language ISOTYPE (International System Of TYpographic Picture Education) to the particular needs of the Encyclopedia; so far it has used this language elsewhere.'³º Neurath's co-editors, Charles Morris and Rudolf Carnap, both had doubts about this component of the plan;⁴º yet that did not deter Neurath from working towards the visual thesaurus. He later claimed that a significant amount of preparatory work for it had been done in the Netherlands, but the material had to be abandoned there.

Neurath realized that support from a publisher or other funding body was necessary to make progress and he continually sent outlines of the project – which he called 'our great plan' – to individuals who could possibly help.4¹ The book packaging firm of Adprint, for which the Isotype Institute designed many charts from 1942, declined to spon sor it, so Neurath proposed an International Isotype thesaurus' to Karl Mannheim (editor of a series of books published by Routledge Kegan Paul), suggesting that a guild of publishers would be necessary, along with additional subsidy. His plans were not Utopian, he explained, because the concrete example of Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft proved that it could be done.⁴² However, the extent of Neurath's plan was daunting: documents were prepared at the Isotype Institute including a preliminary scheme for the first 1,2oo charts' of the encyclopedia. The subjects of these projected charts can be seen as an elaboration of themes from Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft. It was estimated that the scheme would take four years to produce at a total cost of £1oo,ooo.4ª Neurath's concept of the visual thesaurus expanded to encompass all media in which the Isotype Institute sought to work: It is important that all kinds of Isotypes, charts, panels, maps, models, films etc may be regarded as parts of one Encyclopedia', he explained.⁴⁴ Such a plan was unlikely to have found support in wartime Britain.

In chart g6, 'Human groups of the world', the graphic solution for showing relative population is not as obvious as it may seem. It should be compared with chart no. 22, 'States and population 193o' (see p. 207 above), which derives from the same data but features smaller, generic [hu]man' pictograms positioned on a world map. The colour code used for the pictograms is common to both charts, but in chart g6 the geographical dimension is omitted and differences in the form of pictograms are introduced to carry the theme visually. Neurath wanted to show here the striking correlation that emerges if humanity is simply arrayed in three groups – 'white'’, 'brown/black/red' and 'yellow' people (as he called them):

Experience shows that even people with academic training (discounting specialists) are inclined to accept that there are about four times as many yellow' people as 'white'. Lesser educated people are content with two or three times as many. If, on the other hand, the chart about 'Human groups of the world' from the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum, or its elementary work in pictorial statistics Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft, is allowed to work on you, it is difficult to forget – as has been tested – that there are roughly as many 'yellow' as 'white'.45

It is perhaps tempting to perceive the colour-coding and cultural stereotyping in these charts as naive or even chauvinistic, with the 'whites' in neutral shades – as if a 'default' – and the other groups marked as different by colour. But closer examination belies this: the whites here are culturally stereotyped in equal measure to the other groups, with selective headwear providing differentiation. Indeed, in their grey apparel, the whites recede by comparison with the other, colourful groups. This chart is a neardefinitive Isotype presentation of this subject, which had been essayed many times since 1925, and its shows a great reduction of physiognomic, racial features compared to previous iterations (see pp. 51, 68, 13o above). The graphic features here can be considered in terms of their function in a kind of pictorial 'grammar', which gives meaning to changes in single elements. Colour is one such element, and, although it is used here iconically, it also functions as part of a simplified graphic repertoire designed to maximize impact. It seems clear that colour is not intended to be naturalistic, any more than the schematic forms of the human figures. The notes on this chart in the appendix to Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft state:

We have counted as 'coloured' [Farbigen] all those whom the English call 'coloured men'. For the social problems of today the Indians and Arabs belong in one group, not for instance the Indians and Europeans, despite linguistic relations. ... Although skin colour is not the decisive reason for differentiation – south Italians look more brown than white – the human groups are differentiated by colour in the picture.4⁶

Further clarification was given in the Vienna Museum's periodical, Fernunterricht, in response to a reader's question – 'What should one understand by "human groups"?':

In its elementary work in pictorial statistics [Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft] the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum uses the expression human groups' in order to indicate the political situation, not to clarify descent or questions of race.47

It would be only natural, in any case, for Europeans and their descendants to be presented as the 'norm' in such charts, which are contained in a German-language publication intended primarily for a Central-European audience. But one intention of Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft, as of much work in the Vienna Method, was to educate Central-Europeans about their brethren in the rest of the world, and its even-handedness in this respect was undoubtedly forward-looking in its time. It was stated in Fernunterricht (probably by Neurath) that 'Modern empirical sociology attempts to describe all peoples as other natural subjects and not to insert praise and blame from a Central-European standpoint into each description.'48

It is clear from Neurath's writings that he was not a supporter of Western hegemony: Roughly speaking, the whites are above all carriers of capitalism and imperialism. They are the “masters”, with battleships and poison gas at their disposal.' 49
