Rarely was it ideologically motivated, except in its commitment to individuality. The new aesthetic spread through a small network of publications, some—like Emigre (i984-2o05), Typographische Monatsblätter\* (TM, 1933-), and Push Pin Graphic (i957–8o)—specifically dedicated to the field, and others—like The Face (i98o-2oo4) and Ray Gun (i992-200o)—reflecting lifestyle and culture. Although some designers began exploring new directions as early as the i96os, postmodern graphic design began to take hold only after the introduction of the Macintosh computer in i984. The Macintosh introduced a new visual language and enabled unique graphic effects that were previously possible only in film. It also saved time: freed from tedious cutting, pasting, and typesetting, designers could spend their time being creative. The software not only gave designers new options, but facilitated collaboration over distances, even internationally. As it developed, postmodern graphic design had no unifying theme or stylistic standards but represented a new attitude toward the practice, and a new visibility for its practitioners. As its influence grew to incorporate public signage and corporate branding, graphic designers came to see themselves as communicators rather than merely agents for expressing a client's message, and the works they produce have increasingly been influential elements of the contemporary environment. To paraphrase Marshall McLuhan, the medium became a critical part of the message,3 if not the message itself.
