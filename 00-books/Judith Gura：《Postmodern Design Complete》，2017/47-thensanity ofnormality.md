## CARIN GOLDBERG b.1953 NEW YORK DESIGNER WHOSE BOOK JACKETS BROUGHT INNOVATIVE GRAPHICS TO A WIDE AUDIENCE. Carin Goldberg is known for her ingenious use of type to create images and her ability to work at both high art and mass-market level; both have served her well in a career that includes the design of, according to the artist, "a zillion book covers."2ı A native New Yorker, she grew up on Long Island and New Jersey and studied at Cooper Union, where she majored in painting, since graphic design was not yet a strong component of the school's program. She graduated in i975 and, like several other prominent graphic designers of her generation, began her career in the communications industry. She was sent for an interview at CBS, where, despite lacking a design portfolio, she was hired as a junior designer to do the advertising for its TV Guide. Moving to CBS Records, she designed for all levels of the music market, from rock 'n' roll to jazz to classical album covers, and gained further experience at Atlantic Records and Mademoiselle magazine. After she established her own firm in i982, one of Goldberg's early commissions was from Warner Bros. Records for the cover of Madonna's debut album, and she continued to design for labels including Motown, Nonesuch, and EMI. Looking to diversify, and interested in work that would be visible in many retail outlets as opposed to those intended for a narrow target audience,²² she began designing book jackets—perhaps a natural transition—and rapidly built a reputation in that important category, designing more than 125 covers a year for virtually every major American publisher, including Simon & Schuster, Random House, Alfred A. Knopf, HarperCollins, and Doubleday, as well as small university presses. In the era when most reading matter was on paper, the book jacket was the most effective stimulus for sales. Goldberg has designed books by authors as popular as Raymond Carver and Kurt Vonnegut, and as esoteric as Reiner Maria Rilke and Michel Foucault. Her designs have no trademark style or preferred technique, but all make strong visual statements, with arresting and evocative imagery that draws the eye to the title rather than the design itself. Calling herself a transmedia artist, a provocative term that doesn't begin to describe the intriguing range of her work, April Greiman has moved easily between hand and computer, making adventurous choices that have raised her to the top ranks of American graphic designers. Among her important contributions to the profession has been her role as a cutting-edge progenitor of what was called California New Wave style A native New Yorker, Greiman wanted to attend Rhode Island School of Design but was directed instead to the graphic design program at Kansas City Art Institute. At the time, she did not know what graphic design meant, but that did not stop her; she applied and was accepted. Though trained in the prevailing International Style, she was inspired by her instructors at Kansas City to go beyond it, and traveled after her i97o graduation to Basel, Switzerland, for graduate work at the Allgemeine Künstgewerbeschule Basel (now Schule für Gestaltung Basel). There, the innovative designer WOLFGANG WEINGART introduced her to a radical new approach that departed from the grid, introducing varied typefaces and type weights and broadening the communicative potential of typography. This aesthetic would later evolve into New Wave, and Greiman would become one of his most influential students, helping to introduce the style in America. In i976 she moved to Los Angeles— a courageous choice, since it meant leaving an established design community for one with a limited arts culture and little design identity. It was an opportunity to chart new territory, and Greiman named her new studio Made in Space, reflecting her interest in transforming printed matter into something that occupied more than simply two dimensions. For several years, she collaborated with Jayme Odgers in work for clients like the China Club and California Institute of the Arts. In i982, CalArts asked her to head their graphic design program, and she lobbied to rename the department Visual Communications, to more clearly describe the potential scope of the field. She left in 1984 to return to full-time practice. Always intrigued by technology, Greiman was an early adopter of the computer as a tool of graphic design, helping to usher in a new era in the field. While many of her contemporaries resisted computers, considering them a threat ![](images/3ad640c5cae073abfdfd35b90327c52e4449a11749d1702d864c52218ece1657.jpg) to handwork, she embraced them, making use of pixelation and other irregularities as integral elements of her graphic art. She began to work with video as well, exploring combinations of video imagery with New Wave photography and elements of traditional design. One of Greiman's most radical projects was for Design Quarterly, the influential journal published by Minneapolis's Walker Art Center. Issue 133 (ı986), which she designed, presented her work in a radical format: instead of a thirtytwo-page magazine, she created a 3 × 6ft (90 × 18ocm) fold-out poster, composed using a Macintosh, that layered text and images, including video images and one of her own naked body. Titled "Does It Make Sense?" the project encouraged designers to accept computers as integral to contemporary graphic design, or to an entirely new, more multidisciplinary and as yet unnamed practice of communication.
