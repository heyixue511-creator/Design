![](images/4979c3ce60c9823cf0f06737ba99aa6c05e2a8f79d9a1d35bc56c731b15fb1f9.jpg) Hrafnkell Birgisson, graphic rendering of Homobile seating/ storage unit, 1999 # hrafnl birgion 6b. 1969 Rekjvk Iceland) ![](images/fb6182b3e73f12c3e1e32fa682d70dcbaa3c3a93d34bcd3acf0b9e28cb849c09.jpg) The Icelandic designer Hrafnkell Birgisson studied at the Reykjavik School of Art (1991–1992), the Icelandic College of Arts & Crafts (1992–1993), and the Polytechnic Hafnarfjördur (1993-1994). In 1995 he moved to Germany, where he studied product design at the Hochschule der Bildenden Künste (Academy of Art) inSaarbrücken under Andreas Brandolini(b. 1951), Harald Hullmann and François Burkhardt, and graduated in 2000. During thisfive-year period he also undertook a design studies course taught by Professor Achim Heine (b. 1955)at the Hochschule der Künste (Academy of Arts) in Berlin. Working within the fields of graphic design, furniture design, product design and interior design, Birgisson (who now lives in Berlin) has forged an international reputation for his idiosyncraticwork that often playfully subverts function.He has won numerous prizes, including the Derag Design Award (Munich) in 1997 for his Sleep & Go hotel pod. He also received a scholarship from the DAAD(Deutscher Akademischer Austauschdienst) in 1998. Birgisson's Homobile seating/storage unit (1999) reveals his interest in multi-functional design solutions, while hisHábolli teacup glasses(1998) exhibit the quirkiness that is so characteristic of much contemporary Icelandic design, from graphics to fashion. To date, Birgisson'smost interesting and potentially farthest-reaching design is his Sleep & Go (1997), which is an extremelyinnovative example of low-cost product architecture. Designed with industrial mass production in mind, the Sleep & Go could potentially be used as a form of low-cost easy-to-site emergency shelter. The design also predicts a less materialistic and more nomadic way of living. He subsequently worked with the aircraft manufacturers Douglas and Boeing on the design of aeroplanes forSAs – an experience that gave him a valuable insightinto industrial design practice at one of the highest technical levels. In 1949 he met the designer Sigvard Bernadotte who shared his interests, and the following year they established one of the first Scandinavian industrial design consultancies under the name Sigvard Bernadotte & Acton Bjørn, Industridesign. This Copenhagen-based practice not onlydesigned metalwork and ceramics, but also industrial equipment and machines. Among their best known work was plastic kitchenware for Rosti (1950) and office equipment for Facit (1958). In 1966, Bjørn won a Danish ID Prize for his Beolit 500 transistor radio for Bang& Olufsen. Produced from 1965to1969, this design was one of the first radios to achieve a functional simplicity comparable to that of a telephone. The same year Bjørn established his own design practice, Acton Bjørn Tegnestue, in Copenhagen, which specialized in household products and appliances as well as office furniture and packaging. He also received much recognition for his corporate identity work, mainly executed for companies involved in banking andinsurance. Bjørn's product designs were classically Scandinavian in that they elegantly balanced practical function with simple sculptural forms. ![](images/99e0b21736c16cbf2b025350f9d88105f9488b2dfcf18fd6f4c095174d35d41f.jpg)
