The exterior was clearly modernist: white, with ribbon windows, balconies, and a roof terrace. The interior is not: materials, color, light fittings, chair backs, and the clock are solidly Art Deco. The spread of modernism was stymied in parts of Europe by political factors. A repressive government in the U.S.S.R. in the 1920s stamped out the early flickers of Constructivism—an expressionist architectural movement that shared modernism’s enthusiasm for exposed concrete structures without decorative finishes. In the 1930s German and Austrian fascism increasingly made clear its preference for conventional Neoclassicism. Fascist Italy was more tolerant and in the architectural preparations for the Esposizione Universale Roma (Universal Exposition Rome) planned for 1942 seemed to have found a strain of modernism, a stripped classicism, that might have found popular favor. ## BRITAIN Britain’s aesthetes were aware of what was happening in Europe but the architectural anarchy that emerged in France and Germany seemed rather odd. In 1928 the British writer Evelyn Waugh satirized architectural modernists in his novel, Decline and Fall. It features a socialite and white slaver Margot Beste-Chetwynde who wanted a “clean and square” modernist country house and engaged a German architect whom an admirer declared to be “a man worth watching. He was in Moscow at one time and in the Bauhaus at Dessau . . . he’s got right away from Le Corbusier, anyway.” That Waugh could presume that his readers would be familiar with his targets suggests that, even by the mid 1920s, the new architecture had come to the attention of his readers—but was not being taken seriously. French and German aesthetes were iconoclasts. Britain, always intrigued by its own past, was reluctant to take radical departures from it seriously. The nation had few radical practitioners in any of the arts and they were often the frivolous and camp scions of the upper classes. They included the decorative painter Rex Whistler (1905–44) and Oliver Messel (1904–78), a theater designer known for the rococo sets, rich colors, and lavish draperies with which he decorated the London flats of the fashionable wealthy. Arguments have been made for the influence of British Arts and Crafts on architectural modernism but Arts and Crafts practitioners were revivalists rather than seekers after new truths. Architects admire Charles Rennie Mackintosh’s gray exteriors but it is perhaps more credible to claim that he anticipated not modernism but Art Deco on the evidence of his refurbishment of a small terraced house, in 1916–7 in Northampton, England (19.2). There, his continuing commitment to decoration is clear but the density and intense colors of his patternmaking does not look back to the delicacies of his Art Nouveau period. 19.2 ![](images/e5d08033db97ef7a83deaaaa9079eed1bcfcb58d12edfad35cf46f773c17c0e4.jpg) Charles Rennie Mackintosh, 78 Derngate, Northampton, England, 1916–7. If Mackintosh, because of his white interiors and dark gray exteriors, is credited with being a forerunner of modern architecture, then his remodeling of this small house anticipates Art Deco. It can be seen as a bridge between his— and Josef Hoffmann’s—obsession with the square and part of his search for a non-traditional decorative language. The first truly modernist house in Britain is generally considered to be High and Over in Amersham, Buckinghamshire (1930–1) designed by a New Zealander, Amyas Connell (1901–80). It has long ribbons of windows and a roof terrace under a floating canopy—even a small balcony. The interior is lined with rich red wooden paneling and is Art Deco rather than modernist (19.1). Wells Coates (1895–1958) was a Canadian born in Tokyo and therefore another citizen of the British Empire. He raised the bar in 1934 with his Isokon Building (also known as Lawn Road Flats) in Hampstead, London (19.4). It is a long low block of twenty-four studio and eight onebedroom apartments with a restaurant and bar for residents. The units were intended for creative individuals, who, it transpired, included Soviet spies. Other residents were Walter Gropius and Marcel Breuer while en route to the United States. Between 1941 and 1947, it also housed the British novelist Agatha Christie, who compared its exterior, with its long external access balconies, to an ocean liner. The exterior was painted a pale pink but appeared white in photographs and added to the misconception that modernist buildings were white. The architectural critic J. M. Richards said it came as close to being a “machine for living in” as anything Le Corbusier designed. The interiors were rigorously plain and free of moldings (19.3). The upholstered furniture was foursquare and pattern-free, the drapes had discreet patterns, a runner under the window allowed the table to be slid against a wall to make room in a minimal flat. Furniture was machine made and common to all apartments (19.5). ![](images/78710f05d6624fd16d027e85a3321e693ba01e93b8c504207cd611ceef23c4ee.jpg) 19.3 Wells Coates, kitchen, Isokon Building, London, 1934. The curved corners of the communal kitchen that served the residents’ restaurant may be an early example of the Streamline style (see here) inspired by the Supermarine S.6B seaplane created in 1931. Coates had been a pilot in World War I. ![](images/874af590df1823cd7f28900336761410c9b4c6f3c50b4bcb1d5259d3d8d33019.jpg) The horizontal elements, unsurprisingly, made the novelist Agatha Christie think of an ocean liner. Coates’s sliding table, a plywood top on tubular steel legs, is a simple piece of machinery in a “machine for living in.” The plywood stool was by the Isokon furniture company owned by Gordon Russell, who was the client for the building. In 1938 Coates collaborated with British architect Patrick Gwynne (1913–2003), who had worked in his office, to design a house for the latter’s family. Coates’s influence is clear in the rational concrete-and-glass exterior but it is safe to assume that Gwynne, who would evolve his own less rigorous design palette, was responsible for the Art Deco interior, which, as in Connell’s High and Over, was considered more suitable for an interior (19.6). ![](images/0788aefa60724388aa7c2fab851d3463a375afa0c817ddf73406ed7c6c7130a9.jpg) 19.6 Wells Coates and Patrick Gwynne, living room, The Homewood, Esher, Surrey, England, 1938. Art Deco traits are clear in the living room—the built-in furniture, the rug with its abstract pattern, and a carpet with a serpentine edge are presumed to be primarily Gwynne’s work. Coates was creative on a broad front and his innovations included the “D” door handle that has become ubiquitous in modern interiors and plastic Bakelite radio cabinets for the EKCO company. His AD series of circular radios (19.7) took advantage of plastic-molding techniques and was produced in a variety of colors but the brown, suggestive of wood, far outsold lighter, brighter hues. ![](images/0c2b76b62fb26cbddb59ba6c1222c08e56d5f23b134c5db9bcc1465230788014.jpg) EKCO was an early adopter of Bakelite, a synthetic plastic material that could be industrially molded. The company approached several well-known modernist architects to design receivers in keeping with modern technology. It has been conjectured that his childhood in Japan gave him an ability to elevate bare modernist bones into something poetic. Alternatively, his mother, who had studied architecture under Louis Sullivan, may have passed on Sullivan’s proposition from his essay Ornament in Architecture (1892) that buildings should be “comely in the nude.” ## ITALY Benito Mussolini (1883–1945), the fascist leader of Italy from 1922 to 1943, was flamboyant, wore military uniforms, struck heroic poses for cameras, and portrayed himself as the modernizer of the Italian state. Italy had a rich heritage of classical and Neoclassical buildings and, on that secure bedrock, Mussolini was sympathetic to progressive ideas in architecture and the Rationalist movement that had emerged in the 1920s and grown in the 1930s. It was an offshoot of modernism that was committed to eliminating the decorative heritage of Italian Neoclassicist and Neo-Baroque architecture while retaining formal composition elements. Luigi Figini (1903–84) and Gino Pollini (1903–91) designed the Casa Elettrica (Electric House, so called because its only source of power was electricity) for the Fourth International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts in Monza, in northern Italy in 1930. Two years later, in photographic form, it was the only Italian exhibit in the New York Museum of Modern Art’s International Style exhibition. Its interior was sparse, with curtains acting as adjustable partitions between activity areas, three armchairs, and a coffee table displaying Art Deco traits. Figini and Pollini were among the founders of Gruppo 7, the seven Italian architects who, in 1926, founded Rationalism, as was Giuseppe Terragni (1904–43) who designed the Casa del Fascio (House of Fascism, now Casa del Popolo or House of the People) in Como, northern Italy (19.8). The building was strictly rational with a 110-square-feet (10-sq-m) plan and 55-feet (17-m) high elevations. It was half a cube, and the elevations were half a square subdivided into a grid of squares. Internally, the building was organized around a central, double-height space, the Salone delle Adunate (Meeting Hall), which is lit by a ceiling of glass blocks set in square concrete frames. The columns and beams were painted light blue–green and the room was dominated by an effigy of Mussolini—the building was the local headquarters of the Fascist party. On the three intermediate floors, a gallery around the central space connected perimeter offices. On the top story, the ceiling/roof over the central courtyard became the floor of an inner courtyard in which areas of glass block were divided by an opaque central walkway that connected offices at that level. Upper levels were intended for small meeting rooms, an archive, and caretaker’s accommodation. A principal meeting room had tubular metal chairs around a glass-topped table beneath abstract wall decoration, similar to De Stijl imagery. Terragni’s obsession with the square and its predominance over function made for a building that is a visual conundrum. It has a geometric clarity, which is as easily deciphered as an allegorical wall painting but an expression of simple logic cannot be as popular, or as rewarding for lay observers, as figurative imagery. ![](images/f4dc972a110879b8fd821b3b3fcc7917441ecb85eaefdb597422fd26a7c25242.jpg) Giuseppe Terragni, Casa del Popolo, Como, Italy, 1938. Originally called the Casa del Fascio, this public building was intended to accommodate political meetings. Its rational commitment to the square as a planning and elevational device comfortably satisfied the totalitarian appetite for order and monumentality. ## SCANDINAVIA Scandinavians had developed a Nordic model of social democracy, a mix of welfare state and freemarket capitalism and, in the nineteenth century, an unostentatious Neoclassicism in which influences from abroad were tempered with respect for their own traditions. industrialization was also slow to arrive, which helped to preserve traditional crafts. scandinavians were not inclined to create tubular steel furniture, they preferred to bend timbers and mold plywood. The Swedish Society of Crafts and Design, founded in 1845, focused on craft rather than factory production but, as industry grew in the early twentieth century and people migrated from country to town, the society began to concern itself with the quality of mass-produced products. The quest to make objects with high aesthetic values available to a popular market began in earnest during the 1920s and 1930s and, after World War II, schools were established to teach and preserve craft techniques. The country evolved its own humane modernity. ## Alvar and Aino Aalto Alvar Aalto (1898–1976), a Finn, was aware of the emergence of modernism in France and Germany and became the most significant of Scandinavia’s early modern architects and designers. He saw his buildings as complete works of art and designed furniture, textiles, and glassware in close collaboration with his wife, the architect Aino Marsio (1894–1949). He began as a Nordic Classicist, and the simplicity of that style gave him a stepping stone to modernism. His first important modernist project was a building for the Turun Sanomat newspaper (1927–9) in Turku, Finland’s oldest city. The most interesting area held the printing presses in which round-edged columns with flared tops melded into the ceiling slab. In the most functional area of the building, the columns are incongruously organic while the rest of the building conformed to modernist expectations with ribbon windows and white walls (19.9). ![](images/821147eaac96efb582280304d52d35fecb0abdf3f846bfa5e0cb8416ca678781.jpg) Alvar Aalto, headquarters of the Turun Sanomat newspaper, Turku, Finland, 1927–9. The tapering concrete columns give this industrial interior—the press room of a newspaper—a curious monumentality that the more conventional office areas of the building lack. Aalto’s international reputation was established by the Paimio Sanatorium (1933) in Paimio, southwest Finland. It is a long, south-facing, six-story block of patients’ accommodation, a shorter wing of open-air terraces, and a central entrance block of rooms for communal dining and services. The reception desk, elevators, light fittings, and clocks were all bespoke. Furniture included the Paimio armchair with its plywood curves which, along with the four-legged, stackable stool for the Viipurin Library in Vyborg (now in Russia), completed in 1935, has become synonymous with his work. The armchairs are no more comfortable than the cushioned tubular steel models from mainland Europe but wood made them reassuringly familiar (19.10). ![](images/b18512c4e7ead6da9b446062178ab14da0b1fc75c0cbc72e5e52e860b4e5710b.jpg) 19.10 Alvar Aalto, armchair in molded plywood, 1934. The techniques of molding plywood made possible the generous curves that make this chair more visually seductive than the chrome and leather pieces by radicals elsewhere in Europe. At the end of the 1930s Aalto, in close collaboration with Aino, designed the building that marked their transition from modernist rationality to a language of natural materials and organic forms. Villa Mairea (1938–9) in Noormarkku was designed for Harry Gullichsen, an industrialist, and his wife Maire (1907–90). She was a director of Artek, the company that produced the Aaltos furniture and artifacts. The Gullichsens instructed that the house be “experimental.” It was a holiday home deep in a forest with spaces for entertaining, a gallery, and a studio. The building was organized to have activities flow together, to offer flexibility of use and a different, more immediate, connection between interior and exterior than that offered by modernism’s ribbon windows (19.11). ![](images/7510d525e320c557990ff583c7441a1a75201073150e52c951fb3f9ce9434407.jpg) Alvar and Aino Aalto, Villa Mairea, Noormarkku, 1938–9. An excellent example of how Scandinavian respect for their traditional materials and craft techniques influenced their modernity. The pairing and wrapping columns with rattan strip, the rejection of a rectangular grid, are all the result of an impulse to defer to nature rather than to objective logic. The interior transcribes nature. The structural grid is irregular with seemingly randomly placed, circular steel columns that are doubled up and bound together with rattan strips. One column unaccountably changes to concrete, to break from modernist homogeneity. The integration of exterior and interior is reinforced by thin vertical wooden poles set irregularly along the length of the open-treaded stair that allow glimpses of the forest beyond. The connecting of interior to landscape is comparable to that achieved by Frank Lloyd Wright, not in Fallingwater in Pennsylvania (1936), which is imposed on the landscape, but with his versions of his home and studio, Taliesin, in Wisconsin (1897–1959; see here). Alvar and Aino entered the competition to design the Finnish exhibit at the New York World’s Fair of 1939 separately and he won. Within the pavilion’s rectangular walls, he introduced a flowing, free-form wall of wooden strips that leaned over the main exhibition space and screened a secondary exhibition on an upper level (19.12). Wright declared it a work of genius. ![](images/66568dcf2e139f1436939638bfd9ce59b413d9f23903a76937c392644e3e71d1.jpg) Alvar Aalto, Finnish Pavilion, New York World’s Fair, 1939. Aalto’s pavilion was a great success at the fair. The undulating wall of angled wooden strips and the balcony (barely visible on the right) created a dynamic space to display the products of Finnish industry. Aino was integral to the Aaltos’ work and, as in most partnerships, it is difficult to apportion precise responsibilities but, as a woman architect and designer, she was invariably cast in a subservient role, and not necessarily named when credit should have been shared. She joined his office in 1924 and they married in 1925. What evidence there is suggests that she concentrated on designing the interiors of their buildings. She was involved in the Villa Mairea interiors and furniture for the Paimio Sanatorium. It is likely she contributed to the plywood armchair. The Aaltos’ first buildings were small Nordic Classic houses. The first project accredited to Aino is Villa Flor, in 1926, their holiday home in Alajärvi, western Finland, a traditional wooden country house. She was not entirely convinced by modernist ideologies and deployed traditional furniture fabrics and artifacts to achieve comfortable inhabitability. In 1935 she and Alvar set up the Artek company, with Maire Gullichsen and art historian Nils-Gustav Hahlin (1904–41), to sell the furniture and lighting fixtures they designed. Aino became head of design and later managing director. She created hundreds of objects, Alvar only a few. She oversaw commissions for more than eighty interiors and designed light fittings, textiles, and glassware, for which she won a prize at the Triennale di Milano (Milan Triennial) of 1936. The same year, she and Alvar collaborated to create the Savoy vase with its base of differently sized, intersecting circles, the outside edges of which are extruded at a slight angle to give a continuous undulating edge. It is very Art Deco. Alvar is usually credited as its sole designer. Aino died in 1949 but she had created the aesthetic that defined Artek and established Finland as a design force. In 1952 Alvar married an assistant in his studio, architect Elissa Mäkiniemi (1922–94). When he died, she and other staff completed the projects he had begun. ## SWITZERLAND Swiss society was comfortable with rational modernism. The country’s mountainous terrain demanded heroic feats of concrete bridge building. It has even been suggested that the Swiss Le Corbusier’s early training as a watchmaker gave him an appetite for machine precision and underpinned his enthusiasm for objectivity and his propensity for making rules. In 1931 Karl Moser (1880–1936), like Auguste Perret, revealed concrete’s potential as an architectural material. He employed a coffered roof supported on eight slender columns for St. Anthony’s Church in Basel. The church is 71 feet (21.5 m) high and 200 feet (61 m) long with a 200-feet (61-m) high angular bell tower, all built of exposed concrete. Like Perret, Moser’s concrete structure allowed him to devote the side walls entirely to small panels of stained glass that wash color across the raw concrete surfaces. Some locals found the building so outrageous they called it the “soul silo” (19.13). ![](images/e19ffc38b07834bcd452c38e551ac82217ba9b19b43ce62233bb98984b7ca00a.jpg) Karl Moser, St. Anthony’s Church, Basel, Switzerland, 1931. Like French architect Auguste Perret, Moser explored the possibilities of a concrete expression of a traditional ecclesiastic language. The coffering of the ceiling is evocative but structurally unnecessary. Alfred Roth (1903–98) was a student of Moser at the Federal Institute of Technology in Zurich and after graduating went to work for Le Corbusier from 1927–8. Between 1935 and 1936 he and his cousin Emil (1893–1980), working with Marcel Breuer, designed small apartment buildings in Doldertal near Zurich for the architectural historian and champion of modernism, Sigfried Giedion. Alfred went on to live and work in one of the apartments. The buildings’ interiors were lit by ribbon windows and the walls were white with patches of light color to define areas within the open plan, something Roth had encountered when working in Paris with Le Corbusier. Furniture included pieces by Breuer and Aalto. Max Bill (1908–94) studied at the Bauhaus and returned to Switzerland where, as a member of the Schweizerischer Werkbund (Swiss Craft Association), he became a spokesman for modernism. He was a leading graphic and industrial designer whose work was characterized by clarity and elegant proportions, exemplified by the clocks he designed for German watch and clock manufacturer Junghans. He founded the Ulm School of Design in Ulm, Germany, in 1953. He and his colleague, the Indonesian-born designer Hans Gugelot (1920–65), designed the Ulmer Hocker (1954), a stool that supposedly served as a shelf, a lectern, and a side table. Although not obviously suited to any of those functions, it impressed by its fragile, elegant detail. 19.14 ## FURNITURE DESIGN Architects committed to the modernist code had problems furnishing their interiors, even the work of radical pre-modernists, like Josef Hoffmann, or Charles Rennie Mackintosh, carried ornamental baggage, so modernists were obliged to do it for themselves and, in the application of their principles, they were ruthless. Hungarian-born Marcel Breuer (1902–81) became head of cabinetmaking in the Bauhaus. In 1925 he was inspired by the use of steel tubing in lightweight bicycles to evolve a skeletal version of a traditional armchair. The chrome frame retained the rough shape of the original but solid slabs of upholstery were replaced by strips of a strong and shiny waxed cotton fabric known as Eisengarn (iron yarn). The German designer Margaretha Reichardt (1907–84) experimented with the material in the Bauhaus weaving workshop, developing both cloth and strapping. The Thonet company took over manufacture of the chair and catalogued it as Model B3. However, it was renamed the Wassily chair after World War II by a subsequent manufacturer who thought the connection with the Russian painter Wassily Kandinsky would have commercial benefits, since fine artists have greater name recognition than designers and a swashbuckling glamor (19.15). The chair is rhetorically functional but the areas of cloth devoted to seat, backrests, and arms are so reduced that they offer little stability—it is not a chair for lounging. Breuer also created the more amenable Cesca chair in 1928 with its bent chrome frame with woven straw seat and back, on thin wooden frames that stabilize the frame (19.14). Ludwig Mies van der Rohe and Lilly Reich produced something similar to the Cesca, the MR10, in which the solid good sense of Cesca is sacrificed for a more dramatic and elegant profile. ![](images/a17b38f0f1e7580c8c0a5ec9b413e7832ec1332b435fa3d3de57a73bd158179d.jpg) Marcel Breuer, Cesca chair, 1928. Breuer named his chair in honor of his adopted daughter Francesca. The tubular-steel-framed chair had woven cane seats and back made with and without arms. There were also cushioned versions. The chair remains in production. ![](images/d5e6897be83b241be6c9674b8858684807b477f1fdd265a9bf47407b614c8fde.jpg) 19.15 Marcel Breuer, Wassily chair, 1925. This design uses a frame of chrome-plated steel tubing to support seat, arms, and back of stretched canvas or leather. It is an exercise in reductive design at the cost of comfort. While traditional solid wooden frames were rejected in favor of steel and aluminum, plywood, perhaps because it was the result of a manufacturing process, was acceptable to modernists. In 1935, in retreat from Nazi Germany, Breuer moved briefly to London and was employed by the Isokon furniture firm, owned by Jack Pritchard (1899–1992), an early champion of British modernism. Pritchard admired Alvar Aalto’s plywood furniture, and suggested the material to Breuer who developed his earlier thoughts on an aluminum-framed chaise longue to design his long chair in 1936 (19.16). ![](images/07e1c8f66a0458886eb3a7377e6af346fc951ee416acd0d317d4d16fa10b94f0.jpg) ## 19.16 ## Marcel Breuer, plywood long chair, 1936. After the closure of the Bauhaus in Germany in 1933, Walter Gropius suggested Breuer relocate to London. He spent two years in the city, during which he designed the long chair for the London-based firm Isokon. The chair is molded plywood using techniques developed by Alvar Aalto for Artek. Europe, the second smallest continent, is extraordinarily diverse. National frontiers reinforced by language, political, economic, social and climatic idiosyncrasies explain how even an architectural and design style as clearly defined as modernism could generate distinct national and regional variations. Many Europeans, in search of opportunity and prosperity, took the risk of emigrating to North America; most brought ambitions and skills and a few, in the 1920s and 1930s, brought new, modernistic ways to the design of buildings and interiors.
