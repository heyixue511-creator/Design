# 7 Isotype in America Otto Neurath and Rudolf Modley 1930-9

Hisayasu Ihara

Otto Neurath's early efforts to disseminate the Vienna Method internationally were aimed in several directions, including towards the United States. In attempting to spread the Vienna Method to the usA, Neurath came to rely on many friends and allies. But despite the commitment of everyone involved, a number of factors made progress difficult. The first was the restrictive economic situation of the early 193os brought on by the Great Depression. Next were the increasingly common imitations of the Vienna Method that impeded efforts to establish the genuine article. But another – and crucial – factor was Rudolf Modley. Modley, who had worked at the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in the 192os, left for the usA in 193o. There, in the years that followed, he produced pictorial statistics best described as midway between the Vienna Method and its poorer imitations. Although Modley helped introduce the Vienna Method to the USA, he later pursued his own direction, eventually competing with Neurath and even criticizing him. As the popularity of pictorial statistics grew in the usA, the differing views of Neurath and Modley raised a series of issues about how pictorial statistics should be made and used, among them the value of standardized pictograms, and strategies to control Vienna Method imitations. The intersecting activities of these two men during the 193os is the focus of this chapter.

## Neurath's first visit to the United States

In autumn 1932, Mary van Kleeck began formulating plans to bring Otto Neurath to the United States. That October, Mary Fleddérus visited Van Kleeck in New York to discuss the IRı's next annual conference. Fleddérus had recently received a letter from Neurath in which he expressed his desire to open a branch (Nebenstelle) of the Mundaneum in the usa,¹⁴ and with this in mind she proposed bringing him over from Vienna.¹ª In regard to the proposed visit, Van Kleeck remarked that, while 'the present does not seem a favorable opportunity for launching new enterprises in the United States, nevertheless I have been infected by Miss Fleddérus' conviction to believe that now is the time'. Neurath was duly invited and Van Kleeck began raising funds to support the trip from people interested in his work.

Neurath arrived in New York on 3 January 1933 for a ten-day visit. His first engagement was on 9 January at the IRI, where he addressed around one hundred social research workers, statisticians, welfare workers, and a number of economists and bankers; he also showed films made according to the Vienna Method.1 The following day he attended a lunch with the staff of The Survey¹7 and in the evening addressed a joint meeting of the Progressive Education Association and the American Museum of Natural History.18 A few days later his stay was at an end and he returned to Europe.

Neurath was energized by the interest the Americans had shown in his work. Back in Vienna he wrote to Van Kleeck pressing her to set up an organization to support 'service for visualization': 'if we can dispatch a permanent representative for the branch, he/she will prepare the consultation under your help so that the role can be carried out perhaps with the “Flying World Secretariat" for one or two months in a year'.19 Although Van Kleeck needed little convincing, she only began moving the proposal forward in late March; in addition to Neurath's enthusiasm, three factors spurred her on. The first was Van Kleeck's awareness that Neurath had already been contacted by several organizations asking him about the possibility of compiling pictorial statistics in the uSA, based on the Vienna Method. She knew, for instance, that Harry Laidler, president of the League for Industrial Democracy, had asked

Instead Modley worked with greater independence: he drew sketches for charts based on statistical data, then sent these to the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum; the Vienna Museum's work was thus limited to completing each chart's design and choosing or devising any necessary pictograms or guidepictures. Following his dismissal from the Museum of Science and Industry, Modley continued working on pictorial statistics. A letter of March 1933 from Marguerite E. Schwarzman, former member of the American Museum of Natural History, and founder and director of the Children's Laboratories in Westchester county, New York, had met Neurath during his visit to the usA. The meeting sparked her interest in his work and she began investigating ways to incorporate the Vienna Method into school education.³³ Unable to afford Neurath's help in creating instructional materials, she asked Modley instead.³⁴ Schwarzman wrote to Neurath about the plan³⁵ and his disapproving reply included these remarks:

In all other respects, Modley, who has been at the administration of our Museum, is surely adequate to flexibly and suitably participate in the suggested field service. I would not entrust him, however, with control and organization of graphic work. We have realized that special training just for that is indispensable. I hope, however, that we will succeed in creating a real branch in usA and to use Modley, just like any other co-workers, in the formation of a generous propaganda organization.³⁶

Mary van Kleeck was also disturbed by Schwarzman's collaboration with Modley, thinking that it promoted 'similar work without approval by Dr. Neurath or even notification to him'.³7 She met with Modley and asked for an explanation. According to a lengthy letter written by Van Kleeck to Neurath, headed 'Dr. Modley's work in the United States', Modley claimed that personal problems meant he needed to earn a living in an area in which he had some expertise.³8 He told Van Kleeck that he would be quite ready to turn over his work, if he develops it as he hopes, to any organization, when it is established'. Since Modley – like many during the Depression – had no regular employment, Van Kleeck accepted his explanation, though warned him that he should not do anything without communicating with Neurath first, if only for ethical reasons. Van Kleeck remarked to Neurath:

[H]e cannot recognize any ethics in the situation. He believes that if he had taken the matter up with you at first you would have negatived it and that it is a great deal better to show first what he can do and then present it to you as a fait accompl... He said that he had hoped to go into industry but had not succeeded and therefore, in view of the fact that he had spent so much time in the Vienna Museum and that experience was at his disposal he could see nothing else for it but to go ahead and use his experience.

What Van Kleeck found hard to accept, however, was that Modley 'did not believe in the international aspect [of pictorial statistics] and did not wish to work on that basis'. She believed that Modley felt that the drawing of pictorial statistics was exactly like any other statistics, namely, you presented the facts quite apart from any social philosophy back of them'. Concerned that Modley's independent efforts would become an obstacle to developing Neurath's work in the USA, Van Kleeck decided to remain in touch with Modley, to keep an eye on him.

To widen the Institute's range of activities, the workshop might accept other work meeting the requirements ... . Certainly the wider the field of activity, the lower the cost of production and the less the danger of imitation. This work, statistical accuracy of which would be guaranteed, would appear as produced by the 'Workshop of the Institute' and would in no way represent a responsibility for Mundaneum.

Although Modley's report does not identify how individual committee members were aligned, it is likely that Clark favoured a workshop serving a wide field, a view opposed, it appears, by Van Kleeck. Indeed she suggested that ‘instead of pushing the formal organization of an Institute, get the method actually used under the circumstances to make it favorably known'.s¹ For her, it was most important to bring Neurath to the uSA where three or four organizations might be found to employ him as a consultant. Clark, on the other hand, argued that the establishment of a workshop, however modest, should be the priority and that ‘if by corresponding with Dr. Neurath an estimate of production of average charts could be worked out on the basis of costs in New York[, t]hat would afford something definite on which to work. It was felt [by Clark] that to have an actual office, however small, made a good talking point in developing further support'.

The Executive Committee's decision clearly frustrated Modley. In a letter to Brenner (but directed at the committee as a whole) he complained: 'There is no necessity to bring anybody from Europe here to select or transform statistics or to direct the production. Charts of equal or better quality can be produced here immediately.'⁵He went on to emphasize how the operations of his own experimental workshop would compare favourably with Neurath's, arguing that 'the Vienna Museum uses hand methods where hand methods are not only unnecessary but even dangerous'. His workshop, by contrast, would produce charts 'without unnecessary hand work in about half the time' using a photomechanical process. 'Through improved coordination', he claimed, 'charts can be produced here about 5o% cheaper than in Vienna; this is despite wages which are at least 1o0% higher.'⁵

Despite a growing interest in the Vienna Method in the USA, without reliable information about the costs or time for the work, very few organizations or institutions would put so much money into [a] foreigner's trav elling expense for consultation purposes'.6³ Meanwhile, the increasing number of Vienna Method imitations suggested that there were easier and more immediate ways to access comparable work. These were problems the committee was unable to overcome. Had Modley been given approval to establish his proposed experimental workshop within the institute envisioned by the Organizing Committee, it is quite probable that the Executive Committee's efforts would have been successful. But by discounting Modley's abilities, Van Kleeck and Neurath forestalled the contributions he might have made.

Kleinschmidt also arranged for Neurath to attend the 65th annual meeting of the American Public Health Association in New Orleans, from 2o to 23 October, where he delivered a paper to the health education section, illustrated with slides.4 Neurath also visited Talladega University (Alabama) and the University of Iowa before travelling to Chicago. There he discussed plans for the International encyclopaedia for unified science with Charles Morris and Rudolf Carnap on 27 and 28 October, before returning to New York

Back in New York, at the offices of the National Tuberculosis Association in Rockefeller Center, Neurath concentrated on NTA work together with Marie Reidemeister, who had arrived in early November. The NTA had asked Neurath to develop a travelling exhibition to educate Americans about tuberculosis. The situation at the Rockefeller Center was conducive to fruitful collaboration with NTA staff since its office was a‘single large room' that had 'desks everywhere, with enough distance between them; it was always easy to ask a specialist to come over.'9⁵ Neurath and Reidemeister designed a series of twenty exhibition charts, which were then executed graphically under the direction of Gerd Arntz in The Hague.96 Marie Neurath later described the result: 'The 5,ooo reproductions of our charts, in the size of the originals, went to all corners of the United States as a travelling exhibition, and it was reported that they were studied with great interest and understood by Inuit, Indians and everyone else alike'.97 (See pp. 342–8 for a full showing of the charts.) Booklets were produced using the same material.98

While in New York, Neurath also received an unexpected invitation from Mexico's National Council of Higher Education and Science Research 'to collaborate in its program of establishing centres of higher education for the Mexican working class'."Neurath and Reidemeister travelled to Mexico City in mid-February 1937, where they consulted on the foundation of a National Industrial Museum being set up by the council.1ºº They returned to New York on 29 March and sailed for the Netherlands two days later.

During this period, work was also completed on two publications, Compton's pictured encyclopedia (1939–40) and Modern man in the making (1939). Compton's, a popular illustrated encyclopedia, contained 43 Isotype charts presenting a variety of subjects; the charts were interspersed with numerous other, more conventional, illustrations. In preparing the charts for Compton's, Neurath emphasized the importance of their relationship to the text: Each Pictograph must be specially made to fit the text where it appears. Then text and Pictograph work together to give the same message to eye and mind’. 102
