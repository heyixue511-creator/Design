When Dr. Otto Neurath came from The Hague to confer with Compton editors on the Encyclopedia's visual education problems, Ronald Millar, of the editorial staff, took his camera along to the conferences. The result is this series of excellent candid shots that show something of the exuberant personality of this internationally famous educator.

Here we see him applying himself to problems laid before him by Athol E. Rollins, managing editor, and Martin Stevers, editor for the physical sciences; "holding forth" with vigor upon his ideas and theories; enjoying the reproduction of his Pictographs in the recent publicity booklet; and finally, saying good-bye.

He was most enthusiastic about the Compton handling of the Pictographs, which he and-his staff of experts at the International Foundation for Visual Education had worked out.

A clue to Dr. Neurath's truly vast range of expert knowledge and ability may be seen in a comparison of the two projects that shared his time on this trip to America. We see him here as an expert in the visual interpretation of ideas to make those ideas comprehensible to young students.

He was also working on one of the most "high-brow" of current educational publishing projects — the International Encyclopedia of Unified Science, which is being brought out by the University of Chicago Press. It is the object of this work to so interpret the findings in each branch of science as to make them readily available to the specialists in other branches. Dr. Neurath is the editor-inchief of this project as well as the expert for the social sciences. Other editors and writers include outstanding specialists from all parts of the world. To mention but three: Niels Bohr, the Danish physicist, who won the Nobel prize in physics in 1922 for his theory of the structure of the atom. His picture heads the Compton article PHYSICS. Earl Bertrand Russell, the famous English mathematician and philosopher, whose writings have an international following. John Dewey, the American philosopher, psychologist, and educator, whose ideas have so profoundly influenced American educational methods.

Work on Modern man in the making was probably begun in 1936, 103 though its origins can be traced to 1933 when Neurath proposed two publications to publisher Alfred A. Knopf: 'Pilgrimage of man' and 'Atlas: mankind in our times'. The first appears closest to what became Modern man in the making; according to Neurath, it was to emphasise the connection of all ethnic groups and peoples from primaeval times onward, a kind of “Sukzessiver Internationalismus".1º4 To an even greater extent than in Compton's pictured encyclopaedia, Modern man in the making was notable for its closely related charts and text, its picturetext-style'.1ºs As Neurath explained in the book's foreword:

Impressive visual aids do not merely act as illustration or as eye-bait in this book; they are parts of the explanations themselves. The reader may not understand the contents by reading the text only; he must ‘read' the pictures as carefully as the text.1º⁶

In some ways, this 'picture-text-style' was prefigured in a book published two years earlier, The United States: a graphic history, which featured pictorial charts by Modley. Indeed Modley was credited as co-author of the book, with the following reason given in the foreword: ‘The visual aids it employs, pictorial statistics in this case, are an integral part of the book rather than simply illustrative material.' Modley's charts (some of which are clearly based on Viennese examples) were not as closely woven into the text of The United States as were the charts in Modern man in the making, and Neurath's book also benefited from a much more extensive use of colour (see overleaf).

## Standardized pictograms

As already mentioned, it seems that before Otto Neurath's arrival in the USA, he and Modley had been reconciled. In his role as secretary of the Hospitality Committee, Modley had worked out a plan to ensure the success of Neurath's visit. But in practice Modley's plan achieved little, only a lunch held in honour of Neurath hosted by the committee on 28 September 1936.1º7 There is also evidence that the relationship between the two men soured again. This is found in Modley's book How to use pictorial statistics, published the following year, in which he presents a comprehensive study of pictorial statistics in the USA. Polemical statements in the book appear to have fanned the Neurath-Modley controversy', as one contemporary reviewer called it.¹ºª

In his foreword, Modley first expresses admiration for Neurath: 'Any book which attempts to deal with the method of pictographs must acknowledge its indebtedness to the genius of Otto Neurath. He more than any one man created that method and made it into a significant tool'. But Modley then criticizes: ‘This makes it more than regrettable that Dr. Neurath has not found it possible to follow what seems to me the inevitable trend in the development of the pictograph. I hope that the success of our American experiments will convince him that the restrictions he has set up are not fundamental implications of the method.'Modley is here referring to the international standardization of pictograms. 'Dr. Neurath holds firmly to the belief that pictorial symbols should be international, designed within rigid limitations so that a pictorial Esperanto will be created. He wishes to circumvent a pictorial Tower of Babel and produce at once an international picture language. With that ultimate aim I heartily agree, but I believe his method of achieving it is Utopian.' 1º9 Modley felt that a different method was needed:

I believe that pictographs must gradually be accepted as a tool of communication. To achieve this, the symbols must first grope their way into the consciousness of a restricted national audience. They must speak in terms which are comprehensible. Obviously, such symbols must be prepared with their specific audience in mind.110

Modley gave as an example the inevitable differences that would be required between pictograms of an American farmer and a Chinese farmer, if each were to be recognized by their respective audiences. Pictograms of an American labourer and farmer designed by Modley were in turn different from those designed by the Gesellschafts- und

Wirtschaftsmuseum. Modley believed that a kind of 'Americanization' of Neurath's method with respect to pictogram design was essential if they were to be accepted by Americans.

Modley's belief that pictograms should be first understood in the specific community using them had informed his own work in the usA, work that he saw as an experiment' in that process. The 'ultimate aim' of establishing pictograms internationally would take more time. Even signs like‘+’ and ‘-', Modley argued, were only understood and accepted gradually; therefore 'If abstract symbols of such simplicity as these developed so slowly, it would be indeed sanguine to expect pictorial symbols to be internationally accepted without a period of trial.'111 This trial period was essential since 'To accept it [a given pictogram] and learn from it opens the way to a future cooperation between the various agencies from which, in due time, an international symbol language may arise.' 112

Modley's reference to cooperation between 'the various agencies' is notable, as he went on to describe: 'An attempt is being made to establish cooperation between all the groups which produce pictorial statistics in this country and abroad, so that they can make use of the best methods and symbols'.11³ Modley's interest in the problem of standardized pictograms probably grew out of his experiences working with a variety of organizations and agencies. He may have recognized the need for coordination among those producing pictorial statistics, but to Modley, Neurath's approach was counterproductive:

Monopolistic tendencies of any one group are not only hopeless but hurt the cause of graphic presentation since they lead to inferior replacements and necessitate modifications of the highest standards. It is regrettable that Dr. Neurath has not found it possible to cooperate with any group which does not agree with the rigid standardization of symbols he demands. 114

Modley's criticisms arose after his contact with Neurath in September 1936. One might assume that Modley proposed to Neurath that they work together towards a gradual standardization of pictograms, but that Neurath rejected the proposal.

But why had Neurath refused to cooperate? Neurath certainly did not rule out the possibility that variant pictograms were sometimes needed for specific audiences, though he was very careful about introducing them. That would be a disadvantage, even if each variant was actually good.'¹⁶ Such remarks indicate that Neurath did not believe that cooperative work was required to establish pictograms of a higher order since Isotype pictograms were already standardized. His concern was instead to diffuse Isotype internationally, but control it centrally:

In building up such a language, great care has to be taken to see that as much as possible may be said with the help of the smallest number of words. That is to say, for our picture language one general list of a limited number of signs is needed for international use, and this has to be worked out by or under the control of one chief organization. (This organization is now the IsOTYPE work-room at the Hague.)117

Modley was clearly opposed to this approach and sceptical of its 'Utopian' dimension. Instead, he proposed that pictograms should be standardized for a specific community before they were used internationally; to achieve this required cooperative efforts.

In his new role as vice-president of the International Foundation for Visual Education in the UsA, Kleinschmidt took over the work of promoting Isotype, though mainly within the health education field. In addition to the already mentioned exhibition and publication work done for the National Tuberculosis Association, the film On the firing line featuring Isotype charts was made and released in 1939.¹²³ Kleinschmidt and Neurath co-authored Health education by Isotype, a booklet published by the American Public Health Association, also in 1939. It described the principles of Isotype and explored its application to health education. Despite such accomplishments, Kleinschmidt looked back on them with some regret:

No experience in my career has saddened me more than the failure of public health workers to appreciate and adopt a visual education method which I believed to be made-to-order for health education, namely, Isotype, a sharp-edged tool of precision and functional beauty and yet one which received only supercilious attention when it was offered by its author.124

Kleinschmidt drew his own conclusions about why it had been obstructed:

One reason is probably that its value was depreciated by imitations. And unfortunately these imitations came to our country before the genuine. Alert publicists were attracted at once by Neurath's original symbols. They caught the superficial aspects of the new technique and capitalized [on] them without taking the trouble to master the more fundamental principles. The cute little men' were a welcome relief from the pie graphs, bars and curves from which the public shied as from a plague. It was so easy, furthermore, to improve' the symbols by adding here a little, changing there a little to make them more 'snappy'. 125

It was a problem that Neurath and his allies had contended with throughout the 193os.126 Modley was also aware of the problem, and recognized his own part in it:

We take part of the credit for the rapidity with which the Americas have adopted and adapted the language of pictures. We must also take part of the blame for a large number of pseudo-pictographs, which have been produced by others; meaningless or misleading applications of our symbols.1²7

But however contrite Modley may have been, Kleinschmidt and Neurath quite probably included him among the imitators, given his work independent of Neurath; indeed Kleinschmidt's remarks just quoted could be interpreted as referring to Modley, among others. Modley's own view would have been more nuanced: that his work did not imitate Neurath's but followed it and yet was distinctive in its responsiveness to conditions in the USA.

Modley attempted to establish this distinctiveness in part by asserting a general separateness of work in the usA, as suggested by the history of pictorial statistics.
