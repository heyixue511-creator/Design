GN23 glasses for Nuutajärvi, 1947 # gunnel nyman ## (b. 1909 Turku – d. 1948 Helsinki, Finland) ![](images/8549752e06d3ebb97f76c2bb9929f26b56baa684badf0dd9749d9487e3e55dd0.jpg) The Finnish glass designer Gunnel Gustafson Nyman studied furniture design under Arttu Brummer (1891-1951)at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu (University of Art and Design) in Helsinki from 1928 to 1932. In 1932 she joined the Riihimäki design studio and subsequently won third prize in the firm's glassware competition of 1933 and second and third prizes in its second competition of 1936. During the 1930s, she explored the expressive potential of roughly bubbled glass – as did a number of other glass designers, most notably the French glass-maker MauriceMarinot(1882-1960), whose chunky bubbled vessels were highly influential on the younger generation of designers, including Nyman. In the early 1930s, she pioneered an innovative technique in which glass was blown into a pinstudded mould, removed from the mould and then encased in another layer of glass, so that the indentations from the mould would produce a regular pattern of small bubbles. Her thick-walled pieces from the 1940s were not only characterized by her signature use of tightly grouped bubbles, but also by her use of soft and often very fluid forms. The gentle undulating contours that she adopted for her glassware during this period foreshadowed to some extent the bolder andmore asymmetrical forms that became so fashionable the following decade. Nyman's innovative yet simple technique of casing tiny bubbles was relatively inexpensive to implement and could therefore be adopted for everyday tableware such as jugs and drinking glasses. Although her bubble technique was widely copied, other lesser designers were never able to replicate Nyman's virtuoso handling of the materials. In 1946, she designed her well-known Calla vase, which not only marked a stylistic departure in her own work but also signalled the movement in Finnish design in general towards the use of more expressive and abstracted organic forms. Apart from her renowned work for Riihimäki, Nyman designed lighting for Taito (1932-1936) and production glassware for Karhula (1935–1937). After the Second World War, she also briefly worked for littala(1946-1947) and Nuutajärvi-Notsjö(1946-1948)and designed furniture for Idman(1946-1948)before her untimely death at the age of thirty-nine.
