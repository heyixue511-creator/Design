# MICHAEL GRAVES 1934-2015 PRODUCTS LIKE THE WITTY WHISTLING BIRD TEA KETTLE MADE THIS INDIANA-BORN ARCHITECT THE BEST-KNOWN AMERICAN POSTMODERNIST. No single object of postmodern design became as internationally recognized as the pyramidshaped tea kettle, capped with a whistling bird, designed by Michael Graves (also see under "Living with Postmodern Design") for ALESSI (see under “Edited Design") in i985. More than a million were sold in the first three years following its introduction,3º and the kettle, still in production, remains an iconic image of the style. In a career lasting just over five decades, the prolific Graves designed some four hundred buildings and over two thousand products ranging from a costly silver coffee service to inexpensive kitchenware. Although his buildings are his most important works, Graves's distinctive products, often both functional and witty, made him the best-known postmodernist in America. Born in Indianapolis, Indiana, Graves attended the University of Cincinnati and the Harvard Graduate School of Design, where he received a MArch degree in ı959. He worked briefly in the office of George Nelson, but left when he was awarded a coveted Prix de Rome fellowship, spending two years at the American Academy in Rome. He returned to the United States and began to teach at Princeton University in i962. He opened his namesake firm in Princeton in i964, and continued to teach at the university even after becoming one of the country's most prominent architects. Along with Peter Eisenman\*, Charles Gwathmey. John Heiduk\*, and Richard Meier\*, Graves was known in the i97os as one of the New York Five (also known as the “Whites"), a group of architects allied with the Le Corbusier tradition of mostly white buildings. Graves was the only one of the group to move away from that allegiance, adopting a more accessible aesthetic that employed both color and metaphorical references to classicism. In i982 he designed one of the earliest, and certainly the most controversial iterations of postmodern architecture, the Portland Building in Portland, Oregon. A massive slab on a pedestal-like base, the building has stucco facades adorned with pilasters and accented with color, and a 6.5-ton hammeredcopper classical statue over the entrance. It was an instant sensation, provoking both broad media coverage and vociferous criticism for its unconventional form and its impractical fittings in terms of accessibility and function. He also designed residential projects, in which he eschewed modernist open-plan interiors, reverting to the traditional practice of having separately defined rooms. In his own Princeton home, he integrated classical with contemporary elements to create a villa from what had been an old warehouse. Its interiors combine Graves's postmodern furniture designs, which clearly draw on Biedermeier precedents, with an eclectic mix of new and old furnishings. His showroom designs for Sunar-Hauserman\*, a producer of office furnishings, beginning in i98o, combined classical iconography and flowing displays of draped fabrics with a sophisticated palette of apricot, gray, French blue, taupe, and ochre that was widely copied. He divided the walls into three horizontal sections, crested with moldings. The showrooms became archetypes of postmodern design for public interiors. Graves's first project with the Italian producer Alessi was a silver coffee and tea service for their 1983 Tea & Coffee Piazza project; he eventually produced more than 15o different objects for the firm, including clocks, salt and pepper dispensers, cooking utensils, trays, a kitchen timer, and the bestselling tea kettle.3' Though designer-name products at popular prices are ubiquitous today, Graves was the first prominent architect to enter the mass market. In i987 he began working with Target on a houseware collection that included a \$24.99 version of his bird-crested tea kettle, which became the company's most successful product. He enthusiastically took on the challenge of making good design affordable and by the time the association ended in 2oi2, he had designed more than two thousand Target products.3² Though many of his fellow postmodernists moved on to other styles, Graves continued to work with classically inspired forms, though in somewhat less extravagant iterations. Even his late work, including hotels in the Middle East and East Asia, remained identifiably postmodern. Paralyzed by a spinal infection in 2003, Graves was confined to a wheelchair for the remainder of his life, but continued to design, teach, and lecture, redirecting much of his attention to design for people with disabilities, in both architecture and products.
