# 2 The Background of the Ideas and Social History of the Bauhaus

## The Historical Setting

The Bauhaus was founded by Walter Gropius in 1919 and closed in 1933 under pressure from the National Socialists. The beginning and end of its existence coincided with the dates of the Weimar Republic. In this brief span of just fourteen years not only did the Bauhaus make substantial contributions to the foundation of what we now generally characterize as design, but this new kind of art school, this “art school of modernism," also developed and adapted pedagogical concepts whose relevance decades later is evoked repeatedly.1 On the other hand, these concepts have also led to a new form of academicism² that has been met with skepticism, criticism, and even total rejection.³ Despite such attempts to establish distance, which are in some respects quite justified, it cannot be denied that in its day the Bauhaus made outstanding contributions to aesthetic education. Before going into detail, however, to provide a better understanding of the phenomenon we must first sketch out some of its preconditions—namely, first by giving a brief overview of the intellectual and social sources behind the Bauhaus and then (in the following chapter) by examining the stages of the school's development from its founding to its closing in 1933.

The Bauhaus was the meeting place and the seed for crystallization of seemingly contrary contemporaneous trends, which—thanks to the outstanding abilities ofits founder and director of many years, Walter Gropius, as an organizer and coordinator—could be held in a tense but fruitful equilibrium. In its early phase it combined the artistic thought of late expressionism with the craft ideal of the Middle Ages. In a later phase it was dominatedby thevisualconceptsof constructivism andaprogram for design that aimed at practicality (Sachlichkeit) and functionality with an eye to the requirements andpotentials of modern technology.4 At the same time, and this is too often overlooked, surrealism, one of the great artistic movements of the twenties, also left clear traces on the Bauhaus, though these have hardly been acknowledged because they do not sit well with the usual cliché about the Bauhaus.

Although the Bauhaus is universally seen as the germ of modern design—and that is certainly an accurate characterization of its historical significance for the twentieth century—it must not be ignored that the sources of its intellectual and social history extend far back into the nineteenth century. It stands in the tradition of efforts dating from the Industrial Revolution, and thus also from romanticism, to reconstruct the unity of art and the culture of production that had broken down as a result of industrialization, to reintegrate art and life, to undo the splintering of artistic genres, and thus to use art itself as an instrument of cultural and social regeneration.⁵ It was the old idea of the Gesamtkunstwerk (total work of art) that had come so naturally to the Middle Ages and the baroque. However, whereas the medievaland baroque synthesis of all of the artistic genres and crafts engaged in the act of construction was concerned with the “artwork as a manifestation of unity," the Gesamtkunstwerk of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, as it developed at the Bauhaus and other places, was the sign of a "striving for unity,"⁶ that is to say, not reality but a utopian goal.

Simultaneous with and related to this new striving for unity, in its rigorous turn toward craft or industrial design—that is, to applied art that had been judged less valuable than liberal, high art—the Bauhaus marks the “final phase of romanticism's struggle against the tyranny of classicism."7

The complex preconditions of the Bauhaus cannot be portrayed in all their ramifications. In particular, we must resign ourselves not to discuss romanticism's efforts to return to medieval forms of life and artistic production. (The stylistic equivalent to which can be found, for example, in English architecture as early as 1820 with the rise of the Gothic Revival.)

It is also necessary to forgo an in-depth analysis of, for example, the sketches of the Victorian designer Christopher Dresser (1834-1904)—early examples of functionalism whose reduced forms and lack of ornament make them seem in some ways to anticipate Bauhaus design by decades— and to abstain from an extensive discussion of the constructive Vienna Jugendstil of Josef Hoffmann, Kolo Moser, and others, in its possible effects on the Bauhaus, and to forgo as well a closer exploration of the influence of the Dutchman J. L. M. Lauweriks (1864-1932) on the architectural thinking of Gropius, an influence that passed by way of Peter Behrens and especially Karl Ernst Osthaus as its turning points. We must mention Frank Lloyd Wright, whose book—published by Wasmuth in 1910, with its perspective views, site plans, floor plans, and sketches—provided the founder of the Bauhaus and his colleague Adolf Meyer with a series of crucial formal stimuli for their own works (the office building for the Werkbund Exhibition in Cologne in 1914; the Sommerfeld House in Berlin-Dahlem in 1920–21).8

Although the result is fragmentary, the following treatment of the sources of the Bauhaus is limited to those stages that were directly influential on Gropius by his own account. In his essay "Idee und Aufbau des Staatlichen Bauhauses" (The Idea and Structure of the State Bauhaus) of 1923, he referred explicitly to "Ruskin and Morris in England, van de Velde in Belgium, Olbrich, Behrens (the Darmstadt Artists' Colony) and others in Germany, and finally the German Werkbund," who “consciously sought out and discovered .. . the first paths toward reuniting the world of work with creative artists."9 Their goal, like that of the many other forces of reform that must go unmentioned here, was to overcome the historical chasm between art and craft or art and industry.

The historical setting within which this struggle to nullify the above-named antinomies was played out is well known. The rapid development of industry from about 1800 on (earlier in England, later in Germany) resulted in a severe social crisis for the trades, so that Karl Marx and other observers of the time came to the conclusion that "the trades are . .. doomed hopelessly to fall into ruin and the tradesmen doomed to become proletarians."10 In addition, machine production meant a grave loss of quality in the final product compared to craftwork. The mechanical reproduction of—lower quality—craft forms led, in the words of Giulio Carlo Argan, to a loss of the "spirituality of artistic activity" and to a "shocking decline of culture and taste."11

That a prominent art historian like Argan could make such negative evaluations in a work on the Bauhaus that appeared originally in Italian in 1951, exactly a century after the first World's Fair in London with its pivotal role in the early history of design, certainly does not make it easier to approach objectively an epoch as controversial as the nineteenth century. For, without attempting to discern the causes and circumstances behind this alleged loss of spirituality and decline of taste and culture,1² Argan merely reproduces a criticism, common since the World's Fair of 1851, that took aim at the poor quality of handiwork and the inadequacy of the forms resulting from machine products dressed up in historicizing ornaments.

Gottfried Semper (1803–79), in his Wissenschaft, Industrie und Kunst (Science, industry, and art), was among the first to draw attention to the critical points of the design of forms and products in his day. In doing so, he criticized the majority of the objects exhibited at the first World's Fair, because:

[T]he purpose of the product is seldom manifested artistically, except. . . in the trimmings. .. andthe material has to be violatedbefore the intent of the artist is even partly fulfilled. No wonder—because the artist is very skilled and inventive in drawing and modelling, yet he is not trained as a metalworker, a potter, a carpet weaver, or a goldsmith . . . [so] that the ornamental trimming is generally misunderstood and too often merges with the main theme or bears no relation to it.13

What Semper is criticizing here is the yawning gap between industrial, utilitarian form and its “ennobling" by “academic" artists, a deplorable state of affairs for which he blames the "inappropriate" (unzweckmäßig) dualism of “ideal" art and “applied" design.14 We will return later to the consequences for a revision of aesthetic education Semper drew from this dualism in the context of our discussion of the historical stages of the training of artists from the Middle Ages to the nineteenth century (see “Notes on the History of

Artists' Training up to the End of the Nineteenth Century" in chapter 4).

In England the criticism of the aesthetic inadequacy of machine production came especially from Ruskin, Morris, and their circle, and it is no coincidence that Gropius placed those two names at the beginning of his genealogy of the Bauhaus.

## Ruskin, Morris, and the Arts and Crafts Movement

In discussions of the beginnings of modern design, the names Ruskin andMorris are usually uttered in a single breath. However, whereas Ruskin emerged merely as a fiery critic of industrial production, it was left to Morris to draw practical consequences from Ruskin's critique and “to realize the new. ... For that reason, Morris's position in art is far superior to that of Ruskin, even if Ruskin cannot be denied priority in a history of art theory."15

The writings of John Ruskin (1819–1900), a strange mixture of sharp-sighted analysis and nostalgic viewpoints, as unsystematic as it is passionately overstated, had an almost unprecedented influence in England in the second half of the nineteenth century. Ruskin saw industrialization as a danger for both the consumer and the producer. The consumer risked being aesthetically tainted by the supply of low-quality and tasteless massproduced articles. The producer risked being robbed by machine production of the possibility of contented self-realization. Satisfying work was necessary to produce art, so Ruskin called for the eradication of the alien work of the machine and a return to the creative handiwork of the Middle Ages.

Ruskin's implacable rejection of the machine combined with a desire that all materials be unconditionally genuine and a repudiation of materials that were imitations in any way. The artwork should, in Ruskin's view, reveal the work process, the human interaction with the material, the impression of“human exertion and assiduousness."16 "In contrast, the mechanical product was nothing but a surrogate, a dead object."17 It should be said that in addition to demanding that materials be genuine, Ruskin struggled against excessive, historicizing ornament and thus against historicism generally. As modern as all this may sound, Ruskin's nostalgia for the Gothic style, which he held up as the measure for the renewal he sought, kept him just as firmly rooted in historicism as his hatred of the machine and his onesided idealizing of medieval modes of production made him an advocate of restoration of the past. Conseguently, any insight into the possibilities for a new unity of art and industry was denied him.

Although this tendency is also essentially true of William Morris (1834–96), whose thinking was strongly influenced by Ruskin, Morris, as the great renewer of the crafts in the nineteenth century and as one of the spiritual precursors of the Bauhaus, nonetheless demands a more comprehensive assessment. The most striking thing about Morris was his wide range. He was a painter, a poet, a designer, a practicing craftsman, a social theorist—all in one person. He was close to the English Pre-Raphaelites (who, as the name indicates, evoked the period before Raphael) not only because their main representative, Dante Gabriel Rossetti, had introduced him to painting, but alsò, and especially, because he agreed with their return to the aesthetics and society of the Middle Ages. Like the Nazarenes before them, the Pre-Raphaelites swore allegiance to an anticlassical conception of art. In their social conduct they sought to emulate medieval ideals of community by organizing themselves in the form of a fraternity. In contrast to the Pre-Raphaelite fraternity, with its undertones of religion and fanaticism and its clinging to the liberal arts, Morris saw his founding of a commercial enterprise for crafts as a means to come a little closer to his utopia, which looked back to the Middle Ages. The goal of the company he founded in 1861, Morris, Marshall, Faulkner & Co. (later Morris & Co.), was to design items for daily use that excelled in both craftsmanship and artistry. In this cooperative enterprise, artists and craftsmen were to participate jointly in artistic production—just as they had done in the Middle Ages.

The decisive moment for the founding of Morris's company was an experience of a rather private nature. When Morris wished to furnish his London apartment, in 1857, and then, in 1859, his famous Red House, built outside London by a friend, the architect Philip Webb, he discovered that he could not make use of Victorian arts and crafts and that he could not buy a single piece “that would not have been horribly ugly."18As a man of action, he began immediately to produce

By the second World's Fair in London in 1862, the company was exhibiting successfully, and this success grew even greater as Morris conquered one realm of the applied arts after the other: stained-glass windows, tiles, wallpaper, carpets, book design, and more. Morris's special domain was the ornamentation of surfaces. In his use of Gothic patterns he remained a child of his time, of historicism, but nevertheless he succeeded in conspicuously enriching the world of ornament by close observation of nature and by the introduction of folkloric motifs. In the area of furniture design, “utility and constructional design, not originality of form, were the First Commandment"19 for him. Very much in keeping with Ruskin: "modern aids and materials were consciously avoided, from head to toe; the artistic process begins with craftsmanship. The artists, especially Morris himself, learned the old trades from the ground up."2º In an age in which the cult of genius in the liberal arts was at its peak, Morris .came to the realization that "the talk of inspiration is sheer nonsense. There is no such thing; art is nothing but handicraft."21

Morris's efforts to revive handicrafts had not only aesthetic but also social significance. "Healthy" handicrafts were, for Morris, a sign of a "healthy" society. He interpreted the decline of the arts and crafts in the nineteenth century as analogous to a decline of society. It was one of Morris's great illusions to have hoped that a revival of the arts and crafts—the practice of which could, in his view, return to workers their pleasure in labor—would also improve relationships within society. More than a half century later, Gropius and other idealists of “good form" would succumb to the same illusion. Morris was a committed socialist—as his utopian novel, News from Nowhere,²² demonstrates—but he was never really a Marxist. He was rather critical of Marx's theories of political economy, describing them once as "boring nonsense."23 Morris's analysis of society was too heavily permeated by his aesthetic thought to enable an understanding of the structural conditions of nineteenth-century society, which was torn apart by class conflicts. With his blueprint for a quasi-medieval, Arcadian social order, from which drudge work with machinery and social conflict had been eliminated, he stood outside the social reality of his time, Morris's failure to achieve his goal of creating art “from the people for the people" must also be counted among the gaps and inconsistencies in his thoughts and deeds. For his art, which drew its social relevance from its firm connection to practical tasks, in particular from interior design, was nonetheless based “on the designs of a highly talented, highly cultivated man," who, precisely "because he so hated the machine, and because he had sworn an oath to handicrafts ..., could only produce expensive things"24 that were unattainable for the broad public. Morris recognized this himself, and he once said with resignation that what he was doing could ultimately only serve the "obscene luxury of the rich."25

Though we cannot here delineate Morris's successors in detail, it may suffice for this intellectual genealogy of the Bauhaus to mention Charles R. Charles R. Ashbee (1863–1942), who is known primarily for his metalwork and jewelry, was active as both practitioner and theoretician to win recognition for the theories of Ruskin and Morris. In 1888 he founded the Guild and School of Handicraft in London's East End, in which the training took place not in the studio but in workshops. This innovation was of essential importance to the art-school reform movementof the twentieth century, and thus also for the Bauhaus. ## Henry van de Velde

Henry van de Velde (1863–1957), born the same year as Ashbee, is one of the leading figures of Jugendstil.28 Like Morris, he had begun as a painter; like Morris, he had also learned

to feel responsible for society as an artist. He felt he could not fulfill this social obligation as a painter and so he turned to arts and crafts in order to develop objects for industrial production... . It was his principle "to avoid everything that could not be realized by large industry.".. . Technology is the means with which van de Velde sought to realize his ideas of an art that is accessible to the entire society.29

Van de Velde differed from Ruskin and Morris not only in his affirmation of technology—rhetorically, at least—but also in his critical distance from their salvation romanticism inspired by the Middle Ages. He did not, however, abandon ideas of solidarity with the trades; he, too, sought to revive art by starting with handicrafts "but precisely with a mechanized handicraft that had integrated technology."30

Stylistically, van de Velde parted radically from thewhole ballast of historicizing forms, especially Gothic ornament—Jugendstil was the first original style following the period around 1800——and sought a kind of design that allowed the object to reveal its function in an unadorned way. The means to achieve this included, for van de Velde, the line meant to emphasize the construction of the object and to make its function evident, that is, to "give form to the design possibilities contained in the objects that derive from material, construction, function, and use. His 'structo-linear and dynamo-graphic' style of design sought out the 'immanent form of the object itself.'"31 "The line is a force," said van de Velde, and it is this constructive understanding of the line that distinguished him in principle from other Jugendstil artists and their merely decorative use of the line.

These progressive ideas about new artistic creativity—the affirmation of the machine, functional and constructional use of the line—are opposed by the fact that the language of machine art is not characteristic of the craft objects designed by van de Velde. They are marked rather by the signature of a highly cultivated artistic personality and are clearly far from the theoretical postulates of serial or mass production by machine. In the famous Cologne Werkbund controversy of 1914 with Hermann Muthesius—we will return to Muthesius and the Werkbund in a later chapter—van de Velde made his individualistic standpoint emphatically clear:

As long as there are still artists in the Werkbund, and as long as they continue to have an influence on its destiny, they will protest against any suggestion of a canon or standardization. The artist is in his innermost essence a fervent individualist, a freely spontaneous creator; he will never of his own free will submit himself to a discipline that forces him to accept a standard, a canon. He instinctively distrusts everything that could sterilize his actions. . . .32

## Olbrich, Behrens, and the Darmstadt Artists' Colony

Within the scope of our sketch of the lines of intellectual and social historical development—and peripherally within the history of style and form as well—that came together at the Bauhaus, we must briefly consider Olbrich, Behrens, andthe Darmstadt Artists' Colony, precisely because it is a milestone in the history of the effortsrunning from Morris through the Bauhaus—to establish a unity of art and life and to integrate all artistic genres.

The Artists' Colony on Mathildenhöhe was established in 1898-99 on the initiative of Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig of Hesse, who called upon seven artists—the most outstanding of whom were Olbrich and Behrens——to come to Darmstadt. He did so not out of princely patronage but in order to revive the crafts in Hesse by having the artists of the Mathildenhöhe serve as models for the crafts while also having "direct pedagogical effect."33

Before being called to Darmstadt, Joseph Maria Olbrich (1867–1908) had already made a name for himself in Vienna as a founding member of the Secession and as the architect of the Secession building. In 1899 this building, which was perceived as revolutionary, revealed a rigorously cubic composition of masses that anticipated functionalism and was characterized in the magazine Ver sacrum as follows: "Utility was the sole criterion in the disposition of the plan: to produce with the simplest means a useful space for the activities of a modern association of artists."34 In Darmstadt Olbrich left his mark primarily as the architect of the buildings that were constructed in 1901 for the large exhibition Ein Dokument deutscher Kunst (A document of German art). These buildings form an ensemble of unified conception that included, as a "temple to artistic work," a common studio building (the Ernst Ludwig House) and a series of residential buildings “newly constructed, fully furnished, and intended to be occupied later."35

Before he joined the Darmstadt Artists' Colony, Peter Behrens (1868-1940) had come to the fore as a painter, graphic artist, and type designer in Munich, where he had cofounded the Secession and the Werkstätten für Kunst im Handwerk (Workshops for art in the crafts), one of the craft workshops of this period intended to improve the quality of local arts and crafts in Germany by following the model of the English handicraft guilds.

Most of the buildings on the Mathildenhöhe were designed by Joseph Maria Olbrich, but there was one exception: "only Behrens, the former painter, wants to fulfill the whole assignment; he builds his house himself and designs everything from the outer structure to the tableware in a unified way like a single work of art, like a single rich piece of craftsmanship."36 Although the Behrens House, in contrast to most of the other buildings that Olbrich designed, exudes a rather gloomy sobriety, the ensemble as a whole is indeed permeated by the Jugendstil spirit of unity and will to form, and it represents—even in its present reduced state resulting from the effects of the war—a unique document of its time.

It is interesting to see that Behrens turned away from Jugendstil quite early and that—following an interlude from 1903 to 1907 as the director of the Düsseldorf Kunstgewerbeschule (School of Arts and Crafts), which can in many ways be considered a proto-Bauhaus³7—he established a milestone in architecturalmodernism with the construction of the AEG (Allgemeine Elektricitäts-Gesellschaft, the General Electricity Company) Turbine Hall in Berlin (1909). With this factory building, despite its unmistakably traditional features (symmetry, the motif of the pediment, pylonlike emphases in the corners), Behrens opened the door to a thoroughgoing functionalism that would become the credo for Gropius, who was his coworker at the time. ## Hermann Muthesius and the German Werkbund

The German Werkbund, which——interrupted only by the period of National Socialism—has survived until thepresent day, despite manychanges in form, set out in its initial phase to bring together, institutionalize, and give organizational form to the efforts at artistic and social renewal discussed above that had previously been largely the work of individuals. Although the spectrum of opinion within its membership was extraordinarilybroad —even at the time it was spoken of as an "alliance of the most intimate enemies"40it was nonetheless a highly fruitful enterprise. One of the most distinquished andprominent individuals in the early Werkbund was Hermann Muthesius (1861– 1927), who was among the first to recognize clearly the future tasks of a new kind of design operating in the area of tension between art and industry.

At the turn of the century Muthesius was assigned to the German embassy in London as attaché for architecture in order to study the construction of homes in England. There he learned a style of building that—in its comparative freedom from the Jugendstil of the continent-—Muthesius described as "masterly" in its "practicality and utility."41Muthesius saw the historicizing architecture of both the Gründerzeit (the period of promoterism: the years of rapid industrial expansion in the 1870s) and Jugendstil as manifestations of the bogus desire for status felt by the rising bourgeoisie. In his book Style-Architecture and Building-Art— published in 1902, that is, a year after Darmstadt—he criticizedJugendstil for its "luxuriant extravagance of form and [its] rage for sensational designs":

The already mentioned emotional whipping of lines, which originated in Belgium, does not take into account the material; it forces the ornament of the book, the brass candlestick, and the piece of furniture indiscriminately under its spell. With furniture design in particular, it demands irresponsible sacrifices in the treatment of construction and material, since the most evident property of wood is the clear directionality of its fibers. And even if today's technology can overcome any difficulty of construction resulting from this approach, . . . the entire joiner's trade nevertheless works in certain artificial relations. Above all, such practice is extremely expensive andits products remain beyond the broader spectrum of people. Taken very generally, treating a material in a way contrary to its nature is hardly in accord with the spirit of a time such as ours, which is characterized by very practical and sober thought. The new movement would gain much conviction and popularity if it displayed more naturalness and a healthy sense of workmanship. . . . Then, too, its products would be available to the greater number of the people and its impact would be much greater. Today, no movement that seeks to be a reform movement can direct itself only to the production of luxury art; its goal, rather, must be to pursue an art suited to middle-class society, which defines the general character of our modern social condition.42

This quotation summarizes in condensed form Muthesius's stand, his critique of Jugendstil, and his own position in response to it, namely, his call for appropriate use of materials, for economy of production, and for an impact on a broad segment of society. Muthesius recognized that history is an irreversible process, and thus that in the wake of industrialization the machine had surpassed handwork once and for all, and that the engineer was increasingly taking the place of the artist. He was conscious not only of the necessity of machine production for society but also of the new aesthetic possibilities that the machine brought with it. The insight that “spinning a web" over objects with “ornaments produced by machine“ hadonly led to “results of a lamentable sort," and that a mechanically "produced decorative form" had a “repulsive" effect, caused him to conclude: “machine ornament [is] a mistake. The smooth form, reduced to what is useful, is what we expect from machine production."43 For Muthesius, who certainly wished to leave the final decision in all issues of form to the artist (the designer in today's sense was an idea whose time was yet to come), this insight meant that practicality was the goal to be sought: “The more our crafts movement returns to this practicality [Sachlichkeit], the more modern it will be, that is, the more it will suit the overall characteristic of our time, creating out of the spirit of our time."44

Not only in his writings but also in numerous lectures, Muthesius advocated this idea of making things practical, of stripping away ornamental incrustations from machine products. As an aside, it should be noted that Muthesius's programmatic rhetoric was far moreprogressive than his own aesthetic practice as an architect, as one look at the buildings he designed will show.45 Be that as it may, his postulates and arguments met with a scathing rejection from the conservative Trade Association of Workers in the Arts and Crafts (an association of manufacturers), which demanded that the emperor dismiss him as adviser on arts and crafts schools in the State Department for Arts and Crafts in Berlin. This episode provided the external motivation for several progressively minded manufacturers, artists, and writers to join together in an umbrella organization for good design, which led to the founding of the German Werkbund in Munich in 1907. Paragraph 2 of the Werkbund statutes reads: "The goal of the Werkbund is to ennoble work in the arts and crafts in collaboration with art, industry, and handicraft by means of education, propaganda, and united positions on pertinent issues."46 The goal was to unify and reconcile sectors—namely, art, handicrafts, industry, and trade—that until then had found themselves in a latent, or even manifest, state of conflict. In practice this meant that within the Werkbund a wide range of interests, to some degree even opposing ones, clashed; their views often intersected only at the vanishing point of a vague notion of quality:

The Werkbund seeks. . . to select the best works in the arts, industry, crafts, and trade. It wishes to unite all that is available in terms of quality production and effort in the arts and crafts. It represents a meeting place for all who are willing and able to achieve quality, who see work in the arts andcrafts as one part—andnot the smallest one—of cultural achievement in general, andwho want to create a center for themselves and for others to represent their interests with an exclusive consideration of the idea of quality.47

The Werkbund's goals with respect to political economy, which sometimes commingled with undertones of cultural imperialism, cannot be examined more closely here. We can only underscore that backward-looking social utopias à la Ruskin and Morris were replaced by the efforts to contribute to a realistic design of aesthetic and social reality that consciously drew on the possibilities of technology. Despite this fundamental agreement among the members of the Werkbund (to which Gropius belonged from 1912 on), however, on certain questions there was simply no unity to be had, as was demonstrated by the above-mentioned controversy between Muthesius and van de Velde at the Werkbund Convention in Cologne in 1914. While van de Velde advocated the free development of artistic individuality, Muthesius insisted on the standardization of machine-made products.48Although certainly not new, this conflict came to a head in Cologne; it was unresolved at the convention, and, with the outbreak of World War I shortly thereafter, remained so for some time to come. After the war the debate would continue, sometimes in the background, sometimes openly, at the Bauhaus, whose history we will now outline briefly.

## 3 The History of the Bauhaus

From Henry van de Velde's Arts and Crafts School to the Founding of the Bauhaus in .1919

In 1917, three years after the outbreak of the war, the Belgian Henry van de Velde left Weimar, where he had been active for many years, under the pressure of political events, in particular the hostilities to which he was subjected as a foreigner. In 1901he hadbeen called to Weimar as the artistic adviser of the Grand Duke of Saxony, and there he founded the Großherzoglich sächsische Kunstgewerbeschule (Grand ducal Saxon arts and crafts school)in1906. In contrast to the traditional academic education for artists, as it existed at the neighboringGroßherzoglich sächsische Hochschule für bildende Kunst (Grand ducal

Saxon academy of arts), the teaching and instructional activity at the Kunstgewerbeschule was, in keeping with the efforts at a renewal of the arts and crafts that were sketched out in the previous chaptér, directed toward the solution of practical design tasks that were relevant to everyday life rather than the production of autonomous works of art.1

In 1915 van de Velde proposed as his successor, alongside the prominent Jugendstilartists Hermann Obrist andAugust Endell,Wa/ter Gropius (1883–1969), who hadsidedwith him against Muthesius in the so-called Werkbund Debate of 1914 in Cologne (see the sections “Henry van de Velde" and “Hermann Muthesius and the German Werkbund" in chapter 2).²

Gropius, who learned a new conception o architecture while working as an assistant to Peter Behrens (AEG Turbine Hall, 1909), had made his name even before the war with a series of buildings that showed him to be a progressive architect whodidnot deny technology but rather consciously exploited its constructional and aesthetic possibilities (Fagus factory, Alfeld an der Leine, 1911; office building and machine shop at the Werkbund exhibition in Cologne, 1914).³ Already in 1910 he had formulated his forward-looking architectonic goals in a program proposal as follows:

Instead of good proportions and practical simplicity, pomposity and a false romanticism have become the trend of the time. . . .

Enterprises whose production is based on craftsmanship (to a certain extent still including the building trade) can no longer bear the competition of industry.... The fundamental principle of all industry is the division of labor. The inventor concentrates all his mental energies on the viability of the idea, the invention; the manufacturer on cheap and durable production; and the merchant on organized marketing of the finished product. It is only with the aid of such specialists that it becomes possible to make the essential, i.e., the spiritual creation, economically feasible and at the same time provide the public withproducts of esthetically and technically good quality.4

In his program Gropius called for the “founding of a general housing-construction company following artistically uniform principles" that should function on this same principle of the division of labor that was common elsewhere in industry. Only in this way would it be possible for

the projects of this company and the designs for their individual components [to] be .. . prepared down to the minutest detail by responsible designers before they are ready to be executed. . . Thus, art and technology would be happily united and the public at large would be able to acquire truly mature, good art and solid, genuine goods.. ..

To implement the concept of the industrialization of house construction, the company will repeat individual components in all of its designs and hence facilitate mass production, promising low costs and easy rentability.⁵

This program, which discusses, among other things, “functionalism and solidity" (Sachlichkeit und Solidität) and which is an early outline of the basic features of functional building (one senses the proximity to Muthesius and the Werkbund), became reality when Gropius put it into practice some fifteen years later at the Dessau Bauhaus. That it seems valid as an intellectual blueprint, as a utopia in Ernst Bloch's sense, even in the intervening period—though admittedly strangely watered down and obscured by visions of the past— can be seen from his “Recommendations for the Founding of an Educational Institution as an Artistic Counseling Service for Industry, the Trades, and the Crafts," which Gropius submitted to the State Ministry in Weimar in January 1916.

In its first section these "Recommendations" consist of a repetition of well-known ideas from the Werkbund and thus continue the line of the program proposal of 1910. Gropius called on the artist to “sensibly [come] to terms. . . with the machine . . . and [press] it into his service"6 (cf. Ashbee's idea of the domesticatedmachine in “Ruskin, Morris, and the Arts and Crafts Movement" in chapter 2). In his view, the quality of machine production can be ensuredif the artist participates from "the moment the form which will be mass-produced is invented": “For the artist possesses the ability to breathe soul into the lifeless product of the machine. ... His collaboration is not a luxury, not a pleasing adjunct; it must become an indispensable component in the total output of modern industry." Only a"working community between the artist, the businessman and the technician"7 could, according to Gropius, guarantee that the present and future tasks of industrial society would be adequately fulfilled.

In the second section of the “Recommendations," which is concerned with“an educational institution established by the state as an artistic counseling service," Gropius seems, by contrast, to have completely changed his attitude with respect to his position in 1910. This applies first to his attempt to accommodate himself to the craftsmen and smallmanufacturers active in Weimar by seeking "the school's dependence on existing practical firms" in that the students would bring their “work material from the workshop into school."

One might suspect that this is a tactical maneuver "more likely [to] win the confidence of the manufacturer," but in that case it is especially surprising to find the idea that a

similarly happy partnership might re-emerge as that practicedin the medieval “lodges," where numerous related artist-craftsmen—architects, sculptors, and craftsmen of all grades—came together ina homogeneous spirit and humbly contributed their independent work to the common tasks resting upon them. This was done out of respect for the unity of a mutual idea which inspired them and the meaning of which they understood.8

These emotional sentences no longer speak of a synthesis of art and industry, and it is interesting to see that it is precisely this backwardlooking utopia, which aimed at a thorough social andcultural renewal, that three years later formed the heart of the Bauhaus founding manifesto. When, at the end of the war, the question of van de Velde's successor was decided in his favor, Gropius, following the idea of a unified academy of free and applied art, founded State Bauhaus in Weimar as a merger of the former academy of art and the school of arts and crafts. It is clear that the medieval masons' lodge (Bauhütte) actedas the godparent at the christening. The founding manifesto, with a cuboexpressionist title page by Lyonel Feininger, reads in part:

The ultimate aim of all visual arts is the complete building!. . . Architects, painters and sculptors must recognize anew and learn to grasp the composite character of a building both as an entity and in its separate parts. Only then will their work be embedded with the architectonic spirit which it has lost as "salon art."

The old schools of art were unable to produce this unity; how could they, since art cannot be taught. They must be merged once more with the workshop. ... Architects, painters, sculptors, we all must return to crafts! For art is not a “profession." There is no essentialdifference between the artist and the craftsman. The artist is an exalted craftsman. . . .

Let us . . . create a new guild of craftsmen without the class-distinctions that raise an arrogant barrier between craftsman and artist! Together let us desire, conceive and create the new structure of the future, which willembrace architecture and sculpture and painting in one unity. . . .9

In the Bauhaus program that was appended to this manifesto, Gropius stated that a "thorough training in the crafts acquired in workshops and in experimental and practical sites, is required of all students as the indispensable basis for all artistic production" and that "every student must learn a craft." The return to the medieval crafts ideal—to the ideas of community in the guild and the idea of the Gesamtkunstwerk (total work of art)—that is evident from theseprogrammatic sentences stands in remarkable contrast to Gropius's progressive prewar conceptions (for more detail on this, see "An Overview of the Goals and Organizational Structure of the Teachings at the Bauhaus" in chapter 4).

This change in orientation canonly be explained against the background of the specific circumstances of the time. The events of the war and the disruptions of the immediate postwar period gave Gropius strong misgivings about the machine and the doctrine of progress associated with it. Hence, affected by a far-reaching crisis of meaning and existence, he clung to the hope that his own activities could be qiven purpose and direction by evoking the——no doubt idealized—Middle Ages. There is abundant evidence for such an interpretation. For example, Gropius remarked in his speech for the first exhibition of student works from the Bauhaus in June 1919:

We find ourselves in a colossal catastrophe of world history, in a transformation of the whole of life. .. . Before the war we put the cart before the horse and wanted to drag art backward into the public sphere by means of organization. We designed artistic ashtrays and beer mugs, andin that way hoped to work up to the great building.... That was an incredible presumption on which we were shipwrecked, and now things will be reversed. No large spiritualorganizations, but small, secret, self-contained societies, lodges. . . .10

And in a letter to Tomás Maldonado, then rector of the Hochschule für Gestaltung (College for Design) in Ulm, Gropius wrote retrospectively in 1963 that the manifesto had been written out of a

mixture of profound defeat as a result of the lost war and of the breakdown of intellectual and economic life and an ardent hope that something new could be built up from these ruins, without the state, which had been felt so oppressively before, becoming mixed up in it.

A more sober call for sober work would have missed the point at the time, which was to offer to young people, filled with new ideas, a broad basis upon which these ideas could be elucidated and tested in practice. The success of the manifesto speaks for itself; young people arrived from Germany and abroad, not in order to build “workable" lamps but to be part of a community that wanted to build a new humanity in a new environment and to release creative spontaneity in everything. That kind of beginning always has something romantic and utopian. . . .11

In presenting the historical development of the Bauhaus, there are several options for dividing it into periods. One would be basedon the sequenceof directors: the Walter Gropius era (1919–28), the Hannes Meyer era (1928–30), and the Ludwig Mies van der Rohe era (1930–33). Whereas these periods are determined more by external events, Wulf Herzogenrath has proposed a division into five phases, basedprimarily on stylistic aspects:“1. expressionist, individual, focusedon crafts (Itten, Schreyer, and so on), 1919–21; 2. formal, emphasizing basic forms and colors (the influence of Kandinsky, Theo van Doesburg, and so on), 1922–24; 3. The presentation that follows is oriented around the analysis in Friedhelm Kröll's Bauhaus, 1919–1933,16 which, though it keeps to the notion of three phases, focuses more on social psychology (and the history of ideas) in selecting the dates to divide them than is the case of the tripartite divisions based on the directors or the locations, which seem much less persuasive and indeed rather arbitrary. Kröll distinguishes between the founding phase (1919–23), the phase of consolidation (1923–28), and the phase of disintegration (1928–33). Although most art historians have been rather reserved toward this division, it proves to be especially well suited when the focus is on the question of the extent to which, in these individual phases, the Bauhaus as a collective, as a social system, was not just in conformity with the true idea of the Bauhaus but actively took part in its realization.

The pairing of a master of form with a master of craft was intended in the early years to "help make up for the lack of individuals who combined artistic abilities with experience in the crafts."21 Nevertheless, this sensible arrangement provedin practice to be extremely problematic in terms of group psychology. Although Gropius's programmatic statements in the founding document of the Bauhaus amounted to a Songof Songs to the crafts, the masters of craft were, according to the principles, granted no decisionmaking powers in the social hierarchy of the Bauhaus. The final power lay with the director, who was the chairman of the so-called council of masters, an "advisory and decision-making body in all didactic and organizational questions relating to the institute."22 In the council of masters, only the masters of form held the right to vote; the masters of craft were given only an advisory function. This subordination of the masters of craft to the masters of form, which necessarily led to tensions, clearly shows that in the early phase—despite all the praise of the crafts that the rhetoric evoked, despite all of the programs aimed at demystifying the traditional image of the artist—priority was given to autonomously creative artists who were primarily focused on the province of the arts. Without a doubt, one factor in this was that near-Iy all of the masters of form active at the Bauhaus were artists of high standing and distinctive personality, whose artistic authority, as it were, created a distance between them and the masters of craft.

Despite all of the internal controversies at the Bauhaus during the founding phase, Johannes Itten contributed decisively to its stabilization by introducing elementary instruction, the so-called preliminary course, as a fixed element within the teachings of the Bauhaus, leading to some degree of formalization of the teaching system at the Bauhaus, which still remained somewhat indeterminate. The goal of this preliminary course, which was obligatory for everyone entering the Bauhaus and took on something of the character of a rite of initiation (one had to pass it to be accepted into the Bauhaus community), was not just to remove the slag of academic ideas about art and design and to develop free artistic personalities but also to pass on basic qualifications for design in the form of a “supraindividual design language"23 that could serve as the basis for understanding and communicating the self. (That in the end this "design language" differed significantly from one teacher to another and thus in no way equalized individual conceptions will be discussed in more detail in chapters 5 through 7.)

Despite the potential for conflict that was inherent in the structure of dual leadership of the workshops, the Bauhaus derived increasing stability from these very workshops, which Kröll has interpreted as the very “loci of stability."24 Building on the preliminary course, they provided training aimed at the acquisition of specific technical/artisanal and artistic/design abilities in the form of practical work with concrete tasks, some of which had the explicit character of projects.25 Here the private language of the masters of form with which they expressed themselves as painters or sculptors in their own artistic production, was transformed “into a language shared by the institution," a result of the increased "technological orientation of problem-solving attempts."26 Consequently, one contribution of the masters of craft, with their knowledge of materials and practical experience, was to keep the Bauhaus from lapsing back in its goal of facilitation between art and handicrafts and twisting the Bauhaus idea back into an elite avant-garde academy. Nevertheless, the Bauhaus in its early phase was far from being a training institute that would realize the idea of a Gesamtkunstwerk under the “aegis of architecture." Paradoxically, not only was a department of architecture lacking in the first years, which in keeping with the original idea ought to have formed the axis and hinge of the Bauhaus, but also there was no coordination among the individual workshops, so that “one can observe grave differences in the structure of productivity and conformity to the goal."27

The Bauhaus was subjected to one especially difficult test of strength in the conflict between Gropius and Itten, which lasted for some time and was only resolved with Itten's departure from the Bauhaus in 1923. Structurally, the conflict resulted from two fundamentally different, even diametrically opposed, philosophies of life, and—though it was certainly representative of the overall situation at the early Bauhaus, with its preponderance of fine artists—it found a very personal culmination in its two most distinguished figures, Gropius and Itten. It was the conflict between autonomous artistry and design intended to meet social obligations. Although Itten had contributed much to the stability of the institute by establishing the preliminary course, the subcultural/bohemian attitudes and the quasi-religious activities of the Itten group (Mazdaism) found little sympathy with Gropius. He viewedthese tendencies as a threat of sectarian return to the ivory tower of art, which meant a turn from the guiding principles of the Bauhaus. For Gropius's fundamental concern was to find a new place in society for the artists who had become homeless during the nineteenth century, a place that would enable them to participate in a socially constructive way in the design of reality. Moreover, for Gropius it was essential that the Bauhaus "be kept open to productive contacts with the industrial sphere."28

In 1922 Gropius formulated his standpoint in the conflict with Itten as follows:

Recently, Master Itten demanded from us a decision either to produce individual pieces of work in complete contrast to the economically oriented outside world or to seek contact with industry. It is here, in this method of formulating the question i believe, that the big unknown that needs to be solved is hidden. Let me at once clarify this: I seek unity in the fusion, not in the separation of these ways of life. . . .

The Bauhaus could become a haven for eccentrics if it were to lose contact with the work and working methods of the outside world. . . . I see a danger to our youth in the indications of a wild romanticism that derives from an understandable reaction against the predominating state of mind—numbers and force—and from the breakdown of countries. Some of our Bauhaus members subscribe to a kind of misunderstood “return to nature" doctrine of Rousseau's. . . .

How the broad gulf between the activity we practice in our workshops and the present level of crafts and industry outside will someday be closed, that is the unknown quantity. . . . Contact with industry and with the practical work of the world can only be established gradually. It is possible that the work in the Bauhaus workshops will lead more and more to the production of single prototypes [which will serve as guides to the craftsman and industry]. Students who have gone through the Bauhaus will be in a position, with the knowledge they have acquired there, to exert a decisive influence on existing craft [enterprises] and industrial works.29

Hans M. Wingler has noted that the emphasis on industrial production in this text is symptomatic of the first great change within the Bauhaus.3º It marks the return of the Bauhaus director to his original ideas from the prewar years, which were close to those of the Werkbund, and it is summarized in his formula “Art and technology, a new unity! Technology does not require art, but art very much needs technology."31 It is surely no coincidence that this phrase was contemporaneous with the return of the expressionist influence and increased entrée of constructivist ideas at the Bauhaus (and of course with the economic recov: ery of Germany with the end of inflation in 1923).

In 1922, with the appointment of Kandinsky and El Lissitzky's participation in the Dada-Constructivist Congress in Weimar, the Bauhaus came in contact with Russian constructivism. Moreover, in 1921–22, Theo van Doesburg, the theoretician of the Dutch constructivist group de Stijl, was in Weimar several times, where he gave private seminars on the "fundamental concepts of the new art of design" (to quote the title of volume six in the Bauhausbücher series). These seminars were attended above all by those members of the Bauhaus who were opposed to the circle around Itten and its expressionistic and spiritually informed orientation. Walter Dexel emphasized that "the fundamental change that took place there [at the Bauhaus] during 1922–23... would havé been inconceivable without Doesburg's influence.“32 By sharply criticizing the expressionist tendencies and the production of individual works of art at the Bauhaus and by recalling the original intentions of an artistic and social synthesis, of which he could find no trace, van Doesburg's contribution to the clarification of the school's self-perception and the determination of a new course was by no means trivial. As Theo van Doesburg's mouthpiece, the de Stijl member Vilmos Huszár summarized this criticism as follows in 1922:

What the masters do, they teach. What do they achieve? Everyone does what whim proposes, far from any binding discipline. Where are there at least attempts at cooperative work . .. ; where is there a consolidation of several disciplines; where are there even attempts at a unified work of art, at unified design of space, form, color? Paintings, nothing but paintings and little sketches, graphic work, and individual sculptures. What Feininger is exhibiting was done better in France ten years ago (cubism of 1912). His latest watercolors are a diluted rehash of Klee! Klee himself scribbles morbid dreams of the sort that Prinzhorn's paintings by the mentally ill provide just as beautifully, or even more so. Kandinsky constantly repeats hisdaubed decorations.... Arbitrary and whimsical! The works of G. Muche are still more arbitrary and whimsical, intellectual, without any intuitive power, angst-filleddottings. Itten's vacantly pompous smears aim only at superficial effects. Schlemmer's works are experiments of the sort we already know from other sculptors.... Director Gropius places an expressionist memorial at the cemetery in Weimar that, the result of a cheap literary inspiration, cannot even compete with Schlemmer's sculptures. To achieve the goals the Bauhaus has set in its program would require other masters, masters who know what it means to design a unified work of art and whose works have demonstrated their ability to achieve it.33

As unmeasured in judgment and exaggerated in diction as this critique was, it seems nonetheless to have had an effect on Gropius and the council of masters, as can be seen from the development at the Weimar Bauhaus from 1923 on. Admittedly, Doesburg's hope of being appointed to the Bauhaus by Gropius remained unanswered, because Gropius considered him too aggressive and dogmatic as a teacher.34 At the time, Feininger assessed the question of Doesburg's appointment to the Bauhaus as follows:

For most of them the unsentimental—if also completely uninspired—Doesburg is something of a pillar among all the heated and conflicting points of view, something definite and clear, which they can hold on to. . . . If Doesburg were a master at the Bauhaus, it would not harm the work in general, it could even be useful, for he would be a counterattraction to that rampant romanticism that is haunting us. OnlyI don't suppose he would be capable of keeping within his own bounds but would, like Itten, soon want to assume command over the whole works.35

## The Phase of Consolidation (1923-1928)

The Gropius-Itten conflict ended, as mentioned, in 1923 with Itten's departure from the Bauhaus36 and the appointment of Moholy-Nagy. Since Gropius had already appointed Kandinsky in 1922, one can observe with Moholy's arrival at the Bauhaus in 1923 a "strengthening of loyalties"37 within the teaching staff and the end of the sectarian divisions of the early years. The institute was also stabilized internally in 1925 with the acceptance into the teaching staff of several junior masters who had been trained at the Bauhaus. Through this process of recruiting its own, by placing former Bauhaus students and apprentices in leading positions, the conformity and continuity of the work goals at the Bauhaus could be assured. The double qualification of the junior masters as artists and artisans also made it possible to abandon the—conflict-ridden—system of two directors in the workshops. The workshops were now filled with several junior masters:

Herbert Bayer: Printing workshop (focus on typography and advertisements, formerly Feininger)

Marcel Breuer: Furniture workshop (formerly cabinetmaking under Gropius)

Hinnerk Scheper: Wall painting workshop (formeriy Kandinsky)

Joost Schmidt: Plastic arts workshop (formerly sculpture under Itten or Itten and Schlemmer)

Gunta Stölzl: Textile department (formerly weaving; 1925–26 together with Muche; 1926–31 on her own)

This process of reorganization—the new names, which were intended to eliminate connotations of art, should also be seen in this context—was completed in 1927 with the establishment of a department of architecture, under the direction of Hannes Meyer.

It is obvious that the Bauhaus emerged stronger from this process of organizational revitalization, so that the years from 1923until the departure of Walter Gropius, László Moholy-Nagy, Herbert Bayer, and Marcel Breuer in 1928 are quite accurately characterized as a "phase of consolidation." The replacement of the masters of form with junior masters as directors in the workshops created a new problem, however: the connection between the art courses and the workshop activity. The older masters of form—overwhelmingly fine artists—now concentrated more on "elementary questions of the production of individual artworks," whereas "for the junior masters the basic artistic and technical problems of mass production occupied the foreground." Because of a lack of "institutionally provided links," both areas led increasingly independent lives, a development that would soon lead to the "marginalization of the painters" and ultimately to their “loss of function"38 in a production group operating under the guiding principle of the integration of art and technology. In 1926 GeorgMuche spoke out pointedly against the escalating industrialization of the Bauhaus, and in 1927 he left the institute. He was suspicious of the increasing tendency to “emphasize efficiency in an instrumentalized way"39 and to tailor the individual, artistic sphere to a general industrial orientation, because, as Muche put it, “Art and technology are not a new unity; they remain essentially different in their creative worth."40 It can be shown that the Bauhaus from 1923 on did indeed increasingly become a teaching institute and especially a production center focused on the design and production of prototypes for industry. (One reason behind this, by the way, was the director's idea of possibly making the institute independent of public funding or at least to improve the perpetually tight budgets.) The notion ofmechanical reproduction now became a universal guiding principle for action, with the result that the production of individual works of art was by and large banished to the sphere of the private studio. With the gradual elimination of the medievalizing romanticism of crafts and the expressionist cult of the unique object, a rigorous, sober functionalism began to develop, the beginnings of which can be seen in the legendary Bauhaus exhibition of 1923, the school's first large public presentation.

The outstanding contributions to this exhibition were—in addition to designs from the courses—an overview of international architecture, the "model house" (the House “Am Horn") by Georg Muche, and the wall designs by Joost Schmidt (ill. 215), Herbert Bayer (ill. 151, and especially Oskar Schlemmer (ill. 196) in the rooms of the Bauhaus.

In the architecture exhibition concepts were presented that were distinguished by a shared “tendency to achieve the highest form of homo: geneity, relevance, and clarity" that came "much closer to the spirit of our age than all 'Utopian' and 'Expressionistic' architecture."41 The sketches and models from the international avant-garde that were exhibited (Oud, Le Corbusier, Mies van der Rohe, and Gropius, among others) were early examples of the Neues Bauen4² (New Building) movement that was just forming at the time, which would soon lead to the so-called International Style. At the same time, they were symptomatic of the changes taking place at the Bauhaus at the time in moving from the expressionistic early phase to a clear functionalist orientation.

Although there was no architecture class during the Weimar period or even during the early years in Dessau, and despite the financial difficulties (inflation) of the postwar period, great efforts were made in the Bauhaus exhibition not to allow the principles of a new functional architecture to remain a mere theoretical postulate but to turn them into a reality, if only a modest one. The only contribution to a planned large Bauhaus housing colony was the“model" or “experimental" house (Haus am Horn), which was not unproblematic in its distribution of space, designed by the painter Georg Muche⁴³ and furnished by the Bauhaus workshops. Although the discrepancy between the rhetorical pathos of the founding manifesto and the single-family home realized here is unmistakable, one can nevertheless see the effort to achieve an architecturalperspective that aims at practicality (Sachlichkeit)andfunctionality. Equally evident is the effort (also in keeping with the founding program) to bring the individual genres of art, or sectors of the crafts, together in the construction, if not in an ideal unity then at least in a division of labor that contributes to the overall task. This could be seen in the earlier expressionist SommerfeldHouse that Gropius designed in Berlin-Dahlem (1920–21; ill. 213), the favorite pedagogical model at the Bauhaus for project methodology.

To complete this discussion of the Bauhaus exhibition, we should mention the wall reliefs and paintings produced for the occasion by Joost Schmidt and Herbert Bayer in the main building and by Oskar Schlemmer in the workshop building. They were impressive demonstrations of the search for unity called for in the program, which sought to abolish the autonomy of the individual work of art by synthesizing the artistic genres of architecture, sculpture, and painting44 (see "Stages of Artistic Development" in chapter 10 and “Biographical Notes" in chapter 11).

The Bauhaus exhibition of 1923, which contributed decisively toward clarifying the school's self-image and defining its goals, received generally favorable press in Germany and abroad. This fact and the declaration of solidarity with the Bauhaus made by the Werkbund, which was meeting in Weimar at the time, no doubt represented an intellectual reinforcement of its position.45 Financially and politically, however, the situation was extremely precarious. The constant criticism from right-wing parties and the negative attitude of the Social Democratic minister of finance Hartmann, who considered the school "a superfluous and unpromising institution"46 and was not prepared to support the Bauhaus by granting it credit, dramatically limited its ability to function.

The endof the Weimar Bauhaus soon followed when the elections for the state parliament in February 1924—after the army of the Reich had marchedinto Saxony andThuringia in 1923—ended with the right-wing parties victorious. The funds for the Bauhaus were cut so drastically that the Bauhaus masters declared the dissolutionof the institution on 31 March 1925.47

It was thanks to the initiative of the Social Democratic mayor of Dessau, Fritz Hesse, that the Bauhaus was able to find a new home in this expanding industrial city in 1925. This change of location, which could have jeopardized its existence and led to disintegration, proved on the contrary to contribute extraordinarily to stability and consolidation. Under the aegis of Hesse,48 who protected the Bauhaus "with diplomatic skill and exemplary courage"49 until 1932, when the National Socialists took power in Dessau, and now with considerable financial means, the Bauhaus was able to develop more steadily and systematically than had ever been possible in Weimar. Other factors in this were that the entire teaching staff, with the exception of Gerhard Marcks, moved to Dessau intact; that the staff was, as already mentioned, supplemented by a series of junior masters; and that the construction of a new school building, as well as the masters' houses, could be built according to plans by Gropius under optimal circumstances.

The shift away from the expressionist founding phase, already visible in Weimar, was in abundant evidence in Dessau and can be seen most clearly in the workshops, the main sphere of activity for the junior masters.50 Cabinetmaking—renamed the furniture workshop in 1925—was one of the most successful workshops, along with the metal workshop. During the Weimar period it had produced, under Gropius's direction, furniture that was characterized by a severely cubic tectonics and revealed in part the influence of the de Stijl group, especially that of Rietveld (though without Rietveld's constructivist playfulness, which sometimes neglected the function and made the furniture look like autonomous art). Marcel Breuer (1902–81), who had come to the Bauhaus as a student in 1920 and had already focused on problems of the construction of seating furniture during his studies, took over the furniture workshop in 1925 as a junior master. The workshop for wall painting, directed by Kandinsky from 1922 to 1925, was taken over by Hinnerk Scheper (1897–1957) in Dessau. In contrast to Kandinsky's interest in monumental abstract painting—which could hardly be realized within the context of the Neues Bauen of the twenties and thirties, with its tendencies toward dematerialization (glass and steel)--Scheper's focus was on problems of architectural and spatial design with color, and thus he became a representative of what would later be called "color dynamics." The most outstanding examples of this search for new possibilities of architectural design with color are the staircase for the school building of the Bauhaus, opened in 1926, and the masters' houses in Dessau. For example, in the masters' house that Kandinsky occupied the ceilings of several rooms were painted black—an unusually bold approach for the time—and the masters' houses for Feininger and Klee, which were reconstructed in the 1990s, reveal a palette that includes intense red and blue hues. Another result of the efforts to design space using color was the development of Bauhaus wallpapers (1930), usually monochrome with discreet patterns, which continued to be produced by Rasch & Co. even after World War Ill. Alongside experiments with the relationship between space and color, the work program of the workshop for wall painting also included practical experiments with materials and techniques in order to assure a technological means for architectural and spatial design with color.56

The turn at the Bauhaus toward the solution of applied design problems, which resulted in a decline in fine arts activities and the marginalization of the painters, is especially evident in the workshop for the typography and advertising (formerly printing). It was led by Herbert Bayer (1900–1985) in Dessau until 1928 and then by Joost Schmidt (1893–1948). Experiments with typographical elements had already been made in Itten's circle, but in the early years at the Bauhaus the emphasis was on individual style and expressive form, which often led to significantly decreased legibility. As early as 1923, Moholy-Nagy reacted against this: "Typography is a tool of communication. It must be communication in its most intense form.... Legibility—communication must never be impaired by an a priori esthetic. Letters may never be forcedinto a preconceivedframework. .. ."57 Herbert Bayer logically put this thesis into practice, as the sans serif typefaces he designed show (Universal; no-outline shadow typeface). Similar attempts to rationalize typefaces and scripts are the basis for Josef Albers's stencil type and the typographic works of Joost Schmidt. Herbert Bayer set an example in the combination of typeandimages. He consideredhimself a painter but in the late twenties he successfully applied the compositional principles of surrealism to appliedgraphics in a way that was both impressive and useful.58

The phase of consolidation found its end in 1927, when the architectural teachings found an institutional form in thelong-overdue architecture department, which Hannes Meyer (1889- 1954) took over as director.59 Until then budgetary difficulties had prevented the establishment of an architecture department (the site for experimental buildings, for which Gropius had applied in 1923, was not approved) so that the only option available to members of the Bauhaus was sporadic participation in Gropius's building projects, which gave few opportunities to address problems of architecture. Now, however, architectural theory was given the substance within the Bauhaus teaching canon that it deserved. Meyer's contribution was to create a systematic course of architecture on a scientific basis, which he stripped of all aestheticism that contradicted his socially determined conception of function. In retrospect, Meyer summarized his pedagogical effect in the architectural class as follows:

In our architecturalcourse we developeda "functional building," which, unlike the vulgar interpretation of the term, went beyond the "purely technical." By analyzing the state of society and carefully studying all biological factors, we hoped to deepen and enrich architecture, with special attention to psychological factors and the organization of life. We explored the living spaces of individual blue- and white-collar families in order better to standardize their living cells. Finally, we made an overall analysis of urban construction in the city of Dessau, which clearly showed the inadequacies and class nature of this "exemplary city." The workers' quarters lay, without exception, in the harmful industrialzones, andthe cultural establishments were concentrated in theresidential zones of the wealthy population.60

Elsewhere he states the foliowing conception of architecture: "Architecture is no longer building art. Building has become a science. Architecture is building science. Building is not a matter of emotion but of knowledge. Building is thus not a matter of composition determined by emotion. Building is an act of superior organization."61

If we borrow from Kröll the designation "phase of consolidation" for the period between 1923 and 1928, we should add that another sign of the increasing stability of the Bauhaus during the early years at Dessau was the publication of the journal bauhaus (1926–31) and the book series

Bauhausbücher (14 volumes between 1925 and 1931).

To summarize: in the years 1923 to 1928 the school's focus on "goal-oriented task[s] that were (in part) determined by commissions from industry"62 grew stronger and became reality. "Standards, norms, serial production, mass production"—that is, everything that Muthesius and other representatives of the Werkbund had been calling for almost twenty years earlier—became the guiding principle. Under the slogan “Art and technology, a new unity," an "instrumentalist terminology"63 rolls through all of the formulations of goals for the Bauhaus. Both the artistic and the social synthesis remained the true goal as before. It consisted of a comprehensive

plan for transforming society that Gropius would later term “optical culture.". . . Optical culture is presented as a process and the results of a goal-oriented practice that always makes use of the most advanced technologies and technical means. Optical culture is supposed to penetrate into the design vacuum of a secularized, "conflicted community" that has lost the designs that give it unity.64

The Gropius era ended in the spring of 1928. Gropius, who had worn himself out with organizational activities and who remained the focus of conservative criticism even in Dessau, chose to work as a freelance architect in Berlin. With the departure of the man who had given meaning and direction to the Bauhaus for nine years, and the simultaneous departure of Herbert Bayer, Marcel Breuer, and László Moholy-Nagy (Georg Muche had, as noted, already left in 1927), the Bauhaus suffered a substantial loss that, from the perspective of group psychology, doubtless marks the beginning of a gradual process of disintegration.

## The Phase of Disintegration (1928-1933)

On 1 April 1928 the Swiss architect Hannes Meyer (1889–1954) became Gropius's successor. A committed socialist adverse to any form of aestheticism, he presented his views on the theme "Bauhaus and society" with all possible vigor:

Building and designing are one, and they are a social activity. As a "School for Design" the Dessau Bauhaus is not an artistic phenomenon but a social one. As designers, our activity is determined by society, and the circle of our tasks is fixed by society. Does not society in Germany today require thousands of schools, gardens, and houses for the people? Hundreds of thousands of apartments for the people? Millions of pieces of furniture for the people? . . . As designers we are the servants of this community of people. Our activity is to serve the people. . . . We do not see a Bauhaus style or a Bauhaus fashion. No fashionably flat surface ornament divided horizontally and vertically and filled up with neoplasticism. . . . We despise every form that prostitutes itself as a formula. Thus the ultimate goal of all work at the Bauhaus is to gather together all the forces that contribute to life in a harmonious design for our society. . . . Today through our art we long only for knowledge of a new objective order, fit for all, the manifesto and mediator of a collective society. . . . The new theory of the Bauhaus is an epistemological theory of existence. As a theory of design, it is the Song of Songs of harmony. As a social theory, it is a strategy to balance cooperative forces and individual forces within the living community of a people. . . .65

These programmatic sentences make it clear that under Meyer's direction the Bauhaus had broken once and for all with the original idea of a unified art school and that the idea of a production site for satisfying social needs was now dominant. The architecture department now became the heart of the Bauhaus: not in the integrating sense intended in the founding manifesto but as a largely autonomous special department that was separated from the other departments (with the exception of the workshop for interior furnishings). This resulted in yet another decrease in the influence of the painters and, as bourgeois critics have emphasized, a lost of spiritual quality. The establishment of "free painting classes," where no attempt was made to integrate them with other work at the Bauhaus, can surely be seen as a step backward with regard to Gropius's guiding principle of a new unity of art and technology. If we rub this history against the grain, however, and climb down from the high horse of idealistic fantasies, it is impossible to escape the conclusion that this "new unity" ultimately had to remain unfulfilled. Although the artists in the early phase of the Bauhaus did indeed have lasting effects on the sphere of applied design, even under Gropius these influences had begun to decline with the institute's increasingfocus on industry and the American spirit. By transforming the Bauhaus into a modern architectural academy with several associated design departments—one can hardly speak of an“art school of modernism" any longer—Hannes Meyer was merely following the logical consequencesof this development, in which the earlier connections between fine arts and applied design ceased to exist as institutions as well. Under these circumstances, the symptoms of disintegration necessarily followed. Schlemmer left the Bauhaus in1929,Klee in 1931, and Kandinsky became Hannes Meyer's main opponent. Nevertheless, it must be said that between the years 1928 and 1930 the Bauhaus worked with unsurpassed efficiency from the point of view of production and economy.

In 1930, for political reasons, Hannes Meyer was replacedby Ludwig Mies van der Rohe (1886–1969) as director of the Bauhaus. Mies had already proven himself an outstanding architect of the time. Like Gropius, he had worked as an assistant to Peter Behrens, from 1908 to 1911. After the war he designedglass architecture in Berlin that pointed the way to the future, though they could not be realized (office building on Friedrichstraße, 1921; glass skyscraper, 1922). He became famous for directing the planning and construction of the Werkbund housing colony in Stuttgart, the famous Weißenhofsiedlung, which was built in 1927 with the participation of an international group of architects, and for the German Pavilion at the 1929 World's Fair in Barcelona, a prototype of modern architecture for exhibitions. Mies van der Rohe vigorously limited “Meyer's call for social efficiency with his unconditional concept of quality."66 Nevertheless, Mies remained true to the course set by Hannes Meyer in that, even under his leadership, the Bauhaus retained the features of an architectural academy with several courses for design, two classes for fine arts painting, and one class for photography. The last-named was established in 1929 and was led by Walter Peterhans (1897–1960).67 In contrast to the Meyer era, however, the "production by the workshops for industry was drastically reduced in favor of the teaching program"68 during the Mies van der Rohe era.

With the defeat of the Social Democrats in the local elections in Dessau in 1932, the Bauhaus had to find a new location yet again. Berlin-Steglitz was chosen, and there the work was continued as a private institute in an old factory building, with more limited space and materials and a dramatically reduced teaching staff. The following year, however, the National Socialists put an end once and for all to the Bauhaus, which it had defamed as cultural bolshevism and Communist: repression from the police, the SA, and the Gestapo forced the Bauhaus to close its doors on 20 July 1933.69
