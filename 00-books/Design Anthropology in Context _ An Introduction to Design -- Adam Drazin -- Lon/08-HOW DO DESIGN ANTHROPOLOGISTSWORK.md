# HOW DO DESIGN ANTHROPOLOGISTS WORK?

It is 2007. I accompany a woman in County West Meath, Ireland, as she is brought to a hospital appointment. Working for Intel Digital Health Group, I am particularly interested in finding out about all of the informational aspects of the process. The car ride has been arranged by the West Meath Rural Transport with a volunteer driver, Sarah. Sarah takes her volunteering seriously and even drove part of the route the day before, just to check where the house is. The county has a labyrinthine network of lanes, which is one reason people really need the rural transport drivers. Also, since this is a hospital appointment, the lift has to be 100% reliable and on time. Róisín emerges very smartly dressed when we arrive, everything prepared. As we drive, I have the opportunity to sit in the back of the car and ask her about her life and experience. She is very positive about everything. She talks about the strength of the local community and how everybody looks out for everybody else. I ask about how she might prefer to arrange such a thing as a hospital visit, which can be quite private. Would she entrust the details of the visit to the driver, to a taxi driver, to her neighbours, to her children and family? It is a great system, she asserts, and while she would not want to impose upon anybody, she would be fine with any of these people being involved. It seems like all one big, happy community.

All of this information is potentially very useful for working out questions of ‘how’ to design an informational system to assist people like Róisín. Yet as an anthropologist, my interest is in Róisín and Sarah first and design work second. Much as I respect what they tell me, and understand the truth of it, I also know that social and community life is not necessarily idyllic. The very reason I am here is because people have difficulties in life. Róisín has talked to me a bit about being widowed, living in the countryside, never learning to drive and her family. She has not talked to me about the details of the hospital appointment, but during the day, I cannot help but speculate about possible medical conditions, becoming aware of which clinic we are attending, of her mobility issues and of potentially embarrassing bodily issues which remain unmentioned as we drive. Róisín’s social idyll is not the only truth of this community but is also about her connection to the place where she lives, and what is required for her to continue to live here. I infer a determination, motivation, strength, sense of capacity and a social skill behind her words, by which she is able to live as she wishes to. My understandings emerge within a corporate research framework, by which I am able to spend a good half day or day with informants like Róisín, more than feels natural, but I am based in the area for only a week. In some academic projects, one can spend months in a community, and feel one’s way to a social accommodation through many shorter encounters and interviews. Nonetheless, as an ethnographer, I am still mainly working to ‘fit into’ a community, an effort which produces social life as a puzzle in itself, and so by the end of the visit, I have produced more questions than I began with.

Some months later, I return to West Meath. At Intel, we have been exploring all kinds of possible design spaces, and then possible ways to help people in rural areas, by combining informational and mobility resources into design proposals. At first, the team is driven by wanting to design well and to justify the value of our research within the company’s budgeting structures, but as we become familiar with the lives of all the people the ethnographers have met, it becomes more personal. The team feels it is working on behalf of people like Róisín who have helped us. The work is not only about the technicalities of country living, but personal reticence, politeness, social access to information and designs which will ‘make sense’ to people in rural Ireland. Intel’s offices are a world apart from the country lanes and one-storey houses where the designs may work. Intel’s ‘Fabs’ are architecturally similar across the world and numbered globally according to the date of establishment, so Leixlip in Ireland is the site of Fab 10, Fab 14 and Fab 24. Here we work in air-conditioned offices with unusually white walls. Our connection to the field sites is through materialisations of ethnographic information: photographs, maps, quotations and video clips. We deliberately cover the walls and desks with these images and scrutinise them like crystal balls through which we can see the Irish countryside around us.

Returning to West Meath, I now have several ‘mocked up’ design concepts, on a laptop and on paper, which we produced in the labs. Some demonstrations of concepts, or ‘provotypes’, are inspired by actual maps, places and personal profiles from the fieldwork. In other words, the physical form and look of some provotypes looks ethnographic. But they are not ethnographic data; they present imaginary situations and are intended to explore social possibilities. So when I first returned from the field to Intel, I narrated my journey with Róisín to hospital and brought my colleagues through an actual journey. I now return to the field from Intel, so as to bring informants through imaginary journeys in their area. On the face of it, it is very similar, but the ‘factual’ referents of the narrative, in other words the significance of what is read ‘into’ it, have shifted.

In this return visit, I meet various women who use the rural transport busses and cars (a majority of passengers are women). I do not visit their homes this time, but we meet in a more anonymous community hall. In several conversations, I feel there is a watershed moment, a moment of realisation. They have come to me expecting the same as last time, that they will meet somebody who is ‘researching’ the rural transport. They are prepared to mainly report everything that is good, and talk about themselves and their lives. Instead, I present them with an idea which, perhaps, might actually transform their lives. One woman looks long and hard at me, as she realises that I am serious, and that Intel is capable of changing her life, perhaps for the better. The idyll of community life is now presented as more conditional, emotional and stressful. For example, the concepts I show might be about ‘arranging a hospital visit’ (with accompany transport), or might be ‘arranging a lift’ (which happens to be to the hospital); the interfaces could be located at home, in a community centre, in a transport hub or at the hospital; they could be used by yourself, by your son or daughter, by the rural transport organisation or by the district nurse. Depending on how this is handled, different people will know you are going to the hospital. Any particular design will also institute a regime of responsibility of one person for another: so relationships with care professionals, with neighbours and community, or with family, may become primary-care relationships. While previously we established that neighbours, community and family are all willing to help, now we find that a woman may prefer not to engage with community or family for these kinds of sensitive journeys. The designs could transform lives and make people much more independent, but these need very sensitive handling. The importance of design work such as this is not only in its function for medical and transport purposes but also how it builds social relationships and is an important resource for construction of a feeling for, knowledge of and assurance of society and community.

What has changed between my first visit and my second, in terms of the anthropological knowledge work?

Answering that question is about what we think constitutes design anthropology, and how it may differ from other areas of anthropology and from design. Answering it is actually going to take the remainder of this chapter, because it has several levels, and different authors take very different positions on this change. Between my two visits, the conversation shifts and the kind of information changes. This happens on a wider canvas as the relationship changes, which means that this vignette becomes placed within a wider web, a potentially global knowledge economy, which is politicised and morally inflected.

This chapter continues by first discussing the problems and opportunities of design anthropology situations. I then present some of the ways anthropologists and design researchers have risen to the challenges – unfortunately, I cannot present every point of view, but I do show a spread of work. What these thinkers address is really the nature of the coalescence of a knowledge operation, a mode of thinking work which design anthropology does, and how those operations happen within an often corporate or institutional knowledge economy where thinking work happens collaboratively and collectively (this echoes the questions provoked by Wasson 2002 and her ‘bow tie’ model of design and anthropology). In my work, I favour Suchman’s (2011) proposals for ‘frictional’ ethnographies of design’s operations and potentials, which can be productive both for sociocultural interpretation and for design work. I conclude by summarising some of the choices a design anthropologist faces. By working in a spatial way, using movement between contexts to think with, a design anthropologist is able to imbue a sense of trajectory and intentionality into designs. This involves appreciating the social space between anthropologist, designer and community of use and building different kinds of anthropological relationships (such as participation or collaboration). By making use of the spatialised materiality of anthropological work, design anthropologists can in effect produce designs with the qualities of social fluxes, as discussed in the previous chapter, which have a sense of trajectory, alterity, ‘for’ness and social rhythm.

## Design anthropology has problems

My story above of how we tried to move from observing Róisín’s journey towards possible designs illustrates several reasons why anthropology and design should be mutually suspicious of one another. These include issues of methodology, fidelity, ‘having designs’ to change informants’ lives and values.

The most profound problem with design anthropology is very basic, which is that having designs over field sites runs against anthropological methods per se, at least anthropology as it has been practised so far. The most profound cultural understandings have tended to emerge from the work of researchers who really try to participate in the lives of communities and respect and appreciate life as it is being lived currently. In order to learn, an anthropologist does not propose that life can be better, rather they first of all listen and experience. They are prepared to change themselves and to change their original questions in favour of questions which are closer to what informants would ask instead. A huge amount of work goes into building hypotheses in advance of fieldwork, into developing ways of approaching these methodologically in the field, and then a huge amount of emotional work into relinquishing these cherished hypotheses, working out how and why they are not quite right and being prepared to leave them behind and learn from that. Acts of surrender can be moments of learning. In academic anthropology departments, ethnographers who undertake doctoral research are routinely changed by the experience, beyond simply learning new languages (which can take a year or more), eating differently or catching illnesses. They can be left with a sense of sacrifice as well as reward. After their fieldwork, PhD researchers talk differently, behave differently, think differently and often relate to other people differently, while retaining their previous lives as well. Ethnographers who genuinely respect their informants may confront difficult personal issues such as whether they can sincerely undergo a religious conversion, can or should get married or have personal relationships, and whether they should invite informants back home. Not everybody experiences such questions, but they are common. Hence, to ‘have designs’ to change informants’ lives, or to have the notion that one knows better than them, is methodologically problematic and can obstruct the work of learning. Fieldwork commonly exposes the paper curtain between personal and professional life and challenges the institutional structures which support so much design and research work. Clearly, in my vignette above, in my second field visit, I was very openly and deliberately identifying myself as engaged in a project to change my informants’ lives; but in my first visit, my own conduct was more participatory.

Related to the problem of having designs is the question of fidelity. As I have described, I have myself deliberately worked to adapt and change ethnographic information. While the first stage is always to try to interpret and understand the information, this is followed by a reimagination intended to expose where opportunities, difficulties and design possibilities lie and propose new approaches. To what extent has this transformation betrayed either the context of the research or risked fidelity to the people involved?

There is power in fidelity to material context. Let me draw an example from exhibition design in archaeology. One of the most successful heritage centres in the United Kingdom is Jorvik Viking Centre in York, where a reconstruction of Viking life has been erected on the site of the original excavation. Visitors go in small trains which bring them through the muddy archaeological site, followed by a reconstruction of the houses, complete with smells, mannequins of people and recorded voices speaking Old Norse. Millions of people visit the centre. In 2004, Peter Addyman, the archaeologist who was closely engaged in guiding this exhibition’s design and its phenomenal success, as well as in the academic contributions the site made, gave a talk in Dublin at MIT Medialab Europe. It just so happens that in Dublin a Viking archaeological site was excavated in the 1970s at least as rich as York’s, probably more so. Dublin also has a reconstruction of the site at a museum called Dublinia. Why then, Addyman asked, was Dublinia attracting one or two hundred thousand people a year, while Jorvik was attracting around two million? For Addyman, the most important difference was fidelity to context. In York, the centre is on the site itself, with the museum directly on the archaeology. In Dublin, the new City Council building was constructed on top of the archaeology (in spite of large public protests), and the museum was built a couple of hundred metres away, just up the hill. This 200 m makes all the difference because it has broken a connection. It may have lost Dublin many millions in tourist revenue. What such a break with context can do is expose a different truth about the intentions of the people designing such a heritage centre. In York, it seems as though the centre is actually an exercise in engaging with, and preserving, the material remains and the lives they are testament to. In Dublin, the very genuine intentions of the heritage centre designers to connect people with the past still lack the conviction which the original context would convey. This is one example of the social and economic power of a sense of contextual fidelity.

Movement can create problems, and ethnography involves a potentially motile reconstruction of social life. When I work with information about actual places and people to create imaginary design spaces, and then present these back, it creates a situation where there is the possibility of a sense of betrayal. And the more ‘real’ they strive to be, the more falsified they may seem. The visibility of the transformations exercised is crucial, because it is in such transformations that people are able to evaluate whether the underlying motivations and intentions of design studios resonate with the motivations of communities. Just as the design anthropologist conceptualises local ‘designs’ in ethnographic work, local communities likewise experience design prototypes and presentations in terms of the thoughts, intentions and designs of the design anthropologist and their project. Hence, design demonstrations, imaginations and provotypes are not only an index of potential change in a site, but also an index of how the thinking of a design project may have changed.

The potential clash of rationalities in design anthropology means that one of the key outputs of the work is the preparedness of a company or design studio to change their own thinking. This is one of the points made by David Mosse, who has extensive experience of working in development:

Project failure is not the failure to turn designs into reality, but a certain disarticulation between practices and their rationalizing models. Failure, as I discovered, is a failure of interpretation.

(Mosse 2006: 940)

Related to all of those problems (design agendas, fidelity and the potential clash of rationalities) are questions of values (i.e. monies and moralities). This is a systemic and fuzzy issue, not a black-and-white one, and my example of the Irish Rural Transport work illustrates this. In work like that, no individual is necessarily directly profiting from the information given. The research, and the researchers’ salaries, are being paid for by the manufacturing of silicon processors and platforms. There is no immediate financial benefit. Any products and services will happen at some point down the line in the future. Those services are likely to not take the form of commodities but be embedded within transport and health services which are partly in public ownership. So the question of the commercialisation of anthropological work is not simple and obvious but rather concerns how the research occupies a place in a much wider network of exchanges, many of which remain yet opaque and obscure to the people involved, such as myself.

Design anthropology work then can be problematic not only because of specific commercialised transactions but because it embeds people within wider systemic economic relations. An individual project could be criticised for commercialising services based on ethnographic information; but likewise it could be criticised if it never made use of the work. And the work can alternatively be celebrated for either reason. So the issue is really about the kinds of monetised relationships which have been built, and the kinds of value transformations. The ethnographic information and knowledge involved is subject to notions of property relations and has become owned. It is a deliberate exercise in learning about what is really important to people, what they value, and finding ways in which other people are able to make a living based on these, with a range of business and value models in mind.

Because it creates relationships which did not exist before, involvement in design changes both informant and anthropologist. Not only does the work change social conditions around people and for people, but it also changes who these people are. After a project has collected information, informants have been bound into wider social connections, their lives have been explicitly rendered as projects, and their lives have become valuable. Similar transformations happen to anthropologists themselves, although some social researchers maintain the idea that they are unchanged. An anthropologist’s skills, knowledge and ways of working, bodies and minds, have a value and exist within value-charged networks. For example, an anthropologist who works at a university is generally understood as an individual, while the one who works for a company is often understood as an extension of the rationalities, practices and knowledge of that company. In such ways, through design work, projects infuse people with multiple ways of being, which are larger than themselves as individuals.

Design anthropology then is fundamentally problematic, more so than traditional anthropological work. It can be challenged in terms of its fidelity, its embeddedness in commercial relationships, its contextuality and most importantly in the way it has longer term designs over the worlds of informants.

And yet design anthropology also has some advantages, because it can also offer the opportunity to respond to these same areas and draw on them for intellectual gain. In the Irish Rural Transport, it was the design work more than the early ethnographic work which built a sense of commitment to informants’ lives. Fidelity is partly an issue because design anthropology makes a genuine commitment. Although traditional anthropological products are representational and separated from social sites, design anthropological products commit to return and fit back into those ever-changing sites. The necessity of taking values and money seriously mean that one must advocate value models which are realistic and often challenge the profit motive in product and service development. When it comes to having designs, good design anthropology work attempts to connect with peoples’ own notions of possible change. In design work which is anthropological, peoples’ own culture comes to be in some sense visionary, and productive of change within the world, rather than change happening to people. Working in this field brings home to any social scientist that culture intrinsically happens in time, and to work with culture is to work with time, change and stasis.

The movement from ethnographic field site, to design studio and back again, and perhaps repeatedly, is, therefore, anthropologically productive in a range of ways. It is generally a hugely optimistic field, over-burdened with the promises of innovation, well-being, profit, usefulness, community, fashion and modernity, but it is through our response to the problems which emerge that a lot of the contributions are made. The work produces responsibility and relatedness between people who do not know one another, at the same time as it produces ideas, possibilities, proposals and solutions.

Because of all of these dimensions, the work can unbalance anthropological thinking. Design anthropology is not doing design per se (although it can draw on design methodologies), but very much about making explicit the difficulties and problems in a site which design may address, and by doing so producing an interpretive account of culture which is tension-filled and unstable. The material stuff of ethnography, the data of images, recordings, notes and so forth are both representational of a site but also anchor professional relationships to the space of possibility and provide connections in perpetuity to the lives of informants.

I now address the kinds of work which design anthropology does, to illustrate two aspects. First, I set out some of the ways that social scientists and design researchers have characterised the knowledge work of design anthropology, and of the problems they wish to address. The thrust of this argument is that, by developing certain kinds of practices and processes, design anthropology makes knowledge in unique ways which are not traditionally anthropology nor design. Second, and more importantly for anthropologists such as myself, these varied ways of think-working happen within economically and politically charged relationships, and happen because of those relationships. So the chapter proceeds to examine how people consider the nature of work ‘within’ the design relationships which have come to the fore in globalised society, in conditions of inequality. The work of thinking and relating become mutual in design anthropology and happens materially in space.

## Forms of thinking work

So, what sorts of thinking work do design anthropologists see themselves as engaging in? Design anthropologists not only deliver information from one place to another, but think about society and culture in material ways using movement across distance and across the boundaries between design studio and field site. Thrift (2006: 139) refers to this kind of thinking as part of a ‘spatial turn’ in social science, where material intellectual operations happen in space and also form space. Some people think that design anthropology arose because designers, engineers and computer scientists needed a certain form of research, but it is not so simple. Frankly, engineers and designers barely knew what it was until some anthropologists decided to push themselves forwards into design spaces. I argue instead that the various modes of thinking work practised by design anthropologists have arisen from certain intellectual currents in the discipline.

Anthropologists indulge in all kinds of thinking. Sometimes they are working quite directly with data, describing, representing, discovering, mapping or demonstrating. You may find anthropologists who are explaining, explicating, deducting, analysing, reflecting or modelling, while others are interpreting, translating, contextualising, comparing ‘cross-culturally’, locating or contrasting. There are rather more violent tendencies among people who are deconstructing, questioning, attacking, unpacking, critiquing, problematising or indulging in a bit of logical abduction. Conversely, lovers of order can do some justifying and legitimising. In a more designoriented intellectual world, some anthropologists are also imagining, creating, inducting, proposing, hypothesising and constructing. Further layers of complication are added by people who see themselves as a part of a sequence of thought operations and talk about relocating, representing or reconstructing. All of these kinds of thinking work happen not individually but within a wider political economy of conceptual knowledge, and so anthropologists are developing ways of describing and distinguishing the relational paradigms they work within: they are participating, collaborating or making, each of which frames relationships around the thinking work differently.

In considering How to do design anthropology, different anthropologists and design researchers over the years advocate different combinations of thinking work, ways of relating, and work practices. For much of the 20th century, anthropologists saw themselves as studying cultures which existed as objective things. This was a part of the foundation of social science itself. Emile Durkheim worked with ‘social facts’, meaning social principles which one can apply in different places and times. In a comparable vein, the kind of ethnography later advocated by the Chicago School asserted that the subject of study exists ‘out there’ as a social reality to be observed and learned. But anthropologists are not only observers but also participants, who learn by subjectively experiencing life (Malinowski 2014 [1922]). From the

1960s, American anthropologists such as Turner and Geertz advocated approaches which emphasised the anthropologist’s own experience. Geertz’s interpretivism saw the anthropologist as an interpreter, translating across culturally constituted boundaries in ways that others can understand. The idea of interpretivism led to a re-invigoration of the craft of writing in anthropology, emphasising the work of ‘thick description’. Dense descriptions combine observation and personal reflection with an idea of what audience one is writing for, and ground ideas in a visible work process. They use metaphor and rhetoric as a way to help explain. Thick description can be good at the work of association of ideas (which I return to in Chapter 5) and has become fundamental to a lot of anthropological work. After fieldwork, a phase of descriptive work helps organise information into data and becomes a bridge between fieldwork and implications. My spellchecker insists on changing the term to ‘think description’, and in this instance, my spellchecker is not wrong. It is this protracted bridging work which is crucial to many areas of anthropology, including design anthropology, so as to examine the route from observation and experience to such things as concepts and design spaces.

From the 1980s to the visible emergence of design anthropology in the 2000s, many developments shook up conceptions of anthropological thinking work. Three are particularly worth mentioning because they anticipate design anthropology. The Writing Culture critique (so named after Clifford and Marcus 1986) synthesised postmodernist ideas about the implications of anthropological work comprising in largely textual representations, but without a substantial claim to being ‘true’ accounts. As well as drawing close attention to the importance of what sort of representation one might use (textual, photographic and filmic), this critique highlighted the importance of local accounts of culture from within communities. Challenging the previously privileged viewpoint of the outsider, who engages in a steep cultural learning curve, new anthropological work began to see itself as much more collaborative in nature, with different kinds of cultural experts with different perspectives.

Some anthropologists at this time very deliberately presented the work as happening within a politics of knowledge. Keesing (1987) discussed the hierarchies and cultural experts with whom he worked among the Kwaio communities of the Solomon Islands. A lot of his anthropological research involved attempting to build relationships with the respected authority figures to whom people would look for spiritual knowledge, healing, and to learn about what the world comprised of (cosmology). Kwaio society involved many accounts and understandings of the world, but these are neither locally considered wholly equal accounts nor are they equally accessible. Knowledge is both distributed and controlled. Some accounts might matter more than others, and to gain access to information meant commitment and time, if it were possible at all. He described working within this politics of knowledge as an ‘interpretive quest’. As post-modernist ideas gained mainstream currency, different anthropologists took different approaches on the nature and validity of collaborative knowledge production, some aspiring to be able to work ‘with’ informants, while others took a very subjective position in which anthropological accounts must remain at root representations of their own devising.

At the same time as the writing culture critique, the material culture critique was developing (Appadurai 1986; Miller 1987), which had roots not only in anthropology but archaeology, design history (Forty 1986) and museum studies. The intellectual currents of the time had generally problematised abstract ideas such as ‘culture’ or ‘society’, revealing them to be things which masqueraded as observable phenomena (in which one observes culture ‘out there’), but which in fact are explanatory and conceptual ways of accounting for the world. A social researcher who embarks on observing a cultural thing, for example, ‘work’, or ‘family’ or ‘belief’, risks tautology, by trying to observe their own explanation. A certain practice may or may not be understood as ‘work’. Material culture anthropologists’ reaction to this difficulty, unlike some others, retained the idea that there are phenomena in the world which one can work to observe and describe and then collaboratively build multiple accounts or interpretations of how it is experienced. This is one implication of Appadurai’s (1986) call to ‘follow the things themselves’. Material culture sought to bring objects and materials into the remit of human culture, and not depend upon more abstract phenomena such as knowledge, representations, texts and meaning.

The crisis of representation and the rise of material culture were accompanied by a proliferating interest in globalisation, both as a topic and as a transformation of the discipline. Marcus (1995) drew attention to the ways in which one could no longer simply do a study of a certain site as an integral context. ‘Multi-sited ethnography’ involves a range of ways ‘to examine the circulation of cultural meanings, objects and identities in diffuse time-space’ (Marcus 1995: 96). Among other things, this multi-sited mode of research changes the nature of field sites and of research subjects. Sites no longer can be seen as distinct cultural contexts, so one is not engaged in straightforward cross-cultural comparison (e.g. if one were working on smartphones, what ‘smart’ means in a home as against what it means in a lab). The relation between such sites comes more into focus – the flows of objects, narratives, people, metaphors or other connections. The people whom one deals with also become fragmented, playing down the notion of individual persons and instead bringing out multiple cultural phenomena, knowledge, routines and practices which can cross-cut across individuals.

Some argue that there is in multi-sited ethnography an intrinsic loss of innocence, and a disenchantment, as anthropologists and informants become activists. The loss of the ‘subaltern’ (Haraway 1997) implies that everybody seems to have a privileged point of view, without subordinate participants. There comes, therefore, to be more emphasis on how people and communities can ‘map themselves’ – that is, not only communicate or explain their own perspective but adopt a position where they are equipped to produce a new perspective (or ‘map’) on their own situation. One can argue that everybody is a potential expert in globalisation, and with the digital revolution, a potential anthropologist and a potential designer.

This is a radical oversimplification of the arguments of some heavyweight anthropologists, and for some readers my potted history will show how I am merely standing on the toes of giants. What it means is that when a range of people from 2002 began to identify as design anthropologists, that was not only because of the requirements of design research and because of global social transformations, as we saw in Chapter 1, but also sprang from intellectual developments and debates in the anthropological discipline. Design anthropology draws directly on the death of faith in social facticity transcending time and space; it draws on the social economies of knowledge by which we work with vernacular and professional designers; it takes on board the implications of materialities as knowledge; and it is one way to navigate the daunting culturally open oceans of globalisation.

## What different design anthropologists and ethnographers do

A form of material engagement with a critical angle using ethnography. (Wendy Gunn, conference presentation 2010).

Let us first dispose of the idea that design anthropology is only about providing facts about social life. This does seem to happen in many projects, and in a lot of user experience research and market research. But they are not ‘facts’. The ‘requirements’ which are provided to engineers are cultural creations, and many people only do the work as an aside so they can take the money and go off and do something more interesting. That work of producing apparent facts requires social research skill, and the capacity to convince, but is not anthropological.

Where design ethnography gets most scientific and can be honest about it is in ethnomethodological work. This has a long and highly productive tradition, and many technologies around us, the interfaces we use and the sounds we hear when we close a car door or touch a smartscreen, have been developed with these techniques (Crabtree et al 2012; Suchman 1987). The ethnomethodologist will often work at two levels: first to observe and describe work processes and practices and second to find ways to elicit the ways in which people understand, evaluate and ascribe meaning to their actions. In a sense, it provides a record of work and accompanying sense-making. It is not typical of wider ethnographic practice, but quite a specific way of watching people more than participating in their social worlds.

Ethnography is a tool that we can use to unpack members’ mastery of practical sociology in empirical detail. In doing so it makes visible and available the distinctive activities that populate particular settings, the work involved in doing them, and the work practices that members use to concert their actions with those of other parties implicated in the conduct of a setting’s work. Uncovering and recognising the ways in which members concert or coordinate their actions with one another is what practical sociology is particularly concerned with. It is what you and I are concerned with when we walk down the street, buy goods, drive home etc., even if we don’t give it much more than a passing thought, because it enables us to avoid bumping into one another, to determine who is next in line, and to get home without killing anybody…

… Ethnography in design is a very different enterprise from design in social science: the accountabilities are different, the needs different, the results and their uses different.

(Crabtree et al 2012: 2–3)

In ethnomethodology, the world becomes a puzzle. Just the simple way that things are, and work together, needs explaining. This kind of critical angle often works through the way in which the methods constantly counterpoise a sense of the holistic against a sense of the particular, a very compartmentalised and ‘bitty’ social world set against grander sense-making. Ethnomethodologically interrogated worlds often appear as chains of small incidents and situations, which are only interlinked in chains of peoples’ experience. This is partly because the approach is often deployed to explore workflows and practices. It is very good for the examination of action and prioritises praxis as a locus of cultural creation, before discourse and materiality.

A good example is Suchman’s (2000) work on the informational dimensions of a legal office, and how different legal professionals worked together to compile information for case work and meetings. It is not an example which is ‘only’ ethnomethodological, which would be more delimited, but it does illustrate the virtues of what the approach can do. More junior and less well-paid people in the legal office would put together packages of information which could be relevant, in large batches of documents (past cases, evidence, etc.). More qualified lawyers might sort through these sets of information and select items of greater relevance to this particular case. Finally, the most senior lawyers, who would probably be handling the case, would sort and arrange these files of information in ways that would actually ‘make the case’ concerned. So the final compilation would reflect the course of action planned in this instance. Having recorded and described these kinds of chains of informational work, Suchman also worked with individual lawyers to explore how different people made sense of their work at particular moments. More senior people described what the junior people were doing as ‘objective’ work, simply functional but employing less personal judgement, and senior lawyers represented themselves as building more ‘subjective’ information, using their own judgement. This informational transformation from ‘facts’ to ‘ideas’ or ‘agendas’ is quite common. However, more junior people also might present themselves as engaged in subjective work, exercising a lot of judgement based on the experience they had.

By observing, recording and describing what is going on, and by accessing in different ways how people engage in making sense of what they do, Suchman both builds an understanding of what is going at certain moments, and also a much grander sense of the world of the legal office, the spectrum of information; criteria of evaluation; the material constitution of relationships; what motivates actions; how different people view one another and how the people, practices, and things are seen to fit within grander visions. In the hands of an experienced researcher, the possibility of critical angles often emerges in the gaps between the holistic representations and the ‘bitty’ situations.

Although it can be highly socially creative, this form of work is often mistaken as being very factual. Crabtree (above) talks about ‘uncovering’, hence working to get at pre-existent information. Many of the meanings are developed collaboratively with informants, getting people to reflect on situations and express the thoughts which are otherwise silent, but which exist. This impression of factual authority is presumably one of the reasons why these methods, developed for decades in HCI (human–computer interaction), have had such success in developing technologies and interfaces. Dourish (2006: 1), however, points out that many designers have simply taken this kind of work as fact and cherry-picked what they wish, when the work challenges design to innovate even more: ‘… the presence or absence of explicitly earmarked “implications for design” is not the best evaluative criterion for the relevance, utility, or quality of an ethnographic contribution’. Dourish believes this work can be more of a constructive conversation than a set of findings to be transacted.

Ethnography goes ‘Boink!’

(Arnould 2002: 214)

Design ethnographic knowledge is not only about accurate observation, because understanding and interpretation also happen collectively. Ethnography is presented and shared, so that groups can use it to ‘think with’. When Eric Arnould compares design ethnography to his favourite cartoon (Calvin and Hobbes) by saying it goes ‘Boink’, he is drawing attention to how we do not have to be in thrall to social facts. Ethnography is not only observation and experience, but also it comprises in presentational artefacts, narratives, slides, images, and videos. As well as observation, ethnographic work consists also in drawing people into the worlds it evokes. This capacity for attraction and engagement is crucial for facilitating thinking work which is collective, not only individual and which is supposed to have an impact (hopefully a surprising one). Some innovation starts in presenting observations. Madsbjerg and Rasmussen (2014) argue that ethnographic work can help one achieve a perceptual paradigm shift for many companies, especially when reflecting on what their product or service is actually about. Ethnographic information comes to be ‘sticky’, immersive and engaging – and in this very simple new associative dimension, becomes relevant.

In a digital world, some assume that the various kinds of thought and information (facts, ideas and concepts) move as if frictionlessly between people and objects, things and materials. Evidently, the older conceptions of people as possessing thought, while material things possess neutral information, is no longer so tenable. In design anthropology work, the situation is revealed as more subtle. In order for two or more people to undertake thinking work together, the communication of information is not in itself sufficient per se, but rather modes of presentation matter because they can constitute thought in different ways which work across the fosses between one human and another.

The use of PowerPoint slides in corporate design work, much-maligned for its mind-numbing capacities, is studied by Wakeford (2006). She argues that the physical form of information and data (and PowerPoint is material) really matters for the work being done. Presentations using slides may be as artefacts informationally ‘thin’, but they enable informationally ‘thick’ performances by ethnographers. The bare slides frame the person of the ethnographer and highlight the bodies of anthropologists as the deep and rich containers of the important thoughts. The bullet-like bullet points of the slides thus acquire a sense of unexpected depth.

While design anthropologists working with computing (largely with programmers, engineers, and graphic designers) have been concerned with reformulating the idea of social research as science or as facts, that is only one small part of design. Design anthropology can also be artistic and poetic. Fulton Suri (2005: 2016) characterises her work as ‘poetic observation’, and she shows how individual observed moments, places and things can provoke design responses. In her photographic work Thoughtless Acts?, people sit on street furniture which was never intended to be sat upon, spaces come unexpectedly to be communal meeting places, walls near coffee stalls are used to dispose of empty coffee cups, door handles with interesting shapes become places to leave post or parcels, walls become messaging points and doorways can become beds. These are not intended simply as ‘problem statements’, to be solved. They are not intended to be read as images which will elicit a particular response. They are unlike the specific situations which ethnomethodology draws attention to, because they do not arise from casting a systematic net and they do not have to be explored as moments which make sense in a defined cultural schema. Rather, they are intended to demonstrate specific social moments which have happened and recreate them as sites of potential. They are in this sense non-indexical images, not supposed to be ‘read into’. Instead, different viewers (different designers) are invited to respond. One designer sees a problem, another a productive action to be supported and improved.

Observation of the world is natural and essential to design. But ultimately, it’s less what you look at that matters, it’s what you see and what you and the project make of it. From designers we ask for a designed world that has meaning beyond the resolution of purely functional needs, one that also has poetry, communicates subtly something that makes sense, not just by fitting in with the culture and environment in which it lives, but by adding a new dimension to it.

(Fulton Suri 2017)

Work such as this is often undertaken by professional designers, anthropologists not by training but through their compelling interest in people. Very often, their deep interest in humanity shines through. As poetics, this kind of design ethnography is wholly unlike Herbert Simon’s notion of means-ends design. Observation does not make the ends of design clear but is creative in itself. It has the authority of what is real, undeniably real moments, but celebrated through the creative skills of individual practitioners.

Professionals such as Fulton Suri have played a powerful role in the development of design thinking, which is certainly one dimension of design anthropology. Since the 1980s, designers have been arguing that design practice is not only about forming a proposal for a thing or a service but also a distinct way of thinking. In addition, this means that social commentary can be a kind of design work. Cross (1982, 2011; working at the same time as Schön 1982) set out several characteristics of design thinking which distinguish it from thinking in the sciences, arts or humanities. Design thinking examines the world of artificial and human-made things; it addresses loosely defined problems, is heuristic and uses various designerly ‘codes’ or languages to translate thinking between abstract and concrete. Starting from these 1980s foundations, since the turn of the millennium design thinking has exploded across the world (Brown 2009). The wide range of modes of working from design thinking have inspired a lot of anthropologists looking for ways to think about ethnographic information in groups and studio environments.

A significant recent extension to this mode of thinking makes more direct connections between design thinking and ethnography. As we saw in the previous chapter, most design processes emphasise materiality and embodied thinking. This is why Holzblatt and Beyer (2016, Chapter 10) talk about posting research information up on a wall and then ‘walking the wall’. Like design, ethnography traditionally also places great emphasis on embodied ways of learning. Roberts (2020) makes the point that the essential connection from site to studio, the vessel of learning and information, is the body. Although in contemporary commerce, data-mining and big data are sometimes considered valid alternatives to ethnography, he argues strongly that they are not as good at facilitating innovative work. We should, therefore, ‘Bring back the Bodies’ (Roberts 2020: 6) if we really want to think both innovatively and in ways which work in practice.

The spectrum of approaches running from the (social) scientist to the observing poet shows us how design anthropology orients itself in very many ways in the apparently simple act of observation. But not all anthropologists consider that ethnographic observation is necessary in design anthropology. Ingold has downplayed the role of ethnography for anthropology. Rather, the aim of the anthropologist who works in or with design is one of sociocultural understanding.

The objective of anthropology, I believe, is to seek a generous, comparative but nevertheless critical understanding of human being and knowing in the world we all inhabit. The objective of ethnography is to describe the lives of people other than ourselves, with an accuracy and sensitivity honed by detailed observation and prolonged first-hand experience. (Ingold 2008: 69)

Ingold is, among other things, concerned with unpacking the way that so many anthropologists make a distinction between nature and culture, and a corresponding distinction between scientific and artistic versions of anthropology. When we begin to think about culture as a thing apart, it makes sense to go to certain sites ‘out there’ to observe it. But cultural experience is not dependent upon ethnography, and indeed for Ingold some of the truly unexplored frontiers lie much closer to home, in our own bodies and minds, the things around us and the materials of the world. By making use of designerly and artistic modes of engaging with materials, making, sketching and modelling, one is thinking about the human condition in ways anthropologists have hitherto underestimated. Design anthropology as a form of craft-related thinking validates the intellectual contribution of non-textual thinking. ‘Let us call this philosophy of ours anthropology’ Ingold (2008: 90) argues. His approaches also lend themselves to design ideas which argue that designers can make futures, imagine and render them material (Yelavich and Adams 2014).

Ingold’s work (which, importantly, he has changed and updated over the years) invites us to consider how it is not enough to simply undertake thinking work as a form of work, but also how it happens within relationships, and the management and focussing of social experiences. When presented as a process, design anthropology can not only be scientific, fun, poetic, crafty but also can be collaborative. Some thinkers argue that design anthropology is first and foremost a kind of sociopolitical work.

Projects should use design processes and artefacts to work with groups to shift hegemonic value systems that are detrimental to the holistic well-being of vulnerable groups, dominant groups, and their extended environments.

(Tunstall 2013: 245)

There is a long tradition of design-as-politics (Fry 2010), and some design anthropology sits squarely in this space. As we have seen in previous chapters, Participatory Design traditions in Scandinavia arose from political concerns about workers’ rights but then addressed those through systems innovation. In practice, design work is experienced as integrated into political and economic conditions and is often evaluated within those frameworks. Tunstall (2013) offers a particularly clear manifesto for the design anthropologist, about why and how they should work in this way. Design anthropology should seek, she argues, to manifest the values of local communities and find ways to support that value-building. Design here is supporting other’s values, more than creating value for itself or dictating value systems (see Buchanan 1998). Second, she draws on postcolonial theory to argue that a design anthropology, which is oriented towards the interests of the communities with which it works, should unpack and destabilise the ideologies which govern their everyday lives. Recognising and making visible the role of anthropologists, designers and design institutions in shaping peoples’ lives, she considers that this has tended to happen within global structures of inequality, often infused with the narratives of gender, ethnicity and class.

While Tunstall takes a confrontational position on political engagements, others favour more dialogical positions (DiSalvo 2011; Fuad-Luke 2009). Yet there is widespread recognition of inequality within design work, which happens convergently with working to remedy it or destabilise it. This approach to studio work shows us how the institutional settings of design work, and the ‘grass roots’ sites of ethnographic work, are not separate. If they appear to be distinct, that is a result of a cultural construction which is deceptive, in point of fact the two have been bound together over the past century. Design has shaped everyday life across the world and vice-versa. Through orienting towards value systems, the hope is that we can celebrate the diversity of life and culture to create more inspiring designs. Design itself becomes a politicised

act of productive resistance, one which is more realistic about political frameworks after C20th modernism.

In recent years, these various approaches have coalesced into debates around how this kind of critical politics and anthropological thinking is instantiated in particular material ways. The very active Danish tradition in design anthropology argues that there has thus been a move from ‘ways of knowing’ to ‘styles of knowing’ (Otto and Smith 2013: 10); or in parallel work from ‘design thinking’ to ‘design thinking’ (Binder et al 2011). This is to say that a great deal hinges on the form and substance of design anthropology products. Materiality matters. But there are different accounts of exactly how it matters, which have slight disagreements about what this kind of thinking work comprises and achieves. I would gloss these as the ‘studio’ styles of knowing, as represented by Rabinow et al (2008), and the ‘recontextualising’ styles, as advocated by Lucy Suchman.

The idea of ‘studio work’ has two distinct elements here. On the one hand, studios involve non-textual work, such as drawing and making, but on the other they often imply group work. While design disciplines often deploy both modes, anthropologists often place more emphasis on one or another, and in both cases, they problematise ethnography as a core methodology. Rather than depending upon writing as the primary technique, they both create alternative sources of knowledge and understanding, and alternative ways to make knowledge explicit. An anthropologist proceeds from specific descriptions to generally relevant concepts, and in doing so develops modes of critique and scrutiny along the way. When alternatives to writing are employed, their knowledge can be scrutinised earlier and collaboratively. What this does is not only produce a different sense of ‘validity’ but also act to ‘problematise’ work. Anthropological work, through collaboration, produces debates, gaps, and possibilities, rather than coherence, and this problematisation is more useful potentially for design anthropology.

(Adjacency involves) a space of problems. Of questions. Of being behind or ahead. belated or anticipatory. Out of synch. Too fast or too slow. Reluctant. Audacious. Annoying. (Rabinow 2007: 40–1)

Rabinow’s concept of adjacency then does not directly refer to observing coexistant phenomena, but rather to coexistant concepts, often contradictory, between universalist and very localised interpretations. Adjacency emphasises the current conditions of knowledge, above the temporal unfoldings of knowledge which can seem to ‘explain’ the existence of different concepts rather than confronting their difference. It refers to the positioning of the anthropologist who is engaged in a project of an anthropology of the contemporary. For Rabinow, this need for academic problematisation comprises in the possibility of simultaneity in the contemporary moment. We do not necessarily live in a ‘resolved’ world, where past difficulties have been overcome into a coherent present which makes sense to everybody; rather, the contemporary moment often incorporates concepts, practices and cultural phenomena which coexist but which do not together make sense. Anthropology has, he thinks, failed to account for this ‘contemporary’ moment, which tends to seem self-evident, and needs to exist in ‘mid stream, always already embedded in a situation, one both settled and unsettled’ (Rabinow 2007: 8; see also Faubion 2016). Various forms of collaborative and studio work can act as alternatives to ethnography in this event, partly because they help achieve a sense of stepping ‘out’ of time and history, and reduce our general need to explain phenomena in cultural-historical terms when that is only one possible mode of

explanation.

Marcus (1998) also has recognised that looking to the design studio and design methods speaks to current difficulties with anthropological knowledge. He has consistently explored the problems with traditional ethnographic methods, but he does not relinquish ethnography per se. Rather, he reformulates ethnographic work and brings some design methodologies into its remit, often referring to ‘fieldwork’ as a range of methods wider than just ‘ethnography’. He argues that fieldwork provides a necessary pragmatic confrontation with life and materiality. In response to Rabinow’s aspiration for an idea of ‘untimeliness’ in anthropology, Marcus responds:

So, is the untimely, doing conceptual work, something that quite openly is done explicitly with reference to what used to be called ‘key informants,’ once ‘others,’ now more likely to be what I call counterparts, experts, scientists, journalists, anyone who comes to have real stakes in the work they are doing with you as anthropologists in their lives? …

… While it is a temporal term, untimeliness is what creates or imposes the space for the ethnographer in settings where that space of being or presence is otherwise hard to define and establish these days. It, along with other terms that you [Rabinow] have introduced, creates a temporality in which anthropological research can function, in which it can claim novelty, discovery, in its knowledge-making - which is one of those aesthetic dimensions of anthropology that I claim will not be denied despite all the other changes in our practices. Just presenting the ‘stereotypic anthropologist,’ as generally understood, in such settings works against the relations the anthropologist needs to be able to function in contemporary research. The anthropologist must have a different, negotiated relationship with subjects as, to a degree, her epistemic partners. To accomplish this, sameness and difference between anthropologists and subjects becomes a key realm for rethinking method, practice, theory, etc.

(Marcus in Rabinow et al 2008: 65)

This means for Marcus that a wide range of engaged design-inspired methods can be deployed as a part of social research work (e.g. Cantarella et al 2019). The work which develops is highly artistic, attempting to re-present observations from ethnographic work to establish ‘productive encounters’ with audiences. At the same time, these discussions of anthropological studios and design work have largely happened within academic circles. It is notable that few of these theorists have worked with design in commercial circles, within which the monetised and valuedriven nature of the relationships becomes much more evident and can become a burden or a resource. A great deal of the studio work undertaken at the Center for Ethnography at UCI (established by Marcus) or the Productive Encounters Studio (Cantarella et al 2019), for example, presents work in an artistic paradigm, or even within art galleries. Clearly, there is a highly artistic element of design work, especially in universities, but by far the majority of it is aimed at mass-production, or service duplication, and the key audiences for design research are in work environments and offices, not in galleries. Design anthropology, by contrast, has emerged more from people who have from an early stage in their careers worked in or with design. Some wanted to by preference, others had to make a living as anthropologists. The sense of working within the value-infused political economies of knowledge which characterise design research leads to different styles of knowing.

Lucy Suchman, after having spent the majority of her career working for Silicon Valley

companies, presents an alternative viewpoint on the idea of ‘studio’ anthropology. Her work instead emphasises the work as ‘knowledge relocations’, which create potentially constructive ‘frictions’ between different cultural sites. Ethnographic work of a more traditional kind is generally at the heart of this project:

ethnographic projects that articulate the cultural imaginaries and micropolitics that delineate design’s promises and practices.

(Suchman 2011: 3)

Suchman advocates a design anthropology which is ethnographic and which turns a (constructively) critical eye on design rather than necessarily being ‘for’ design. In some ways then, this is in favour of traditional kinds of anthropological work and approach, but the intellectual positioning of design anthropology for Suchman is particular, and often difficult. She draws on the work of Haraway (1988) and Tsing (2004) to show how design anthropological knowledge are ‘situated knowledge’, having a close relation both to location and to the perspectives of the observer. This situatedness evokes an alternative, highly reflexive design politics. Design, she points out, in the form of technological innovation, reflects a contested landscape of local and global power gradients. Places such as Silicon Valley, where Suchman worked for a long time, present themselves (to others and to themselves) as places where design happens and originates, as places where ‘futures’ happen first and as places where knowledge about the world is gathered and centralised. One of the potential pitfalls, for Suchman, is moments in organisations when change and transformation comes to be privileged for its own sake, and when organisations (perhaps inevitably) become introspective, directing their efforts to self-transformation, perhaps because the managerial classes are rewarded for management within organisations more than for their wider social engagement.

Suchman’s work then places how organisations relate to the world at the heart of design anthropological processes and appreciates that materiality negotiates this boundary. Neither the studio ‘in here’ nor the field site ‘out there’ is really the site of designerly creativity, ultimately that kind of viewpoint on sources of innovation will ignore the very real, close and personal problematics of design and of design anthropology. Instead, she has worked to appreciate the frictions, problematics, boundaries, materialities and movements and work with them. Design anthropology is a kind of critical work which takes place very much within a politicised knowledge economy of society, culture and economy. It does feed into better products, things and services, and indeed overtly in the workplace those may seem to be the driving ultimate aims of the work which are used to justify it; but nonetheless in point of fact design anthropology happens at one remove, working with, on and for design, probably in that order.

(Tsing 2004: 1–2)

The idea of knowledge relocation as advocated by Suchman has advantages. It emerges from long commercial experience of work which has played a part in developing (but not creating)

numerous designed products and services. So it is not hypothetical or an expression of an aspiration. Partly because of this, it seems conservative in some ways, adhering to a traditional anthropology based on ethnographic observation. Yet the cultural politics underlying the ideas it implies are (relatively speaking) new and progressive. The idea of friction recognises a profound cultural alterity which has parallels with third wave feminist critiques of global human cultures (Rosner 2018). Relocation places equal emphasis on the informational content of design anthropology work and the relational dimensions. Because it takes account of both field site and studio as equal but dissimilar contexts, it blurs the boundary between business meeting and art event. Although a great deal of studio anthropology leads to considered, art-like exhibitionary events, the notion of relocation leads also to business and policy-driven meetings and conversations. Intellectually speaking, these kinds of presentation are different in terms of audience and as cultural contexts, but not necessarily in academic substance. On the negative side, the political stances which relocation and the concept of cultural friction imply have not yet (at time of writing) born the fruit expected of them. They are in themselves inconclusive and partial understandings of power and economy.

## Knowledge ecosystems

There are, therefore, many ways that design anthropologists rise to the challenges and problems inherent in the work, ranging from scientific-empirical, to poetic, to presentational, to making, political activism, studio-based and frictional relocation. The range of approaches take very different stances on the empirical aspects of anthropological knowledge. For some, knowledge arises ‘out there’ through observation, and they give a very high interpretive value to the accounts of informants in a field site, and how people consciously understand their own situations. For others, the expertise of the designer, artist, engineer or programmer is privileged as a way forwards. In theory, the various approaches are not mutually exclusive. There is no logical reason why an ethnographer cannot simultaneously observe a sequence of actions (like Crabtree) and also look out for poetic serendipitous inspirations (like Fulton Suri), or why one cannot both undertake fieldwork and also undertake studio-based making or sketching (like Ingold). However, in practice, it can be difficult. Attempting two approaches simultaneously and adequately is a feat of mental juggling which is a step too far for many ethnographers. There is no logical reason why one cannot record observations in a lab experiment while simultaneously writing a poem. But few can. Likewise, only some are skilled in both ethnography and studio skills. In addition, resources are limited and conditional. A project gets a certain amount of time and resources (by which I also mean, the project staff are not fully free), so if you get a day or a week in a site, you have to guarantee results. So these various ‘styles of knowing’ in design anthropology are more like Plans A and B. They combine as parts of a wider knowledge ecosystem involving different moments, different sites, different practitioners collaborating, and different institutions, companies and disciplines.

Most approaches try to expose and clarify problems and possibilities, rather than trying to answer them. This work of problematisation emerges much more coherently when there is a clearer conception of the importance of diverse relationships becoming involved in the work, and of the importance of the knowledge artefacts involved, that is the material culture. The material artefacts are conceived in different ways by different approaches: as observable facts, as creations of anthropologists, as representations or communications, as interpretative accounts, as materialised relationships or mediators of friction. These stances on knowledge and materiality are in practice interconnected. When I evoke the metaphor of an ecosystem, it is to remind ourselves that the design anthropologist both studies culture and exists within it. That existence is as contingent and strategic as for any organism within its habitat. The design anthropologist studies culture in the way that a fish studies water. The existence of the fish, its living, comprises in ongoing actions and experiences, in swimming with or against the current, in eating and being eaten. The sense of place emerges in the process and the movement. The existence of the design anthropologist within a design knowledge ecosystem likewise is very ‘real’, it succeeds and it fails. The frictional experience of culture is both how effects are achieved but also how an understanding of culture and society emerges. Mosse, working in the anthropology of development and policy, suggests that knowledge contestations when working with collaborators are a crucial part of what this kind of work can contribute: ‘contests “after the field” may be crucial to the recovery of anthropological claims, not least because they do not reveal any stable constellation of power in social research’ (Mosse 2006: 951).

In this formulation, the recognition of the anthropologist ‘within’ the corporation (Cefkin 2009) is as important as the anthropologist within the field. Corporations are the primary way in which humanity culturally organises and understands rational action in the contemporary world.

It is important to recognise that anthropological work here involves different kinds of relationships, each productive for the anthropologist in different ways. Each design anthropologist should recognise the different kinds of knowledge-producing relationships which they experience and engage in, in each piece of work. Some newly trained anthropologists, with a major piece of ethnographic work under their belt from a Masters or PhD, seem to think that ‘participation’ is the only mode of anthropological research. That limited understanding is one of the problems which limits the scope of what their subsequent research projects can achieve.

However, it is better to recognise that some anthropological relationships do not involve participation, but other paradigms. Collaboration happens between parties who maintain different identities and have different aims. Collaborators work to understand one another, but not to become more alike to one another. ‘Making’ as an anthropological activity does not learn from personal dialogues, although they may happen at other stages, it stimulates a more personal embodied conversation with oneself. Figure 4.1 attempts to break open the idea of one paradigm. On the one side, the anthropologist may work to participate in a field site, and on the other to collaborate with design studios. In participation, one works to change oneself to become more similar to others. In collaboration, one works to maintain difference, and a separate, parallel existence. These paradigms are not wholly distinct necessarily, because in the middle, one has many experts and authority figures, local and designerly, who may not sit easily into either a professional community or a site of study. The relationship with designers can be thought about as a form of lateral ethnography. While typical fieldwork involves a sense of depth and immersion, collaborative work evokes a sense of adjacency.

It is also important that the design anthropologist recognises that they are not the only means by which field sites and professional communities are related. The work always involves more direct relationships. Companies constantly provide services and products and shape peoples’ lives in advance of the work; and afterwards, if a project has been productive, its success or failure is measured in how that relation of field site and institution has been reconstituted, conceptually and materially. That relationship of design studio with field site is the one which Tunstall argues needs to be decolonised, within which a sense of value needs to be maximised, a sense of appropriate change given and a sense of productive friction engaged. It is a relationship which continues with or without the anthropologist. At times during the work, an anthropologist may take a stance where they ‘stand apart’ and view professional and local communities as one social phenomenon, while at other times, they may take a stance where only the field site, or only studio work, are within their scope of focus.

Ethnographic observations are re-presented in design environments, where new kinds of artefact are developed and re-presented in the contexts of field sites. One important point, however, is its circularity. Not every piece of work has time to go back into the field and evaluate ideas. Yet even when projects do not do that, design outputs and understandings may still make that journey. Design tends to re-engage, with or without the anthropologist, and whether by direct or indirect routes. The second point of the visualisation is to draw attention to the nature of the act itself, and to think about what it does. As we have seen, different design anthropologists argue that very different kinds of work are being achieved by these acts, and of course it is not as simple as just shifting data and prototypes from one place to another. Design artefacts appear in different places as microcosms of other locations, and the work is constantly evoking, representing and presencing other places and times. Escobar (2018) might refer to this as working to manifest a ‘pluriverse’ in design.

## Consequences: working with design materialities

I began this chapter with an outline of some of the Irish Rural Transport work, at Intel Digital Health Group, and I asked what kind of difference had been achieved, between the ethnographic research phase and the provotyping phase? The chapter has also addressed the question, how is this kind of work to be achieved?

I suggest that the design anthropologist works with design things which are flux-like, as I described in the previous chapter. Although the designer works always with a design output in mind, sometimes moving on before conceptualising a problem completely; and the anthropologist works with a focus on the field site, always trying to think about it more; the design anthropologist works to prolong the space between, attempting to maximise the space of potential, possibility and opportunity until it can be better conceptualised. It is across the social space between powerful institutions and sites of everyday design use that a sense of design purpose is produced, by which designed things can be appreciated as intended for particular kinds of people. Working across political gradients in an iterative way, back and forth, between one site and another, makes waves. In order to do this, design anthropology works to produce conversations and knowledge artefacts which it makes sense to talk about. Messy video clips are usually more productive than mini-documentaries, provotypes often more useful than polished designs and clear questions very often more useful than clear answers. Artefacts can prolong these conversations more easily than bulky written reports. These half-thought artefacts enable collaborative thinking using ethnographic things.

By suggesting that this is how material culture work plays an effective part in design anthropology work, I am subscribing to particular sets of anthropological ideas. I am suggesting that empirical observation is a necessary element, preferably ethnographic; that the work of relocation of design anthropology knowledge artefacts is necessary; that it is necessary to recognise that the work happens within a globalised, capitalist, industrial economy; that collaborative presentations are a core element; and that it is therefore preferable to have a figure involved who is an audience or client for the work, because people are a necessary medium for design anthropology work and knowledge.

I have also suggested that the work of problematisation is convergent in design anthropology with the work of developing understandings and I outlined some of the specific problems which design anthropology has. These include the problems of fidelity, of having design agendas and of values. In the chapters which follow, I go into more depth into the kinds of conceptual and relational work involved in these three fields, by discussing the work of contextualisation, of value and of temporality. In each of these three fields of transformation, design anthropologists are called upon to make their distinctive contributions in particular ways, working on and around problems to produce new knowledge, understandings and conceptual framings.

## Summary

This chapter has argued that design anthropology consciously works within a wider knowledge economy, by which a collaborative and collective system of relationships develop around social knowledge.

A very important question for design anthropology is how it manages the relationship between field site and design studio, in order to be able to make use of the space between these two sites for the production of anthropological knowledge, interpretation and critique.

There is almost inevitably a sense of inequality built into most design work. People depend upon corporations and professional institutions for change to happen or not happen in their lives, while likewise powerful institutions depend upon the people who use their designs. Design anthropology happens within this often unequal but mutually dependent situation.

The knowledge economies of design, both professional and popular, are permeated by institutions. These include corporations, the public sector and third sector and communitarian organisations. Many of these institutions are best thought about not only as property-owning economic groups but as a way by which intentions and progressive work are collectively organised. Corporations as cultural phenomena are crucial to design work, and they pervade social life, but they are not appreciated enough by anthropology, or they are treated as culturally neutral in ways that render them opaque.

Traditional anthropology proposes a few kinds of relationships by which anthropologists build anthropological knowledge, such as ‘participatory observation’, or ‘key informant’. In design anthropology, there is a wider spectrum of different kinds of anthropological relationships. I have called the knowledge production work which happens in the relationship between anthropologists and designers ‘lateral ethnography’, a collaborative rather than participatory relationship.

Design anthropology work can be seen to have a range of epistemologies, by which it produces design-relevant knowledge within the network of relationships. Prime paradigms include ‘making’, ‘participation’, ‘collaboration’ and ‘relocation’.

The relationship between anthropologist and designer, in design studio environments, achieves various projects. There is the project of revealing culture, making it visible. There is the project of making ethnographic observation enchanting, or poetic, both to achieve effects in design institutions and also to enable people to re-think the situations they observe. There is also the project of challenging and critiquing design work, often in indirect ways by holding up a mirror to itself.

Design anthropology is often understood as a range of participatory methodologies in the service of better design, when this chapter has argued that it is preferable to see it as the use of design methodologies and epistemologies in the service of better sociocultural interpretation and understanding.
