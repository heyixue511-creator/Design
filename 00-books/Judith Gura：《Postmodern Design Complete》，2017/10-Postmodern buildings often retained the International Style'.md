Almost every new corporate structure erected in America in the i98os had the requisite columns, pediments and arches—as formulaic an approach as had been taken with Postmodernism's much-criticized predecessor style. There are limits of permissibility, however, for architecture: it cannot abandon the requirements of function, security, and comfort. It is, then, not surpris ing that Postmodernism was expressed most boldly in facades, and often did not migrate to interiors. Spaces like showrooms, retail stores, and restaurants were, to some degree, exceptions, being redecorated every few months or years with changing fashions. Hence Postmodernism ran rampant in malls, giving birth to elaborate projects including Jon Jerde's Horton Plaza (i985) in San Diego and FRANK GEHRY's Edgemar center (ı988) in Santa Monica. In other countries, architects were more likely to use classical elements as integral parts of a structure, rather than as surface appliqués, as in RICARDO BOFILL's monumental Espaces d'Abraxas or Kengo Kuma's spectacular M2 Building. Some, like MARIO BOTTA and ALDO ROSSI, preferred muscular, geometric forms, with little ornament. In Japan, architects like ARATA ISOZAKI and Kisho Kurokawa drew on both historical tradition and the ideas of the Metabolists, a i96os movement that developed utopian ideas for urban planning.
