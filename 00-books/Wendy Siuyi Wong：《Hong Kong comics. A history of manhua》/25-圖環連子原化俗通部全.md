## 1.

## The Eastward Conquer of Xue Ren-gui, 1920 Art: Liu Bo-liang

This Shanghai comic shows what early martial arts works looked like as evolved from the traditional sequential picture book, or lianhuantu. Chinese line drawings appear on the lower part of the page and descriptive text at the top. There were thirty pages in each issue of the series and sixty issues in the entire volume. This example tells the traditional tale of a national hero of the Sung dynasty, Xue Ren-gui.

## 2-4.

Wu Zong Wrecks Hou's Mansion: Superheroes Lianhuantu Pictorial, 1920s

Art/Script: Unknown

This 1920s series follows the adventures of superheroes in full historical costume, focusing not only on sword fighting but also on traditional Chinese mythology. Published in Shanghai, it ran for twentyfour issues, making up three volumes.

All Quiet on the Western Front, 1930s

This series, though based on an American movie version of the German novel about the First World War, followed the traditional form of lianhuantu: pictures below and text above. T’he characters are in Western costume but are drawn as Chinese, not Europeans.

Nan-yang Gate, 1947

Like All Quiet on the Western Front, this volume, printed in Shanghai and based on an historical incident during the Tang dynasty, followed the traditional form. Special characters in the lower corner highlight details of the story.

16-36.

Assorted titles, late 1940s Art/Script: Various artists

After the Communists took control of mainland China in 1949, Hong Kong suffered a shortage of popular reading materials. The owners of the city's lianhuantu kiosks——where readers rented the illustrated books for a small fee—needed to find an alternative supply, which they did either by drawing their own or by hiring artists to draw them. In style and format, these lianhuantu closely followed those made in Shanghai before the Second World War; most were based on classical Chinese mythology and historical tales. They feature fighting, war, ghost stories, or sword-fighting epics and they appealed mainly to male readers. The lianhuantu covers were often removed and glued to posters at the rental kiosks to advertise the titles available. As a result, many of the surviving comics from this era are missing their original covers.

37–51.

Uncle Choi, 1958

Art/Script: Hui Guan-man

Uncle Choi (Choi Suk) is one of the most memorable characters of Hong Kong manhua. At first, Hui told Choi's story in a humorous manner, but the tone soon became more serious as he focused on the war with Japan and made Choi a war hero. Uncle Choi was the best-selling comic in Hong Kong for several years and continued publishing for more than a decade and a half. When it first appeared, Uncle Choi was considered a creative break from traditional lianhuantu: it used modern fashion, and its story was much closer to real life than those of classic comics. During its long run, its content was adjusted to fit readers’ evolving demands. When James Bond movies and Japanese spy manga became popular, for example, Uncle Choi too became a spy, with machine guns and lasers rather than traditional weapons. However, some of these changes were not well received by readers, and the comic began to decline. It finally ceased in the mid-1970s.

52-57.

Boy Scout, 1960 Art: Ng Gei-ping

This popular comic follows the adventures of two Boy Scouts, Siu-ming and Siu Sam-ji. However, they are not typical Boy Scouts. Instead, like Uncle Choi, they are war heroes in the fight against Japan. Their adventures included combat, bombings, capturing enemy prisoners, and other similar events. Since the comic used the official name of the Boy Scouts, it was banned at the organization's Hong Kong headquarters. Eventually the Boy Scouts sued to prevent the writer from using the name, and the series title was changed to Siu-ming.

58–63.

Flying Black Batman, 1962 Art: Ho Yat-kwan

The late 1950s and early 1960s saw many locally produced science-fiction adventure comics. Most of these superhero manhua were directly influenced by American action comics, and it was common to see exact copies of Superman, Batman, Spiderman, and other American characters. When it first came out, Flying Black Batman was the number-two best-seller, following Uncle Choi. Other titles by the same artist include Clean-face Conqueror, Super Black Conqueror, Atomic Seven, and King Detective.

## 64-65.

Movie Martial Arts Novel Manhua, 1966 Art: Various artists

The popular sword-fighting films of the 1960s inspired many comics with similar themes, including Reflective Cave, by Ng Gei-ping, and The Killing Order ofMo-lum, by Ho Yat-kwan. These particular titles were simply comic-book versions of the films.

## 66-75.

Assorted titles, 1966–1972

Art/Script: Various artists

In the late 1960s and early 1970s, the most popular manhua were published as five-by-seven-inch comic books that cost twenty cents each. They covered a wide range of themes and subjects, from science fiction, romance, and kung fu to humor, soft porn, and ghost stories. The science-fiction stories were often direct copies or imitations of American comics, although some were technological updates of traditional Chinese stories. Romance manhua dealt with the traditional issues of love stories, such as betrayal and suffering. Soft-porn comics walked a thin line between sex and censorship, although some titles included more explicit images of women and sex on their covers than others.

## 76-77.

Science Fiction Series, 1966–1974

Art/Script: Dung Fong-yung

This series of science-fiction manhua illustrates the influence and eventual integration of American comics and Japanese manga. Science Wonder Boy is an imitation of a Japanese manga, Tetsujin 28, by Mitsuteru Yokoyama, and Black Batman features the hero and all of the villains of the American Batman stories.

## 78–80.

Lies of the Journey to the West, 1976 Art/Script: Tsui Yu-on

In the face of the strong influence of American action comics and Japanese manga, even traditional Chinese stories had to be modernized in order to compete. This title was based on the traditional story of The Monkey King, The Buddhist Monk Tung Sam, and His Fellows. Another popular title by the same artist was Electric Pig, which follows the adventures of a pig with superpowers——he could resist lasers and other weapons——who helped fight criminals.

Little Vagabond, 1968   
Art/Script: Wong Yuk-long   
Little Vagabond, one of Wong Yuk-long's earliest comics, follows the humorous action adventures of a drunken Chinese god, Vagabond.

## 82.

The Son of Ultraman, 1969 Art/Script: Wong Yuk-long

The great popularity of pirated Japanese manga inspired many local imitations of the style, especially of the manga hero Ultraman. Wong's version, which stayed very close to the original's story and style, is the best of these copies.

## 83.

Solar Lord, 1969

Art/Script: Wong Yuk-long

Solar Lord, an original creation of Wong Yuk-long, features battles between good and evil in a futuristic setting. The action and drawing style in this comic became Wong's signature; they are also seen in his best-selling comic Little Rascals.

84-101.

Little Rascals, 1970

Art/Script: Wong Yuk-long

Wong Yuk-lung's most popular comic, Little Rascals, was translated literally to English from Siu Lau-man, and carried connotations of a little gangster with antisocial behavior. It was an original creation about a group of unemployed young hoodlums living in Hong Kong's public housing. The story revolves around Wong Siu-fu and his elder brother, Wong Siulung, whose one hair-covered eye brands him as “bad." He turns away from his criminal behavior, however, to help Wong Siu-fu fight evil. Little Rascals's stories emphasize brotherhood, and although the brothers are fighting, they are fighting for justice.

In the first issues, the fighting often involved chains, which led to spilled blood. Knives (and ropes, clubs, and daggers) appeared in later issues, and the enemies’ internal organs, guts, and bones were seen, often quite graphically. Little Rascals quickly acquired a large following, and although it had a positive message, its main attraction was violent action. Within a few years of its first appearance, many people in Hong Kong were criticizing the violence in Little Rascals and other fighting comics. The Indecent Publication Law of 1975 called for less-graphic violence. Depictions ofbloodshed remained common, but in response to the pressure, Wong changed the title to a more positive name, Oriental Heroes (Lung Fu Mun) and experimented with other venues.
