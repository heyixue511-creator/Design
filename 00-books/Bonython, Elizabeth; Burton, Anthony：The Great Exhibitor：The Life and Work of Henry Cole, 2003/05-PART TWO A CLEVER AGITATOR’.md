# PART TWO: A CLEVER AGITATOR’ CHAPTER 3 1830-1838 Work in the public record service occupied a considerable chunk of Cole's career: 27 years, from the time when he joined Palgrave in 1823. At first his work, agreeably scholarly and not too demanding, left him opportunity to develop his social life. But in due course disputes in his workplace brought his life to its first, character forming, crisis. On the accession of William IV in 183o, a new Record Commission was appointed, consisting of 25 members, headed, as before, by the Archbishop of Canterbury. It included some historians and scholars, but also politicians, peers, a bishop and two judges. Cole thought it too large a group. At least, so he said in retrospect. 'Its members were numerous. The first principle for securing good administration by concentrated responsibility had not then been recognized as necessary to effective government, although Jeremy Bentham had been preaching the doctrine of "single-seated responsibility" for years.'1 Single-seated responsibility - the allocation of administrative authority to identifiable, accountable individuals, rather than to fluctuating boards and committees - was to become Cole's favourite Benthamite doctrine, which he propounded relentlessly. It was from his experience of the Record Commission that he learned its importance. The new commission did include some reformers. One was Edward Protheroe, jun., MP, who was to become an ally of Cole. More significant, initially, was the new chairman of the commission, Henry Brougham, the famous reforming lawyer, recently appointed Lord Chancellor. At the first meeting of the board of the new commission, an attempt was made to re-appoint John Caley as secretary, but Brougham pushed through the appointment of his own nominee, Charles Purton Cooper. Cooper was a barrister of about 4o, with a busy practice at the Chancery Bar; an enthusiast for legal reform, and a writer of pamphlets. It was through his writing that he had met Brougham, who had introduced him into the circle at Holland House. Cooper had no professional knowledge of archives, but regarded this as an advantage. He claimed: 'I believe it was the circumstance that I did not possess a knowledge of the ancient records that induced the board to force upon me (for Lord Brougham forced upon me) the office of secretary rather than a record man; I was appointed for the purpose of checking the zeal of those lovers of ancient records.'² He also wrote that 'the condition on which I accepted the office of Secretary was, that its duties should be made in all respects secondary and subordinate to my professional avocations'.3 Notwithstanding this detached attitude, Cooper began by trying to make reforms. He was scandalized by the amounts of money spent by the commission, and at the first opportunity brought all its publications to a stop, and arranged to sell off old stock. Unwanted volumes were sold by weight at $_ { 4 } d .$ a pound, and the book collector, Sir Thomas Phillips, bought two hackney coaches full.4 Cooper was soon to become Cole's new master. After nine years of ‘servitude' working as a clerk to Palgrave, Cole was restless. While Palgrave received at least £1,ooo a year (f5o,ooo at present rates),5 Cole was still being paid £125 a year, though he was working as an editor rather than as a mere transcriber. His horizons had widened, for away from the office he moved easily among aristocratic and radical voung Members of Parliament and he had the friendship of John Stuart Mill and Thomas Love Peacock. Palgrave, for all his high income, would not pay him more in his present post.6 On 16 April 1832, Thomas Hardy, who was collating the Parliamentary Rolls for the board, and was by now a friend of Cole's, suggested to Cole that he might like to work with him at the Tower. The next day they called on Cooper (Cole meeting him for the first time), who took him on as a clerk under the Record Commission at a salary of fr 5o. Cole wrote that 'Mr Palgrave did not half like my leaving him under such circumstances',7 for Cole was Palgrave's most experienced clerk.8 Thomas Hardy had disliked Palgrave since the early 18zos, when, as one of his clerks, he had been in his power. Now they quarrelled seriously over the way Palgrave interfered with Hardy's work. At the end of the week in which Cole gained his promotion, he was present at the Tower during an altercation between Palgrave and Hardy that boiled over into fisticuffs. Palgrave, Cole recorded, 'got a thump of the face for his pains'.9 Palgrave's position under the commission, as an almost independent editor, was insecure under the new regime, for Cooper was trying to dislodge him. In June a committee was appointed to try to curb Palgrave's excesses, but it was made up of his friends, and had little effect.10 Cole continued to be wary of Sir Francis – who was knighted in 1832 - and gave vent to his antagonism in his diary.11 Cole's new master, Cooper, found himself 'so vigorously opposed by five or six of the Commissioners that he repeatedly stated his determination either to perform his duty or to resign his situation'.12 In fact, he did not resign. For the commission chiefly consisted (apart from Cooper's few opponents) of busy people who had no time or energy for it, and Cooper took advantage of their lack of interest to do what he thought fit. He did his best to control 'those lovers of ancient records', and became more and more high-handed in his treatment of the commission's staff. Cole, having been taken on by the commission, began to work at the secretary's chambers on the 1 February 1833, transcribing a parliamentary roll. Cooper's chambers were in New Boswell Court, just north of Theobald's Road, and he had had them nicely fitted up at the expense of the commission, calling the work 'repairs'.13 Later in February, Cole's cousin Richard Bond left Christ's Hospital, and joined Cole in his work. When the weather became a little warmer in the middle of March, they were able to begin work at the King's Mews, a storehouse that had previously been 'the stables and riding-school of Carlton-house, in which the Princess Charlotte used to delight herself with horse exercise'. Cole described this 'huge barn': 'the inside of this building presents a series of dark alleys – the sides of which are faced with Records reaching up some thirty feet high. It is a wretched makeshift.'14 The records had been terribly affected by damp and neglect: 'some were in a state of inseparable adhesion to the stone walls ... many were in the last stages of putrefaction ... others were so coagulated together that they could not be uncoiled. Six or seven perfect skeletons of rats were found imbedded'. With a dog to chase away live rats, and three Irish labourers fortified with liquor against the putrid filth, stench, dirt and decomposition', Cole set about putting these records in order.15 Cooper had told Cole that in addition to his salary, now £1 5o a year, he would be able to earn another £15o by extra work, writing reports for the agenda of the board.16 Cole waited a few weeks before asking for a letter from the board formalising his employment. He also suggested to the secretary that as he was doing the work of an editor, he ought to be paid as such.17 This made Cooper ‘exceedingly wroth'.18 Although Cole had escaped from Palgrave, he did not find Cooper an easy person to work for, especially since Cooper seemed disinclined to provide Cole with any definite statement of his terms and conditions of work. From now on, Cole was in an increasingly bitter struggle with Cooper. This must have been a formative experience, which caused him to discover and develop the tough side of his character that was to make him such a formidable controversialist and organiser. It is therefore worth following in some detail what might otherwise be dismissed as a tiresome office quarrel. Cole made an alliance with the reforming commissioner, Edward Protheroe. Soon after his appointment to the Record Commission, Protheroe had a period of illness, but by April 1833, having been elected MP for Halifax, he was back in London and at his parliamentary duties. Throughout May, he and Cole met almost daily. Cole helped Protheroe with reports for the board, and Protheroe advised Cole to tone down the letters of complaint that he was sending to Cooper. Cole discussed his troubles with the Hardy brothers constantly, as well as with Peacock, Grant, Elliott and Chadwick. From Mill in particular he derived 'sympathy and counsel'.19 In thus involving others in his troubles Cole was beginning to exercise his talents as a networker and agitator. On I May 1833 Cole, while keeping his own office at the King's Mews, went to work in the Augmentation Office, Westminster, which contained the records of the Court of Augmentation, set up by Henry VIlI at the time of the dissolution of the monasteries to handle his augmented revenue, and amalgamated with the Court of Exchequer in I554. This store, containing extremely important and valuable' records,20 was in a house near the south-west corner of Westminster Hall, and overlooked New Palace Yard. Next to it was Bellamy's Chop House, which politicians and lawyers treated as a club. The kitchen fires, where the famous steaks, chops and pork pies were cooked, were a constant worry to successive Record Commissions, but nothing was done. Though the storehouse was sufficiently airy and spacious', no one slept there and the doors only had ‘common locks'.21 Protheroe wrote a report for the board on 22 June 1833: These rooms had long been in a filthy state; it is possible that when the operation of cleansing the Records commenced, the rooms may have been so encumbered as to render order and neatness scarcely attainable; but it has long been unnecessary to leave the Records on the floor or to permit the workmen to tread them under foot when engaged in picking out those which might merit repair; and it was slovenly to leave heaps of dirt in the chimnies [sic], to be blown over the bound books and cleaned Records every time the doors were opened.22 Despite these dismal conditions, Cooper would not allow Cole to employ a cleaner. Out of office hours, Cole was writing reports for Cooper that were printed in the agenda of the board, but by June he realised that Cooper was not going to pay him for this extra work. With the help of Protheroe, he wrote to him on 28 June. He reminded him of his promise, and also asked again what were his exact conditions of employment, and whether he was employed by Cooper personally, or by the commission. Cooper replied the next day, with notable insouciance. You were lately a clerk of Sir Francis Palgrave, at a salary, as well as I recollect, of 1 25l. a year. You now fill a similar situation under the Secretary, but your salary has been raised to I sol. per annum. What is the precise authority that the Secretary may possess over you, and the other clerks in a similar situation, I will not pretend to determine, having never given the subject a thought, nor do I apprehend circumstances are likely to arise that will render a decision of this question necessary; and if it be of any importance to you, permit me to observe, you should have mentioned it at the time when I engaged you.23 With the help of Mill and Protheroe, Cole wrote several more letters to Cooper, always with no satisfactory result. Supported by his friends and feeling that he was in the right, he began to collect this correspondence and turn it into a pamphlet entitled Record Commission. A letter addressed to the Right Honourable the Speaker of the House of Commons, as chairman of the Commission on Public Records, on the conduct of C. Purton Cooper ... and the general management of the Commission, which he published after matters came to a head in December 1835. Meanwhile, he kept Charles Buller up to date with his troubles,24 and had meetings with Thomas Hardy at the Tower to discuss the behaviour of 'that outrageous jobber Cooper'.25 He was asked by the radical MP, Henry Warburton (nicknamed 'Philosopher Warburton'), to tell him of the developing turmoil in the record service, and therefore 'rode into Town in his carriage for the purpose of communicating with him upon the Record Commission'.26 The increasingly tense situation was unexpectedly defused by Cooper, perhaps because he was well aware of Cole's friendships among the young radicals in Parliament. On 16 August, he asked Cole to call on him, and, as Cole later said, 'to my very great surprise, he told me he thought it was for the good of the public that I should make at least 3ool. a year; that although the board had determined I should make only I sol., he was satisfied that the interest of the public required that I should make at least 3ool. a year'.27 Although Cooper thus offered Cole more money, he still did not clarify his terms and conditions, and Cole's opinion (recorded in his diary entry for that day) was that Cooper had 'tried to bamboozle me with offers of increased salary'. In another conversation three weeks later, Cooper 'renewed in substance the previous conversation only tincturing it with more examples of selfish lying & vanity'.28 In early September Cooper went to Bognor, and, with Parliament in recess, the early autumn was a quiet time for Cole. He allowed himself a few jaunts, such as the visit he made with Grant to see the steam boats intended for the Ganges that were being built at Maudsleys' yard. In the evenings he was transcribing an early thirteenth-century roll at home, and he took it with him on a visit to Epping. There he was courting his first cousin Marian Bond. She was governess to three little girls called Maitland, who lived at Loughton Hall, not far from Epping. Though the Maitlands were an established county' family, providing magistrates and high sheriffs for the locality, Cole was not impressed by the little girls’father, whom he described as ‘a hasty bluff good humoured, not very cultivated or informed person. A modern specimen of antient John Bull, hating the French, Up for the King and so forth'.29 Marian brought her charges to see her mother and Henry, and they all 'passed the day very blithesomely & happily. The children enjoyed themselves with glorious boisterousness'.30 On 21 October Protheroe came back from the country, and he and Cole resumed their almost daily meetings. Protheroe had had his own rows with Cooper – notably about a report that he had written but Cooper had changed. It was Protheroe who discovered that Cooper's chambers had been done up at the commission's expense, and that he did not keep proper accounts. He became ‘very inveterate against the Sec[retar]y threatening to cane him & calling him a “Beast",.31 Cooper was well aware of Protheroe's antipathy, and wrote to one of the commissioners a couple of years later: Mr P's interference has been productive of great mischief and [departments] which had begun to assume a form of order are again involved in the greatest confusion. Some remedy must be found for this or it will be useless to attempt to continue successfully the operations of the Commission.'32 At the end of October Cooper wrote Cole an extremely polite letter'33 and, when Cole called on him on 2 November, he 'received me with great politeness and deference'. Four days later he strived to be humourous and civil'. It may have been to disarm Cole's opposition that Cooper put him in charge of the Augmentation Office in the middle of the month. The board was anxious not to increase the salaries of the junior staff, and Cooper reassured Protheroe on this point: 'You are quite wrong in supposing that I have increased Mr. Cole's salary, or given him any emolument inconsistent with what passed at the Board. Sir Robert Inglis and the other members of that Board objected only to an increase of salary, but not to any additional emolument arising out of transcribing done out of office-hours.,34 Although Cole distrusted Cooper, his prospects seemed to have improved. Perhaps that was why, after a ‘boisterously merry' and 'very jovial' Christmas with his cousins at Epping, Henry married Marian at St Martin-in-the-Fields on 28 December 1833. Henry's preparations for the wedding, apart from going to hear the banns read, consisted of buying a jug for Toast & Water r 6'. The marriage was kept a close secret. Marian's sister Laura chaperoned her to London, but they did not reveal the event even to Lindsay, who came to see them in the evening. Marian went back to the Maitlands, presumably concealing the marriage because she wanted to keep her job for a while. She eventually came to live with Henry at the end of March, as posts were re-allocated in the record service. On 27 March John Caley, still keeper of the records at the Chapter House, Westminster, died, and Palgrave, whose edition of Parliamentary writs now came to an end, took responsibility for the Chapter House at a salary of £1,ooo, the amount he had previously received as a sub-commissioner. Cole was now officially appointed as temporary keeper at the Augmentation Office. He told Lindsay that the new post 'probably .. . will lead to more important results and be the means of conducting a better Salary to me. The post happens to be one which is not worth making a job of & thus it is likely I shall have no opponent. I have the keys in my possession & that is much & I do not intend to relinquish them without struggling.'35 Cole's work was now arranging and cataloguing the collections of records at both the Kings Mews and the Augmentation Office. He was also editing one of the Record Commission's folio volumes entitled Selections from the Miscellaneous Records of the Office of the King's Remembrancer of the Exchequer, but doing it after office hours and being paid as a mere transcriber or clerk, rather than as an editor. Cooper told him not to mention ‘editorship'when he promised to pay him £1oo for extra work,36 while Cole felt justified in calling himself a sub-commissioner. This title was very important to the status and self-esteem of the officers of the Record Commission: to be a clerk was to be on the lowest rung of the career ladder. Both Hardy and Cole had been clerks when they began their work, but after so many years of experience in transcribing and then editing they both considered themselves on a par with Sir Francis. Cole's new authority allowed him to reduce the fee to see a record to 8s. 8d. instead of the f3. 3s. od. that had been charged by Caley, and he allowed a free search of the indexes.37 Caley's library was sold by Hodgsons in July in a nine-day sale. Cole went to watch the indexes of the Augmentation Office sold. They were not bought by the Record Commission, but went to Sir Thomas Phillips for £230. Cooper continued to be friendly, and encouraged Cole to write a pamphlet calling for reform of the exchequer records. Cole produced and distributed this briskly in May.38 Here he first hit his form as a controversialist. Though the pamphlet had a bafflingly ironical title,39 the text was boldly, even floridly, polemical. In encouraging Cole in pamphleteering, Cooper was helping his young colleague to sharpen a weapon that Cole would soon use against him. In late July, Henry and Marian made a delayed wedding journey to Cornwall. Cole may have intended to follow in the footsteps of John Mill, whose journal of a walking tour in Cornwall he read before setting out.40 Henry and Marian travelled by coach (outside) to Portsmouth and by steamer to Plymouth. They toured the coast, covering about 1o miles a day mainly on foot, sometimes getting a lift in a cart. The weather was good and only one day was spoilt by rain. They came back from St Ives by steamer to Bristol, and then by coach to London. They reached home on 1 September, having spent in total £26. 3s. 1d. Apart from this holiday, they visited their relations at Epping, and made Sunday expeditions with members of the philosophic radical circle - Grant, Elliott and Chadwick. Also in this circle were Samuel and William Hickson, with whose parents Henry and Marian spent 12-14 October at their home at Fairseat, near Sevenoaks in Kent. Cole found the family 'particularly remarkable for their great simplicity and their benevolence'.41 They were radicals and shoe manufacturers, and the sons gave up their business to follow political and reforming careers. William, in particular, was a friend of Henry's, and shared his interest in music and education. Life in the record service was changed cataclysmically on the evening of Thursday 16 October 1834. Marian and Henry were sitting in their chambers in Adelphi after dinner when 'we observed a great light' and very soon afterwards Peter Paul, one of the workmen in the record service, came to fetch Cole as the Houses of Parliament were on fire. The House of Lords had caught alight and the fire had taken hold with alarming speed. A south wind was blowing the flames towards Westminster Hall and the Augmentation Office. When Cole arrived, Protheroe was already there. He had happened to be passing very soon after the fire began, and he had broken down the office door and was throwing the records out of the windows. Soldiers and policemen helped carry them over the road to the parish church of St Margaret's. Luckily at about 8 o'clock the wind changed to the west, and the fire, though it destroyed the House of Commons, did not reach the record repositories. Some of the records, however, were inevitably trampled underfoot and soaked with water, which was knee deep in the street. On the morning after the fire, Cole was at the church before eight, putting the records into sacks and arranging for them to be dried before taking them back across the road to the office. The upheaval caused by the fire gave Cole the chance to re-organise the office. He sorted the records chronologically, and had presses made to hold them. Caley's indexes having been sold, Cole had to make new ones. For the extra work that he did during 1834, Cole sent in a bill for £220, which, with his salary of £1 50 brought him up to $\mathscr { L } _ { 3 } 7 \circ , \mathscr { L } _ { 7 0 }$ more than Cooper had promised him. However, this sum was paid, and later the accounts were audited without questions being asked. Cole's total income for that year was £424. 13s. Id. (say, £21,00o in today's terms), and he was able to invest $\mathscr { L } _ { 5 0 }$ on the Stock Exchange through his cousin Samuel Bilke. The extra income came from fees at the Augmentation Office (at 8s. 8d. a document), and small sums from his articles in magazines and newspapers. He was still writing reviews of plays, exhibitions and operas, many of which appeared in The Examiner. He always praised the music of Marielli, an accomplished composer of drawing-room songs, for she was his friend Mrs Kingston, whose husband had given him singing lessons. Henry, Marian, Lindsay and Laura, with Mrs Bond, spent Christmas 1834 with the Webbs at Epping. With Charlotte they saw the New Year in at a dance given by the Hicksons. Outside the family circle, Cole was consolidating his friendships with the philosophic radicals, and at this time he also got to know Thomas Carlyle. He and Marian were invited to tea in the evenings at the Carlyles' house in Chelsea. Mrs Carlyle called them 'niceish talkative people',42 and she was grateful to Marian for helping her to find a servant.43 After a busy first half-year in 1835, Cole sent in an enormous bill in August, totalling nearly £oo for the extra work he had done, partly as a result of the fire and partly to provide indexes to the random records bound up by Caley, besides the usual evening work on individual records. This caused relations with Cooper to deteriorate again. Cooper did not deign to answer himself, but had his clerk, George Smith, write to Cole. 'I return you the account, which I have shown to Mr. Cooper, who was very angry with it. He says the amount claimed by you for work during extra hours, is more than double what he intimated you could receive, and that it would make up your emoluments higher than any other in the employ of the board, except Sir F. Palgrave, which, he says is quite out of the question.' Cole replied with a polite letter, meekly speaking of his feelings of delicacy in mentioning his remuneration and continuing, 'I shall be happy to have any conversation with you, and to be corrected in any of my errors, if you will allow me to speak openly on the subject'.44 Cooper tetchily answered: 'Last year your total remuneration was about 3ool., and I never dreamt of its exceeding that sum in the present year. . . . The claim you have made I consider as putting an end to any arrangement that may have been come to as to your spare time, which you will consider your own until the matter can be brought before the board.'45 Cole consulted Protheroe, who reassured him that the board already knew about his extra work and approved of his being paid for it. Cole's resolve to stand up to Cooper was strengthened by the trouble that Thomas Hardy was also having with him. After many years working at the Tower Record Office and as a Record Commission editor, Hardy considered himself a sub-commissioner, and indeed Cooper had called him by this title frequently, both in conversation and in writing.46 Thus encouraged, Hardy, who was editing the Close Rolls, asked to be paid extra for the preface. When this was denied, he declined to hand it over, and Cooper thereupon halted work on all projects, ‘every one of them, even those on the eve of completion'.47 Hardy did not take this treatment lying down, and wrote 'a rather taunting letter'.48 Then his younger brother William was rude to Cooper. Finally Cooper ended Thomas's editorial employment on 24 August 1835, saying that he himself was the editor appointed by the Board, and I {Hardy] was his mere employee'.49 Hardy thought Cooper had no right to dismiss him without the sanction of the board. In this worsening situation, Cole again wrote to Cooper on 9 November to ask when a board was to be held which would discuss his fees. Two days later Cooper replied: If I recollect right, I have more than once informed you that, as the Board in 1832 had refused to allow you any remuneration beyond I 5ol. a year, all that you have received beyond that has been paid on my individual responsibility, not only without the sanction of the Board, but in contravention of its order. I decline to incur this responsibility any longer, and I make bold to state that your conduct has been such that you cannot be surprised at my determination.50 Cooper was trying to imply here that he had been personally favouring Cole, and had now withdrawn his favours; but he only succeeded in making the damaging statement that as secretary to the commission he had acted against its instructions. Protheroe was able to support Cole by quoting from an earlier letter from Cooper,51 which acknowledged the arrangement that Cooper now tried to disown. Protheroe tried to protect Cole and Thomas Hardy. As he wrote to one of the sub-commissioners at this time: 'with respect to Mr Cole I am disposed to watch with jealousy any omission which I think unjust towards him as I think he stands at present in a very undeserved position'.52 Cole's diaries for 1835 and 1836 are missing, presumed destroyed either by Cole or his family. As the disputes in the record service became more heated, Cole doubtless recorded feelings that were not thought suitable for disclosure to posterity. Although everything in Cole's life conveys an atmosphere of unruffled geniality up to about 1833, he was now fighting with ever increasing determination, and even anger. In early December 183 5, in the office, he was shown the preface to a record publication, written by one of his junior colleagues, Frederick Devon. Here Devon praised Cooper, and when Cole read this the years of frustration that he had suffered caused him to lose his head and let fly with a most ill-judged outburst. Several other people were in the room and one of them bore tales to Cooper, who summoned Devon to verify the story, and then seized his opportunity to dismiss Cole.53 Cole fought back. After taking counsel's opinion, he wrote to Cooper on 7 December, declining to accept dismissal, and disclosing that he had already prepared an attack of his own upon Cooper, in his pamphlet, A letter to the Speaker, which was ready for publication. He wrote again the following day,54 implying that Cooper had exceeded his powers, to which Cooper replied: ‘The principal and immediate cause of my dismissing you was disparaging language used by you in speaking of the Board, and grossly insulting and slanderous words spoken of myself.'55 Cole's answer, on 14 December, while demanding chapter and verse from Cooper, was evasive about his own contribution to the quarrel: I conceive, though greatly provoked by your unjustifiable conduct towards me, and your general management of the Commission, by which you have seriously affected both public and private interests, I have never used language unjust or unbecoming: and now, Sir, after that condemnation which you have taken upon yourself to pass upon me, I again request to know the charge upon which you assume the right to condemn me, the precise language attributed to me, the names of the witness or witnesses, and the time and place of the alleged occurrence.56 Finally Cooper wrote to him enclosing a letter that he had persuaded Devon to write, describing Cole's outburst.57 This, said Cooper, will disclose to you one of the many grounds upon which I have dismissed you'.58 Cole was not allowed an interview with the board, and when it met on 18 December, it found against him. It was a large meeting, and many of those present did not know what was going on. They concluded that Cole and Hardy were not employed directly by the board and therefore could not be sub-commissioners. A committee was appointed to discuss their claims for fees. The board alsoOrdered that in future the engagement of all persons employed in the service of the Board shall as far as possible be defined in writing specifying the nature of the service and terms of remuneration'.59 Perhaps encouraged by this attempt to regularise Cooper's management of the staff, Protheroe moved to have Cole appointed a sub-commissioner and for him to continue his work at the Augmentation Office, but the board would have none of it. As Protheroe said later, ‘The question of Mr Cooper's authority seems alone to have been discussed by the board. At least, the contents of the memorials affecting the characters and livelihoods of the cleverest and most efficient gentlemen in our service did not undergo that attention, consideration and discussion which I thought and contended they deserved'.60 The young clerks who were connected with Cole and Hardy were also dismissed. Cole's cousins Edward and Richard Bond, together with William Hardy, all lost their jobs. A committee to settle the fees met on 21 December 1835, when Cole and Hardy were present, as were Cooper and his clerk. Cole was given his arrears of salary and a further £127. 13s. od. to make it up to £3oo for the period that he had worked under Cooper. He was also given 8ogns for the extra work he had done after the fire at the Houses of Parliament. Hardy's claim was sympathetically received but not settled. Cole later recorded that he 'received a message from Lord Brougham, through Mr. Protheroe, telling me that the Commissioners would consider my complaint favourably at a future time if I would only be quiet'.61 But he would not be quiet. As soon as he was dismissed, he distributed his pamphlet and published another, A second letter . . . to the Speaker of the House of Commons ... on the conduct of C. Purton Cooper . . ., in which he said: Mr. Cooper well knew how entirely I condemned his mismanagement of the Commission from the day it was taken into his hands . . .. He knew that a parliamentary inquiry was suspended over his proceedings; - he knew the danger of my evidence; – he knew, too well, the humiliation he had undergone in his endeavours to compromise and silence my disapprobation of his proceedings; – he knew that I would not suppress my testimony, and therefore sought to damage its character by a manoeuvre of dismissal.62 Cole's pamphlets were defiant, cocksure productions. He had to pay for them himself, and could ill afford it. He was now out of work, with no resources to tide him over this hard time. He may have been free to speak his mind about the iniquities of the commission but that brought in no salary. His chances of being re-employed were slender, given his reputation and his publications. To help out he took in Edwin Chadwick as a lodger in his house in Notting Hill Square (now Campden Hill Square) and borrowed money from him. Chadwick was now secretary to the Poor Law Commissioners and, an ardent and rigid reformer, was hard at work implementing the Poor Law Act of $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 \ : 3 4$ .Cole also borrowed from Horace Grant, from his father and from Lindsay. He earned small sums from articles about the Record Commission in the Law Magazine and the Penny Magazine. John Mill, lending Cole froo, spoke of 'the honorable & public spirited course' that he had taken.63In the tangle of disputes, Cole was on the whole in the right. But what stands out is his sheer, unappeasable persistence. It might now seem that he had fought his battle to a bitter defeat. But he would not give in. He resolved to escalate the fight to the utmost, taking on the entire Record Commission. What had started as a dispute about personal grievances at work now modulated into a programme to reform a public institution. ## Cole secured the support of Charles Buller, who, on 18 February 1836, moved for a select committee of the House of Commons, to inquire into the Record Commission. This was Buller's first important speech, and he told the House of the excesses of the commission in such a way that the members laughed at almost every sentence. He told them of the waste of money, and of the neglect of the records and of the mismanagement of the office. One of the follies of the commission had been to publicise itself and its new members by having leaflets printed in foreign languages and sending them all over Europe. He held up a pamphlet in which the secretary was 'immortalized' as 'Viro illustri, excellantissimo, clarissimo, doctissimo C.P. Coopero equiti Anglo'. This brought Roars of laughter'.64 As Charles Greville put it after Buller's death 12 years later, 'by universal acknowledgement he was an admirable speaker, full of matter, lucid, never dull, and generally very amusing. His greatest speeches were on dry and serious subjects, such as colonisation, emigration, or records, none of which became heavy or uninteresting in his hands'.65 A contemporary wrote in 1837: His face is like his speaking in Parliament; almost too lively and gay to correspond with our general notions as to the proper degree of dulness which ought to belong to a modern member of the Commons.'66 Buller's charm and grasp of the facts, almost certainly supplied by Cole, did the trick and the House granted him the committee without a division. At this period the select committee was the government's device to keep the radicals quiet. A committee could easily be granted; and the process of gathering evidence and preparing a report (which could then be quietly buried) kept the complainants busy. The members of the committee examining the Record Commission were all radicals with two exceptions, these being Sir Robert Inglis, a die-hard Tory and a Member for Oxford University, and Charles Williams Wynn, a moderate conservative who sat for Montgomeryshire. A witness's evidence before a select committee was taken down by a shorthand writer, and then sent to the witness to be corrected before it was printed. Thus it was possible for a witness to alter his answers to give a better slant to his replies. Cole, with his usual thoroughness, checked the answers that had been given and found that Cooper was the worst offender, rewriting not only his answers but the questions put to him as well. This pamphlet caused a stir, and Sir Robert Peel spoke about it in the House of Commons. The way of taking and printing evidence at select committees was changed as a result.76 Cooper joined in the pamphlet war, with a long compilation of 1285 imaginary questions and answers, printed as if they were part of the official papers of the committee. The commissioners themselves embarked on pamphleteering. They published anonymously as a pamphlet their Observations on the record report, and this gave Buller the chance to tease Mr Williams Wynn and Sir Robert Inglis in the House of Commons, with one of his witty speeches on 24 February 1837. The winter of 1836-7 was an exceptionally hard one with snow on the ground for most of January. There was a bad epidemic of influenza and many of Cole's friends caught it, though he did not. He was busy drafting a bill to establish a General Record Office, and his consultations with Charles Buller and the others had to take place at the bedsides of those who were ill. Buller, John Jervis and John Mill all succumbed. Cole also consulted Edwin Chadwick and Tom Hardy. The final draft was not settled until after Charles Buller had got the House of Commons's permission to bring in the bill. He introduced it for its first reading in February, with the government's backing. The plotters often met at the Reform Club, which had just been founded to be a meeting place for radicals and Whigs. The prime movers, Sir William Molesworth and Joseph Hume, succeeded in persuading a good number of Whig Members of Parliament to join. Molesworth had high hopes that the club would help to bring about the hoped-for realignment in British politics.The effect will be to break up the Whig party by joining the best of them to the radicals, and the club will be the political centre of Europe, and augment our power immensely.'9 The club opened in May 1836, in an eighteenth-century house in Pall Mall altered by George Basevi, with over 2oo MPs and 1,ooo ordinary members. Despite his poverty, Cole was one of the founding members and paid the annual subscription of £5 os. od. The members included almost all the MPs who helped Cole, but curiously Charles Buller did not join, though his brother Arthur did. Protheroe joined in 1 842. Cole remained a member until that year, and thus enjoyed, if only briefly, the amenities of the Italianate palace, opened in 1841, which Sir Charles Barry designed as a new clubhouse in Pall Mall. After the first reading of Buller's bill, the government decided to bring in its own Records Bill. Meanwhile, in March 1837, Lord John Russell, the Home Secretary, persuaded the Master of the Rolls, Lord Langdale, to take temporary responsibility for the records. This was a stroke of good fortune for Cole, because the recently ennobled Lord Langdale was Henry Bickersteth, a philosophic radical, and well known to Bentham, James Mill, Grote, Place and other reformers. Originally trained as a doctor (and son of the Bickersteth who wrote on contraception), he changed to the Bar, and rose to become Master of the Rolls and Lord Langdale in January 1836. James Mill commended Cole to Langdale, and on 3o March Buller reported to Cole that Langdale meant to take him and Thomas Hardy on to the staff of the new record establishment he had in mind. Cole was very anxious to get back into the public record service, but short as he was of money, he would never accept a post if it meant apologising to Cooper. To keep up the pressure for reform, both of the public records and in other areas, Cole and his friends started a weekly newspaper, The Guide.The aim of the Guide will be to advocate the interests of the Many, and to promote the steady and rapid progress of those Reforms in our Institutions which all but the bigotted [sic] and interested Few are determined on effecting'.80 As editor and one of the registered proprietors, Cole had to make himself an expert on all the dismal and frivolous formalities of obtaining sureties against libel, and for payment of stamp duties, [and] with all the intricate mysteries of the rights and privileges of newspapers, divided between the Treasury, the Stamp Office, and the Post Office'.81 The arrangements completed, the first issue appeared on 22 April. There were immediate complaints that it was too small, even though it claimed to be, at 3d., the cheapest weekly paper ever printed. It was distinguished by containing Peacock's 'Paper Money Lyrics', which he had written at the time of the financial panic of 1825, and which he allowed Cole to print for the first time. The paper, despite Cole's working all night to get it out on the streets on time, was a failure, lasting only for nine weeks before being sold off to a paper maker. Cole's need for money was now urgent, and Horace Grant offered to lend him £5o. To add to his worries, Marian was expecting their first child, who was a boy, born just as The Guide was failing. Henry was a delighted father, spending whole evenings nursing the child. 'In the Evening about little else than nursing Baby.'82 He was christened Downer Peacock: Downer after the rich uncle, and Peacock ambiguously after Thomas or William. Help was at hand, for in July Lord Langdale arranged with the board of the Record Commission for Cole and Thomas Hardy to be given some work. He summoned Cole to see him on 12 August and told him that he was ‘authorized to engage me to arrange the Records of the Exchequer of Pleas', a task that Cole began on 1 5 August. The older records are deposited in the only remaining portions of the Old Whitehall Palace, converted into a dwelling House wh: was inhabited by Oliver Cromwell. Went there & looked over the work proposed to me.' The house stood in Whitehall Yard, an open space beside the elm trees of the Privy Garden, with a distant view of St Paul's Cathedral beyond the Thames. In August, Marian, Henry and the baby spent a week's holiday at Greenhithe, taking Mrs Paul with them as nursemaid and travelling down the Thames by the steamers Vesper and Topaz.83 On their return, Cole, enjoying the security of a permanent post again, was bubbling with ideas for new journalistic projects (further discussed in chapter 5). He tried to start an educational newspaper, the Portfolio of Public Instruction; contemplated publishing a penny paper, compiled from other newspapers and journals; and in January 1838 took over as editor of the British Ladies Newspaper. The Record Bill, however, was at a standstill. The government had undertaken to bring in its own bill and Buller had stood down. In May, a bill was drafted with the advice of Lord Langdale, but, after the death of William IV on 2o June, when the current Record Commission lapsed, the bill languished too. In October Thomas Hardy was desponding and 'expecting Coopers ultimate triumph & advancement'.84 But in Lord Langdale Cole had a friend and a patron, who not only supported him but also understood him. After an interview on 31 October Cole wrote, 'I found him very affable and counselling against too rough & direct bearing, & that diplomacy was always necessary to effect objects. He said my character was always to give people pokes & that it was too critical. For his own part he did not mind it, but the world in general disliked it'. This long entry in Cole's diary shows that he took Langdale's advice in good part; but the experiences of the last few years had made him much more abrasive, and that was how he continued. Through the summer of 1837 Cole was working on his first book, Henry VIII's scheme of bishopricks. An account of the dissolution of the monasteries, the reorganisation of the dioceses and the fate of the free schools. It was a scholarly work, based on the records that Cole knew so well. It was published by Charles Knight, who was an important figure in the dissemination of educational and radical ideas. Cole first mentions Knight in his diary in March 1837, and he put the idea of publishing his Scheme of Bishopricks to him on 29 September of that year. After that, they were on friendly terms until Knight's death in 1863. Knight had first set up as a miscellaneous' publisher in 1822 but failed. He was rescued when Henry Brougham's Society for the Advancement of Useful Knowledge appointed him as its publisher in 1827.85 Knight's most successful publication was the Penny Magazine, launched in March 1832. It had a circulation of 2o0,ooo within nine months, and ran until December 1845. Cole was able to place articles on his chosen subjects in this popular periodical, and in its sister publication the Penny Cyclopaedia: and, in the rapidly developing field of pictorial journalism, where Cole was making a mark, Knight was a very useful contact. Although Cole's professional life was again secure, his domestic life was not proof against disaster. On the morning of 23 November 1837 he and Marian were awoken at five in the morning by the baby. He was ill, and Henry spent the whole day nursing him. He developed a cough and the doctor was called. A week later it was clear that he was very alarmingly ill', and a second doctor intimated that he thought there was great danger'. Two days later, ‘Marian after taking leave of Baby, who even in his coffin was beautiful, left the House with her mother & Charlotte. The various depositories of records were now united to form a Public Record Office, under the Master of the Rolls. Working to him there was now to be a chief keeper of records, confusingly titled deputy keeper' (so as not to prejudice Langdale's position as overall head). He was in charge of the records of the Common Pleas and Exchequer of Pleas.88 Cole also managed to get his brother Charles taken on to the staff of the Public Record Office, in the face of Palgrave's opposition. Despite increasing eccentricity, amounting to mental instability, Charles stayed there until 1868, when he retired on grounds of ill health.89 Henry, secure in his post, might have become a scholar-archivist. But he was not cut out to be a desk-bound scholar, and became increasingly involved in administration, putting right some of the inadequacies of the records service, and preparing for the establishment of the new Record Office in Chancery Lane in 1851. In February 1839, with the cautious backing of Thomas Hardy, he submitted to Langdale a scheme for re-organizing the records, offering it with rather emphatic deference: 'Should it seem, that I am hereby overstepping my proper position, I trust your Lordship will overlook the indiscretion & bear in mind that I am sincerely zealous for the good of the Service."90 Many of his ideas came through into practice, though his proposed reduction of search fees did not meet with approval from Palgrave.91 Cole's practical streak led him to take an intense interest in the cleaning, binding, labelling and shelving of records, and in Fifty Years he takes pleasure in recalling how he colour-coded the records in his charge, to prevent them from being misplaced, and had labels printed, on a hand press in the office, making clear what was in each item.92 Cole kept up a 'continuous agitation'for an enlarged force of porters and workmen who would look after the physical state of the records. He tried to get them a graduated pay scale, and concerned himself with their welfare to the extent that he was reproved for buying them beer in 184o. When, in 1842, Palgrave tried to favour junior clerks at the expense of workmen, Cole fiercely defended the latter, stressing their precarious status, with no insurance or pension.93 In 1845-6 Cole was again in dispute with Palgrave over the workmen, when Palgrave complained that they were taking too much sick leave, and that expenditure on their beer had gone up again. Cole became 'very angry' at these reflections on his administration and threatened to take the matter to Langdale, at which Palgrave backed down.94 From the official files, then, Cole emerges as a champion of the workers. This is in contrast to the picture he paints, 40 years after the event. Utilitarianism was often concerned more with efficiency than with benevolence and compassion (as witness the New Poor Law), and in Fifty Years Cole paints himself as a hard manager. The workmen, he stresses, were paid by the hour and hired by the day . . . an arrangement which especially commended itself to the Treasury, as involving no long engagement and superannuation. As far as I know, this was the only Government office which had daily servants of this sort. Some years afterwards, there was an extensive strike in the Metropolis between the builders and their workmen upon the question of the length of hours of working in the week, and what interpretation was to be put on the word 'day.' I told my experience of the hourly working to some large contractors, who adopted it, and maintained it till the strike was ended. The practice is now, I believe, universal in metropolitan trades, and public clerks are paid by the hour.95 Thus Cole, writing as a grand old man with a reputation as a disciplinarian, tries to tone down the subversive activities of his youth. He himself enjoyed considerable latitude in his terms and conditions of work. From 1838 onwards he was constantly engaged in campaigns which had nothing to do with his official work, and for which Langdale gave him time off. It was Langdale's liberal view that the salaries of assistant keepers were too low and that they could not be blamed for doing other things'.96 As to what other activities might be permissible, he again took a very tolerant line. What endeavours are proper? Is it clear whether extra employments are more or less inconvenient or dangerous to the service in proportion to their being more or less connected with the public or official employments[?] Writing histories, or dissertations or reviews on subjects of history as shewn or illustrated by Official documents in charge of the Officers, will by some be thought more fitting, & by others be thought most likely to seduce the officer from the strictly official line of duty – plausible reasons may be given for very different opinions. Cultivating - or even teaching – abstract science or painting – drawing - music etc - or exhibiting specimens of art - will by some be thought particularly eligible, because the subjects are in general remote from those which are the special object of official consideration.97 This is, in effect, Langdale's charter for Cole's activities between 1838 and 185o. With the select committee report on the records and the subsequent legislation, Cole discovered his abilities as a reformer, and almost at once, having retrieved his principal employment, he turned his attention to major reform in another feld: the Post Office. ## CHAPTER 4 The Penny Post 1838-1842 ‘Of all the events with which my career has been connected, no one I feel surpasses .. . the adoption of the Uniform Penny Postage. All the progress of mankind is helped on by freedom of thought expressed in writing. ... It will be the glory of England for all time that she was the first country to adopt this ray of light, and the fame of Rowland Hill will be imperishable, as having discovered Uniform Penny Postage.'¹ These are exalted words with which Cole describes the next project in which he was involved. But the penny post was a momentous development, the Victorian equivalent of today's internet. Rowland Hill's famous pamphlet, Post Office reform; its importance and practicability, came out at New Year 1837. A small first edition, privately printed, and circulated to friends and politicians, was quickly followed by a larger edition, published by Charles Knight. Cole had a copy of the first edition (which remained with his books until they were sold by Sotheby's in 1 893) so he may well have been a favoured recipient. His diaries are missing for 1835 and 1836, but it is likely that he and Hill (plate 1 3) got to know each other during these years. Hill must have known Cole by repute, if not personally, and he would have read the article on the public records that Cole had written for the Penny Magazine. Their first known meeting was at the house of Francis Place in the spring of 1837. Place was also a favoured recipient of the first edition of Hill's pamphlet, but, as he told a friend, he had not thought it worth perusal, having supposed that it was only some nonsensical scheme for carrying letters all over England for a penny, and being wearied out with wild-goose proposals for all sorts of impractical measures'.2 Hill's scheme was indeed for carrying letters, weighing half an ounce or less, all over England for a flat prepaid rate of one penny. The existing postal system, the Royal Mail, had been set up as a government department in the late seventeenth century. Letters were carried by post boys on horseback until about 178o, when improved roads made possible the Post Office's dark red and black mail coaches, with coachmen and guards in red and gold uniforms. Postage was charged by distance and paid for on arrival by the recipient. While some towns, including London, had a local penny post, the average cost of postage was 8%d. in the 183os, a huge amount for poor working people, who often refused to accept letters. Hill well knew this problem, for when he was a child in a struggling family, his mother, expecting a letter and having no money in the house, sent him out to sell half a bag of white rags (more valuable than coloured ones), which raised three shillings and set her mind at rest. Another way of escaping the charges was to send news by a coded message that formed part of the address on the outside of a letter. A glance at the address would transmit the news and the letter could be declined. Hill himself had once, when on holiday in Scotland, conveyed his whereabouts to his family by messages on the wrappers of old newspapers, sent through the post. Newspapers, which bore a stamp duty, were carried free of charge by the Post Office. A curious regulation was that a letter had to be one sheet of paper only, no matter how big or how heavy. If there was any enclosure, double postage was charged. There were various ways of evading the high charges. Businessmen, wanting to write to several addresses in the same town, would write the letters on one very large sheet, which was cut up on arrival and the pieces distributed by hand. Letters were often entrusted to travellers going to the appropriate destination: once, when Cole and Marian went to visit their cousins in Essex, they found that every passenger in the coach was carrying a letter for somebody else.3 The government, and by extension members of the Lords and Commons, were exempt from paying postage. They could', as Cole mischievously remarked, 'receive an unlimited number of letters free of postage, of any weight, even a pianoforte, a saddle, a haunch of venison.' Their signature at the bottom left-hand corner of the outside of the letter meant that the post office would stamp it ‘Free'. Politicians would spend hours signing their names on blank sheets of paper in order to have a supply of 'franks' to give away, or even sell, to friends and supporters. They were supposed to send out 10 a day and receive 15, but in the 183os over 7,000,o0o franks a year were sent or received. Cole would often get franks from Charles Buller or Edward Protheroe. The government regarded the postal service as a revenue-raising tax, so attempts to reform it had to ensure that changes did not reduce revenue. Although this requirement inhibited change, it is obvious that the obstructive system was ripe for reform. Rowland Hill came from a radical, reforming background. The Hill family's school, Hazelwood, near Birmingham, became famous throughout the world for its liberal methods of teaching, and was visited by such figures as Jeremy Bentham, Henry Brougham, Thomas Malthus, Joseph Hume and Robert Owen. The school was so successful that Hill and his brother Matthew started a branch school in 1827, at Bruce Castle, in Tottenham, north of London. At the school there was no beating - discipline being maintained by committees of the boys - and no fighting or bullying. Science was taught as a core subject, which was unheard of at the time. By 1833 Rowland and Matthew had handed this school over to their other brothers. Matthew then read for the Bar and entered Parliament, and Rowland devoted himself to inventing and to radical reforms. With his brother Edwin he developed and patented a rotary machine for printing newspapers, which would have been successful but for the fact that the excise tax had to be impressed on each sheet of a newspaper, and the Treasury would not allow it to be done on a continuous roll. Hill was also concerned with the development of South Australia. After publishing his pamphlet, Hill had an opportunity to explain his ideas on the penny post to a government Commission of Inquiry into the Twopenny (local) Post on 13 and 23 February 1837. Here he dilated on his idea that the fact that payment had been made should be signified by ‘a bit of paper just large enough to bear the stamp', a sticky-backed 'label'.4 Hill's scheme was so novel that in early discussions envelopes had to be described as little bags called envelopes' and stamps as 'small stamped detached labels - say about an inch square – which, if prepared with a glutinous wash on the back, may be attached without a wafer'.⁵ After the publication of the second edition of Post Office reform at the end of February, Hill's ideas gained the support of the press. He sent Cole papers for him to use in The Guide. Petitions were presented to both Houses of Parliament. In November 1837 Robert Wallace succeeded in obtaining a select committee of the House of Commons to look into the matter. Wallace, MP for Greenock and a founding member of the Reform Club, had been agitating in Parliament for the improvement of the postal service since 1833, even before Hill took up the cause. He had given Hill a half hundredweight of raw material' to help him write his pamphlet.6 It was as the select committee got under way that Cole became involved. At this juncture, Cole was on the point of taking a decision that would have changed the whole path of his career. For in February 1838 he was asked by Charles Buller to accompany him, as his secretary, to Canada, where Buller was bound on official business with Lord Durham. Durham was the white hope of many radicals, as the man who would become their prime minister if they ever achieved power. A strange mixture, he was a fabulously rich coal-mine owner, possessor of a Gothic castle and a peerage bestowed by the Tories, and had a romantic Byronic air, intensified by his poor health. He was, nonetheless, genuinely accessible to the humbler classes. His radical credentials were established by the part that he played as lord privy seal in Lord Grey's cabinet, in the preparation of the Reform Bill. The fact that he got quite out of step with his colleagues and eventually resigned, only enhanced his reputation as radical leader-in-waiting. After some time on the fringe of political life, he was sent out as governor-general of Canada, to settle ominously rising tensions between French and English colonists, with Buller as his right-hand man. Cole would have liked to go with them. He said that the temptations to do so were very great, but the tears of my dearest and best adviser, and my recent return to public service with the Records, prevented my acceptance'.7 Cole did well not to join this mission, for Durham tried to take a strong line, miscalculated, to the embarrassment of the government at home, and soon resigned in an atmosphere of muddle and recrimination, from which his reputation never recovered. At the time when Marian shed tears before Henry, she was expecting another child, and there was relief when, on the night of 31 May/1 June, a girl was born, fat and healthy', after Marian had been in labour for only two hours. Hill's postal reforms were being promoted by a pressure group of city merchants – the Mercantile Committee' - led by George Moffatt, MP for Herefordshire, a wholesale tea importer. Hill, meeting Cole by chance in Piccadilly, on 9 February 1838, asked him to 'assist him with Post Office Agitation', and a few days later Cole became secretary of the Mercantile Committee. The Circular was written by Cole, and it ran during the parliamentary session. If we could bring together 2o such men as yourself & Ashurst we should quickly carry the question'.9 The report finally appeared in March 1839, when it was welcomed by the press. The Spectator devoted a special issue to it and printed it in full. The reformers kept up the pressure, urging the government to implement the select committee's suggestions through legislation, and, indeed, to go farther and introduce the penny rate, as at first envisaged. The Mercantile Committee revived the Circular to this end, and Cole clearly relished the opportunity again to exercise all the arts of persuasion. In reply to the charge that the penny post would greatly increase the amount of mail carried in the mail coaches, to the extent that the transport costs would increase twelvefold, Cole resorted to graphic communication. He drew a caricature in the number for 30 April 1839, showing a mail coach, laden with parcels of different sizes, demonstrating how a small bag of letters paid the cost of transporting a large bag of newspapers and another of franked letters that were taken free. He took the statistics on which the caricature was based from those published by the Post Office itself. Doubtless pleased with his work, he had an extra 3,ooo copies of this issue printed and sent them to all members of the Lords and Commons. He even tried drama as a means of persuasion. The previous autumn he had written a scene about postal reform to go into a Christmas pantomime, Fair Rosamund. It was accepted by the eminent actor-manager William Macready, and handed on for performance at the Drury Lane theatre, but there was not enough rehearsal time to put it on. Now Cole followed up this dramatic experiment by writing a skit on postal reform, which put the arguments for and against the penny post into the mouths of the Queen, Lord Melbourne, Lord Lichfield (the postmaster general) and Rowland Hill. The Queen, speaking on behalf of reform, has much the best of the argument, and concludes by suggesting that Lord Lichfield and the secretary of the Post Office should step down.10 Some 2,ooo copies of this were printed and sent to the Lords and Commons, and another 2,ooo were handed out. A cheap edition of 1oo,ooo copies was published and was either sold or given away. Then in March 1 839 Cole arranged with the publishers, Chapman and Hall, for a further 40,ooo to be bound into one of the monthly parts of Dickens's Nicholas Nickleby, then being serialised. Cole did not restrict his efforts to literary11 and pictorial persuasion. On 2 May a large deputation of MPs and others went to see the prime minister, led by Henry Warburton, because Robert Wallace was away in Scotland. Cole organised this. Lord Melbourne received them, supported by Francis Baring and Thomas Spring Rice. Warburton made a long, incisive speech, and Daniel O'Connell stood on a table at the back to plead on behalf of the Irish poor, whose family affections were frustrated because they could not afford to write to each other. Moffatt said that his consortium of merchants was ready to take over the postal service from the government, if it shrank from reform. Lord Melbourne told them that the government would give the whole matter prompt and earnest attention'.12 At the end of the meeting Warburton was loudly cheered. That evening, Cole was taken by Hill to the dinner of the Political Economy Club, which had been founded by William Tooke, Malthus, Ricardo, James Mill and others in 1821. In his after-dinner speech, John Ramsay McCulloch, the controller of the Stationery Office, attacked Hill's plan 'in toto'. Hill, Chadwick and S. J. Loyd were able to refute his reasoning, and at the end Cole, forsaking obscurity as a back-room organiser, intervened with a sharp question: if McCulloch was against postal reform, why, not long before, had he signed a petition in favour of the penny post? This, Cole noted, 'raised a laugh?. Gales of laughter were soon raised by another mischievous stratagem, aimed at demonstrating the stupidity of the existing postal system, in which a letter of one sheet, however large, went at a lower rate than a letter of two sheets, however small. A single-sheet letter weighing fractionally less than the limit of one ounce was prepared, together with a two-sheet letter made up of two tiny, thin pieces of paper weighing only eight grains. One of Cole's staff took 5o copies of each to the Charing Cross Post Office. He produced first one of the largest letters. The clerk looked at it suspiciously. He held it before the lamp to see if it were really a single sheet. He summoned another clerk to help his judgment. All this caused a delay, and a crowd began to collect at the window, who watched the process with interest. At last the clerk marked it with the single rate, and the spectators laughed. Then the smallest letter was produced, and the Post Office official turned crimson, became furious, and cursed a little, but he could not help marking it a double postage. Roars of laughter came from the crowd. Then fifty more of each letter were produced . . . Despite such mettlesome campaigning, the prospects of legislation still seemed remote. Fears were not allayed when, on 25 May, Lord John Russell, then Home Secretary, assured Warburton privately that the government hoped to introduce the penny post. Moffatt, with Cole's help, immediately arranged a public meeting at the Mansion House to collect more petitions. After a few days, when nothing more had been said in Parliament, he ostentatiously cancelled the meeting and announced that it was postponed until the government did more than ‘hope' to bring in the measure. Cole thought that it was Moffatt's ‘sagacity and courage' in doing this that forced Lord John to announce that there would be a resolution to introduce the penny post. This encouraged but did not satisfy the reformers, for they felt that a resolution could easily be rescinded and what was needed was an act of Parliament. Petitions in favour of the penny post continued to come to both Houses of Parliament. Cole exploited this device of persuasion to the full, estimating that by 2o July 1839, 2,007 petitions with 262,809 signatures, had been gathered. On 5 July Spring Rice, the Chancellor of the Exchequer, presented his budget and promised a bill, provided that the House would pledge itself to make good any resulting reduction in the revenue. Possible loss of revenue was still the great impediment to change, and enabled the Chancellor of the Exchequer to keep the whip hand in matters of postal reform. A few days later the postponed meeting at the Mansion House was held and passed off brilliantly'. A petition with 12,5oo signatures, that had been collected that day, was presented to the House of Commons, and despite further obstruction the Postage Duties Bill was brought to the House on 18 July. On 24 July it went through the committee stage unopposed in three quarters of an hour. There was still the possibility that the Lords would throw it out, and Cole occupied himself, during much of this critical period' before the bill came on, in calling on peers to get them to attend the debate and vote for it. He found that considerable support could be expected in the Upper House, provided that the bill was well explained. Accordingly he prepared some notes for Hill to take to Lord Melbourne. When the bill reached the Lords on 5 August, Cole and Hill sat on the steps of the throne. They heard Lord Melbourne introduce the bill in a good humoured and jocular manner'.13 He was answered by the duke of Wellington, who was against the change on the score of depression in the finances, but fully recognized the evils of high postage rates'.14 Cole wrote: 'Rowland Hill and myself felt alarmed, and my heart beat quickly, but we were relieved when he said, “what was called Mr Rowland Hill's plan was, if it was adopted exactly as proposed" (this said with emphasis) “of all plans most likely to be successful." He ended by saying, "I throw the responsibility on the ministers, and I shall, although with great reluctance, vote for the bill, but I earnestly recommend your lordships to do likewise"'. The bill was read without a division, committed in about two minutes' on 8 August, passed on 9 August and received the royal assent on 17 August. Richard Cobden, with whom Cole had stayed in Manchester when campaigning for postal reform, exclaimed, There go the Corn Laws!' He felt ‘that new flood-gates of knowledge were . . . opened' by the new postal legislation, and that prospects were thereby improved for reform in the feld with which he himself was most concerned, the Corn Laws. Cole was very nearly caught up in Cobden's movement. The Corn Laws had been introduced after the Napoleonic Wars, to protect Tory landowners, who grew most of the wheat in the country. Their effect was to keep the price of bread artificially high, which was a great hardship to the poor, particularly in the new industrial towns like Manchester, where Anti Corn-Law associations had been established. Manchester's association was much more active than that in London, and in 1839 Cobden sent John Smith, chairman of the Manchester Chamber of Commerce, and some others to London to try to revive the London association. The Manchester party called on Henry Warburton, who sent them to see Francis Place, and he gave them a letter of introduction to Cole. Consequently they tried to persuade Cole to become secretary of the Anti-Corn Law League. He was too busy with postal reform, and declined this interesting offer. He did what he could to help Cobden, however, and persuaded his new friend William Makepeace Thackeray to do some drawings that pointed out the price difference between bread in England and on the Continent, where it cost less by a third.15 In a letter to Cobden, Cole recommended Thackeray as a genius, both with his pencil and his pen. His vocation is literary. He is full of humour and feeling'.16 He and Cole had first met two years before when Thackeray was editing The Constitutional. They had much in common, as they were both struggling to support their families, and they were both grieving over the death of a child (Thackeray's second baby daughter having died that spring). Thackeray's sketches were published in The Anti-Corn Law Circular, which had been founded to campaign in the same way as Cole's Post Circular had done for the penny post. Cole also helped Henry Warburton collect material for a speech on the Corn Laws in the House of Commons. It was another seven years before the potato famine in Ireland precipitated the repeal of the Corn Laws in June 1 846. Cobden would later pay tribute to Cole as 'a clever agitator, and a public spirited fellow'.17 Cole's decision to stay with postal reform meant that he was involved not only in campaigning for the act, but in implementing it. Implementation fell to the Treasury, and Francis Baring, the new Chancellor of the Exchequer (who was appointed on 17 August, the same day as the act received royal assent) offered to employ Rowland Hill on this work for two years at the Treasury. After his initial salary offer of f5oo had been raised to £1,5oo, Hill accepted. Baring assured Hill that he would support the penny postage Heart and Soul'.18 He also gave permission for Cole to help Hill. Once again Cole had to get his chief's permission for this extra work, and he 'called on Ld Langdale to tell him of the Treasury offer which he agreed to my accepting if it did not interfere with the Records.' Cole's diary entry continues: 'Whitehall No Records', a rather prompt confession of backsliding.19 He often wrote no records' in the diary, indicating that he was not at work at his official desk, but after this interview he became more conscientious about his records work, writing down how many pages of transcription he completed each day. On 27 September Cole joined the whole Hill family at dinner, to celebrate Rowland's 1 2th wedding anniversary. A big silver candelabrum presented to Hill by the people of Wolverhampton was on the table for the first time. Its inscription read: 'To Rowland Hill, Esq., ... Founder and able Advocate of the Plan of Universal Penny Postage, A.D. 1839'. Cole and Hill now turned to making the planned penny post a reality. One of the matters in which Cole was to become most involved was methods of pre-payment. Even before Cole joined Hill at the Treasury, the lords of the Treasury had, on 23 August, invited ‘artists, men of science, and the public in general' to send in ideas of the best way to arrange for this. Four desiderata were laid down: convenience for the public; prevention of forgery; simplicity of checking by the Post Office; and control of the costs of production and distribution. A first prize of £2oo was offered for proposals, and two second prizes of £1oo. Cole himself was a competitor, among over 2,soo others. Many of the proposals were sent in by printers or inventors, who had patented ways of water-marking the paper to be used for stamps, or who knew how to engrave so intricately that forgery would be almost impossible. Others had perfected an indelible ink for the cancelling stamp so that the label or cover could not be used more than once. Several people known to Cole competed: Dr John Bowring, one of the founders and the former editor of The Westminster Review sent in three entries. Alexander Bain, the pioneer of electricity, competed, as did the physician and inventor Neil Arnott, and Isaac Pitman, who had just published his book Stenographic Sound-Hand. John Mill's hanger-on, John Robertson, joined in. Charles Whiting submitted several examples of machined printing and helped Cole with his proposal, on which he worked in the evenings during September and early October. He employed William Kingston, his singing teacher, to act as his clerk to fair copy it, and paid him £4 5s. od. for doing so. Two days before Cole had finished his proposal, he began work informally with Hill, and on 8 October he began his official duties. He described his work as being of a most miscellaneous character; consulting the Post Office Officials, ... inquiring into modes of conveying letters by public carriages, discussing daily with Rowland Hill points of difficulty'. His chief task came to be the superintendence of the production of the three forms of stamps which it had been resolved to adopt; namely, an adhesive stamp to be attached to any letters; envelopes; and a stamp to be embossed upon paper of any kind sent to the Stamp Office'.20 This last idea was shelved for several years, though the other two went ahead. When Cole began his work at the Treasury, Rowland Hill was away in Paris, sent by Francis Baring to study the French postal system. The day after Hill's return a week later, Colewalked home with R Hill who advised my not urging him onwards too much because he should overwork himself'.21 Poor Hill was frequently ill from overwork, and he drove himself to the limit without any encouragement from an energetic young man such as Cole. Cole himself was overworking: he went to his office in Whitehall Court in the morning to do his work as a record keeper, had his dinner brought to him there, and then in the mid-afternoon joined Hill at the Treasury, where he would often stay until after seven o'clock. He was still writing The Post Circular and arranging its distribution. In the midst of all this, Marian had another baby on 1 October, born in 45 minutes, before Dr Merriman could arrive. She was named Henrietta. In early November Cole had a boil come up on his leg, which was so bad that he consulted Dr Merriman, who ordered linseed poultices, poppy & camomile fomentations'. After a few days ‘Dr Merriman called, & took out the core of my wound. Still desired I should keep quite quiet, & forbade even my riding out'. While Cole was housebound, he spent most of his time analysing the Post Office Returns. His brother Charles ran errands for him, and he received a constant stream of callers about the penny post. He could not return to work for two weeks, and, when he did get back to the Treasury on 23 November, he was faced with a difficult meeting with officials from the Post Office. At the Post Office, both the staff and the politicians were against any change at all. Lord Lichfield, the Postmaster General, had said of the penny post that ‘of all the wild and visionary schemes which he had ever heard or read of, it was the most extraordinary'.22 But he eventually came round to acquiesce in the measure, because he chose to believe that the revenue to the Post Office would go up. Colonel Maberly, the joint secretary of the Post Office, was a more serious adversary, as he worked from within the institution itself. He was socially smart, having served in a fashionable regiment and married the sister of an Irish peer. He looked down on Hill, calling him 'that man from Birmingham', and resisted every change until he was finally moved to the Board of Audit in 1855. His entry in the Dictionary of National Biography says that for more than seven years Maberly continued in authority, and improvement of every kind was delayed and some millions of public money were wasted'. This is less bland than is usual for an entry in the DNB. It was written by George Boase, the Cornish scholar and bibliophile, who came from Penzance and fully appreciated the need for an efficient post to outlying parts of the country. Hill had already encountered Maberly in the course of his earlier agitation, and when he met him formally for the first time in September he wrote of the meeting: 'I was received with great apparent civility, but little things were said & done, I firmly believe with the intention of annoying me. The less I have to do with the gallant Colonel the better.'23 Cole found Colonel Maberly's deputies in the Inland Office and the Twopenny Post Department, William Bokenham and Robert Smith, 'very civil', at first. They accepted that, since the penny post had passed into law, they should now do their best to make it succeed'.24 But at the end of the month, Bokenham's manner revealed to Cole that they still had reservations. 'It was clear', Cole noted, 'that he still disliked the P.P. He spoke of "Mr Hill as our Ruler". He thought P.P. wd be a total failure'.25 However, a few days after temporary dual rates had been brought in, Bokenham had the good grace to tell Hill that ‘a greater saving than he expected results from uniformity of rate', and that owing to flexible administration the new system ‘has required no increase of strength' in the workforce.26 There were still attacks in the papers on Hill's scheme. An article in the Quarterly Review in the autumn said that ‘prepayment by means of a stamp or stamped cover is universally admitted to be quite the reverse of convenient, foreign to the habits of the people’.27 Cole called the article ‘a smartish & shallow production',28 but Hill took it seriously, and had his brother Matthew write a reply in the Edinburgh Review. Cole helped Hill assess the pre-payment proposals entered in the competition, and to write the report on them. The result of the competition was announced in December: Cole, Charles Whiting, Bogardus and Coffin, and Benjamin Cheverton shared the £4oo equally, despite the original terms of the competition. Bogardus and Coffin joined together to send in examples of complicated engraving for the stamps; and Benjamin Cheverton suggested printing the stamps on a mile-long coil of paper and that the adhesive stamp should show the head of Mercury. It is hardly surprising that Cole did well: he was working from the inside and had a deeper knowledge of the subject than anyone else. Not only had he worked closely with Rowland Hill for two years but also he was writing The Post Circular, and composed over 65 articles, draft petitions, pamphlets and analyses about the penny post. All this in addition to his experience as an agitator and journalist. His essay incorporated many of Hill's ideas, and in advocating these he was able to quote with precision from the Minutes of Evidence of the Postage Committee and from government papers. He did not agree with Hill's idea that postage might be paid by having the stamp printed or embossed on the sender's own writing paper. He pointed out that this would be very inconvenient, involving at least two visits to a post office to deliver and later to collect the sheets after printing. There was also the difficulty of guarding against forgery. Cole recommended adhesive labels and stamped covers – that is to say envelopes - printed on watermarked paper. Envelopes were rarely used up to this time, as their use involved the extra expense of double postage. Once charging by weight came in, envelopes became common, though it was another ten years before a machine for making them was invented. To promote the new system, Cole compiled a Post Office Almanac. But this was stopped when Kelly's, the big publisher of directories and almanacs generally titled Post Office Directories', took out an injunction against it. In granting the injunction, the Vice Chancellor said that ‘nearly all which gives value to the Almanac has been pirated verbatim et literatim from the Directory, or from proof sheets thereof surreptitiously obtained'.29 Cole (who kept a press cutting of the judgement) thought this very harsh. In his opinion, no copyright had been established by Kelly's. Within a few days he issued a new edition of his Almanac, with a defiant statement inside the cover pointing out that 'the Injunction obtained by the Proprietor of "The Post Office London Directory" applies chiefly to the old Post Office Regulations', which had now lapsed. Cole's Almanac, on the other hand, contained the new regulations, just issued'.30 Another by-product of Cole's work was a letter balance. He foresaw the need for accurate small weighing machines to be used at home to ensure that letters were under half an ounce in weight, and accordingly designed one. It had a coin-shaped counter balance and could weigh up to 4 ounces. It was patented in August 18 39 and was made in various versions by Silvesters of the Strand, who were engravers. It was published by Henry Hooper and it quickly sold out.31 Cole made £3o out of it, paid him by Henry Hooper, which may have been a commission or a payment for the copyright. Hill, with his brother Edwin's help, had meanwhile arranged for scales with weights to be designed and made for use in Post Offices. After stamps went on sale, other weighing machines and spring balances were quickly available to save the public visits to the Post Office. Edwin Hill joined the team to supervise the machinery to print the stamps. The penny rate for London was brought in on 5 December 1839, with a uniform rate of fourpence for the rest of the country. On 1o January 184o the penny rate for the whole country began, even though no stamps were ready and the public had to take their letters to a post office to pay their id. Hill and Cole tactfully stayed away from the main Post Office at St Martin's le Grand on that first day, but they heard that when the windows closed 'the mob, delighted at the energy displayed by the officers, gave one cheer for the Post Office and another for Rowland Hill'. Two days later, Cole visited the main Post Office 'to see operations', and found it ‘very noisy & crowded at the Pay Windows'.32 The great hall was nearly filled with spectators, marshalled in a line by the police, to watch the crowds pressing, scuffling, and fighting to get first to the window. ... Formerly one window sufficed to receive letters. On this evening six windows, with two receivers at each, were bombarded by applicants. As the last quarter of an hour approached, and the crowd still thickened, a seventh window was opened, and that none might be turned away, Mr Bokenham made some other opening, and took in letters and money himself.33 The famous penny black' stamp was introduced on 6 May. There had been dire warnings in the press that stamps would be forged or used repeatedly. To combat this many complicated designs had been submitted in the prize proposals, but in the end an engraved portrait of the Queen was chosen as the motif on the stamp, because she was unmistakeable.34 A medal designed by William Wyon, engraver to the Royal Mint was used as the source of the Queen's image. Copied by Henry Corbould, and engraved by the Heaths, it had an engine-turned background, and was printed in sheets of 24o. The Treasury decided to publish a decorated cover to celebrate the new cheap postal system. While Cole was still laid up with his bad leg, he made a drawing and discussed it with his friend, the engraver John Thompson. Several well-known painters were asked to submit ideas, but Francis Baring did not like any of them and on his advice, Cole approached William Mulready, one of the most successful painters of the time, 63 years old, and a Royal Academician of over 2o years' standing. When Cole called on him on 13 December, he found him 'very intelligent and affable'. He appeared readily to enter into the idea & agreed to make a trial.' Within two days Mulready made 'a highly poetic design', which Cole showed to Hill, who liked it. Over the next week there were several meetings at Cole's house with Mulready, Hill and Thompson to discuss the design and its engraving. Cole admired the extremely careful finish of Mulready's work: what Mulready himself once called ‘an ostentatious mastery of execution'.35 His 'poetic' design (plate 14) showed Britannia waving her arms over figures symbolic of Britain's far-flung empire. Angels fly out, apparently at her command, as winged messengers (symbols, perhaps, of a world-wide postal service). To either side are groups representing Asia (with elephants) and the New World (with Pilgrim Fathers and American Indians), while two smaller, more domestic, scenes below show figures eagerly reading letters. Behind can be seen some old-fashioned ships, perhaps packets, and a sledge pulled by a galloping reindeer. There was a blank part in which to write the address, but no room on the front of the cover to put on a stamp, because the cost of the cover, 1½d., included postage up to half an ounce. The design was approved by the Royal Academy and the cabinet, and all thought well of it. William Wyon took the trouble to write to Cole about it: Although I expected much from him [Mulready] it exceeded my expectations & I should really regret it if so admirable a design should be lost to the public'.36 John Thompson engraved it on brass, William Clowes printed it, and it went on sale on I May. On 2 May the first of the rude comments about it came out in The Times. 'We must say that we never beheld anything more ludicrous than the figures or allegorical device by which it is marked with its official character . . .. Cruikshank could scarcely produce anything so laughable'.37 Comments like this were published over the next few weeks, and there were some cruel caricatures. Many poked fun at Mulready's Irish origins. All observed what even the Queen, who was sent a proof - and also Mulready, Thompson, Hill, Cole, Baring, Wyon and the Royal Academicians – had failed to notice: that one of the angels lacked a leg. Hill wrote in his diary on 12 May: 'I fear we shall be obliged to substitute some other stamps for that designed by Mulready, which is abused on all sides. In departing widely from the established "lion and unicorn" nonsense, I fear we have run counter to settled opinions somewhat rashly. I now think it would have been wiser to have followed established custom in all the details of the measure [the introduction of the penny post] where practicable.'38 In his autobiography, Cole wrote: The postage cover was for dry commercial use, in which sentiment has no part . . . Had an allegorical fresco for any public building been required to symbolize the introduction of the universal penny postage, nothing could have been better than Mulready's design.' The adverse publicity was a disaster for Mulready, but it did bring him to the notice of Francis Baring's younger brother who became one of his patrons. And for Cole the episode brought lifelong friendships with both Mulready and the whole of the Thompson family. Cole went on working at the Treasury until 1842, trying to bring in a parcel post and to improve the sending of letters abroad. Characteristically, he preserved all his work relating to the penny post in albums, which are now in the Victoria and Albert Museum. Then the paths of Cole and Hill parted, Hill becoming a director, and later chairman, of the Brighton Railway. He returned to the Post Office in 1846, where Maberly was still in charge and still opposed to every change. He failed to mention, however, that he briefly served as editor of the Westminster. Founded in 1824, the Westminster amalgamated with John Mill's London Review in 1835. But the resulting London and Westminster Review, financially backed by Sir William Molesworth, did not prosper. Molesworth, who was losing money, withdrew in 1837, and Mill, after trying to manage as well as edit the journal, wanted to step down by 184o. After the political demise of Lord Durham and the weakening of the radical party in parliament, he felt that he had done all he could for the cause of reform. It is also possible that he was tired of dealing with the (nominal) editor of the Review, John Robertson, whom Carlyle had once described as ‘a burly Aberdeen Scotchman of seven and twenty, full of laughter, vanity, pepticity and hope',8 but later dismissed as a ‘a flathoofed jackass'." Furthermore Mill was 'drained so dry financially by the journal and other calls on his purse that I cannot incur the smallest extra expense on account of the next number of the review, and, all things considered, I would not recommend your doing so'.10 Mill's recommendation to Cole to steer clear of the journal indicates that Cole was already angling to take over. He and William Hickson pushed hard and achieved their aim, since there were no other obvious or eager candidates to replace Mill. In these rather unpropitious circumstances, Henry Cole thus got a chance to fulfil a great ambition: to edit a prestigious journal. He was to be unpaid editor, while Hickson was to provide the money. Mill hoped that they would keep Robertson on as editor, as he was poor and needed his salary, but he had to go home to Scotland. Mill insisted that the name of the journal be changed back to Westminster Review, so that the readers would know that he was no longer involved with it. Cole bustled round, calling on contributors at home or catching them at the Reform Club. Romilly, Buller and Thackeray all promised him articles. But Carlyle, recognising that Hickson and Cole lacked the prestige of John Mill, declined to allow them to reprint his series of lectures on heroes and hero-worship. He wrote disparagingly to John Sterling: ‘The Westminster Review has come into the hands of Cole and Hickson; to be conducted on new principles! What words are adequate? No words'.11 In writing to his mother he called Cole a ‘Dud' as editor.1² He seems to have been right, for Cole reported that the sales of the first number were ‘very languid'. Cole soon lost heart. His'enthusiasm waned with surprising speed', comments one scholar, for a man who had recently struggled so arduously to own the quarterly'.13 While Cole was clearly a talented controversialist, and a skilled manipulator of information, it may well be that his forcefulness and partisan intensity deprived him of the breadth of sympathy (and the range of contacts) that a successful editor needed. By the end of July i84o, after only a few months, he left the Review to Hickson, who remained owner and editor for over 1o years. Hickson's wife, whom Cole liked, was cool to him for many months afterwards for having abandoned the loss-making Review to her husband. It was to be nearly nine years before the Hicksons again came to dinner with Cole and his family in 1849. Though Cole had forsaken the Westminster, there were still openings for him elsewhere in journalism. One of them was the weekly paper, the Athenaeum, which was primarily a literary review, but covered all cultural matters to some extent. Cole first wrote for it in 1837, but a lifelong connection developed when he became friendly with Charles Wentworth Dilke, the son of its proprietor (plate 29). He was two years Cole's junior, and a sort of pig-in-the-middle in his family, caught between a famous father and a notorious son, who both shared all three of his names. 'Old' Charles Wentworth Dilke is best remembered today as a friend of the poets Keats and Shelley, but later in life, alongside a post in the navy pay office, he became proprietor and editor of the Athenaeum. Founded in 1829, this was faltering when Dilke bought it in 183o. He transformed its fortunes by reducing its price from a shilling to fourpence and increasing its size, and it so far succeeded that in 1845 it was said that it ‘is now to literary journals what the "Times" is to newspapers'.14 Cole already knew Dilke senior, for the Athenaeum supported the penny post and Cole had met him during the campaign. When Cole was asked to dinner on 1 October 1842 to meet the whole family, he wrote that there is a homeliness ab[ou]t them which puts you at your ease and in old Dilke especially good common sense with decision of character'. The middle Charles Wentworth Dilke, usually known as Wentworth, married Mary Chatfield in 1843, and they had the third Charles Wentworth Dilke, later to become an MP, whose promising political career was blighted by his involvement in a divorce scandal. The three generations of Dilkes lived together in a house in Sloane Street. Old Dilke was able to retire from the editorship of the Athenaeum in 1847, and, as proprietor, live on the proceeds of the paper, as indeed did Cole's friend, who 'gave up his leisure to the affairs of three or four of the Learned Societies'.15 Middle Dilke was a prominent member of the Horticultural Society, and founded a periodical, The Gardeners Chronicle. Cole began to write articles for this, as well as for the Athenaeum, to which he often contributed 'gossip', 'pars' (i.e. paragraphs) and 'miscellaneous notices'.16 In the early 184os Cole was very busy with literary work, producing guidebooks, children's books and some artistic publications, which will be discussed in the next chapter. In the midst of this, he found time for yet another attempt at being an editor: of a newspaper dealing with births, deaths and marriages. Cole started proposing this to publishers on 6 June 1844. It sounds like the sort of journal that might appeal primarily, perhaps only, to people with a highly developed interest in public records, like Cole himself. Indeed, he described it in his promotional literature as ‘a kind of personal Doomsday book – highly interesting and useful to the present time, and indispensable to the future'.17 The principal point that Cole advanced in its favour was that the compulsory legal registration of births, marriages and deaths had been in force only since 1836, and that these hitherto unpublished facts should be made available in published form.18 One of the publishers whom he lobbied conceded that births, deaths and marriages were the most interesting of human events & most read in the N[ews] Paper',19 and Cole made this point with characteristic emphasis in his publicity. In England and Wales, he claimed, a birth happens every minute, a death every minute and a half, and were marriages spread over the whole day, every fourth minute would witness the celebration of one. The hopes, fears, and sympathies of twenty millions are stirred by these events'.20 Longmans, with whom Cole was already working, agreed to take on the project on 27 August, even though the senior partner, Thomas Longman, 'could never get over the idea' (as he confessed on 21 October) that births, deaths and marriages ‘were not sufficiently interesting' to the general public. Cole urged his projected paper, which was to be titled the Historical Register, upon all his contacts. Macaulay, on 9 November, had doubts; the scientist Richard Owen, on 2o November, thought it ‘one of the Happiest Ideas' of the time; on 27 November 'Old Dilke thought the preparations for Hist: Register as good as possible & if we failed it wd be for want of a public'. Cole was so busy rallying contributors that, he noted on 2o December, 'diary neglected: but every minute at Register'. The Register started publication in the first week of the New Year, and there is nothing in Cole's diary for the first 11 days of January, so hard was he working. The Historical Register described its subject matter in its masthead as Family Events, National and Private Biography, Life and Health', and announced that it was produced ‘with the Sanction and Assistance of various Government Departments'. Presumably the latter supplied the details of 'B.D. & M.', as Cole referred to the prime subject-matter of the 16-page, folio-size weekly journal. All Victorian newspapers looked grey and grim, with tightly packed columns of small type, and the Historical Register was as grey and grim as any. The core of each number consisted of several pages in minuscule type, simply listing births, marriages and deaths, together with a statistical analysis of mortality in London. After this were a few articles, usually biographies or obituaries. The lightest of these was a piece on ‘The Christmas Pudding' in number 1; the darkest, a piece on 'Prussic Acid' (as a poison) in number 5. In the pages preceding the B.D.& M. came news, mostly copied out of other newspapers. On the front page was a section headed 'News of the Week. Summary'. In number 4, this began: 'The news of the week is in general unimportant'. Number 5 started with: 'Her Majesty, the Prince, and Royal Family continue in the enjoyment of excellent health'. The level of excitement rose a little in number 6: 'Her Majesty opened Parliament in person on Tuesday'. But number 7 was back to 'Her Majesty, Prince Albert, and the Royal Family continue to enjoy good health', and number 8 recorded that 'Her Majesty, Prince Albert, and the Royal children, returned to town from Brighton on the zoth, in the enjoyment of excellent health'. Although this is hardly a fair sample of the paper's contents, it does indicate that the Historical Register did not have a very keen edge. Alarm bells began to ring as soon as 14 January, when Longman said that the Register 'did not look very promising'. He brought in a Mr Maunder on 18 January, in the hope that he could 'work . . . the paper into Success'. On 4 February, Longman agreed to enlarge the paper to 24 pages, and ‘to placard and try for 4 weeks more'. By the end of February, a shutdown seemed inevitable. Cole tried to get Bradbury and Evans to take over the paper but they declined on 27 February. The final number appeared on 8 March. Cole's diary suggests that at this point he may have experienced a degree of agonising re-appraisal. He rearranged his room on 1o March (‘taking down shelves &c.'). On Sunday 16 March and through the following week he did his accounts for the last three years, hitherto neglected. And he was slightly ill, with 'unhealthy urine'. His doctor and friend Seymour Haden warned that this portended gout, but his prescription was that 'I need only take some Soda in the Ev[enin]g'.21 Cole continued to be very busy with other projects, including journalism. He now found an outlet in collaborating with Wentworth Dilke in writing about railways, a highly topical issue. The 184os were the days of 'Railway Mania', as the network of new railways spread out across the country. In t844 Dilke started the Railway Chronicle, to cater to the rising public excitement over this transport revolution. There were already many such journals in the market,22 the principal one being the Railway Times, which had been started in the 183os and which claimed for itself a weekly circulation of 27,000 by 1845. In 1845 there were over 700 railway companies, already working or being floated, many of them fraudulent. Cole wrote a spoof of a prospectus for the Railway Chronicle: 'The Great National Direct Independent Lands' End and John O'Groats Atmospheric Railway, with Steam Ferries to the Scilly and Orkney Isles, and Coasting Docks at both Termini. Provisionally registered Capital, £9,500,000; in 95,000 shares of £100 each'. Such was the hysteria at the time that some people took it seriously.23 Cole wrote not only about the politics and economics of rail travel, but about the interesting new experience that it offered to sightseers. Guides for railway outings had been published since the late 183os, and Railway Rambles'to places near London had already appeared in the Penny Magazine in 1841. Cole came to this genre through writing a series of ‘Day's Excursions out of London’ in the Athenaeumn, in autumn 1842 and 1843. At first, he envisaged that his readers might ramble by foot, carriage or steamer as much as by rail, but gradually rail became the preferred method, and Cole offered thoroughly practical advice: 'The seats at the end receive the most pitiless currents of wind and rain - air at all times - seasoned with the cinders from the engine.'24 The purpose of the expeditions was antiquarian, to see old churches and historic buildings in such places as Esher, St Albans, Rochester or Winchester. Some of Cole's articles were re-issued in book form in 1843 as Felix Summerly's day's excursions out of London. Cole proceeded to a further series of articles in the Railway Chronicle,25 which later appeared in book form as Felix Summerly's pleasure excursions.26 His personal railway mania then led him to produce 'travelling charts'. His innovation here was to make the railway journey itself the central feature. The Railway Travelling Charts: or iron road books, for perusal on the journey included details not only of towns, villages, churches, mansions, parks', but also of 'stations, bridges, viaducts, tunnels, cuttings, gradients'. These features were all marked on a sort of map, which adopted a traditional format for road maps, in which the road meandered down the middle of a page, with details of places and landmarks on each side. In Cole's charts the railway line went vertically down the centre, flanked by text, and by illustrations in the form of charming little wood engravings after such artists as Richard Cox, jun., Charles C. Pyne, and Fraser Redgrave, the half-brother of the painter Richard Redgrave. The charts were individually printed on long strips of paper, which folded up into handy pamphlets, and were published from the Railway Chronicle office. They described the lines from London to Brighton (fig.3), Cambridge, Oxford, Southampton, Tunbridge Wells, Guildford and Birmingham, among others. Cole also wrote general articles, and paragraphs of news called ‘gossip, accident and progress of works' for the Railway Chronicle, and on at least one occasion, in March 1 846, acted as sole editor. His work brought him into contact with Wentworth Dilke's associate, John Scott Russell, a Scot whom Cole first met over dinner at Rowland Hill's. Russell had arrived in London with a huge reputation as a naval architect, an engineer and an experimental scientist. He had made discoveries about the behaviour of waves and the displacement of ships' hulls. In 1837 he was given the Gold Medal of the Royal Society of Edinburgh for his paper $\mathfrak { s } _ { \mathrm { O n } }$ the Laws of Resistance of Floating Bodies' (reprinted in the Athenaeum), and he was elected a fellow and made a member of the council. He also made a name at the British Association. He was at the cutting edge of experiments with steam propulsion, both on land and at sea. He would have liked to be a university academic, but had to earn his living as manager of a shipbuilding firm on the Clyde, where he designed and supervised the building of some of the first ocean-going steam ships for the Royal Mail Line. He realized that if he stayed there he would never be anything more than an employee, so he moved to London with his wife, two children and hardly any money. They lived in a two-roomed flat in Westminster, which was so full of books that they could hardly move. He and Cole became close friends and they were in many ways very similar: within a few weeks of the same age, clever and energetic, and in their different ways ambitious. Russell was determined to reach the top in his chosen career, while Cole's ambition was tempered by a public-spirited streak that he had learned as a youth from John Stuart Mill. Russell was un-pompous (though pomposity was an occasional vice of Cole's), but not unaggressive. Cole, never physically violent, was amazed to see Russell knock a man down for pulling at his arm at Croydon Station one day (5 October 1845) when he and Cole had been out together for a long Sunday walk. Russell was in charge of the money at the Chronicle and at first Cole was paid £2 2s. od. a week and then $\mathcal { L } _ { 3 }$ , with extra for compilation' or editing. London to Exeter by way of Bristol, was the most efficient and successful company at that time. Its track was brilliantly engineered by Brunel, and ran on a gauge of seven feet and half an inch. Brunel had invented this width of rail for his first railway to Bristol, and he believed passionately that this was the best and only width to use, clinging to his invention through thick and thin. However, the first railway in Liverpool in 1829 had been built to a gauge of 5 ft 8½ ins – thenarrow gauge'– which became the almost universal width for the rest of the country. By 1845 there were 2,ooo miles of narrow gauge to only 30o miles of Brunel's broad gauge'. Early in that year the Great Western applied to the government for permission – a private act was needed – to extend its network to Oxford, Worcester and Wolverhampton and to join it with the South Wales Railway, of which Brunel was also the engineer. This brought opposition to the broad gauge into the House of Commons, and on 25 June Cobden asked for a royal commission to consider bringing in an act to make all railways, existing and to be built, conform to the narrow gauge. The report of the commission was published early in 1846 and favoured the narrow gauge. Brunel, who 1o years earlier had written It's a proud thing to monopolise all the West as I do',27 was not prepared to give up easily. He rallied his supporters, and several of his directors and investors were influential people. He wrote a long pamphlet, Observations on the Report of the Gauge Commissioners and distributed it to all Members of Parliament. The result of this lobbying was that a clause was inserted in the Gauge Bill, which allowed him to continue using the wide gauge and to build the railways that he was planning to his seven foot width. After the bill became law in August 1846, Cole was inspired to intervene with a pamphlet of his own, addressed to George Carr Glyn, the chairman of the London and Birmingham Railway. This trumpeted his frustration at the way that the supporters of the narrow gauge had stood by doing nothing while Brunel was allowed to have his way. 'Keeping aloof' from controversy, said Cole, was ‘an egregious mistake'. Public questions are not carried in this country upon their intrinsic merits. It is by the energies of those whose private interests are touched by them.'In the battle of the gauges, the London and Birmingham directors had shown 'feeble supineness', and now uniformity of gauge is temporarily sacrificed, and worse still for us, a ruinous competitive battle will have eventually to be fought between us and the Great Western'.28 The London and North Western Railway was the company most threatened by the incursions of the broad gauge, and, roused by Cole's pamphlet, 'they engaged my services to create a public opinion to support Uniformity of Gauge as best for national interests'.29 Once more Cole was in business as a lobbyist. By this time the L.N.W.R.'s lines stretched from London to Edinburgh, but anyone wanting to travel from the South or West of England to Scotland had to change gauges at Gloucester. Those in favour of the narrow gauge foresaw that if the broad gauge continued its expansion there would be more junctions where passengers would need to change trains, as Cole had pointed out in his pamphlet. Cole got permission from Dilke and Russell to spend £1 5o on leaflets and maps to be put out by the Railway Chronicle. His principal allies in the agitation were the two Dilkes, John Scott Russell and John Chorley, who wrote for the Railway Chronicle and reviewed for the Athenaeum. Cole himself wrote at least five pamphlets, besides his week-toweek work for the Chronicle. His most successful pamphlet, which ran into three editions, was entitled A railway traveller's reasons for adopting uniformity of gauge addressed to I. K. Brunel, Esq. Cole suggested that Brunel should put himself in the place of a commercial traveller who had to change trains at Gloucester at night to go to Cheltenham, with all your proper equipments, of samples, and packages, and great coats, and feet wrapper, and neck wrapper, and night cap and umbrella, and carpet bag, and dressing case. Make an honest actual experiment of it, and though you yourself are the inventor of the Broad Gauge, I will wager any stakes, that you pronounce the break of Gauge an unsufferable nuisance, and that you return to London a wiser man, bent on annihilating the monster you have created, and were contemplating to spread throughout the length and breadth of England.30 Although most of Cole's railway pamphlets were printed in ordinary, everyday type, this one was printed, perhaps as a courteous gesture to a great man, in Cole's favourite Old Caslon typeface, its anachronistic look being enhanced by the use of long S's. The old-fashioned appearance of the pamphlet was in comic contrast to its subject – the latest technology of the day. As in his earlier lobbying, Cole was not content with words alone. He arranged for Henry Townsend to make drawings of travellers and porters struggling with goods, luggage, children, babies, horses and dogs to change platforms, which were reproduced in the Illustrated London News in 1846 (plate 1 5). Cole and Thackeray visited Gloucester together, which resulted in Thackeray writing two sketches published in Punch in May and June 1 846. In the previous year, Thackeray had written a series, 'Jeames Diary', about Jeames Plush, a footman, who makes f3o,ooo out of railway speculation, leaves his place and sets himself up as James de le Pluche (of Norman descent). Then he loses all his money, and has to revert to being Jeames Plush the footman again. In $\mathtt { r 8 4 6 }$ Thackeray had a new series in Punch, ‘The Snobs of England', but he interrupted this to write two more Jeames skits, to help Cole in his campaign. These efforts were of no avail, and the broad gauge continued its advance until Brunel's death in 1859 caused his campaign to lose its impetus. The broad gauge was supplanted, though it lingered on the Great Western line to Penzance until the 1 89os. Somehow Cole remained on friendly terms with Brunel, though his opposition was so outspoken. They knew each other personally, meeting sometimes at the house of Cole's friend, the artist John Horsley, whose elder sister was Brunel's wife. Brunel's sister was married to Benjamin Hawes, whom Cole knew from the select committee on the records. A further link existed in Scott Russell, with whom Brunel collaborated, towards the end of his life, in the building of the Great Eastern steamship. The campaign for the single gauge may not have been a success, but as a result of his involvement in railways Cole obtained another commission as a lobbyist, when he was made the public relations man for a newly projected dock at Great Grimsby. Railways and docks were often constructed at the same time, as Cole realised when he was writing his spoof prospectus. In the past, docks had been built as far inland as possible on tidal rivers, in order to cut down the cost of transporting goods over land. With the advent of railways and larger ships, it became more practicable to build large docks on the coast and move goods to and from them by rail. Grimsby, in Lincolnshire, was well placed at the mouth of the Humber, and had been a prosperous port in the middle ages. But by the late 183os it had declined so much that little ports along the East coast like Boston, Wisbech, Gainsborough and Spalding had more trade than it had. Lord Yarborough was the principal landowner in the town and in the country around, and he and other worthies took over some small railway companies to form the Manchester, Sheffield and Lincolnshire Railways Company, so as to bring the line to Grimsby. There was also a new gas company, and a company to build a pier. The earl of Yarborough was chairman of all three companies. In the early $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 \mathrm { { O S } }$ , they expanded their ideas into a project for a deep water dock, capable of holding over a thousand ships, with connecting railways to carry goods to and from London, Sheffield and Manchester. When the news of the passing of the necessary act of Parliament came through in $\tau 8 4 5$ , the good citizens of Grimsby rang the bells of their beautiful but crumbling medieval parish churches. In the autumn of $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 7$ Cole stayed two nights at Brocklesby Park, Lord Yarborough's house about 1o miles from Grimsby. Also present were John Fowler, engineer for the railway, John Jobson Smith, a stove manufacturer from Sheffield and deputy chairman of the railway company, and Mowbray Morris, manager of The Times. They discussed the project and travelled about to Louth, Grimsby and New Holland to inspect the works. His lordship amused them in other ways. He took them to see the family mausoleum, and after dinner he led them by a covered way to the stables to watch his 6o or so horses being tucked up for the night in their white nightcaps.31 Cole set to work with his usual vigour to get publicity for the cause, and pictures and paragraphs of news soon began to appear in The Times, the Illustrated London News and the Railway Chronicle. A feature in the Illustrated London News of 15 August $\textrm { r } 8 4 8$ praised the new link between the railway and the Humber ferry. An illustration shows passengers tottering aboard the ferry from the old landing stage, and the text emphasises that wet feet will be a thing of the past when passengers can alight from their trains and walk down a covered staircase to the boats. On 7 June Lord Yarborough asked Cole to find out whether Prince Albert would lay the foundation stone of the new docks. Cole called on the prince's equerry, Colonel Phipps, on 19 June, taking with him a pamphlet written by his friend Samuel Sidney on the importance of the new railway lines to agriculture in Lincolnshire. This seemed to do the trick, and Phipps sent a formal letter of acceptance. For a salaried civil servant to be moonlighting as a public relations consultant might have been deemed odd. What might be deemed even odder was the involvement of the Prince Consort in this affair. Indeed, Cole did get into hot water. On 22 June, Palgrave told Cole that 'his attention had been called to my visit to Pr: Albert', and that he had reported it to Lord Langdale. The latter summoned Cole for an 'unofficial' interview, where he stressed that 'literary labour was admitted but not other without leave'. Presumably he gave Cole leave to carry on working for the Grimsby project, since to ban him from it after he had involved Prince Albert would have been too embarrassing. But discretion was called for. When the time came for the laying of the foundation stone, Cole was put in charge of the ceremony, planned for 18 April 1849. The prince was to travel up in the royal train, and, anxious that the royal mind should not be unoccupied during the journey, Cole assembled a collection of books on the archaeology and agriculture of Lincolnshire, together with a portfolio of prints and watercolours. He also borrowed some paintings from Richard Redgrave, Thomas Creswick and Charles Cope, but the authorities would not let him hang them in the prince's carriage. At the London station on 17 April, Cole was pleased to be acknowledged by a bow from the prince, and he travelled in the same train, arriving at Grimsby at I o'clock. The prince received a loyal address from the mayor before going to Brocklesby Hall to stay with Lord Yarborough. On the way he was accompanied by a cavalcade of 8oo horsemen. He was then taken out in an open carriage to admire the farming of Lincolnshire. The weather was very cold, with unseasonal snow. It snowed again early on the morning of 18 April, but nonetheless huge crowds came into the town. There was fluttering bunting everywhere, and a squadron of ships lay off-shore decorated with flags borrowed from the dockyard at Woolwich. On the offices of the Dock Company 'were the Royal Colours, purple, quite new, and inscribed as follows:- Long Live Queen Victoria. Welcome to the Prince. Prosperity to the M. S. & L. and Grimsby Docks. Long may the noble Earl of Yarborough's efforts be crowned with success'.32 The prince and his party arrived by train, and at the entrance to the docks the carriages were unhitched from the locomotive, and 'were drawn along by a whole host of navvies dressed in clean white frocks, and showing as they pulled "with a will" the great brawny arms by the strength of which they earn their livelihood'.33 'During this part of the ceremony a formidable snowstorm came on, and the scene in the amphitheatre [where the ceremony was to take place] was ridiculous in the extreme. The wind blew in the faces of the spectators, and the snow and hail fell so copiously that at length every back was turned from the platform, and for five minutes nothing could be seen of the company but an indistinct mass apparently consisting of white umbrellas, nether garments and hoar frost.'34 This was a pity for the mayor and aldermen of the town, who had ordered new robes from London. However, the sky cleared, and the royal party came down the steps to a platform well below the low tide mark, where there were seats for five thousand spectators. The ships in the Haven fired a royal salute, and six military bands played nearby. Here the prince laid the foundation stone (plate 16), attended by a small procession which included John Fowler bearing a glass vase to contain coins under the stone, and Cole carrying in a purse coins which he had specially obtained from the Royal Mint. After cheering, gunfire, and a blessing from the bishop of Lincoln, lunch was taken by 6oo guests, with the prince, in a tent put up by Mr B. Edgington of Duke Street, Tooley Street, London, partaking of food provided by the London Tavern. All were transported back to Grimsby by the railway. Cole was there overseeing it all, and travelled back to London in the prince's train. This was the first time that Cole choreographed a royal ceremony, a task that he was to find absorbing in later life. It is to be noted that on this occasion he programmed himself into the proceedings as ceremonial coin-carrier. After the foundation stone of the docks had been laid, and only after ‘weeks of perseverance', Cole persuaded The Times to publish an announcement in English, French and German advertising the docks. The German text appeared in German Gothic print (fraktur), the first time that such an announcement had been printed in this way in an English newspaper. Thackeray noticed it when he was in Hamburg, having dinner with Thomas Baring. Seeing the unusual type in The Times, Thackeray exclaimed, 'That's Cole's doing!' Cole claimed that the effect of the advertisement on the trade of the docks was immediate.35 This brought to an end Cole's work for the railways. He had not influenced the battle of the gauges very much, and interest in it had tailed off. The Railway Chronicle ceased publication in 1849, when Dilke, Scott Russell and Cole took on the publicity for the Great Exhibition of 1851. This great project came as the culmination of Cole's interest in matters of art and design, which had increasingly occupied him through the 184os, and to which we now turn.
