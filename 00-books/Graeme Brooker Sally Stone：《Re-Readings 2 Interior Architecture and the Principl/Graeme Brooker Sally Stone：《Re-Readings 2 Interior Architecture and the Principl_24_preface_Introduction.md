## Introduction

The design and positioning of particular objects and elements that inhabit the existing building is a process of exploring and taking advantage of the opportunities and constraints inherent within the original structure.

– Graeme Brooker and Sally Stone, Elements and Objects. <sup>25</sup>

Objects – whether an element of furniture, a pair of shoes in a shop, or an exhibit in a museum – are focal points in an interior environment. The positioning of these signifi cant entities is an act that can exemplify the essential principles of any interior. In other words, the careful selection and thoughtful situating of the object, will exemplify a number of different ideas and narratives about a space, and indicate that this is how the space or room will be used. For instance, the placement of a piece of furniture, such as a chair, will delineate how the designer anticipates how a space will be utilised through the particular placing of its occupants. The orientation of the chair, its relationship to others, and if it is a low or high chair, formal, or informal, at a table, or low and reclining, particularises how the space will be used and in turn how it will be viewed when the user is seated. Fixed furniture elements like banquettes and tables ensure that the occupant is invited to use and to view space from one angle or place,

In other words, the way in which any objects are located and exhibited, whether it is a person seated in a space such as a bar or restaurant, an object in a museum, or products in a shop, such as clothing, will exemplify distinct narratives about a space.

The story of the interior can be told through the medium of objects: elements that enclose and thus disclose all of the narratives of the domain of inside space. Objects are an interior entity that can be realised at a variety of scales: from the size of the city, to the dimensions of a human body. They are the defi nitive interior elements, things that can represent all inside environments through their physical formation. Utilising intellectual and psychological dimensions: all components that are material constructs, elements designed in order to portray particular spatial identities and atmospheres. This narrative also includes objects that are ‘found’, elements that are selected and used in a particular way in order so that their existing properties can ‘resonate’ with the new uses that they are subsequently prescribed. Found objects can be selected precisely because they come already imbued with meaning and loaded with resonance.

For instance, Maidan, a project by Bureau A, took a number of 100 by 60cm aluminium shields used by Ukrainian soldiers and security forces during the months of resistance in Kiev in 2014, and reappropriated them, in order to turn them into an expandable shelter, capable of protecting small or larger groups of resistance workers. The resonance of the found object lay in its appropriation from an object of attack and shielding its user from missiles, to a more passive roof, covering its occupants and keeping them from harm.

Bureau A have described it as a metaphor for architecture:

Give them a shield, a shelter or a barricade and they will create a small project out of it, the manifesto for how architecture will be in years to come. <sup>26</sup>

Objects are by no means static. Their movability will lend them a liquid dimension and one that will allow them to affect differing environments through their infi ltration of particular zones. Bao House by Dot Architects consists of an unusual cube mounted on the back of a bicycle. The cube is formed from polyurethane foam inserted into a mould. Bao means ‘bulge’ in Chinese, hence the name of the house. The house was designed as a moveable shelter that was human powered. One side of the cube slides open and there is enough space for three people to sleep in the foam-insulated water-resistant space.

The object as a receptacle of stories and an element in which each interior can be analysed using its own theoretical principles, or terms of reference, will include an examination of spatial organisation, atmospheric conditions, and will often demonstrate pioneering technological innovations. In short, each object positioned in a space, whether it be a pair of jeans or an impressionist painting, will often convey a clear vision of the spirit of the age, a paradigm of the zeitgeist in the matter of the interior.
