This new enterprise was intended as a showcase for modern furniture and household fittings, and as a forum for design exhibitions and the Modernist cause in general. By 1939, Alvar and Aino Aalto had designed the majority of the pieces in Artek's extensive collection, which ranged from traditionally joined straight-legged stacking chairs to formed plywood and laminated wood designs such as the seminalNo.41(1931-1932)andthecantilevered No. 31(1931–1932). Their intention was to produce experimental “new wave” furniture, and in this they were quided by their belief that it was better to produce small production runs than to compromise aesthetics in order to achieve a greater universality of appeal. Soon after its founding, the Asplund gallery became famed for its Neo-Nordic furniture and rug collections, which were designed by Sweden's leading young designers, including Thomas Eriksson, ThomasSandell, Jonas Bohlin and Matz Borgström. Some Asplund products such as Matz Borgström's O rug (1995) powerfully communicate meaningthrough a system of code-like symbols, while others such as ThomasSandell's Lucia candlestick (1995) are characterized by a striking elemental simplicity. Often the carpets, such as Bohlin's Rug on Rug (1998), have a playful post-modern quality, while typically the furniture is functionally innovative, as in Helene Tiedemann's Towe/ Table (1997) with its rail for hanging tea-towels. Asplund's stylish products are informed by the classic Scandinavian humano-centric approach to design that focuses particularly on the demands of the domestic environment. Since 1995, the company has marketed its designs through the Swedish distribution organization Swecode, which was founded in 1994 by the furniture manufacturing company CBI. In the late 1990s, Asplund began manufacturing designs by avant-garde British designers, such as Tom Dixon (b. 1959) and James Irvine (b. 1958).
