There is nothing in zenga which does not appertain to the whole. The equilibrium between the Zen will and the practical demands of life is a road to unity. The depth and breadth of Zen experience determines the handling of the brush. A grandiose theme such as The Second Patriarch puts his spirit in order is surely beyond the capacity of a European. Identification remains a central point in Far Eastern artistic practice; transposing oneself into another's spirit is a refined faculty not possessed by Europeans. The contemplative language of the brush and the contemplative language of prayer are directed towards the like-minded, the fellow-believer, selectively recognized. Both are sacred – the creative process and the ordering of the spirit. The idea as contemplation, which derives from this, is combined with sure mastery of technique. At first the Europeans only tried to imitate the bravura of the brush language; they forgot that the Oriental writing-masters rested on a much stronger stock of rules.

In fact, the European painters often tried to integrate the script into the drawing itself.'A good example among many is Karel Appel's Animal executed in 1957 (1053). The playful process is combined here with something animal-like, and the script with its calligraphic features has a special function. In heightened alienation, an unknown creature has appeared, executed with a ruleless spontaneous technique - for the European too everything is still open, everything is changeable, flowing, nothing is rigid.

The swing of the brush and its value is recognized by Karel Appel in his depiction of a horse with rider (r074) and in his Figurative calligraphy (xo75). This is work based on the Oriental principle of boneless painting', painting which manifests the highest degree 'of spontaneity. The ink traces resulting from the frenzied technique and apparent artlessness produce works of the highest quality. The massive, heavy form of the figure sketched by the artist displays the interplay of heavy and scrawny lines (1o75). By contrast Ekaku Hakuin's modulation of the flowing ink is fully adapted to the personality of Daruma (ro76). The casual diagonals signify the physical quality of the body, which acts as a pedestal bearing the head with its huge eye. The intensive conception is delimited and held fast in the mobile traces of the ink.

Gibon Sengai is equally successful in grasping the introverted Daruma in his contemplative pose by means of brief shorthand lines (1o77). The artist's impulse matches a certain kind of emotional behaviour, and a unified result is obtained through the use of a related kind of painting and writing action. The cyclical ordering of sequences of pictures conveys ¿mpulses of direction which, among the Tachist and informel artists, stimulate temporally bound, individually coded, forms of expression.

Oriental script was at the centre of the modern European and North American artists' interest; script seen as a ‘sign’ seemed to release language from its limitations. The semiological meaning of script impressed André Masson as early as 1955. One of the etchings has the title Chinese actors (xo99) ; he makes the scripted signs into figures and sets them dancing'.17 The writing field is threaded with light traces', and the result is a plurality of arabesques with a certain literary undertone. Masson had seen some Japanese dedication leaves' with black background (īo98), and these stimulated the composition of his etching. The figurative aspect flows through all these scriptural’ efforts, and is brought out openly by Asger Jorn in the title pages of his portfolio From Head to Foot (xxoo). Here too the grammalogue of the monogram is transformed into a calligraphic mask. These are reconnoitring experiments, not direct observations. They are phrases of play which appear related to the Orientals’ play of ink.

Julius Bissier displays the big sign-like form (ro91) that he was worked out in a few jabs of the brush. Initially his work consisted of experiential experiments developed in collaboration with the Sinologist Ernst Grosse, of Freiburg. Bissier has become one of the main representatives of West-East synthesis in the field of calligraphy. He is pressing for a new orientation of the line, the linear solid form and the point, and he creates a certain calligraphic rhythmic order. Traces of the uninhibited act of painting' can be seen in his work, but it is not any dependence on a particular style which leads him to his abstract signs with the effect of symbols.

A process underlies his attempts at conditioning the Oriental formulae, a process which is dependent on his own environment and that of his group. He had agreed with Grosse that he could not operate according to the cultural norms of the East. Certainly the creative act of the Western painter can combine visionary conceptions with manual dexterity; but in Europe the sign is not a confirmation of what has been learned, and it does not reflect the necessary response of familiarity as it does in the East. Therefore Bissier takes the route of aesthetic gestalt forms, with emphasis on the avantgardistic. Familiar’ images were impotent to elicit any direct participation from the viewing public of the 195os and 196os; and there is still a lot of strength in the exotic.

The general public, and especially the artists of Europe, have shown sympathy and'understanding for the spontaneous outpouring of individuality in Action Painting. Bold running script with a big sign at the beginning – this is in the Oriental tradition. Mokuan Shōtō is able to introduce a balanced graphic structure into the field of force created by form and ductus (1o97). In the East, but not in Europe, positive joyful experience is the centre of the contemplative.idea and its practical expression. The Tachist artist, by contrast, often creates in a mood of suffering and tragic entanglement. ‘He secretes his drawings as a snail secretes its shell; it is a natural process, but a process accompanied by pain."8

André Masson ceases using signs reminiscent of the human figure and selects abstract forms whose contextual meaning is‘to be sought in their animated rhythms (1089).

Western painters are gradually demolishing the barriers to comprehension separating us from the countries of the Éast. They are attempting to grapple with Chinese and Japanese signs as carriers of meaning. Bissier chooses the Oriental symbol of male/female unity (1o91) and constructs from it an emblematic figure, using his experiences and combinations to unite his creative conception into a kind of East-West symbolism.

Hans Hartung follows Sengai, and transforms the moon in autumn' calligraphy into active brush gestures (1o92). His uninhibited speedwriting drives the ink over the page in a pictorial performance of the action of painting (ro92). Hartung says: The point is to create the impression that everything is improvised.' But the Western painter does not surrender himself to chance; he plans, composes, and organizes the expressive effect. For him this impression is experience of the world. With him, the smallest fleck, the smallest sign, has to be a totality. . . . A cry is nothing at all, yet. For a cry to become art, it must submit to certain laws which are hard to define."

Action Painting, Abstract Expressionism, Lyrical Abstraction, Tachism - these have become accepted style-substitutes. The information gain for European and American painters has been considerable. Modifications can be demonstrated which favour emotional processes; experiences of learning and thinking can be transformed into pictures. The Eastern paradigm has been noted down in short order. Just as the Chinese sage grasps the truth in a flash, so the painter in the grip of inspiration finds his own truth with a single stroke of the brush. Uncertainty or hesitation in the spontaneous act of painting is the death of the idea. The unprimed ground of white silk or paper represents the emptiness of space; often the sign floats in this space, because reality lies behind the surface.

Thus Sengai's Symbols of the universe (x094, 1095) are more than words and thoughts; they concretize forms that lie beyond language. And Pierre Alechinsky develops his Variations on Sengai's Symbols of the universe.2º Executed with relaxed brushstrokes, square, circle and triangle are framed in a rectangle of 18 lithographic pen fantasies. They represent an attempted liberation from formalism, and they end in emptiness. Here too it is a question of 'becoming one' through a multiplicity including all errors.

The expansion of the sign on large flat surfaces advances. European painters seek rapid action and concentration. The optical effect is still their premise and pre-condition. They strive for swift reaction to - and transformation of - the space/time situation. By strong sensitization of the linear flow, which swings out beyond the edge of the picture, Emil Schumacher achieves the excitement and tension of sequences of motion (ro86).

Right up to the 197os the public has displayed a growing readiness to look at calligraphy. The pictorial themes do not strive for symmetry and measurement; they strive to balance out the sign, which takes concrete form only on the surface itself, guided by unmediated spontaneity.

The colossal boldness of the Oriental writing-masters has an overriding spiritual function. But the intensity of reaction can also fall off. The span between the extremes is then translated into optical terms through inward quiet and equanimity. The handwriting of à master is evident even in his most excited creations. Hans Hartung smashes into his canvas, totally uninhibited, and the heavy black forms and frenzied spattering are like a volcanic eruption (xo87). The speed of the creative process is rendered visible. The sect calligraphers of the East were capable of such uninhibited actions as soaking their own hair in paint or spitting paint from the mouth. Such impulses eventually led in Europe and America to the elimination of any fear of the empty surface. The spontaneity of chance governs the process of creation, but it would be incorrect to assume that active' chance, acting like an explosion, necessarily leads to frenzied sketching. Chance movements not released by extreme stimuli are also effective in the process of creation. They function without visible justification, without instinctual behaviour or habitual expressions. The operations of chance in the work of the Eastern calligraphers were often enough unintended. In Hans Hartung's work, the products of the late 193os show attempts in this direction. But only in the work of André Masson and particularly Mark Tobey do we find such harmony of inner orientation.

Spontaneity in the creative process acquires quasiemotional traits that can tend towards the goal of meditation in the work of Masson (1o89), Pollock (11o2) and Tobey (xxox). Concentration in the face of the empty canvas generally creates a store of energy which demands to be released. Such concentration can bring about a state of receptivity (often merely sensitivity) to stimuli of very low intensity. The artist then functions as a sensitive navigator'’ of these stimuli. Georges Mathieu, under the influence of the doctrines of Zen, set up four dogmatic principles for the process of painting:

(1) speed;

(2) absence of predetermined forms;

(3) absence of premeditated gestures;

(4) a state of ecstasy.

In comparison with the Zen master, the work of the European painter is a profane copy, because the ritual framework of the former is replaced in the European artist by a creative activity called into being by stimuli which are not so much spiritual as motoric in origin and nature. The transformation of technical skills, for instance the achievement of ink effects with oil paint, is also a reversal. The absorbent paper of the East, the spreading of the ink, the pressure and relaxation of the line achieved by brush exercises - all this had to be fundamentally changed, so that each European artist could impose his own rules on himself. The supposed agitation of the Zen master when writing is something that emerges out of compensatory contemplation; inner calm remains the centre of Zen painting. By contrast, the excesses of Tachism are determined to a large extent by a senso-motoric process. Hence it is not rare to find painters heightening their emotional responsiveness to a level of supersensitivity by means of drugs.

The gestural painting' of Adolph Gottlieb is a kind of regression to rigid communicative signs, for by the use of convention gestures can and do become a sign language. To this extent Gottlieb is striving for greater comprehensibility within the sphere of life he is familiar with. Big signs fixed and braced in the form of beams on a frame-related surface communicate a new gestalt quality whose concomitant is an aesthetic of shifting proportions.This has nothing to do with Oriental calligraphy', Pierre Soulages assures us. 'For me these black signs represent the possibility of saying everything at once."i Edge-related compositions are clearly emphasized in the work of Soulages (ro8o), Mark Rothko, Eduardo Chillida (1o81), Franz Kline (1o82) and others. The energy content of the big brushstrokes does not lie, as it does in Far Eastern calligraphy, in the quality of floating on a spaceless ground. Nor is the configuration open on all sides: often only a section is presented (1o82). Through this planned edge-relatedness there emerge other reference points, and these are associated with the general context of the experience of Western painters (1o8o, 1o8z). The fields of utterance of the Western painters are nevertheless very broad. In his free handling of the whiplash line', Gerd van Dülmen proceeds from the spontaneous flow of the script-sign, but then he waits and later reworks the structure on the micro-level. So his painting process is spontaneous only in the beginning phase; spontaneity is then superseded and differentiated by a variety of working processes (1o84).

But there is a third form of play to be demonstrated. Modern painting has been affected not only by Far Eastern calligraphy, but also by the Islamic Kufic script. An important feature of this calligraphy is the fact that it is always bound to the page of the Koran; the ordering of the rows of signs is subordinated to this. Dependence on this format is demonstrable even in monograms of the sultans. Christian Dotremont fits his cuneiform characters into three columns in a horizontal rectangle according to this system (xo78).²²

Jean Degottex goes through the original script exercises for the Western painter and, writing according to the European system, articulates a large black surface (1ro3). This is not a matter of known signs being transformed; rather the rhythmic flow of the European ductus from left to right is spontaneously hurled onto the surface. The left-to-right reference makes the signs more familiar; in comparison with the motions of Oriental calligraphy, his work introduces a certain element of European autonomy. The familiar flow reinforces the sense-impressions of the viewer. The picture is not legible’, but this use of the picture surface does produce a stronger conceptual image.

For many European and American painters the calligraphic painting process remains an adventure, because no one can predict what will happen on the canvas. No system is binding; each picture has its own system. Georges Mathieu informs us about such individual conceptions. In 1957, one hour before the opening of his exhibition in Tokyo, Mathieu painted a picture 15 metres long in the display window of a big department store in the presence of a crowd of onlookers. He declares, not without arrogance:

Without knowing it, I gave this people, who have been seeking for nearly a century to combine the privileges of their traditional art with the seductions of Western painting, an answer to their question. I painted in oils, straight out of the tube, I used big flat brushes and long thin ones, I struck the canvas with folded towels dipped in liquid paint, I worked out the backgrounds by simply moving along the horizontal canvas. I improvised my signs before their eyes in an atmosphere of religious ceremony. I was in ecstasy from the close contact with the people; I worked 53,482 times as fast as Utamaro', as a statistician happily informed me. In this manner, without noticing it, I brought about the fusion of their rooo-years-old art and European oil painting, which has influenced and seduced them since the time of the Impressionists.'3

Mark Tobey was active on the West Coast of America and belonged to the Pacific School. In 1934 he went to live in a Zen temple in Japan and also spent some time in China studying Čhinese ċalligraphy.

It is an indisputable fact that Tobey's essentially reflective work found a way, in and through the East, to express what cannot be expressed. ...24 Tobey's White Writing Paintings are permeated with the spirit of Zen. Like the calligraphy of the Zen masters, they express their inner self and are no longer concerned only with the outer form. Like the Zen painters, Tobey was a mystic, more interested in his inner visions than in the external phenomena of the world around him.²⁵

Tobey's work is not bound by spatial limits (1ror). The expansion of his paradoxical surface-space is contingent on a kind of multiplication which owes everything to the pictorial structure itself.

Working under different presuppositions, Jackson Pollock introduced the idea of the ‘all-over field'. Apparently without emotion, he trickles the paint from edge to edge of the painting and beyond (1roz). He deliberately starts wrong, and then goes on to build up the drip process to a perfected surface structure formed out of threads of colour. The distance to the object is muted, yet the tendency towards action and the motivation towards a definite goal and direction are strongly evident in these drip paintings. Pollock developed rhythmic qualities in his random pouring method. The artist's hand as a determinant is largely excluded, yet the plurality of threads forms its own individual language which pays tribute to the calligraphic manner as the highest mode of expression.

## Scmi-precious stones and glassware (pages 302-13)

I Jade is a general term for the minerals nephrite and jadeite. Mineralogically nephrite belongs to the hornblende or amphibole group. The material was easy to work, for with a degree .of hardness of about 6, it is related to felspar. The colour variations are great, from brown-black via yellow, green, grey to white. The places where it occurs on Lake Baikal and in East Turkestan had an almost sacred, mythical significance. Jadeite was also seen as a cult material. Mincralogically it belongs to the augite or pyroxene group, it is harder, comparable to quartz, measuring roughly 7 on the scale. It is found in East Asia, though more in South Asia, in Upper Burma, in the Mekong and in the S.W. Chinese Province of Yüman. The places where it occurs are very widespread, almost in every part of the world. In prehistoric times it was valued as a precious stone. In China both minerals are grouped together under the term yü. 1 The horizontal or hand scroll (emakimono) is an exceedingly broad format with a particular function. Whereas the tall, narrou format occurs with many different measurements. ## A note on block books and painters' manuals

Japanese and Chinese publications in block book form (single prints bound up together) are a substantial and indispensable source of material for the study of Far Eastern art. The seventeenth- and eighteenth-century books tend to be considered the most valuable and interesting, but on close examination those of the nineteenth century also turn out to be a treasure-house of art and culture. The nineteenth-century painters' manuals are as valuable as they are little known, and Western scholars have had little access to the later examples (after about 187o). The books are wide-ranging anthologies of art, often augmented with examples of the work of the great masters. They include many otherwise unknown sketches, designs and calligraphic symbols, often organized into themes, as in the present volume. They incorporate, for instance, a comprehensive survey of Japanese heraldic art.

The choice in each block book is wide; they include the newly imported media of copper engraving and etching as well as the woodcut. In about 19oo, a Japanese version ofArt Nouveau' appeared, and the ancient tradition of the woodcut returned to preeminence. The resulting books, and those that continued to be produced up to the 193os, were themselves undervalued in Japan at the time, but their importance was quickly recognized by Europeans, and they became prized collectors' items. Over and over again, one is astounded by the consistent high quality of Japanese colour prints on all themes, which caused much excitement among European painters, draughtsmen and craftsmen.
