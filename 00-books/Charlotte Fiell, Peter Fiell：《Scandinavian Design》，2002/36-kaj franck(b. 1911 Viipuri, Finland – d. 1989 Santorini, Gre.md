![](images/377655dbe7cf38b1726f6537091c95b53cd93a7cbb2e26c0223c392c387f18eb.jpg) > Kaj Franck, Kartio vases for littala, 1958 # kaj franck (b. 1911 Viipuri, Finland – d. 1989 Santorini, Greece) ![](images/3d0915035d3a6793c66782f91ac10fe8b5d03c7a10a16f58d66e6512df22884b.jpg) One of Finland's most celebrated designers of glass and ceramics, Kaj Franck initially studied furniture design under Arttu Brummer (1891– 1951) at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu (Institute of Industrial Arts), Helsinki, graduating in 1932. He subsequently worked as a catalogue illustrator for TaitoOy (1933) and the Riihimäki glassworks (1934). Between 1934 and 1938 hewas employed as a decorator and interior designer by Te-Ma Oy Tema and later designed printed textiles for the United Wool Factory in Hyvinkää in 1938. The following year, Franck was conscripted into the military where he met many fellow countrymen from less privileged backgrounds – an experience that profoundly impacted on his views of society. In 1945, he began working for the Arabia ceramics factory and a year later, designed a range of utilitarian tableware for the Väestöliition (Family Welfare Association). During the 1950s, he designed several ranges of everyday modern crockery and kitchenware that were intended for multipurpose use and that used geometric rather than fashionable streamlined forms. In an article published in 1949, Franck wrote: “New conditions of life and a new lifestyle have forced us to modernize home fittings ... (however) ... You can see hardlyany change in everyday porcelain ware ... There is no need for compromising on the need for appropriate utility items. Durable, solid, waterproof, easy to wash ... our times will build its design idiom on the conditions of life and forms which belong to us." Franck realizedthese ideals inhisglazed-faïence Kiltarange (1952), which was devised not as a traditional-style dinner service but rather as individual pieces with a shared aesthetic. In 1955, Franck received an Asla grant to study design teaching in America, and during his time in the USA he researched eating habits, which had a direct bearing on his subsequent design work. From1954, Franck also designed limited-production wares, such as his Lumipallo (Snowball)series, until he left Arabia in 1973. He also designed pressedglassware for littala between 1946 and 1950 that included the practical Kartio range of water jugs and drinking glasses. In 1950, frustrated by the lack of experimentation at littala, Franck left the company and a year later was appointed design director of the Nuutajärvi-Notsjö glassworks, which had recently been acquired by theWärtsilä group. The Nuutajärvi ![](images/d43e25e4dc484c0053115f4d383c440b636867ce02e3e2aed7d47cf761fa06cc.jpg) △ Kaj Franck, Lilli glasses for littala, 1956 factory had already established a reputation for the excellence of its studio glass, and while working there Franck was able to explore the creative potential of glass by experimenting with different techniques and pigments. In 1952, he recruited two Italian glassblowers who were able to bring with them the expertise of the Murano glassworks, and following this some of Franck's designs bore the unmistakable influence of Venetian glass. Franck's blown art glass for Nuutajärvi varied tremendously in style, from delicately engraved decanters torustic-style glasses with encased bubbles and boldly coloured bands, to “spun”glass withswirling stripes. One of Franck's last projects was the redesign of the Kilta range for Arabia. The resulting Teema range (1977–1980)ofnineteen pieces based on elemental geometric forms (the circle, square and cone) reflected his belief that, “the only possible way to solve the problems of utility ware was to be radical and social”. From 1919 to 1927 he was a professor at the Kunstgewerbeschule (School of Arts and Crafts) there. Between 1925 and 1934, he also ran a home furnishings business Haus& Garten with Oskar Wlach (1881-1963). As one of the most prominent architects in Austria, Frank designed for the Wiener Werkstätte and participatedin theDeutscher Werkbund's landmark 1927“Die Wohnung” exhibition in Stuttgart. His specially designed flat-roofed reinforced-concrete double house was extremely modern, but his furniture from the same period was Neo-Classical in tone and completely at odds with the Modernist functionalist aesthetic. In 1933 Frank began designing for the Swedish interior design company Svenskt Tenn. Two years later, he moved to Stockholm, became the firm's chief designer and adopted Swedish nationality in 1938. He designed numerous textiles and furniture for the company inspired by the 19th century British Arts & Crafts Movement and by turn-of-the-century Viennese design. Although he rejected functionalist ideology and viewed personal taste and comfort as the basis of domestic design, Frank's work was essentially modern, being distinguished by a purity of form. Hisfurniture was remarkable for its harmonious proportions and high quality craftsmanship, aswell as its practicality and comfort. From 1942 to1944, he taught at theNew School for Social Research in New York, and as a theorist wrote several books, including Architektur als Symbol (1930)and Accidentism (1958). He returnedto Stockholm in 1946and remained there until his death in 1967. His work enjoyed a revival in the 1980s and a number of his designs have remained in production with Svenskt Tenn. Like Carl Malmsten and Bruno Mathsson, Josef Frank is regardedas one of the three classicists of Swedish design.
