Humanism and Luxury: International Modernism and Mass Culture After World War II (1945–1960) he United States and the Soviet Union assumed leading and competitive adversarial roles internationally after World War II, in the period generally known as the Cold War. Design demonstrated and promoted each of the two superpowers’ political and economic interests and ambitions both at home and abroad. In Europe, as well as in Japan, severe hardship haunted the years immediately following the end of the war, with massive displacement of people, food shortages, material and housing insufficiencies, interruption of transportation and communication, high unemployment, and fears of working-class unrest. While urgently needed supplies and aid came in the form of charity and relief from agencies that included the newly established United Nations (chartered in 1945 with headquarters in Manhattan), economic recovery required more substantial investment. The role assumed by the United States in this effort was paramount. Through the Marshall Plan (also known as the Economic Recovery Plan or ERP, 1948–52) the United States government provided funding to corporations abroad to promote industrial production, stimulate employment, and improve the standard of living. The motivations behind such investment included expanding the market for the USA’s own goods and business-related services abroad, fostering free trade and economic cooperation among European nations (including Germany), as well as containing Soviet expansion in Eastern Europe, Asia, and Latin America. On both sides of the Cold War, design was a component of “soft power,” a form of political persuasion that included marketing the products of modern industrial design as evidence of technological, material, and social progress whether the result of capitalist free enterprise or of a communist command economy. The prospect of international markets spurred the global ambitions of large multi-national corporations such as IBM and CBS in the United States, Braun in West Germany, and Olivetti in Italy, and their conscious development of prominent and unifying visual identity programs, as well as efforts to link their products and services with responsible business practice through exhibitions, sponsorships, and publications. At the same time national identity persisted as a marker of sovereignty and independence from American hegemony in the forging of distinctive cultural values that might include a commitment to social responsibility parallel with welfare initiatives to improve living standards as well as to reduce social ills, income inequality, and avoid class conflict. At times there was a didactic, mildly polemic tone accompanying the promotion of international modern design in the early post-war years, reminiscent of strains of nineteenth-century design reform. In the United States, advocates such as the Museum of Modern Art, under the rubric “good design,” embraced familiar paternalistic themes of improving and educating the public to appreciate the aesthetic, social, and practical advantages of modern industrial design, including its broader contribution to fostering democratic values and a contemporary lifestyle that harmonized individualism and materialism with the self-imposed responsibility of citizens and businesses toward the wider community, in effect embodying a “new humanism.” At times the advocates of this approach leveled criticism against a narrow and monolithic view of commercialism, the advertising industry, and what designer Brooks Stevens termed “planned obsolescence,” which implied the absence of responsible standards and the exploitation of novelty, hedonism, and insatiable consumer desire. Standards for post-war design featured technology in the form of new industrial materials such as plastic, fiberglass, and plywood with techniques such as molding to create furniture and furnishings that implied costefficient production, comfort, innovation, and an association with forms of modern abstract art. This is seen most clearly in the relationship between furniture and abstract sculpture associated with the movements of Automatic Surrealism and Constructivism, and in the international recognition of artists such as Henry Moore and Alexander Calder (1898–1976), whose sculptures appeared not only in museums but also in large urban public places and parks. Graphic designers in the United States such as Alvin Lustig and Paul Rand also adopted a sophisticated approach to advertising and magazine cover design that showed affinities with the symbolic associations of abstract art, while in Germany and Switzerland graphic designers often viewed their work in ethical terms, simplifying and clarifying complex information using the discipline of a two-dimensional grid system. Addressing urgent material and housing shortages after World War II along with high rates of unemployment, Western European governments supported design as an element of economic recovery for both domestic and export markets, attempting to equal and then to surpass prewar trade and production levels. Expanding beyond earlier design concerns with economy and efficiency in relation to minimum working-class needs, industrial designers and advocates in the postwar period often characterized their approach more broadly as “fitness to purpose.” This term embraced ergonomics and anthropometry, defined as the science of designing for the ease and comfort of human use. Research-based design of this kind attempted to place the profession of industrial design within an empirical, “problem-solving” context and to enumerate criteria, beyond profits and sales, by which products might be judged successful and innovative. Such views shifted the designer’s role from that of mere “product stylist” whose intentions were subverted or compromised by fashion and commercialism to one whose focus was upon meeting specific human needs. Practitioners and advocates promoted the tenets or standards of modern international design as being in the public interest and distinct from the relativism implied by fashion and novelty. Shared standards and limits to the expression of individual will, whether in product design, graphic design, or political or ethical attitudes, were viewed as bulwarks against manipulation by advertisers or demagogues, especially in an age of mass communication and amid lingering fears of totalitarianism and propaganda. Indeed, mass media, whether as a vehicle for marketing products or for disseminating political views, permitted an unprecedented expansion of the ability of corporations and governments to influence public opinion. The power of totalitarian propaganda prior to and during World War II suggested that ethically responsible standards provided designers with a political role, even a mission, in shaping and strengthening shared cultural values and democratic ideals. As mentioned above, large and diverse corporations with interests in global markets and raw materials and resources (such as oil) were among the strongest advocates and clients for modern design: their headquarters, office interiors, products, organization, and promotional materials both expressed and shaped shared values of efficiency, rationalism, progress, dependability, responsibility, and public education. A coherent design policy, or “house style,” not only informed the look of products, but also bred familiarity and trust through a more generalized brand identity. It also reinforced loyalty and conformity among employees. At the same time, fears of the concentration of power in the hands of too few emerged in United States President Dwight Eisenhower’s farewell address in 1961, where he noted the connection between industrial technology and the arms race, and possible conflicts with the national interest. Post-war industrial design, promoted as “good design” or “Gute Form,” was effectively marketed through museum exhibitions seeking to raise awareness and increase the level of public discrimination, and through international events such as World’s Fairs and the Milan Triennale, the latter offering competitive awards. In the USSR and its proxies, modern industrial design also flourished following the death in 1953 of Soviet leader Joseph Stalin as the basis for low-cost housing, domestic furniture, and household products. Post-war modern design was also the subject of articles and illustrations in journals such as Domus, Industrial Design, and Design Quarterly, and was supported by organizations such as the Council of Industrial Design (COID) in Britain and its counterparts in Japan and Scandinavia. Also important were schools such as the Hfg (Hochschule für Gestaltung) at Ulm in West Germany and graphic design schools in Zurich and Basel. Yet for many consumers, the “good design” model was not understood as a universal set of standards that raised general cultural awareness or instilled responsible democratic values. Rather its products were considered a matter of taste, simply offering one among many possibilities for individual fulfillment in an age of increasing affluence and expanding leisure, a matter of “both ... and” rathe than “either ... or.” Perhaps seeking to escape the shortages and governmentimposed restrictions on materials and manufacturing during the war years, the American middle class in particular responded enthusiastically to constant novelty, fantasy, and instant gratification, as manufacturers and advertisers of household goods recognized a seemingly insatiable appetite for products or gadgets that promised to reduce housework and provide outlets for spending leisure time, acknowledging and celebrating social and cultural difference, an exercise in freedom of choice rather than directed by museum professionals and public intellectuals. Detroit-manufactured automobiles of the 1950s and their presence in Hollywood Technicolor films and on television most easily exemplify mass design after World War II, driving desire through the identification of materialism and consumer choice with freedom, leisure, luxury, power, and social mobility. Another medium that stimulated mass taste was popular magazines, whose images and product advertisements promised a better life for the American family and vicarious thrills for a generation of youthful consumers. Almost immediately such images were appropriated by the Pop Art movement in the United States, Britain, and elsewhere in Europe, whether as critique or celebration. The allure of mass culture for artists ranged from a desire to challenge abstraction with the accessibility and immediacy of popular imagery, to the pure visual excitement of bold aesthetics, to a recognition of the seductive power of idealized mass-media images of beauty and fulfillment supplied by commercial artists, photographers, and illustrators, masking persistent racial inequality, discrimination, and other barriers to social advancement for minorities. Post-war suburban housing communities were another part of the broader idealism embodied in mass design, a vision of life sometimes referred to as the American Dream. Many Americans moved from inner-city neighborhoods to newly created suburbs, where the availability of land for affordable single-family housing and incentives for purchasing new automobiles helped to define post-war lifestyles. Cheap gasoline prices, made possible by American-based corporate control of West Asian oilfields and refineries, made commuting both affordable and reasonably comfortable. At the same time, mass-produced methods of construction for detached housing, initiated in communities such as Levittown, New York, offered alternatives to city apartment living and were equipped with conveniences such as automatic washers, dryers, and modern kitchen appliances. For many Americans, individual home ownership brought with it the promise of leisure, green space, independence, security, and the freedom of personal mobility. The exodus from city to suburb only exacerbated racial inequality and deteriorating inner-city living conditions, addressed with massive public-housing projects with limited success. The emerging mass culture was criticized during and after World War II from a number of perspectives. Some commentators complained about consumers’ lack of discrimination and questioned the morality of conspicuous consumption in encouraging submission to manufactured ideals of beauty and health, and in which superficial differences and consumer choice masked an underlying conservatism and conformity. Others viewed mass culture as a form of social control reinforcing traditional family and gender roles of the white middle class. Still others dismissed popular culture as “kitsch,” debased mass-produced imitations of authentic culture standing at the lowest common denominator of taste, that is, of pure physical sensation renewed and manipulated by a commercial culture industry. In this view, culture is synonymous with leisure and entertainment rather than with more abiding values that contribute to well-being, enlightenment, self-realization, and active participation in a responsible democracy. And as noted above, race was yet another area of concern: many suburban communities and subdivisions either prohibited or discriminated against Black people, leading to civil rights protests and finally through legislation addressing forms of racial segregation in education and voting rights. Eventually the monolithic view of mass culture derided by social critics and educators crumbled, as it was too narrow to embrace a strain of vitality, popular expression, subversion, and irrationality found in comic books, film noir, tailfins, and rock ’n’ roll. The dualism between “good” design and mass taste, between high design and popular culture, reflects a limited viewpoint demanding a broader and more nuanced cultural perspective. The attitudes and artifacts of both approaches to design emerge as expressions of capitalist expansion, soft power as a means of persuasion in response to Cold War tensions that made consumer goods one of the battlegrounds of competing ideologies for material progress in a democratic society. As in Part IV, generalizations about design approaches or movements are often belied by looking more carefully at examples in relation to their local contexts. In sum, despite an acknowledged critical dichotomy between the new humanism and mass taste, both strains of post-war design coexisted, occasionally overlapped, and contributed to the identification between designed goods and an improvement of the social and material conditions of modern living. Whether conditioned by a new and broader set of criteria for a discriminating international audience or giving playful, inventive form to popular images of power, dissent, freedom, and luxury for the American middle class, design thrived in the climate of economic recovery and growth in the United States and abroad during the postwar period amid the emergence of new technologies, unprecedented increases in production, and escalating levels of consumption. In the Soviet Union a consumer culture emerged in the later 1950s as well, shifting from austerity and sacrifice in the pursuit of a collective work ethic to a focus upon material goods as barometers of an improved working-class standard of living, including kitchen appliances and entertainment in the form of radios, phonographs, and televisions. A focus upon leisure was even more apparent in non-aligned countries such as Yugoslavia who brokered a greater degree of independence from Moscow, sometimes referred to as “Second World” communism. In the United States, a number of modern industrial designers adapted new wartime materials and technologies to domestic goods: some products of this era continue to be manufactured today. In 1947, Earl Tupper (19071983) began to market Tupperware (founded in 1945), a line of flexible, translucent plastic storage and serving containers with self-sealing airtight lids to prevent spilling and preserve freshness (fig. 11.1). Tupperware utilized smooth, simple, often stackable forms in standardized sizes with interchangeable lids. After being sold initially as conventional store-bought items, Tupperware was available after 1951 exclusively through catalogs supplied by neighborhood vendors who demonstrated the products in the home. Such merchandising was essential to Tupperwares commercial success, identifying buyers with users rather than the less personal connection between buyer and salesperson, avoiding comparisons and competition with related shelf products. Tuppers marketing strategy reduced consumer resistance to industrial materials being used in the home rather than traditional ceramic or glass storage containers. His commercial success dated to the 1950s but was based upon his invention of a new pliable plastic called Poly-T in 1942, manufactured by DuPont and other chemical companies during World War II. This interest in harnessing wartime technologies and high-volume industrial production to improve domestic living through new, inexpensive, and practical products played a large role in shaping positive attitudes toward industrial design in the decade following the end of World War II. It also demonstrates synergy between chemical corporations and modern design, and encouraged further research and development of new materials and products that included synthetic fabrics that in turn demanded the services of designers. Chemical and pharmaceutical companies also employed graphic designers who helped to explain and promote scientific concepts and their useful applications in visual terms to the public and to the business community through popular and professional magazines as well as trade fairs. Philadelphia Museum of Art. ## Problem-Solving, Collaboration, and Marketing Industrial Design Consultant designer Henry Dreyfuss (see here) continued to emphasize, and to help define, research as a determinant in product housings for a variety of manufactured goods. For his redesigned combined handset for Bell Telephone in 1946, Dreyfuss replaced the units metal housing with lightweight plastic and a receiver whose form could be cradled between ear and shoulder, the result of trial-and-error testing, freeing the hand and creating a more natural interface between product and user (see fig. 10.26 for the earlier model of 1937). He was a staunch advocate for the study of measurement in the design process that would contribute to safety, and facilitate ease of use, efficient use of spaces, and the reduction of fatigue and injury whether at home or in the workplace (more recent examples of the danger of not considering such factors are lawsuits stemming from unstable IKEA dressers that were responsible for toddler deaths when they tipped over). Dreyfuss published his The Measure of Man: Human Factors in Design in 1960 (fig. 11.3), a manual of detailed drawings in which measurement rather than aesthetics or guesswork informs design decisions. Employing a series of average body sizes for men and for women that acknowledged different general types of users, The Measure of Man used observation, testing, and measurement to determine heights of tables, distances between operators and machines or appliances, and the form and placement of knobs and dials on devices and control panels for ease-of-operation, safety, and the reduction of error and injury. While still involving choice, access to such information reduces the range of options available to designers in the interests of consumer safety, whether mandated by designers themselves, manufacturers, or government regulation. ![](images/33e108454959497039560f36c6314fb7c38dd4a1b95a5cf5f621d35ba6190db5.jpg) Henry Dreyfuss, illustration from The Measure of Man: Human Factors in Design, height 13 in (33 cm), New York, 1960. Another iconic example of the Dreyfuss offices post-war industrial design is the round, wall-mounted thermostat manufactured for the Honeywell Corporation (fig. 11.4). Honeywell dominated the market for thermostats to regulate temperature in homes and offices, but the idea of a round control was attractive to the company from a marketing standpoint as it would differentiate the companys product visually from its competitors. Although company records indicate some interest in a round model from the early 1940s, the new model did not appear until 1953. Adapting the thermostat mechanisms to a round housing necessitated significant technical invention, credited to the company engineer Carl Kronmiller (18891968), who developed a spring coil to replace the conventional glass-enclosed mercury-filled thermometer that was difficult to manufacture in a curved form. While the costs of development were considerable, the final manufactured product was less expensive than earlier models. Dreyfuss liked the idea of the round control from the standpoint of mounting: that is, rectangular models almost always looked crooked when set on the wall. A further refinement that Dreyfuss developed was a concentric metal plate that easily snapped around the clear plastic readout and control. He was also responsible for suggesting that the concentric plate be manufactured in a variety of colors to match wall colors or blend in with wallpaper patterns. Dreyfusss name appeared frequently in advertising campaigns, but the development process of this product was a collaboration and began long before he was hired. Inexpensive, easy to operate, inconspicuous, and attractive in its compact concentric form, the Honeywell Round exemplified the advantages of post-war industrial design, and companies often promoted the integral and creative role of the industrial designer as a marketing tactic rather than a reflection of the complex nature of product development. ![](images/63a90f28e000fe137d36a7dcdb9ce3ae42ca74250cf14c07fbb521578c8b5867.jpg) Henry Dreyfuss, Honeywell round thermostat, plastic and painted aluminum housing, 3 in (7.6 cm) in diameter, manufactured by Honeywell Corporation, 1953. American designers were eager to demonstrate the advantages of modern materials and methods of production for furniture that contributed to a contemporary, efficient, and comfortable standard of living that accompanied the post-war boom in housing. Clients included corporations and businesses whose offices projected an image of modern efficiency and increasingly sophisticated taste. Original designs were manufactured and effectively promoted by the Herman Miller Company of Zeeland, Michigan (see fig. 10.29). Following Gilbert Rohdes untimely death in 1944, company president D. J. DePree hired architect and writer George Nelson (19081986) as chief designer. In turn, Nelson encouraged DePree to manufacture and market the furniture of contemporary designers, including Charles and Ray Eames. Charles Eames (1907 1978) studied architecture at Washington University in St. Louis and was hired as artistin-residence and instructor at the Cranbrook Academy of Art in Bloomfield Hills, Michigan, where he taught from 1938 until 1941. He met Ray (19121988) at Cranbrook, where she was a student in the weaving workshop, having first studied painting in New York. Charles Eamess design for a molded plywood chair, a collaboration with Eero Saarinen (19101961), won first prize at the Museum of Modern Art-sponsored competition for Organic Design in Home Furnishings in 1940 (see fig. 10.44). While working for the United States Navy during World War II, the Eameses experimented with plywood molding processes to develop lightweight, flexible splints to treat wounded soldiers. After the war they adapted the technology to the molded forms for seating. Unlike earlier plywood molding techniques used, for instance, by Alvar Aalto, the Eameses approach to molding was three-dimensional  what design historian Peter Dormer termed vessel and valley forms. In 1946, the Eameses designed a molded plywood chair with separate seat and back supported by a thin, welded tubular-steel frame and mounted with rubber shocks, eventually manufactured by Herman Miller (fig. 11.5). The couple continued to experiment with molded plywood and fiberglass in the later 1940s, and their unupholstered dining armchair of 1950, a single piece combining armrests, seat, and back, also mounted on a tubular-metal frame, won another international competition sponsored by the Museum of Modern Art for low-cost furniture, promoting economical solutions to meet the needs of returning veterans starting families with limited means. In 1954, Miller introduced an armless, stacking version of the fiberglass chair; it was widely adopted for seating in schools and other institutions by virtue of its light weight and ability to be easily stored and moved. Fueled by optimism stemming from faith in wartime technology, modern designers in the first post-war decade more expansively defined the relationship between their products and modern living standards, allowing for the self-expression of the designer and a projection of the owners taste. In addition to a continuous supply of new designs with advantages to the consumer in terms of comfort, lightness, efficiency, or beauty, marketing strategies focused upon the individual designer, who lent uniqueness to serially manufactured goods. Herman Miller, for instance, manufactured abstract sculptural designs by Japanese-American artist Isamu Noguchi (19041988), such as his glass table (fig. 11.7), supported by an ebonized wood base consisting of two irregular forms, suggestive of Automatic Surrealism. Although elements of interwar Modernisms egalitarian social ideology remained after World War II, it was the transformation of that ideology that often best exemplifies post-war modern design in the 1950s. This transformation produced a benevolent marriage of commerce and culture, in which an elevated taste merged with upper-middle-class economic prosperity. Like Dreyfuss, Teague established his reputation as a consultant industrial designer in the interwar period and was committed to a consideration of human factors and an understanding of manufacturing processes (see fig. 10.23). Teagues airline interior features recessed indirect lighting and curving molded plastic surfaces without projections or patterned decoration; such attributes met rigid industry safety standards and had a calming psychological effect, tested through the construction of a full-scale interior model to observe passenger reaction. Teague remained a vigorous spokesperson for design research that included user experience, publishing books as well as articles in the journal Industrial Design. Recessed surfaces, concave moldings around windows, and indirect interior lighting in commercial airliners remain legacies of Teagues approach. More recently headphones and tilting touchscreen personal movie screens on the backs of seats on longer flights provide added means of more comfortably occupying travelers during longer flights and delays. The same cannot be said of the industrys efforts to maximize profits by increasing the number of seats in coach (economy) class and of making flyers pay for added leg room. When American Airlines began to advertise more leg room in coach class in 2000, the company based its claim on the measurement (known as pitch) between the bolts that secure each row of seats to tracks on the floor of the aircraft. American had increased this measurement to 33 inches (83.8 cm) from the industry standard of 31 inches (78.7 cm). In 2016, 31 inches remained standard for most carriers, while low-cost carriers reduced pitch to 29 inches. By comparison, in his 1960 book The Measure of Man, Dreyfuss recommended the minimum pitch measurement for airline seating at between 34 and 35 inches (86 and 89 cm). Today airlines direct much of their design efforts toward the higher end of the market, with millions of dollars spent on first-class seating that allow seats to convert into beds, making the benefits of design research a luxury rather than a reasonable standard expectation. ![](images/4e3ad9194fb300be1da533a0bc1a1f376868b4ba087e588432ef50b32cef25a4.jpg) Walter Teague, full-scale model for interior, Boeing 707, published in Interior, 1955–1956. Promoting Post-War Design: Museums, Art Direction, and the New Advertising In 1946, the Museum of Modern Art organized a solo exhibition of the Eameses furniture designs. In 1949 the Detroit Institute of Arts also featured the Eameses work as well as that of other modern designers in an exhibition entitled For Modern Living. The furnishings for these exhibitions displayed characteristics of organicism and flexibility as well as new materials and technologies directed toward redefining the practical and aesthetic character of modern offices and domestic living spaces. In addition to the Museum of Modern Arts exhibition of Good Design and Useful Objects, the Walker Art Center in Minneapolis, Minnesota, installed an Idea House in 1947 outfitted with modern furniture and furnishings (fig. 11.14) and intended as a public service in the immediate post-war years to encourage new homeowners to adopt the style and the values of modern design rather than more commercially motivated alternatives. The museum avoided endorsing particular manufacturers or products, but encouraged consumers paternalistically to recognize and appreciate the advantages of modern industrial design in terms of quality, comfort, convenience, and the aesthetics of modern fine art. ![](images/9cbf855071bb4d53db2bd0b87ccf8c8c84507b2d0c88bfa75022cb5f1ea27c4a.jpg) “Idea House,” reconstruction of 1947 exhibition (2000), Walker Art Center, Minneapolis, Minnesota. Edgar Kaufmann, Jr. (19101989), who directed the MoMAs industrial design department, was responsible for curating and promoting the museums good design program. He was the author of a promotional booklet in 1950, and his advice recalls some of the prescriptions (and proscriptions) of design reform: modern design should fulfill the practical needs of modern life, express the spirit of our times, benefit by contemporary advances in the fine arts and pure sciences, take advantage of new materials and techniques and develop familiar ones, develop the forms, textures and colors that spring from the direct fulfillment of requirements in appropriate materials and techniques, express the purpose of an object, never making it seem to be what it is not, express the qualities and beauties of the materials used, never making the materials seem to be what they are not, express the methods used to make an object, not disguising mass production as handicraft or simulating a technique not used, blend the expression of utility, materials and process into a visually satisfactory whole, be simple, its structure evident in its appearance, avoiding extraneous enrichment, master the machine for the service of man, and serve as wide a public as possible, considering modest needs and limited costs no less challenging than the requirements of pomp and luxury. Modern designs provided interior settings for Hollywood films such as The Man in the Grey Flannel Suit (1956), and were visible in manufacturer showrooms and in department store windows in major cities across the United States. Post-war modern designs appeared in journals such as Art and Architecture, Industrial Design, and Architectural Digest, whose readers included the architects and interior designers who served as consultants for corporate, professional, and individual clients. Articles featuring modern interiors also appeared in mass-circulation magazines in the early 1950s, such as Better Homes and Gardens (fig. 11.15). Alternative voices in todays design community ask us to consider just who good design was good for, and remind us that the designers and manufacturers who promoted the approach represented a mostly privileged, Eurocentric, and white elite. ![](images/955a8926d47c55efcbee2e65dc720ce10db900dd2ea79341a7df069a87b807f4.jpg) Modern interior, from Better Homes and Gardens, July, 1953, vol. 31, no. 7, here (“Houses can be built for less money”). Advertisements for contemporary furniture and product designs were part of a marketing strategy known as the new advertising that favored photography over illustration and simple rather than narrative copy. Graphic designer Herbert Matter focused on the element of surprise to advertise the inventive pedestal support of Eero Saarinens Tulip chair for the Knoll Corporation on two successive pages (see fig. 11.12; the first page shows the pedestal with seat and back covered with brown wrapping paper). The advertisements ample white space and minimal copy were characteristic of the new advertising that emphasized bold and immediate visual impact with little or no copy, and the models direct confrontation with the viewer on the successive pages adds surprise and satisfies expectation. The New York-based Art Directors Club featured examples of such advertising in its yearly publications (the Annual of Advertising and Editorial Art and Design) that illustrated award-winning ads and other forms of promotion in a variety of categories. This professional focus emphasized creativity in the field with designers serving the needs of business, providing a stronger degree of independence and authority, touted in industry journals such as Printers Ink and Advertising Age. The new advertising owed much of its power to an interdependence between copy and image that actively engaged the viewer. Visual metaphors and puns informed the approach, as did inventive typography. The more widespread use of photographic printing processes (rather than letterpress) stimulated experiment and the free use of techniques that combined to create layouts from which flat printing plates could be produced. Newer agencies such as Doyle Dane Bernbach, founded in 1949, helped to introduce the more visually arresting approach to advertising, also seen in the work of Paul Rand, whose extensive work in advertising is sometimes overshadowed by his consultant work for corporations (see here). Rand worked for the Weintraub Agency in New York, the same office where William Bernbach worked as a copy editor before forming his own firm. Rands Thoughts on Design (1947) is a short but well-illustrated book on the new advertising, which discusses the enduring value of symbols, humor, and surprise to convey messages about products through a variety of pictorial means available to the designer. Intuition and experiment play a major role in the designers toolbox, and freshness of vision is both creative for the artist and persuasive, even educational, for the viewer. Rands quotations from philosopher and educator John Dewey (18591952), and his invocation of the integration of the beautiful and useful to describe advertising and other forms of visual communication, suggest a link to design reform, here directed against the outworn visual and narrative conventions of advertising illustration: Visual statements such as illustrations which do not involve esthetic judgment and which are merely literal descriptions can be neither intellectually stimulating nor visually distinctive.... The visual statement, on the other hand, which seeks to express the essence of an idea, and which is based on function, fantasy, and analytic judgment, is likely to be not only unique but meaningful and memorable as well. Rands 1946 magazine advertisement for Coronet Brandy (fig. 11.16) features a collage of cocktail glasses being effortlessly juggled by a waiter whose head is shaped like a brandy snifter. Rand explains that the dotted half-tone screen background suggests the effervescence of soda water used for mixed drinks. As an added element of surprise, Rands flat cut-out shapes contrast with a photograph of a flower pinned to the waiters lapel. Rands approach to visual communication stressed creative solutions to the presentation of products in which the artists originality and engagement of the viewers attention through surprise, symbol, and humor were compatible and satisfying to the ![](images/625a141351e1f5568d116e48614a6732f9afddfdb7fdc227692c250bb8436a00.jpg) Paul Rand, advertisement for Coronet Brandy, reproduced in Thoughts on Design, Wittenborn, New York, 1947. The element of surprise, relationships with components familiar from an exposure to contemporary fine art, and expressive use of typography also characterize progressive post-war graphic design for book covers and record album covers. Alvin Lustig (1915 1955) harnessed these vehicles in original designs using a wide variety of techniques for New Directions Publishing in New York. Lustig employed a wide range of formal means, including photography, color, texture, and typography to convey a visual equivalent to content. Whether using montage or abstraction, Lustigs book jackets, magazine covers, and album covers, like Rands advertisements, are suggestive rather than literal or illustrative in their integrated approach to the relationship of word and image. A book cover for Rimbauds A Season in Hell from 1945 uses an asymmetrical arrangement of amorphous shapes reminiscent of Joan Mirós paintings against a fiery red background (fig. 11.17). The theme of duality is suggested by the division of the shapes into equal areas of black and white. While the red background may suggest an inferno, the lettering with its small circles as serifs resembles the star-filled heavens. In an essay from 1954, Lustig characterized the artistic nature of the graphic designer: The role of the designer and his most important must is to remain free, as free as he possibly can, from the prejudices and the ruts which affect so many others in the field of design. He must be constantly on guard, cleansing his mind of the tendency to relax into a routine format, ready to experiment, play, change, and alter forms. If he lacks the inherent ability, the insight, the intuitive selection of what is right for his time, he will not go far and will eventually be forced to enter another field. If he is equipped with these essential characteristics, he will lead the way to new and more effective approaches to design in all its forms. Some of these characteristics are apparent in the art-directed magazines that circulated widely in American households after World War II. Dr M. F. Agha (see here) remained as art director for Vogue, Vanity Fair, and other Condé Nast publications during most of the war years. He was responsible for the modern, integrated approach to layout for feature articles on fashion, travel, health, and other activities characterizing modern living aimed at a discriminating readership. Aghas approach to art direction stressed experiment and intuition in design decisions regarding typography, photography, illustration, margins, color, and composition. ![](images/a6592e8376673a3d6c47c87dc01f27a1b227b7c73940822dd8c7b0748ac0f9aa.jpg) Alvin Lustig, cover for Rimbaud’s A Season in Hell, New Directions Publishing, New York, 1945. Such an approach demanded large staffs of copywriters and assistants, freelance photographers and illustrators, and also took advantage of the most up-to-date techniques for color reproduction and high-quality printing. Fashion or style magazines were large in format (Vogue and its rival Harper’s Bazaar measured 9¾ × 12¼ inches; 25 × 32 cm) and replete with color. Their covers used striking compositions of photography and inventive typography, while feature articles on fabrics or hair styling also used photography and color to advantage in conveying the vitality and excitement of contemporary life. Such qualities are also found in new or revamped publications that appeared during and after the war, such as Seventeen, Glamour, and Charm. Cipe Pineles (19081991) served as art director for Seventeen and Charm, after working under Aghas direction at Vogue in the 1930s. Pineles, editor-in-chief Helen Valentine, and promotion editor Estelle Ellis were unique as a team of women in the male-dominated publishing industry, and Pineles became the first woman named as a member of the Art Directors Club in New York. Seventeen acknowledged the particular tastes, interests, and buying power of young women in the United States as distinct from their adult parents, and was specifically geared, through the choice of models and the editorial pages, to both reflect and shape their experience. Fiction articles were illustrated in full color by artists including Ben Shahn, who was often given wide latitude in interpretation for his images. An often-repeated story involving creative freedom, related by Pineles, involves an illustration assignment for a short story: It [the story] concerned a 14-year-old boy, a keen tennis player, who is ashamed of his mother because she is very pregnant, and he is determined to keep this fact from his friends. To do this he keeps them from using the family tennis court, which up to the time of the pregnancy had been the center of social activity. I gave Ben a two-week deadline. He could do anything he pleased, in any shape and under any number of colors. There was only one restriction. The hero and his friends must be clearly recognizable as youngsters in their teens. Three days later the finished job came in and it was plenty clear. There was no hero. There were no friends to be seen. Instead, stretching across two pages in a long, thin picture, was the most deserted, clearest, biggest tennis court in a brilliant color, marked with the sharpest, neatest, traditional white lines. It was a breathtaking beautiful shock of a painting to go with that story. Layout for Seventeen was generally asymmetrical but less varied than at Vogue or Vanity Fair under Agha. Photographs rarely employed bleeding and were usually arranged with parallel vertical edges. Typographically there was more experimentation, both in the sizes used for titles and in occasional integration of drawing for emphasis or visual puns. An example of the latter is a feature that appeared in a 1951 issue of Seventeen on rainwear, where a sequence of models was juxtaposed with the black vertical lines of musical staves (rotated 90 degrees) that become a metaphor for rain (fig. 11.18). The musical metaphor helps to connect rainwear with fashion, suggesting that inclement weather need not be an impediment to being active and having fun. It anticipates the association between precipitation, fun, and romance in the 1952 MGM film musical Singin’ in the Rain. ![](images/e93607c68dc918526450abb5e020a9b2a411f5837aab45c9a2b070dc1ffabf37.jpg) Cipe Pineles (art director), feature layout for Seventeen, July 1949. Utilizing an increasingly broad array of techniques derived from modern art, elements of the New Typography, creative latitude, and technical mastery, advertising designers and magazine art directors brought visual excitement and intellectual sophistication to the presentation of products and to feature stories focused upon the appeal of modern living as revealed in travel, fashion, entertainment, and cuisine. Editorial features also addressed contemporary political and social issues and permitted considerable freedom of expression. Beginning in the early 1960s, Doyle Dane Bernbach initiated a series of poster advertisements for the tiled station walls of the Metropolitan Transportation Authority (MTA) in New York City in a campaign for Levys real Jewish rye bread with the tagline You dont have to be Jewish to love Levys real Jewish Rye. The posters included Black and Asian addressing figures, who were rarely represented in massmarket product ads (fig. 11.22). Generally manufacturers and advertising firms were reluctant to include racial and ethnic minorities in ads despite the potential of expanding their markets. The MTA poster campaign followed an earlier series of ads for the rye bread that featured working-class models eating rye bread sandwiches. The sense of surprise in the series derives from the frontal poses of the smiling child and juxtaposition of the word Jewish with a careful selection of an easily recognizable non-Jewish addressing figure. While this shift serves as another example of creative risktaking on the part of Doyle Dane Bernbach in rejecting the predictable whiteness that characterized mass advertising at the time, the campaign acknowledges industry recognition of an untapped market for products, one of the many economic and social benefits of inclusion. After the war, the clear visual presentation of technical information became a focus of Burtins long and distinguished career. His assignments covered a wide range of activities, from art direction for Fortune magazine (19451949) to design consultant for the Upjohn pharmaceutical company where his work also included the design of three-dimensional exhibits. Guiding Burtins varied projects was an abiding conviction that the increasingly complex and specialized scientific information upon which technological progress depended could be clearly and positively communicated to a broader public by graphic designers. Translating information into effective graphic design was an act of creative problem-solving, a form of public education, and an effective means to associate technology and big business with progress and improvement. Burtins success was testimony to his great powers of analysis and organization, as he searched for the underlying structure of complex processes. As art director both for Upjohn and its house journal, Scope, as well as for the monthly business magazine Fortune, Burtin frequently juxtaposed images that included diverse media, drawing with photography, color with black-and-white, precise line drawing with spontaneous brushwork. Cover designs were highly suggestive: juxtapositions between technical drawing, photography, and painting, with roots in Dada and Surrealist photomontage and collage in the interwar period, stimulated a creative, imaginative reading of medical research developments for an audience less familiar with particular technical knowledge of the field, while charts accompanying articles within the magazine clarified new research developments and their applications (fig. 11.23). Post-war designers such as Burtin and the Eameses saw themselves as contributing, through information design, to the shaping of shared beliefs and public confidence in broad cultural progress through technology within the framework of corporate capitalism and government investment, both nationally and internationally, as a component of soft power (see here). Burtin occasionally used the services of the Czechoslovakian designer Ladislav Sutnar (18971976) for the presentation of charts and other quantitative information. Sutnar had come to the United States to design and construct the Czech national pavilion for the 1939 New York Worlds Fair. When the Nazis invaded Czechoslovakia in the fall of 1938, plans for the exhibit had to be modified and Sutnar remained in the United States, eventually bringing his family to join him. Committed to the principles of the New Typography, Sutnar worked for Sweets Catalog Service, a company specializing in visual systems to communicate information for products primarily serving the architecture profession, building industry, and parts manufacturers. In books such as Catalog Design Progress (fig. 11.25), published in 1950, Sutnar and his collaborator K. Lönberg-Holm (18951972) created criteria for the graphic presentation of technical information based upon reduced use of text, visual clarity, and immediate impact. The authors cited instances from the transportation industry, such as road signs on highways, as examples of the necessity and importance of clarity and recognition as guiding principles of information graphics. They explained further in the introduction to their book that: To many people, standards mean only uniformity and restriction, something negative and static. Opposed to this concept of the word is one which may be illustrated by a commonly used expression, like living standards. This may suggest variation, as among the living standards of different parts of the world, or progress, as from the time of the earliest American settlers to the present. In short, the word has potential for implying something dynamic, not static  something which is always changing, advancing. Sutnar brought the same clarity and order to the creation of unified graphic standards for Carrs self-service department stores in New Jersey (19561957), and a system of easily recognized symbols for the Bell Telephone Company. Both projects are examples of the adoption of visual systems for identifying products and communicating a sense of organization through visual order and consistency. Whether through cover art, advertising, art direction, or information design, designers such as Burtin, Brodovitch, Agha, Rand, Sutnar, Bayer, and Lustig expanded the techniques and awareness of graphic design in a variety of contexts in the decade following the end of World War II. These ranged from product advertisements to magazine covers and layouts, to logos and house styles, technical manuals, and information design for exhibits and displays. Design projects used high-quality color reproduction of illustrations and photographs, and solutions proceeded with creative and technical freedom made possible through reduced reliance upon metal type. Large corporations and publishers helped to provide designers with a wealth of varied opportunities to develop original solutions to the challenges not only of selling products, but also of building public support for the promise of research and technology to a post-war audience. ## Scandinavia Nordic designers figured strongly in the expansion of post-war modern design through the manufacture of domestic and institutional furniture as well as glassware, metalwork, ceramics, and lighting. Designers and companies consistently won prizes at post-war triennial exhibitions held in Milan, Italy, were represented in the Museum of Modern Art and other museum collections, and their work was manufactured by or influenced the production of furniture companies such as Herman Miller and Knoll. The Nordic reputation for both new and traditional materials and efficient production technologies, and attention to comfort and fitness to purpose, included a respect for individual expression and national identity. The combination of variety and standardization preserves a balance between individual and socially responsible design considerations. The Egg chair and accompanying ottoman were designed for the Scandinavian Airlines System (SAS) hotel and terminal in Copenhagen. Jacobsen also served as the architect for these buildings, and designed their full range of furnishings from carpeting to cutlery. The organic forms of the Egg chair, with its deep cavities and winglike projections, go far beyond considerations of ergonomics and the psychology of comfort, recalling the abstract sculpture of Henry Moore or Jean Arp (see fig. 11.8). Jacobsens designs are an intersection of technology and art rather than technology as a vehicle for economic production. In addition to furniture, Scandinavian industrial design included other products with strong craft traditions in glassware, ceramics, textiles, and metal tableware. In Nordic glassware and ceramics, a more practical, restrained expression in the postwar period continued with the work of Finnish designer Kaj Franck (19111989). Franck served as artistic director for the Arabia Company in Helsinki, for whom he designed the Kilta earthenware table service beginning in 1952 (fig. 11.30). This tableware exhibits a preference for slightly tapered cylindrical forms in a small range of colors (originally white, black, yellow, and green) with undecorated surfaces. Handles and knobs are equally simple and unobtrusive, and the consistency of these elements surpasses in its purity and unity the equally utilitarian Fiestaware (see fig. 10.30), whose pieces retain molded and scalloped handles and bases. ![](images/2ff6383cbb2b7dd8b1af447542beec11cc80b508afffeaa4ed1c49e9d3460cb3.jpg) Kaj Franck, Kilta table service, glazed earthenware, diameter of largest plate 9 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>8</sub> in (23 cm), manufactured by Wärtsilä Arabia Company, Helsinki, Finland, 1952 In lighting, the hanging lamps of Poul Henningsen (18941967), manufactured by the Louis Poulsen Company in Copenhagen as early as 1926 (exhibited even earlier at the 1925 international exposition in Paris), combine simple curved geometric forms in concentric and inverted arrangements to evenly diffuse interior light (fig. 11.31). After World War II, Henningsen expanded the vocabulary of elementary geometric components so characteristic of interwar Modernism into more intricate and sculptural designs, such as the Artichoke hanging lamp of 1958 (fig. 11.32). The transformation is characteristic of an increasing artistic freedom in Nordic design, seen as part of an ideology stressing comfortable living as a refuge from a cold climate, long distances between major cities, and significant time spent indoors. ![](images/05431e7df9563b82649d49741a7351cf4cf42888aa9c3847361a19c96e5051fa.jpg) 11.31 Poul Henningsen, hanging lamp, opal glass and brass, diameter $1 9 \%$ in (50 cm), Louis Poulsen Company, Copenhagen, 1926. 11.32 Poul Henningsen, Artichoke hanging lamp, copper, height 27 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>8</sub> in (69 cm), diameter 33 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>8</sub> in (84 cm), Louis Poulsen ![](images/6966a9f92da93c5e1c9a6116f5af2a6da37b7fa7d1f331a04611898110aa0cb2.jpg) Company, Copenhagen, 1958. Qualities of comfort combined with fitness to purpose may also be seen in Scandinavian industrial design, particularly in the Saab 92 automobile manufactured in 19501952 by Svenska Aeroplan Aktiebolaget Company in Linköping, Sweden. As a consultant to the airline company that produced the original model, Swedish industrial designer Sixton Sason (19121967) contributed to the Saab 92 (fig. 11.33). With its swept-back hood and tapered body, the Saab is streamlined and strongly unified in appearance, and less geometric in form than the German Volkswagen. Perhaps owing to the aviation orientation of the company that produced it, the gauges and controls on the Saab 92 and Saab 93 were placed to facilitate easy recognition and operation. As a result of enabling drivers to operate controls intuitively and integrating an aerodynamic approach to the form of the body, these early models underwent few changes, remaining in production for almost 30 years. The design of the dashboard and controls for the Saab 92 provides a model for later interest in user-centered design that incorporates a natural relationship between controls, the functions they perform, and the ease with which those functions and movements can be understood and effected by those who operate them. These themes emerged in books by Donald Norman such as The Design of Everyday Things, published in 1988. The Saab represented an alternative to the American automobile industry and its focus upon visual appeal, annual model changes, power, individual identity, and leisure. ![](images/01f860ad0022cb3ff174d3906e13f65be7d9d2052625ac62abe46088773c0046.jpg) 11.33 Saab 92 automobile, Svenska Aeroplan Aktiebolaget Company, Linköping, Sweden, 1950–1952. ## Post-War Modern Design for Housing Flexibility remained a principle of Nordic design. For instance, when designer and critic George Nelson praised the virtues of Scandinavian design in housing, he also cited the efficiency of design planning for open, multi-purpose apartments maximizing interior space with built-in storage and modular cabinets and seating arrangements, neither austere nor indulgent. According to Nelson, such an approach extended to the provision of social services for childcare and emphasis upon how a sense of community reduced the need for individual families to own large homes or purchase products to satisfy every need. As in other western European and Nordic nations after World War II, social democratic governments taxed citizens to provide funding for a social safety net to protect the unemployed, provide healthcare, guard against social unrest, and regulate the 11.34 Le Corbusier, interior, Unité d’Habitation, Marseilles, 1951. distribution of wealth, sometimes referred to as welfare capitalism. This approach may be seen in the apartment complex known as the Unité dHabitation built in Marseilles (France) and designed by Le Corbusier between 1947 and 1952. The project was funded by the French Ministry of Reconstruction and Urbanism and aimed to provide housing for civil servants in the local region. The complex is constructed with rough reinforced concrete and rests upon stubby concrete pilotis, with 17 stories and 337 apartments housing around 1,600 inhabitants. Primary-color balcony panels in an asymmetrical pattern enliven the design of the exterior, and the design included several typical floor plans. Individual apartments, while modest, consist of two floors, with an open staircase connecting them along with balconies (fig. 11.34). Le Corbusier tried to incorporate the various elements of modern family living: privacy and common social spaces, a kitchen that opens onto the dining area, a loggia, and communal areas for exercise, play, shopping, a hotel, school; in sum, a vertical city. The built-in furniture in the illustration and the color scheme appear to require some conformity from residents, hardly allowing for heirlooms or shopping for knick-knacks aside from books and prints to hang on walls. Something of the rejection of the past remains in the period after World War II in the Unité dHabitation, along with the urgency of meeting the challenges of housing and material shortages on a large scale, requiring standardization to achieve an egalitarian ideal for modern, modest responsible living. ![](images/614bc035eada4f29654b0f95067b8def6c37a8d1704f3f49817283e6665a6d69.jpg) ## Britain A gradual shift in modern industrial design from standardized and low-cost solutions toward more individual interpretations of Modernism may also be seen in Britain following World War II. Between the wars, many British industrial designers followed a utilitarian and economical approach, seen, for instance, in the robot-like Anglepoise desk lamp designed by George Carwardine (18871948) in 1932 (fig. 11.35). The exposure of the lamps mechanical parts suggests a relation to technical equipment rather than domestic furnishings. Following the war, lingering austerity stemming from material shortages and rationing (not lifted until 1952) contributed to an economical approach to design at the expense of aesthetic considerations. The liberal Labour Party government brought to power in 1945 instituted nationalized services, including healthcare and subsidized housing estates built to meet minimum working-class needs. The legacy of Modernism was strong, favoring standardization, industrial materials and production, and modest modern living. Government regulation required that furniture designs be approved before they could be manufactured on the basis of economy and a sense of aesthetic discrimination along restrained guidelines, as seen in photographs of a domestic interior from 1945 (see fig. 10.46). Such guidelines formed the basis of the 1946 exhibition Britain Can Make It. Such narrow parameters for post-war design in Britain broadened through the later 1940s and began to incorporate the heritage of craft traditions and natural materials to give products a distinctively national identity in the hope of competing in the export market and appealing to popular taste. At the same time, government-sponsored design initiatives such as the Design Research Group emphasized testing and research to encourage manufacturers to hire industrial designers, while department stores such as Heals offered modern furniture designs inspired by Scandinavian (chiefly Danish) models, focusing upon ease of assembly, lightness, industrial materials, and plain wooden components. British examples of postwar modern design include Ernest Races (19131963) Antelope chair featuring the use of enameled metal wire (fig. 11.36). The chair was exhibited at the 1951 Festival of Britain. The timing of the Festival sounded a note of optimism, commemorating the 100th anniversary of the Great Exhibition of 1851 and Britains Victorian past (see here). Also in Britain the legacy of craft and values associated with the dialogue between artisan and materials found expression in the oeuvre of Bernard Leach (18871979). Leach practiced the technique of raku ceramics, fired at lower temperatures and modeled rather than thrown on a wheel. Until 1948 communists were the political majority in Italy; the Christian Democratic Party gained control of Italys Parliament in elections that year, and sought to address pressing needs for housing, high levels of unemployment, and increasing industrial output. One of the most potent symbols of progress allied with industrial design and production to emerge in the immediate post-war years was the Vespa motor scooter, designed in 1946 by Corradino DAscanio (18911981) for the Piaggio Corporation (fig. 11.43) located in the Tuscan town of Pontedero. The Vespa contributed to overall growth in industrial output in Italy (averaging 6.3 percent per year beginning in 1945). It was a practical, inexpensive mode of personal transportation, but streamlined housing, enclosed mechanical parts, and the cutaway profile of the seat added a graceful, expressive element to the design, communicating mobility, spontaneity, and personal freedom in relation to economic reconstruction. Other standardized and affordable industrially manufactured products, for instance the compact Fiat 600 (1955) designed by Dante Giacosa, aligned with utilitarian and practical considerations in both economical production and reliability, providing four-wheel transportation and contributing to a tremendous domestic increase in automobile sales, estimated at 400 percent between 1950 and 1961 (fig. 11.44). The independence and contributions of the consultant designer is an acknowledged feature in Italian industrial manufacturing for corporations such as Olivetti after World War II. Industrial designers were integral to a process that included marketing and engineering. The dialogue among participants was often distinguished by a sense of drama and conflict rather than hierarchical directive. As described by Sybil Kircherer in her study of Olivetti, the strategy resulted in creativity and a dynamic design identity. Although he had been hired in the later 1930s by Adriano Olivetti, son of the companys founder, painter and architect Marcello Nizzoli (18871969) assumed a more significant role in product design after World War II. Contemporary with American and Nordic industrial designers, Italy helped to define the new humanism of the post-war era, embracing mechanized mass production and new materials technology while establishing a forceful role for the artist-designer within industry in the creation of useful, elegant products and an acknowledged aesthetic and individual creative element. Through periodicals such as Domus, the Milan Triennale and other exhibitions, Italian design achieved visibility and national identity within an expanding international market for consumer goods, and as commerce in consumer goods and services became a benchmark for economic progress. Italian designers explored the creative use of modern materials and technology, individual expression parallel to modern abstract organic sculpture, and modern reinterpretations of enduring craft traditions. With the help of the Marshall Plan, design organizations, education, and the initiatives of companies such as Olivetti, post-war Italy exemplified designs role in expanding industrial production, rebuilding national economies, defining a modern, consumer-oriented lifestyle, and creating an aesthetically sophisticated and industrially progressive image of Italian design in the two decades following the end of World War II, widely perceived as the nations Second Renaissance. American travel magazine Holiday devoted its April 1955 issue to Italy, combining photographs of the iconic treasures of Ancient Rome and fifteenth-century Florence, the nations craft traditions and opportunities for shopping, and a vibrant modern film industry, recommending the joy of total relaxation, sunshine and balm, brilliant colors, constant, pleasantly distracting to and fro of Italys idlest hours, often focusing upon an active, youthful lifestyle. This character is also captured in the posters, packaging, and window displays for the Milan-based La Rinascente department store designed by Max Huber (fig. 11.55). Hubers poster creates a playful, visually active typographic composition out of the letters l and R. In addition to abrupt scale changes, overlapping, and inversions that recall the bold use of typography by the Italian Futurists (see here), Huber uses a combination of the sans serif R in Futura Bold with the more elegant (and Italian) Bodoni lower-case l. According to graphic design historian Richard Hollis, the juxtaposition of the two typefaces may symbolize Italys position between northern and Mediterranean cultural traditions. This progressive image masked long-standing disparities in Italy that remained in the distribution of wealth and in cultural differences between the industrialized north (centered in Milan) and the more agricultural south that fueled a politically charged anti-design movement in the 1960s, critical of its exclusivity and the capitalist economic system. ![](images/c43d5597cc651ccfd193e45cab201af943ce7b1434d3f0f48cb29d6ca531b3c6.jpg) Max Huber, advertisement for La Rinascente department store, Milan, c. 1954–1958. M.A.X. Museum, Switzerland. ## Germany Design played a large role in the economic reconstruction of West Germany following World War II. German industrially manufactured products demonstrate a kinship with the humanizing tendencies expressed through organic forms molded from modern materials for some hand-held products and compact kitchen appliances, but more typically German industrial design is associated with the systematic investigation of materials and processes, most often identified with the grid, modularity, and unobtrusiveness, blending in with the rectilinear forms of much post-war modern architecture. Precision and functional simplicity were factors in the international success of electrical appliances manufactured by the Braun Corporation of Frankfurt, founded in 1921 and rebuilt in 1945. The company collaborated with the newly founded Hochschule für Gestaltung (Design School) at Ulm (1953) through Fritz Eichler (19111991), a member of the schools board of directors. Swiss-born Max Bill (19081994), the Hochschules first director, was a graduate of the Bauhaus and sought to revive an approach to industrial design that emphasized studio practice and social responsibility in the design of prototypes for simple, practical, and efficiently produced furniture and objects of everyday use to improve the general standard of living in an increasingly technological age, and that equated industrial technology with simplicity of appearance and function along interwar Modernist lines. In many ways Bills approach to industrial design shared many elements not only with the Dessau Bauhaus but also with the stable, enduring product types debated at the German Werkbund in the early twentieth century. This approach, under the label Gute Form, was the subject of exhibitions organized by Bill beginning in 1949 in Switzerland, which traveled in Germany and Austria during the 1950s. Bills wall-mounted kitchen clock with timer (fig. 11.56) made of glass, ceramic, and plastic is an example of Gute Form: self-contained, free of decoration or sculpturally molded housing, and contributing to a wholesome but restrained modern lifestyle in the industrial age. Corporations such as Braun and the Design School at Ulm tended to limit the role of individual expression and embraced a belief in research-based design solutions linked to performance and fitness to purpose. At the same time the establishment of a free-market-based international economy in Germany also played a role in promoting a national identity for German products, embracing pan-European cooperation as envisioned under the Marshall Plan. All of these efforts were aided as well by the formation of a German Design Council in 1949 to stimulate industrial production, economic recovery, and the restoration of consumer confidence. Between lingering animosities toward Germany among the allied countries and the nations division into zones occupied by the USSR, the USA, Britain, and France, the German economy was slow to develop, but the rebuilding of German industry was essential to overall European recovery, and levels of production rose quickly after 1949. In that same year, the Russian zone was renamed the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG). During the 1950s the West German economy led Europe in industrial production, mostly in the export of manufactured products. While Italy might tout its own Second Renaissance, French political economist Jean Monnet (18881979) declared that Germany was the prize of Europe, a source of optimism in recovery and growth. ![](images/5d0139a6033942abfb1befb9bbd146d0c09b3fb1daa562ee74129dd42573e7fe.jpg) Max Bill (and Ernst Moecki), kitchen clock with timer, metal, glass, ceramic, and plastic, 10 ${ } ^ { 1 } / _ { 1 6 } \times 1 1 / { } ^ { 7 } / _ { 8 } \times 2 { } ^ { 1 } / _ { 2 } \mathrm { i n } ,$ manufactured by Gebrüder Junghans, Schramberg, Germany, 1956–1957. The SK4 radio-phonograph was designed by Max Braun, Rams, and Gugelot in 1956. Nicknamed Snow Whites Coffin (fig. 11.57), the SK4 is a long rectangular box with hinged transparent plastic cover, housing controls, speaker grille, and other moving parts (tone-arm and turntable). All visible parts are reduced to circular or rectangular forms placed against smooth and flat surfaces. The arrangement of parts is controlled by a rectangular grid system. The aesthetic purity and systematic approach to Braun design retain a relationship with the understated geometric abstraction of the period, seen for instance in Josef Albers series of paintings entitled Homage to the Square, or to Max Bills unexecuted project for a Monument for the Unknown Political Prisoner in Stuttgart in 1952, consisting of a connected series of boxlike structures set amid benches, far removed from traditional monumental heroic figural imagery. Such examples declared a belief in art as a life-affirming activity in the wake of despair and destruction during World War II. Just as these minimalist approaches to fine art seem to demand a sophisticated and educated audience, so Braun products and design policy were not directed toward a mass market but rather to a discriminating clientele with a more conscious awareness of abstract aesthetics and a belief in the connection between simple, undecorated forms and responsible, efficient modern living, rebuilding the German nation economically, socially, and spiritually. Braun products emphasized enduring forms and determinants for design based upon considerations of production technology and fitness to purpose rather than upon rapid style change and obsolescence. In addition, products such as the Kitchen Machine of 1957 (fig. 11.58) possessed a sense of restraint, precision, and laboratory-like efficiency expressed through smooth white surfaces, qualities mentioned above in relation to contemporary abstract fine art and seen as well in the cool impersonality of buildings such as the Lever House (see fig. 11.10) or Mies van der Rohes Seagram Building of 19541958. This approach to architecture dominated large public and corporate building projects in the decades after World War II and was equally praised at the time for its beauty, efficiency, modularity, and industrial methods of construction. A devotee of jazz music (oddly in a sense, since the improvisational nature of jazz seems at odds with the predictability of the grid), Dieter Rams designed housings for the majority of Brauns compact stereo equipment, including radios, televisions, speakers, and other components. The more softly molded forms for kitchen appliances such as the Kitchen Machine were designed by Ramss associate at Braun, Gerd Alfred Müller (1932 1991). Brauns design program also reveals an affinity with corporate identity and branding as a commercial strategy in international business: company house styles familiarized consumers with families of products and with logos and symbols that provided immediate recognition and differentiation among competitors in a capitalist free enterprise and increasingly international markets. They also provided parameters and constraints for designers that managed change and emphasized design practice in the context of corporate communication and policy. Designers not only gave form to products but also created an association between products and an image of efficient, technologically modern living. Some designers criticized Gute Form for its failure to acknowledge diversity and playfulness within the German national heritage, but the approach helped to build an international reputation for German consumer goods and contributed to unprecedented economic growth after World War II. ![](images/187221725c221fd50fb3ad39498b3afa9201bff627e5f2da50e021cd1f54a38f.jpg) 501 limousine, manufactured by BMW, AG Bayerische Motorenwerke, Munich, 1951. In Germany, the EastWest division was marked by the building of the Berlin wall in 1961, a visible reminder of EastWest tensions emerging after World War II and known as the Cold War. Like their western European counterparts, eastern bloc countries were eager to address severe housing and material shortages along with unemployment after the war, at least in part with a focus upon urbanization and industrialization as means to stimulate the economy and improve working-class living standards along socialist lines, with state rather than private ownership of production and ambitious multi-year plans with goals for technological progress and growth along both domestic and military fronts. Attitudes toward design were not monolithic; they varied in time and location within the Soviet bloc. Until the death of Soviet leader Joseph Stalin in 1953 architects and designers adopted vernacular and traditional rather than Modernist industrial approaches to architecture and design, with party officials labeling international modern design as formalism, lacking in character and eschewing national traditions through its rejection of decoration and historicism, even employing terms such as primitive and degenerate at a conference held in east Berlin in 1953. Grandiose urban redevelopment projects, such as the Stalinallee in Berlin (19531958), featured monumental government office buildings, department stores, a movie theater, bookstore, cafés, and spacious apartments in an updated and grand Neoclassical style, but rents and household furnishings during this time were beyond the means of laborers and out of touch with the scale of production required to meet urgent housing and material needs. During its construction the project was the site of worker demonstrations for higher wages and better working conditions that prompted military and police intervention. While criticized officially in the GDR (East Germany), however, interwar manufacturers of international modern furniture such as the Hellerau Workshops in Dresden found favor with the public, and under Stalins successor, Nikita Khrushchev (18941971), the official Soviet position on design showed more tolerance for standardized assembly, industrial materials such as steel and plywood, and a clean, pared-down aesthetic, succumbing to pressure to compete with the West in improving living standards as presented at international exhibitions and gaining ground in capitalist countries and colonial territories. An example is a set of economical stacking plastic dishes offered in varied pastel colors, illustrated in figure 11.61, designed by Albert Krause in 1959. Despite differences in the political and economic systems in which they were manufactured, goods on both sides of the Berlin wall and on both sides of the Iron Curtain might convey similar meanings in demonstrating broad access to modern, durable, industrially designed goods in a democratic society. Social housing in the Soviet bloc during the 1950s resembled high-rise housing estates in West Germany, Britain, France, and elsewhere, built with rough concrete or using steel and glass construction, and open, flexible plans, adopting the efficiencies of labor and material costs through prefabrication, a greater or lesser degree of uniformity, and the elimination of decorative detail. ![](images/b8cc5e76ccc8148bb4f6090f2e7713558e3b58aa61e5d69f8319997ab6589a99.jpg) 11.61 Albert Krause, set of dishes, plastic, long dishes 9 <sup>1</sup>/ × 5 <sup>3</sup>/ in (23.5 × 13.9 cm); square bowls 4 × 4 in (10.2 × 10.2 cm), manufactured by VEB Plast-Werke, Sonneberg, East Germany, 1959. Consumer products were known in the Soviet government as kulttovary, roughly translated as cultural goods, affordable home products that enhanced everyday living in relation to household chores and leisure. Musical instruments, sports equipment, radios, cameras, and phonographs are examples of kulttovary, and making them available provided an opportunity to bolster confidence in the Soviet economic system and communicate shared egalitarian cultural values. An example is the standardized Moskvich radio (c. 19491957), made of Bakelite (resembling woodgrain) with rounded, streamlined edges reminiscent of American models of the 1940s (fig. 11.62), and whose manufacturing specifications were followed by several manufacturers throughout the Soviet Union. Exposure through exhibitions and travel familiarized Soviets with Western design and by the later 1950s the Soviet government touted its own kitchen appliances to compete with American and western European products, an approach dubbed refrigerator socialism. While institutes were established to create prototypes for such goods, and while designers visited the West for inspiration and models of how design functioned in industry, it was difficult for Soviet bloc countries to compete with free-market capitalism in the area of consumer products without adopting many of its forms and strategies, including an increasing emphasis upon marketing and advertising. And while Cold War competition existed in the realm of consumer products, political and media attention tended to focus more upon competition in the fields of space exploration, military strength, and athletic performance at the Olympic Games. At the same time, party leaders continued to support collective rather than individual need and socialized rather than private services. As an example of this position, speaking in response to an exhibition of American automobiles in France in 1960, Khrushchev proposed that the Soviet Union would develop a national system of rental cars to serve the needs of the Russian public rather than face the problems of too many cars caused by private ownership. At the 1958 Worlds Fair in Brussels, the USSR pavilion proudly displayed its Sputnik satellite as a measure of technological superiority to the United States, raising fears in the West of military advantage should the Cold War become hot. ![](images/058e2e7d5a43a48bd9a2c894b7c89480ecb5bd85247e5c4fbc3b72f7c88ecdd3.jpg) Alexey Lavrov, “Moskvich” Three-Tube Radio, 1950. Since around the second decade of the twenty-first century historians have revealed the diversity in architecture and design within the Soviet Union and especially its proxy states, including the independence exerted in Yugoslavia that extended to consumer products, fashion, and leisure. Under Marshal Josip Broz Tito (18921980), Yugoslavia benefitted from a growing tourism industry, visits from celebrities, and an embrace of modern fashion. Industrial furniture designer Niko Kralj (19202013) introduced tubular steel and molded plywood furniture for the 1956 stacking chair illustrated in figure 11.63, meant to attract residents who moved to the high-rise apartment blocks being built in Belgrade, Zagreb, and elsewhere during the decade. Rather than reject the comforts of time and labor-saving appliances and materialism, the USSR vowed to equal the American industrial output and wave good-bye as they surpassed the West. Whether by state ownership and controlled distribution or private enterprise and competition, both political and economic systems judged progress by the dynamic of production and consumption and their relation to the everyday lives of their citizens. This represented a paradox, as materialism seemed to conflict with collective values and sacrifice to a centralized state that accommodated their needs in exchange for compliance and conformity. And in both systems, on both sides of the Cold War, attitudes toward consumption were a mixture of attraction and skepticism. As seen in chapter 12 (see here onward), consumption and materialism might signal productivity, social progress and individual identity, the rewards of hard work and the cultivation of refined leisure or the saving of time and labor; at the same time, luxury goods might exacerbate class difference and inequality or manipulate rather than satisfy desire as a result of planned obsolescence and aggressive advertising. ## The International Typographic Style (Die Neue Grafik) Also connected with Germany as well as Switzerland in the post-war period were standards for graphic design and typography often referred to as the International Typographic Style or Die Neue Grafik. This approach to graphic communication for book design, posters, advertising, logos, and other business and organizational needs was promoted in such journals as Graphis and Die Neue Grafik, and formed the basis for educating graphic designers at the Hochschule für Gestaltung at Ulm and the Kunstgewerbeschule in Basel. The combination of theoretical principles, the exploration of the visual components of graphic expression, and the use of those components for effective communication in an international context gave the movement a reputation for high professional practice standards and ethical responsibility in design. The hegemony of the International Typographic Style as the voice of modern graphic design was promoted in the widespread adoption of corporate identity systems for business, museum exhibitions, and book design, and was contemporary with a similar faith in the modern aesthetic and practical advantages of the tectonic steel-and-glass architecture of the post-war International Style. Max Bill (see here), who lived and worked in Switzerland during and after World War II before accepting the directorship at the Hochschule für Gestaltung at Ulm, contributed to the development of the International Typographic Style. Along with several Swiss and other European artists and designers, Bill developed and worked within a framework of formal and ideological principles for graphic communication. These designers consciously moved away from illustration, were committed to photomechanical reproduction, and adopted a carefully considered and judicious use of photography and color. Their shared interest was to communicate ideas effectively with simple, reductive means. At the foundation of their approach were an acknowledgment of the increasing complexity, fragmentation, and interdependence of knowledge, and a mistrust of the growing sophistication of the mechanical reproduction of images used to manipulate the public in mass advertising. Bills posters show a mastery of Jan Tschicholds New Typography (see here), gained during the years he spent as a student at the Dessau Bauhaus from 1927 to 1929. Allied with a commitment to modern, clear communication of ideas visually and simply, the abstract geometric relationships found in his exhibition posters and related print production also stem from an approach to abstract art known as lart concret that emphasized imagination and creativity. Bills 1945 poster for an exhibition of architecture (USA baut) presents a coordinated approach to abstract composition including photographic and typographic elements (fig. 11.64). The poster is composed of nineteen diamond shapes of five different sizes, each containing photographic views of contemporary American buildings, urban complexes, and highways. The lower-case sans serif letterforms (Akzidenz Grotesk) and asymmetrical layout owe much to the principles of the New Typography, as does the careful attention to the relative weight and hierarchy of information. Bill balances, for instance, the two larger diamonds right of center with the USA baut lettering to the left that gains visual strength from being isolated against a large area of white space. Beyond Bills interest in eye movement as a basis for poster design, in this example the design is rich in other dimensions of visual communication: the blue and red tints applied to the photographs, along with the activation of white space, are, of course, the colors of the American flag, while the active arrangement of diamond shapes recalls the gridlike geometry of urban streets, intersections, and traffic as symbolized in Piet Mondrians late Boogie Woogie series of paintings, completed in the early 1940s while the artist lived in Manhattan. ![](images/deeeff8609b138c064a43757d0616abe5b4910f6efe730124ede25ec51ec511c.jpg) Max Bill, USA Baut exhibition poster, offset lithograph, 50 <sup>5</sup>/<sub>16</sub> × 35 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>2</sub> in (127.8 × 90.2 cm), 1945. One of the main features of the International Typographic Style was the use of a grid system to govern layouts, subordinating all individual typographic elements to a system that maintained unity and governed relationships between those elements. The grid utilized modular shapes as units of measurement to establish relationships for a hierarchy of information, and designers likened its standardized units to those of the prefabricated units and industrial standards of modern architecture. Visual symbols also played an important role in the International Typographic movement. Armin Hofmann (19202020) and Anton Stankowski (19061998) are but two among a group of artists who used simple shapes together with sans serif typography to convey complex or mundane information with striking clarity and elegance. Swiss graphic designer and educator Hofmann was responsible for extensive series of posters for museums and other arts organizations in Switzerland, and used typography to convey the essence of a subject with utmost economy. His work reveals how much may be accomplished with minimal means, seen in the Gute Form exhibition poster of 1954 (fig. 11.65): the closely spaced, white, monoweight sans serif letterforms appear against the black ground as interchangeable mechanical parts. The viewer supplies each letters missing parts, becoming an active participant as if being rewarded for solving a puzzle, while the words become an image of a clean, economical functional aesthetic. ![](images/d53aaecc9518b7424e01f018d31e84059be47ab0e90138de5131c61eda817d99.jpg) Armin Hofmann, Gute Form exhibition poster, offset lithograph, 35.4 × 11 in (90 × 28 cm), 1954. Museum of Design, Zurich. Poster collection. Cultural institutions were often the patrons of the Neue Grafik; dance, theater, film, and art contributed to the quality of life beyond the satisfaction of basic material needs, and fueled the shared optimism of both graphic and industrial designers. Moreover, journals such as Neue Grafik and Graphis promoted the democratic social and political benefits of design and the arts rather than their association with commercial advertising or elite leisure. In a remarkable series of posters, Anton Stankowski demonstrated how complex technological processes might be simply and effectively communicated. This is seen in the diagram illustrating the chemical transfer of heat using a series of broken undulating lines that change color from cool blue to warm red as they cross a barrier indicated by a heavy red vertical line (fig. 11.66). Stankowskis exploration of reductive abstract form in relation to the design of information recalls Isotype as well as the earlier and more intuitive research of Kandinsky to determine a systematic psychology of color while teaching in Russia in the early 1920s (see here). Both the artist and the designer shared an interest in research and experiment as part of the creative process, as well as an inclination to see abstract form as being universal in nature, but whereas Kandinsky used abstraction to communicate and liberate emotion in his paintings and prints, the focus of Neue Grafik designers was the creation or shaping of a better-informed public. Designers such as Stankowski and Otl Aicher (see here) used their skills to forge a trust between the public and the corporate or government clients who contracted for their services. By the 1960s (and even before) in the debates that took place at Ulm and elsewhere, the bonds and trust between business, government, and design were beginning to fray (see here onward). ![](images/f2d51860afd6279e184cb2f90274428c82c765136abf1c4552f3b17e0948c30d.jpg) Anton Stankowski, diagram for the process of heat transfer, page from a calendar for Viessmann, 1957–1958. The grid system and basic sans serif type families were well suited to the consistent presentation of information for corporations, disseminated in house-style manuals to ensure conformity to standards and identity. An example is Stankowskis public graphics for the city of Berlin, all of which incorporated a thin horizontal line divided by a vertical line to symbolize the citys division into East and West in 1947 and the wall that was built in 1961 and remained in place until 1989. The interest of large municipal organizations and multinational corporations in visual systems for products and publications during the post-war period created broad support for the International Typographic Style. Clean and recognizable systems of information reduced the visual noise and confusion of urban landscapes and enabled designers to impart a sense of order and easy navigation in the manner of the London Underground maps two decades earlier (see fig. 9.54). The International Typographic Style appeared in journals and was the subject of museum exhibitions in Europe and in the United States at the Museum of Modern Art. The styles practitioners held teaching positions enabling them to publish guides and exercises intended to encourage students to develop the skills and adopt the values of the Swiss approach. Armin Hofmann taught at the Applied Arts School in Basel and held faculty positions at the School of the Philadelphia Museum of Art (now the University of the Arts) and at Yale University. The Massachusetts Institute of Technology created a graphic design program that utilized a unified visual system for the institute and its press that could be applied to a variety of academic and public relations materials. In 1959, Swiss designer Karl Gerstner (19302017) published a trilingual history of modern graphic design entitled Die Neue Graphik – The New Graphic Art – Le Nouvel Art Graphique. This book presented the history of graphic design as the linear development toward clarity and simplicity as principles for the presentation of information to suit the needs of advertising and technical information. Gerstner illustrated canonical examples of chromolithographic posters from the later nineteenth and early twentieth centuries as beginnings, and the more systematic use of asymmetric layout and vertical typography from the interwar period as breakthroughs, all leading to the present, in which system and an economy of means prevail. Gerstner illustrated numerous logos and reductive imagery, as well as product photography. In one sequence of images he contrasted advertisements in which womens high-heeled shoes were photographed in profile and from a birds-eye view for Lord & Taylor (1949) with an advertisement for I. Miller Shoe Company in which a womans leg is photographed wearing the shoe (1956) with the following caption, indicating the strong distinction that was drawn at times between the International Typographic Style and persuasive techniques of the advertising industry: One way of advertising shoes is to show a picture of them. Another is to make the picture promise the fulfillment of a wish: If you buy my shoes you will have beautiful legs (the shoe itself seems to have become a minor consideration). It is all rather like a fairy story and nobody troubles to check the truthfulness of such promises. Gerstner and others presented graphic design as a creative and socially responsible professional activity, combining visual imagination and problem-solving skills, rather than as a branch of advertising whose role was primarily to stimulate consumer desire. Comparisons to modern art and the near-universal application of the grid provided subjective and objective parameters of modern graphic design practice and the higher purpose of educating the public and cleansing public spaces of clutter with clear, concise, and unified information systems. While it is quite common to equate the International Typographic Style with objectivity and emotional restraint, this definition mostly applies to advertising and concert or theatrical performance rather than to areas of public service and advocacy. In 1952 to 1953 Zurich-based graphic designer Josef Müller-Brockmann (19141996) won a competition to design a series of traffic safety posters for the Automobile Club of Switzerland (ACS). The competition brief asked designers to illustrate the tagline Schützt das Kind! (Mind or Protect the Child!; fig. 11.67), and was part of an ACS initiative that included a changing display of statistics and photographs reporting traffic accidents and deaths, shown on an Unfallbarometer (Accident Barometer, also designed by Müller-Brockmann) located in a prominent Zurich public square. His approach rejected illustration in favor of black and white photography and employed a limited color palette, including a horizontal yellow band at the bottom of the poster that draws attention to that area; unprinted white space to the right of the motorcycle wheel that contrasts with and isolates the running child and emphasizes the disparity between him and the speeding wheel; and the crisp sans serif Akzidenz Grotesk font in the upper-left corner silhouetted against the cycles motor housing. The tension created by the unequal formal arrangement of asymmetrical abstract shapes, dominated by the aggressive dark triangle of the wheel at the left and its lighter, smaller counterpart to the right; the repetition of parallel diagonal lines that includes the outer edge of the wheel and shock absorber and the left contour of the running child; and the overall simplicity of the composition produce a unified image with a striking and immediate impact upon the viewer. And yet, when using the term objective, authors appear to conflate technical practice with emotional restraint, as if objectivity was the natural or inevitable result of applying the formal principles of the Swiss style and the use of photography. Indeed, in his own A History of Visual Communication (1971), Müller-Brockmann stated that objectively informative designs are a socio-cultural task. And in commenting on the program at the Kunstgewerbeschule in Zurich, Müller-Brockmann tells his readers he developed an objectified course of training, that visual communication is a means of conveying objective facts (my italics), giving designers the ability to cope with highly complex problems of human society. The section of his survey devoted to graphic design after World War II bears the subtitle The Development of Objective Visual Communication after World War II. Yet there is nothing factual about Müller-Brockmanns use of photomontage in Schützt das Kind!: the dramatic contrast in scale between motorcycle and child creates a sense of impending, even frightening danger and elicits a strong emotional response. Schützt das Kind! makes a statement about the relationship between drivers and pedestrians, about the power and aggressiveness of the former and the vulnerability of the latter. Swiss graphic designers sought common ground in visual communication, but the movements international pretensions were limited to industrially developed nations, multinational corporations, the Latin alphabet, and a theory of abstract reduction that may be less obviously universal than imagined. Such work embodied a sense of restraint as well as reform, removed from the excesses of commercial art direction at one end and the indulgence and unrestrained freedom of individual expression for a more exclusive audience at the other. In addition to the examples cited above, Ulm faculty member Otl Aicher (19221991) developed corporate identity and advertising for the German airline Lufthansa in the early 1960s, with specifications that included grid layout, typography, and symbols (see fig. 13.34). On a more theoretical level, International Typographic Style relates to semiotics, defined as the study or science of signs. Emerging first in the field of linguistics, semiotics holds that the meaning of language is the outcome of a process involving a signifier or means of communication (letters, words, images) and a signified, that is, the way in which those means of communication are understood. Words, as well as images, do not have fixed meanings in relation to the objects or ideas they represent. Rather, meaning is constructed or mediated socially in an active rather than passive process that involves both intention as well as reception. In the absence of objective communication, graphic designers focused attention upon controlling the range of meanings in the signs they constructed and establishing clearly, even universally, understood information. Word, symbol, image, composition, and hierarchy of information constituted the elements of a responsible approach to graphic communication within the framework of semiotics. As we saw in Müller-Brockmanns Schützt das Kind! poster, designers could exercise considerable latitude dependent upon the nature of the commission and the expectations of the client. The close relationship that developed between the International Typographic Style and corporate identity systems eventually complicated its claims of neutral or objective communication. In 1955 Max Bill resigned as director of the Hochschule für Gestaltung (HfG), and Argentinian-born Tomás Maldonado (19222018) was appointed as his successor. Maldonado shifted the curriculum of the school away from experimentation with materials and visual practice toward a stronger involvement with social theory, reducing emphasis upon studio work and concentrating upon redefining the role of design and the designer in a global context critical of materialism and capitalism. As a practicing designer he served as a consultant to the Erbe medical equipment company in Tübingen, Germany, in the early 1960s, where his product housings employed hard-edged white geometric forms framed by black strips, simple manufacturing techniques, smooth surfaces to promote hygiene, and subordination to the impersonal architecture and sterility of the surgery (fig. 11.72). 11.72 ![](images/e4572aa0b6d21fd2f16e1071e2346ca927aadedce35a80c04e0d41e48dad3489.jpg) Tomás Maldonado, medical equipment, Erbe Company, Tübingen, Germany, 1961. Maldonado questioned the consumer orientation of post-war industrial design in the context of broader global considerations including hunger, disease, and pollution. Following financial problems that forced consolidation with a nearby vocational school, the Hochshule für Gestaltung closed in 1968. Maldonados interest in environmental design, however, was echoed by other contemporary theorists who were equally concerned with more radical possibilities of linking design with varied fringe efforts directed toward social change. These views strayed farther from the more product-and-process-oriented mainstream of international modern industrial design into the realm of social and political activism. In fine art as well, contemporary artists such as Joseph Beuys (18881958) were less concerned with making objects (objects-as-commodities) than with reaching audiences and provoking a response alternatively through performance and demonstration, suggesting the possibility for a relevant public role for the arts outside the gallery or museum. Maldonado envisioned an expansion of the definition of design to include a wider range of activities that engaged users with technology, integrating information and other media to address pressing human and social needs. The paradigm for the designer went beyond form-giving and products to services and human interaction with technology and mass media, promoting designs increasing role in modern life as a broadly defined tool of communication connecting people with the world around them. These issues are further explored in Part VI in relation to the counterculture (see here onward) as well as to applications of digital technology to design (see here onward). The primacy of global concerns also informed the theory and practice of other, more radical design practitioners and critics, including American architect and designer Buckminster Fuller (18951983) as well as Austrian-born architect Richard Neutra (18921970). In the 1940s, Fuller, inspired by the development of lightweight building materials, devised a technique for constructing geodesic domes using triangular components. Fullers ideas were often couched in utopian terms, and his embrace of prefabrication and uniformity implied a form of collectivism that did not resonate with manufacturers in an age of advertising and expanded materialism. Fullers hopes for sponsorship of his building plans failed to materialize, perhaps as they were too far removed from the suburban ideal of individual home ownership and customization that was taking shape in Levittowns and other communities throughout the United States (see here). Nevertheless, Fullers message of social responsibility and the conservation of natural resources helped to make him something of a cult figure in the later 1960s in a more volatile political and social climate. Not surprisingly, he has made a comeback among those in the design community who think globally about the effects of design upon the environment. Neutras response to post-war design was a book entitled Survival Through Design, published in 1954, an indictment of the commercial values inherent in post-war popular culture and a search for reform standards based upon basic human needs in a global setting. In his writing Neutra championed the indivisibility of the man-made with the natural, and hoped that ecological thinking would inform the future of architecture. Many large multinational corporations recognized the advantages of consistent policies toward design after World War II. Recalling the precepts of the Werkbund (see here), standards of good design translated easily into industrial corporate culture, subordinating the individual to the group whose shared ethos was guided by dependability and consistent performance, building familiarity and trust. The economic climate of aggressive growth through takeovers and mergers with related businesses often created additional pressure to maintain a single image or company identity, and professional designers met the need, shaping marketing strategies and reinforcing organizational behavior. Their contributions included logos, liveries, products, and packaging to establish corporate organizational standards and project a company ethos. Product and logo design form only part of corporate identity. Many corporations rely upon slogans, packaging, architecture, signage, advertising, and exhibition design to promote their image, particularly if their business is in similar products such as soft drinks, energy transmission, or oil and gasoline rather than in a line of consumer domestic wares such as coffee-makers, toasters, or cassette players. Even in these areas, packaging and prominent logos often create as much differentiation as product housings, especially when miniaturization imposes a stronger degree of uniformity among similar goods, for instance, in electronic products, whether transistor radios or flat-screen televisions. The broader context for the flourishing of corporate identity also includes legislation that permitted companies to protect their identities (and investment) through copyright. More broadly, corporate identity dovetailed with soft power, mitigating the fear or threat of greater government control over private enterprise. The corporate image of the Olivetti Corporation, as seen in showrooms throughout Italy in the later 1940s and in Manhattan from 1954 (see fig. 11.48), prompted IBM president Thomas Watson, Jr. (19141993) to pursue a unified corporate identity program. To direct this program Watson hired Eliot F. Noyes (19101977), an architect who had served as curator of industrial design at the Museum of Modern Art. IBM identity applied not only to the coherent design of products and promotional materials directed toward clients and investors, but also toward reinforcing community and exclusivity among employees, that is, a unified corporate culture. Such a team-based approach had advantages in providing guidelines or standards for dress and behavior. Some observers noted a downside to the new corporate culture. In his book The Lonely Crowd (1950), sociologist David Riesman wrote that a corporate image could have an alienating effect upon individuals, who felt that they had to fit in with the group in order to be successful. In Riesmans view, corporate behavior was part of a shift from inner-directed to other-directed values that he saw emerging as the dominant form of conformity particularly in the United States. The attitudes and expectations of this broad and diverse market absorbed but also were not limited in their consumer preferences to the collections, exhibitions, and paternalism of museums, the consistency and dependability of corporate identity systems, and the new humanism that embraced social responsibility, aesthetic awareness, fitness to purpose, and comfort. Indeed, postwar consumer experience was shaped not only by economic growth and post-war optimism but also by the unprecedented role assumed by mass media. The term mass culture may be used to describe the United States market, especially after World War II. This term includes the designed products that reached an expanding consumer base, but appreciating those products requires understanding the ways in which mass media shaped and reflected the experience of post-war Americans, as well as the perception of that experience abroad. The apparent indifference of the mass market to good design may be seen in the success of a wide variety of post-war products ranging from automobiles to kitchen appliances and portable electronics. In all of these examples there was tremendous receptivity (or susceptibility) on the part of consumers to planned obsolescence: that is, the calculated efforts of manufacturers to generate demand with a seemingly unending supply of new products, along the lines of seasonal fashion. In fact, the term was coined by the Milwaukee-based industrial designer Brooks Stevens (19111995), acknowledging the connection between design, consumer desire, and continuous novelty: what advertising executives had referred to as consumer engineering from the later 1920s. Obsolescence conditions the buying public to continual cycles of consumption, replacement, and seemingly unending desire. In addition, while modern materials and methods of mechanized production prevailed in manufacturing, products made frequent references to the past and present, as well as to the future, replacing principles such as fitness to purpose and fine art or architecturally based aesthetic principles with greater freedom of choice, integrating expendability with a broader spectrum of tastes. Indeed the phenomena of newness and an accelerated pace of change tended to erode in some degree the very notion of stable aesthetic, ethical, or performance standards and the authority they imply. After all, standards suggest permanence and durability for products, while obsolescence and novelty imply a perpetual state of becoming, of desire, where consumption itself becomes a way of life, and the manipulation of consumer behavior a business strategy. British design historian and critic Reyner Banham (19221988) termed this phenomenon a throwaway culture. Despite its commercial potential, implementing frequent product change entailed great economic risks: the retooling required for changes in the manufacturing process as well as the increased investment in the complex production process ran contrary to the tenets of Fordism, translating either into higher, less competitive prices or lower profit margins for companies and their investors. Thus obsolescence required corporations to manage risks of higher investment costs, often by limiting the role of design to superficial elements of product housing rather than integrating design with engineering, ergonomics, or other research-based considerations (after all, it was called planned obsolescence). Moreover, success also depended upon marketing products through a variety of media ranging from popular magazines and radio to the cinema and the emerging medium of television. In many ways media outlets employed similar strategies for the mass production of entertainment. An example is serial television programming, utilizing the same familiar characters facing new conflicts or crises each week, or stereotypical Hollywood film heroes following the same plotline in an array of different times and places. In such cases originality was managed, limited to particular elements of the creative process in order to generate ever new products for consumers to watch or to buy while containing the costs of capital investment and time pressures. Today, film studios greatest profits generate from sequels of their popular titles such as Spiderman, Rocky, or Star Wars. Also, popular design and entertainment were even more interrelated, since advertising, whether on television or in print magazines and newspapers, was sold to corporate sponsors of products and services on the basis of delivering an audience for the purposes of marketing. Most historians acknowledge that the success of product obsolescence for the mass market could not have taken place without advertising along with added incentives such as term and credit buying, allowing consumers to enjoy their purchases before they owned them outright. Whether through illustration, photography, or television, advertisements linked products to individual identity, self-improvement, and social acceptance, the ability to look or feel like the person you imagine yourself to be. On the basis of polls and other consumer studies, advertisers often targeted housewives and children, who played a large role in buying patterns for a majority of middle-class families. Techniques extended Veblens theory of conspicuous consumption to the age of expanding industrial production and mass consumption. Manufacturing and new strategies for design, marketing, and advertising combined to create forms and associations that appealed to a diverse audience linked by a belief that consumption and materialism were the means to individual fulfillment as well as contributing to national prosperity and stimulating the economy. Such a seductive combination of forces was hard to resist. Suburban strip malls, discount stores, supermarkets, seasonal sales, coupons, promotions, and expanded channels of distribution further reduced impediments to shopping for the products of mass culture. The origin of national brands dates to the early twentieth century, but the post-war era accelerated the substitution of the national for the local and regional through franchised chain stores, hotels, motels, and restaurants  these include Korvettes, Holiday Inn, Sheraton Hotels, and Colonel Sanders Kentucky Fried Chicken or McDonalds. The result was an emerging shared culture of shopping that defined American lifestyles. Journalist Thomas Hine labeled the phenomenon Populuxe, a term that encompasses the dream of leisure, abundance and self-indulgence that exerted such widespread appeal especially during the 1950s. The relationship between advertising and mass design reminds us that the interaction between products and users was mediated by images as seen in print and on television, with the latter assuming greater importance after the war, particularly in the United States. Beyond the gallery, corporate office or showroom, or museum collection and exhibition, displays in retail outlets, catalogs, magazine and newspaper advertisements, on radio or on television, manufactured goods were entwined with the everyday lives of Americans and barometers of material progress that contributed to the achievement of the American Dream. ## Detroit: Transportation as Symbol The post-war Detroit-manufactured automobile offers a paradigm for mass design and the attitudes of manufacturers and buyers that shaped its practice. The manufacture of new automobiles for the civilian market had ceased in 1942 as automobile makers contributed to meeting the governments material needs in the war effort. Although demand for new cars was strong when domestic manufacturing resumed after the war, there were signs of market saturation and a slowed rate of growth in automobile purchases in the early 1950s. Post-war automobiles resembled their pre-war counterparts, and design was secondary to production efficiency and controlling capital investment costs. Stimulating sales through annual styling changes was not a new idea in the automobile industry, and it re-emerged with renewed vitality in the early 1950s. At General Motors, company president Alfred Sloan renewed his plan for greater brand differentiation and more noticeable annual styling changes, minimizing costs through standardized parts for the chassis and other mechanical elements that could be shared among the companys numerous brands and models. It was Sloans dream to create a vehicle for every purse, defining a series of gradations from economy to luxury in terms of visible differences in styling and comfort. Encompassing the ends as well as the broad middle of the market, General Motors effectively helped to equate car buying with social mobility as well as personal identity and to stimulate more frequent automobile purchases while remaining competitive with smaller manufacturers of both luxury and economy cars. ![](images/baae04e85efe7ebd71c0ea5b56e27c65482ac3a4f2de6fcb530000e74b47cf45.jpg) ## 12.1 Advertisement for Cadillac Eldorado, from Life, September 9, 1957. The broad marketing strategy and financing of annual model changes across different brand names such as Oldsmobile, Pontiac, Buick, Chevrolet, and Cadillac favored large auto-makers and led to their increasing control of the market and production supply chains. Despite the apparent variety and choice available to consumers in terms of brands, models, customized colors, and other options, American automobile manufacturing became more homogeneous, controlled by fewer large manufacturers with less room for competition in the mass market from smaller scale entrepreneurs. These large companies were successful in strengthening differentiation between brands and particular groups of consumers. Advertisements for the American automobile of the later 1950s often combined references in the copy to practical advantages of roominess or advanced engineering with images that identified automobile ownership with luxury living. These generally featured attractive, well-dressed (and exclusively white) couples returning home from an evening out or arriving in style at an upscale hotel (figs. 12.1 and 12.3). Contemporary cinema captured the appeal of varied activities associated with personal mobility. There is also a global component underlying the growth of the automobile industry in the United States during this period. The development of the powerful V-8 engine for regular production vehicles took advantage of inexpensive gasoline prices in the United States, a result of favorable business agreements between American-based oil companies and refineries located in West Asia. By the early 1970s, the oil-producing nations would assert their independence from Western oil companies, raise the price of fuel, and reshape automobile design as established by Detroit-based manufacturers in the period under discussion. GM began to shed brands in 2004 with the elimination of Oldsmobile, followed by Pontiac in 2009 amid infusions of government investment and calls for restructuring of its business operations. The rapport that GM had been able to establish with its customers eroded amid changing economic conditions, environmental awareness, and foreign competition. When the once-mighty corporation declared bankruptcy and massive restructuring in May 2009, analysts cited a long list of miscalculations, perhaps none more significant than a failure to connect with consumers whose loyalty the company seemed to take for granted. But for a time after World War II a large segment of the middle-class consuming public in the United States enjoyed the associations of styling, brand identity, low fuel prices, the anticipation of new annual models, and the comfort and emotional appeal of the open road. ![](images/48ee9e09a2fe04db0c2b28970766985673292e6148a7046669e18d8187794de0.jpg) 12.5 Virgil Exner, Plymouth coupé, Chrysler Corporation, Auburn Hills, Michigan, 1957. Industrial manufacturers also employed consultant and in-house designers in areas outside of the automotive industry. Brooks Stevens was a successful consultant designer for several companies both before and after World War II. He anticipated the pent-up desire for eye-catching consumer goods following wartime shortages and rationing, and defended planned obsolescence on the basis of the joy and pride new designs provided their owners and the overall economic benefits of increased levels of employment and consumption. Stevens designed engine housings for outboard recreational motor boats manufactured by Evinrude and Johnson that featured cutaway housings and two-tone colors reminiscent of Chryslers forward look in the later 1950s (fig. 12.6). Other designers worked in-house for consumer electronics companies such as Motorola, or Zenith, both based near Chicago, Illinois. These companies produced a wide range of portable and table transistor radios and phonographs. In contrast to Braun (see fig. 11.57), their products were varied in form, color, and the design of operating controls (fig. 12.7), resembling automobile dashboards. American industrial designers for the mass market rarely saw their role as educating consumers to appreciate restrained or sophisticated Modernist tastes; rather their designs acknowledged a wide range of consumer preferences and motivations, as well as perpetual technical innovation and novelty in which the only constant was desire, as Stevens stated in his definition of planned obsolescence: the desire to own something a little newer, a little better, a little sooner than is necessary. ![](images/7f0e8fb8f351af4056f7a47d178bf64bedaf391b24d5b27642f2c4cc157c6a17.jpg) 12.7 Feature on portable radios and phonographs, Look, vol. 20, no. 21, October 16, 1956. ## Critics of Styling By the later 1950s there was a tendency, even within the industrial design community, to criticize tailfins and other automobile styling features. Raymond Loewy, for instance, who served as design consultant to the automobile manufacturer Studebaker, took aim at the American automobile industry in a speech of 1955, comparing that years new designs to gaudy merchandise and jukeboxes on wheels: even the author of Never Leave Well Enough Alone, who well understood the need to demonstrate the commercial advantages of design in industrial manufacturing, was offended by the degree to which design had departed from a more substantive relation to the automobiles mechanical basis, or from the ideal of beauty embodied in the characteristics of a unified streamlined Modernism. Loewys own designs for Studebaker were original and aerodynamic, pioneering the sloping roofline later adopted by former associate Virgil Exner for the 1957 Plymouth (fig. 12.8). But the Studebaker lacked the prominent symbolic jet-age tailfins of its General Motors, Ford, and Chrysler counterparts. In the end it proved commercially unsuccessful; the company ceased automobile production in 1966. ![](images/bf8fc03ad512246379d40653c44e7b1b6a44efb685acbbb3c4cd665586016efd.jpg) ## 12.8 Raymond Loewy, advertisement for Studebaker, Life, June 1954. Critics of the post-war American automobile industry also viewed manufacturers as manipulators of consumer desire: the manufacturer is seen as active and the consumer as passive, the latter cast in the role of unwitting victim, conforming to social pressures and somehow programmed for shopping (see here). And yet not all of the large automakers styling initiatives were successful: efforts to manipulate the public often resulted in disaster (as easily observed in the high number of short-lived network television series produced each autumn). In other words, the phenomenon of mass design and consumer behavior was and remains an unpredictable enterprise, certainly more complex than many of its detractors imagined. One of the most widely known mass-market miscalculations was the Ford Corporations 1958 Edsel, a project that failed to attract consumer interest despite a heavy investment in styling and promotion. Historians have noted that family cars such as the Edsel were not attuned to consumer excitement at the time for luxury or sportier models aimed at youthful buyers. As an exercise in design, the Edsel debacle reveals the complexity of predicting public reaction and figuring out whether a new product will prove profitable. To summarize, two viewpoints toward mass culture emerged after World War II. On one hand, mass design was seen as a form of exploitation manipulated from above and masking the desire for corporate profit and control under the banner of freedom of choice and the democratization of luxury and leisure, heavily dependent upon advertising and stimulated by product symbolism. On the other hand, mass culture was also viewed as being empowering, responsive to the everyday experience and aspirations of ordinary folk, a popular and healthy assertion of individual expression that addresses common desires as well as fears for a diverse audience, increasingly geared to identifying, creating, and attracting segmented markets. The rich strains of resistance, escape, self-expression, solidarity, and difference within popular culture emerge through fashion, products, and also through the medium of popular music, particularly rock n roll, with its roots in African American culture, its celebration of youthful energy, and its rebellious challenge to conventional behavior and authority. The link between popular music and accessible grassroots expression appears in the career of rock musician legend and songwriter Bruce Springsteen (b. 1949). When Springsteen accepted the Oscar for Original Score at the 1994 Academy Awards for the film Philadelphia (1993; the movie dealt with AIDS and discrimination against its victims), he accepted the award with the following remarks: You do your best and hope that it pulls out the best in your audience, and some piece of it spills over into the real world and peoples everyday lives and allows us to recognize each other through our veil of differences. Whether celebrating humanitys differences or recognizing our common emotions, however, the status of commodities, whether under capitalist free enterprise and the seductive images and slogans of advertising or Soviet economic efforts to demonstrate material progress, remains paramount, for even resistance often takes the form of consumption rather than threatening social or political action. Moreover, whether owing to pent-up desire in response to rationing and shortages during World War II, or policies of Cold War containment that linked consumption to productivity and patriotism, designed consumer products acquired a wide range of meanings and associations, raising hopes as well as fears in popular and critical terms. Author and journalist Thomas Hine noted that when United States Vice-President Richard Nixon met Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev in 1959 on the occasion of an exhibition of American goods in Moscow during a well-publicized meeting between leaders of the global superpowers during the Cold War, the Vice-President proudly showed the Soviet leader an American washing machine as evidence of the high standard of living enjoyed by a majority of Americas middle-class families. Nixon identified, in effect, materialism with the achievement of democratic ideals under capitalist free enterprise. While Americans might have feared competition with the Soviets in space and military technology in the wake of the two successful 1957 launches of Sputnik satellites, there was little doubt that in translating technological advancement into domestic products the United States occupied a position of strength and confidence. Despite conflicting attitudes in the United States toward consumption and obsolescence, proponents of good design and the products of mass culture in the later 1950s could both promote design in terms that equated technology, leisure, and material prosperity with the achievement of democratic ideals under capitalism. In a climate of free markets, investment in European industrial production, and international commerce, differences regarding consumption seem more a matter of taste than of ethics, as outlined by journalist Russell Lynes. Lynes observed in his 1949 essay titled Highbrow, Middlebrow, Lowbrow that American society could be divided into the three broad social and cultural groupings of the articles title, each identified not primarily by wealth or family background, but by lifestyle and consumer behavior, that is, the goods and services they chose to purchase, the clothing they wore, the furniture with which they decorated their homes, the music they listened to, and the magazines they read. Lyness categories have become part of our general twentieth-century cultural discourse, but the categories are not as rigid as they may first appear: according to Lynes, the three categories of taste in post-World War II America were neither monolithic nor entirely distinct: each could be divided into sub-groupings, and there were areas of overlap and fluidity among them. ## Resorts and Luxury Together with the Detroit-manufactured automobile and the new suburban tract home (see here), popular design in post-war America was also defined by the resort hotel, first built in Miami Beach, Florida, and later in Las Vegas, Nevada. Miami Beachs Fontainebleau, built in 1954 and named for the sixteenth-century French royal château outside of Paris, was one of several Florida beachfront hotels designed by Morris Lapidus (19022001). Trained as an architect in New York, Lapidus had spent much of his career prior to the war as a successful designer of retail storefronts and interiors (see fig. 10.37). Post-war affluence and paid vacations expanded the market for travel and leisure, marketed through television, film, and photograph-filled monthly travel magazines such as Holiday. Investment in travel and tourism provided Lapidus with the opportunity to design on a larger and more lavish scale, and to indulge the popular middle-brow imagination for drama, escape, fantasy, and luxury gleaned from both the past and from a popular vision of a glamorous and indulgent future. Lapiduss resort hotels often displayed sweeping curves and recurves, for instance in the opposition of entranceway and façade at the Fontainebleau (fig. 12.9). Curving hallways rather than the repetitive axial rhythm of doorways and hall-lights created a sense of anticipation for the guest. Mezzanines, grand staircases, suspended ceilings with circular holes for chandeliers, and support beams hidden beneath sheathings of wood and textured materials were closer in experience to movie sets than to reception areas. In art historical terms, Lapiduss designs tended toward the openness and grandeur of the Italian Baroque, and seemed to reduce the barrier separating Hollywood from the experience of Eastern or Midwestern snowbirds escaping cold winters for a week of vacation at the beach. Many of Lapiduss ideas derived from the expressive possibilities of shaping modern reinforced concrete. He admired the experimental architecture of German architect Erich Mendelsohn (18871953) and the organic forms and recesses of Surrealist art. Yet his hotels also featured imaginative if eclectic decoration drawn from eighteenth-century French furniture and Italian Renaissance architecture, as in the coffee shop at the Fontainebleau from 1954 and the Mona Lisa room at the Eden Roc, also in Miami Beach and built in 1956 (figs. 12.10 and 12.11). It is no surprise that, paraphrasing Raymond Loewys Never Leave Well Enough Alone, Lapidus entitled his own 1996 autobiography Too Much is Never Enough. The variety of Lapiduss conceptions, intended to satisfy his clients and appeal to the aspirations and fantasies of the hotels clientele, characterized a both ... and rather than either ... or attitude toward design that fueled desire and the promise of upward mobility. It was this broad eclecticism that was often criticized at the time for its commercialism and lack of discrimination between architecture and amusement parks, but later in his long career Lapidus began to gain critical acceptance for his imagination and sense of playfulness and fun. However indulgent or escapist, Lapiduss ideal of luxury gave to the many a glimpse of what they believed to have been the entitlement of the privileged, if only for a week or even a weekend. New approaches to home building were a response to a severe housing shortage for returning veterans coupled with slow growth in housing starts during the Depression. The elastic markets for housing and automobiles after the war were not unrelated. Indeed the growth of new suburban communities, constructed on inexpensive tracts of land located further from commuter rail lines, increased dependence upon the automobile and the need for new multi-lane highways for commuting to the workplace. They also account for the commercial success of new sprawling shopping centers and malls, designed to bring dozens of businesses together in an area built with abundant parking space. In fact, both industries supported and even lobbied successfully for a government-funded interstate highway system. Also contributing to the success of the suburban development was the ease of obtaining federally sponsored low-interest mortgage loans from government agencies such as the Veterans Administration (VA) and the Federal Housing Administration (FHA), encouraging young couples and families to purchase new homes rather than rent apartments in inner cities. Builder William Levitt pioneered mass-production techniques in the housing industry after World War II in Levittown communities located in suburban New York, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania. Using pre-assembled parts and teams of specialized workers, each repeating a particular task in a rationalized approach to the home building process, Levitt became the Henry Ford of the housing industry. Houses were built in less than six weeks on inexpensively purchased land beyond the limits of urban centers, and in communities with as many as 17,000 new houses, suburbia began to come into focus. Levitts houses were built on concrete slabs rather than on foundations with basements, and his construction process quickly became the industry standard due to the affordability of the units, which began as low as \$7,000. In addition, buyers could purchase homes with as little as a one-dollar down payment and federally guaranteed mortgages at low interest that minimized the builders risk. Post-war housing in the United States differed significantly from its European counterparts. Whereas dense, efficient, and uniform apartments after World War II were the norm in communities within or outside of many large European cities, in the United States new building materials, inexpensive land, and mass-production techniques reinforced traditional associations between property ownership, independence, and security. Individual home ownership implied stability and permanence, linked in the popular imagination with self-sufficiency and levels of comfort formerly available only to wealthier Americans. Buyers were attracted to the new suburban homes by modern practical features intended to make life more convenient and efficient. Kitchens featured picture windows to allow parents to watch their children play in the yard, and attached garages kept automobiles off the street and allowed direct entry into the home. The attraction of individual home ownership and the added convenience of appliances such as washing machines and even television sets, often included in the selling price, seemed to outweigh the conformity often noted by critics. Author Lewis Mumford (18951990) was one such critic, who saw few advantages to the post-war suburb in comparison with its more idyllic, villagelike or garden city predecessors, predicting negative consequences should the trend continue: The ultimate outcome of the suburbs alienation from the city became visible only in the twentieth century, with the extension of the democratic ideal through the instrumentalities of manifolding and mass production. In the mass movement into suburban areas a new kind of community was produced, which caricatured both the historic city and the archetypal suburban refuge: a multitude of uniform, unidentifiable houses, lined up inflexibly, at uniform distances, on uniform roads, in a treeless communal waste, inhabited by people of the same class, the same income, the same tasteless pre-fabricated foods, from the same freezers, conforming in every outward and inward respect to a common mold, manufactured in the central metropolis. What has happened to the suburban exodus in the United States now threatens, through the same mechanical instrumentalities, to take place, at an equally accelerating rate, everywhere else. The identification of home ownership with the lifestyle of the average family was normalized in the emerging television industry. Television families, such as the Nelsons of Ozzie and Harriet or the Cleavers of Leave It to Beaver, owned modern homes in suburban communities. Notable exceptions were the childless Cramdens of The Honeymooners, who suffered renters frustrations of noise and lack of privacy found in urban apartment houses, or the family of nightclub entertainer Danny Thomas. Thomass career required that his own precocious children live in a spacious Manhattan apartment dominated by adults rather than by other children. While weekly entertainment shows such as the Sunday evening Ed Sullivan Show might feature Black entertainers, the casts and characters of weekly sitcoms were almost invariably white, with Black people generally cast in familiar stereotypes as cooks, servants, or dutiful companions. While cracks in the color barrier were more prevalent in the fields of entertainment, music, and professional sports, racial discrimination continued to dominate the fields of business, including advertising and the design professions. In this illustrated story the child of a young family who recently moved into a new subdivision is troubled by an inability to distinguish his own home from the others on his block. The problem is solved when each family creates its own weathervane to add character and individual identity to otherwise bland and uniform surroundings as critiqued by Mumford and others. The housing industry also offered solutions to the practical as well as social desire for differentiation in the market. Attracting new buyers from the city to the suburb or encouraging suburbanites to trade up during the 1950s led to an expansion of the range of housing types, featuring much inventiveness in introducing variations and choices available for consumers to exercise taste and customize their purchase. In addition to the standard Cape Cod home, builders began to include a wider variety of traditional types such as Colonial or Tudor, as well as modern Ranch models. The last included large expanses of windows and low single-story designs, or a split level with half-stories to either side of a central entrance (fig. 12.15). Informal family rooms and eat-in kitchens became ways to expand living space and move beyond basic housing needs toward comfort and entertaining guests. 12.15 ![](images/c5ffaad6595fb0c754a46a5cc27a776d643787f4062f627cc34dd85f76ca726e.jpg) Feature on prefabricated homes from Better Homes and Gardens, August 1954. The reduction of standardization in interior design and decoration resulted from new industrial materials whose potential for variation was exploited by manufacturers. Examples include Formica, a hard plastic available in a variety of patterns for use in kitchen surfaces to replace enameled metal or stainless steel, and Linoleum, a softer surface used for flooring in kitchens and recreation rooms. The more colorful and textured effects of Formica and Linoleum, as well as Naugahyde upholstery, made the kitchen less of a laboratory or labor-saving workplace than a room for leisure and relaxation. The reduction of toil was to a great degree supported by advertising, in which appliances such as dishwashers as well as household cleaning products were viewed as improvements bringing new quantities of freedom and leisure. Fashionably dressed housewives were generally pictured along with the dishwasher or new dinette, marveling at the ease of loading the new appliance, admiring the sparkling cleanliness of the glassware, or enjoying time with family members (fig. 12.16). Comfort found expression less in the plywood and pedestal-based Eames lounge chair than in the more massive and heavily upholstered reclining chair known as the La-Z-boy or variations such as the Barcalounger and Stratolounger (fig. 12.17). The La-Z-boy derived from cushioned railway cars. Advertisements focused upon the variety of available fabrics and upholstery styles from which the buyer could choose, as well as upon the relaxed (mostly male and, again, always white) occupant resting after a long day at the office. The world of childrens leisure was filled with a never-ending array of ephemeral fads and toys available at the corner drug store and soda fountain in city neighborhoods or at the suburban mall, from spinning tops to butterfly yo-yos, hoola-hoops and frisbees, creating a market that is today augmented by an endless number of phone apps, puzzles, and videogames, all requiring the considerable contributions of a new generation of designers with a wide variety of increasingly computer-based skills that appeal to consumers and provide new opportunities for making use of leisure time. ![](images/1dc2f5d917edfff391b6100aa4645d5c1cde5fa2037f034d00e0f22448320f4b.jpg) Advertisement, refrigerator, manufactured by Frigidaire Corporation, Dayton, Ohio, Good Housekeeping, January, 1950. ![](images/4f029f5e29b95e908b93550cf4ac4e579767496c7c336fd85a0a9707ee61c47a.jpg) 12.17 Advertisement, Barcalounger lounge chair with Naugahyde vinyl upholstery, manufactured by U.S. Naugahyde, Better Homes and Gardens, June, 1951. Aluminum was another industrial material that appeared in domestic production and was successfully marketed in the 1950s in an effort to maintain wartime production levels. The Alcoa Aluminum Company advertised creative uses of aluminum in masscirculation magazines as part of a forecasting program called imagineering. One of the more popular products to emerge was sets of anodized aluminum tumblers in rainbow colors manufactured as Heller Hostess ware (fig. 12.18). Lightweight and certainly unbreakable, aluminum drinking cups were easily dented, especially around the lip, and their uniform weight created a high center of gravity that made them tip over easily when filled. Despite such shortcomings, the festive colors, low cost, novelty, and associations with the informality of outdoor picnics and barbecues helped to make aluminum tumblers commercially successful. Such products might be contrasted with the use of heavier and more sober or natural stainless steel in Scandinavian dinnerware of the same period, characterized by a plain metallic finish and used for serving rather than eating or drinking (fig. 12.19). Another example of imagineering in the 1950s was the lightweight, portable and tubular folding aluminum chair (first developed for seating in the aircraft industry) with plastic webbing (fig. 12.20). There was nothing durable or particularly ergonomic about this anonymous outdoor chair. Unstable if placed on uneven ground and irreparable when the supporting skids bent or the plastic webbing was torn or detached from the rivets that held it to the frame, the chairs were and remain the kind of expendable, replaceable, and anonymous everyday product characteristic of mass culture, a paradigm of Banhams Throwaway Culture and a mainstay of our designed world. The ephemeral nature of such designs and other fads contrasted with the tenets of good design, where research provided enduring solutions to problems or individual creativity contributed to an added, elevated aesthetic awareness. While museums and international exhibitions seemed to favor the latter over the former, it was perhaps popular design that better illustrated the realities of design in the post-war economy on a mass scale. The increased availability and popularity of nationally branded packaged and processed foods was another component of post-war mass culture, a further instance of the equation between leisure with progress. Instead of baking from scratch, cake mixes included pre-measured ingredients to enable housewives to perform instant miracles in the kitchen. Endorsed by the matronly Betty Crocker, a creation of the women employed by the home services department at General Mills in 1921, the companys immensely popular Betty Crocker cookbook helped to preserve the comforting sense of pride in being a traditional homemaker (fig. 12.21). As American studies scholar Karal Ann Marling relates, consultants to the General Mills Corporation (who manufactured and sold baking products) recommended that recipes call for using real eggs in cake mixes rather than powdered eggs to enable housewives to make a creative contribution to the process. This combination of new goods with reassuring values of home and family is a characteristic often found in the products of mass culture. Processed foods such as cake mixes and TV dinners were modern in their accommodation to contemporary lifestyles, and also in their adaptation of the technology of dehydration and packaging used in the preparation and manufacture of meals for servicemen during World War II. The conflicting desires for both increased convenience and ease along with the satisfaction of being a responsible homemaker are thus reconciled. Packaged foods exemplify a shift from the commodity itself to the psychological motivation for its purchase, that is, from production to consumption. Packaging, from toothpaste to dishwashing liquids, was often bright and festive, with lettering in different colors and in different alignments, a playful, even carefree accent to bathroom or kitchen. Other products meant to reduce toil and encourage impulse buying included Jiffy popcorn, where the aluminum-foil fry pan itself could be disposed of after being used on top of the stove, and synthetic fabrics such as Dacron Permapress, which eliminated the need for ironing. ![](images/9e3c3b34593c3b64e005757d8ff3120207cbdc31f177720c0b8f43ee11bb4361.jpg) 12.21 Advertisement for Betty Crocker products manufactured by General Mills Corporation, from Better Homes and Gardens, December, 1954. While advertising outlined an array of products for improved living, and shopping occupied more newly found leisure time, some housewives found the conveniences of modern domestic life problematic. Dissatisfaction with the suburban lifestyle and the ways in which it defined fulfillment found expression in Betty Friedans The Feminine Mystique (1963), an outgrowth of the authors experience as a mother and housewife in the 1950s. Following the end of World War II, hundreds of thousands of women were displaced from jobs by returning soldiers and found little opportunity to resume or begin careers. Mass media, in the form of womens magazines such as Ladies’ Home Journal, McCalls, and Redbook, as well as most network television housewives, encouraged women to focus their energies upon the home, defining their role primarily as consumers and caretakers while husbands went off to work. Friedans experience demonstrated that the patterns of post-war suburban living and the ideals of beauty, comfort, luxury, and well-being advertised in magazines and on television did not always provide satisfaction for women outside a range of activities some found unrewarding. It is no wonder that surveys conducted at the time indicated less satisfaction with suburban life among women in comparison with men. In addition to the conformity described above, other issues involving design arose during the post-war suburban housing boom that conflicted with the idealized images and narratives presented through the mass media and advertising. Particularly in the United States, Black Americans and other minorities were conspicuously absent from these new suburban neighborhoods and their television and advertising counterparts. Historians of the period such as Kenneth Jackson have noted that in addition to the uniformity implied by mass production and standardization, suburban housing communities tended to produce racial as well as economic homogeneity. This occurred particularly through zoning laws that inhibited industrial building and prevented housing construction for lower-income families, as well as overt policies by builders that prohibited access to Black buyers. The process of redlining meant that banks deemed Black or racially diverse neighborhoods an unwise investment and would not issue mortgages for properties in those areas. At the same time increasing numbers of Black families continued their migration from the rural South to the industrial North, seeking to escape Jim Crow laws, obtain a better education and higher-paying jobs, or start a new life following service in the armed forces during the war. Equating American life with its image in advertising and through the mass media presents a limited, flawed picture that masks racial and gender inequality as well as revealing white privilege. As in much of Western Europe, the seeds that blossomed into protest, activism, legislative reform, and ethically directed design in the 1960s were planted in the immediate post-war period. Other critics have argued that the link between technology, consumer goods, and an improving standard of living not only masked racial or gender discrimination, but also brought into light the connection between technology and the Cold War arms race, arguing that the focus upon peacetime consumption and affluence diverted public attention from an unsettling alliance between big business and government in contracts for military hardware and information technology. This alliance in turn concentrated power in both capital and in government, in effect an arms race that brought with it the potential for the outbreak of international conflict and fears of a cold war turning hot. Such tensions between the United States and the Soviet Union emerged in the capture of an American pilot in 1960 (the subject of the 2015 film Bridge of Spies that chronicled negotiations for his return in exchange for a captured Soviet spy), or the failed American invasion of communist Cuba in 1961. The threat of hard rather than soft power raised ethical questions for some designers concerning their own relationship to industry, government, and the role of the designer in society. These issues are treated in Part VI. Throughout the first two decades of the post-war period, a number of writers and critics condemned virtually every manifestation of the emerging mass culture and mass media on political, ethical, and aesthetic grounds. Most of this strain of criticism was based upon the views of an educated and sophisticated elite seeking to universalize a set of criteria that itself preserved and extended a reform-minded design discourse inherited from the very beginnings of industrialization and concerns about class conflict. Much criticism adopted a polemical tone and dismissive treatment of its target. In part this was a response to the exaggerated claims of early advertising as well as to the control of media and propaganda by fascist regimes during the 1930s and 1940s. Common to both charges was the belief that the essentially passive masses could be easily manipulated with false narratives and promises. Fears of the dangers of relativism (at the aesthetic or ethical level) may be sensed in the remarks of Robert Hutchins, president of the University of Chicago, in 1940: In order to believe in democracy we must believe that there is a difference between truth and falsity, good and bad, right and wrong, and that truth, goodness, and right are objective standards even though they cannot be experimentally verified.... Political organization must be tested by conformity to ideals. Its basis is moral. Its end is the good for man.... These are the principles which we must defend if we are to defend democracy. Are we prepared to defend these principles? Of course not. For forty years and more our intellectual leaders have been telling us they are not true. They have been telling us in fact that nothing is true which cannot be subject to experimental verification. In the whole realm of social thought there can, therefore, be nothing but opinion. Since there is nothing but opinion, everybody is entitled to his own opinion.... If everything is a matter of opinion, force becomes the only way of settling differences of opinion. And, of course, if success is the test of rightness, right is on the side of the heavier battalions. In 1957 consumer manipulation was the subject of a popular, sensationalist book by Vance Packard entitled The Hidden Persuaders. The book brought to light sophisticated psychological techniques and motivational analysis by market researchers as well as advertising used to sell products to consumers. Packards analysis emphasized the role of insecurity in the promotion of consumption, for instance in the need for men to feel masculine being met by cigarettes or aftershave lotions in a society where insecurity and conformity dictated the patterns and frustrations of everyday life. Packard quoted extensively from advertising executives and marketing consultants, using the terms motivational research (abbreviated as MR in the book) and depth analysis as labels for the persuasive techniques of selling derived from psychological surveys of consumers. Packard concluded that what motivates people to buy is not need but rather a desire to feel better about themselves. He was alarmed that such techniques of persuasion were being used not only for consumer goods but also in the political arena, and feared the consequences. Basic to Packards rhetoric is the manipulation of the publics irresistible unconscious urges by advertisers, what at times amounts to a big-business conspiracy. The Hidden Persuaders saw advertising techniques as dehumanizing, suggesting that they were not benign, and might be used to program our actions and thoughts: What the probers are looking for, of course, are the whys of our behavior, so that they can more effectively manipulate our habits and choices in their favor. This had led them to probe why we are afraid of banks; why we love those big fat cars; why housewives typically fall into a hypnoidal trance when they get into a supermarket; why men are drawn into auto showrooms by convertibles but end up buying sedans; why junior loves cereal that pops, snaps, and crackles. We move from the genial world of James Thurber into the chilling world of George Orwell and his Big Brother, however, as we explore some of the extreme attempts at probing and manipulating now going on. The criticism of mass culture was equally severe in Western European Marxist circles. Here issues of taste were combined with bitter disappointment and disillusionment at the subversion of techniques of mass production and mass communication first by autocratic fascist regimes in the interwar period and then after the war by capitalist enterprise and the fetishization of commodities rather than for the attainment of more enlightened progressive egalitarian ends. Part of the historical critique of capitalism in general focuses upon the need for large corporations to reap a return on their investment by guaranteeing a market for products of mechanized mass production through seductive advertising and marketing techniques. The result is the combination of design change and mass advertising that magnifies the symbolism of the product in a process often referred to as commodification or reification. While recognizing the much larger scale of mechanized mass production in the mid-twentieth century, the process nevertheless recalls the role of commodities as criticized by design reformers in Victorian England or social critics in turn-of-the-century America. German critic Theodor Adorno (19031969), for instance, used the term culture industry to refer to the methods of mass media and the capitalist control of the means of production that underwrote the system of entertainment and related industries. By equating culture with leisure there was a tendency to devalue and corrupt the entire enterprise of design under capitalism. In this view consumption is the reward of compliance within a system so pervasive as to eradicate even awareness of its power to compromise and subvert individual freedom. Such circumstances offer no escape aside from revolution or the pursuit of an alternative lifestyle akin perhaps to the Arts and Crafts communities of the late nineteenth century and their rejection of industrial society. More recently the views of Adorno and other critics have been seen as being based upon a narrow understanding of mass culture, one which presupposes a monolithic cultural homogeneity and an elitist view of the ethics of taste. Students of mass culture argue persuasively that even allowing for the manipulative, dehumanizing, and alienating associations decried by critics, the phenomenon of popular design produces a richness and complexity that earlier authors were too willing to dismiss. As a result the facile dualism of many critics, so essential to the storied history of Modernist art and design, appears less tenable. The appreciation of mass culture is based upon a number of considerations, including a better understanding of the complex and interconnected system of production, distribution, and consumption of cultural products, as well as recognition of the various ways in which an increasingly modern and diverse audience responds to those products. Rather than the presumed passive role for the consumer, students of mass culture suggest that the audience participates, is active in its response to the products of design  thus the product not only is created for an audience, but is also in effect created and shaped by its audience, and expresses alternative preferences and values. One example of the rich and varied reaction to American popular culture was demonstrated during the later 1960s when it was embraced by West German youth as a form of protest against government and corporate regulation of education and politics, or the popularity of Levi-brand blue jeans in the Soviet Union in the post-World War II era as a symbol of freedom of expression. Other observers have described and championed the sociability of mass culture, that is, the way in which it appeals to a broad audience and communicates a variety of meanings. The Marxist critique of popular culture has lost little of its ability to illuminate the underlying structures and mechanisms at work in our daily lives as citizens and consumers in a capitalist world, even if its political motivations and revolutionary aspirations have lost some of their credibility and if angry and revolutionary motivations tended to exaggerate their authors polemical tone. In the end it is perhaps worthwhile to keep in mind the range of responses to popular culture and the dynamic relationship between the culture industry and an expanding, diverse new audience. The middle class found an array of invitations to consume products, react to billboards and advertising images in weekly magazines, listen to commercial radio, attend cinema, and watch television at home. As noted by author and literary critic Clive James (1939 2019), popular culture represented a step up for the working and middle classes, though perhaps not a step toward the enlightened culture envisioned by the advocates of good design who continued to lobby for educating the public in matters of consumer taste. Curiously, while proponents of good design defended their activities on the basis of noble aims that included the education of the masses, the fine arts generally retreated from public engagement. Instead, the emerging New York art world sought a more rarefied atmosphere in which creativity was unfettered either by commercialism, the mass market, or the international standards of good design and its ideology of shared cultural values and social responsibility. Influential American art critic Clement Greenberg (19091994) neatly divided the post-World War II art world into three groups. There was the avant-garde, advocates of high art who were the guardians of individualism and originality in the arts, seeking pure abstraction and autonomy as a field of interest. A second group consisted of practitioners of middlebrow art, who interpreted aspects of high art in more useful goods but undermined and compromised their aesthetic purity and independence. Finally there was low (mass) art, described simply as kitsch, catering to the uneducated masses with the appeal of the traditional and debased borrowings from more authentic forms of expression. Such hierarchical distinctions are not to be found in Russell Lyness essay on taste, relinquishing their authority in the 1960s with the growing phenomenon of Pop Art and the development of more pluralistic approaches to the study of culture. ## Conclusion The post-war period witnessed the expansion of industrial design involving the application of wartime technology to domestic products, fueled by the United States and its investment in the reconstruction of Western European and Japanese peacetime economies through support of manufacturing and corporate free enterprise. There was a broadening of the industrial design profession that included ergonomics, psychology, and a wider range of problem-solving skills pertaining to product development and information design. Industrial design continued to be associated with the names of individual designers, but their activities often involved a variety of skills requiring dialogue and cooperation. The emergence of mass communication also served to affect the design of products for an ever-increasing audience through obsolescence and product symbolism. In the context of Cold War tensions in the later 1950s, consumption, technology, and materialism, whether motivated by aspirations of social mobility or by social responsibility, became increasingly identified in the West with democracy and freedom under a system of capitalist free enterprise. The result was a blurring of the distinction that critics had drawn between commercial styling and the more restrained approach understood as good design. Such critical awareness tended to provide justification for the appreciation of mass culture, while at the same time raised some doubts about the benevolent association between industrial design and social and economic progress. An appreciation of the immediate post-war era includes thinking less in terms of a dichotomy between high and low art than in terms of a continuum in which commercial, aesthetic, reformist, scientific, and technological considerations operated in varied measure. At the same time the activity of design began to overlap into related fields of communication and information, expanding and diffusing the practice and understanding of the term. Whether through corporate identity, complex systems for transportation, consumer products, or advertising, design was seen increasingly as an interdependent rather than an isolated, individual activity within a larger enterprise that connected designers to users through technology, products, services, and the mechanisms of distribution and sales. # Global Inspiration V Japan Japan was an especially active participant in the vibrant network of international commerce in the period from 1945 to 1960, though the nation has had a long history of designing goods for foreign markets, and Western markets have shown a fascination as well as a certain degree of kinship with particular aspects of Japanese design. Japan was central to the history of modern design in a variety of media during the interwar period, for instance in fashion accessories as well as in advertising and the success of department stores in Tokyo and Kyoto. Frank Lloyd Wright visited Japan in 1905 and designed the massive Imperial Hotel and furnishings in Tokyo (1913 1923) that survived the great Kanto earthquake of 1923; and Jan Tschichold illustrated a page of advertisements from a Japanese newspaper as an example of modern asymmetrical layouts in his New Typography of 1928. During the early post-World War II period the phrase made in Japan regularly connoted cheap toys and knock-off electronic products of often inferior quality, manufactured to the specifications of foreign agents and merchandisers interested in competing in the elastic market for inexpensive goods for the mass public that lacked the reputation or quality of the original items they imitated. Design patent infringement plagued the reception of Japanese products, eventually leading to government efforts to create design organizations and policies that emphasized a Japanese identity or version of good design that might compete for foreign markets. In the aftermath of Japans defeat in World War II, the American occupation (19451952) meant that the Japanese people had to work for an occupying military force. Japanese industries provided necessary housing and furniture on an industrial scale for American armed forces and administrative personnel, requiring investment (spurred through the Marshall Plan) in mechanized mass production. In addition to these lucrative contracts, Japanese companies such as Matsushita (later Panasonic) and Nippon Columbia turned their attention to the domestic market for standardized household appliances such as sewing machines and fans. Akio Morita, founder of the Tokyo Telecommunications Engineering Corporation that later became Sony, pioneered portable electronic products in Japan, purchasing the technology for transistors from the American corporation Western Bell Laboratories in 1953. Morita initiated Sonys production of inexpensive pocket radios for the domestic market, with round-edged plastic housing (fig. GI5.2). Signal and volume controls operated by turning crenellated disks that barely protruded to one side of the housing. The station read-out was barely visible, the range of stations limited, the sound quality poor, and in general little attention was paid to usability that industrial designers were exploring at Braun, Philips, and elsewhere in the electronics industry. Yet at the time there was little competition for affordable transistor radios whose portability satisfied a desire for entertainment while on the go, particularly among an underserved youth market who made up in numbers what they may have lacked in purchasing power. Economist Clayton Christensen (b. 1952) cited the Sony transistor radio as an example of a disruptive technology, achieving success and recognition at the lower end of the market ignored by competing manufacturers who devoted more attention to aestheticor performance-based criteria. However, improvements in these areas (such as a more legible read-out and better speakers) bolstered Sonys reputation for industrial design internationally. This extended to the more expensive and highly coveted portable television sets, also initially manufactured and promoted for the domestic market. Sonys portable electronics began to receive attention at international exhibitions including the Milan Triennale and the 1958 Worlds Fair in Brussels. The Japanese reputation for affordable electronic products internationally continued with the Sony Walkman personal tape player in the 1970s. With government support, Japanese manufacturers began to make a conscious, pragmatic effort to enter the export market for the more sophisticated products of good design, focusing upon electronic communications, motor scooters and motorcycles, cameras, and eventually fashion design, ceramics, and furniture. Through sponsored prizes for original products of industrial design, manufacturers were encouraged to hire designers and discouraged from pirating the original designs of other countries. The role of government agencies in the success of Japanese industrial design emerges as a feature of the countrys post-war development, contemporary with similarly sponsored efforts in Britain, Italy, and Germany. The Ministry of International Trade and Industry (MITI), Japan External Trade Organization (JETRO), the Good Design Selection System, the Design Promotion Organization, and the Japan Design House are only the most well known of the agencies responsible for establishing standards for export products, for developing markets for Japanese goods, for promoting design through conferences and competitions, for integrating design as an element within corporate organization, and for helping to sustain a system for design education. The image of Japanese design for the home included points of contact with good design internationally, in particular open, flexible plans for interiors based upon modular units of space, large expanses of glass that permitted a closer relationship between interior and exterior, the use of tatami mats on floors, and a sparing use of simply constructed furniture. These characteristics were presented as innately Japanese, drawing upon historical precedent. Such is the case for the Japanese House that was sent from Japan to New Yorks Museum of Modern Art for display in the museums sculpture garden in 1954 and 1955 (fig. GI5.6). The beauty of natural materials, sensitive craftsmanship, original designs, and simple construction also characterizes the furniture of designer George Nakashima (1905 1990). Nakashima was born in the United States but worked in Japan for an architectural firm after completing studies in architecture at MIT. After World War II (during which he spent a year in an internment camp for Japanese Americans), Nakashima established a workshop for making his own furniture in the small town of New Hope, Pennsylvania (1946). His Conoid Bench with back from 1961 (fig. GI5.8) preserves the irregular beauty of natural forms often combined with slightly irregular and uneven dowel-like supports or rails for chair backs. Nakashimas oeuvre relies upon a balance of modern and traditional approaches to furniture design. Although a small number of early designs for chairs were manufactured by Knoll, Nakashima preferred workshop production and direct contact between craftsmen, materials, and processes. The quiet beauty of his designs may also be appreciated in the bedroom furniture for the Japanese House built on the grounds of the Bloedel Nature Reserve on Bainbridge Island in Washington State in 1961. Set in natural surroundings, the house and furnishings provide a refuge and sanctuary from the nearby city of Seattle. Nakashimas approach to craft emphasized the workmans partnership with materials as related to Zen Buddhist philosophy and the connection between animate and inanimate nature. ![](images/f0d1c31a90c0f236dff0cea039b2356d451566c52446757a2d1a1143a34eb476.jpg) George Nakashima, Conoid Bench, walnut, 32 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>2</sub> × 84 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>2</sub> in (82.6 × 214.6 cm), Nakashima Studios, New Hope, Pennsylvania, 1961. The policies initiated through MITI and other design organizations in Japan revitalized the Japanese economy, raising the income level and standard of living at home, and establishing a reputation for quality, efficiency, affordability, technological improvement, and aesthetic refinement abroad. At the same time the products of Japanese industrial design made personal electronic products a virtually ubiquitious accessory to a modern, mobile, active lifestyle, from transistor radios and portable television sets in the 1950s to pocket calculators and Sony Walkmen in the 1960s and 1970s, karaoke in the 1980s, and compact disk players (the Sony Discman) in the 1990s, all before the advent of streaming. The 1964 Tokyo Olympic Games offered a showcase for Japanese design and featured a carefully constructed graphic identity including a series of pictograms as well as a logo that incorporated the Japanese flag with the five rings of the Olympic symbol that linked Japan to the international community. And, as seen below, in transportation fuel-efficient and economical Japanese automobiles gained a foothold in the lower end of the American market in the 1970s amid rising gasoline prices (see here), making Honda, Mazda, and Nissan (then Datsun) household brands. Whether at the high or more accessible end of the market, Japanese design triumphed after World War II domestically and internationally, accompanying economic recovery and growth. As elsewhere in this survey of the period, the carefully cultivated image of Japanese design for the international market masked alternative approaches at home that included resentment of Japans close alignment with the continued US military presence, and an emerging counterculture movement that questioned the alliance between business and government and used design, primarily graphic design, to work outside of the aesthetic and political parameters of the mainstream professional design community.
