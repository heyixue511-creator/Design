# 530 Edvin Öhrström, Ariel vase for Orrefors, c. 1947 # edvh (b,. 906 ulrlren d  1994) The Swedish glassware designer Edvin Öhrström studied sculpture and graphic design at the Konstfackskolan (University College of Arts, Crafts and Design), the Tekniska Skolan (Royal Institute of Technology) from 1925 to 1928 and the Kungliga Konsthögskolan (Royal University College of Fine Arts) from 1928 to 1932 in Stockholm. He also studied drawing in Paris from around 1932 to 1934 at various art schools, including the Maison Watteau, the Académie Colarossi, and the Académie de l'Art Moderne under Fernand Léger (1881–1955). From 1936 to 1956, Öhrström worked at the Orrefors glassworks on a part-time basis – two months a year – which allowed him time to pursue a career as an independent glass sculptor in his own studio using space and technical assistance provided by the Lindshammar glassworks. Upon joining Orrefors, he quickly discovered “a deep affinity with the new medium”, which led him to begin evolving soft organic forms that were influenced by his previous artistic training. Öhrström developed a novel glass-making technique in which the interior wall of vessels could be decorated by spinning a fluted wooden mould on a wheel. The raised teeth-like elements of the turned mould produced a ribbed pattern of internal refractive rings, the optical effects of which were further enhanced by the faceted cutting of the vessels' outer surfaces, Although the A technique, as it was described by Öhrström, was highly innovative, his Art Deco style vases that incorporated it were not a commercial success and only a limited number were produced. Later in 1937, working alongside Vicke Lindstrand and the master glass-blower Gustav Bergkvist, Öhrström developed another technique for art glass known as Arie/. This innovative production method was developed from the earlier Graa/ technique that had been pioneered by Simon Gate at Orrefors in 1916. Like Graal, Arie/ was an overlay technique in which several layers of glass made up the blank. Some of the layers were engraved, etched, cut or sandblasted to form decorative motifs, which were then sandwiched between layers of clear glass. With the Arie/ technique, however, bubbles of air were trapped between the hollowed section formed by the incised pattern and the external layer of transparent glass. Named in homage to the airy spirit in William Shakespeare's The Tempest, the Arie/ technique was used extensively by Öhrström for his very expressive work, which demonstrated the virtually unique sculptural sensibility he was able to bring to glass-making. Many of his thickwalled Arie/ vases were decoratéd with abstracted figurative motifs of animals, people and flowers, which revealed the influence initially of the work of his mentor Fernand Léger and then later that of Joan Miró (1893–1983) and Hans Arp (1887– 1966). Periodically, these designs also incorporated purely abstract decoration. Öhrström's Ariel vases were not only aesthetically pleasing but also managed to convey something of the molten material they were rendered from – a number of examples almost resonating with the drama of their fiery birth. His work was shown at both the 1937 Paris “Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne" and the 1939 New York World's Fair, where it won much critical acclaim. Although best known for his superlative art glass pieces, Öhrström also designed glassware for domestic use that incorporated simple undecorated forms, while retaining a sculptural boldness.
