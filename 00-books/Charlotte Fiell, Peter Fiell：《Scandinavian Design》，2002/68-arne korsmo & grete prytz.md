He subsequently designed metalware for the goldsmith and jewellery firm Jacob Tostrup (founded in 1832), which was headed by Jacob Tostrup Prytz (1886-1962)who became his father-in-law when he married his daughter, Grete Prytz. In 1937, Korsmo designed the Norwegian section at the “Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne” in Paris. He later received considerable recognition for the silver cutlery he designed in 1949 (produced by Jacob Tostrup from 1953), which was remarkable for its soft, ergonomically-inspired melting forms and was awarded a gold medal at the 1954 (X) Milan Triennale. From 1936 to 1956, Korsmo was director of the Statens Håndverks- og Kunstindustriskole (National College of Arts and Crafts) in Oslo and later became a professor at the Norges Tekniske Høgskole. Apart from his metalware, Korsmo also designed several pieces of wooden and tubular metal furniture. During the post-war years, Arne and Grete Korsmo were Norway's “design couple” – equivalent to Charles and (Norwegian b. 1917) Ray Eames in America and Robin and Lucienne Day in Britain – and as such, had enormous influence on the Norwegian design community. Coming from a well-known goldsmithing family Grete Prytz was the daughter of Jacob Tostrup Prytz, who was the fourth generation to head the family's Oslo-based goldsmith firm, Jacob Tostrup. From 1936 to 1941 she studied at the Statens Håndverks- og Kunstindustriskole (Nation al College of Arts and Crafts) where her father, who was one of the leading advocates of functionalism in Norway during the 1920s and 1930s, taught and became rector in 1945. After completing her studies, Greta began designing jewellery and metalware for the family firm, which often involved enamelling – a technique that had become a Norwegian specialty during the early years of the 20th century and one for which the Jacob Tostrup company was especially well-known. Learning from her family's long-held expertise in this field, Grete revived the technique by using it for contemporary abstract motifs and vibrantly coloured decoration. Between 1949 and 1950, Grete was a Fulbright scholar at the Chicago Institute of Design, and on her return to Norway she ![](images/b9823270643eae622f4d8c31af726e2852a4bdd3ada9235df3711903eba3ebe1.jpg) collaborated with the Sentralinstituttet for Industriell Forskning (Central Institute for Industrial Research) on the development of techniques that would allow large pieces of silver to be enamelled. The results of these experiments, which included her chunky Domino rings (1952), were shown at the “Norsk emaljekunst i lyset” (Light on the enamel art of Norway) exhibition held in Oslo in 1952. Now known as Grete Korsmo (after her marriage to Arne Korsmo), she was awarded the Lunning Prize for her advancement of enamelling techniques and in particular for her introduction of freehand engraving on the “to-be-enamelled” surface using a cutting tool that was similarto a dentist's drill. This innovative process pioneered by Grete enabled a greater degree of artistic freedom than had previously been obtainable. After the death of Arne Korsmo in 1968, she remarried and became known as Grete Prytz Kittelsen. During the 1970s she designed a number of enamelled stainless-steel bowls that were industrially produced by Cathrineholm and which reflected her commitment to the design and manufacture of inexpensive yet high quality products.
