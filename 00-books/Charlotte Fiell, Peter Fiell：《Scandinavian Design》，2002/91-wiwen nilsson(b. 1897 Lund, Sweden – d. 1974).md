He undertook further artistic training at the Académie de la Grande Chaumière and at the Académie Colarossi while working in the Georg Jensen studio in Paris. In 1923, Nilsson began designing for his family's silversmithy and five years later took over the management of the firm. He was awarded a gold medalat the1925 Paris “Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes” for his designs, which were notable for their unadorned surfaces and pure geometric forms. The angularity of his cubistic designs was, however, often softened by a gently curving handle or a simple edge of reeded moulding. His large-scale vessels were distinguished by the use of faceted surfaces and by the adoption of streamlined hemispherical or cylindrical forms. Although never intended for large-scale manufacture, the simplicity of Nilsson's silverware designs would have made them eminently suited to serial production. During the 1930s, Nilsson and fellow countryman Erik Fleming (1894–1954) produced a large number of beautifully executed Modernistic designs that epitomized the Swedish Art Deco style and reflected the growing international influence of functionalism. Nilsson's work was exhibited at the1939 New York World's Fair and included in the landmark Helsingborg1955 “H55” exhibition. The origins of this remarkable Finnish company can be traced back to 1865, when the engineer Frederik Idestam established a wood-pulp mill on the banks of the Emäkoski River in southern Finland. Christened by !destam, the Nokia company benefited from the increasing demand for paper and cardboard that accompanied the rapid industrialization of Europe during the latter half of the 19th century, and exported its products initially to Russia and then later to Britain and France.Soon, a thrivingcommunity grewup around the pulp mill (which eventually also became known as Nokia) and in 1898 a neighbouring manufacturing company was established known as Finnish Rubber Works, which produced a wide range of products including rubber bands, industrial components, raincoats, rugs, balls and other latex toys. During the 1920s, this second firm began selling its products under the Nokia brand name. In 1922, the Finnish Rubber Works company acquired a majority shareholding of the Helsinki-based company Finnish Cable Works (est. 1912), which supplied the cable required for telegraph and telephone networks. Over the succeeding decades the three firms' business activities – paper, rubber and cable – became increasingly integrated and eventually in 1967 the companies merged to form the Nokia Group. Importantly for this new venture, the Finnish Cable Works had previously established its own electronics department in 1960. From 1962 onwards this department dedicated itself to the research and development of radio transmission equipment – including radio telephones that were made for the army, public utilities and emergency services. These important precursors of the cellular telephone utilized pioneering semiconductor technology. It was an extremely opportune time for the Finnish Cable Works to be entering the telecommunications field because semiconductor technology was “making its way from the lab to the industry”, and “not only newcomers, but also the industry leaders at that time had just begun learning the ABCs of new technologies”. In 1969, the recently merged Nokia Group became the first company to introduce transmission equipment based on Pulse Code Modulation (PCM)that met the requirements of the CCITT (Consultative Committee on International Telegraphy and Telephony). Significantly for the company's future, PCM converted analogue sound signals into digital form and because of this, Nokia was able to establish itself at the very forefront of digital technology. During the early 1970s, Nokiadeveloped a digitalized switch, the DX200, for telephone exchanges – the majority ofwhich werestill electro-mechanical and used analogue switches. Thanks to its use of “high level computer language" and Intel microprocessors, the digitalized DX 200 equipment had a remarkablecapacity that could handle the requirements of whole networks and could be easily extended by adding more computersinparallel to the system. Importantly, the DX 200 digital switch developed in the early 1970s remains the basis of Nokia's mobile and fixed networks. One of the reasons why modern design has not flourished in Norway to the same extent that it has in these other Nordic countries is that since the 1970s, Norway has enjoyed unprecedented prosperity through oil revenues, which in turn has meant that the economic importance of design has been overlooked. In an attempt to readdress this situation, especially now that Norway's oil reserves are predicted to become seriously depleted within the next twenty years, the Norwegian government has at long last begun actively promoting Norwegian design abroad. Against this background of state support, the design group Norway Says was founded in 1999 by five young furniture designers who were studying at, or had recently graduated from either the Statens Håndverks- og Kunstindustriskole (National College of Art and Design), Oslo, or the National College of Art and Design, Bergen. Its five members, Torbjørn Anderssen (b. 1976), Tore Borgersen(b. 1966), Andreas Engesvik(b. 1970), Frode Myhr (b. 1974), and Espen Voll(b. 1965), initially formed the group with a view to collectively showing their work at the Salon Satellite at the Milan Furniture Fair in 2000. Sponsored by among others the Norwegian Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the group was eventually “able to put their own prototypes in a van and drive the 2,500 kilometers down to Milan." This first group exhibition included a number of innovative and elegant designs, such as the Dock chair (2000) designed by Espen Voll and Tore Borgersen, the Comfort Living multi-functional chaise longue(1998) by Andreas Engesvik, the 24 Meccano modular storage system (2000) by Torbjørn Anderssen, and the Kazu ceiling lamp (2000) by Frode Myhr. This work consciously rejected “the over-representation of cabin-looking furniture all made of pine” and was born out of the belief that “design is an essential tool in manufacturing good everyday products." Later in 2000, the Norway Says group participated in the London “Designers Block” exhibition.
