One of the carliest examples from the printed page appears in 1831 in an advertisement for a volume by Dr Ludoff de Garbenfeld against tobacco. It shows a girl suffering through the addiction of her suitor to a pipe, and another victim who is himself a sick man through smoking. Its counterpart is an advertisement of 1866 for Les Pipes Aristophane, in which pipes are given human properties; it includes an orchestra of pipes. Both these examples appear in Ernest Maindron's Les Affiches Illustrées, Volume I, of 1886. In the same volume there is a reproduction of an illustrated advertisement of 1702 in which the artist shows an orchestra of cats. This sort of humour, which these early announcements share with the picture postcard, was carried into the first poster designs.
