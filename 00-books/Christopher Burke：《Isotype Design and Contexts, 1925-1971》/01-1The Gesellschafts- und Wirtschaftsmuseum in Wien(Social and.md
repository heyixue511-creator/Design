# 1 The Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in Wien (Social and economic museum of Vienna) 1925-34

Christopher Burke

## Background

The Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum originated from the interaction of Otto Neurath's educational ambitions with the context of Social-Democratic Vienna in the 192os. Its direct predecessor was the Museum für Siedlung und Städtebau (Museum for settlement and town-planning), which had grown from exhibitions prepared under Neurath's direction by the Österreichische Verband für Siedlungs- und Kleingartenwesen (övsk; Austrian association for settlement and allotment gardens). Neurath had co-founded this organization in 1921 to assist in formalizing a grass roots movement of settlers who had been erecting makeshift houses and establishing allotment gardens on the periphery of Vienna since the end of the First World War. Amid astronomic currency inflation, this example of self-help to fulfil the pressing need for housing included elements of a barter economy, which fitted Neurath's brand of socialism.

Neurath had returned to Vienna in February 192o on being released from an eighteen-month, suspended prison sentence in Munich. Having been held in custody for six weeks pending trial, he was convicted there of assisting high treason in his role as president of the Zentralwirtschaftsamt (Central office of economics) during the short-lived Bavarian Soviet Republic.³ Although there is a remarkable consistency in the ideas and principles underlying the varied activities of Neurath's adult life, his participation in Bavarian social reform marked a kind of break with his academic trajectory before the First World War.4 At the time he wrote:

The hesitations and vacillations of those called upon to act, the advice of my friends and sundry accidental circumstances, finally moved me, after much reflection, to conclude my life of contemplation and to begin one of action, to help to introduce an administrative economy that will bring happiness.

It could be argued that the real break occurred even earlier with Neurath's field work on war economy, which led to the initiation of a new department at the war ministry in Vienna during the First World War, and to his becoming the director of a new Deutschen Kriegswirtschaftsmuseum (German museum of war economy) in Leipzig. The ‘aims' and means' of that museum were recorded as follows:

Aims: general education and research in political economy. Means: comprehensive collections of objects of economic importance, samples, models, photographs, statistics, graphic representations and documents, printed matter, cuttings etc. Sections: exhibitions and travelling exhibitions, archives, lecture service, scientific and educational publications.

As Marie Neurath pointed out, these plans were very close to what was later realized at the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum.?

Twenty years later, Neurath summarized this period in his life:

Then came the war. Then my directorship of the museum in Leipzig (14 days Vienna, 14 days Leipzig) and then the testimony about socialization that had such far-reaching consequences for me. Then it all came crashing down on me and even good friends considered any further career as ruined. But then it began anew. After a few attempts, the settlement association and museum. Collapse of the foundations. New construction.8

This architectural metaphor could also serve to describe the hope that Neurath, among many others, felt for the new era arising from the ruins of the old order in Central Europe. Nowhere can this rupture have been felt more acutely than in Vienna, former centre of the enormous Austro-Hungarian empire that was now splintered into nation states. 'Austria' did not yet exist, and its borders remained unclear while peace treaty negotiations continued. On 12 November 1918 a provisional government proclaimed it to be 'Deutschösterreich' (German-Austria), part of the new German republic established a few days earlier. When the Treaty of St Germain was finally signed in September 1919, it outlawed Anschluss (union) with Germany and forced the new, independent repub. lic to change its name to 'Austria'. The first Austrian Federal Republic has been characterized as ‘a mutilated trunk that bled from every vein' and as ‘not only a state which did not want to exist, but a predicament which could not last'.1º This new country was generally felt to be lebensunfähig (unfit to live), cut off from the resources that now lay outside its borders. Essential supplies were scarce during the first few years of its existence: butter and milk were unobtainable; the population was

undernourished and Vienna would have starved without American aid. Until 192o electricity was cut off at 8 pm; neither streetcars nor metropolitan trains ran until 1925 due to fuel shortages.11

Nevertheless, Neurath was fired up by efforts for reconstruction: 'Before 1914 I felt completely helpless and hopeless in the face of the prospects for society. Not that I personally had any excessive complaints. After the lost war there were more difficulties in the world, but more chances that things could change.'¹² A colleague remembered the striking figure he cut in those days:

I just remember the day when I first met you – attending one of your crowded lectures – you with your marvellous beard, wrapped in a k.u.k. 'Attila' (I think that was the proper name for the short officer coats with fur collar) because there was no coal at that time and we had to sozialisieren' without.1³

In this respect, Friedrich Stadler has characterized Neurath as a promoter, innovator and fiery orator before common people, occupying a space between proletariat and bourgeosie'.¹4

On resettling in Vienna in 192o, Neurath attempted unsuccessfully to revive the academic status he had secured at Heidelberg University in 1917, which had been revoked due to his imprisonment in Munich. Instead he turned his formidable energy towards other enterprises, although education remained central in his priorities. The Austrian novelist Robert Musil recorded a meeting with Neurath in his diary around this time, describing him as follows: Has a notebook with very many energetic entries. Matters dealt with are neatly struck through. Is apparently always somewhere else with his thoughts. ... Is now up and about a great deal, setting up contacts in all directions.'1⁶

## Conception of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum

In July 1924, within a year of the Museum für Siedlung und Städtebau being established, Neurath proposed a new museum to replace it. As he later explained, the Siedlungsmuseum already contained ‘a section devoted to the Social Sciences in general because the understanding of city planning has to be based on a better understanding of the pattern of national and world relations'.2 It was this aspect he sought to expand, and so he drafted an outline titled Enlistment of the collections and work of the Austrian settlement museum for the preparation of an exhibition about the Austrian workers' movement', which he sent to Julius Deutsch. The stress he laid in this title, as well as in the outline, on the place of the projected museum within the socialist movement reflected his instinct for the political priorities of those he sought to persuade:

It would be of great importance if we possessed a museum as a means of education, which, directed in the spirit of the workers' movement, could fill the gap left by all that our schools systematically neglect. It is not the lies that are the worst things in schools, but the concealment! One does not learn in school that people get much sicker from tuberculosis in those areas where they are poorest; one does not learn in school of how bad the quality of housing is; one learns nothing in school of slavery, nor of the fight for freedom. Now, a museum should not carry propaganda in a vulgar sense – it must give plain facts! But in the choice of material there is already propaganda. Most museums have a dead effect because they appeal too little to the wants of people! Indeed often a small change would suffice to bring people en masse into museums! If a museum shows, for example, how one can have better housing, clothe oneself better, live more healthily and more purposefully – that would surely be of general interest.27

At this point Neurath was suggesting the name 'Wirtschafts- und Gesellschaftsmuseum' (an inversion of its final title), but the central idea of a popular, educational institution that would 'bring things of daily life into meaningful connections' was already clear.28 In this sense his proposal fitted perfectly the profile of the Social-Democrat regime in Vienna, which laid great emphasis on education: 'Against the idea of force, the force of ideas', was one party slogan.29 Although Neurath held no office in the Social Democrat Party, he was involved in many of its educational initiatives - he even gave classes at the Wiener Arbeiterhochschule, which trained promising young workers to be party functionaries.30

The new museum, Neurath explained, 'must be especially prepared to treat the political struggle of the working class, the development of revolutions, legislation, social policy and much else'; yet he also indicated the broader 'geographical-historical' view that would become typical of work in the Vienna Method and Isotype:

The development of capitalist economic order must be shown in its historical and geographical specificity. It is important to show the whole of the earth's surface with its variety of economic forms in different eras, in order to reflect how the capitalist order gradually takes hold of the whole world and eradicates all other forms of economy.

Additionally, here in his initial outline, Neurath stated the intention to exploit the great experience we have gained in exhibition, museum and advertising technique' at the Siedlungsmuseum, and to build on it. He hinted specifically at the elements of standardization and consistency that would become so important in the Vienna Method:

In order to guarantee that the graphic representation, the method of demonstration, shows a certain unity, the existing depictions and overviews must first be reworked so that the difference between forms of representation do not unsettle the viewer: that applies to the smallest detail. When countries tend to be listed on the vertical axis, it is disturbing when they occur in a certain table on the horizontal axis, merely because this was the case in the source information. Quite apart from this, consistent, connecting colours are important (red for city population, industrial workers etc; green for countryside, etc....)

Already in this early conception by Neurath, the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum represented a unique combination of social commitment and design methodology.

One month later, in August 1924, Neurath rewrote his proposal for the new museum into a Denkschrift (memorandum) addressed to Anton Weber, city councillor for housing. Perhaps, in the meantime, he had been advised by Deutsch to tone down the socialist rhetoric; when sending him a copy of the new memorandum, Neurath explained: 'It is phrased so as to take account of the neutral position of a proletarian municipal administration; on the other hand, you will see that the general conception and consideration of details rests thoroughly on the basis of the workers' movement.'³²Indeed Neurath now emphasized the broader educational purpose of the projected museum. The clarity and detail of his vision were remarkable:

The modern museum should be a teaching museum, a means of education, a schoolbook on a grand scale. ...

The Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum is not a technical museum, the objective of which is to show and explain the achievements of the human intellect insofar as they reveal themselves in machines and tools. The Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum shows social connections. It only takes note of technology as a social phenomenon and reflects its effects on the social life and the spatial distribution of people. 33

Apart from revealing Neurath's suspicion of the technological determinism becoming common in the avant-garde of Central-European culture at this time, this also signals the holistic approach that he maintained throughout his life. Already at the beginning of the First World War, Neurath had written to his intellectual hero, Ernst Mach, explaining how Mach had influenced him to derive the meaning of the particular from the whole, and not the whole from a sum of particulars'.³4 Hence his idea of the Museum as a big schoolbook – addressing various subjects, but under one cover, so to speak. The same approach is reflected in his attitude to town planning: in his article of 1924 about the 'general architectural plan' for Vienna, he suggested that ‘a whole city may be considered as one, single architectonic construction'.³

Neurath recognized that such an ambitious plan for the Gesellschaftsund Wirtschaftsmuseum could only be achieved 'step by step', and that the existing Siedlungsmuseum would serve as a foundation to be systematically built on. The Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum occupied a place firmly within Social-Democratic Vienna, at least during its first five years. Some of its earliest charts were printed in the city's statistical newsletter. The national newspaper of Austrian municipalities stated the following: ‘This Museum is generously supported by the Vienna municipality and will become increasingly important for all municipalities, for it undertakes to represent the municipal, state, cooperative and private economy of Austria in its totality.'⁴ While the outlook of the Museum spread beyond Vienna, it was inextricable from the city's administrative context. Later Neurath reflected: 'Vienna, where we have been in a position to do something for education, presented a kind of island from more than one viewpoint.'4º

Marie Neurath recalled that the Museum's ‘activity, number of employees and stock increased through commissions for many exhibitions', most of which stemmed from the city council.4º Otto Neurath listed three milestones in the rapid development of the Museum's work: the exhibition about ‘social hygiene' in Vienna (1925), ‘Gesolei' in Düsseldorf (1926), and 'Wien und die Wiener' (Vienna and the Viennese, 1927).⁵º The first and third of these took place in Vienna's Messepalast (trade fair palace). He explained that the social hygiene exhibition was the first opportunity for the Museum to show its work to a wide public. For ‘Gesolei' the Museum was asked to 'systematically represent' the achievements of Austrian social insurance.⁵¹ In the same year it also made an important contribution to the Internationale Städtebauausstellung (International town-planning exhibition) by preparing charts, models and plans about Vienna's building programme for display in the city's Künstlerhaus gallery. The Museum's magnetic maps were first shown here: a plan of Vienna was painted on an iron base, enabling a changing display of magnetic symbols. This, along with a backlit map indicating the growth of the Vienna municipality, was a deliberate adoption of advertising techniques for a different purpose.⁵²

## Marie Neurath recalled:

The size of the stands was determined like this: the centre of a chart of 126 cm square should be at the level of an average person's eye; underneath a chart sometimes a table is needed for display. The size of the bay should allow a group of 12–2o people to stand within it so that they can look and discuss together. Very often groups of people came from abroad to see what modern Vienna had achieved, and came also to the Museum; there were also school classes in the mornings, and some of us took them round. Very often this was my lot. Frankly, it was a useful experience. As long as we had a museum we had a testing ground for our work; it could be observed whether a chart was successful or not, what was too difficult, what was stimulating etc.'

(Quotations from Marie Neurath's English typescript of the article published in Dutch, 'Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in Wien en Mundaneum’; ic 8.1a).
