# Cold War, Mergers, and Computers In the summer of 1959, the U.S. Information Agency (USIA) mounted an exhibition in Moscow, the American National Exhibition, as the first of many such cultural exchanges between the Soviet Union and the United States. The exhibit, designed by George Nelson and Charles Eames, turned into the site of a vigorous Cold War debate between Soviet premier Nikita Krushchev and then-vice president Richard Nixon. Featured at the exhibition was an innovative geodesic dome by R. Buckminster Fuller, which attracted worldwide fascination bordering on a space-age fad. Nineteen similar such exhibits in the USSR by American designers over the next 32 years would be seen in a total of 87 cities across 12 time zones by an estimated 20 million Soviet citizens to promote U.S. national political objectives, and to win the hearts and minds of the public with personal cultural diplomacy. Eventually, by 1991, Soviet citizens would be convinced that Soviet promises of free consumer goods would never be realized, and they rejected communism for a more democratic government. Also in 1959, the Museum of Modern Art displayed three Structures by Buckminster Fuller, including his geodesic dome, plus other designs of his from 1927, which included a circular house on a central pole called a 4-D Utility Unit, and the development of a half plane, half car called the 4-D Zoomobile. In Los Angeles, the famous “Skyscraper" furniture designer, Paul T. Frankl, passed away in 1959 at age 73. He had left New York in 1934 to establish a gallery in Los Angeles, where his clients included many Hollywood stars of the late 1930s. He had applied his talents to many projects, from interior design, posters, greeting cards and theater sets, to selecting objects from Europe, the Orient, and Mexico for sale in his shops. His fortunes had shifted often in his career from prosperity to poverty, and his personal life ranged from depression over the death of his first wife, bitterness over divorce from his second wife, and emotional security with his third. A month after President John F. Kennedy was elected president in 1960 came the death of the most highly esteemed and famous pioneer of industrial design, Walter Dorwin Teague, who died on December 5 in Flemington, New Jersey, just two weeks shy of his 77th birthday. Just the year before, Teague had addressed the Royal Society of Arts in London, and in June of that same year, he had spoken to the Detroit chapter of the Industrial Designers Institute. In 1963, the American Society of Industrial Designers (ASID) would honor Teague, one of its founders, with the organization's first scholarship program, the Walter Dorwin Teague Scholarship, eligible to selected junior students majoring in industrial design. Teague's papers are archived at Syracuse University Library, Special Collections, and at his surviving original organization, now called Teague. His son, W. Dorwin Teague, who had worked with his father from 1934 to 1942 and had later rejoined the organization in 1953 as partner, now was number two in Teague's professional office, Walter Dorwin Teague Associates (WDTA), with Robert Harper as CEO and president. Dorwin Teague and other WDTA associates in 1960 received a national design award from the Industrial Designers Institute (IDI) for the design of the Euphorian dental chair for the Ritter Company. A functional patent for it is in Teague, Jr.'s name. That same year, WDTA also designed the new Polaroid Electric-Eye 900 camera and a landmark mathematical model for the U.S. Navy's Missiles and Quality Control System, still in use today. Another IDI award in 1960 went to Dave Chapman, Inc., in Chicago for its research report and recommendations on the design of facilities for U.S. schools, produced for the Educational Facilities Laboratories, Inc., and to William L. Mitchell and the General Motors Styling Staff for the Chevrolet Corvair. The latter, a so-called compact car, was a significant improvement in styling, with no tail fins, but its oversteering tendency earned it the wrath of Ralph Nader, who dubbed it “one of the nastiest-handling cars ever built." His 1965 book, Unsafe at Any Speed, would prod Congress, which in 1966 would pass the National Traffic and Motor Vehicle Act, empowering the government to set safety standards for new cars after 1968, which would soon be enacted. Designs of 1961 included the Tower Capri portable typewriter for Sears, Roebuck by the Sundberg-Ferar design firm in Detroit, which won an IDI national award; the IBM Selectric typewriter by Eliot Noyes, which by 1975 accounted for 75 percent of the U.S. electric typewriter market; and the Singer 660 sewing machine by Henry Dreyfuss Associates. HDA also designed the Civil War Information Building in Richmond, Virginia. George A. Beck, head of GE's industrial design light military electronics department, received one of Alcoa's annual industrial design awards. In a major corporate move, Eliot Noyes was retained by Westinghouse to dramatize its image, including logo and design standards, and to reorganize its internal design departments in order to better compete with General Electric. Also in 1961, Raymond Loewy established Raymond Loewy/William Snaith, Inc., and Raymond Loewy/William Snaith Associates, Inc., with William Snaith as president and Raymond Loewy as chairman of the board. In 1954, the Studebaker Corporation had merged with Packard to form Studebaker-Packard, and with its combined internal design staffs, as well as its financial difficulties, it felt that it no longer needed to pay Raymond Loewy's extravagant design fees, so it canceled his contract in 1956, even though Loewy had designed Studebaker cars for the previous 20 years. Loewy's Studebaker studio in South Bend shut down, and Robert Bourke relocated to Loewy's office in Chicago. But now, in 1961, Studebaker was in serious trouble, and the company engaged a new president, Sherwood E. Egbert, from the McCullough Company, where Egbert had worked with Brooks Stevens on a number of design projects. Egbert engaged Stevens to design the 1962 models of the company's mainstays, the Lark sedan and the Gran Turismo Hawk, with a meager budget of \$7 million to tool both cars. Stevens accomplished the task with minimal changes to the previous year's models, which had been designed by Loewy. But Egbert knew such minor changes were not enough to save the company and wanted to demonstrate that Studebaker could still produce an innovative design, so as a last ditch effort he rehired Raymond Loewy to design a sensational new sports car, which would become the 1962 Avanti. Loewy retreated to his Palm Springs home with designers Bob Andrews, Tom Kellogg, and John W. Ebstein. The resulting design, sleek, graceful, and unlike any car from Detroit, was an instant success, but it was too late for the company, which would fold in 1963. Still, the Avanti would be brought to life again and again by sequential private interests until 1991. John Tjaarda, the exceptional automotive designer of the 1936 Lincoln Zephyr, passed away in 1962 at age 65. In 1961, he had pursued an interest in solid-state rocketry and joined the Aerojet-General Corporation in Sacramento as a technical advisor. His son Tom Tjaarda (b. 1934) would follow in his dad's footsteps as an automotive designer and would establish his own automotive design office in Italy, Dimensione Design, in 1984.1 In 1962, Chauncey "Chick" Waltman also died at age 66. A well-known Chicago furniture and lamp designer starting in 1920, he joined the industrial design movement in the 1930s practicing as Waltman Associates at 165 Chicago Avenue. The office later moved to 310 E. Chicago Avenue. His son, Charles T. “Chubb” Waltman (1919–2006), joined him after World War II, and the office operated until about 1960. Also in 1962, H. Creston Doner chaired the Advisory Board of the Showcase National Corporation for the United States and Canada, and over a number of years, he was a consultant to the architects and designers of the 1964 World's Fair in New York, Expo 67 in Canada; HemisFair 68 in San Antonio; Tomorrowland in Disneyland, California; and the Bay Area Regional Transit (BART) system in 1969. ![](images/330dd2ba605140460ff00f8b1fc9006863f41c2ef82a649260e5b3be1ddfd532.jpg) Chauncey“Chick”Waltman, ca. 1950s (courtesy William Waltman). In the world of expositions, Walter Dorwin Teague Associates designed the U.S. Science Center at the 1962 Century 21 Exposition in Seattle, which was called by some “a virtual cathedral of science." The Spacearium held 750 people for a simulated voyage first through the solar system, then through the Milky Way Galaxy and beyond. In 1963, Walter Dorwin Teague Associates would design the Maxwell House Automatic Coffee Machine for the General Foods Corporation. The new International Council of Societies of Industrial Design (ICSID) convened its Third Congress in Paris, and the Industrial Designers Institute (IDI) held an exhibition at the Louvre, organized by Alfons Bach, who had moved his practice to Palm Beach, Florida, in 1959. Among the large selection of American designs were the 1962 Avanti by Loewy and the 1956 Lincoln Continental Mark II by George Walker and his staff at Ford. By 1963, international design was becoming a common practice. Sixteen American industrial design firms had offices abroad, and manufacturers from 22 foreign countries had contracts with American designers. The Cold War pitted the United States against the Soviet Union in providing a better standard of living to their respective citizens. After Russia's Luna I flight to within 3,700 miles of the moon, Soviet premier Nikita Krushchev had boldly predicted in 1959 that by 1966 the Soviet Union would “catch up” to America and would be distributing basic consumer goods to Russian citizens “free of charge." Industrial designers were well aware that they were now competing globally. At ASID's annual conference in 1962, Raymond Loewy had spoken of such competition: Nothing great has ever been achieved—whether by a nation, a business, an individual—without quality.... Now, we are at a point where that belief is being put to the test. Americans are in a massive race to maintain our leadership against other nations in trade, training, goods, and taste.² Raymond Loewy had just designed the interior and external livery for Air Force One when President Kennedy was assassinated in Dallas, Texas, in November 1963. The Boeing 707 was a specially modified Boeing C-137 Stratoliner. Loewy had met with Kennedy and conducted research in the National Archives for the assignment, for which he did not charge a fee. He found the uppercase typeface called Caslon, which was used widely spaced in the country's name in the first printed copy of the U.S. Declaration of Independence, and used it for the large lettering on each side of Air Force One's fuselage, reading, “United States of America." A presidential seal was on both sides of the fuselage, and an American flag on the tail. The plane took the president to Dallas and returned his body to Washington. The following year, at the request ofJacqueline Kennedy, Loewy designed the 1964 five-cent John Kennedy memorial postage stamp for the U.S. Postal Service. That same year, Loewy established Market Concepts, Inc., a subsidiary for confidential research operations. Now reorganized as Raymond Loewy/William Snaith, Inc., Loewy's office won a national Industrial Designers Institute (IDI) Award for the design of the Time-Master, made by the Dictaphone Corporation, as did William Mitchell of the General Motors Styling staff for the Pontiac Grand Prix. Kem Weber, the pioneer who began designing modern furniture in 1926 and was inspired by the Paris Exposition of 1925, passed away in California in 1963 at age 74. After the war, Weber had moved back to Santa Barbara, California, and worked mostly as an architect, building a handful of houses there and in nearby Montecito. By the late 1950s, his health had begun to fail. Also in 1963, Henry Dreyfuss Associates designed the first touch-button phone, the Touchtone 2500 for AT&T, and the Automatic 100 camera for Polaroid. In 1964, Ford's sporty Mustang won the Industrial Designers Institute's national design award. It was Ford's answer to GM's Corvette and was a huge success with the 16- to 24-yearold baby boomer crowd. George Walker had resigned in 1961, so the Mustang was designed by the new vice president of styling, Eugene Bordinat (1920–1987), and his staff, driven by Lee Iacocca, who had been named Ford vice president in 1960. Even as Studebaker was dying, Brooks Stevens, without Studebaker's agreement, had prototyped a promotional concept car for Studebaker, which was initially called the Mercebaker, a "contemporary classic" sports car based on the antique Mercedes SSK of the 1927–1929 period. Stevens' sons, Steve and David, built the prototype in six weeks during 1964 and were in the process of towing it to the auto show to create a sensation for Studebaker when Studebaker executives told Stevens they would not be showing it. But Stevens knew the director of the auto show, who agreed to display the prototype as a product of Brooks Stevens Associates. The car was so well received that Stevens funded the production of the car by his sons as a business enterprise, renaming it the Excalibur SSK Roadster, which enjoyed success as the first "replicar," a design based on a historically classic car.³ In April 1964, the New York World's Fair opened at Flushing Meadows in Queens, with the theme of "Peace through Understanding," and dedicated to “Man's Achievement on a Shrinking Globe in an Expanding Universe." The fair would run from April to October in 1964 and 1965. The grounds were dominated by a 12-story high, stainless steel, skeletonized model of the earth, appearing to float on a ring of fountains in the center of a circular lagoon. It was designed by Peter Müller-Munk Associates for U.S. Steel and was called the Unisphere, which still remains in Flushing Meadows today. Attendees traveled the fairgrounds on a monorail designed by Walter Dorwin Teague Associates, who also designed the Festival of Gas, including the House of the Future, featuring a gas-powered refrigerator and a gas oven designed for Norge. Most regarded Futurama II as the most popular exhibit, designed by Henry Dreyfuss Associates (HDA) for General Motors. Like its 1939 fair predecessor, designed by Norman Bel Geddes, it was a show in which visitors seated in moving chairs glided past elaborately detailed miniature 3-D model scenery, showing what life might be like 60 years in the "near future" of 2024. Bel Geddes, who had died six years earlier, would have been proud, but he may have wondered why, after 25 years, Dreyfuss hadn't come up with something better. Also in 1964, HDA designed the symbol for American Airlines, the Swinger camera for Polaroid, and the Trimline desk phone for AT&T, which would be accepted into the permanent collection of the Museum of Modern Art. George Nelson won a prestigious Alcoa award for Herman Miller's Action Office I, an innovative system of office furniture, but later it became known that Robert Propst had actually designed it. Like other major designers, Nelson claimed credit for all designs in his organization. Following in the footsteps of Raymond Loewy, Buckminster Fuller, now famous for his geodesic domes, was featured on the cover of Time magazine. Now it was time for the three separate industrial design organizations to get together. After much discussion and negotiation since 1958, the three industrial design organizations, the Industrial Designers Institute (IDI), the American Society of Industrial Designers (ASID), and the Industrial Design Education Association (IDEA), agreed to merge and form the Industrial Designers Society of America (IDSA) in 1965 to provide a single voice for industrial design in America. The first president of IDSA was Henry Dreyfuss, one of the founders of the SID in 1944, and the first chairman of the board was John Vassos, one of the founders of the ADI in 1938 (in 1951, it had become the IDI). Vassos had retired in 1964 as a consultant of RCA design, where Tucker Madawick had taken over as manager of radio, phonograph, tape and television design in 1959. ![](images/3373adc3b01cfd2b6a0614ded891866126b62e7c6d8d0236e376578faf749d51.jpg) John Vassos, ca. 1950s (courtesy Industrial Designers Society of America). IDSA membership at the time was estimated at 650. The first casualty was the IDI national design awards, which had been initiated in 1951. As we know, Dreyfuss did not like the idea of public awards because they insisted on identifying and crediting the specific designer by name. Big-name designers required that any design made in their firm must be credited to the principal's name, no matter who actually designed it. There would be no further national design awards after 1965 until 1980, when Dreyfuss would be gone. Russel Wright was invited to Japan in 1965 to work for more than 100 companies. Later, he would work with the Johnson administration to establish the Park Beautiful Program, forming an association with public land and its reclamation for public use. Alfonso Iannelli passed away in 1965 at the age of77 in Chicago. In 1955, he had sculpted the large-scale Rock of Gibraltar relief for the Prudential building (now called Prudential Plaza) in Chicago, which was his last major work. In 1982, the entire contents of Iannelli's studio would be purchased by Kelms Cott Gallery in Chicago. That same year, Egmont Arens passed away from a heart attack at age 78. He was one of the founders of both the Society of Industrial Designers (SID) in 1944 and the Package Design Council (PDC) in 1952. A month after his death, his widow, Mathilde, published a collection of his early poetry. She wrote that he would often say, “You know, I came to New York to be a poet." His papers are archived at Syracuse University Library, Special Collections. Winners of the 1966 Design in Housewares Award Program included Hamilton Beach's "hole-in-handle" electric knife and its Gourmet Center by Chapman, Goldsmith & Yamasaki, and West Bend's coffee percolator, broiler rotisserie, and Teflon-coated cookware by David Painter Industrial Designers.4 That same year, Raymond Loewy/William Snaith designed the Exxon logotype. George Nelson's office designed an exhibition, Hand Tools USA, for travel within the Soviet Union by the U.S. Information Agency (USIA) as a cultural exchange between the two Cold War countries in 1966. Another USIA exhibition, Industrial Design USA, would also be designed by Nelson's office in 1967. These exhibits, mentioned earlier, demonstrated the power of design as public diplomacy to change the hearts and minds of people and helped win the Cold War. In 1967, Raymond Loewy/William Snaith Associates, Inc., designed the trademark for the Shell Oil Company. That year, Raymond Loewy/William Snaith Associates, Inc., in Chicago was merged with Raymond Loewy/William Snaith Associates, Inc., in New YorkHenry Dreyfuss Associates designed a prototype videophone for AT&T. The HDA organization included James Conner, Niels Diffrient, and Donald Genaro. W. Dorwin Teague resigned from Walter Dorwin Teague Associates in 1967, which had been headed by Bob Harper since Walter Dorwin Teague passed away in 1960. Milton Immermann soon took over as president and CEO of WDTA, and Dorwin resigned to start his own business, Dorwin Teague, Inc., in Englewood Cliffs, New Jersey, which specialized in both industrial design and engineering.6 Peter Müller-Munk, head of Peter Müller-Munk Associates in Pittsburgh, died unexpectedly and tragically in 1967 at the age of 60. Müller-Munk had been instrumental in the formation of the International Council of Societies of Industrial Design. Joseph Platt, one of the founders of the Society of Industrial Designers in 1944, passed away at age 73 in 1968. That year, Henry Dreyfuss retired at age 64, the same year as the tragic assassinations of Martin Luther King, Jr., and Robert Kennedy. These events, the never-ending Vietnam War, antiwar student demonstrations, and desegregation struggles created a sense of national unrest and helplessness. They were emotional and traumatic times that would be lifted only temporarily in 1969, when U.S. astronauts first landed on the moon. The industrial design profession also suffered from mistrust by virtue of its prominent role in the corporate business world, which was now seen by many as being responsible for environmental damage, obscene profits, and neglect of global social responsibility. In 1968, R. Buckminster Fuller was awarded the World Medal of Architecture for his 250-foot-diameter, twenty-story-high geodesic dome that had housed the entire U.S. pavilion at Expo 67 in Montreal, Canada. Fortune magazine, which had introduced industrial design to the business world in 1934, now in 1968, exactly 34 years later, published an article, “The Decline of Industrial Designers," in February, claiming lack of innovation. This seemed to be more a matter of perception than reality. The profession continued as normal, even though many of its famous pioneers were aging or passing away, and design news was dominated by younger personalities, who were as innovative as ever. More likely, the headlines of civil unrest, campus rebellions, and other pressing social issues had pushed industrial designers and design off of the front pages. Many in IDSA were grappling with the organization's declining membership, financial problems, and its diminishing reputation among young designers, who were criticizing IDSA as an “old boy's club." There was a kernel of truth in this, since many of the “old guard” were longing to return to a time in their youth when leading industrial designers commanded top dollar, and were celebrated as national personalities. But this time had passed. Still, some of the "old guard” were still in the thick of things. In 1969, Detroit design firm Sundberg-Ferar designed the Bay Area Rapid Transit (BART) system in San Francisco, the first U.S. regional transit system since 1907. Frank Del Guidice now headed Walter Dorwin Teague Associates, and Raymond Loewy opened his third London, England, office. Nathan Horwitt designed wall clock No. 4601 for the Howard Miller Clock Company. Henry Dreyfuss Associates designed American Standard fixtures and fittings, and the model 500 helicopter for Hughes Aircraft. In Philadelphia, Clyde Shuler, designer of "tombstone" wooden radios of the early 1930s, passed away at age 77 in 1969. While regarded as passé at the time, the gothic shapes of his radios in the early 1930s were simply a logical extension of “skyscraper” furniture design of the 1920s. Shuler had taught industrial design at the Philadelphia College of Art since 1933 and had retired from there in 1952. He had established his own office, Clyde Schuler Associates, at 1717 Sansom Street, working on residential architecture and commercial design. By 1961, he was a partner in the office of his old friend, William Heyl Thompson. Boeing 747 “jumbo jets” made their first flights. Walter Dorwin Teague Associates (WDTA) designed Boeing interiors, and Henry Dreyfuss Associates (HDA) designed American Airlines interiors. Design in Steel design awards, initiated in 1963 by U.S. Steel, were awarded in 1969 to Henry Dreyfuss Associates for a transmission tower for the Southern California Edison Company, and to Sundberg-Ferar for a subway transit vehicle designed for the New York Metro Transportation Authority. ![](images/2bf05b3524e337b2ef4a172409dc75b75a6888f9e1b755f9da4fa257f4116c16.jpg) Carl Sundberg with Bay Area Regional Transit System, 1969 (courtesy Sundberg-Ferar, www.sundbergferar.com). ![](images/ab814f139a3536ac32085022eb6eaf4efff9dc00be32df39e9af761cd95ed6c6.jpg) Playmate cooler, by Marlan Polhemus of GYS. In Detroit, Harley Earl, the man who had most influenced automotive design over the previous 30 years, passed away at age 76 in 1969. They say he is still remembered at General Motors with this prayer: “Our Father, who art in Styling, Harley be thy Name." Amen to that. His private consulting firm had merged with Walter B. Ford II (1920–1991) Design Associates in 1964 to form Ford & Earl Design Associates. Ford had been in GM Styling under Earl. In 1970, Dave Chapman, Inc., of Chicago was reorganized with Paul Specht as president and partner in Goldsmith Yamasaki Specht, Inc. (GYS), which that year designed the ubiquitous Playmate plastic picnic cooler for the Igloo Corporation. Dreyfuss closed his Pasadena office in 1970, and his staff was relocated to 4 West 58th Street in New York, where Bill Purcell and Don Genaro were senior partners and Niels Diffrient and Jim Conner were junior partners. They moved the office to 888 Seventh Avenue with more space. In the early 1970s, Dreyfuss had sold his business to these four partners, and focused on international symbols by himself.7 ![](images/966d83eb4b3d60924a1321e9d8ba774f30566717153c0774fcfb0c7b88aa61fd.jpg) Henry Dreyfuss, ca. 1955 (courtesy Henry Dreyfuss Associates). Dreyfuss, representing the American National Standards Institute (ANSI), chaired the first meeting in 1971 of the International Organization of Standards' Technical Committee (ISO/ TC) in Berlin, which set international standards for 145 signs and symbols. In 1972, Dreyfuss would use these in his publication, the Symbol Sourcebook, a virtual encyclopedia of international symbols. HDA designed the Hyster lift equipment and the Flicker, a woman's razor for the American Safety Razor Company. Sadly, that same year, Henry Dreyfuss and his wife, Doris, both terminally ill, designed their own death by carbon monoxide at their home in South Pasadena. Until his death, Dreyfuss had been a faculty member in the engineering department at Caltech in Pasadena. The personal archives of Henry Dreyfuss would be donated to the Smithsonian in 1973. Henry Dreyfuss Associates, however, remained active and profitable. New associates Jim Ryan and Bill Crookes were hired. Industrial design now came under attack by Victor Papanek (1925–1998), chair of design at the Kansas City (Missouri) Art Institute, whose 1972 book, Design for the Real World, the book jacket of which characterized Papanek as “an ingenious designer who charges the industrial design establishment with criminal negligence of almost everybody," featured such declarations and accusations as, "It is about time that industrial design, as we have come to know it, should cease to exist," and, "Today, industrial design has put murder on a mass production basis." Papanek claimed the moral high ground in his book using his students' designs, which addressed child disability problems, created radio receivers made of juice cans and cottage industries for Third World countries, designed human-powered vehicles, and created packaging made of pea pods. Papanek's idealistic, socially conscious, and antibusiness philosophy appealed strongly to 20-year-old young adults who optimistically aspired to change the world, which to them appeared to be failing in many respects. They felt that their unique role, as a generation, was to challenge authority and the establishment to achieve change of any sort. Within a year, the Vietnam War would be over, conscription into the military would end, and the volunteer army began. The focus of youth would then be more on social and environmental issues. Over the years, change did occur in industrial design as well. Public design emphasis on profits and sales was reduced (although this still occurred), museum preoccupation with aesthetic purity was reduced (although this still occurred), and public commitment to solving social and environmental problems increased. The government increasingly became involved with legislation to address these social and environmental problems, and the design profession participated where it was relevant. In January 1973, the Industrial Designers Society of America (IDSA) relocated its national office from New York to Washington, D.C., where an expanding government was becoming activist in nature. IDSA was receiving grants for working with government programs, such as the Product Safety Commission and Barrier Free Design. IDSA at the time had a total membership of 723 and 10 regional chapters. New York, Chicago, and the West Coast were no longer the only centers of design. Manufacturers were moving south for lower wages, and small, local design offices were popping up all over the country. A 1973 study, "Industrial Design Education in the U.S.A.: A Factual Summary," by Joseph Carriero, chair of the Department of Design and Environmental Analysis at Cornell University, revealed that there were 41 higher education programs of industrial design, and approximately 2,000 students pursuing a career in industrial design, of which 363 were female and 1,737 male. There were also 21l graduate students pursuing master's degrees in 29 programs. Faculty members totaling 1882 were devoted to full-time teaching of industrial design, plus 129 part time, and 200 support people. That same year, Raymond Loewy/William Snaith Associates, Inc., designed the Skylab 2 space station for NASA, launched on May 25, 1973, a project Loewy regarded as the most important of his career. It was the first manned mission to Skylab, and the first U.S. orbital space station. Loewy emphasized habitability and comfort for the astronauts, with a wardroom for meals and relaxation, and a window to view the Earth and space. Skylab missions could last for months. Each astronaut had a private sleeping area the size of a walk-in closet, with a curtain, sleeping bag, and locker. That same year, Loewy signed a contract with the Soviet Union to be its consultant for mass production and over the next few years collaborated on the design of Russian automobiles, farm tractors, cameras, and hydrofoil vessels. Henry Dreyfuss Associates designed an open-sided street telephone booth for AT&T, a Bard Parker surgeon's scalpel, and the Polaroid SX-70 camera in 1973, and in 1974 published Humanscale 1/2/3, extensive ergonomic data and diagrams laminated in plastic for durability and frequent use for industrial designers to determine accurate dimensions for human use of products and equipment. President Nixon resigned as a result of the Watergate scandal, and Gerald Ford, vice president, took the office of president. It was in 1973 that Virgil Exner, who started in automotive design in 1933, passed away at age 64. He had a heart attack in 1956 but resumed work in 1957: In 1962 the president of Chrysler ordered him to reduce the size of their cars. The standard-size models Exner had already designed were downsized by associates over the objections of Exner. When they failed on the market, Exner was fired as the scapegoat. He retired in 1964 and was replaced by Elwood Engle (1917–1986). Buehler Corporation. He also designed “revival cars," including the revival of Stutz in the 1970s. The year 1975 saw the passing of Greta von Nessen in Murraysville, Pennsylvania, at age 75. She and her husband, Walter von Nessen (1889–1943), founded Nessen Studios in 1927 to design lamps. She continued the family studio, which survives to this day as Nessen Lamps, Inc. That same year, Jo Sinel, one of the founders of the Society of Industrial Designers, passed away at age 86. A New Zealander who was designing products in 1923 for an advertising agency in New York, and eventually worked for 55 such agencies, he became a noted industrial designer and educator. His papers are archived at the California College of Arts, where he taught for so many years, and where he received an honorary doctorate of fine arts. The Smithsonian's Renwick Gallery in Washington, D.C., in 1975 mounted a retrospective exhibition of the work of Raymond Loewy, which included his Studebaker Avanti. In 1974, Loewy's partner, William Snaith, had died, and the Loewy firm was nearing bankruptcy. By 1975, Loewy had already established Raymond Loewy International by merging all his coexisting organizations. That year, Henry Dreyfuss Associates designed the Athena 2000 sewing machine, the International Women's Year symbol for the United Nations, and in 1976 Polaroid's Pronto camera. The Concorde supersonic jet began service in 1976 with interiors designed by Raymond Loewy/William Snaith, and the Cooper-Hewitt Museum in the Andrew Carnegie mansion in New York became the National Design Museum, a branch of the Smithsonian. Russel Wright, one of the founders of the Society of Industrial Designers in 1944, passed away in 1976 at the age of 72. He had transformed his 79 acres of quarry land in Garrison, New York, into a natural scenic wonder called Manitoga. His papers are at Syracuse University Library, Special Collections, and at Manitoga, operated by the Russel Wright Design Center. Manitoga is designated as a National Historic Landmark by the U.S. Department of the Interior and is open to the public, with tours and hiking trails. Also in 1976, Walter Dorwin Teague Associates designed the Kenworth Sleeper truck, the first to house a sleeper cabin. The design enabled more effective and efficient long-haul driving. In 1977, the Raymond Loewy/William Snaith, Inc., office in New York closed under Chapter 11 bankruptcy, and industrial design operations were sold to former employee David Butler to form Lister and Butler, Inc. Loewy's offices in Paris, London, and Sweden remained open. In 1976, Raymond Loewy International had been sold to Sheridan Funding Corporation, James Sheridan, president. He established Museum Administration and Planning Services, Inc. (MAP), as a subsidiary and opened an office in Atlanta, Georgia. Function and Vision, Inc., was formed as "debtor in possession" to handle liquidation of Raymond Loewy International, with James Sheridan as president. Sheridan Associates was also established, specializing in interior design.8 George A. Beck, who began his career in 1931, passed away at age 69 in 1977 after retiring from General Electric; Eliot Noyes, the head of the Museum of Modern Art's first industrial design department in 1940, also passed away at age 67; and Otto Kuhler, designer of early streamlined 1930s steam locomotives, who had relocated in 1969 to Santa Fe, New Mexico, and in 1976 to Denver, Colorado, died at age 83. By 1977, the International Style architecture of Walter Gropius and Mies van der Rohe (both had died in 1969), which had been promoted by the Museum of Modern Art from 1932 throughout the 1960s, was coming under increased criticism as sterile, bland, and characterless, or worse, hostile to the natural environment and humanity. That year, the groundbreaking design of the Pompidou Center in Paris, a complex of public library, museum of modern art, and music center, violated all the International Style rules by its dramatic “inside out," hightech look, with exposed plumbing; electrical, ventilation and safety systems in bright color codes; and with exterior staircases, somewhat like antique urban fire escapes. Architects began abandoning the International Style by designing buildings that blended in with the surrounding buildings, rather than standing alone as rectangular, glass-box monuments to modern design. The term “postmodern” began to be applied to such architecture, which was generally described as witty, hybrid, eclectic, and concerned with issues of history, local context, metaphor, and ambiguity. It was a sea change in architectural style that would soon find its way into product design. Henry Dreyfuss Associates designed the Singer Touchtronic sewing machine in 1978. That year, Dave Chapman, who had worked as a designer at Chicago's Century of Progress Exhibition in 1933–1934 and founded Chicago's most successful industrial design firm in 1936, passed away at age 69, but his firm would survive under new management as Goldsmith Yamasaki Specht. Chapman's papers are at Syracuse University Library, Special Collections. Charles Eames, who dominated postwar furniture design, also died that year at age 71. Deskey, Inc., was founded in Cincinnati, Ohio, in 1978 as a division of Donald Deskey Associates of New York. During the 1970s, the computer was becoming the center of a technological and informational revolution. The first prototype personal computer, the Altos desktop, appeared in 1974; in 1975, 19-vear-old William Gates III and 22-year-old Paul Allen founded Microsoft; in 1977, Steven G. Wosniac and 21-year-old Steven Jobs founded Apple Computer in a California garage; in 1978, the first electronic typewriters with memory appeared; and in 1979, Sony introduced its Walkman. By 1983, the first cell phones would appear. These new technologies would generate hundreds of new consumer products, and when they entered mass production, industrial designers would design most of them. Technology continued to provide the raw material that industrial designers transformed into products for humans. By 1979, membership in the Industrial Designers Society of America (IDSA) would top 2,000. It had taken organizational steps over the previous few years to replace its leadership with younger members, and to address some of their concerns. IDSA was no longer a sort of small private club governed autocratically by an older generation, including many of the pioneers, to be sure, but by a more democratic representation of younger chapter chairmen on the board. Membership requirements were relaxed, automotive stylists were included, and it became professionally managed by specialists in association management, rather than by a closed-room board of past presidents. Chapters had doubled to 20, as had membership, and financial health was restored. There were 675 consulting offices nationwide, and over 6,000 industrial designers working within corporations. At the time, it was estimated that there were 10,000 industrial designers worldwide. Also in 1979, Raymond Loewy published Industrial Design, a comprehensive, illustrated book about his design career. He was the surviving member of the original Big Three founders of the profession, and therefore still a public figure who was featured in any historical review of industrial design. The Industrial Designers Society of America initiated annual national design awards in 1980, known as the Industrial Design Excellence Awards (IDEA), which were presented at IDSA's annual meeting that year, and which continue to this day. They were the first such awards since 1965, when the first IDSA president, Henry Dreyfuss, shut down the previous IDI awards. Also in 1980, Henry Dreyfuss Associates designed the Polaroid 600 Sun Camera and published Humanscale 4/5/6, additional ergonomic reference tools for industrial designers, following Humanscale 1/2/3, published in 1974. The final publication in this valuable series would be Humanscale 7/8/9, published in 1991. Helen Dryden, who was the “highest-paid woman artist of 1927” and a famous cover illustrator of Vogue magazine in the 1920s, passed away in 1981 at age 94. Since 1937, when she designed the interior of the Studebaker as Raymond Loewy designed the exterior, the only thing known about her was that in 1956 she was living in a \$10-a-week hotel room paid for by New York City's Welfare Department. At the time, she referred nostalgically to her (1925) \$200-a-month Tenth Street apartment in New York. Other pioneer designers were passing. Both Montgomery Ferar and his partner at Sundberg-Ferar since its founding in 1934, Carl W. Sundberg, died in 1982, both at age 72. Sundberg had retired in 1975. From 1970 to 1980, Ferar had taught industrial design at the Center for Creative Studies in Detroit. The firm they established continued active practice after their death and is still in business today. Raymond Dietrich, the well-known automotive designer, passed away at age 88 in 1982. He had left Chrysler in 1940; founded Ray Dietrich, Inc., in 1949; worked for Checker Cab in 1953; and for the Lincoln and Mercury divisions at Ford until his retirement to Albuquerque, New Mexico, in 1969. His wife, Marion, donated his papers and records in 1995 to the Gilmore Classic Car Club of America Museum at Hickory Corners, Michigan. In 1983, George Nelson installed an exhibit, Design since 1945, organized by Katherine Hiesinger, at the Philadelphia Museum of Art, along with a 247-page, illustrated catalog of the exhibition by Hiesinger. The exhibition and catalog were a sort of obituary of modern design or “Good Design" as defined by the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) from 1950 to 1955. At the time of the exhibit, museums were no longer trying to dictate good design but were focused on its history, a sure sign of age. As early as 1979, architectural and design critic Ada Louise Huxtable (1921–2013) had written about the difference between the Bauhaus/Museum of Modern Art concept of industrial design in the 1950s versus the pragmatic and successful reality of industrial design as developed and implemented by the designers of the Machine Age: Those who think they know how it was in those days [the 1950s] are in for a surprise. What was admired then is discarded now, and what was rejected is being revived.... Industrial design, that curious amalgam of promotion, marketing, technology, and taste invented by the 20th century to serve and shape a consumer economy ... [whose practitioners] shared the optimism of the architectural theorists ... who believed that the marriage of art and industry could provide answers for most of the ills of society, or at least put beauty and utility within the reach of all. But while the tastemakers were in hot pursuit of a mythical "machine art" realized largely in handmade prototypes or limited production for educated users, the industrial designers were successfully creating mass-produced objects for a universal market.9 However, the pragmatism, simplicity and repetition of so-called Good Design had its limits. The design community was searching, as it often had, for new styles, new looks, and new directions.
