![](images/2030575fcf902f9295d5e18247ebdb741c312f7325b68d31acd4e666b9991e7e.jpg) Johan Rohde, Model no. 62 Acorn flatware for Georg Jensen, 1915 # johan rohde(Danis 1856-1935 Having initially trained in medicine as a young man, Johan Rohde developed a strong interest in the arts and in 1881 enrolled at the Kongelige Danske Kunstakademi (Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts) in Copenhagen to study painting and graphics. The following year, he established the Kunstnernes Studieskole (Artist's Studio School), where he taught anatomy classes to the art students. Six years later, his paintings were exhibited for the first time at the Royal Danish Academy. In the mid-1890s, he metGeorg Jensen while he was still working as a foreman in the metalware workshop ofMogens Ballin (1871-1914). Rohde subsequently commissioned Jensen in 1903 to fabricate a number of objects that he had designed for his own personal use. Slowly a friendship developed between the two men and around 1906, Jensen persuaded Rohde to produce several designs for his newly established silversmithy. The following year, the Danske Kunstindustrimuseum (Danish Museum of Applied Arts) in Copenhagen staged a one-man show of his work, and from 1908 to 1912 Rohde headed the Kunstnernes Studieskole. In 1915, Georg Jensen introduced Rohde's most famous flatware design, known as the Acorn pattern, which remains one of the firm's best-selling products today. The highly stylized Acorn pattern can be seen as an evolution of Jensen's more naturalistic Arts & Crafts style designs. Although Rohde was Jensen's closest collaborator, it was not until 1917 that they entered into a formal contractual agreement together. During the 1920s and 1930s Rohde's silverware designs, such as his exquisite pitcher from around 1925 or his spunsilver teapot with ebony handle of 1933-1936, became increasingly simplified and organic in form. Apart from his well-known silverware patterns for Georg Jensen, Rohde also designed textiles and furniture that reflected his own highly personal and idiosyncratic style. Much of his early furniture, including his sumptuous sofa of 1897–1898, was distinguished by a simplified geometric vocabulary of form. Like many turn-of-the-century Scandinavian designers, Rohde mastered a number of different materials by initially creating objects for his own home.
