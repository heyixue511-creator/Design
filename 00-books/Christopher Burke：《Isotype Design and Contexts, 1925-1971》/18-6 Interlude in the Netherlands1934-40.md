# 6 Interlude in the Netherlands 1934-40

In 1934 the International Foundation for Visual Education in The Hague became the base for a small group from the Gesellschaftsund Wirtschaftsmuseum in Wien: Otto Neurath, Marie Reidemeister, Gerd Arntz, Erwin Bernath and Josef Scheer. Among the first projects undertaken there were two books that broadened the international dimension of their work: International picture language and Basic by Isotype, both written in Basic English, a stripped-down version of the language developed by Charles Kay Ogden. It was in this context that Marie Reidemeister thought of a new name to replace the Vienna Method of Pictorial Statistics – Isotype, an acronym for International System of Typographic Picture Education.

The Foundation in The Hague did not benefit from any government subsidy equivalent to that given to the Vienna Museum, and so the first few years in the Netherlands were difficult financially. Additionally, Neurath was often occupied with organizing conferences of the International Institute for the Unity of Science. His frequent visits to the USA during the 193os resulted in several substantial jobs being carried out for American clients (see chapter 7). Some commissions in the Netherlands took the work into new areas: there was a foray into children's theatre with a multi-layered production of a Punch and Judy'-style puppet show; and the first Isotype exhibition about art, 'Rondom Rembrandt'.

Exhibition work in the Netherlands and in the USA maintained continuity with the work done previously in Vienna. The team made plans and photomontage visualizations of ideal exhibition layouts, in the hope of re-establishing a permanent exhibition centre. Otto Neurath pursued this ideal for the rest of his life, yet it was never to be achieved.

## Basic by Isotype 1937

This book is a pictorial/verbal primer of Basic English, presenting vocabulary and simple formulations in Basic with accompanying visualizations in Isotype. The examples in the book are all descriptive ('boy on a bridge', 'boy among girls’); emotions are not dealt with (there are no pictures for 'happy boy' or 'sad girl'). This accords with Ogden and Neurath's shared principle of separating 'the symbolic and the emotive' functions of language.

The book is atypical of Isotype in its attempted equivalence of words and pictures. Their functions were usually complementary in Isotype work.

## Rondom Rembrandt (Around Rembrandt) 1938

The exhibition 'Rondom Rembrandt' was innovative in several ways: it did not include original artworks but instead examined the historical background and social context of Rembrandt's work; and it was not housed in a museum or art gallery but instead was shown simultaneously in three branches of the Dutch department store De Bijenkorf.

Otto Neurath explained the curatorial approach: 'We object to a mixture of original paintings and explanations, we want to separate explanation and immediate impression, therefore no originals within the room, where the explanations have to be presented, only copies, in colours or in black and white.' (ic 3.2/42)

The idea for the exhibition occurred to Marie Neurath when, as a visitor to a major exhibition of Rembrandt's paintings in Amsterdam, she was struck by how little had been done to help the public understand them. In 'Rondom Rembrandt' techniques,such as colour coding and interactive apparatuses were employed to make the subject appealing and understandable to a diverse audience.

## Which belong together?

These columns of grouped buildings, ships, and paintings formed part of an interactive apparatus. Visitors to the exhibition were asked to choose which groups they thought belonged to the same historical period. When a button underneath a column was pushed (as shown in the diagram on the left, T1814), a recording of 'right' or 'wrong was played. (The right answers are columns 2, 6 and 7.) The apparatus represents a pioneering example of a technique that has since become familiar in exhibitions.
