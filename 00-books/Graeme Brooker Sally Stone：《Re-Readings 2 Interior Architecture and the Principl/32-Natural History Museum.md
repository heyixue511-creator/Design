# Natural History Museum

Location Berlin, Germany

Remodelled 2012

Designer Diener & Diener Architects

![](images/9fdb92afcc5507d09dfbbeebc1a79bdbf5bea57dbac162c659a7dc7b1ac3bfc3.jpg)

Like any other instrument (including language), the window is an apparatus that separates and unites.

– Georges Teyssot. The Topology of Everyday Constellations. <sup>40</sup>

Figure 3.77 The ruined East wing.

Openings in a building’s façade, such as a window, are usually made in order to facilitate light entering a space, and to increase the possibility of fresh air being invited into the interior. Windows are often considered vital when organising the design of a building, and a particular view enclosing a specifi c point or vista can make the interior space unique, as well as connected to the outside world. When the specifi c characteristics of a window are removed, and light and air are considered harmful to the contents of the interior, then the tactics of making openings within what is extant takes on a new and unusual signifi cance in the remodelling of existing buildings.

The Berlin Natural History museum has existed since 1889, but in the early

20th century its East wing was shattered by a direct hit from a bomb. The resultant destruction of the building left just a shell of the wing extant, with few sections of the outer walls remaining. In the latter part of the century a competition to renew the ruined wing was arranged, but due to fi nancial constraints it took many years for the winners Diener & Diener’s ideas to be fully realised.

The original East wing was the host of a large collection consisting of 276,000 zoological specimens. These were housed in differently sized glass containers, all of which were fi lled with 70% proof alcohol to preserve the samples. The collection was deemed highly sensitive, and had to be stored in a dark, carefully controlled climatic condition.

The specimens are very important and are still studied as signifi cant exemplars of natural history by the scientists of the museum. Therefore the samples needed to be accessible to the scientists and at the same time viewable by museum visitors,

and the design brief stipulated that their conservation was of utmost importance.

The contradiction of displaying a collection of sensitive specimens in a visible yet environmentally controlled environment was not lost on the designers. In response to these constraints, the winning entry achieved these conditions by rebuilding the East wing on three fl oors, each with ceilings of up to six metres high, of which only the fi rst fl oor was open to visitors. The wood and glass shelves of the display cabinets extend from fl oor-to-ceiling but can only be viewed from the outside by the public. The design allowed the scientists to carry on their work amongst the samples inside the display room, separated by walls of glass vitrines fi lled with the specimens preserved in alcohol. Lighting was installed in order to emphasise the dramatic effect of the spectacle of this relationship between viewer, object and worker.

The rooms housing the collection were conceived and constructed as closed cement containers, as though precious jewel boxes were inserted inside the building protecting the contents. In order to heighten this sense of an interior treasure box, the interior walls were painted dark red, similar in colour to the velvet used to line caskets.

Fig. 3.78 In-fi lled windows imitated the existing windows of the wing. Subtle variation in colouring illustrated the distinction between what was new and what was existing.  
![](images/7d2bac5b4ee49be59482d74e1e33ea87257f6217229128f851bd417a00d5247d.jpg)

In order to protect the sensitive contents of the interior, the designers constructed an exterior that was directly infl uenced by the dimensions and details of the ruined walls of the East wing, but which would effi ciently control the climate and atmospheric conditions of the space. They did this by rebuilding the demolished walls of the wing, carefully mending and contrasting the new walls with the old, and copying the scale and proportions of the existing with new-build.

In this reconstructed wing the designers chose to imitate the openings of the demolished walls, and removed the normal light- and air-affording properties of the windows. They did this by infi lling the existing openings with brick and then, where necessary, created new windows from cement that replicated the exact details of the old. An extremely subtle variation in the colour of the brick, for the new façades of the wing, was used to signal the intervention of the new-building into the old. This ensured that a reading of the history of the building had been inscribed into the construction of its façades. The new window design resisted this reading; instead the bricked-in sections look more recent whilst the preserved windows appear older. The reconstructed sections, replete with windows and frames (albeit in cement) appear somewhat older. The colouring of the new and old sections compounded the confusion that arose from this reading, with the grey-brown bricks which were used to repair the old sections standing out, whilst the grey cement for the new was deliberately subdued. This confused situation was highlighted by the

The ability to refl ect change, along with the capability to express the brand and the relationship between customers and the objects they are consuming, is considered central to the design of any retail environment. The numbered panels echoed the range of stock. They were symbolic of the timebased qualities of the 24 brand, itself an implicit reminder of the chronological dimension of the passing seasons of fashion design and production.

Openings create relationships between people and things. They do this by both physically and visually connecting people, spaces and objects, forming and sometimes reinforcing interactions between all types of human activity in a number of ways and in differing environments. In domestic spaces, the delineation of openings becomes hugely signifi cant. They will reinforce or obscure distinctions between the public and private aspects of the home. Openings such as windows and doorways, along with walls that have openings within them, can be organised and constructed in such a way as to either separate or connect the many forms of occupation that are being undertaken within them. This can be done in a permanent or a temporal fashion. Historically, domesticity was undertaken in one open space, with little or no recourse to

private or separate spaces. Subsequently walls have been constructed to divide the home into specialised areas. In more recent times it may be argued that developments in technology and our changing social relationships, as well as a change in attitudes towards privacy and societal structures, have encouraged a return to living in one large space. However we consider our domestic arrangements, openings, technology and furniture service and facilitate our public and private needs in our living spaces.

In order to accommodate maximum fl exibility in a domestic space, and explore new ways of inhabiting a home, Reinhardt Jung carefully analysed the requirements of the clients of two neighbouring apartments they were commissioned to design in central Berlin. They surmised that the

Within what were essentially two well-proportioned rooms of the block, the designers created a fl uid interior that contained spaces for living, sleeping, and the preparation and consumption of food, as well as the normal provision for services such as a bathroom and a shower.

The centralisation of all of these services allowed the interior to be free of clutter and mess. Separating and at the same time uniting the front and back spaces of the apartment, and in turn allowing a degree of fl exibility between private and public space, the designers constructed a large rotating wall. This was a screen that when in operation created a series of openings between the private sleeping spaces of the apartment and the social sides of the rooms. In this interior a screen wall based opening has been utilised in order to create a fl exible aperture that can be fi lled or opened and which blurs the boundaries between living, eating, sleeping and abluting.

Light enters through the window. The interior maintains its intimacy. The exterior landscape is a painting on the wall whose frame is that of the window. – Elias Torres, Zenithal Light. <sup>43</sup>

Openings can demarcate the thresholds between inside and outside. A door or a window will separate as well as connect both interior and exterior environments. A window will admit light as well as allow a view from both within and outside. This view will change throughout the day, and at night will offer a very particular aspect of the life of the inside of the space. Through providing a boundary between interior and exterior environments, openings can

initiate movement, and establish rhythm in a space via the framing of a view that can invite the beginning of a journey, from outside to inside and vice versa. Whichever way openings are utilised, their realisation and focus is often accompanied by an ambiguity, a spatial opacity that confers importance upon what can be seen beyond their delineating qualities.

Black Pearl was a project initiated and designed by Rolf Bruggink to create a studio and a home for himself in a suburb of Rotterdam. Based in an abandoned ‘Metier’ dwelling, a run-down, turn-of-thecentury, ex-municipality house located in an ungentrifi ed suburb of the city, Studio

Rolf was invited by the council to purchase and convert the three storey building into one large house. Acquiring the 180 square metre building for the derisory sum of €35,000, Bruggink eviscerated the interior of the former three-apartment building, creating a large fi ve metre wide, ten metre long and 11 metre high volume within the existing walls of the building: making a large open space behind the enigmatic façade.

Acting as both client and designer, Bruggink could fashion the space to his own exacting requirements. The lower fl oors contained a studio and workshop for Bruggink. Large doors opened onto the street. At the back of the house another set of doors opened into a small courtyard garden, fi lled with bamboo and slate tiles from the roof of the building before it was renovated, scattered across the fl oor. A long stair, placed against a whitewashed brick wall ascended to the fi rst fl oor living and dining spaces. The study and bedroom was placed on the second level, and was accessed by a stair placed against the opposite wall; one that was left completely uncovered, exposing the wear and tear of the building and the scars of its history of uses and subsequent transformation. The upper level was arranged around a walk-in closet and bathroom, formed into a sculptural cube that appeared to be suspended in the space. A private stair from this level, positioned back against the whitewashed wall, lead to a rooftop terrace and a bathroom, provocatively positioned in a glass house. This allowed views across the surrounding garden and the rooftops of the adjacent houses from the centrally placed jacuzzi.

Figure 3.86 In stark contrast to the serious black façade, the gable end of the building was clad playfully in artifi cial grass.

In order to signal the new use to the street, Studio Rolf changed the external appearance of the building by reworking both the gable end and façade of the structure. The gable was covered in artifi cial grass and new glazed openings were set within it. The façade was rendered in black shiny oil, covering the windows and any details of the building. This suppressing of the architectural features created what Bruggink referred to as a ‘shadow’ of the original façade. All of the brickwork, fenestration, and window details, including the frame, transoms, mullions and ledges, were silhouetted in black. With little regard for the existing fenestration, new apertures were brutally cut into the façade. Overlapping with the existing windows, the new interior spaces were externalised through the new openings. The new windows announced a very different way of living, and created a dynamic relationship between the original façade and the new interpretation of the building.

In this project the old and the new were juxtaposed in a straightforward manner, with a series of powerful and confi dent new openings incised upon the fabric of the building.

## Movement

## Introduction

Throughout the 20th century, the stair as an element of architecture has maintained the importance which has been attributed to it since the Renaissance. Such a status still derives from a combination of utility, or functional considerations, with an inherent aesthetic potential in terms of both the articulation of the structural form, and its further enhancement. – Michael Spens, Staircases. <sup>44</sup>

Stairs, corridors, bridges, lifts, escalators, walkways, ramps; there are numerous ways in which movement will take place in the built environment and in the interior. Whether public or private space, all of these elements facilitating movement, circulation, route and journey, will have been carefully considered and designed to facilitate their particular function in an effi cient, safe and economical manner.

Like all of the six tactics explained in this chapter, movement will often be considered in conjunction with other tactical ideas. Objects placed in specifi c spaces will be organised in relation to how people may circulate around them. Planes such as fl oors, walls and ceilings will be designed to accommodate fl ow, as will surfaces, openings and the articulation of light. Therefore it is important to note that movement and the elements that facilitate it are one part of the tactical jigsaw that the designer of inside space is required to assemble.

Figure 3.87 BUDA Arts Centre, Kortrijk, Belgium. 51N4E.

The design of a bespoke stair is one of the most invigorating challenges for a designer of the interior. Whilst appearing simplistic, its key elements, combined with the relevant code and regulations prescribing the correct measurements for tread, riser and balustrade, provide a multitude of ways of realisation of what can be a mundane journey between fl oors or an epic fl ight of fancy between spaces. Whichever way stairs are utilised, their design and their placement in the space will provide the designer with not just the movement they require but also an opportunity to articulate space through the rhythmic journey that an ascent or decent of a staircase can provide. This can be seen in both the BUDA art Centre by 51N4E and in the Caixa Forum in Madrid by Herzog & de Meuron, where the quite differently constructed stairs were each utilised in order not only to celebrate the spaces they were in but to offer very particular and distinct views through the interior environments in which they were moving.

The design of lifts and escalators are the overlooked elements of interior movement. Often, off-the-peg or readymade solutions are the workhorses of mostly large public interiors such as airports, shopping centres and train stations. These are environments where large numbers of people need to be processed effi ciently and safely. Occasionally they will be deployed with some thought in their design and positioning.

![](images/4ce5467f3896efd8d7c7b39666a90751f4068dedefddc99049e6c86ad1563265.jpg)

The unforgettable escalators of the Lloyds building in London, by the Richard Rogers Partnership, provided a metaphorical focus for the atrium of the banking building. The exposed cogs and wheels of the workings and mechanics of the robotic stairs signalled to the traders using them how the fi nancial markets they were manipulating constantly fl uctuated like the stairs moving up and down between fl oors.

As soon as [architecture] is built it becomes the scene and sometimes the scenario of comings and goings, of gestures even, even of a succession of sensations.

– Pierre Von Meiss. Elements of Architecture. <sup>45</sup>

Walkways, bridges, ramps and corridors facilitate circulation and movement between spaces in a way to articulate sequence and in some cases heighten sensation. In the Kunsthal in Rotterdam by Rem Koolhaas, the floor of the side gallery was articulated in a steel mesh, creating a journey across the space that was not for the faint-hearted (or for the wearer of a kilt or a skirt). The large open squares of the mesh dematerialised the floor, collapsing the distinctions between solidity and floating, as viewers in the gallery below would have a view up through the extremely tall space of the quadruple height gallery.

Whichever movement tactic is used, it does not need to be banal or mundane. Movement through a space, when articulated with thought and creativity, can make space exhilarating and dramatic.

## Casa C Apartment

Location Milan, Italy

Remodelled 2010

Designer Francesco Librizzi

![](images/b8db019c0ce933bbe5900ad7db7dd37dbf36b146d3585094527dea330db7f240.jpg)

One of the key components of movement in interior space is the staircase. Its carefully considered balance of proportion and geometry, combined with stringent regulatory requirements and codes, is often the result of design and the combination of four key elements: risers and treads, stringers, and a balustrade. Their combination will lend the space, as well as the user, a particular interior rhythm. Whether off-the-peg or bespoke, lightweight or monumentally heavy, it is an element of interior space that has fascinated designers for a long time. Hence its appearance can undertake myriad forms even when constructed from a few simple elements.

Experiments with this type of vertical movement through a space have often revolved around the complex mix of imagination with the harsh realities of the relevant building regulations. This combination of poetry and pragmatics can vary, depending on the user, and whether in a public or a private space. For instance, the design of stairs in a domestic space can take on a different trajectory as their public duties are relinquished, leaving them freer of some of the more burdensome regulatory requirements of particular measurements of riser, tread and balustrade. Therefore domestic space stairs can sometimes prove to be the elements of movement in the interior where experimentation and imagination can really take fl ight.

Figure 3.89 As the user descends from the mezzanine to the lower level they are neatly framed within the slender lightweight steel stair sculpture.

In a small, 45 square metre apartment in Milan, Francesco Librizzi has experimented with a stair that gives its user access to an upper level mezzanine sleeping space. The intimate proportions of the apartment were contrasted with the high ceilings of the space. A full height slot in the wall, separating the two main rooms of the space, suggested an opportunity to utilise the upper heights to gain more space for the occupant. Therefore a mezzanine, providing a sleeping room, was placed above the kitchen. Access to it provided the next challenge.

The designer developed a slender lightweight vertical steel stair, linking the lower fl oor and the new sleeping level. The proportions and geometry of the stair refl ected the simple confi guration of the space of the apartment. Librizzi pared back the basic elements of the stair; (tread, riser, balustrade, and stringer) to become as economical as was possible in their barest essentials. He then reordered them into a tall and slender steel frame, constructed with a 14mm solid section steel bar. The thin treads were welded to a stringer, one that also diagonally braced the box, connecting to a landing and affording the slender construction some stability as it connected back to the wall. Each tread connected to a side bar that reinforced the other side of the frame. The side bracing carried onto the top of the frame, reinforcing the solidity of the construction and lending some stability, as well as something more reassuring to grab onto, as the user ascends the stairs to the mezzanine level and to the bed.

The stair was fi xed to the fl oor and to the ceiling. This afforded the object some structural stability as it transfered its user from the landing to the mezzanine. When in use, the dynamism of the occupant of the apartment is palpable as they carefully and deliberately ascend or descend the stair. The slender, deliberate and economically rational stair neatly frames the space and the user in a static, thin frame of this adroitly considered sculptural construction.

## Hankai House

Location Japan

Remodelled 2010

Designer Katsuhiro Miyamoto & Associates

![](images/0f2ae5144af8f1aedd0f9035709cab348b558ef4bde621fba81b6f5534b5dfa2.jpg)

The ramp is an instant creator of scenography. It strives towards architectural infi nity. It generates mythical proportion…it directs the view skywards, leaving unknown what is at the end of the road.

– Koolhaas, Elements of Architecture.<sup>47</sup>

The Japanese word Hankai can be translated into English in a number of ways. It can be used to describe a partial understanding of something or it is used to depict something that is ‘semi-civilised’. In this project Katsuhiro Miyamoto used the word to illustrate the ‘partial destruction’ of a building, utilising it in order to give the semi-demolished building on the site a title that encapsulated its own demise, whilst at the same time signifying its survival and a possible future new role after the disaster that had befallen it.

The existing building was a traditional 300-year-old wooden gatehouse that was partially destroyed in the Great Hanshin earthquake of 1995. Its ruined status ensured that any changes to the building needed to be robust and at the same time needed to restructure the building in order to halt its collapse and demise. As well as saving the building from collapse the new additions needed to ensure its future survival by protecting it against any further earthquakes. To achieve this, Miyamoto fi rst of all stripped away the extraneous elements that had grown up around the original square building. He did this in order to reinstate the original orthogonal and traditional four-room home.

Figure 3.93 Views from the ramp frame the old building.  
![](images/5e7faff00dfadb9befa4e7b4937d9052836987789357fe0973d85b3a5d4837b5.jpg)

In order to stabilise the house, and then more importantly to ensure it would survive any future seismic activities, Miyamoto wrapped the old building with a new volume. The construction acted like a ‘collar’, or a beam that rings the old building and reinforces and protects it from any lateral or vertical movement. Whilst acting as a structural device, the new insertion also solved some of the access and servicing requirements that the old building lacked. The new element contained new circulation and services such as bathrooms and storage. On the top level, where it was separated from the original house, it provided a terrace and a private room. Both overlooked the street below. The new wing followed the exterior boundary of the old property and created a new gatehouse, reinstating the original function of the ancient building by creating a new boundary threshold.

Whilst the memories of the Hanshin earthquake may fade, the house contained the effects of the disaster in its reconstructed self. In this project the utilisation of a ramp that wrapped the building, as well as stabilising it, helped to restore a sense of domestic protection, whilst at the same time ensuring that the occupant was aware of the volatile nature of the environment in which they were housed. The new home was once again made habitable by a new structural device containing circulation and services that were nonetheless haunting reminders that a rapid destruction of the spaces in which they feature could take place at any time.

## BAYSIXTY6

![](images/a733f013e3f5e7580ce3edd5f90c984f34a038d11d5c98c2b5ced67855a65d32.jpg)

The interior is often designed from the point of view of the occupant, as they traverse its spaces. But movement in the interior does not always have to be considered from the perspective of an occupant being on foot.

Figure 3.95 The cavernous skatepark underneath the huge expanse of the Westway above.

The legendary BAYSIXTY6 Skate Park has occupied a unique location under the Westway fl yover in London since the late 1990s. But whilst the world of skating and the design of parks has since moved on and become more complex and sophisticated in their demands, BAYSIXTY6 did not. In order to ensure that the much-loved park continued to inspire future riders, it needed to change. For Adam Brinkworth, a keen skateboarder, it was a dream job.

Appointed by brand/event agency Urb-Orbis on behalf of Nike, Brinkworth were presented with a unique opportunity to help resurrect one of London’s most iconic skate parks: updating it for a new generation of skaters. Challenged to develop a scheme that encouraged a spirit of inclusion and community, Brinkworth reworked the complete layout of the site to offer the best possible skateable area, whilst improving the amenities offered to enhance the experience for both skaters and nonskaters alike.

Consolidating the new ramps and obstacles to a single area of the park ensured that the expanse of the site could be celebrated, championing the park’s core activity – skating. Importantly, lines of sight were designed to be uninterrupted across the entire park, save for the impressive structure of the Westway supports, maximising the visibility for spectators during competitions and demos and at the same time also ensuring safety for the participants.

Brinkworth worked closely with one of the world’s best timber skate park designers and builders, Skatepark Amsterdam (NDSM), who were tasked by Urb-Orbis to design, develop and build a set of ramps and obstacles. To achieve this, a number of unique features were specifi cally produced, catering for a broad range of skaters across all levels of skill and ability. A hydraulic box allowed the height of one to be adjusted at the push of a button – meaning it could be set to suit any level. Another was a new kicker ramp, directly in front of a viewing window, offering spectators the chance to catch a glimpse of boarders as they fl ew past. And an impressive bowl featuring innovative hexagonal corner panels offered the smoothest and most fl uid ride possible.

To complement the vast improvements in the skateable areas, Brinkworth developed the facilities to include a shop and a canteen that doubled up as an educational space, housed within brightly painted green shipping containers. These were positioned to back onto the ramps with a window framing sightlines down the spine of the skate park making it an ideal shelter for spectators in any weather conditions.

These new facilities were strategically positioned around a central courtyard – a communal zone clad in recycled decking and containing bench seating, for visitors to congregate to socialise or unite as part of organised events. The clean and utilitarian space was intended to be a fl exible venue, and a blank canvas to host events such as premier skate fi lms or exhibitions.

The shop, developed by Brinkworth specifi cally for BAYSIXTY6, mixed concrete wall panels and walnut shelves to offer a sophisticated pallette, not regularly associated with skate shops, further refl ecting Nike’s commitment to elevating the overall perception of the park.

One of the most iconic parts of Brinkworth’s scheme was a graphic approach to lighting throughout the site. The creative yet functional application of fl uorescent tubes followed the contours of the Westway’s dramatic concrete underbelly overhead.

BAYSIXTY6 was transformed into an inclusive and stimulating skate park, animated by the numerous riders who use it daily – ensuring its legacy will continue to inspire generations and attract more and more skaters to the ramps and facilities.
