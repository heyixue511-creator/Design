The conflict over the use of the Jänecke ink, described in Chapter Four, resulted in a serious rebellion by the pressmen in Sussex Cottage, and only by threatening to close the Press altogether was Morris able to persuade them to adopt the ink he wanted. Afterwards ‘the number of sheets turned out per day was extraordinarily small', according to Walker's sarcastic summary of the episode.4 The perspective that Walker is able to offer on Morris's printers is helpful in this case because, although he became strongly identified with the private-press movement, Walker, unlike Morris, was also accustomed to producing books under normal commercial conditions. The Kelmscott Press, notwithstanding its modest financial success, represented a special kind of experiment that would not have been possible in any ordinary printing establishment. When Morris was unhappy with the appearance of a trial page, he sent it back to be redone for the sixth time. When he discovered an error on the first sheet off the press, he had the passage reset while the pressmen stood idly by. This pursuit of perfection both impressed and horrified visitors to the Kelmscott Press. Sparling, rather complacently, tells the story of a head of a large commercial printing firm, who watched Morris's compositors carefully setting, and stilī more carefully justifying, line after line; looked with a discontented eye at the pressmen heedfully pulling sheet after sheet, minutely examining each one to see whether it were up to the mark; and as he left, summed up his impressions: "We-e-ll? One writer, so overwhelmed by the medieval splend– our of the Press, even made Kelmscott House four hundred years older than it was.4 In fact, as we have seen, Morris was using nineteenthcentury hand-presses - Albions - that had been commonplace fixtures in nearly every English printing office until the middle of the century. The hand-press did of course seem slightly archaic by 1891, but it was still widely used for pulling proofs, printing posters, and producing limited editions; hence we must avoid the vulgar error of admiring Morris's reversion to medieval technology. It should be observed that Morris had no great difficulty in finding printers who were skilled in the use of the hand-press, for what he revived at the Kelmscott Press was a handicraft whose survival had been threatened only within the previous three or four decades. Nevertheless, Morris was engaged in a deliberate simplification of the printing process when he founded his Press. But, unless one returns to scribal methods (which Morris always longed to do), books cannot be manufactured without machinery of some sort; even an Albion press is a machine, simple though it may be. The choices that Morris made in acquiring equipment for the Kelmscott Press were complicated by his well-known aversion to mechanical devices, yet his hostility appears to have been directed primarily against what he called the great intangible machine of commercial tyranny' rather than this or that tangible steel and brass machine'.46 His types were cast mechanically; his inks were manufactured in large factories; he used photography constantly as a labour-saving device at the Kelmscott Press; Frank Colebrook records that Morris even visited and admired the modern printing establishnent of R. and R. Clark in Edinburgh, and that a Linotype salesman who called at the Press positively escape[d] with a whole skin'.47 Morris's attitude towards Victorian technology was altogether more ambivalent than some of his doctrinaire disciples supposed. The small cottages strung along Hammersmith's Upper Mall in which Morris housed his Press were as unpretentious as the equipment the printers used. The rent book4⁸ for 16 Upper Mall, the first home of the Kelmscott Press, shows that Morris's tenancy began on 12 January 1891; it ended in June of the same year, because in the meantime (from 2o May⁴9) Morris had begun leasing Sussex Cottage, 14 Upper Mall, which remained the primary building ofthe Press until its closing. In 1893 Morris persuaded Cobden-Sanderson to lease 15 Upper Mall, opposite Sussex Cottage, for the Doves Bindery, with two rooms to be reserved for the Kelmscott proof-readers: one of Cobden-Sanderson's workers recalled that ‘this brought Morris in and out many times a day, and with his marvellous interest in the detail of all craft-work, he made himself a familiar figure to the employés . . .'.sº This, however, was only a temporary arrangement, for on New Year's Day 1895 Morris took possession of still another small house, 21 Upper Mall (Sunderland Cottage), which was used for the large Albion press printing the Chaucer and for Cockerell's office. W. Irving Way, the American publisher, visited the Kelmscott Press in April 1895, and the vivid (albeit slightly sentimentalized) description he offers is probably of Sunderland Cottage: No sign marks the Kelmscott Press, . . . but . . . the right one is finally reached, and here Mr. S. C. Cockerell . . . greets the visitor with a smile, and at once puts him at ease. A quiet, tidy, orderly place it is, but with nothing modern about it. The furniture is plain deal like the floor, and with a “gray goosequill" the visitor registers his arrival in the guest-book." ## 率 The modesty of the Kelmscott Press's machines and buildings, though it had an ideological basis, also reflected Morris's limited goals. His original intention had been to print only a small number of books — just enough of each title, perhaps, to distribute to his friends — and he did not at first envision beconing his own publisher. Morris's publisher during the 187os and early 188os was F. S. Ellis, but in 1888 he turned to Reeves and Turner, who operated a bookshop and small publishing establishment in the Strand.² Thus there was nothing unexpected about Morris's decision to allow Reeves and Turner to publish most of the early Kelmscott books. Nevertheless, those close to Morris grumbled occasionally about the inefficiency they detected in his publishers. ‘You don't see [Morris's] books on bookstalls and I think it's for want of a businesslike publisher,' Burne-Jones commented; Jane Morris, in a similar vein, described them as the most unbusiness-like publishers but extremely honest'. Reeves and Turner published eleven of the Kelmscott Press books, the last of which was More's Utopia, issued in September 1893, and Morris's connection with them was officially severed in June 1 896.4 His relationship with Reeves and Turner, while it lasted, seems to have been happy, but he was uneasy about any suggestion that the Kelmscott Press books were less than completely his own productions. Reeves and Turner were merely the distributors for the Kelmscott Press, not the publishers in the usual sense, and Morris emphasized this in the formula he adopted in the Kelmscott colophons: ‘Sold by Reeves & Turner.' (In the proofs of The Defence of Guenevere that line read Published by Reeves & Turner' but was changed.) I don't mind having a publisher', Morris declared, ‘so long as he has nothing whatever to do with any question except purely business ones.' One possibility that he considered briefly was to print a book and then sell the entire edition at auction, thus eliminating the need for a publisher at all.s6 Four of the Kelmscott Press books were published by Bernard Quaritch, who, following his arrival from Berlin in 1842, had rapidly made himself London's leading bookseller. To his first English employer, Henry G. Bohn, Quaritch announced, ‘Mr. Bohn, you are the first bookseller in England, I mean to become the first bookseller in Europe.'7 Energetic, aggressive, learned, Quaritch was a very different person from nice, inoffensive old William Reeves, and there were serious misunderstandings between him and Morris from the beginning. Since the price of The Golden Legend was not specified in the contract, Quaritch raised it on the day of publication and eventually doubled it. ‘The trade, being dissatisfied with the terms which Mr. Q. offered to supply them, did not push the book at the time, & the result was that a good many copies remained on Mr. Q's hands,' Cockerell wrote two years later.s8 There was other evidence of what Morris took to be sharp dealing by Quaritch, and early in 1893 he reached a decision that infuriated Quaritch. Yet there was little inducement for Morris to seek the services of another publisher, for J. and J. Leighton, his binder, performed some of the same functions as Reeves and Turner (the books were sent directly to buyers and book dealers fron Leighton), and Morris was eager to replace, as much as possible, the middlemen between the craftsman and his customers. Acting as his own publisher, as Sparling pointed out, also gave Morris exact information with regard to the real and immediate sale of each book, and thus placed him in a position to guard against over-printing'.º Many of the Kelmscott Press books, especially the 16mos and octavos, were completely sold before they were off the press. Any advertising of the books was at first done by Quaritch, and Reeves and Turner, but in May 1892 Morris issued a simple, two-page list indicating the titles already and soon to be printed at the Kelmscott Press, and periodically thereafter he announced his books by means of these lists (which grew longer and more elaborate) and similar circulars describing individual volumes, some of the latter containing specimen pages. We only send out special announcements of books which are not nearly sold out when they go to the binder,'Cockerell explained.63 They proved to be an effective, low-key method of advertising: in all fifty-one such circulars were issued by the Kelmscott Press, and they are prized by collectors today. Because the Kelmscott Press was operated in the glare of publicity – the British Bookmaker (July 1892) summed up a popular attitude when it declared simply, ‘The doings of the Kelmscott Press are of interest' — Morris did not have to advertise his books widely. Every bibliophile, bookseller, and librarian in the English-speaking world seemed to be aware ofthe existence of the Kelmscott Press. Even when the Press was attacked in print, its fame increased. And strong attacks there were: someone writing in 1895 under the pseudonym ofArthur Pendenys' addressed an open letter to Morris which began, I presume that the Kelmscott Books are published for your own amusement, because I have inquired extensively and find they do not amuse any one else. They are very dull books, much more dull than Law books and Medical books, and there are only two on your list, the Shakespeare Sonnets and the Keats, which I should prefer to a Post Office Directory.'4 The letter went on to condemn Morris's prices, types, and every aspect of the Kelmscott books. Morris was presumably enraged when he saw this abusive yet amusing document, but he might have found consolation in the commonsense principle — subsequently elevated to a divine truth in Hollywood — that bad publicity is better than no publicity at all. None the less, it was necessary for the Kelmscott Press to communicate with its potential customers in some systematic fashion. Despite the commercial success of the Kelmscott books in general, there were naturally variations in this pattern from one volume to another, and Morris never ceased to worry about the possibility of printing too many copies. The first Kelmscott book, The Story of the Glittering Plain, stunned everyone when the entire edition was disposed of long before publication — indeed, long before even the price was announced by Reeves and Turner - and Morris found himselfshort of copies for his friends.7² Another of the early books, The Poems of William Shakespeare, was probably the most popular volume ever issued by the Press. At the other extreme, The History of Godefrey of Boloyne, as glumly predicted by Quaritch, sold so badly that Jane Morris had to donate the overstock to British public libraries in 1897. Morris abandoned one of his announced books, Vitas patrum, when the advance subscriptions proved to be too meagre. The last circular announcement distributed by the Kelmscott Press - dated 25 February 1898 — reveals that, as the Press was winding down its operations, it still had on hand copies of the following books: The History of Godefrey of Boloyne (two), The Well at the World's End (eleven), Sidonia the Sorceress (six paper, two vellum), and The Tale of Beowulf (thirteen). William Morris, of the Kelmscott Press, has modified these shapes from the originals, but in all the essentials they belong to the Jenson font. The form is round and clear, with great boldness and dignity; the lines are full of strong black and white effects, adapted to old-style work. Jenson derived his Roman characters from a mixture of alphabets of various countries, modelling these letters into marvellous perfection. The original font is on about a 14-point size, and consists of twenty-three capital alphabets (J, U and W then not being in use); twenty-three lower-case characters, fifteen contractions, six double letters and three points (period, colon and interrogation), making a total of seventy-three punches. Aldus Manutius finally inherited these punches, matrices an types, and later became famous for the fine alphabets designed by himself. The attempt to lessen Jenson's fame as the originator of the present forms of Romans by crediting their use to the Weidenbach monks, at their monastery, in 1465 (five years before Jenson's publication), has not succeeded with thoughtful students. Under various names many of them became available throughout Europe. Eventually some of these reproductions reached England, where Morris and Cockerell struggled to keep them off the market. Early in 1896, for example, Cockerell was consulting a solicitor about the piracy by an American firm of a great quantity of Kelmscott initials & ornaments' which were being sold in England (Cockerell diary, 15 and 2o January 1896). A more serious problem arose in December 1897 when Cockerell discovered that Sir Charles Reed and Sons, Morris's own typefounders, were advertising an American imitation under the name Kelmscott Old Style' in their latest specimen sheet. Cockerell, always an alarming adversary when he thought Morris's interests were in jeopardy, moved against the Reeds at once. Threatened with legal action, they agreed to withdraw the offending specimens, to announce that the name of Kelmscott could not be used by any customers who had already purchased the type, and to surrender the matrices to Cockerell. It was total capitulation. Reeds have climbed down & are reprinting their spec[ime]ns but I don't feel very warmly towards them,' Cockerell wrote grimly.89 The anxiety that Morris felt about American imitations of the Kelmscott types and books indicates more than an artistic concern that his designs might be used inappropriately. Like the Victorian industrialists that he condemned, Morris was trying to protect his market against cheap foreign imports. This point is worth emphasizing because, whenever one attempts to assess the business side of any of Morris's activities, the evidence suddenly becomes obscured by a smoke-screen of socialist piety. Jeanette L. Gilder, for instance, interviewed Morris in the last year of his life and recorded this characteristic exchange: I asked Mr. Morris to tell me, if it was not an impertinent question, whether it paid him pounds, shillings and pence to print such handsome books. He answered that it did; but his secretary [Cockerell], who had just entered the room, begged leave to contradict this statement. “You have not," said he, “counted your work and your time, Mr. Morris. You are thinking only of the mechanical part; but if you put in your time and your skill, at what you would be paid for them by another person, you could hardly say that your publications made money."Mr. Morris smiled but said no more on the subject. The profit did not seem to interest him very much: it was the artistic results which he accomplished that pleased him.'9 Cockerell's point is of course well taken: Morris's own invaluable contributions were never appraised in calculating the profits and losses of the Kelmscott Press, and we must never forget the genuinely impressive idealism that lay behind the whole of Morris's career, including the Kelmscott Press. Nevertheless — whatever Miss Gilder may have supposed — he was indeed preoccupied with the profits made by his books. This is not to say the profit motive was his main concern at the Press, which really was, as he said, a typographical adventure and therefore financially risky, but Morris never lost sight of the need to sell books and to pay his employees. This unexpected combination of hard-headed business sense and Utopian idealism has puzzled many admirers of Morris, and it has prompted others to accuse hin of hypocrisy. Morris himself, as one might expect, had thought long and deeply about the problem. He argued that, until the socialist revolution came, the employer was obliged to work within the capitalist system, no matter how much he might personally despise it.9 What Morris failed to take sufficiently into account was how tainted even the most pure craft activity might become through commercial considerations; this, after all, is what happened to the Kelinscott Press, which began as an attempt to restore aesthetic (and moral) integrity to bookmaking and ended as the publisher of expensive books purchased mainly by wealthy collectors. Morris's friend and fellow-socialist, Walter Crane, saw very clearly the extent to which the achievements of craftsmen and artists were coloured by the corrupting ethos of the market-place. My difficulty', he wrote to Ashbee in 189o, . . . is how to establish any good system of working or teaching while the present economic system exists to work its destruction ultimately. Do what one will it seems one is bound to be devoured by commercialism in some form or another."92 Another source of difficulty in analysing the financial state of the Kelmscott Press is that Morris, despite his business acumen, took a decidedly casual attitude towards record-keeping. When he sent Crane a cheque for his share of the profits from the second version of The Glittering Plain, Morris promised to get an account made out which I shall take care is not too much humbugged by the accountant, who (any one of them) could easily make out that there was no profit at all. But I will see that it is all fair, as I really know what the profit is effectually.'93 There was a confusing overlap between Morris's personal bank account and that of the Press, and during Sparling's time as Secretary the financial records, like much else at the Kelmscott Press, were in disarray. In future we may be able to keep a closer record of expenditures than hitherto,' Cockerell reported to the Press's accountant in August 1894, shortly after replacing Sparling.94 In addition to invoices and various other kinds of scattered records, there exists a ledger (in the Morgan Library) describing the cost of production of all the individual Kelmscott books printed between 1893 and 1896 as well as other profit-and-loss accounts and inventories; but the ledger has unfortunately not yet been subjected to careful scrutiny by scholars familiar with nineteenth-century bookkeeping conventions, and so it is not now possible to say with absolute certainty how often, and to what degree, Morris realized a profit on his books. The safest generalization one can offer at present is that he nearly always broke even (bearing in mind, again, that his services were unpaid) and that sometimes he came out slightly ahead. Selecting one title completely at random, for instance, one can readily calculate, based on the most conservative assumptions, that Morris's gross receipts from More's Utopia, an octavo published in an edition of 3oo paper copies (thirty shillings) and eight vellum copies (ten guineas), could not have been less than £3oo, whereas it cost only about £255 to produce. Morris was eager to explain to journalists that he was not a bloated capitalist: You have, no doubt, heard a great deal of the enormous profit I must be making on these books,' he told an interviewer in 1895, because of the high prices I ask for them. I assure you that, if the people who go about talking of my profits were to see my balance sheet, they would speak quite differently.'9s The two examples he cited were Beowulf, which he said he sold at a loss, and the Chaucer, which represented Morris's largest investment and thus caused him much apprehension before it was finally published. When he feared that word about his ink troubles with the Chaucer might spread to the public, he wrote in great agitation to F. J. Furnivall (who had a notoriously loose tongue): I must beg you not to say a word about it to anybody or you will do me very serious injury.' Only after the pressrun of the Chaucer was increased from 325 to 425 copies did Morris feel secure from financial disaster. On the other hand, Morris sold his smaller books without difficulty and, as a matter of deliberate policy, used them to finance the printing of the folios and quartos. I count rather on these little ones for getting cash to carry on the Chaucer, as the little books pay me much better than the big,'Morris admitted to Quaritch.97 There is also evidence to suggest that Morris squeezed every penny he could from publishers who commissioned Kelmscott Press books: Maud, for example, cost him only £2o2 14s. 1d. to produce (according to the Press ledger), yet he charged Macmillan £4oo for the paper copies alone.98 It is obvious that the slow, careful methods of design and production followed at the Kelmscott Press increased the cost of the books, and there are thirteen known instances of alterations being made in the Kelmscott volumes after printing had begun, usually involving the resetting and reprinting of a signature or a pair of conjugate leaves.99 Another unusual expense at the Press was Batchelor's paper, for which he charged about five or six times the price of machine-made book paper; Walker pointed out that the cost of the paper in a single copy of the Chaucer was approximately 25s.1º On the other hand, Morris recovered some of this expenditure by allowing a smaller trade discount than was usual, a policy which caused occasional friction between him and the booksellers. For Reeves and Turner, and Quaritch, the discount was 2o per cent; for all other booksellers, and for a few friends like Robert Proctor, it was 15 per cent. There is an undated Kelmscott Press circular printed in the Golden type and addressed ‘To the Trade only' which reads as follows: ‘'We send you the enclosed, and shall be glad to receive your orders. The discount to the trade will be 15 per cent., and we must ask you to understand that these teris are strictly for cash. We shall write you when the book is actually ready for delivery, and on receipt of your cheque your order will be executed.' The tone of this communication would not have been uncongenial to Dickens's Mr Bounderby. Beneath the beauty and elegance of the Kelmscott Press books lay a hard, flinty foundation of commercial shrewdness.
