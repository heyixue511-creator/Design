As the decade progressed and the political situation on the continent continued to deteriorate, international ties became increasingly difficult to maintain and the possibilities for artistic collaboration in the form of publications and exhibitions quickly dried up. Arntz, Alma, and Tschinkel did participate together in one last group exhibition in Amsterdam in 1936 – an anti-fascist exhibition titled 'De Olympiade onder dictatuur' (Olympics under dictatorship; for which the acronym Dood ominously spelled out 'death' in Dutch), organized to coincide with the Berlin Olympics.6⁸ The three artists remained active as painters and printmakers through the remaining years of the decade, though each survived primarily on commercial work, which largely involved the design of statistical charts. Of the three, only Arntz followed Neurath to The Hague, where they continued a collaboration that produced the major publication Modern man in the making (see pp. 325-9 below). ## Conclusion

The relationship between figurative-constructivist graphics and pictorial statistics may best be understood as one in flux. But as the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum's expanded activities brought other members of the Progressives to Vienna (who in turn began to promote pictorial statistics in their own publications and saw in the Museum an opportunity to promote their free work), the connection was increasingly framed in terms of shared values. Such values included a belief in art's communicative and social-educational function, and the conviction that education could be reconceived in more democratic and participatory terms. In promoting these values, both projects sought to create strategies of presentation that would communicate social content at the broadest level possible and provide audiences with new analytical tools with which to reflect upon social issues that affected them. Above all it was in their intention to appeal to a mass audience at an intellectual level that both projects most clearly reflect the shared belief that the general population possessed the ability to draw well-informed conclusions and participate responsibly in a democratic society. However, after the dissolution of the Progressives group in 1933 and the forced exile of the Vienna Museum's core team (including Arntz) in 1934, the initial excitement over the two projects' potential connections began to abate. By the mid-193os, Alma and Tschinkel appear to have backed away from the claim made earlier in their writings that sociological graphics and pictorial statistics represented two fronts in the same struggle. The short-lived period of their collaboration in Vienna, from 1929 through 1930, represents the high-water mark of this notion.

## Photography at the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum

The Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in Wien kept extensive files of photographs, mostly taken by its staff photographer Walter Pfitzner (right). In addition to the photographic record of its pictorial charts, known as T[afel] files, there were the N[atur] files, which documented aspects of life in Social-Democratic Vienna. Achievements of the municipality were photographed, including its housing programme and provision of modern Kindergartens. In addition, several sequences of photographs documented professions for use in vocational guidance seminars. Otto Neurath described this part of the Museum's work:

A photographic studio is engaged mainly in producing factual photographs – not intended to evoke aesthetic sensations, but to emphasize the more significant aspects of the object, in preparation for drawings that will further simplify the outlines for graphic presentation. ('Museums of the future' [1933], p. 479.)

This implies that the photographs were used as source material for the design of pictograms (see p. 92 above); but also that the aim in making the photographs was an objectivity akin to that of the 'New Photography' developed in the 192os. Pfitzner's attempts to capture typical actions, particularly in the photographs of professions, can be likened to the typological portraits by August Sander published in his Antlitz der Zeit (Face of our time; 1929). Indeed Sander was an associate of the Group of Progressive Artists (three of whom contributed to the Vienna Method), and he visited the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in 193o. (cB)
