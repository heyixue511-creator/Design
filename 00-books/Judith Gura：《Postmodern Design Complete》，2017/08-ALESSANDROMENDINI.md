![](images/e0c0a047a0fbab9cdf1de804668cf3dba4dd6b07a037c15c66da317b68a2f65d.jpg) # ALESSANDRO MENDINI b.1931 ITALIAN AVANT-GARDE SPOKESMAN. CELEBRATED AS AN INFLUENTIAL EDITOR AND CRITIC AS WELL AS DESIGNER. A contemporary incarnation of the Renaissance man, Alessandro Mendini (also see under “Living with Postmodern Design") has exercised his creative talents in virtually every area of design, despite the fact that he originally aspired to become a cartoonist, His accomplishments in architecture, urban planning, furniture, and product design parallel a career as a critic and editor of three of Italy's most influential publications. In that position he was perhaps the most influential of all postmodernists in spreading the new gospel of design and inspiring a generation of practitioners across Italy and internationally. A native of Milan, Mendini trained at the prestigious Politecnico di Milano, but sees himself as a generator of ideas rather than exclusively a designer or architect. From the i96os on, as a member of two important Radical Design groups, he was an articulate and highly visible spokesperson for the Italian avant-garde: a founding member of Global Tools\* (i973), he was later a partner in Studio Alchimia\* (i979) with ETTORE SOTTSASS (also see under "Living with Postmodern Design") and others, where he explored “banal design" in objects like the black-and-white-patterned Ollo collection (i979). When Sottsass left to form MEMPHIS (see under "Edited Design”), Mendini declined to join the group, though he contributed to Memphis's first collection. While Memphis intended to sell its products, even if to a limited market, Mendini preferred to focus on design that bypassed commercial considerations. From his earliest work, Mendini showed a fondness for flamboyant gestures and borderlinekitsch. His designs are always visually arresting but are often ironic or allegorical commentaries that combine concept with function. His “redesigns," which include the Kandissi sofa and a reworking of Marcel Breuer's Wassily chair, are improvisations on existing archetypes, whose witty embellishments are implicitly critical of the originals. The Poltrona di Proust (ı978), a piece that Mendini has referred to as a “hermaphrodite design" and a "fake antique,"36 is a replica eighteenth-century armchair painted all over with a multicolored, Pointillist-style motif. It has become an iconic image of transgressive Italian design, and is still in production. Mendini's editorial career began in 1970, when he left an architectural office to work at Casabella, where he remained until ı976. In 1977, he founded the magazine Modo. Mendini documented the emergence of Postmodernism in those critical years, promoting its designers and their designs, including his own. In so doing, he was chief proselytizer for the style to a broad international audience. During his years as an editor, Mendini continued his other activities. In i983, he co-founded with ANDREA BRANZI (see under "Design") the Domus Academy in Milan, the first school ever to offer graduate degrees in architecture and design. The firm's architectural projects included the Casa della Felicità (ı988) in Lago d'Orta, for the Alessi family, to which he asked fellow postmodernists ALDO ROSSI (see under "Architecture'), Ettore Sottsass, and ROBERT VENTURI, among others, to contribute. Mendini also coordinated the design of the Groninger Museum in Groningen, Netherlands (1988–94). He designed one wing of the building himself and invited PHILIPPE STARCK (see under "Design"), Coop Himmelb(l)au\*, and MICHELE DE LUCCHI (see under “Design")—architects of entirely different attitudes—to design others. The whole is a tour de force of brilliantly provocative architecture. Mendini continues to negotiate the boundaries between art, architecture, and design, moving effortlessly between them and contributing to each. And while others of his compatriots and collaborators now reject the term Postmodernism, Mendini embraces it.
