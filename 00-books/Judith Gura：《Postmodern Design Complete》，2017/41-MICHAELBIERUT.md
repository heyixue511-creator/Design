# MICHAEL BIERUT b.1957 AMERICAN GRAPHIC DESIGNER WHOSE STRIKING DESIGNS COMBINE LEGIBILITY WITH HUMOR. ![](images/c5afc10fb58fd4f1288a00b54b80a8d1a7f7281c31a4c037a350de12b98024a9.jpg) If he hadn't happened on a book about graphic design in a small school library in suburban Ohio, Michael Bierut might never have found his way to what was then a relatively new profession. To him, Aim for a Job in Graphic Design/Art suggested that there might be a career in art-making that actually had a function in the outside world, rather than leaving one languishing in the studio or cloistered in a museum. He immediately decided that was what he wanted to do. He enrolled in the University of Cincinnati, graduating in ı98o from the graphic design program at its College of Design, Architecture. Art, and Planning. After an internship with a Boston public television station, he moved to New York for his first real job, with Vignellı Associates. Massimo Vignelli, with his wife and business partner Lella Vignelli, had emigrated from Italy to become a leading name in the design profession and was known for graphics with a spare, modernist aesthetic, a strictly limited color palette (mostly black, white, and red), and an allegiance to the Helvetica typeface. Bierut remained at Vignellı for ten years, becoming vice president of design, but developed a broader design vocabulary than that of his mentor, expressing himself in work for friends and non-profit groups. When it came to corporate projects, his goal was not only to make the printed pieces legible, but to make them something people actually wanted to read. He became as much a communicator as a designer, with the result being that his designs are invariably eye-catching and often sparked with wry humor. Bierut is a compulsive collector of information, always armed with a schoolboy's notebook in which he jots down ideas, inspirations, and interesting things he hears through the course of a day. That accumulated knowledge, which has filled more than one hundred notebooks, informs the work he produces. "Being interested in things," he has said, "makes you a better designer."14 Bierut's work became more varied as he explored the possibilities offered by the computer, which came into use just as a postmodernist worldview was making unconventional design acceptable. His flexibility of style brought him assignments from both luxury and mass-market clients. Though not generally considered transgressive, he is postmodern in his versatility and his openness to new ways of doing things. ![](images/0afbc2445b1b268828ac3bb821785ad10fae9d5e7bc10bc24cd3110d7a9f966f.jpg) In 1990, Bierut became the youngest partner in Pentagram, one of the world's leading graphic design firms, where he has continued to design striking logos and graphics for larġe corporations including United Airlines and DISNEY (see under "Edited Design"), educational institutions such as Yale and Princeton universities, and progressive groups such as the Architectural League of New York, along with promotional pieces for sports, and book jackets. He has combined his design work with teaching and writing; as a founder and frequent contributor to the respected online journal Design Observer.
