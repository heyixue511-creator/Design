As Morris's critics have often pointed out, his Golden type (which he first called the regenerate type or Jenson-Morris'²) differs from Jenson's Roman in several respects, most notably in its darker colour. Morris admitted to Walker that designing the Golden type was the most troublesome task he had ever undertaken; admirable in appearance though it is on the pages of many of the Kelmscott books, one senses that Morris was not wholly at ease in producing a typeface associated historically with the Mediterranean world rather than northern Europe. Even Emery Walker acknowledged that the Golden type was not as elegant as the Venetian faces after which it was patterned.²3 Morris emphasized in an interview in 1895 that the typefaces of the earliest printers could scarcely be improved upon, and ‘my own types', he added, hardly differ from theirs in essentials. They are not the same, of course; but they are not altogether original. It would be impossible to obtain absolute originality where the sphere of action, so to speak, of the designer is so very limited.'4 Morris was also to argue, with justice, that he had succeeded in emulating without directly imitating Jenson's fount. And here Walker's camera came into play once more. Having been supplied with photographic enlargements of the types, Morris traced them repeatedly until he felt he understood the essence of the letterforms. The next step was to make a group of free-hand drawings, still to the same large scale. Since it is hazardous to design type (or anything else, for that matter) on a scale too far removed from its ultimate size, Walker then photographed the best of the drawings and produced prints of them to the scale of the original type. In at least one instance he also made a line block of a drawing in the smaller size so that Morris could see how his design looked when actually impressed on paper. At each stage Morris continued to modify, touch up, and revise the drawings. At last he felt ready to turn them over to his punch-engraver. Those faniliar with modern type-designing will recognize that this procedure is still used, with minor variations, although of course nowadays the final stage involves the manufacture of a film or electronic matrix rather than a metal punch. When the Monotype Corporation launched its great series of historic type revivals in the 192os under the direction of Stanley Morison, it followed closely the steps laid down by Morris and Walker in the previous century. What is slightly startling about all this is that there is no record of any type-designer before Morris using the camera as an essential tool: Morris and Walker seem to have stumbled upon a technique that is now considered standard in many fields of design, and Morris managed to do this despite some unusually strong prejudices against technological shortcuts. Edward Prince, to whom Morris now turned for the cutting of his punches, was a sober, old-fashioned craftsman destined to perform a similar task for many of the most celebrated private presses of the following generation, including the Doves, Ashendene, and Vale Presses.2 Indeed, at the time of his death in 1923 he was the last living independent punch-cutter in England, for by then the process of engraving punches had become thoroughly mechanized. Frederic Goudy and others have suggested that Prince's contribution to the creation of the Kelnscott types has been underrated, yet it must be said that Prince also engraved types that were superlatively ugly (such as C. R. Ashbee's Endeavour face) with the same skill that he lavished on Morris's founts. Moreover we have the testimony of Herbert Horne, whose Montallegro type he engraved, that he was an excellent workman, but very British; and the difficulty of making him perceive the finer qualities of form is enormous’.26 Once the punches were engraved, it was possible to make smoke proofs', which Morris tended to carry about with him in a matchbox so that he could inspect them from time to time. Even at this stage a few letters — the lower-case ‘h', for example - were rejected and engraved a second time. For the casting of the type, Morris sought the services of the respected typefoundry of Sir Charles Reed and Sons, established in the mid-eighteenth century, and now under the management of Morris's friend Talbot Baines Reed, the author of a scholarly history of English typefoundries. The actual casting, however, was done by machine; Sparling records Morris's wry comment that from all I hear, there wasn't much fun in it [hand-casting] for the poor devils who jogged and bumped the moulds about'.27 The Golden type was completed by the winter of 189o—1. It was cut in the generous size then known as ‘English’ - i.e. 14 point. (The point system for measuring type-sizes had not yet been adopted in Morris's day.) They disclose that three characters ('h', 't', and ‘6') had to be recut. It is not accidental that Morris's favourite among his own types was also the largest, the Troy, a ‘Great Primer' (18 point). The reduced version of the Troy type, the Chaucer — a Pica' (12 point) — seemed too small to Morris, and only with the greatest reluctance did he accept it as an economic necessity in the enormous Kelmscott edition of Chaucer. For a number of years Morris toyed with the notion of producing a larger version of the Golden type as well, though nothing came of the wish. He did, however, have his binder, J. and J. Leighton, employ several sizes of an enlarged Golden type (with dies produced from line blocks rather than new punches) on the spines of many of the Kelmscott books. Cockerell also records the significant fact that the first character of the Golden type was cut by Prince in 189o in Great Primer, but Morris then decided to adopt instead the English size.29 Morris was not alone among the Victorians in his admiration for the types of Jenson. Henry Noel Humphreys, in A History of the Art of Printing (1868), a work which Morris owned, had praised Jenson's first four books for being printed in Roman characters . . . more perfect in form than those of any previous printer' (p. 112). In the United States, Theodore Low De Vinne, while acknowledging in 1886 that Jenson's letters might appear ‘rude and coarse' to modern eyes, was still able to commend the simplicity and beauty of his design'.3° Talbot Baines Reed, when lecturing to the Society of Arts in April 189o, showed a slide of one of Jenson's books and discussed his types in complimentary terms.³ Horatio F. Brown's The Venetian Printing Press 1469–180o (1891), published just as the Kelmscott Press was being launched, offered a detailed and mostly admiring account of Jenson's work as a printer. Emery Walker, it goes without saying, held Jenson's typography in high esteem and turned to it again for inspiration when he subsequently designed the Doves Press type. Morris himself remarked that, when it is so obvious that this is the best and clearest Roman type yet struck, it seems a pity that we should make our starting point for a possible new departure at any period worse than the best'.³² (This is undoubtedly a reference to Miller and Richard's Old Style, which was a pallid imitation of Caslon.) The Golden type was not, in its final form, a mere facsimile of Jenson's fount: Morris insisted that he did not copy it servilely', and Walker admitted that Morris took only the general proportions' of his type from Jenson and Rubeus.3 The chief modifications introduced by Morris were to thicken the strokes and make the serifs more slab-like, producing a printed page that was much darker than Victorian readers were accustomed to. The heavy, bold Golden type represents his reaction to the anaemic typography of late-nineteenth-century England, and it also reflects his search for a typeface that would match the broad lines of wood-engravings cut in the medieval style. Crane when he saw it beside Jenson thought it more Gothic-looking: this is a fact, and a cheerful one to me,’Morris said in a letter to Ellis.³⁴ Another anachronistic quality of Morris's fount is that he did not design an italic alphabet or certain other characters that most modern printers would regard as indispensable (e.g. brackets, dashes, small capitals, bold face). This decision was to produce a grand simplicity and uniformity of texture in the Kelmscott Press pages, but at the same time the lack of an italic made it almost impossible for Morris to print any editorial apparatus such as footnotes or bibliographies (which he was reluctant to include in his books for other reasons as well). One notable departure from the practice of early printers was Morris's avoidance of abbreviations and ligatures — except of course for the usual ones, ‘fi', 'ff', 'f', '', ‘f, ‘x', and 'α'. Writing in September 189o to Ellis about The Golden Legend, which was then envisioned as the first Kelmscott book, Morris declared, 'In one thing I think I differ from you a little, i.e., about the joined letters or queer signs: since our book is to be a reprint, not a fac-simile, I do not think that we need reproduce these: indeed I should extend the abbreviations in order to make the book more readable.'s Morris's Golden type evoked a mixed response. Professional printers were quick to observe that, because of the great breadth of the individual letters (not to mention the 14-point size), it was wholly unsuitable for books produced under commercial 'conditions. The adjective used by De Vinne in writing about Jenson — coarse — cropped up frequently in discussions of the Golden type. Typographers in the early decades of this century, influenced probably by the blight of ubiquitous, inferior imitations of Morris's design, tended to offer harsh estimates of it. Stanley Morison, for example, referred to the Golden type in private as ‘positively foul’, and in a famous essay published in 1924, ‘Towards an Ídeal Roman Type', he made it one of his chief targets in an attack upon Jenson's large capital letters.36 Examined individually, the Golden type characters, especially the capitals, do indeed seem heavy and stiff, yet they fit well together (always an important test of any typeface), and their total effect on the page of a Kelimscott book is undeniably attractive. If the Roman type of the Doves Press, created only a few years later and based on the same model, strikes many of us today as a more satisfactory adaptation of Jenson's letterforms, the Golden type must, at the very least, be viewed with the generous tolerance that is accorded a pioneering effort. Having produced a Roman type more or less to his satisfaction, Morris — even before his Press was in operation – began to lay plans for a Gothic type as well. Morris ‘talks of setting to work at once on a blackletter fount!' Cockerell recorded with astonishment in his diary on I February 1891. It was not in fact a surprising decision, for Morris's deepest aesthetic instincts always drew him back to medieval forns, and his own library was filled with the finest German and French illustrated books of the fifteenth century, mostly printed in Gothic types. Lecturing on ‘The Ideal Book' in 1893, Morris made a strong case for the possibility of reviving blackletter: 'I have hitherto had a fine and generous Roman type in my mind, but after all, a certain amount of variety is desirable, and when you have once got your Roman letter as good as the best that has been, I do not think you will find much scope for development of it; I would, therefore, put in a word for some form of Gothic letter for use in our improved printed book.'37 Morris acknowledged that such a proposal would startle' some of his listeners; in another lecture delivered a few months later he offered the even more startling suggestion that ‘if he had his own way he would have all books printed in it [i.e. Gothic type]’.3⁸ This may be a characteristic piece of Morrisian exaggeration, but it does reveal his extraordinarily strong preference for Gothic rather than Roman types, a preference based ultimately on their respective connections with the Middle Ages and the Renaissance. Here again Morris was swimming against the current, for, outside Germany, there was an almost universal consensus in the nineteenth century that Gothic typefaces were dead. In The Printer's Grarnmar (18o8) Caleb Stower offered this pious graveside homily: We may reasonably hope, from the pleasing aspect of the present age, when mankind have dared to burst the fetters of prejudice and antiquity, and have determined to exercise their better judgement, and adopt plans more congenial to true taste, that the Roman character will be universally enployed in all civilized states' (p. 35). The assumptions behind Stower's assertation are familiar, because they echo the charges that were still being made in his day against medieval churches and cathedrals: that Gothic types, like Gothic buildings, belong to an earlier, more barbaric period of European history, whereas Roman type, like neo-classical architecture, is associated with order, rationality, enlightenment — with, in a word, civilization. Romanticism, the Oxford Movement, and Victorian ecclesiology were to turn such assumptions on their heads, but English typography was surprisingly late in responding to these new intellectual and aesthetic forces. By the 189os English cities and towns were crowded with examples of Gothic Revival buildings, and the Pre-Raphaelites had successfully aroused public interest in medieval styles and subjects in paintings; yet, apart from a few isolated experiments by the Chiswick Press and other old-style'firms, there had been no serious attempt to restore Gothic types to a central position in the world of printing. Morris was to describe his Troy type — named after The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye (1892), the first book in which he used it — as a semi-Gothic type designed . . . with special regard to legibility'.39 What he attempted to achieve in the Troy was a modern adaptation of the best fifteenth-century typefaces of Germany that would not seem exotic or illegible to Victorian readers: ‘the task I set myself was to redeem the Gothic character from the charge of unreadableness which is commonly brought against it,'Morris declared in the Note . . . on his Aims in Founding the Kelmscott Press. And I felt that this charge could not be reasonably brought against the types of the first two decades of printing: that Schoeffer at Mainz, Mentelin at Strassburg, and Günther Zainer at Augsburg, avoided the spiky ends and undue compression which lay some of the later type open to the above charge. . . . I entirely eschewed contractions, except for the “&," and had very few tied letters, in fact none but the absolutely necessary ones. Keeping my end steadily in view, I designed a black-letter type which I think I may claim to be as readable as a Roman one, and to say the truth I prefer it to the Roman. One of the two earliest trial pages set in the Troy type was a passage from Chaucer's Franklin's Tale', and it is obvious that from the beginning Morris had the type in mind for his great edition of Chaucer. Morris may have been a crusading idealist, but he had a firn grip on financial realities, and he saw that printing the collected works of Chaucer in a type as large as the Troy was out of question. In December 1891, therefore, Emery Walker made several photographic reductions of passages (both prose and poetry) set in the Troy type to help Morris envision the best size for his third fount. Morris wrote to Walker on 2o December: Thanks for the photos: which look very well indeed. I must say that I am in some doubts as to which I shall have cut, the English or the Pica; I am almost inclined for the Pica, as it will give us more variety.'4² Since the Golden type was an English (14 point), Morris finally settled on Pica (12 point) for the Chaucer type, a decision that always left him uneasy. He was driven by the necessities of the Chaucer (a double-columned book)’to create this smaller version of the Troy type, Morris said;43 the Chaucer type was cut by Prince between February and May 1892 and was ready for use in June. There was another historical model towards which Morris was drawn, a typeface used by the German printers Sweynheym and Pannartz in three books produced at the monastery of Subiaco near Rome during the 146os. Morris described it as ‘an exceedingly beautiful type' and ‘a transition between Gothic and Roman', and when in November 1892 he purchased one of their books, St Augustine's De civitate Dei, he designed a lower-case alphabet patterned after the Subiaco fount.⁴4 It was never completed, but even Morris's discarded ideas created spreading ripples in the typographical waters, for in 190o Cockerell persuaded St John Hornby to have a private typeface cut for his Ashendene Press: Walker photographed pages of Cicero's De oratore printed by Sweynheym and Pannartz, tracings and drawings were made. Prince engraved the punches, and Hornby, to his surprise, found that an investment of less than £1oo had made him the owner of the Subiaco type, one of the most successful of all the privatepress founts. The Kelmscott Press began with only one proprietary typeface, but by the second year of operation Morris had at his disposal a 14-point Roman (the Golden type), an 18-point Gothic (the Troy type), and a 12-point version of the same Gothic (the Chaucer type). Because the press was an exercise in deliberate simplification, Morris found these three faces more than adequate for his needs. Likewise, once he had discovered a paper that satisfied him, he had it produced in several sizes and used nothing else thereafter.
