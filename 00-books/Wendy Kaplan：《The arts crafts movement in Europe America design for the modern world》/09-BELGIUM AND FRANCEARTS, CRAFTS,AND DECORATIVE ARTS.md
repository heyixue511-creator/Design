Gustave Soulier, a prominent supporter of the decorative arts in France, commented, "We must recognize in any case that regarding the position and esteem accorded to decorative art endeavors, it is not our country that has set the tone...we have submitted to the influence of England."The extension of Arts and Crafts ideas into francophone Europe was profound and transformative, although many precepts of the British movement were not adopted transparently. British designers, schools, and shops were known, studied, and emulated, but the ideas of the movement were interpreted selectively in Belgium and France.² While there were francophone studies of John Ruskin's criticism and of English painting and poetry as early as the 1860s, English decorative arts gained popular attention only around the mid-189Os.3 William Morris appealed not only to radical intellectuals, who published translations of his political writings, but also to conservatives, who saw in Morris and his circle the example of looking to national tradition as a source of renewal. Broadly tracing the reception, social and historical contexts, and critical arguments around the Arts and Crafts movement in fin-de-siècle francophone Europe, I aim to show how designers and critics encountered British goods and ideas through publications, commercial shops, and exhibitions. While I discuss the ways that the Arts and Crafts movement affected visual culture in both Belgium and France, French-speaking Belgians more readily accepted the lessons of the Arts and Crafts; therefore, I have given greater weight to their example." In both countries, and throughout Europe, debates around the social effects of industrialization, the importance of national identity, and a desire for a cohesive vision of art and life were tied to the flourishing applied arts movement and the example of British Arts and Crafts design. The British monthly magazine The Studio, which began in 1893, found a ready audience in France and Belgium, even before the integration of French-language supplements after 1898 (71). One of the most significant agents in the transmission of ideas, The Studio employed European correspondents and used the latest techniques of reproduction. The Belgian cultural weekly L'Art Moderne commented that "no other art review is comparable to it, neither in England, nor especially on the continent, and, in perusing it, one is convinced of the extent to which English decorative art has already arrived at the highest degree of perfection.... It is an influence, a movement that will be counted in the history of art."Modeled on The Studio, Art et Décoration and L'Art Décoratif, whose first issues appeared in 1897 and 1898 respectively, continued the promotion of English design and furthered the burgeoning idea of the decorative arts as a vital form of modern artistic activity. Through publications, commerce, and exhibitions, Arts and Crafts theory and design were interpreted to serve different cultural histories and national identities. Arts and Crafts ideas from England were assimilated more thoroughly in Belgium than in France because of Belgium's widespread Anglophilia and its similar heritage of early industrialization. Established as a sovereign nation only in 1830, Belgium was already the most highly industrialized and densely populated on the continent.6 In Wallonia (the southern half of Belgium), with its concentration of textile, steel, and glass-making factories, bourgeois leaders advocated a laissez-faire attitude toward the development of industry, but the more agricultural northern region of Flanders was also dotted with industrial enterprises. The first passenger train on the European continent ran from Brussels to Mechelen in 1834. Although industrial manufacture enriched the Belgian bourgeoisie throughout the nineteenth century, the working classes suffered from long hours, less-than-subsistence wages, and some of the most belated and ineffective labor laws in Europe. While heavy industry and colonial enterprise (following King Léopold Il's sovereignty over the vast African Congo in the mid-1880s) dominated the young country's economic outlook, the traditional industries based on agriculture and craft production were central in the formation of its national identity. Crafts such as spinning, lace making, and weaving, if hugely diminished in the face of industrialization, were still supported by government subsidies and shown in exhibitions as evidence of a national heritage of industriousness that predated modern borders. In 1880, with grand celebrations marking the fiftieth anniversary of the Kingdom of Belgium, national self-consciousness was at its height. With the support of the bourgeoisie came a surge in avant-garde activity in turn-of-thecentury Belgium. The Brussels exhibition society Les XX (Les Vingt, or The Twenty, after the number of founding members in 1883) showed an international range of like-minded artists at their annual salon.8 Among the first in Europe to include the decorative arts as a medium of artistic expression on a par with painting and sculpture, the Belgian groups Pour l'Art (For Art), Les XX, and especially the latter's successor, La Libre Esthétique (The Free Aesthetic) exhibited numerous examples of British Arts and Crafts work before 1900. Books made at Morris's Kelmscott Press, C. R. Ashbee metalwork, James Powell & Sons glassware, Jeffrey & Co. wallpapers, T. J. Cobden-Sanderson bindings, prints from the Fitzroy Picture Society, and C. F. A. Voysey drawings were all shown in Brussels in the last years of the nineteenth century. One of the most influential English designers and proselytizers, Walter Crane, achieved considerable fame in turn-of-the-century Belgium. Georges Lemmen, a painter, graphic and decorative artist, and founding member of Les XX, praised Crane's decorative sensibilities in a two-part article in L'Art Moderne.9 Jane Block has shown how Lemmen's admiration for English graphic design is also legible in his cover design for Les XX's 1892 exhibition catalogue (7.2).1° Adopting the symbolic imagery of the fruit-bearing tree that Selwyn Image had designed for the Century Guild's magazine The Hobby Horse (one of the first Arts and Crafts publications), Lemmen created a bold composition with saturated colors that also owes a debt to Crane's woodblock-printed children's books, of which he owned a significant number. The prominent red flag emblazoned with the name of the group hints at the radical politics and aesthetics that Crane and many Les XX members supported." While the introduction of Arts and Crafts ideas found favor among the avant-garde in Belgium, in France, a country with an altogether different cultural and industrial heritage, ancien-régime notions of artistic integrity preoccupied modern designers, critics, and government officials. As Nancy Troy has observed, France's economic competition with England and Germany was a significant factor in the renewed attention it gave to the applied arts at the turn of the century.'² The waning exports of French decorative arts industries during the second half of the century stimulated close examination of British design schools, factories, and major figures, but France, which experienced large-scale industrialization only in the 186Os, still cherished its image of prerevolutionary craftsmanship. Debora Silverman has shown how an increasingly conservative Third Republic employed associations with eighteenth-century artistic and decorative traditions in an effort to reconcile the political and social tensions of the late nineteenth century.'3 Admiration of aristocratic taste was bound up with the image of France as the worldwide purveyor of elegance and craftsmanship. The luxury industries of the ancien régime, such as fine furniture making, porcelain, and silk weaving, continued to carry connotations of refinement. ![](images/7174d16854b1f330a7bca58bb3629467155905543d945e438e6ce7e364151edf.jpg) The critic and poet Jean Lahor (the pseudonym of French doctor Henri Cazalis), in an article entitled "M. William Morris et l'art décoratif en Angleterre" (William Morris and Decorative Art in England), recognized that the expansive success of the English decorative arts movement was due, in large part, to the veneration of national heritage.14For Lahor and many other turn-of-thecentury critics, indigenous architecture and decoration seemed to reveal, unconsciously and without pretense, the true character of national style. He argued that the French Revolution had severed the rightful heritage of popular culture from the French people. Folk songs, dances, pottery, and architecture of the eighteenth century were, for him, the "primitive" soul of the nation. Since this "authentic" art had been cut off from the people, a new version had to be developed. Unlike Morris, Crane, and Ashbee, who believed the workingman not only had the potential to create art but also would be uplifted by such endeavors, Lahor charged the bourgeoisie with developing "an art for the people, if not an art by the people."15 Although French and Belgian critics advocated their own national traditions, they shared an international enthusiasm for the English dwelling and its furnishings. Revered for its common sense and for its picturesque qualities, the English house was praised throughout the nineteenth century by continental architects, such as the influential French proponent of the Neo-Gothic Eugène-Emmanuel Viollet-le-Duc, who was also an architectural theorist and restorer. Viollet-le-Duc emphasized principles of rationality in structure and decoration, which he believed were embodied by medieval architecture. He wrote prolifically on domestic architecture of all periods, and he especially admired the forthright qualities of vernacular buildings in northern Europe, which he saw as a model for contemporary culture. He commented, "The English have maintained, in the disposition of the country houses that they erect today, these traditions of the Middle Ages without regret and apply without difficulty these true principles to modern life. We happily recognize that the English are our masters in comfort (they discovered the word)."6While the English house was venerated for its practicality, many francophone writers also stressed its fresh, cohesive, and artful decoration. Gabriel Mourey, the French correspondent for The Studio, was a key figure in the popularization of the Arts and Crafts movement in France. In his 1895 book Passé le détroit (Across the Channel), Mourey discussed the centrality of the dwelling in English culture and praised the Arts and Crafts artisans for their fresh designs and devotion to the idea of "home," a word that was often left untranslated in French texts. Mourey suggested that the English proclivity for practicality, intimacy, and honesty was evident in their preference for the well-designed details of the domestic interior—furniture, wallpapers, lighting fixtures, and textiles—all of which indicated that "the special love, the profound Anglo-Saxon passion for home [is] without a doubt a strong aid in the efflorescence of this renaissance of industrial art."' In Belgium Olivier-Georges Destrée, who visited England to study poetry, painting, and the decorative arts, also emphasized the English idea of home and the design of the interior. In his 1894 book about the Pre-Raphaelite painters and poets, Destrée observed that the "decorative unity" of the English house distinguished it from the "horrible medley of all styles and all eras," and from the poor quality that marked continental dwellings.'8 The idealization of the British dwelling focused principally on the aesthetic coordination of the interior. The availability of artistically designed textiles, furniture, and silver through firms such as Morris & Co. and Liberty & Co. perhaps suggested to Europeans that interest in well-designed decorative art was more widespread in British culture than it actually was. By the mid-1890s several continental entrepreneurs had also established businesses that both handled English goods and commissioned European designers to produce items for the dwelling. The Belgian architect Gustave Serrurier-Bovy was one of the first continental dealers to carry Liberty products, and he had been in the business of selling English objects at his shop in Liège since 1884. (By the early 1890s he was producing his own designs as well.) Ten years later Edmond Picard, a lawyer and art critic, turned his townhouse in Brussels into La Maison d'Art, a commercial venture dealing in decorative objects.'9By at least 1895 the architect and designer Georges Hobé was selling Essex & Co. wallpapers and James Powell & Sons glass as well as his own furniture at his shop in Brussels. These commercial efforts, together with illustrated magazines and exhibitions, aimed to transform continental bourgeois taste. Reformers also hoped to stimulate a new visual culture suitable to the modern era. Designers refined the long-standing argument that a country's culture was legible in its national style and offered new ways to reconcile tradition with modern life. Among the late-nineteenthcentury Belgian bourgeoisie, romantic longing for a national style was embodied in the Flemish Renaissance—its revival paid homage to another era of bourgeois prosperity and artistic ![](images/196b8ec98e35e35630a0bc7b1a7c9fece4be5679fcdb800b902a073d274742d5.jpg) achievement. The architect Paul Hankar, who spent a long apprenticeship in the office of Henri Beyaert, one of the most prominent Flemish Renaissance Revival architects, transformed the conventions of historicism in his architectural and decorative works. Hankar often designed both the architecture and the interiors for his projects. The Brussels townhouse he built for himself between 1892 and 1893 included an ensemble of woodwork, furniture, and other details. A lantern from this interior shows how Hankar interpreted reviv alism in wrought iron (7.4). Following Viollet-le-Duc, who advocated the use of iron both in structure and in decoration, Hankar produced designs for iron from the 188Os onward. As he did with the iron details on the façade of his dwelling and in many other projects, he had the metal on the lantern manipulated to resemble plantlike tendrils extending from each corner. Born in Antwerp and trained as a painter in Belgium and France, Henry Van de Velde is another central figure in the diffusion and reinterpretation of Arts and Crafts ideas on the continent. Van de Velde's activities in Brussels and then in Wilhelmine Germany show how the ideas and forms of the British Arts and Crafts movement were shifted to reflect conditions in continental Europe. Amid the influx of decorative arts into the Belgian salons and the Post-Impressionist interest in the decorative surface, Van de Velde's search for an artistic identity culminated in his adoption of Arts and Crafts ideas. Already experimenting with decorative design by 1892, Van de Velde declared that he had been introduced to the ideas of Ruskin and Morris through Alfred William Finch, his close friend and fellow member of Les XX.3² Finch, who was born in Belgium of English parentage, was active as a printmaker and painter in Brussels. In the 1890s he learned how to make ceramic pots, exploring in particular the tradition of low-fire peasant wares. For Van de Velde, Finch, and others, the English handicraft movement manifested the ideal that welldesigned and well-made objects, rather than painting or sculpture, could uplift and embellish daily life. Van de Velde's zealous conversion to craft as an ethical code is embodied in his appliqué called Lα Veillée des anges (The Angels' Watch), which was executed with the help of an aunt (7.13). Shown at Les XX in 1893, The Angels' Watch was listed in the catalogue with a quote from a thirteenth-century guild leader about the masterpiece tradition required of a craftsman. In 1898 he published a study of Morris through the socialist press L'Avenir Social, and he continued to lecture and write about Morris throughout his career."In francophone Europe, Morris became even more revered after his death in 1896, a moment that coincided with the renewed attention on the decorative arts.45 As the Arts and Crafts movement gained popular attention, its ideas were fitted into existing frameworks that suited established values and theories of decoration in continental Europe. While British goods and ideas were widely respected in turn-of-the-century France, decorative artists did not accept them with the same enthusiasm as their Belgian counterparts. Historical tensions between France and Britain and perceived cultural differences in temperament (in the racialized language of the nineteenth century "anglo-saxon" was opposed to "Iatin") were frequently offered as justification for not following British design. Belgium, by contrast, often saw itself as a close "cousin" of the British, in part because of historical alliances. Artists such as Emile Gallé, Jean Dampt, Auguste Delaherche, Pierre-Adrien Dalpayrat, Ernest Chaplet, Alexandre Charpentier, Charles Plumet, and many others who designed and made objects as modern artistic commodities, never formed a single cohesive group that followed the tenets of the British Arts and Crafts. Critics such as Lahor and Soulier believed that this was because of the lack of a charismatic leader such as Morris, the cultural weight of the historical styles, and pride in French design. The classical and medieval traditions remained forceful aesthetic and cultural models for decorative artists of the nineteenth century, and eighteenth-century Rococo forms were upheld as examples of "good taste" and the epitome of French craftsmanship during the 1880s and 1890s. Our silk fabrics are real silk, our gold jewelry is made of gold, chased by hand; our furniture is innocent of all "trickery" and "disguise" and that alone—though it may seem paradoxical—is rare enough that it deserves to be pointed out. Artistic honesty, respect for materials, the necessary usage of objects destined to be a part of our lives—that is what we are searching for.48 Emploving some of the key ideas of the Arts and Crafts, such as the elevation of the decorative arts and respect for "honest" use of materials, Les Cinq (which became Les Six in 1897 when Nocq left and was replaced by Etienne Moreau-Nélaton and Tony Selmersheim) created restrained but deliberately luxurious objects, such as a dressing table by Plumet and Selmersheim (7.25). In formal terms, however, these artists held to French decorative traditions, updating and assimilating eighteenth-century models.49 Like other continental nations, France's established art industries were not dependent on the adoption of British Arts and Crafts ideals. They did flourish, however, under the new attention and acceptance given to the decorative arts. Emile Gallé transformed his father's ceramics business into a large factory best known for the production of art glass and furniture. Even the potters Chaplet, Delaherche, and Dalpayrat, who were mostly trained within the conservative ceramics industry, rose to become celebrated figures whose work with porcelain, stoneware, and high-fire glazes was widely praised at international exhibitions, avant-garde galleries, and the reformed French Société Nationale des Beaux-Arts, which began to include decorative arts in 1890. These fin-de-siècle French craftsmen relied on the established aesthetic traditions of production and artisanal potteries, but they also drew from the concerns and fashions of their own time. In the 1880s Chaplet, for example, embellished his pots with imagery from the British illustrator Kate Greenaway's books for children, which had become a sensation in Paris. As their pots found a sophisticated audience at the salons and in the decorative arts magazines, they were received into a climate that increasingly favored an Arts and Crafts romanticism of the deliberately untutored, or "primitive," craftsman. The widely promoted image of the humble potter, for example, was extended to Chaplet's porcelain, as well as Delaherche's matte stoneware and his exotic sang-de-boeuf flambé glazes emulating Asian examples. Delaherche's work particularly impressed American potter William Grueby when he saw it at the World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago in 1893 (7.26). As modern decorative arts and crafts found sophisticated collectors and began to enter museum collections, critics and artists called for the applied arts movement to play a social role. Since they were associated with indigenous popular culture as well as artisanal and national tradition, the decorative arts were given a prominent place in the rhetoric of social reformers who sought an art for everyday life. Morris and his colleagues famously championed the applied arts as a popular expression and as a means of elevating the worker socially and spiritually. Yet many continental designers sympathetic to Arts and Crafts ideas were not craftsmen, nor did they practice the same kind of anti-industrial skepticism concerning modern machinery. The French critic Roger Marx believed that a combination of efficient production and authentic cultural form would constitute a vital "l'art social." Avowedly anti-Ruskin, Marx held ![](images/3e38723d880dd09e8fdeadf5cc275dad572f903a9c6e4db16ed9be7af9c366ae.jpg) that rather than looking across the channel, the simple lessons of national tradition would provide a genuine model.5°Designers in Belgium and France did not emphasize the personal satisfaction of making things as much as they did the possibility of making life joyful through the integration of art into daily life. This idea was taken up by both socialists, who sponsored model housing and programs of art education, and conservatives, who sought to improve taste and inculcate bourgeois values. At the center of this discourse was the dwelling and its decoration. Serrurier-Bovy made his name on the rustic Artisan's Room, but he found commercial success with his flamboyant designs in exotic woods. The obvious luxuriousness of his designs and commissions of the late 1890s, including the exclusive Pavilion Bleu restaurant for the Exposition Universelle in Paris in 1900, did not detract from his image as a humble artisan.5 ![](images/a256e8c1e3b2276b2a0ef41ad9fab18fb0314e5cd1f78d88bca7b3d1783a844f.jpg) After 1900 Serrurier-Bovy returned with renewed determination to egalitarian ideals. Projects for an exhibition of worker housing in his native Liège and an interior from 1904 published in L'Art Décoratifillustrate how he reconsidered his designs from both a social and a visual perspective (7.27). The cohesive, wholesome image of the dining room interior not only recalls his exhibition designs from the mid-1890s, but also shows the new line of furniture in varnished woods that he introduced around this time. Serrurier-Bovy designed this series, called Silex, as an inexpensive, easy-to-assemble answer to the challenge of producing low-cost furniture. With increasingly angular forms and the prominent use of metal screws and stencil decoration, Serrurier-Bovy's Silex furniture embodied the abstract qualities of forthrightness and economy. A night table from a bedroom suite in production around 1904 demonstrates how he substituted plain oak, painted steel plate, and stenciled motifs for the costly materials and enameled mounts of more expensive objects. Although Silex was intended to be cheap and plain, Serrurier-Bovy did not neglect the relationship between aesthetics and utility, leaving space for a chamber pot behind the night table's sliding metal door (7.28). The full assimilation of Arts and Crafts ideas and values was evident in the cooperative organization Le Cottage and its periodical of the same name, which was edited in Brussels (with headquarters in Paris) from 1903 to 1905 (7.29). Dedicated to "all that concerns habitation," Le Cottage took its name and mandate from an Arts and Crafts ideal of simple, healthy living in the countryside. The magazine contained articles on contemporary and vernacular domestic architecture, interior design, and the decorative arts. It published extensively on British reform culture, particularly on philanthropic model villages, such as Bourneville and Port Sunlight. Another focus was on the development of the Garden City, and Le Cottage published translations of works by Ebenezer Howard, who originated this particular ideal of a town where the municipal center contained a park, surrounded by houses and gardens and finally by an agricultural belt.5² Contributors to Le Cottage insisted on the countryside as a site of physical and societal health and looked to an Arts and Crafts model of decentralized housing. Published decorative schemes by the Chevalier de Wouters de Bouchout, a librarian and amateur designer, frankly imitated those by M. H. Baillie Scott and other English architects. Acknowledging the wholesome image of Arts and Crafts design, Wouters de Bouchout offered readers practical solutions for "cottage" interiors. Gustave Serrurier-Bovy ![](images/4a9713ca6dbf1458350005831b57cab6ea7ea017e9e2f9ef0010c946e52b90e0.jpg) Critical differences between Art Nouveau and Arts and Crafts aesthetics and ideas, however, are more difficult to distinguish than Wouters de Bouchout insinuates. Art Nouveau, the name Bing chose for his Paris gallery, is a term now used to describe an international range of decorative and architectural expressions that flourished around 1900. Yet in its own time, Art Nouveau was one of many different names, including Modern Style, Jugendstil, or Nieuwe Kunst, that suggested the visual and ideological diversity of its many incarnations. The first scholars of turn-of-the-century design established a set of formal characteristics to define Art Nouveau style, including a preference for curvilinear shapes, abstracted natural motifs, and aestheticism, which seemed to set it apart from the Arts and Crafts emphasis on rectilinearity, close observation of nature, and socialism.3 While continental Art Nouveau has often been defined in contrast to the Arts and Crafts movement, its close relationship to British design and design reform ideas has always been acknowledged. This contradictory situation is made more confusing in writings of the period. Although Walter Crane famously distanced British design from the "disease" of Art Nouveau, Jean Lahor opined that the works and ideas of the British designers constituted an art nouveau."⁵4Instead of focusing on formal appearance, recent scholarship has emphasized the underlying values-including the significance of regional and national identity—that so many of the international applied arts movements shared.⁵⁵ ![](images/d34368afcfe4a7872d15d9d1e0b316c6ea05c2a2c79992de785decf80edd58a1.jpg) ![](images/3168b01294c7c7418476173bc6d21e17b259f5dbbe0f9ce0aece3690fe1604c8.jpg) The image of the Arts and Crafts movement, received through illustrated magazines and in artfully arranged shops and exhibition salons, stimulated wide debate on politics, economics, and artistic culture in Belgium and France at the turn of the century. Belgian artists and intellectuals praised the reformist vision of the British designers and used the model to reinforce their image of Belgium as a modern industrial culture with a splendid heritage of craftsmanship. While Belgian turn-ofthe-century design is rarely discussed without mention of Arts and Crafts ideas, the reverse is true for French design of the same period. Economic competition and cultural nationalism were perhaps the chief factors in the wary assessment of British ideas and designs in France. Although French critics and designers admired British products and theories, most did not emulate them formally or ideologically. Instead, the British Arts and Crafts movement was received largely as an aesthetic theory in France.5⁶The emphasis on beauty, evident in studies such as Robert de la Sizeranne's Ruskin et la religion de la beauté (Ruskin and the Religion of Beauty, 1897), also extended to forthright followers such as Van de Velde and Serrurier-Bovy, whose stridently socialist commitment to improving the life of working people through objects for the home was achieved as a grand Gesamtkunstwerk.5Utopian in outlook, Arts and Crafts theory and design had tangible consequences, nourishing a profound and long-lasting reassessment of the production and broader cultural value of the applied arts throughout Europe.
