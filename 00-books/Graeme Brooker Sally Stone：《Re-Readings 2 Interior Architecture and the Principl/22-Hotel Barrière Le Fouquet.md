# Hotel Barrière Le Fouquet

Location Paris, France

Remodelled 2006

Designer Maison Édouard François

To enclose a building with a new cover or a façade ensures that the manner in which the building is presented to its surrounding environment is altered. These changes may range from the straightforward, such as a repainting, or the reordering of non-structural matter, to something much more complex and significant such as restructuring the façade and altering how the building is communicated to its existing environment. The form of the alteration is critical, because it can be utilised in order to signal a number of things. Sometimes in a sensitive location any changes to a façade will have to accommodate certain restrictions, often through specifi c regulatory conditions such as listing or

heritage concerns. But this does not mean that the resultant façade cannot still be a radical projection of the new use of an existing set of spaces.

In 1999 new owners acquired the traditional Parisian Brasserie Le Fouquet, along with the surrounding block of buildings. The block contained seven buildings that ranged in age and architectural distinction. They were in a central location and faced onto the Champs-Élysées, the Avenue George V, as well as two surrounding side streets. The brief to the designer was to transform the seven buildings into a whole building, unifying them to become a single

monument. Due to the dense fabric of the block, internal courtyards were to become terraces for the hotel, and a garden for the brasserie was to be retained in amongst the block of buildings.

New facilities for a modern hotel, such as a spa, other restaurants and servicing, were constructed in the shells of the seven buildings. More importantly, 107 rooms were to be created. These would have a very particular ‘service architecture’, a layer of support services that created a series of internal streets, starting in the basements of the buildings in the block. Along with new lift towers, this system could ensure that the highest levels of service to each of the rooms could be maintained, making sure that food, cleaning and all manner of deliveries to individual rooms could be received within minutes of being ordered.

The overwhelming problem was that of the façades of the existing buildings. These consisted of numerous different styles. The four buildings facing the Champs-Élysées were authentic Haussmann style and were listed. The two buildings on Avenue George V were built in the 1980s but were copies of earlier styles. The latest edifi ce to be built was a 1970s smoked-glass façade bank building. The precious, yet mixed, qualities of the architectural heritage of the site prohibited the design of a contemporary style. Instead the designer took a radical strategical approach. The most recently built contemporary façades of the existing buildings were demolished. The Haussmann style listed façades were selected and copied: scanned like sheets of wallpaper, and then applied to the exterior of the contemporary façades of the block, unifying them as one.

Rather than rendering the new façades in the same colours and materials as the

originals, extensively camoufl aging them or passing them off as old, the new exteriors were constructed and fi nished in raw concrete, ensuring a clear distinction between the new and the old. The scale, form and details of the original façades were retained, yet fl attened in the concrete. To add to the dissonance between the new and the old a seemingly irregular array of glazed openings were punctured through the façade. These were the windows of the new hotel rooms behind, on fl oor levels that did not necessarily correspond with the details and scale of the interior levels and the copies of the Haussmann façades. The traditional relationship in a historic façade between a window and the surrounding details of the opening, such as architraving, stringcourse, pilaster and balcony was irrevocably lost in this approach. Yet the dissonance projected in the relationship between new and old created a striking new façade.

Designer 6a Architects

The remodelling of an existing building will often provide the opportunity to rework the exterior as well as the interior of the space. This transformation may take the form of the application of a new external surface or screen with which to communicate or symbolise the identity of a new interior. In the case of making a new public space, one where visitors are invited to come and browse, and in turn hopefully consume what is inside, the portrayal of the ideas of the brand, the message of the company and the interior, will need to be communicated in a desirable and pleasing manner.

new screen façade surface of the store. The existing brick façade of the building was painted black, creating a stimulating but subtle backdrop for the new screen surface. The ground fl oor level of the building was then clad in an interlocking relief of cast-iron panels. This was

Each panel was digitally modelled, and the circular patterns of the surface CNC carved from plastic blocks to make a former. The panels were then cast in a traditional manner using a sand-casting mould. The iron elements of the screens were allowed to partially rust and then were sealed in order to form a rich dark patina, reminiscent of industrial machinery, evoking the textile manufacturing equipment of days gone by.

Each panel was then hung on the façade, incorporating a series of precise lap joints to ensure a seamless application. This type of jointing was very important as it accepted any movement or expansion, a given condition especially as the panels would adjust and compress and move during disparate ranges in temperatures. This system of fi xing also ensured adequate ventilation to the wall ensuring no damp or ‘cold-bridging’ of the existing walls.

The circular pattern created an optical complexity that added dynamism to the streetscape, especially when moving past the façade, ensuring that the eyes of passers-by would be drawn to the shop front. To enhance the window display,

a series of curved glass shop windows were positioned in the openings previously occupied by sash windows. The glass vitrines were lit, and in a nod to the nearby Burlington Arcade, as well as other adjacent Mayfair shops, the view into the carefully crafted display cases enhanced the beautiful and eclectic carefully selected products for which the Paul Smith brand is infamous.

The dynamism of the screen was reinforced vertically by placing a solid non-textured cast-iron panel at the bottom of the façade, and counterpointing the heaviness with a thin balustrade balcony emerging at the fi rst fl oor. The thin and elegant handrail protected shoppers from any danger of falling out of the upper fl oor sash windows, when opened in agreeable weather, as they loiter on the fi rst fl oor of the shop stupefi ed by the glorious goods on show.

The continuity of the pattern was maintained even in the detail of the wooden fi re door that opened onto the street. The circular motif used on the façade was carved into the timber panel, and the door was stained a dark hue in order to remain anonymous and fi rmly in the background of the new woven textile-like façade.
