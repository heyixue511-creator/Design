Illustration by Ene Agi. The most favorite talk that I have given was for MoMA in 2019. Six weeks before the winter holidays, they had asked me to give a short talk in January of about twenty minutes on design reparations (that is, how design can help repair the damage that design has caused). I was flattered but also livid, because I was already surviving on energy vapors and they, as a powerful and prestigious institution, knew that I would drop everything, especially much-needed rest, to prepare for the talk. My therapist tells me that the wonderful thing about anger is how clarifying it is. As my anger built with each late night in preparation, I was very clear about what I wanted to say to MoMA and its wealthy patrons about reparations. Luckily, Rihanna had already expressed it very eloquently in the song “Bitch Better Have My Money.” So, on January 14, 2019, as part of the XXII Triennale di Milano Symposium in New York, I gave a talk entitled “‘B\*tch Better Have My Money’: Reparations and Design's Values, Design, Experiences for BIPOC Communities.” I opened the talk with a land acknowledgment to the Lenape peoples and lyrics from the song. Then, I laid out how design has done harm to IBPOC communities in what was basically an abbreviated version of chapters one, two, and three. I discussed the reparations that were happening at OCAD University, highlighting our Indigenous cluster hire, the work of faculty member Howard Munroe, and the work of two students, Graham Nhlamba and Pupul Bisht. Finally, I gave a call to action to help me raise \$600,000 CAD per year for a Black cluster hire. The talk was successful in changing the conversation for the rest of the symposium, but it did not raise the \$600,000. After my talk, money and resources as forms of reparation were up for discussion. Where the money and resources were coming from and to whom they were going became important follow-up questions from the moderator, Paola Antonelli, to each panelist. To have these hard economic discussions about design's harm and potential to make repairs is crucial to decolonizing design. In this chapter, I am going to talk about money and resources, and how design firms, institutions, and organizations need to reprioritize our spending as part of decolonizing design. ## The Cost of Evil Colonization is an economic system that seeks to transfer wealth from Indigenous, Black, and other POC communities to white people in Europe or colonial settlers of European descent. A phrase came into my head the other day, “If you are looking for evil, follow the money.” It is an original, so I get to claim the meme. I think it came to me because I've been watching a lot of film noir movies on YouTube, but also because I have been trying to calculate the full cost of reparations to Indigenous nations for the land stolen from them. There are benchmarks for Canada of what the reparations might be. As of February 2021, the Canadian government owed an estimated CA\$15 billion to First Nations families harmed by the intentional underfunding of child and family services on reserves, leading to Indigenous youth being placed disproportionately into the foster system. This is the total cost of \$40,000 payouts to approximately 250,000 Indigenous people.<sup>1</sup> As of the writing of this book in December 2021, the Canadian government has set aside \$40 billion to compensate First Nations children, their parents, and their grandparents.<sup>2</sup> Decolonization is about the land and the relationship of Indigenous Peoples to the land. If I did a back-of-the-envelope calculation based on the 2020 average costs of an acre of land (CA\$3,393) times the number of acres in Canada (2.467 billion), which equals \$8.37 trillion, then divided that by the Indigenous population in Canada according to the 2016 census (1.67 million), each Indigenous person would need to receive compensation of approximately \$5 million. And yet, decolonization is not about the Canadian government paying Indigenous people off and then fully owning the land. Decolonization is giving back the land. If we settlers and those who descended from enslaved Africans were to give back the land to Indigenous nations today, and they decided to allow us to remain on the land, what would it cost us non-Indigenous folks to pay rent on the lands from the Indigenous land custodians? There are 33,481,728 of us non-Indigenous Canadians. We would all be responsible for paying the value of \$8.37 trillion, which is approximately \$249,987 for each of us to pay in rent to Indigenous custodians to continue to live on the land each year. This number does not represent back rent due, which would be reparations and thus need to be calculated for many across the generations. This number also does not represent the economic, ecological, and cultural values of the activities that have been carried out on the land, an amount which is now also due to Indigenous Peoples. The \$249,987 per person in Canada reinforces the concept of “Have I paid my annual rent to Indigenous Peoples?,” which reminds us non-Indigenous folks that we are perpetually indebted to Indigenous Peoples for living and working on the land. Thus, the perceived costs of the efforts to decolonize design are minor in comparison to what is owed Indigenous Peoples. Even when I think of the things that I have done to “pay my rent,” it does not come close to paying my \$249,987 due in annual rent. For example, I facilitated the 2018 Indigenous cluster hires at OCAD University, and have therefore provided employment opportunities to Indigenous people. If, for round numbers, I say that each Indigenous faculty hire provides \$100,000 in salary and benefits, the 2018 Indigenous cluster hire of three faculty in design provided \$300,000 in possible rent payment in the first year. But of course, I, individually, do not get full credit for the \$300,000. I must credit each of the approximately 600 faculty and staff at OCAD University who helped to make the first Indigenous cluster hire happen. Therefore, I get to claim only \$500 of that across the three faculty that we hired. So even in one of my best years, I still only paid a rent of \$500 out of the \$249,987 which I owed. And since two of the hires were not Indigenous to Canada, my payment in the Canadian context is only \$166. The purpose of this hypothetical exercise is to show the level of indebtedness that those of us who live on the lands of Indigenous Peoples have to those peoples, not just in symbolic ways, but in hard currency that would affect their self-sufficiency and self-determination. We need to pay them what we owe them. We need to make similar calculations for reparations to Black communities. We need to do so for immigrant POC communities, whose labor was and continues to be exploited. Even if we know our debt is limitless, how do we begin to pay, especially if we do not have a lot of resources? ## Reprioritizing Your Design Budgets I tell my faculty that whether your budget is expanding or contracting, all budgets are finite. Therefore, the paramount question is, what are your budget priorities? OCAD University's Indigenous cluster hires and Black cluster hires happened without gaining additional funding. I wish we could have been as fortunate as RISD to have a donor contribute money for a Black cluster hire. The majority of the work of decolonizing design that I have done across design institutions has been possible because leadership decided to prioritize the funding of decolonizing activities within their existing budgets. OCAD University has been quite remarkable in how much it has committed to decolonizing our institution in the form of financially supporting our three faculty cluster hires, Indigenous cultural competency training, anti-racism and anti-oppression training, the hiring of IBPOC staff, the prioritizing of research funding, our Indigenous elder and scholars/practitioners lecture series and residencies, our Indigenous Education Committee and Elders Circle, and our outreach to IBPOC communities. All of this was accomplished when the university itself had annually contracting budgets, which makes its financial support even more remarkable. One design institution that I admire for its use of existing resources to decolonize design is California College of the Arts (CCA) in San Francisco and its support of the Decolonial School. The Decolonial School is “an interdisciplinary cohort of CCA faculty committed to building a new curricular model for art and design education that foregrounds Indigenous practices and acknowledges decolonization as a continuous process.” <sup>3</sup> I am familiar with it because I gave a keynote at their Decolonial School Unconference in February 2020. Yet, as I described in a Communication Arts article, my relationship goes further back: “Their Decolonial School project came from assistant professor Juan Carlos [Rodríguez Rivera] hearing my talk and meeting me in 2018 at Cranbrook. He came back to CCA and shared my work with his colleagues, where they started the Decolonial School project. Over the past eighteen months, they had been holding meetings with faculty across the college and wrote a new decolonial curriculum that they piloted with students this year [2020].” <sup>4</sup> According to Juan Carlos, the original cohort of faculty included “Rachel Berger, chair and associate professor of the Graphic Design program; Shylah Pacheco Hamilton, chair and assistant professor of the Diversity Studies program; Susanne Cockrell, associate professor of the Community Arts and Graduate Fine Art programs; Rebekah L. Edwards, interim chair and associate professor of the Critical Studies program; and myself, Juan Carlos Rodríguez Rivera.” <sup>5</sup> Its current coordinator as of the writing of this book is Shylah Pacheco Hamilton, and its interdisciplinary faculty members include Susanne Cockrell, Community Arts, Social Practice and Community Engagement (SPACE) minor, and Graduate Fine Art faculty; Rachel Berger, chair of Graphic Design; Shalini Agrawal, associate professor, Critical Ethnic Studies program; Allison Yasukawa, associate professor, First Year Core Studio Program, and director of Multilingual Art and Design Pedagogy; and Angela Hennessy, associate professor, Textiles program, Critical Studies program, and Graduate Fine Arts program.<sup>6</sup> The Decolonial School programming started with the Decolonial School Curriculum Development pilot in fall 2019, which consisted of monthly meetings, readings, and talks among fifteen to twenty faculty members. Its programming went on to include the Decolonial School Unconference, which I attended, in February 2020,<sup>7</sup> and a list of resources. Finally, revised Decolonial School Curriculum Development sessions were held with monthly guest facilitators on three separate tracks: one for those who identify as IBPOC, one for those who identify as white, and community sessions that bring the two groups together.<sup>8</sup> Faculty committed to the Decolonial School contributed their time and effort, receiving only service points and often uncompensated. Yet, guest speakers, the coordination of the program, and unconferences cost money. The 2019 pilot year of the Decolonial School was funded from a US\$5,000 curriculum grant from CCA's Academic Affairs budget and \$4,000 in monies collected from the four division deans. The grant was reestablished in August 2020, and \$6,000 in funding was designated to two faculty coleaders for their ongoing work. In terms of the prioritization of funding for diversity, equity, inclusion, and belonging initiatives, the college states on the DEI section of its website that “a discrete budget for these efforts will be created through existing operational funds and fundraising.” <sup>9</sup> There is recognition that the Decolonial School requires more funding to expand its activities, and thus it applied for a 2021 National Endowment for the Humanities grant of \$150,000, which is pending, but the majority of its efforts over the duration of the program have been funded internally by CCA. The examples of OCAD University and CCA may make it seem as if it is easier for academic institutions to reallocate existing budgets. And yet, for all institutions, firms, and community organizations, the true discipline of decolonizing design is deciding what you are willing to give up in order to cede space for Indigenous first, Black, and other people of color who have been structurally excluded, exploited, and sometimes demonized in design. And when we fail to cede space and to cede power, it is IBPOC communities who continue to experience harm. If I have convinced you in the previous four chapters that decolonizing design is important, what are you going to give up in order to make it happen? Here are some ideas to consider: ► If your organization was planning for a capital expansion, you might have to decide to put that on hold so that you have the resources to do a cluster hire. ► Some people, or maybe even you yourself, might need to step down from leadership in order for an Indigenous person to have the opportunity to step up. ► Eliminate or reduce your office's monthly budget for flowers, Starbucks, M&Ms, or soft drinks to hire an IBPOC illustrator full time instead of part time. ## The Key Takeaways for Reprioritizing Existing Resources to Decolonize Each action that you take to decolonize design will help pay your rent for being on Indigenous lands in reparation for the harms that design has caused and continues to cause until it is decolonized: 1. We all owe a debt to Indigenous people for being on their land and that debt has hard financial as well as symbolic costs. Pay them what we owe them. If you are not on Indigenous land as a settler, consider where the wealth comes from in your country and to whom you might be indebted for that wealth. 2. The ethos of decolonization is stronger when you have to give up something precious in your existing budget in order to support it. But additional monies to support decolonizing are fine too. 3. There is no amount of money that will compensate the IBPOC communities for what they have lost through colonization, slavery, and systems of white supremacy. But you still need to pay up so that these communities can gain economic self-sufficiency.
