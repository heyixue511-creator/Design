## 1–4.

The China Punch, 1867

Art/Script: Unknown (British)

Humor, satire, and other elements of Western caricature were introduced to Hong Kong by the Englishlanguage The China Punch, the first cartoon magazine produced in Hong Kong. The magazine was created by British journalists and aimed at Englishspeaking readers, who would have been familiar with its style of satirical caricature, borrowed from the British Punch. The China Punch's content often maligned Chinese customs while praising the superiority of British culture, but it also combined Western and Chinese elements, and as such is early printed evidence of Hong Kong's East-meets-West culture. The cover of the first issue, for example, depicts a Westerner in the costume of a Qing government official, his arms open to welcome the reader. The symmetrical design of dragons holding a circular plate mimics the British seal while the dragons, landscape, and bamboo are Chinese.

## 5.

## The Situation in the Far East, 1899 Art: Tse Tsan-tai

The first cartoon created by a Chinese artist in Hong Kong, this cartoon depicts the political dangers then facing China. Various animals represent the countries that had a presence in China: a bear for Russia, a dog for Britain, a frog for France, an eagle for the United States, a round sausage for Germany, and a sun for Japan. A Qing government official waves invitingly at the Chinese border. Near Beijing, another official lounges calmly. Below him, a wealthy citizen enjoys a lavish meal. Together, these three figures illustrate the nonchalant attitude of the people and the government toward the situation.

Because the Qing government censored it in Hong Kong and China, Tse's drawing was printed in Japan. It was reprinted several times and widely circulated in China for many years, usually without credit to the artist. The best-known reprinting is in Alarm ofRussian Incident, a 1903 Shanghai-based anti-Russian publication. The artist later cofounded the South China Morning Post, the longest-running English-language newspaper in Hong Kong.

## 6-7.

## Man Fu Daily, 1909

## Art: Various artists

At the turn of the twentieth century, many Chinese newspapers included supplements that illustrated current events. These illustrations, usually line drawings made with a Chinese brush, are considered the beginning of Chinese cartoons. Although they were known by several different names, they can be grouped into three basic types: current events, satire, and humor.
