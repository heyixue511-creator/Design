# AFTERWORD: A POSTMODERN FUTURE? Denise Scott Brown This book illustrates works of a broad range of postmodern designers. Their achievements have all the joy and relevance of Postmodernism. But the movement was later abused, and ROBERT VENTURI and I (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") have tried to distinguish our approach from what was subsequently called PoMo. The roots of our Postmodernism lie in thoughts on social and moral responsibility and turning a blind eye, formulated during and after the Second World War. Some artists joined in, but architects and planners who re-envisioned cities during the war, and began rebuilding quickly thereafter, followed their own visions and their idealistic but uninformed convictions that their projects would benefit the world. Their naive idealism, ignorance, and lack of questioning had a part in the damage done to the poorest in cities, but I like to think that Le Corbusier, had he been given the chance, would have faced urban realties with more insight than have generations of architects since, who merely ask, “what did Corbu do?" In the 196os, we formulated our ideas by researching, describing, teaching, and practicing. In all these we argued against the late Modernists and with the early Moderns, not in their Cubist vocabulary (though we love it) or their ignoring of history, but in their support for Neue Sachlichkeit. New Objectivity meant facing the realities of industrialization and new ways of life in Europe. From these conditions and the ugly industrial architecture across the tracks they intended to draw a new beauty for a new era. We did too. With Corbu, we cried out against “eyes that will not see" and hoped to bring seeing eyes to social reality, the messy vitality of the existing city, and the beauty of some disdained environments. In aligning ourselves with early Modernism we felt we were updating, not overturning, the movement. But at times we shifted its focus, trying for example to broaden Modern concepts of functionalism to cover issues missed by early Modernists. One is that the client's brief is the first but far from the last use of a building. How should functionalists plan for changing activities over time? Another is architecture's communication function, its many types and levels. A third concerns the rela tionships of our projects with the city. Architects do “site analysis," but until we vastly broaden its scope and our understanding of urban functional relations, we will continue our urban mistakes. In i976 Philip Johnson\*, studying our exhibition “Signs of Life" (Renwick Gallery, Smithsonian Institution, Washington, DC), concluded that the relation of Modernism and the Chicago School was not being repeated between Postmodernism and 197os commercial architecture. He set about righting the situation and for some years PoMo branded commercial architecture. Then, it died. Although descended from Postmodernism, PoMo was not our offspring. It was a specious version that borrowed Postmodernism's forms, though not its ![](images/df174aa138d38ebd9d83a20deff8e1f277a4046e61f630c809313bb01762e39e.jpg) principles, and applied them without talent. But in about 2ooo, a new generation of architects began to reconsider Postmodernism as we defined it. This interest continues, and, as Judith Gura suggests, the story of Postmodernism is not complete. But I feel it now has a good chance of surviving as a continuation of the main trunk of Modernism because it reflects the movement's brave objectivity, its good causes, and its intention to draw art from hard reality. And although postmodern rule-breaking is sassier, it's pushed by the same high aims.
