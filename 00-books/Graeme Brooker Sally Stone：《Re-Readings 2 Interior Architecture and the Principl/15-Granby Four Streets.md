# Granby Four Streets

Location Liverpool, UK

Date 2013 – ongoing

Designer Assemble

![](images/1f65363dc3822e7df4088b5bbe08c34accd1fd4602c5b180609ad5b830c379c1.jpg)  
Figure 1.50 Conceptua image of Granby Winter Garden, courtesy of Assemble.

Art activism can be seen as a key element of social protest; art-making can often be regarded as subversive in its very nature as it can challenge existing and established systems. The artist who works outside the system chooses to make art and precludes what conventional wisdom would deem to be productive work. Political art with a social conscience can be regarded as having a sense of responsibility or concern for the problems and injustices of society. It is not uncommon for artists, designers and architects to produce work that questions collective wisdoms and attitudes. It could be argued that one of the main purposes of art is to convey an idea and open the mind of the viewer to seeing the subject in a

fresh, provocative way: it is the responsibility of the artist to shake people out of their complacency.

Over the last generation, the position of the designer or architect has radically changed. Just as the house is considered to be much more than a machine for living in, so architecture and design is far more diverse than the Corbusian model of the ‘masterly, correct, and magnifi cent play of masses brought together in light’. Architects and designers are people who can solve problems, and these problems have expanded beyond the traditional expectations of the profession. Nontraditional models of practice mean that designers fi nd their own projects, they can act as client/developer/user all in one, they use processes of self-organisation to make things that subvert traditional hierarchies.

Assemble are a collective based in London who work across the fi elds of art, architecture and design. Their practice seeks to address the typical disconnection between the public and process by which places are made. Their architectural spaces and environments promote direct action and embrace a DIY sensibility. By employing unconventional means, their projects are self-initiated, self-made or self-organised: they subvert the typical hierarchies and procedures that surround the realisation of architectural spaces. Assemble, regard the built environment as important. They feel that they can make a difference, not through the traditional craft of architecture and design, but through non-antagonistic, small, local interventions, whether are social, physical and/or temporal. As custodians of the built environment, they use nontraditional models of practice to generate a feeling of civic responsibility within the local population of urban users and dwellers.

Assemble worked with the local community of the Granby Four Streets project to transform a corner of Toxteth in Liverpool. A small collective of local residents refused to move out of this row of red-brick terraced houses which were scheduled to be demolished. The houses were originally constructed for artisan workers in the 1900s, but had become dilapidated after a long period of deliberate neglect. Initially the residents of the neighbourhood worked on their own to once again bring positive life and character to the area. They set up a community land trust which was reinforced by quite small scale but productive ideas such as organising a monthly market selling cakes, vintage clothes and Caribbean food, and planting fl owers with a guerrilla gardening project.

Assemble were invited to help improve the houses and neighbourhood. They worked very much with what was there. The project did not include massive new structures but was based upon a clear understanding of the quality and worth of the existing. The sustainable and incremental vision for the future of the area included the refurbishment of the housing and the public space, and the provision of new work and enterprise opportunities by recruiting young residents to work on site, training them in job skills.

An environmentally sound approach was taken. Rather than refurbish every home to the same specifi cation, each property was carefully examined and the best possible solution for the individual structure was pursued. So for example, one house, which was so dilapidated that the ceiling had collapsed, was provided with a doubleheight living room. Another pair of adjoining houses which were completely uninhabitable were converted into a winter garden and fi lled with palm trees and ferns.

The project was awarded the highly prestigious 2015 Turner Prize, which is an annually presented British art award to celebrate new developments in contemporary art. This is the fi rst time that the prize has been won by an architectural and design collective, and they were commended for offering an alternative model for how society can work. The project and the practice refl ect an international movement where socially engaged art or social practice is recognised.

## The Barn

Location Herefordshire, UK

New function Studio

Date 2016

Designer Connor and Darby

![](images/e33396f551a7efecdda291cda889cc6cba770e997bf857f1477a072401b542c2.jpg)

David Connor was one of the most expressive of the postmodern interior designers in 1980s London. He revolutionised design with a small collection of domestic and retail projects. These remodelling projects were as anarchic as the clients that they were designed for and included the Seditionaries shop for the ultimate punk couple, Vivienne Westwood and Malcolm McLaren, and an apartment for the pop punk, Adam Ant. The liberation of money meant that interior design was at the time on the cusp of changing from a very proper pursuit for classically trained designers into something considerably more fashionable, provocative and at times outrageous. Connor’s enormous and expressive freehand drawings for these interiors

epitomise this approach. Thirty fi ve years later he has completed, with Kate Darby, a much more serious project: an ecologically sound architect’s studio.

There are a number of options of what to do with an existing building that is in quite an extreme state of disrepair. These range from demolishing the building and then reconstructing it to look somewhat like an identical but pristine version of the original, to simply accepting the condition of the structure and attaching new elements to complete the whole. Another approach is to construct a completely new and independent structure around or within the original building, thus preserving the existing but getting the benefi t of a new structure. The new can contrast strongly

with the old, so each is different and strongly contrasts with the other, and yet are intrinsically linked by virtue of occupying the same site, responding to the same environmental conditions and being dictated to by the same parameters.

The approach that Connor and Darby have taken is to very carefully preserve an utterly dilapidated farm building and then sublimely wrap this ruin with a pristine black corrugated metal skin. The conservation of the original was somewhat obsessive. The tumbledown structure was indeed ruinous. There was no roof, and just three walls had survived, but everything was saved in exactly the state that it was found, down to the much too low doorframes, the broken glass in the windows, and even the cobwebs.

The new structure sits precisely around the original building and responds intensely to its form. The doors and windows are positioned immediately in front of those of the original cottage, so the user has to peer through the gaps in the cracked and uneven glass, past the old and dusty cobwebs and through the new triple glazed windows to view the outside world. The only place where this acute relationship is broken is in the location of the missing fourth wall. Here the architects have installed an enormously tall window, which faces east and provides a fantastic view past a tremendously old oak tree in to the surrounding pastoral landscape.

The new structure, which is almost childlike in its simplicity, is very sustainable. The corrugated metal was locally sourced. The nearby market town of Leominster has a long tradition of working with sheet metal. By virtue of its position on the border between England and Wales, the town was once highly prosperous mainly due to the woollen trade, but also because of linen

and leather. As this dwindled during the Industrial Revolution, it began to specialise in agricultural machinery and, most importantly for this study, corrugated metal for farm buildings. The minimally detailed black corrugated cladding of the studio is supported by a simple steel frame and hides almost 40cm of insulation. Thus the studio has a relatively constant temperature, which is supplemented when necessary by a wood-burning stove, the fuel for which is collected from the woods behind the building. The remodelled building, though obviously highly contemporary, doesn’t look out of place among the historic collection of farm buildings: its scale, colour and form are all sympathetic to the surroundings and the view from the huge east-facing window allows the occupier to feel at one with the landscape.

An archive needs a particularly stable atmosphere. The life expectancy of a collection is signifi cantly affected by the environmental conditions in which it is stored, and if problems within buildings occur they can cause signifi cant damage to that collection. A range of environmental factors can affect the preservation of artefacts, including light, pollution, movement (including vibration), insects, temperature and humidity. Of these factors, a number are relatively easy to manage. Radiation in the form of light, which can cause paper to yellow and ink to fade, can be controlled with covered windows and enclosed storage. Dust and atmospheric pollutants, and vibration damage which

can produce decay, need to be monitored; and insects defi nitely need to be eliminated. But it is temperature and humidity that can have the most signifi cant impact upon the condition of archive collections. Relative Humidity (RH) is a ratio expressed as a percentage of the amount of water vapour held in a specifi c amount of air compared with how much it could potentially hold, so for example, when completely saturated the RH of the air is 100% and condensation becomes inevitable. The RH is directly linked to the temperature; the higher the air temperature, the greater amount of water it can hold, so when air cools it can hold less water and thus there is a greater chance of condensation. So, day-to-day fl uctuations in temperature, caused by natural factors such as sunlight or artifi cial reasons such as heating can lead to a fl uctuating RH and make condensation unavoidable. Thus within all archives (and also libraries and museums) it is important for the temperature and humidity to remain constant. The RH can be controlled with the use of air-conditioning, but this is not always a desirable answer. A natural solution, one which is more sustainable and has less environmental impact, is often required.

Hugh Strange Architects have inserted a perfect little self-contained architectural archive within the derelict walls of a disused farm building. This is just one of a collection of highly considered, environmentally sound new and reused buildings on the working farm. The new sloping structural insertion is actually in two parts, linked by a tiny courtyard. The upper area is an offi ce or studio with casement doors opening out onto the small terrace; the lower part houses the architectural archive. It contains necessarily clean and plain walls, and is lit by roofl ights. The structure is set back from the retained walls and rises above them, in a sort of simulacrum of the form of the original building.

The most prominent feature, as seen from the farmyard, is the fi brous cement roof that extends for the full length of the building;

the ridge and eaves drop from end to end in parallel with the gentle fall of the ground.

This gesture maintains the impression of a unifi ed form but the fabric supporting the roof actually comprises two independent enclosures framing the covered entrance yard between them. The new and complete insertion was constructed from locally sourced cross-laminated timber panels; these range in depth from 300mm for the wall panels to an even thicker 420mm for the roof. This simultaneously provides the building with insulation and thermal mass, thus creating the environmentally stable internal atmosphere necessary for an archive.

The insertion is deliberately very pure and simple; everything else required of the building is expressed as clearly additive; conduits, curtain rails, even windows are all attached to the face of the structure. Underfl oor heating, powered by an air-source heat pump, is set beneath inset wide boards in the middle of the fl oor to provide constant temperature; this is supplemented by a black wood-burning stove which acts as a centrepiece to the space.

A sustainable environment can foster good health and positive attitudes through ideas of community, fellowship and social structures. The architect or designer can deal with the physical aspects of the environment, but without also addressing the social relations, the project may not fully succeed. Lefebvre discussed this in The Production of Space when he commented that when ‘no heed is paid to the relations that inhere in social facts, knowledge misses its target’.<sup>5</sup> Social structures are important, whether recreational or within the workplace, and the designer can facilitate these within the commercial environment. Contemporary ideas about community and cooperation

can be installed within the remodelled building. By deliberately encouraging interaction, and discouraging excessive solitary working, the offi ce environment can become a productively healthy place. The nature of the interior can ensure that knowledge workers will focus more upon inherent communal activities such as communicating, sharing and developing ideas. Over the last 30 years, the proportion of internal offi ce space given over to individual workspaces has dropped signifi cantly in comparison with the increase in spaces for informal collective activities such as gathering points, social hubs and meeting places. (It is interesting to note that this increase in spaces for

social interaction has actually spilled out from the offi ce, and it is now quite normal for meetings to occur offsite in a coffee bar or park.) Thus the architect can help to create empowered and self-reliant people who are equipped with powerful information technology, enjoy their work and are more productive.

Figure 1.59 The circulation route is a dynamic force within the corporate atmosphere of the atrium.

When it opened in 1928, Number 50 Martin Place in Sydney was not only the city’s tallest building, but it also contained the world’s largest banking chamber. It could be described as a rare Australian example of the American-infl uenced inter-war Beaux-Arts style. The 12 storey façade was constructed from a solid red granite base, topped with four soaring Ionic columns and pilasters clad in pink-glazed ceramic tiles. It was originally crowned with a double attic and a most extraordinary rooftop rifl e range. The interior was equally triumphant, with richly veined marble columns placed at strategic points within the grid of the immense decorated banking hall.

Johnson Pilton Walker and BVN Donovan Hill remodelled the building; it is still a bank, so the primary function has not changed, but the methods of work have dragged the building into the 21st century. The fi rst job was to strip the extraneous accretions from the building, including the plant room that had been installed in the 1980s and completely blocked the atrium. This ensured that natural light once again illuminated the huge banking hall. The building was constructed in an era when offi ces depended on natural ventilation and light to work. This was exploited even further: the width of the atrium was increased by about 50% by cutting the fl oor plates back from the internal supporting columns, thus ensuring a massive increase in the natural light fl owing into the building and the air circulating throughout it. A very contemporary insertion was placed within

![](images/6d4358eac1ff8129c11df58c16d3ca71cea1ffeb92b8f33fe2fd51cb94ea6a9d.jpg)

the conserved atrium: two futuristic, circular glass lifts, which whizz visitors up to a two-fl oor stainless steel canopy on top of the building. This dome was designed as a fi fth façade; it can be viewed from neighbouring buildings as a shimmering lantern. Another contemporary technological addition with a sustainable approach was the replacement of the 1980s air-conditioning ducts and their massive ceiling voids, with water-based chilled beams that take up much less space.

The building could be described as being deliberately functionally inconvenient: that is, the building is organised in a slightly disruptive manner, so that the users have to navigate their way around the building in a less streamlined manner and therefore meet each other. There are a series of strategically placed, specifi cally designed destinations that the users of the building have to pass through. These meeting points encourage staff to speak to one another, to share ideas, to have spontaneous conversations. This reduces the need for formal meetings and can help solve problems more quickly. For example, there is a staircase that rises through the atrium, and like a department store that forces shoppers to take a roundabout route through different fl oors of merchandise to reach their destination, this interna circulation route runs through the fi fth fl oor coffee station. Likewise the kitchens: instead of the previous three per fl oor, there is now just one every other fl oor, thus increasing the chance of people just bumping into one another and the serendipity of meeting: giant water cooler moments.

And so the remodelled structure has symbolically been updated. It has embraced ideas of sustainability not just in the reduction of the amount of energy used, but also in the creation of a community among the users of the building.

## Chapter 2: Strategy

“I am for richness of meaning rather than clarity of meaning; for the implicit function as well as the explicit function. I prefer ‘both-and’ to ‘eitheror’, black and white, and sometimes grey, to black or white. A valid architecture evokes many levels of meaning and combinations of focus: its space and its elements become readable and workable in several ways at once. But an architecture of complexity and contradiction has a special obligation toward the whole: its truth must be in its totality or its implications of totality. It must embody the dificult unity of inclusion rather than the easy unity of exclusion. More is not less.” <sup>6</sup>

## Introduction

Designers and architects will employ a strategy when they design or adapt a building. This strategy is the guiding force that generates the form of the architecture or the interior. It is the set of rules that the architect or designer develops to ensure that they are controlling the design process. From the analysis of the existing situation, the architect or designer will establish a position or opinion. This strategy may be a simple method of organising space and form, it may govern the manner in which structure is distributed within the building or it may be based upon complex contextual factors.

The strategy that is developed when working with an existing building is founded upon the presented situation combined with the needs of the new users. A rapport is established between the old and the new. This association between the new and the old will always be the uppermost consideration. This is because the original structure is already there, and thus any architectural or design interventions will have to take into consideration its presence.

The architectural strategy employed within the adaptation can generally be classified into three distinct categories: Intervention, Insertion and Installation. Of course there are exceptions to this, but this general taxonomy is a fairly good guide to the strategies of remodelling. The groupings are based on the amount that the new parts of the building associate with the original elements. These classifications were presented in volume 1 and so it is expedient to use them again within volume 2.

Intervention is a strategy in which there is a great level of amalgamation. The new elements will so completely alter the existing that it will lose its original integrity.

The changes can be additional, in that they are new constructed features, or subtractive, pieces of the original building may have to be removed to facilitate the adaptation. This strategy is often used with buildings that have already fallen into disrepair, or that need large changes to facilitate the new use.

Insertion is the act of introducing a new element into the existing building. The insertion is generally a single identifiable element that is built to fit exactly within the confines of the host building. The fit is normally exact and removal of the new elements would probably be fairly difficult. The new element may not always be actually within the boundaries of the existing building; it could be next to or even surrounding it. This strategy does not generally alter the existing building that much, and usually results in a very distinct difference between the original buildings and the new insertion.

Installation is adding of new parts or elements to the existing building, but not altering the basic structure. It allows for these new pieces to be subsequently removed and the building restored to its original condition. This strategy is often used in historic buildings where great care has been taken over conservation.

This chapter will explore each of these categories in much greater depth. Situations, happenings and other types of installations will be discussed alongside more traditional examples of adaptation or building reuse as a way of reinforcing the concept while widening the subject.

## Intervention

## Introduction

The etymology of the word intervention is from the Latin inter meaning between, and venire, to come. It is an imposition: to come between, to interrupt, to interfere, to intrude. The most common, non-architectural, use of the word in the 21st century is as a deliberate act of involvement in the affairs of another person: an interpersonal intrusion intended to reform the life of a friend or family member judged to be going wrong. Thus intervention is a highly charged and extremely active expression that means to change something, to create difference, to generate an alteration, and in doing so, to make things better.

An architectural intervention is comparable. The architect or designer will impose a series of related changes and elements into an existing building to facilitate the needs of its users, and in doing so will make it structurally sound, easier to use, and provide it with the required aesthetic quality. Interventions within remodelled buildings can be both pragmatic and visionary. They can exhibit the narrative of the existing building and express the character of the new users; they are wilful, knowing and intentional. The sheer amount of integration between the old and the new means that neither is any longer independent of the other; the interventions will permanently alter the building. There will be direct and irreversible changes to the existing to facilitate the imposition of the new.

The architect will not usually make a single intervention, but a series or collection of them and this invariably involves two processes. The first is stripping back: the removal of extraneous additions or unnecessary parts of the building. This can be a brutal and uncompromising process, which may destabilise the structure. These changes to the existing building

are permanent. The second part of the process is the imposition of newly designed elements. These are designed to fit exactly within the confines of the original building; indeed they may provide the structural support necessary to carry the sabotaged building. The integration is so extreme that neither is now independent; they have become a single entity. This does not generally mean that that they will use a similar language, an intervention rarely resorts to pastiche. The new elements are generally easily distinguishable and of their own contemporary time and place.

In late summer 1988, the Portuguese capital Lisbon suffered one of the most violent fires in its history: the ‘fire of Chiado’. It destroyed 18 buildings in total. Some offices, others residential properties. In the aftermath of such devastation, the city council invited the architect Álvaro Siza to develop a masterplan for the neighbourhood. The historic area, which is very close to the centre of the city, was once somewhat impenetrable, and the approach that Siza took was to develop a series of interventions that would control and liberate the area. It was once inaccessible, closed off, and this strategy opened it up to residents and visitors. Siza reopened an ancient link by creating the Carmo Terrace, a walkway that joins an ancient convent with the street below. This was a brutal stripping away of the firedamaged buildings plus others that Siza felt were obstructing the regeneration of the area. Within this new pedestrian route, or architectural promenade, are a collection of paths, ramps, patios, courtyards, waterfalls and steps. The new route releases this once isolated area without destroying the historic character. It is a series of spaces that appear to be completely natural to the environment and also highly specialised. It has the quality of having always existed, and yet is definitely contemporary. These interventions released the area. They

breathed life into the once closed and dense historic urban neighbourhood through firstly removing things that were causing obstruction, then with the addition of new elements, providing the new route. The interventions were of the appropriate magnitude, the language was sympathetic and they provide the area with a suitable and optimistic future.

Ruins are perceived as romantic. They allow the disenchantment with an overmaterialistic society to be indulged and permit the viewer to both engage and disengage their imagination though a connection with the ruin and a disconnection with the intact structure. The ruin also provides a physical connection between man and nature – as the undergrowth begins to take over, so the relationship between the building and the earth is emphasised.

Figure 2.3 The interventions that complete the manor house contain a magnitude that complements the original structure.

Astley Castle is a fortifi ed manor house that dates from the 12th century but was almost entirely rebuilt in the 1500s by the Grey family. This important family played a signifi cant role in Medieval English history; Lady Jane Grey reigned for just nine days in 1553. The house was utterly devastated by fi re in the late 1970s, and had been empty and ruinous since. At the beginning of the 2010s, the ruin was acquired by the Landmark Trust. This is a charitable organisation that collects endangered historic buildings, restores them and then makes them available to all by letting them as holiday homes.

The approach that the architects Witherford Watson Mann took to the project was to recreate a sort of completeness, to restore the sense of the entire building, while still retaining the consciousness of history. This was achieved with a collection of small slightly contrasting additions that facilitate the new users, but didn’t overwhelm the building. Witherford Watson Mann explained: “We haven’t restored it, nor left it as a broken, romantic relic. We re-established a kind of wholeness, making it stable, binding it together; but we retained a feeling of incompleteness, leaving it porous, its wounds still open.” <sup>7</sup>

![](images/843c4b216638c314d2bcc1e1f6825992d3a886d7825cc9cdaaba972e926a9dee.jpg)  
Figure 2.4 The open upper fl oor living room.

The 19th century additions, which had been particularly damaged by the neglect after fi re, were demolished. This left the earliest structures plus the later Grey additions of the 16th century, and those from the 18th century, to be carefully repaired. The subsequent architectural interventions deliberately contrast with the original building, but not to the extreme. They are subtle, complementary and are recognisably related to the original structure. They facilitate the needs of the holidaymakers who will occupy the building, but without compromising the sense of history that permeates the castle. The aim was not to make a drama of the contrast between new and old but rather to engineer a reading in which successive periods of building activity contribute to a holistic effect.

Placing the bedrooms within the thick walled spaces of the ground fl oor reinforced the appreciation of a fortifi ed castle. New brick walls provided suffi cient contrast with the original sandstone to make clear the distinction between old and new, and thus

explicitly expose the palimpsest of the situation. These new walls were constructed using a diaphragm construction to ensure compatible size with the original sandstone walls, which were up to two metres thick in places. The brick infi ll walls slip between the gaps in the walls and fi ll the empty spaces. They also provided support for the much more lightweight upper fl oor, and it is up here, in the much brighter rooms that the living spaces are positioned. Another intervention was the new wooden structure that was stretched over the walls of the 16th- and 18th century parts of the house, but instead of completely containing the spaces, it merely indicates the outline, thus creating a sort of courtyard or external reception room.

The new additions feel as if they complete the building. The deliberate subtlety of the new interventions and the lack of a massive contrast between them and the original structures certainly allows the sense of history to sit lightly within the building. There is also an awareness that that history is ongoing, that changes could be made in the future. The building could be extended, adapted, changed and that the simple robustness of this project can allow for that.

Richard Wilson is one of Britain’s most respected and challenging contemporary artists. He is internationally celebrated for his interventions in architectural space which draw heavily for their inspiration on architecture, engineering and construction.

He has made some of the most memorable and arguably the best ‘outside’ works of recent years. This is opposed to public art, which Wilson thinks has connotations of something second-rate that is situated in municipal parks. All of Wilson’s work builds upon its context through surprise, incongruity, humour and a pervasive sense of unease. He questions the relationship between materials and construction, and deliberately confuses the viewer by a juxtaposition of one element against another in an unconventional manner. Probably his most memorable sculpture was Turning the Place Over. This sculptural intervention was created in Liverpool in 2008, during its tenure as City of Culture. Here Wilson cut an oval shaped section from the façade of a disused offi ce building; the piece was then attached to a pivoting rod which revolved. The cut section was then returned to the façade. The revolution was not quite true to the building, so as the sectional piece turned it appeared to fl oat free from the façade before settling back into its original position. This created a sense of surprise, relief and then recognition among the viewers. Another dramatic artwork is 20:50, a permanent installation in the Saatchi Gallery, London. A huge one metre high tank was installed in one room of the gallery and fi lled with pure black sump oil, the sort that is recycled from car engines – indeed this is where the title of the piece comes from. A slanting walkway was constructed which allows the visitor to walk into the centre of the tank, and is invited to contemplate the highly refl ective surface of the oil, the relationship between the oil and

It could be argued that Wilson is working in a tradition of changing the perception of a building by altering it, from the picturesque follies of the 18th century, through Gordon Matta-Clark’s cut buildings to Rachel Whiteread’s castings. Square the Block is a major architectural intervention which has been installed on the corner of one of the London School of Economics’ most prominent buildings. The sculpture appears to an abstraction of the corner that both mimics and subtly subverts the existing façade. The stonework looks as if it has been jumbled and crumpled up, then reattached to the building. It deliberately upsets the perception of the building from something quietly solid and dignifi ed into something unsettling and fallible.

The corner of the building was actually originally chamfered, which allowed Wilson to construct elements that projected from it. To construct the artwork, the artist, working with engineers Eckersley O’Callaghan, created copies of a number of fi ve storey vertical slices from the neighbouring buildings. Moulds were taken from these and the resultant casting fi xed to a threedimensional aluminium truss, which was six storeys in height and approximately triangular. The pieces of the sculpture were constructed from the water-based acrylic composite Jesmonite. This was selected because it was economical to produce, light enough to be carried by the existing building structure and had a close visual match to the Portland stone cladding. The completed sculpture was assembled on the ground and the complete piece lifted into place. It was hung on the side of the building from the fi fth fl oor and tied back to the building at the second and sixth fl oors for stability.

The sculpture makes no architectural and functional sense other than matching the cornice work and completing the corner. Its position means that it is sometimes unnoticed, overlooked. The moment of incongruity and recognition is often delayed and therefore amplifi ed. It is an intervention that both mimics and subtly subverts the existing façade of the building.

The French anthropologist Claude Lévi-Strauss describes the bricoleur as ‘someone who works with his (or her) hands and uses devious means compared with those of a craftsman’.<sup>8</sup> Bricolage is the skill of recombining things to create something new. Thus the bricoleur puts pre-existing things together in new creative and meaningful ways.The interior architect is also profi cient at working with the existing, of using whatever is there already and making the most of it. The problem is always how to exploit and manipulate the existing building or situation in a manner that enhances the original while serving the needs of the new function.

conceived and procured for the purpose of the project. His universe of instruments is closed and the rules of his game are always to make do with “whatever is at hand,” that is to say with a set of tools and materials which is always fi nite and is also heterogeneous because what it contains bears no relation to the current project, or indeed to any particular project, but is the contingent result of all the occasions there have been to renew or enrich the stock or to maintain it with the remains of previous constructions or destructions. <sup>9</sup>

The bricoleur is adept at performing a large number of diverse tasks; but, unlike the engineer, he does not subordinate each of them to the availability of raw materials and tools

It is also an expression used to describe a collage-like approach to reconfi guring buildings. It relies on available materials, the ease of their reuse, on contingency, availability and ease of supply. This idea of collage or texture is something that Colin Rowe and Fred Koetter explored in their

seminal book, Collage City. Here they discuss how a city (and by extension the existing building or situation) is assembled from a multitude of fragments. This texture is the outcome of:

assimilation, distortion, challenge, response, imposition, superimposition, conciliation, in short a process of “bricolage” mediating and negotiating between the platonic ideal, technological progress and the pragmatic situation at hand. <sup>10</sup>

The Prada Foundation is an assemblage of different buildings, ideas, spaces, and functions. It is located in a former gin distillery from the early 20th century, in the Largo Isarco, a post-industrial area on the southern edge of Milan. The original complex included warehouses, laboratories and brewing silos, and a number of open yards. It does seem that at the beginning of the 21st century, the most common type of new art gallery is the one that is imposed upon a large abandoned industrial space. The typologies are strangely similar; art and industry both need big open spaces which allow for excellent fl exibility and a relatively neutral background. This adaptation project was indeed within a redundant industrial series of buildings, but the complex of buildings and spaces was much more complicated than is generally used; it contained variety, intricacy and difference.

OMA respected the existing approach: they carefully analysed what was there and decided that contained within the surviving structures were a number of useful conditions. They also identifi ed how the requirements of the new users could map onto these spaces. They realised that there were insuffi cient spaces for those needs and thus the collection of buildings would need remodelling and extending. The ambition of the architects was not to work

with contrast, but to create a situation where old and new worked seamlessly. This continuity was sometimes so great that the two actually merged. At times it is impossible to tell whether the area is old or new, whether a building, interior or space is part of the original structure, or a contemporary addition. The Foundation is not a preservation project and not new architecture: the two conditions which are usually kept separate, confront each other here in a state of permanent interaction, offering an ensemble of fragments that will not congeal into a single image, nor allow any part to dominate the others.

The project physically consists of seven existing buildings and three new structures. The museum contains the podium, a space for temporary exhibitions; a cinema, a multimedia auditorium; and the tower (or the Torre). This is a nine storey permanent exhibition space for displaying the Foundation’s collection and activities. The new podium building, which hosts temporary exhibitions, and the cinema were inserted into the centre of the 19,000 square metre complex. They divide the space into a series of small courtyards, some of which are level, others are sloping. The mirror-clad cinema is a standalone structure which is partially sunken underground, while the glazed podium was constructed around another building which is known as the Haunted House. This is the landmark structure in the complex. It is neither the tallest nor the most distinguished, but it is certainly the most extraordinary. To add emphasis to this older structure, OMA carefully clad the exterior of the building with 24-carat gold leaf. Only the glass of the windows was left exposed. Rem Koolhaas, the director of OMA, claimed that it was a last minute decision – an attempt to give value to this simple and mundane element.
