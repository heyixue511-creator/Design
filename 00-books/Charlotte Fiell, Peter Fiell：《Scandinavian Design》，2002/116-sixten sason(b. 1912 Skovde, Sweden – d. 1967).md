![](images/c8fb40b69562247cb4febf39356d51272de3129538c590b3de467b9aed7b0304.jpg) Sixten Sason, Zig-Zag sewing machinefor Husqvarna, 1953 # sixten sason (b. 1912 Skovde, Sweden – d. 1967) One of the first Scandinavian industrial design consultants, Sixten Sason initially trained as a silversmith and studied with Swedish artists while living in Paris. He later trained as a pilot and then worked for Husqvarna making motor-cycles and small arms. In the 1930s he worked as a graphic designer and magazine illustrator. During the Second World War he joined Saab, then a fledgling aircraft manufacturing company, where he was initially employed as a technical illustrator. Later, he was appointed the company's designer-inchief – a position he held until his death. Around 1945Saab decided to diversify, and its chief engineer Gunnar Ljungström and his team began developing the company's first car, the Saab 92 (1947). Inspired by aeronautical design and American streamlining, Sason transformed this frontwheel-drive vehicle into a streamlined vision with integrated side panels, bonnet and boot – an innovation that waslater adopted by Pininfarina and Raymond Loewy (1893-1986). The Saab 92 was Sweden's first small car and became the blueprint for the company's subsequent models, such as the best-selling Saab 95 estate car (1959), which Sasson also designed. He later developed with Ljungström the ground-breaking Saab 99 (1969), but sadly died before it went into production. As one of the earliest European proponents of streamlining, Sason also designed several highly influential products for Electrolux, including the bulletshapedModel 248 vacuum cleaner (1943). In 1948, he developed the compact yet elegant housing for the 1600F Hasselbladcamera through the use of simple block-like wooden prototypes. This landmark design, which featured an easy-to-use arrangement of operating buttons, was the world's first single-lens reflex camera with interchangeable lenses, magazines and viewfinders, and when first launched at an exhibition in Cleveland it caused a sensation. Ergonomically refined and superbly functional, the seminal 1600F quite literally set the highest standards for camera layout and handling and quickly became a favourite choice among professional photographers. During the 1950s, Sason was also responsible for the design of various appliances for Husqvarna that were similarly aesthetically and functionally resolved, such as his Zig-Zag sewing machine of 1953.
