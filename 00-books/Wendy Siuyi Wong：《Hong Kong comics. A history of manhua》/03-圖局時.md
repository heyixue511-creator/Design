foreigners living there. The China Punch had a heavy colonial flavor, it made fun of local customs and in general upheld the assumed superiority of British norms and values. Notwithstanding its Western bias, The China Punch is significant beacause it introduced Hong Kong to humorous and satirical cartoons as a genre.

The first cartoon drawn by a Chinese person in Hong Kong was Tse Tsan-tai's The Situation in the Far East, which was printed in Japan in 1899. Born and raised in Australia, Tse moved to Hong Kong in 1887, at the age of 17. He soon became a supporter and collaborator of the revolutionary Sun Yat-Sen, and in 1903 he cofounded the South China Morning Post, now the oldest English language newspaper in Hong Kong. Tse and others were upset about the foreign enclaves and“treaty ports” that had been established in the decades following the First Opium War by way of often unfair agreements signed by the Qing government.

# 圖局時

![](images/da484f049bb8b65aac6a00221c4460f0bb11cba8c0c826eea4ecc186af5ed7b2.jpg)  
The Situation in the Far East, by Tse Tsan-tai, 1899

Tse's goal with his cartoon was to educate his countrymen about the aggressive ambitions of foreign powers in China and to caution them about their destructive intentions. It used animals and other symbols to represent foreign countries and their interests. The cartoon was widely reprinted in mainland China and overseas without crediting Tse. Because of this unauthorized and uncredited distribution, Chinese historians often credit Tse's cartoon to a Shanghai cartoonist.

Political cartoons were one of the most accessible ways to express opposition to the Qing government; caricature was particularly effective in depicting its weakness and corruption. Due to its unique position—detached from both the mainland and the government—Hong Kong served as an outlet and an important base of support for early political cartoonists. From 1895 until the revolution in 1911, the territory was home to revolutionary newspapers that encouraged the overthrow of the Qing government and the establishment of a new Republic of China. And when the anti-Qing The Journal of Current Pictorial was banned on the mainland in 1905, its cartoonists moved to Hong Kong and published ten more issues before the colonial government ceded to Qing pressure and shut it down.

Before Sun Yat-Sen established the Republic of China in 1911, his revolutionary group, the Chinese Alliance, often used Hong Kong to produce and circulate anti-Qing materials. In 1907, they circulated a supplement to their newspaper, the People's Journal, called Condemnations from Heaven. A cartoon in the supplement titled The True Face of the Nation’s Betrayer in the Present, depicted high-ranking officers of the Qing government with their heads chopped off or split open. Not surprisingly, this triggered a strong reaction by Qing officials, who protested to Hong Kong's colonial government.Hong Kong quickly established a new regulation to stop the printing and distribution of these revolutionary cartoons.

In 1912, He Jian-shi, Zheng Nu-quan, and the other

artists who had published The Journal ofCurrent Pictorial began a new publication in Shanghai, The True Record. This magazine criticized the government of the new Republic of China, especially the political struggles within the Guomindang (GMD, or Nationalist Party). The True Record was banned in 1913, and some of its artists, including Zheng Nu-quan, fled to Hong Kong. The owner of Hong Kong's Leung Kwok-ying Drugstore admired the social power of cartoons and supported Zheng to continue his work. However, due to illness, the cartoonist did not live to see the publication of Renjian Pictorial in 1920, a book of approximately 190 cartoons criticizing the politics and culture of mainland China.

The True Record and Renjian Pictorial are typical of early manhua. The genre matured in the shadow of the revolutionary struggle in China and was closely connected to politics and war. While cartoons gradually became less political and more mainstream in the 1920s, they were still a popular

medium for promoting political messages both in Hong Kong and in mainland China.

The foundation for the development of manhua as an art form rather than a political venue was laid in the 1920s. The word manhua” itself became more widely used—an important linguistic shift——after the famous writer Feng Zi-kai published his first collection of cartoons, Zi-kai Manhua, in 1925. Feng’s fame drew attention to his use of the word; the phrase “Zi-kai Manhua” was soon commonly used, associating the artist's style with Japanese manga. Although the term manhua had existed before, the publication of Feng’s collection led it to take precedence over the many other descriptions of cartoon art. Under this more unified term, cartoons

Zi-kai Manhua, by Feng Zi-kai, 1925

began to attain the status of a specialized art form, rather than being the loose collection of styles of previous decades.

## Emerging: 1927–1949

Prior to about 1927, most manhua focused on revolutionary politics and current events. By the 1930s, though, their content, style, and manner of expression began to change and improve. Chinese cartoonists became aware of the three essential ingredients of manhua—a powerful idea, the representation of people or events, and a thought-provoking style of narrative—and their work was soon recognized as a powerful art form, attracting the praise and involvement of famous artists, such as the writer Lu Xun. While Shanghai remained the center of manhua production, comics' popularity began to spread to other cities, including Guangzhou, Tianjin, and Hong Kong.

Before the Second World War, Hong Kong manhua followed the stylistic developments of mainland comics, particularly those drawn in Shanghai and Guangzhou. With the establishment in 1927 of the Cartoon Association (Manhua Hui), the first such trade organization in China, the activities of cartoonists became more organized. The association helped to solidify the loosely organized network of artists that made up the comics industry, and it encouraged efforts to raise the quality of its products.

The first Chinese cartoon magazine, Shanghai Sketch, appeared in 1928. The eight-page, tabloid-size journal was printed with two-color lithography and included four pages of manhua, by such artists as Ye Qian-yu, Zhang Guang-yu, and Huang Wen-nong. The first serial comics with regular characters—heavily influenced by Western serials such as Gilbert Dalziel's Ally Sloper's Half

Holiday and Richard Outcault's The Yellow Kid—was Ye Qian-yu's Mr. Wang, who appeared in Shanghai Sketch in 1928. Numerous imitators sprang up to compete with Shanghai Sketch, first in Shanghai and then south into the Guangzhou area.

Between 1934 and 1937, about seventeen cartoon magazines were published in Shanghai. Modern Sketch, first published in 1934, was the most important after Shanghai Sketch. It included many outstanding artists of the time and established a standard for emerging art. Most of the cartoons produced during these years were political and satirical cartoons, often anti-Japanese propaganda.

Beginning in 1934, many newspapers added sections devoted to manhua. This new outlet for Chinese cartoons was put to propaganda use in 1937, as Japan threatened and then launched war. For the next eight years, almost all manhua focused on the war, which China was losing. When the Japanese occupied Shanghai, many antiwar cartoon magazines relocated to Guangzhou, until it too was occupied in 1939. After that, Hong Kong became the headquarters for the manhua industry, and many of the cartoonists oved to the British colony. In addition to publishing antiwar manhua, Hong Kong hosted an antiwar cartoon exhibition in 1939, The Modern Chinese Cartoonist Exhibition, organized by Ye Qian-yu. All manhua activity stopped in 1941, when Hong Kong was also captured by Japan

With the defeat of Japan in 1945, on both the Chinese and Allied fronts, political cartoons rapidly diminished, and the content of manhua shifted to serial stories about everyday life. However, the continued instability of mainland China kept alive some political

The Modern Chinese Cartoonist Exhibition, brochure cover, artist unknown, 1939

cartoons that documented the struggles between the Nationalists and the Communists. The most significant work of this period was This Is a Cartoon Era, published in 1947 by the anti-Nationalist manhua artists group Renjian Huahui, which included mainland cartoonists who had been purged by the GMD and fled to Hong Kong. After the Communists seized power in 1949, most pro-Communist artists returned to the mainland.

## Rising: 1949–1968

After the physical damage of the war with Japan—and given the ongoing political damage of the civil war on the mainland—Hong Kong was eager for redevelopment and a return to normalcy. The reappearance of newspaper comic strips was one of the first signs of this return. These four-panel strips, which focused on daily life rather than on politics, became the main format for Hong Kong manhua for the next thirty years. They were a decidedly popular medium, with dialogue written in colloquial Cantonese rather than in literary Chinese.

Newspapers were the main venue for manhua between 1945 and 1949; the ongoing civil war curtailed the market for stand-alone comic books. Yuan Bou-wan's Kiddy Cheung, Miss Lau, and Wong Chai, which represented the lower classes, were very popular newspaper strips. In 1950, Kiddy Cheung was made into a movie featuring the six-year-old Bruce Lee. Yuan's strips were later collected into best-selling comic books.

Hong Kong began to flourish in the 1950s as money and people fleeing the Communist mainland poured in. Demand for cultural goods exploded, and the variety of publications steadily increased. Before the revolution, the mainland had been the primary source for Chinese-language publications. After 1949, however, non-Communist subject matter was considered evil, and Hong Kong became the only place in China with the experience and talent needed to produce manhua for commercial purposes. With the end of the civil war, the rebirth of the Hong Kong market, and the increasing demand from Chinese-speaking markets in Southeast Asia and North America, manhua production in Hong Kong expanded rapidly.

Cut off from the popular culture of mainland China, Hong Kong's cartoonists turned to American comics for inspiration and gradually evolved a unique hybrid of Eastern and Western styles. American comic books had been available before the war, but because of their language and cultural differences, as well as their high price, they were never very popular.

Hong Kong, like America, experienced a baby boom after 1945, which created a ready market for children's manhua. By the early 1950s, many American kids' comic books had been translated and published in Hong Kong, popularizing such characters as Pinnochio, Mickey Mouse, Donald Duck, and Elmer Fudd. Many locally produced comics carried obvious American influences too. Little Angeli, created by the Bao brothers in 1954, featured small, round-faced characters modeled after Little Abner, Popeye, and other American examples.

But Hong Kong manhua also had a style of their own. The characters had exaggerated features and Chinese clothes; the story lines dealt with Chinese themes or events; and there were more panels per page than in American comics. The longest-running children's magazine was Children's

Paradise, first published in 1953. Created and edited by Law Guan-tsiu, a Guangzhou artist who came to Hong Kong shortly after the war, the manhua also became popular among Chinese outside Hong Kong, particularly in Southeast Asia. More than 1,000 issues of Children's Paradise were produced before the title was finally retired in 1995.

The era's most important and influential manhua magazine for adults was Cartoons World, first published in 1956. It published work by established cartoonists but also nurtured a new generation of manhua artists, who eventually provided the talent for the comics boom of the 1960s. As with children's manhua, American comics had a big influence on the developing Hong Kong style. In Hui Guan-man's best-selling Uncle

Children's Paradise, edited by Law Guan-tsiu, 1953

Choi—which initially described the life of a simple-minded soldier but quickly transformed into a more serious wartime narrative——American comics elements were integrated with traditional Chinese line-art drawings. As a result, more panels could be fit on a single page, better story lines created, and more sophisticated characters developed.

Uncle Choi was a huge success, and some of its editorial and formal innovations proved very influential. Unlike most manhua of the time, for instance, Uncle Choi presented characters in modern clothes, and its humor was contemporary. It also came in a different size, five by seven inches, rather than the then-traditional 3.6 by 5 inches. Other artists began using the larger format, and soon a new periodical, Caricature Weekly, switched to the even greater size of a tabloid newspaper. This format, two sheets of eight unbound pages,

sold for ten cents and was referred to as a penny paper.

The inexpensive journal was so successful that it soon became a daily; it also spawned many imitators. Some of its notable progeny were Entertainment News, New Comics Weekly, Epoch Comics Weekly, Daily Comics, The Cartoon and Comic Gardens, Radar Pictorial, and Companion Comics. The main attractions of these tabloid-format manhua papers were cartoon strips and short pictorials created by local comics artists. American cartoons and comics, reprinted without permission, were also included. Readers of these manhua were not always interested in quality, and most of the papers were poorly produced; but the penny papers were still a great success, and they contributed to the rapid expansion of the Hong Kong manhua industry in the early to mid-1960s. The tabloid format itself began to disappear in the late 1960s.

By the middle of the decade, more readers were interested in comic books with a single, ongoing narrative. The best-sellers among these serial manhua were Old Master Q, by Wong Chak, Dumb Detective, by Song Sam-long, and 13-Dot Cartoons, by Lee Wai-chun. Each of these manhua characters represented a different social stereotype, and each had its own audience. Only Old Master Q is still published today; it is the longest-running Chinese manhua serial.

From the mid-1950s to the mid-1960s, publishers and artists tended to avoid controversial subjects. Most manhua were based on everyday life in Hong Kong, with only the occasional criticism of social problems such as housing, inflation, education, and crime. The late 1960s, the height of the Cultural Revolution in mainland China, saw some exceptions to this general avoidance of political subjects. Editorial pages in both pro- and anti-Communist newspapers published cartoons criticizing the opposition. Pro-Communist cartoons focused on the evils of imperialism and criticized Hong Kong's colonial government. Even children's manhua in pro-Communist newspapers were full of propagandistic fervor.

One of the most prominent anti-Communist cartoonists was E-king Yen. His early work in Dai Jung Daily and Express Daily dealt with international politics and the Cultural Revolution-era power struggles on the mainland. He also strongly criticized Communist activity in Hong Kong, especially the riots of 1967, and other Hong Kong artists began to direct their attention toward these events. Even so, political cartoons never again played as prominent a role as they did before the war. After the Cultural Revolution ended in 1976, most people lost interest in politics, and ideological comics receded into near invisibility.

In the 1960s, the manhua industry was challenged by the widespread distribution of pirated comics. Bootleg Japanese manga and Taiwanese comics were a bargain compared to Hong Kong manhua since the same ten cents bought more pages in the unauthorized versions. The industry's response was to change the standard size of manhua to five by seven inches, increase the number of pages, and boost the price to twenty cents. Some Hong Kong manhua were published in books as large as seven by ten inches. Five by seven inches became—and still is——the most common size for locally produced manhua. The effort might have helped local comics compete with pirated foreign books, but it did little against a more serious adversary. Television was introduced to Hong Kong in 1967, and manhua would never regain its peak level of popularity.

## Establishing: 1969–1989

Television proved to be a particularly attractive medium in Hong Kong because it introduced citizens

to foreign popular cultures, especially that of Japan and the United States. As this new competition for leisure time grew, Hong Kong manhua were forced to move in new directions.

The most popular and lucrative of these new directions was the kung fu manhua, modeled after the martial arts movies, especially Bruce Lee’s films, that dominated the Hong Kong box office in the early 1970s. Kung fu fever let the masses escape the unpleasant aspects of their daily lives. Comics based on the same story lines and action became immensely popular, and they remained one of the dominant strains of manhua throughout the 1970s and 1980s.

Little Rascals (Siu Lau-man), by Wong Yuk-long, 1970

![](images/57939aa40570fe8ddbc4ce5ef3aa8c8e69b21954827127bfa482dd9143bf0ad3.jpg)

The visual style and drawing techniques of kung fu comics were veró much influenced by American action comics and Japanese manga of the 1950s and 1960s. Yet this new genre also combined illustrations and storytelling in a manner quite similar to the traditional lianhuantu format. Among the most successful artists to workon kung fu comics was Wong Yuk-long (Tony Wong), who combined Japanese techniques and characters with his knowledge of traditional lianhuantu. His first successful comics were Little Vagabond (1968), Son of Ultra-Man (1969), and Solar Lord (1969), but his biggest hit was Little Rascals (Siu Lau-man) (1970), a serial lianhuantu about a group of young people living in a Hong Kong public housing project. These good guys used violent means to enforce justice against gangsters and other dubious characters. Not only was Wong's comic a best-seller, so

too were its many imitators, the best of which was Seung-gun Siu-bo's Lee Siu-lung (Bruce Lee's Chinese name).

The explicit violence of kung fu manhua may have helped sell comic books, but it also attracted the attention of the Hong Kong government. In 1975, a new Indecent Publications Law took effect, which limited the scope and types of violence and characters allowed in manhua magazines. Little Rascals (the literal translation of the title is actually “little gangsters") was renamed Oriental Heroes (Lung Fu Mun). The new name suggested strength and fortitude rather than antisocial, underworld behavior. The story line too was changed: the characters no longer lived in Hong Kong's public housing projects, but were instead usually based in Japan. Plots were less realistic and more clearly fictional, thus avoiding the criticism that they acted as negative role models for young readers or reflected a negative image of Hong Kong.

The law applied only to magazines and comic books; daily newspapers were spared the censorship. This created a big loophole, which was exploited by artists such as Wong and Seung-gun, who changed the format of their comics from a magazine format to a daily tabloid newspaper. They did, however, make the violence in their manhua less graphic. Even with this slight self-censorship, Wong's and Seung-gun's work soon dominated the Hong Kong manhua market, and their story lines and drawing styles remained fairly constant for years.

A breakthrough in drawing style finally occurred in 1982, in Ma Wing-shing's The Chinese Hero (Chung-wah Ying-hung), first published as a supplement to Wong's other major kung fu title,

Drunken Master. Ma's innovative, realistic drawings utilized bold, solid colors and detailed faces resembling real people. The style was heavily influenced and inspired by Japanese manga artist Ryoichi Ikegami. The Chinese Hero was an immediate success when it then appeared separately from Drunken Master, making Ma, who had received little formal education, the protagonist of a rags-toriches story. The fame he received helped change the perception of manhua drawing, legitimizing it as an art form.

While kung fu comics came to dominate the Hong Kong market, they were just one of several new directions manhua took in the early 1970s. Some experimental publications appeared, although they were inevitably short-lived. Experimental Cartoons and Cartoons News, for example, were published in 1975 by a group of young artists and edited by Yuan Yat-mu, Mok Wah, and Yeung Waipong. These two magazines were the first to introduce manhua criticism to Hong Kong. They also encouraged a serious attitude toward making manhua and introduced new concepts and forms from foreign comics. Unfortunately, both magazines managed to put out just three issues before their meager budgets were depleted. Yet despite the magazines' failures, the manhua artists who created them continued to organize manhua projects outside the mainstream.

As Hong Kong comics developed, certain genres, such as social, satirical, and political manhua, were often overlooked. Not until the 1980s, when the handover of Hong Kong from Britain to China began to loom, did political cartoons become an active genre again. The most prominent artists of these new political manhua were Zunzi and Ma Long, both of whom were established editorial cartoonists for daily newspapers. Their work focused primarily on British and Chinese

policies in Hong Kong and the incompetence of the Hong Kong government. In addition to their daily work in newspapers and magazines, they regularly published their manhua collections in book form, such as Zunzi's Grandpa Deng (1994 and 1997) and Ma’s Political Comics Collection (1990). These political manhua were considered “alternative” because the mainstream market was dominated by action, martial arts, and manga-inspired comics.

## Exploring: 1990 to the Present

Hong Kong experienced a bloom of cultural diversity during the years before the handover to China, a bloom that extended to manhua. While the mainstream market was divided between manga and locally produced comics that involved gambling, romance, ghosts, and sword fighting, a few artists were experimenting with new drawing techniques, more complex story lines, and more diverse subject matter. Different types of work gradually emerged, though it was difficult for this material to find a niche within previously existing genres.

![](images/f813fdf2bb3c49706c9fea1865d0b2cb890a98044058b8fb3d102e98a0631a3f.jpg)  
Editorial cartoon by Zunzi, 1993

In the early 1990s, “three-part stories," which were violent comics set in Hong Kong, became increasingly popular. These manhua—such as Teddy Boy, Portland Street, and Red Light District, all published in 1992——depicted violence in a new way, with realistic locations and situations that suggested their events were actually happening (or at least could happen) in Hong Kong. In response, the government amended the indecent publications law by restricting some of these new manhua to adult readers and making the publishers put them in a sealed wrapper with a warning message attached.

Other artists continued to work on political cartoons, children's comics, and experimental titles. The most popular kids’ comic was Yellow Bus (1996), drawn by Mak Ga-bik (Alice Mak), with script by Tse Lap-man, and featuring a pig named McMug. The drawing style and story line appealed to both adults and children, and as had happened with other “cute” characters in Japan, Mcmug products, such as stuffed toys, stationery, and pens and pencils, greatly extended the title's popularity.

The trend toward alternative manhua for young adults began in the late 1980s, when the artist Li Chi-tat began to create highly detailed manhua heavily influenced by Japanese manga. He and other artists, including Au-Yeung Craig and Lai Tat-wing, appealed to audiences looking for a more sophisticated style. Au-Yeung, who has a degree in graphic design, approached manhua as a combination of illustration and art, and created story lines that are at times fragmented. Lai Tatwing also had a distinctive drawing style, which is sometimes an experiment with different compositions, points of view, and new ways of using dialogue (he even created whole works without any dialogue). The success of these artists illustrated the possibilities for self-expression and artistic exploration within the medium and provided a model for a new generation of artists.

![](images/7027c68c7c8e13572bdae33f63f93ce0ff1853a7c7b2fce4d4bd2ad54c9e495a.jpg)  
Mom's Drawer Is at the Bottom, by Lau Lee-lee, 1998

One of these younger artists, Lau Lee-lee (Lily Lau), is the first self-proclaimed feminist manhua artist. Her first comic, Mom's Drawer Is at the Bottom, was published in 1998. Though her work, infused with feminist themes, such as a critique of gender inequality and heterosexual relationships, might seem radical for the general public, it was met with a positive reception. Her drawing style is graphic, and she did not hesitate to depict women's and men's bodies in sexually explicit scenes.

Recent work by young, alternative manhua artists has

brought new vigor to the industry. Still, support for manhua as part of Hong Kong culture— especially for preserving its history and promoting the understanding of its cultural importance—is uncommon. A few prominent artists, such as Au-Yeung Craig and Lau Lee-lee, have received funding from the Hong Kong Arts Development Council, but that organization's focus is more on the production of comics than the analysis of them. In 1998 the Council did sponsor Cartoonet, a critical publication that explored the importance of manhua in Hong Kong's popular culture, but the project only lasted for two issues.

Prior to Cartoonet, some short-lived manhua reviews were published. Monthly Comic Magazine published five issues in 1990, then ceased, and published three more issues in 1993. It was the only record of the industry in the mid-1990s, and while it is a partial record, it is an improvement on previous decades, for which no overall record exists at all. Notwithstanding critical shortcomings, Hong Kong manhua have thrived creatively over the past decade. Though begun as a satellite to the mainland comics industry—an unstable collection of titles rented from temporary street kiosksthe Hong Kong manhua business has become strong and independent, able to withstand successive shocks of foreign influences. Now that Hong Kong is once again under Chinese rule, the industry may face more competition. On the other hand, the new relationship with mainland China offers the chance for Hong Kong manhua to reach a much larger audience. So while it is still unclear how reunification with China will affect Hong Kong manhua, judging from the events of the past century, it seems likely that new innovations will sustain the industry and encourage ever changing forms of comic art. Hong Kong manhua will continue to evolve, changing in accordance with economic, political, and cultural shifts.

Satirical and political manhua were never considered mainstream reading under British colonial rule. The China Punch, a magazine begun by British journalists in 1867, was one of the first venues for political cartoons in Chinese territory, but it was aimed at English readers, and its content often denigrated Chinese customsand practices. The first truly Chinese political cartoon didn't appear in Hong Kong until 1899, when Tse Tsan-tai published The Situation in the Far East, which illustrated Western threats to China. Since then, and despite the fact that they were never popular reading, cartoons have been used to express critical views of Chinese government and society.

In the early decades of the twentieth century, politically active artists opposed to the Qing imperial government used cartoons to spread revolutionary messages. Leading illustrators—among them Pan Da-wei, Gao Jian-fu, Xie Ying-bo, He Jian-shi, and Zheng Nu-quan—began to publish this kind of work in The Journal of Current Pictorials (1905) and The True Record (1912). After the establishment of the Republic of China in 1911, the same artists used comics to critique the new government. When the Republic banned The True Record in 1913, Zheng Nu-quan fled to Hong Kong. The comics he drew there were printed posthumously in 1920 in Renjian Pictorial, then considered one of the few important political comics produced in Hong Kong before the Second World War.

Shanghai, not Hong Kong, was the center of satirical and political cartooning before the war. The periodicals produced there during the 1930s, such as Shanghai Sketch (1928), Modern Sketch (1934), and Shanghai Manhua (1936), are the era's classic examples of political and social manhua. However, after Japan invaded China in 1937—eventually occupying Shanghai—Hong Kong became an important source of comics, especially those with an anti-Japanese or antiwar agenda.

During the Chinese civil war of the late 1940s, many cartoons with anti-Nationalist Party, pro-Communist messages were published in Hong Kong, notably in the journal This Is a Cartoon Era. After the Communists established the People's Republic, relations between the mainland and the British colony were strained, but refugees fleeing Communist rule came to Hong Kong looking for a new life, not continued political activity. Political cartoons quickly declined in importance.

In the decades following the revolution, Communist propaganda comics produced on the mainland were readily available in Hong Kong. However, these ideological manhua were never popular. On the other side of the political spectrum, anti-Communist messages were found in such Hong Kong manhua as Three Men Going Home (1955) and The World of E-king Yen (1968), which were often sold alongside the pro-Communist titles.

From 1960 through the late 1980s, political comics were an insignificant part of the Hong Kong market, which was dominated by humor and kung fu manhua. As the handover of Hong Kong to China grew closer, political cartoons came back into style. After the protests on the mainland in 1989——and the massacre at Tiananmen Square on June 4—people in Hong Kong became more politically aware and active. During this period, editorial cartoonists for the daily newspapers, including Zunzi, Ma Lung, Local Boy, Yuan Yat-muk, and Apink, recorded and commented on political events in Hong Kong. Zunzi, one of the most productive artists of the era, was particularly successful with political caricature. He drew familiar figures, including top Chinese and Hong Kong officials, as well as fictional figures representative of Hong Kong politicians. Many other artists produced satirical and political comics, capturing this important moment in Hong Kong's history.

After Hong Kong was handed back to China, artists produced political manhua that criticized the Special Administrative Region (SAR) government and even mainland China's top officials. Although political criticism was not banned by the new government, the work of many artists reflected some degree of self-censorship. More damaging, though, has been the decrease in the number of outlets for political and satirical cartoons since the handover in July, 1997.
