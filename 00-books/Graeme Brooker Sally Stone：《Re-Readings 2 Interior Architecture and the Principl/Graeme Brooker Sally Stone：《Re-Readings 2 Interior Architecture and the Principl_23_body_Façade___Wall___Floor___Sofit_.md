## Façade

When it came to prominence as part of architectural discourse, the decorous façade stood as an architectural super-element, coordinating the door, windows, and walls, united into a singular and signature composition.

– Alejandro Zaera-Polo and Stephan Truby. <sup>15</sup>

The façade is often described as the plane endowed with the most signifi cant meaning in architecture. As Zaera-Polo suggests, throughout history it has provided the

opportunity to create a prominent plane, one that is replete with aesthetic, cultural and political signifi cance. This is still the case, but technologies have impacted upon its role as an exterior-facing wall. Its responsibility for controlling both interior and exterior environments, excluding inclement weather, and retaining interior temperatures, has been radically altered through the development of glass and curtain walling systems. Because of the signifi cance that the façade of a building will consist of, and combined with the complex planning laws of most cities, the façade is often one of the few remaining planes left standing after a building is demolished. Therefore the aesthetics, and subsequently the meaning of a good façade, are often highly prized. In the remodelling of the iconic Everyman Theatre in Liverpool, Haworth Tompkins utilised the signifi cance of the façade to project a meaningful image of the building and its occupancy back to the city.

The main west-facing façade of the building was adapted to portray a large-scale public work of art consisting of 105 moveable metal sunshades, each one carrying a life-sized portrait of a Liverpool resident. Typographer and artist Jake Tilson created a special font for a new version of the iconic red ‘Everyman sign. Collaborating visual artist Antoni Malinowski made a large painted ceiling piece for the foyer, to complement an internal palette of brickwork, black steel, oak, reclaimed Iroko, deeply coloured plywood and pale in situ concrete.

As well as signifying what a reused building can mean to the city, the façade of a building can provide a direct view into what the interior has become. Brinkworth were commissioned to rethink the All Saints brand’s identity. The designers created an unforgettable façade display for the retail spaces through which they wanted to refl ect the All Saints image of ‘elegant decay’. The interior of the fi rst store in Spitalfi elds, London, was partly ruined, with walls and bare structure exposed through a process of creative demolition and the intuitive stripping back of the layers of accretions in the existing interior space. The exterior façade was enclosed in rusted steel and then the shop window was fi lled with Singer sewing machines, projecting the tailoring background of the brand’s founders and the function of their clothing retail space to the street outside whilst enticing the shoppers in.

## Wall

The wall is a plane that both divides and unites space. Whether permanent or temporary, a wall will organise and demarcate what is public, private, formal or informal in a space. It is a spatial device that will provide protection, separating various visual, acoustic and environmental situations. For instance, in the Casa De Musica in Porto, opened in 2005, both the visual and acoustic properties of the main hall were enhanced through the utilisation of corrugated glass screen walls, allowing light and view into what is normally designed to be a sealed off black-box performance space. The wall is an element that can be designed and made to work very hard in an interior, containing storage in the form of cupboards and shelves upon which items such as books and possessions can be placed. Walls can also be less matter-of-fact, and instead, when made very thick, can contain surprises in the form of secret passages, stairs or libraries. Poche, or pockets, is a wall tactic where spaces such as rooms can be ensconced in the thick walls of what are often historic buildings. Utilising this wall tactic means that whole rooms can often be found secreted in the walls of historic palaces and castles.

As well as being secretive, walls can be used to impart information through the display of objects in an exhibition. In the Palazzo della Ragione, Milan, the design of the exhibition Convoy RSHA January 30, 1944, designed by Morpurgo de Curtis Architetti Associati, utilised a series of dynamic walls that were placed amongst the historic space of the existing building. Each wall told the story of the movement and deportation of Jewish people through this time. Walls were used to not only impart information but were also placed dynamically against the rigid structure of the building, in order to symbolise movement and denote the terror and sadness of these enforced transitions across space and time. Whether thick or thin, permanent or temporary, hard or soft, static or in motion, as Corbusier stated, walls can call forth emotions.

## Floor

The fl oor is a surface upon which our daily lives are performed. It is a plane that negotiates the space between the body and the substance of the ground. In an interior context, it is sometimes found to be a highly decorated element, patterned and embellished in order to make a distinct feature of a space. In other interiors it is reduced to invisibility, abstracted to form a continuous unbroken surface upon which use can take place unguided and without distraction. It can be textured, smooth, elevated, moveable, transparent, fl oating or solid. Whatever form it has been manipulated to take, it always establishes conventions, cultural habits and the techniques for territorialising inside spaces.

In the Towada community centre in Japan, Kengo Kuma designed the floor to be an undulating surface, providing a playful surface for children to roll around on inside the building when inclement weather kept them indoors. The timber floor was constructed from different shaped sheets of timber, stacked to form a contoured interior landscape, a series of rolling timber hills, where the children can roam and play.

In essence, in all projects, the floor is an instrument of territory making, an overlooked surface that is arguably a support player to the dominance of the vertical plane to which it is often attached – the wall, the surface of endless scrutiny in architectural history. The floor is often a discrete surface, yet one that is fundamental to our daily lives.

## Sofit

If the fl oor anchors us to the earth, the ceiling allows our spirits to aspire to the sky.

– Edwin Heathcote, The Meaning of Home.<sup>17</sup>

Usually opposite the floor and atop the wall, the soffit, or ceiling, provides an enclosure for a space in the form of a lid or a cover. It is a plane that can be mundane and banal, merely a cover for hiding the services of a space. The ceiling or the soffit – a plane installed on the underside of the floor above – has become synonymous with the contemporary requirement to artificially heat, cool, light, power and also to protect interior spaces from dangerous phenomenon, such as fire.

Throughout the 20th century, the space between the ceiling and the soffit became a deepening cavity that held all manner of pipes, ductwork and cables, detectors and alarms, neatly secreted behind a plane of modular tiles suspended in a metal grid. This has not always been the case and in some projects the ceiling can be afforded a vital role in the design of the interior, making an expressive surface in which even with the suspended ceiling removed, the exposed ductwork, cables and sensors are organised and used to delineate movement or lighting in a space.

When fully considered the ceiling can become expressive to such a degree that it becomes the dominating interior element of the space. The Camper Store by Atelier Marko Brajovic in São Paolo in Brazil has been fashioned from thousands of red shoelaces, hung from the ceiling. The ceiling plane undulates through the space and is atmospherically carefully controlled with lighting inside it. In carefully chosen spaces the laces hang lower right down to the floor and connect the ceiling with the floor plane.

A soffit can be designed to perform its practical duties, such as lighting and climate control, but rather than hiding these elements away it can be configured in a way that celebrates their existence. Lazzarini and Pickering’s design for a villa in Positano, Italy, included a table whose surface continued up exuberantly to the ceiling, fulfilling the duty to light a particular piece of furniture as well as creating a sculptural element in the interior space.

Whichever way a plane is utilised in a space, and for every example where it has been deployed without thought for its expressive potential, there are a multiplicity of ways of realising dramatic walls, floors, ceilings and the façades of interior spaces.

# Hotel Barrière Le Fouquet

Location Paris, France

Remodelled 2006

Designer Maison Édouard François

![](images/ce4a3f94729ac77d9eef3b3f576ba75188687ed526b10d70b7c9f9de0d4238cf.jpg)

Figure 3.8 In order to unify the whole aesthetic of the block, concrete facsimiles of scanned existing façades were applied to the fronts of the existing buildings.

To enclose a building with a new cover or a façade ensures that the manner in which the building is presented to its surrounding environment is altered. These changes may range from the straightforward, such as a repainting, or the reordering of non-structural matter, to something much more complex and significant such as restructuring the façade and altering how the building is communicated to its existing environment. The form of the alteration is critical, because it can be utilised in order to signal a number of things. Sometimes in a sensitive location any changes to a façade will have to accommodate certain restrictions, often through specifi c regulatory conditions such as listing or

heritage concerns. But this does not mean that the resultant façade cannot still be a radical projection of the new use of an existing set of spaces.

In 1999 new owners acquired the traditional Parisian Brasserie Le Fouquet, along with the surrounding block of buildings. The block contained seven buildings that ranged in age and architectural distinction. They were in a central location and faced onto the Champs-Élysées, the Avenue George V, as well as two surrounding side streets. The brief to the designer was to transform the seven buildings into a whole building, unifying them to become a single

Figure 3.9 The new approach disrupted the relationship between the window and the detailing of the façade.

monument. Due to the dense fabric of the block, internal courtyards were to become terraces for the hotel, and a garden for the brasserie was to be retained in amongst the block of buildings.

New facilities for a modern hotel, such as a spa, other restaurants and servicing, were constructed in the shells of the seven buildings. More importantly, 107 rooms were to be created. These would have a very particular ‘service architecture’, a layer of support services that created a series of internal streets, starting in the basements of the buildings in the block. Along with new lift towers, this system could ensure that the highest levels of service to each of the rooms could be maintained, making sure that food, cleaning and all manner of deliveries to individual rooms could be received within minutes of being ordered.

The overwhelming problem was that of the façades of the existing buildings. These consisted of numerous different styles. The four buildings facing the Champs-Élysées were authentic Haussmann style and were listed. The two buildings on Avenue George V were built in the 1980s but were copies of earlier styles. The latest edifi ce to be built was a 1970s smoked-glass façade bank building. The precious, yet mixed, qualities of the architectural heritage of the site prohibited the design of a contemporary style. Instead the designer took a radical strategical approach. The most recently built contemporary façades of the existing buildings were demolished. The Haussmann style listed façades were selected and copied: scanned like sheets of wallpaper, and then applied to the exterior of the contemporary façades of the block, unifying them as one.

Rather than rendering the new façades in the same colours and materials as the

originals, extensively camoufl aging them or passing them off as old, the new exteriors were constructed and fi nished in raw concrete, ensuring a clear distinction between the new and the old. The scale, form and details of the original façades were retained, yet fl attened in the concrete. To add to the dissonance between the new and the old a seemingly irregular array of glazed openings were punctured through the façade. These were the windows of the new hotel rooms behind, on fl oor levels that did not necessarily correspond with the details and scale of the interior levels and the copies of the Haussmann façades. The traditional relationship in a historic façade between a window and the surrounding details of the opening, such as architraving, stringcourse, pilaster and balcony was irrevocably lost in this approach. Yet the dissonance projected in the relationship between new and old created a striking new façade.

# Taichung Infobox

Location Taichung, Taiwan

Remodelled: 2011

Designer Stan Allen Architects

![](images/7a6a5a61c778d928b1b18af4ab6ee388e2757972ac869c1764bcc01346f50375.jpg)

Planes such as walls, fl oors and soffi ts can be seen to link and unite disparate spaces. Through their construction and detailing they can communicate important aspects of a project. Qualities such as permanence or temporality can contribute to their character, and in turn they will convey distinctiveness. Unusual materials may initiate complex and unusual readings of their identities. Even the timescale that it takes to construct them, and the period of time they are expected to remain on site, can provide the impetus to convey important messages regarding how they have come to symbolise the meaning of a particular project and the site in which it is located.

Fig. 3.14 The two storey pavilion is situated as a freestanding element in the large open space inside the obsolete aircraft hangar.

The decommissioned 240-hectare city centre site of Taichung airport is being regenerated in order to become the new Gateway Park City. Stan Allen designed the masterplan, a mixed-use development,

arranged into four distinct neighbourhoods distributed around a long, runway-like, thin park. In order for the general public and other interested parties to feel a part of the development and also be able to monitor the progress on the site, the designers proposed the construction of a pavilion from which to oversee the work, a space that would also contain an exhibition on the site’s history and future use.

One of the key aspirations for the new city plan was the ecological footprint of the project and the long-term sustainability of the place. In order to exemplify these ambitions the designer decided to ensure that the new pavilion summarised and communicated an environmentally friendly message. The 1160 square metre pavilion was sited inside one of the now defunct large aircraft hangars on the edge of the airport. This location provided a huge open space, one that prompted the designers to utilise the full height of the building, creating a freestanding two storey element within the grand hall. The rest of the hangar around the Infobox was designated as temporary exhibition space, allowing visitors to circulate generously around the large open shed space before entering the Infobox.

Bamboo is in abundance in the Far East, and is an economically expedient and practical building material. It is often sustainably grown, and managed and harvested appropriately. Bamboo is stronger per centimetre than steel, and because of this it is a material often found on local building sites where it is used for scaffold. The Taichung Infobox was constructed using a steel frame structure that was then clad in bamboo. Bamboo was chosen not just for its sustainability but also for its strength, where it was utilised in order to bind the steel frame together. As well as its structural capabilities it was also used in order to exemplify identity and

make historical and responsive connections to the visitors to the site through reminding them that this development will not be alien to the city, but was conceived as an extension of the fabric of Taichung. The temporal nature of scaffolding also provided a connection in that it was envisaged that the pavilion will stand for two years before it is then demounted and recycled, with both the steel and the bamboo being reused elsewhere, echoing the changing nature of the cityscape around the visitors to the site.

The walls of the Infobox were made utilising a 75cm-deep three-dimensional grid made from 40,000 pieces of bamboo. The plants were cut and boiled and then dried for one month, giving them durability as well as a tea-coloured appearance. Bamboo construction is highly regulated in the Far East and it must only be worked upon by suitably qualifi ed installers. # Maison, Floirac

Location Bordeaux, France

Remodelled 2012

Designer Petra Blaisse / Inside Outside

![](images/377cadf09ab73f10b201379f4613a2ad045dd9409931c88f3db63d17594a1671.jpg)

In the essay ‘Curtain Wars: Architects, Decorators, and the 20th century Domestic Interior’, the author Joel Sanders suggested that the curtain embodies many of the tensions and prejudices that have divided the roles of the decorator, architect and designers of interior space.<sup>19</sup> He suggested that it is here that the hard surfaces of the architecture meet the soft fabric of the decorator in a juxtaposition that reinforces the common perception that architects work conceptually, with tough and durable materials, while the decorator works intuitively with moveable and often ephemeral objects and surface coverings. Sanders concluded the essay with a call fo the invention of a new design vocabulary that overrides conventions that would collapse distinctions between ‘masculine’ or ‘feminine’, ‘decoration’ and ‘architecture’, ‘solid’ and ‘ephemeral’ and instead embody a new design language for domestic space and beyond.

## He stated:

The time is ripe for a new generation of designers to move beyond Curtain Wars and invent a hybrid design vocabulary that will allow a range of human activities to transpire in domestic spaces. <sup>20</sup>

![](images/d4854cff20c3ce1fd3bc31d018823e3db96eabc291343629e27297458e602fd4.jpg)

In 1998 OMA/Rem Koolhaas designed a house for a client who, during the project’s undertaking, was involved in a serious car crash that left him paralysed from the waist down. Rather than create a one storey, fully fl at and easily accessible bungalow style building, ensuring the wheelchair-bound client encountered no change of level, the resulting project was arranged with three fl oors stacking vertically into a hillside house.

The ground fl oor basement was carved into the hill and contained private spaces such as bedrooms. The ground fl oor contained a garden room and the upper fl oor housed parents’ and children’s rooms. The fl oors of the vertical building were connected via a 3 by 3.5 metre central platform ‘room’. This level plane, with no walls, was operated by a hydraulic lift that would rise through the building allowing the wheelchair user to access all spaces of the house as well as his library of books, artwork and a cellar of wine.

Fifteen years after the original design and construction of the house, Inside Outside were commissioned to revisit the spaces and reassess their qualities. They were asked to do this in order to propose some new interventions that would revitalise the domestic space for the new occupants of the home.

Inside Outside devised a series of curtains that would be utilised to accentuate or edit various aspects of the existing qualities of the house. The bottom fl oor was sunk into the landscape, with an interior courtyard facing the caretaker’s residence and guesthouse. Here Inside Outside introduced a brown and silver, double layered, silk curtain, enhancing the light quality when closed and drawing light into the lower spaces.

The middle fl oor was the most transparent space, boasting a huge glass façade. The client wanted to be able to shut out the light coming in from changing angles of the sun during the day. The designers created and installed a white cotton curtain, a surface that contained a horizontal slit, and a panel that refl ected the bright light of southern France outwards, while subtly diffusing it inwards. An exterior curtain track on the southern façade was reused to house a grey net curtain. In its early years, this part of the house was accompanied by a jute curtain. The new and large net screen fi ltered the view and the harsh sunlight, creating an extra in-between space on the terrace.

When carefully considered, the fl oor plane can provide much more than just the pragmatic requirements of covering a surface. The fl oor is a surface that can be utilised by a designer to create a variety of effects. It can be employed in an overtly functional manner, providing a covering that supplies direction, warmth and colour, as well as a distinct surface or texture, inside of a building. It can also be used to link, as well as to separate, spaces. Whichever way it is deployed, as in all tactical moves, the selection and use of a particular fl oor plane will reveal the designer’s full intentions for the realisation and ultimately the identity of the space.

The Carrer Avinyo apartment was designed for two Spanish brothers to share, and in

which to spend their family holidays in the city. The apartment occupies the fi rst fl oor of a building that is located at the intersection of two important roads in the city: Carrer Escudellers, and the street that gave the apartment its name, Carrer Avinyo. Through the demolition of a number of internal partition walls, a large, angular, double-height space with many small rooms, was converted into one large, well-proportioned single space. The demolition of all of the interior walls ensured that the single space was now ordered and articulated with a series of full-height windows, each with a balcony.

The preponderance of windows and balconies united and also animated the room. This enlivenment of the interior was through natural light, now illuminating the interior, and by overlooking the streets below – allowing the hustle and bustle of the busy city to become part of the interior. A ‘stack’ of bedrooms was placed at one end of the large room in the form of a large, over-scaled piece of two storey furniture. At the other end of the room, and above the kitchen, the designer placed the other bedroom. A mezzanine level, incorporating a library, was designed to afford the occupants access to the bedrooms and also the upper level bathrooms.

While the interior of any apartment is usually a resolutely private space, the Carrer Avinyo is treated as a semi-public space, drawing in the atmosphere of the immediate urban context and distilling its essence into the main family room of the interior. The formation of the large room opens up the interior to the city through the light and views of the numerous windows of the space. Akin to the adjacent George Orwell square, the room was designed to be like a public plaza, a place where the brothers and their families could congregate and enjoy each other’s company around dining and socialising. In order to accentuate this, Kohn installed a large, irregular, lozenge-shaped table at the pointed end of the triangular shaped apartment.

Figure 3.24 Triangular fl oor tiles and the lozenge-shaped table reinforce the dynamic shape of the apartment.

Unifying the interior, yet subtly shifting the emphasis from the private to the public, is an intricate and highly articulated mosaictiled fl oor. It was designed with a triangular pattern profi le, one that emphasised the geometry of the plan of the apartment. The tile pattern was graded in colour from green at one end of the apartment to red at the other. This was done in order to subtly differentiate the brothers’ private spaces. Ensuring continuity with other great designers of the Catalan capital, the encaustic tiles were manufactured by Mosaics Martí, suppliers of tiles to the projects of Antoni Gaudí. As well as symbolising the geometry of the apartment, the triangular profi le of the tile was set up to mirror the adjacent George Orwell square: an even deeper connection made with the urban environment inside the interior space.

In the smallest detail, in this case a fl oor tile and its colouring, the story of the interior, its occupants, and their relationship to each other, and the city they inhabit can be told.

Floor planes can be used to separate as well as link interior spaces. After the success of the Zip Bag, a bestselling luxury bag for the fashion brand Longchamp – which was made from a long run of Zip that allowed it to double or half its size by unfastening it – Thomas Heatherwick was commissioned to design the new fl agship store for the same company in New York. Location London, UK

Remodelled 2008

As well as enclosing services and ordering internal spaces, a soffi t can be used to exemplify the narrative of both its context and the function of an interior space. Sake No Hana, an exclusive Japanese restaurant owned by Alan Yau, the originator of Wagamama and Hakasan, occupies the ground and fi rst fl oor of the Grade II listed Economist building, in Mayfair, central London. Designed in 1964 by Alison and Peter Smithson, the building was highly regarded for a number of innovative features, including its carefully thought out approach to not just the buildings themselves but the open public spaces in-between. The structural simplicity of the building, along with its

‘Roach’ cladding, an expressive fossil-rich Portland stone, created a landmark building, described by Reyner Banham, and the Smithsons, as an example of ‘New Brutalism’.

The restaurant was designed to occupy two fl oors of the smallest of the three towers, marking the corner of Ryder and St James Street. The ground fl oor was designed to accommodate a sushi bar, while the upper level would contain a more formal Japanese restaurant. The corner entrance to the restaurant encouraged the diners to choose the more informal sushi bar experience, arranged around a counter-kitchen and facing St James St, or to ascend the escalator to the upper-level restaurant.

Both the informal sushi bar and the upper-level main dining space were enclosed by an atmospheric tree-like framework of bamboo, on the ground level, and Japanese Cypress on the upper fl oor. On the ground fl oor the bamboo columns were 100mm in diameter, and formed into a series of columns that supported a soffi t consisting of a series of 60mm bamboo beams bolted together to form an overhead lattice. The frame of beams undulated through the interior and across the ceiling. Like an inverted ziggurat, its edges were just one beam thick as they reached the edges of the space, and the external windows, but becoming much deeper as it signalled something important, such as the entrance, where it acted as a signalling device to passers-by on the busy street. Lighting, in the form of Japanese paper lantern pendants, were suspended over the sushi counter. The interior walls were

lined with bamboo while the exterior walls were constructed from cypress veneer.

Upstairs the soffi t was constructed from a framework of Japanese Cypress. The 100 by 100mm columns supported a lattice of 100 by 30mm beams. These were fastened with wedges, as opposed to bolts, aligning the craftsmanship of this level’s interior shell with the slower pace of the consumption of the food in this part of the restaurant and hinting at the quality and time expended on not only the food’s intake but also its preparation. The interior wall was constructed from simple cedar louvres.

The main dining space was placed on a raised platform, and organised around the human scale of tatami mats and low tables. Guests are invited to sit on cushions on the mats, cross-legged in a traditional Japanese dining style.
