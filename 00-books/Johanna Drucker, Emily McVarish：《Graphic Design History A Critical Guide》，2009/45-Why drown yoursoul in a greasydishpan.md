The advertisement for Conover's electric dishwasher announces its virtues in a straightforward way. But the ad for Ransomes "grass machines” suggests a lifestyle of grace and ease. The older paradigm of promoting a product through literal description gradually gave way to associations that placed products, and thus their prospective buyers, in settings of personal fulfillment and cultural favor. Electricity was no longer in its infancy, but the organized delivery of well-regulated supplies to homes and businesses was still novel. In addition to providing light, electricity was used to power small appliances, creating a new market in devices that redefined domestic chores. Likewise, gasoline had become a readily available source of energy for smali motors. This "grass machine" appears to be a push-model, rather than a power-model, but the ease of operation suggests that the labor involved does not raise a sweat. The denigration of physical labor is another subtext in these images: Polite society set itself at a distance from any form of hard work—even as athletics became a sign of middle-class leisure.
