# introduction: a history of hong kong comics

The Chinese word manhua is the equivalent of “comics” or “cartoons" in English; its Chinese character is the same as that of manga, the Japanese term for “comics." Although it’s difficult to trace the exact origin of the term, most historians of Chinese comics believe the contemporary usage of manhua was borrowed from Japan at the beginning of the twentieth century. Today, manhua means all the forms and styles of cartoons, comics, and lianhuantu (a traditional illustrated storybook) in Hong Kong. In tracing the development of the general term manhua, more specific terms are sometimes used to refer to smaller subgenres. Car-ton, as direct translation of the word “cartoon," for example, refers to political satire and caricature as well as more lighthearted works depicting everyday life. In this book, however, manhua refers to all Hong Kong cartoons, caricatures, political and editorial satire, comics, graphic novels, and lianhuantu.

In Hong Kong, as in most East Asian societies, comics are one of the most popular things to read. Although the city's population is only about 6.7 million, more than forty titles are regularly published there. The market is divided into two parts: manhua produced by local artists and Chinese versions of Japanese manga (which dominate the newsstands). Even though manga are currently more popular, only local manhua reflect the cultural values and details of everyday life in Hong Kong. Over the past century, Hong Kong manhua have evolved parallel to the changing and sometimes turbulent social and political environments of mainland China and Hong Kong.

## The Beginning: 1867-1927

Chinese drawings similar to modern cartoons existed in ancient times and appear throughout the country's history. The oldest surviving examples are stone reliefs from the eleventh century B.C. and pottery from 5000 to 3000 B.c. Other examples include symbolic brush drawings from the Ming Dynasty (A.D. 1368–1644), a satirical drawing titled Peacocks by the early Qing Dynasty (1643– 1911) artist Zhu Da, and a work called Ghosts’Farce Pictures from around 1771, by Luo Liang-feng.

Another ancestor of the modern Hong Kong comic is nian hua, the traditional New Year prints made during the Ming and Qing Dynasties. These mass-produced, colored wood-block prints were important decorations for the New Year and were hung in houses for good luck. The development of modern Chinese cartoons began with the introduction of Western lithographic printing methods at the end of the nineteenth century. This new technology not only boosted the circulation of newspapers and magazines, but it also allowed for the printing of cartoons, even in color. Beginning in about 1875, caricatures and satirical drawings appeared in numerous periodicals as complements to their editorial text. The nascent art forms were then known by several names: fengci hua (satirical drawing), yuyi hua (allegorical picture), zhengzhi hua (political picture), shishi hua (current pic-

ture), baodao hua (reporting picture), jilu hua (recording picture), huaji hua (amusement picture), and xiao hua (funnies).

Another predecessor of the Chinese comic is lianhuantu, a palm-size picture book of sequential drawings. This format, which has existed for centuries, consisted of a series of pictures with brief captions above them. Their purpose was to tell stories in a way that was accessible to illiterate or marginally literate readers and typically depicted traditional Chinese stories. It entered the Hong Kong market in the 1920s. Usually imported from Shanghai, they could be found at street kiosks, where they were rented for a small fee. These rental kiosks were a common sight even during the Japanese occupation, and they didn't completely disappear until the 1970s. As with modern comic books, lianhuantu were considered an unschooled medium, meant for a mass audience with little education or aesthetic sophistication.

Chinese manhua was born in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, roughly during the years 1867 to 1927. Early manhua aimed at satire and caricature, and though they were influenced by traditional drawing styles, they were essentially a new art form introduced by foreigners. Hong Kong's first magazine of satirical cartoons, The China Punch, was founded by a British journalist in 1867. It was based on the British satirical journal The Punch and retained the style of political caricature, illustration, and humor of its model.
