## Introduction

Materials are selected and deployed in the design of an interior in order to create specific spatial identities. Surfaces are critical tactics for the designer to consider, as they are the cover or overlay of the space with which the inhabitant or occupant of the environment will interact. The ‘palette’ or ‘sample’ is the language used to describe the assemblage of materials that designers and architects will collect in order to portray the proposed new identity of a space.

The importance of selection can also be extended to the ‘found’ or existing surfaces of the building that is to be reused. These may embody useful narratives from the history of the building. These stories may be symbolic, as they may be materials and surfaces that illustrate the previous life and uses of a space, or they will physically embody its history through the scratches and indentations of their previous lives. However found surfaces are considered, if they are to be reused and retained, their incorporation, along with new applied surfaces, can create a rich weave of a spatial identity that is a complex representation of the past, present and future of the interior space.

A diffi culty facing the architect is how to negotiate a past that concerns the detective and the historian and a future that is of interest to a client and the occupants.

– Irénée Scalbert. <sup>33</sup>

In the design of the Raven Row gallery, in Spitalfields, London, a detailed examination of the history of the building enabled 6a architects to understand how two 18th century silk mercers’ houses, and a 1970s concrete-frame block, could be meaningfully combined to make a space for art. The history of the buildings included the devastation of a fire in 1972. Some of the ornate details and mouldings of the houses survived, and inspired by the surface qualities of the conflagration, the architects reused the fashioned and charred timbers as moulds for the design
