![](images/82b70b33585cdb22a84da39443bf013ea753edfe9a2fc88d6b34c034f5600cb8.jpg) Nils Landberg, Tulpan (Tulip) glasses for Orrefors, 1957 # nils landberg (b. 1907 Västra Vingaker, Sweden) The Swedish glassware designer Nils Landberg studied at the Konstindustrieskolan (School of Arts and Crafts) in Gothenburg from 1923 to 1925 and trained at the newly established school of engraving at the Orrefors glassworks from 1925 to 1927 – as did his compatriot, Sven Palmqvist. Landberg later studied in Italy and France before returning to Sweden, where he began working under Edward Hald as an assistant and engraver at Orrefors. Around 1935, he began creating his own designs for the company and became a member of its in-house design team, producing elegant classical decorative motifs for glass shapes devised by Simon Gate. His own engraved glassware from this period was shown at both the 1937 Paris “Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne” and the 1939 New York World's Fair. During the 1950s, Landberg experimented with abstracted and attenuated forms using both clear and delicately tinted glass. The resulting designs possessed an inherent tension due to his pushing the limits of the medium's tensile strength as far as possible. His famous Tulpanglas (Tulip glass)of 1957 with its slender waist was blown as a single piece and received a gold medal at the 1957 (XI) Milan Triennale. This exceptionally refined design not only epitomized the elegance of post-war Scandinavian organic design but also predicted the bold and colourful nature of Swedish glassware in the 1960s. Revered as a classic Orrefors' design (it remained in production until 1981), the Tulpan range's fragile poise testified to the sheer technical brilliance of Swedish glassblowers. Landberg's Illusion glassware line(1957) similarly emphasized according to Orrefors the “inherent purity and softness of theglass”. Apart from glassware, Landberg also designed lighting and decorative doors and windows. In 1972, he retired from Orrefors and in 1986 the Orrefors Museum held a retrospective exhibition of his glass designs. Landberg's work not only perpetuated the “grace” that had been historically associated with Swedish glassware, but with its exaggerated organic forms it also exemplified the “New Look” of post-war Scandinavian design.
