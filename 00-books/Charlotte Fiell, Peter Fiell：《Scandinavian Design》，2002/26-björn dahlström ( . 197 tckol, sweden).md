![](images/29b6dbeab59a4b99af7e00b250b9ef221c763695c942f5049b18b152e2fce9c3.jpg) Björn Dahlström, BD-1 easy chair for CBI, 1994 # björn dahlström ( . 197 tckol, sweden) ![](images/edcb7f09fc899b4160140d1f2e1bfa84f6aeacaeabd09e0265c39a9190e96dcd.jpg) One of Sweden's leading furniture and industrial designers, Björn Dahlström beganhis career in the mid-1970s designinggraphics and animations for filmand television productions. After having been employed as an art director in an advertising agency in 1982, he founded his own graphic design company, Dahlström Design, and worked for a variety of clients including Ericsson, Scania and Atlas Copco. Eventually, Dahlström turned his attention to three-dimensional design – the RockingRabbit (1985) and streamlined Toycar (1996) for Playsam, and furniture for CBl in the early1990s, were all influenced by his earlier work in animation and graphic design. Dahlström's aesthetically pleasing product designs are often distinguished by their soft yet elemental forms and straightforward functional practicality. Believing that “the designer has to be part of the industrial process, taking the role of free-thinking catalyst”, Dahlström expanded his practice in the 1990s to encompass the design of a wide range of productsincluding a flashlight and soldering equipment for Primus, a pneumatic drill for Atlas Copco, toys and water-play systems for Aquaplay, electric handicap aids for Gewa, bicycles for Skeppshult, and a walking stick and plastic furniture for Magis. One of Dahlström's great skills lies in the way in which he is able to make even “hard” industrial design look and feel attractive. His Cobra MK1 pneumatic drill forAtlas Copco, forexample, which took four years to develop, not only set a new aesthetic precedent for such a product, but also maximized ease of handling by reducing vibrations to a minimum. Throughout the 1990s Dahlström won numerous awards for his product designs, including sixteen Excellent Swedish Design Awards and two Plus Design Awards. In 1999 he became Professor of Furniture Design at the Konstfackskolan (University College of Arts, Crafts and Design) in Stockholm and has since also taught at Beckman's School of Design, Stockholm. Here, Dalén undertook much research on gases and turbines, which resulted in the improvement of hot-air turbine engines and the de Laval steam turbine. Dalén became managing directorof the company in 1909, and subsequently invented Agamassan – a substance that absorbs acetylene allowing the gas to be concentrated without fear of explosion. In 1912 he won the Nobel Prize for Physics for his invention of the solventil, an automatic sun valve which uses sunlight to regulate a gas light-source so that it is turned off during daylight hours. This innovative design was later used internationally for the lighting of unmanned lighthouses and buoys. Tragically, Dalén was blinded in 1913 by an explosion that occurred during one of his experiments. While he was recuperating, he became aware of the problems his wife was encountering with her antiquated and highly temperamental cast-iron cooking range, which needed constant supervision to keepthe temperatures from fluctuating. By applying the concept of heat storage, Dalén invented a cooker that combined a small and efficient heat source, two large hotplates and two generous ovens into one robust and compact unit. Known as the Aga (taking its name from the initials of his company), Dalén's design achieved a high accumulationof heat that could be deliveredin precisely controlled quantities, with a minimum of fuel, within a wellinsulated environment. The heat generated was radiant, so that every cubic inch within each oven was utilized. The heat was also thermostatically controlled, which meant the elimination of all knobs and dials. Since the ovens and hotplates maintained different temperatures depending on their distance from the heat source, theAga could roast, bake, steam, simmer, fry, stew, grill, boil, broil and toast – all at once. Agas were first imported into the UK in 1929 and are now solely manufacturedbyAga-Rayburn in England. All theprincipal castings for Aga cookers are made at the historic Coalbrookdale Foundry in Shropshire – birthplace of the Industrial Revolution. The remarkable Aga revolutionized the design of cookers; even today it has many loyal users who still consider it the ultimateappliance.
