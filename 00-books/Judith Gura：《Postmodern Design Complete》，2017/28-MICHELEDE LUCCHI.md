# MICHELE DE LUCCHI b.1951 FERRARA-BORN DESIGNER OF BEST-SELLING PRODUCTS FOR BOTH AVANT-GARDE AND CORPORATE CLIENTS. Like many of his contemporaries, Michele De Lucchi has worked in several areas of design throughout his career but he has also worked in different markets, in an admirable balance of theory and practice. He has been an important contributor to two of the most influential Italian avant-garde design groups, but has also designed award-winning objects for corporate clients as concerned with basic function as cutting-edge aesthetics. De Lucchi studied architecture in Padua and at the University of Florence, completing his studies in i975, as the Radical Design movements were at their peak. In i973 he joined other architects and designers in Padua to form Cavart, a Radical Design group that, like Global Tools\* and others, staged events and organized seminars. He taught in Florence for a few years before moving to Milan in i978 to work for the manufacturer Kartell\*. But most of his career has followed more adventurous paths. As he recently explained about the appeal of avant-garde groups, "The style was always the problem...it's very rare for an established style to lead to innovation."16 bulbs, He later joined MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design"), designing the colorful Lido sofa (ı982) and, in i983, the First Chair, a space-agelooking piece that marked Memphis's first collection using industrial fabricátion to make furniture in quantity (though the quantities were not large). First became a bestseller and gained its designer star status.'7 De Lucchi designed more furniture for Memphis than anyone other than Sottsass, all imbued with characteristic lively color and wit. These included the perky Kristal table and the distinctive Horizon bed. Pursuing a parallel path in the commercial world, De Lucchi was a design consultant to Olivetti froin 1979 and was appointed its director of design in i988.t8 After Memphis disbanded and Postmodernism began winding down, he began to work for other corporations on less radical products. Its sleek, polished aluminum arms and swivel head became status symbols for design-conscious desks in the high-tech era. Though he has also designed for Compaq, Philips, and Siemens, he has said, "For me, the objects which function really perfectly are not the most interesting ones,"'9 implying that financial considerations rather than creative opportunity motivated his work for industry. His creative side was nurtured in jewelry designs for ACME (see under “Edited Design") and CLETO MUNARI (see under “Edited Design"), where he could play with shape and color. A man of many facets, modernist De Lucchi has remained convinced of the value of artisanry, and in i99o he founded Produzione Privata to produce and market designs made with traditional handicraft techniques, thus feeding his personal interest in diametrically opposed areas: technology and crafts. The two are not incompatible, in his view: "When you design, you try to give a sense of things, a sense about life. A lot of the work we do is about creating differences that make things personal."2o Though trained as an architect, De Lucchi has designed relatively few buildings, yet like many postmodernists he considers design and architecture to be opposite sides of the same coin. In recent years he has applied his architectural talents in designing or restoring buildings for several banks, power plants, and major corporations, while continuing to explore ideas for innovative design by working as a curator for art- and design-related museum exhibitions. De Lucchi is one of the postmodernists who left the fold, disavowing the association with Memphis and labeling the collaborative a “disaster" and a “catastrophe."²' But his contributions remain among its most admired, and his name is inseparable from its history.
