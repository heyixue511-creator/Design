# Modern Design Meets the Depression Another profession attracted to the new field of modern design was the creative theatrical and stage world. The theatrical set design profession was suffering from the increasing popularity of motion pictures, which in 1927 had just introduced sound in The Jazz Singer, starring Al Jolson. Films were drawing crowds away from Broadway, and many theaters that in the past had staged live performances were now converting to movie theaters. ![](images/1f4bc5ded9f06fc31e88feeea26e250047220eb99c35d45b962d6792832133ce.jpg) Norman Bel Geddes (1893–1958), a nationally recognized set designer, saw the potential for his talents in automotive design when urged by Ray Graham, of the Graham-Paige Motor Corporation in 1927. Then 34, Bel Geddes proceeded to design a series of five brass concept car models for Graham, each design representing a 20 percent increase in modernization over the previous one, demonstrating progressive annual model changes, each of which was not to be out of style for five years. Car No. 1 was designed in 1928, intended to be prototyped in fall of 1929, but it was never built due to the stock market crash and the onset of the Depression. Despite this setback, he was to become one of the most flamboyant and imaginative pioneers of the new profession. Norman Bel Geddes, ca. 1934 (February 1934 Fortune magazine. Photograph by Maurice Goldberg). Bel Geddes was born in Adrian, Michigan, as Norman Melancton Geddes. Raised in New Philadelphia, Ohio, he was a high school dropout and tried unsuccessfully to acquire formal art training at the Cleveland Institute of Art and the School of the Art Institute of Chicago. In 1913 he began work as advertising illustrator and art director for the Peninsular Engraving Company in Chicago, and then for the Barnes-Crosby Advertising Agency in Detroit. He also worked in 1916-1917 for Aline Barnsdall's Little Theatre in Los Angeles as a set designer, where he was attracted to the theater. In 1916, when he married Helen Belle Schneider, they incorporated their names to form the surname Bel Geddes. In 1918, he became scene designer for the Metropolitan Opera and designed and directed a number of very successful theatrical works, as well as an ice show starring worldwide skating star Sonja Henie. Bel Geddes's daughter, Barbara Bel Geddes, was born in 1922 and would become a well-known Broadway and film actress starting in 1947. The essence of Bel Geddes's theatrical set design career was to create environments and costumes to entertain and take people into magical worlds of imagination. In the mid–1920s, Bel Geddes, for his own amusement and that of his friends, began creating elaborate table games. In the previous ten years, a public fascination with scale models had been developing. Construction of model airplanes that actually flew became popular with young boys, and electric model railroads at family Christmastime evolved into hobbyists constructing elaborate and extensive basement layouts with realistic miniature trains, bridges, lakes, mountains, trees, people and buildings. The idea of realistic miniature replications of the real world apparently also fascinated Bel Geddes. He started with a five-by-three-foot football field, with players kicking, running, and tackling. That was followed by a mechanical baseball game and an indoor golf game, built with wooden blocks, wire screen, and paste-soaked newspaper. He added green velvet and “shrubbery." The game took over the dining room table and disrupted family life. In 1925, Bel Geddes was creating film set designs for D. W. Griffith and the Cecil B. DeMille film Feet of Clay. He also designed costumes for Max Reinhardt, the Austrian-born stage and film actor and director. During this period, his wife of ten years left him and took his two children. Depressed, Bel Geddes was taken to Belmont Stakes racetrack for distraction, and he was inspired by the pageantry there to invent a new game, which he called the Nutshell Jockey Club. It was a 20-inch-wide racetrack built on a 28-foot-long table. Running times were identical to real track times. There was green velvet turf, white fencing, bleachers, a judges' stand, and boxes for stable owners. The game was electrical and featured 800 cast-iron horses, each three inches long and realistically painted. Twenty horses lined up behind the electric starting gate and were pulled by invisible silk threads connected to unseen pulleys. A “chance machine" varied the speed of individual horses to simulate an actual race. The setup took over the whole basement at his Manhattan brownstone on East 37th Street that doubled as his office, and was estimated to have cost Bel Geddes \$4,000, plus the labor of his staff. Every Saturday evening, eight races were run, announced over an amplifier, with a recording of racetrack bedlam and the sound of horses' hooves to lend atmosphere. Race betting, sale of horses and stables, and odds were conducted through a racing secretary. It was a live horse race in every respect except the weather. Within two months, nearly 100 people owned stables in the Nutshell Jockey Club. The game was a magnet for visiting Hollywood notables, since racing in California would not be legalized until 1933. They included Charlie Chaplin; Douglas Fairbanks, Jr.; Leslie Howard; Ethyl Barrymore; Adolph Menjou; and Basil Rathbone. It was so successful that New York mayor Jimmy Walker, one of the game's enthusiasts, assigned a patrolman to keep “the wrong people” out.¹ Bel Geddes was already famous. After he opened his industrial design office in 1927, Bel Geddes left the theatrical field, except for creating sets for the 1935 Broadway production of Dead End. Despite offers by gambling syndicates to operate the Nutshell Jockey Club (one offered "the blond little Leonardo ... with habitually tousled hair" a thousand dollars per night to operate the Nutshell at Saratoga Springs, New York, for a season), Bel Geddes declined and put it in storage. Department stores continued to enthusiastically promote modern design. In 1928, the B. Altman department store in New York held an exhibition called Twentieth Century Taste. Lord and Taylor announced an international competition in design and trade symbols. Franklin Simon engaged none other than Norman Bel Geddes to develop a modern approach for window displays. His windows featured dramatically lit, very modern-looking, Cubistic pyramids and metallic mannequins. When revealed to the public, they created massive traffic jams and crowded sidewalks. Within 90 days, every store on Fifth Avenue had changed their windows to the new modern style. Also in 1928, Bel Geddes was commissioned by the Simmons Company to design steel beds that would go to market in 1932. Macy's staged a dramatic International Exposition of Art in Industry in 1928, organized by the new head of its department of design, Austin Purvis. The exposition included 15 rooms, most designed by well-known European designers, such as a powder room with products from the Wiener Werkstätte (Vienna Workshops) by Josef Hoffmann, director of the Austrian Academy of Fine Art; a dining room by French jewelry designer Maurice Dufrêne; a man's study and dining room by Bruno Paul, head of the German Vereinigte Staatsschulen für Freie und Angewandete Kunst (United State Schools for Fine and Applied Arts); and a butcher shop and living room by Italian designer Gio Ponti (1891–1979), who had just founded Domus (Home), an Italian architectural and design magazine that promoted the Wiener Werkstätte as well as the Italian Novocento (Nine Hundred) group to which Ponti belonged since its founding in 1926. European émigré designer Kem Weber, who had just designed a skyscraper side table/ vanity; architect William Lescaze (1896–1969); and architect Eugene Schoen (1880–1957) designed the American rooms, which included work by German silversmith émigré Peter Müller-Munk, who later would became a leading industrial designer. European design was still the main draw. From February 12 to March 24, 1928, Richard Bach, of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, decided to emulate the 1925 Paris exposition with French room ensembles in an exhibition, Contemporary American Design, the eleventh in the series, which was intended to conclude in March, but by then, 100,000 had attended and he extended the exhibition to September 2. The subtitle, The Architect and the Industrial Arts, revealed that Bach favored prominent American architects such as John W. Root, Ralph Walker, Raymond Hood, Ely Kahn, and European architects such as Eliel Saarinen, Joseph Urban, and Eugene Schoen, to lead the new profession of industrial design, just as architects had led in Europe, and that was in fact the very purpose of the exhibition. As Bach said in the catalog, [The Museum] has secured the enthusiastic cooperation of nine architects of distinction in this and other cities, who have made the exhibition practically their own by planning every detail of it themselves, from the general design of the gallery as a whole to the minutiae of their individual exhibits. Hence the appropriateness of the special title of the exhibition, "The Architect and the Industrial Arts," as it is a fine and instructive illustration of what the architect can do outside of the field which we commonly associate with his profession, and it was gratifying to us to learn that they welcomed the opportunity of showing this very thing.² American designers and AUDAC members were understandably appalled that they were ![](images/0e53fa05293282ed792c6240e5b8d111b28239bd17067d35cf8bc6f5faee6af8.jpg) excluded from these shows in favor of European and American architects, particularly in the industrial arts arena, where they had demonstrated their abilities in product design far more than architects had. In retaliation, they began planning their own show for the following year. Another designer, 36-year-old Raymond Loewy (1893– 1986), opened his New York office in 1929. His first product client was Sigmund Gestetner, head of the British Gestetner Company, for which he redesigned a Model 66 duplicator (mimeograph) machine from a hodgepodge of shapes into a smooth single form after building a clay model. Gestetner liked it so well he paid Loewy \$2,000. It would be introduced to the market in 1933, and the same design would be used for 15 years afterward. Raymond Fernand Loewy was born in Paris, and as a 15-year-old in 1908, he saw the Brazilian inventor Alberto Santos Dumont (1873-1932) fly his handmade airplane about a hundred yards over a polo field in the Bois de Boulogne in Paris. Inspired, Loewy designed a rubber-band-powered scale-model airplane and named it the Monoplan Ayrel. It won the James Gordon Bennett Cup for model airplanes. Bennett was the American editor of the Paris edition of the New York Herald and the Paris Herald. Teenage boys offered to buy Loewy's plane in such numbers that he patented it and formed a company, the Ayrel Corporation, to produce quantities in rented space. It was advertised and merchandized all over France, and it sold so well he had an offer to purchase his company, and promptly sold it so he could continue his studies.³ He graduated from Paris University in 1910 and from 1914 to 1918 served in the French Army as a captain in the Engineering Corps during World War I, where at the front he decorated his dugout with flowered wallpaper, draperies, and tufted pillows. During four years of war, during which he was burned by mustard gas, he rose from private to captain and received the French Legion of Honor. In 1918 he graduated from the Ecole de Lanneau in Paris. Loewy immigrated to America in 1919 to join his brother Georges, a doctor in Manhattan, with only \$40 in his pocket and still wearing his captain's uniform (the only clothing he had). His sketching on the ship so impressed Sir Harry Gloster Armstrong, then British consul general in New York, that he gave Loewy a note of introduction to the publisher Condé Nast, where he got his first job. He also worked as a fashion illustrator for Vogue magazine, Harper's Bazaar magazine, and Wanamaker's, Saks Fifth Avenue, and Bonwit Teller department stores. He designed costumes for Florenz Ziegfeld before establishing his office. Later, when asked his first impressions upon arriving in America, Loewy replied, To put it briefly, I was amazed at the chasm between the excellent quality of much American production and its gross appearance, clumsiness, bulk, and noise. Could this be the leading nation in the world, the America of my dreams? I could not imagine how such brilliant manufacturers, scientists, and businessmen could put up with it for so long.4 As mentioned earlier, the acclaimed "Dean of Advertising," Ernest Elmo Calkins, had been a vocal proponent of more stylish and attractive products for several years. Now he put his money where his mouth was. In 1929, he appointed 40-year-old Egmont Arens (1889–1966) as art director for Calkins & Holden, where Arens initiated and headed an internal industrial design department. Arens was born in Cleveland, Ohio, and at a young age was sent by his family to New Mexico to recover from tuberculosis. From 1911 to 1914 he attended the University of New Mexico, and from 1915 to 1916, the University of Chicago. He began his career as sports editor of the Albuquerque Tribune-Citizen in 1916, and from 1917 to 1923 operated his own Washington Square Bookstore on West Eighth Street in New York, where he established the Flying Stag Press, printing magazines with an upstairs press. His interest in the arts led to editing and printing Playboy magazine (no, not that one) in 1919, the first American magazine devoted to modern art, named after Irish playwright John Millington Synge's 1907 play, The Playboy of the Western World. In 1922, while still publishing and running the bookstore, Arens was art editor of Vanity Fair, one of the leading style and fashion magazines in the country, and later served as managing editor of Creative Arts magazine. A prolific writer, Arens's interest in modern design led him into products, because by 1927 he was designing and seeing to the manufacture of a line of lamps and other consumer products, which had caught the eye of Calkins & Holden. At an American Management Association (AMA) annual meeting in 1929, an officer of the KitchenAid Manufacturing Company of Dayton, Ohio, E.B. French, reported that Egmont Arens's 1928 redesign of his company's KitchenAid mixer (possibly KitchenAid Model G) had cut its weight in half and improved its appearance. As a result, sales had increased 100 percent. Another attendee reported that while a simple radio costs only \$50, “in an artistic cabinet it brings \$150." A representative of the L.C. Smith typewriter company said that his company introduced color in 1926 after years of black only, and soon only 2 percent of sales were black. Business leaders were beginning to accept the reality of modern design as something they needed to deal with. Earlier in 1929, the AMA had conducted seminars for businessmen on whether “intelligent and profitable use [can] be made of the modernistic style of design." Seminar titles included “How the Retailer Merchandizes Present-Day Fashion, Style, and Art"; "How the Manufacturer Copes with Fashion, Style, and Art”; and "The Renaissance of Art in American Business." At the AMA annual meeting cited in the paragraph above, keynote address speaker E. Grosvenor Plowman, advisor on merchandising problems to the Associated Industries of Massachusetts, stated that the modernistic style was the “offspring” of the Jazz Age, and that “the modern style will remain as long as the machine in its present form is the characteristic of world production, [changing] its outward form from year to year, just as women's dress styles change," and that manufacturers would have to learn to balance faddism and permanence.⁶ Plowman clearly thought that modern design was "faddish" and that "permanence" (the way products always looked) was quite acceptable. The date of this particular AMA annual meeting was coincidentally on October 29, 1929, the exact day of the stock market crash that initiated the Great Depression. Investors had been buying stocks on margin, that is, on credit, for years. Brokerage firms would lend \$9 for every \$1 an investor deposited, which vastly overpriced the stock market. When the market fell, brokers called in these loans, which could not be paid back, so many investors were wiped out. RCA stock, at \$549 per share in 1929, dropped to almost \$0 by 1932. Even before the stock market crashed, many businesses were making more products than they could sell and were overstocked. But now, many Americans drastically reduced their spending on consumer goods and services. Manufacturing and sales were soon down by 50 percent, and unemployment would rise to 25 percent. Many people lost their jobs, and among these was Helen Dryden, the "highest-paid woman artist." She held her art director job at the Dura Company until the stock market crash of 1929, when she was let go and replaced by George W. Walker (1896–1993), who had also just lost his job at Graham Paige. Walker was born in Chicago and attended the Cleveland School of Art and the Otis Art Institute, Los Angeles, in 1916. In 1920 he began his career as an advertising illustrator in a Los Angeles department store. He returned to Cleveland as a freelance illustrator and established his own firm, working for department stores and Premier Press. He entered the automotive business in 1928 by working for the Peerless Auto Company, and then for the Baker-Raulang body company, both in Cleveland. Amos Northup (1889– 1937), then chief designer for the Murray Corporation of America in Detroit, visited Baker-Raulang and urged Walker to come to Detroit, where he interviewed and worked briefly for Harley Earl and John Tiaarda at General Motors, and then for Graham Paige, working with Amos Northup until the company failed in 1929 after the crash. Now he was with Dura, which was also an automotive parts supplier. Northup, an automotive designer born in Bellevue, Ohio, had attended Cleveland Polytechnic Institute, where he studied design, and began his career as a furniture and interior designer. In 1918, he joined Pierce Arrow as the company's truck designer, but soon he was designing interior colors and trim for passenger cars. In 1921 he left Pierce Arrow and opened his own private studio, doing interiors for Pierce Arrow, Leon Rubay, and the 1924 Wills Sainte Claire. In 1927, he was hired by the Murray Corporation, the second-largest independent body producer, and shared in the design of the 1928 Hupmobile. In 1928, he would leave Murray to join Willys-Overland in Toledo, Ohio. Norman Bel Geddes was now concerned with other enterprises. In 1929, Bel Geddes was designing products and buildings for the Toledo Scale Company, a commission enabled by his uncle, Frederick Geddes, a director of the company and a trustee of the Toledo Museum of Art, within which Bel Geddes would design a theater stage. But because of the stock market crash, his product and factory designs for Toledo never saw fruition. One of Bel Geddes's former apprentices, also a successful set designer, ventured into the new profession. In 1929, 25-year-old Henry Dreyfuss (1904–1972) opened his own office at 580 Fifth Avenue, New York, for stage and industrial design activities. He had just won a competition for the design of a “Phone of the Future” sponsored by Bell Laboratories and would begin work on it in 1930, although it would not be produced until 1937. Dreyfuss was born in Brooklyn, New York, to a family in the theatrical materials supply business. He completed studies as an apprentice to Norman Bel Geddes in 1924 and produced 250 stage sets for a number of theaters before 1928, when, as we know, Bel Geddes closed his career in the theater and entered the industrial design field. In Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, that same year, Westinghouse Electric requested an evening course at Carnegie Institute of Technology (CIT) “in the fine art of design as applied to electrical machinery” for engineers, which began in 1929 and was called Design and Appearance. Wilfred Readio, head of CIT's Industrial Arts program, Department of Painting and Decoration, which taught commercial illustration, costume design, textile design, interior decoration, stage design, and printing, headed the course.7 There were three sections in the course, and Donald Dohner, who taught design, and Alexander Kostellow, who taught drawing, taught the other two. They were both on the faculty under Readio. Westinghouse in 1930 hired Donald Roscoe Dohner (1892–1943), one of the CIT instructors, to become director of art in the engineering department at Westinghouse, a position in which he controlled the appearance of all products manufactured in the company's 25 plants. Over the next four years, with a staff of eight, most recently graduated students from CIT, Dohner would design many Westinghouse products from locomotives to ashtrays, including meters, control panels, and switchboards. He worked with toolmakers, die makers, shop foremen, and superintendents. He studied markets, materials, and manufacturing operations and made a point of working in close contact with engineers, not only on appearance and sales appeal, but on cost, ease of manufacture, convenience, and economy of maintenance. Dohner had grown up in Indiana and starting in about 1912 studied at the John Herron Art Institute, Indianapolis; the Academy of Fine Arts in Chicago; and the Chicago Art Institute. He devoted two years to work in advertising, one year in merchandising, and in 1916 he studied the sales influence of product design. In 1918 he went to Pittsburgh to study set design at the Carnegie Institute of Technology (CIT), but while waiting for classes to begin, he took a job as industrial arts instructor in the Pittsburgh public school system. After his graduation from CIT in set design, in about 1925 he began teaching at CIT as an associate professor of design. Now, at age 38, he became one of the first corporate heads of U.S. industrial design departments. Robert Lepper, a 1927 graduate of CIT's industrial arts program in painting, had just returned from Europe and was hired as an associate professor to teach Dohner's night course. Forty-two-year-old famed sculptor Alfonso Iannelli (1898–1965) in 1930 turned to industrial design by beginning to work as a designer for Sunbeam Appliance Company. He designed logos and appliances with modernist shapes, including a Cubist style, chrome-plated faucet for the Mueller Mixing Valve Company. Born in Italy to a poor family, he apprenticed with a decorator before immigrating to America with his family in 1898. He studied at the Art Students League in New York and in 1900 moved to California to work in architecture, architectural decoration, and stained glass. From 1910 to 1915 he designed hundreds of posters and show cards for the Orpheum Theater Chain. In 1914 he came to Chicago to design sculptural ornamental work for Midway Gardens, a huge entertainment center on Lake Michigan, designed by Frank Lloyd Wright, but Wright claimed all of Iannelli's work as his own. Iannelli refused to ever work for Wright again. In 1915, he established Iannelli Studios and began teaching at the Art Institute of Chicago, where in 1922 he was honored with a one-man exhibition of his work, which included modernist lamps and furnishings. In 1930, Nathan George Horwitt (1898–1990) designed the Beta chair, using steel, chrome-plated tubing, for the Howell Manufacturing Company. Horwitt was born in Russia and as a child immigrated to the United States, where he attended the City College of New York and New York University. He later attended the Art Students League of New York and served in the U.S. Army during World War I. He was hired as an advertising copywriter and was eventually promoted to director of advertising. Bel Geddes was actively promoting his work during 1931–32 with similar magazine articles and mounting dozens of exhibitions of his work across the country and abroad, including Toledo, New York, and Vienna. Designers were beginning to work together to promote their work. Members of the American Union of Decorative Artists and Craftsmen (AUDAC), formed in 1927 but ignored by museums and department stores in 1929, quickly reorganized and finally got a chance to display what they could do. They mounted their own exhibition in early 1930 at the Grand Central Galleries in New York to counter museum promotions of European designs. The exhibition featured five modern interiors filled with American products already on the market to demonstrate that human comfort, practicality, and utility alone were appropriate American design criteria, rather than the typical European artistic designs favored by museums. Well over 120 exhibitors were represented, including 27 industrial designers. The October 1932 issue of Popular Mechanics reviewed the exhibition and reported that the exhibition was "proof that art and the machine have formed a partnership” and that “the best manufacturing enterprises ... were seeking counsel from the small group of men who are equipped to design beauty into the products of factory machines." Among the many exhibits of utilitarian objects were Gustav Jensen's Monel metal sink, Walter Dorwin Teague's scale model of his Marmon car design, Jo Sinel's International ticket scale, and Russel Wright's cocktail shaker. Wright also designed an elegant set of sterling silver flatware with simple, geometric shapes that was totally modern and would much later be reproduced by the Museum of Modern Art. Wright's designs were so successful that he began a business, Russel Wright Incorporated, with his wife, Mary, designing and producing spun aluminum accessories for the home. Russel Wright (1904–1976) was born into a historic old American family in Lebanon, Ohio. His mother had direct lineage with two signers of the Declaration of Independence, and his father and grandfather were local judges. Wright trained in art under Frank Duveneck, American figure and portrait painter, at the Art Academy of Cincinnati while still in high school. In 1921 and 1924, he attended the Art Students League in New York and the Columbia University of Architecture. Despite his interest in art, he dutifully followed his family tradition by studying for a legal career at Princeton University. While there, he was a member of the Princeton Triangle Club and won several Tiffany & Company prizes for outstanding World War I memorial sculptures. When he left Princeton, he abandoned law as a career and immediately became a theatrical set designer for Norman Bel Geddes in New York, and later for other film and theater directors. In 1928, however, he had started his own design firm, making theatrical props and small decorative cast metal objects. In 1931, AUDAC would publish its Annual of American Design to promote its members and to record its exhibition of 1930 with 176 pages, 300 photos of products, and essays about design. Frank Lloyd Wright (1857–1959) wrote about the "Principles of Design"; historian Lewis Mumford (1895–1990) wrote about “Culture and Machine Art"; Norman Bel Geddes wrote about “Business Interiors"; Kem Weber described the “Modern Shop"; Paul Frankl wrote about the “Home of Yesterday, Today, and Tomorrow"; and Richard Bach, of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, wrote about the "Resurgence of Quality," among other contributors. Sections of the book included "The Shop," "The Home," “Textiles," "New Materials," "Industrial Design," "Architecture Halls, Walls, and Fittings,"“"Graphic Arts in Advertising," and “Commercial Photography." Bel Geddes praised the successful collaboration between art and industry. The publication, a 9/ × 12/ inch hardcover book, was a masterpiece of Art Deco design and probably the earliest documentation of American industrial design, as it entered the public scene. Soon there would be much more visibility for industrial designers, with the help of museums, business magazines, and national merchandizing organizations. From May 2 through July 1, 1931, the Brooklyn Museum held an exhibition of Modern Decoration and Design by members of the American Union of Decorative Artists and Craftsmen (AUDAC), the organization's first large general museum exhibition, which was billed by the museum as "about the first opportunity the public has had to see a representative indication of the trend and importance of modern design among American craftsmen." Well-known exhibitors included Frank Lloyd Wright, Rockwell Kent, Lee Simonson, Joseph Urban, Paul T. Frankl, Egmont Arens, Donald Deskey, Helen Dryden, Norman Bel Geddes, Nathan George Horwitt, Gustav Jensen, Walter Dorwin Teague, Walter von Nessen, and Russel Wright, among others. Kem Weber, of Hollywood, California, designed a suite of rooms for the investor of moderate means."10 By 1931, several additional major organizations were initiating departments of industrial design. One was Montgomery Ward in Chicago, the mail-order giant, which hired 31-yearold Anne Swainson (1900–1955), a Swedish émigré and fashion coordinator, as head of its new Bureau of Design, which was described as “a service to buyers in evaluating the quality and appearance of products that Montgomery Ward ordered from thousands of outside suppliers." Swainson was educated in applied arts at Columbia University and had been working as a “fashion coordinator" at Chase Copper & Brass. The Bureau concept was her invention." She and her staff redesigned Ward's catalog, replacing old-fashioned woodcuts with photography, and insisting on the authority to approve or reject all projects. She was the first woman executive at Montgomery Ward and staffed her department with arts graduates from the Armour Institute ofTechnology (later to become the Illinois Institute of Technology, IIT) and the Art Institute of Chicago. Among them was George A. Beck (1908–1977), a recent graduate of the Chicago Academy of Fine Arts. Over the years, the Bureau would employ dozens of designers who would later become prominent in the field. With her staff, Swainson assisted in merchandising, product, or packaging design for many of the companies that produced soft or hard goods to be listed in the catalogs, as many often had no internal design capability. Products repackaged or designed by the Bureau of Design were adorned with the “MW” (for Montgomery Ward) logo. Another major manufacturing firm, the American Radiator & Standard Sanitary Corporation, the Kohler of its time, which produced bathroom and kitchen fixtures in porcelain enamel, as well as heating equipment, engaged a 34-year-old designer from one of the art industries, glassware. At age 19, in 1916, he composed the text Machine Design and Descriptive Geometry. George Sakier, ca. 1934 (February 1934 Fortune magazine). During World War I, he worked as a camouflage technician, and after the war he continued to work as an artist, combining technology with classicism. In the 1920s, he worked in art direction for Vogue, Harper's Bazaar, and Modes and Manners. As a contributor to European art periodicals, he stimulated the rescue of a Mayan collection of artifacts from the cellar of the Palais du Trocadéro, a palace built in 1867 for the World's Fair in Paris, proving to France that America had an ancient art. Sakier's fine art paintings were shown in the Julien Levy ultramodern gallery in New York, established in 1931, which also displayed the work of Pablo Picasso, Max Ernst, Marcel Duchamp, and Salvadore Dali. From 1929 until 1979, Sakier worked as primary consultant for the Fostoria Glass Company of Moundsville, West Virginia, and designed the company's Art Deco designs, including black Art Deco vases No. 2404 and No. 2428 in 1930. Other companies were engaging design consultants to ensure that their products were of the latest modern design. The Toledo Scale Company in Toledo, Ohio, in December 1931 engaged a firm to design new public scales in various colors using a new plastic material, Plaskon, to complement different store interiors. The new design firm formed that year was the partnership of Harold Van Doren and John Gordon Rideout, forming the firm Van Doren and Rideout. Toledo Scale was their first client, and their first assignment was for a line of public health “person weigher” scales in colorful, Art Deco, skyscraper styles. Museums were still promoting modern design. The Twelfth Exhibition of Contemporary American Industrial Art was held at the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, October 12 to November 22, 1931. Industrial designers among the exhibitors of furniture included Norman Bel Geddes, Donald Deskey, Paul T. Frankl, Walter von Nessen, Gilbert Rohde, Joseph Sinel, and Walter Dorwin Teague; exhibitors of metalwork and lighting fixtures included Donald Deskey, Henry Dreyfuss, Paul T. Frankl, and Walter von Nessen; and an exhibitor of silverware was Peter Müller-Munk.12 The public was increasingly aware of modern design through popular motion pictures, which almost inevitably were filmed in sets that were completely modern in Art Deco décor, costumes, furnishings, and accessories. While most people suffered economically because of the Depression and lived quite frugally, they seemed to love escaping to theaters to see their favorite stars in modern, luxurious and extravagant settings. With all the bad economic news described in the Depression, it is easy to assume that many did not have enough money to spend on consumer goods. But the most prominent advertiser of the time disputed that. In 1932, Elmo Calkins of the leading advertising agency, Calkins & Holden, wrote an introduction to a book, What Consumer Engineering Really Is (an unfortunate title for what would have been better called industrial design), written by Egmont Arens and Roy Shelton of Calkins' industrial styling division. Analyzing the Depression, Calkins wrote, Many stopped buying while still able financially to continue, and many are still able but restrained by fear and misplaced thrift. The sudden cessation of buying slowed up the entire industrial machine. Retail storekeepers curtailed orders to factories. Factories cut down production, reduced wages, laid off men, still further reducing the number of customers for goods. In a comparatively short time, with all the resources of the country still intact, we had Depression. There were no longer enough buyers for the large quantities of goods we had learned to make and distribute so abundantly.ⁿ3 Not only did Calkins correctly analyze the problem, but he had a theory, “obsoletism," for solving it: Obsoletism is another device for stimulating consumption. The element of style is a consideration in buying many things. Clothes go out of style and are replaced long before they are worn out. That principle extends to other products—motorcars, bathrooms, radios, foods, refrigerators, furniture. People are persuaded to abandon the old and buy the new to be up-to-date, to have the right and correct thing. Does there seem to be a sad waste in the process? Not at all. Wearing things out does not increase prosperity, but buying things does. Thrift in the industrial society in which we now live consists of keeping all the factories busy. Any plan that increases consumption of goods is justifiable if we believe that prosperity is a desirable thing. If we do not, we can turn back the page to earlier and more primitive times when people got along with little and made everything last as long as possible. We have built up a complicated industrial machine and we must go on with it, or throw everything in reverse and go backward.14 Calkins expressed what all industrial designers sensed instinctively but many manufacturers and businessmen of the time did not. Although “planned obsolescence” would acquire a negative connotation 25 years later (Hidden Persuaders, by Vance Packard, 1957), nevertheless it was precisely what was needed to turn around the Depression, and is still a basic source of our material prosperity and economic health. Calkins could have added that by providing a "previously owned” market for goods, many lower economic classes were able to own products at far less cost than new ones, thus sharing in an ever-expanding standard of living. The Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) had been founded in 1929 to promulgate and become an arbiter of modern art. Its first exhibit that year featured the “Big Four" of Postimpressionism: Cézanne, Gauguin, Seurat, and Van Gogh. In February and March, 1932, MoMA held its first architectural exhibition, The International Exhibition of Modern Architecture, and published a book, The International Style: Architecture Since 1922. It was MoMA's intention to establish architecture as a form of modern art to rival painting, and as the intellectual origin of international modern design. Organizers of the exhibition were Alfred H. Barr, Jr. (1902- 1981), the founder and head of the museum; Henry Russell Hitchcock (1903–1987), an architectural historian; and the wealthy connoisseur/collector/architect Philip Johnson (1906- 2005). Hitchcock and Johnson were fascinated with European modernists such as Bauhaus architects Walter Gropius and Mies van der Rohe, as well as architects Le Corbusier and J.J.P. Oud, all of whom Hitchcock had called the “New Pioneers” in his 1929 book, Modern Architecture. Other architects represented included George Howe, Richard Neutra, Raymond Hood, and Rudolph Schindler. This new style featured spare, rectangular, steel and glass structures. The term “International Style" became the formal name of modern architecture and would dominate the architectural scene for the next 30 years. Interestingly, Frank Lloyd Wright (1867–1959), America's greatest architect, was invited to participate but declined because he had misgivings about this new style, which he detested because it was counter to his own “organic" style of architecture. In fact, Wright had inspired many of the lauded European architects of the so-called International Style, including Gropius, Theo van Doesburg, Le Cobusier, and Richard Neutra, with his Wasmuth Portfolio published in Europe in 1910. Now, in 1932, Wright was regarded by many U.S. architects as an old-school, irascible, has-been. Philip Johnson had in fact disparagingly referred to Wright as “America's greatest 19th-century architect."15 The architect that Wright had inspired in Germany, Walter Gropius, was now, however, having difficult problems with the increasing power of Adolf Hitler.
