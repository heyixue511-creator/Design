## Animated inserts for documentary films

Otto Neurath was pleased with the result of A few ounces a day, but problems arose during the production of further animated sequences that were to be cut together with live-action sequences in subsequent Rotha films. At first glance these would seem to have been simpler to animate than A few ounces a day, but they provided continual difficulties due to the division of labour between conception, design, and execution. For A few ounces a day, Neurath remarked, 'we composed the whole story and our layouts were exactly executed by the animating firm'.³º After completion of the next film, Blood transfusion (1942), Neurath complained to Rotha that 'some points in the blood transfusion animation [were] made by the animator and not by us, which directly destroy our Isotype method'. He continued:

Please believe me, we make these remarks not as pedants, but knowing the audience. You see, subconsciously they feel the difference. ... since we have to bear the responsibility that Isotype diagrams are really made by us, please show us, without exception the diagrams before they go to distribution, we shall come immediately to London. You see we are very interested on our prestige, and of course, too that a Rotha production with Isotypes should be the best diagram animation that we can make.³¹

Yet the difficulties continued and Neurath was forced to reiterate: 'Let me repeat our suggestion that Science Film[s] should change not even a bit of our designs without our consent.'³²Marie Neurath recalled later: For this film work we provided charts for the key positions and gave verbal instructions for how the transitions were to be made.'³3 They had no effective means of proofing the animation themselves and were sometimes unpleasantly surprised by the results. ## World of plenty and Land of promise

The two most significant films directed by Paul Rotha and featuring Isotype animation were World of plenty (1943) and Land of promise (1945), the first two parts of a loose trilogy about planning for wartime and its aftermath (the third film was The world is rich, 1947). In these films Rotha originated a distinctive, dialectical style based on a tight combination of stock footage, interviews, animated diagrams and multi-voice commentary.⁵⁶

World of plenty – known as the strategy of food' film until a late stage of production⁵7– was mainly inspired by the views of Scottish nutritionist John Boyd Orr, who appeared in the film directly addressing the audience in extreme close-up. Yet there was a group of like-minded collaborators involved in writing and making the film, including Eric Knight and Otto Neurath.58 In his book Fighting for what? The Times Educational Supplement added that 'often, however, the time allowed to absorb both the figures and their significance is too short, and much of the argument is left to the sound-track alone'.7º

One project which would have contained even more thematic influence from the Neuraths than Land of promise was a film called 'I am a scientist', which did not progress beyond the script stage. The idea for it may have arisen from the First Scientific Films Conference, organized by the Association of Scientific Workers in London, August 1942: Rotha's production Blood transfusion was shown there and Otto Neurath attended the event.71 Rotha's intentions to incorporate ideas on science and planning into documentary film coincided with the preoccupations of Neurath, whose work – in all its aspects – was concerned with furthering a 'scientific attitude'.7²

The Neuraths submitted their ideas for 'I am a scientist' to Rotha in the form of a typed dialogue between themselves about how they would like to show science on film. The initial treatment was inspired by Neurath's recollections of his boyhood interest chemistry, which he also included in his 'visual autobiography.'7³ The film was to have been sponsored by icı but it was presumably not developed due to more urgent projects for the war effort commissioned by the Mor.

## Unifilm – a joint initiative

By late 1942 Neurath had already proposed forming an Isotype unit within Rotha's film company:

An arrangement like this would only be achieved (briefly) after Neurath's death with a joint company named Unifilm.

In his 'visual autobiography', From hieroglyphics to Isotype, Neurath pondered the relative merits of moving and still images. In his view, several still images presented simultaneously had the advantage of comparability, whereas filmed images disappear to be replaced by others, without the audience being able to retrieve them. Home video technology now enables the viewer to control the sequence of moving images, but Neurath's objection could have been overcome to some extent in his time by the use of split-screen technique – showing two sequences side by side with a dividing line in the image. Rotha seems to have implied to Neurath that splitting the screen was troublesome; for live action it would have entailed expensive optical processing but in animation it could have be done on the drawing board, with no extra cost. Yet it was not a technique ever exploited in Isotype animation.87

The suitability of Isotype style for combination with live action could perhaps be questioned. Neurath's persistent struggle to protect the rigour of Isotype from the pitfalls presented by motion reflect an uneasy marriage. The simplicity and clear definition of Isotype charts and diagrams combine well with type in print, but the transition from cinematography to animated statistics in some of Rotha's films can be jarring, despite the sophisticated soundtrack accompaniment. (In addition, on film Isotype was typically deprived of a key component of its armoury – colour.) Neurath insisted: We stress the point, that the “realistic” and the “symbolic" should be separated, scrupulously.'\*s This may make sense intellectually but it creates a conscious disharmony that counteracts the familiar viscerality of many movies. Rotha may well have valued this effect as an antidote to Hollywood superficiality.
