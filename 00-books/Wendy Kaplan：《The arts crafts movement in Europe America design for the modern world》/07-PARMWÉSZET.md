In 1897 and 1898 Radisics had purchased tapestries by the Norwegian Frida Hansen (see p. 182), and the German Otto Eckmann, thus providing a model for the development of home-industry weaving.3 Although Hungary had no tapestry tradition, weaver and textile artist Sarolta Kovalszky was encouraged to establish the Scandinavian Scherrebek weaving technique (which Eckmann in particular had popularized) at Nemetelemér in Torontál County, a provincial town on the southern edge of Hungary. The strategy was to take advantage of the region's traditional methods of producing kilims and update them. Between 1898 and 1903 Kovalszky organized the weaving of tapestry designs by artists such as Pál Horti, Sándor Nagy, Aladár Kriesch, and János Vaszary, which were showcased at the expositions in Paris (190O) and Turin (1902). Despite critical acclaim, however, it was clear that there was no market for such work in Torontál, and in 1904 Lippich closed the workshops, transferring Kovalszky, her students, and their looms to Gödöllő, just outside Budapest, where Kriesch and Nagy were establishing a small art colony. Here Kovalszky continued her association with these artists. Lippich, Radisics, and other members of the Decorative Arts Association were also actively involved in the selection and design of Hungarian exhibitions abroad. Since the international exposition in Vienna (1873), the richness and diversity of Hungarian folk arts had attracted an international audience, though initially serving to reinforce rather than challenge the perception of "magyars" as a subject people within the Hapsburg Empire.32 At the Paris Exposition Universelle in 19o0, however, visitors could see how folk sources had been combined with sophisticated assimilation of progressive currents worldwide. At the same time Horti was on an economic mission for the Hungarian government: he was to report on American decorative art manufacturing, especially in the furniture industry. The following year he was commissioned to design furniture and graphics for two American companies, Limbert Co. of Grand Rapids and Holland, Michigan, and the Shop of the Crafters in Cincinnati, Ohio. Horti was attracted to these manufacturers as entrepreneurs purporting to operate businesses along Morrisian lines, particularly in the sense of promoting quality craftsmanship and improved working conditions for artisans, but combined with mechanized production methods and a modern approach to marketing and distribution. His desk and chair (5.19) were produced at the height of his professional career and international reputation. The more traditional home industries attracted considerable popular interest, and the products, particularly the embroideries, sold in huge quantities to an international audience. By contrast, the artist-designers garnered more critical attention and were given greater official support. This pattern was established by about 1900, and it has continued to shape the analysis of the movement in general. In Hungary, as elsewhere, there has been a tendency to discuss the Arts and Crafts movement in terms of a relatively small number of individual artists, architect-designers, and intellectuals who were given special attention in art magazines of the period. This type of craft activity, iparművészet, has been emphasized at the expense of the more diffuse, traditional, and nonprofessional dimensions of the movement represented by the háziipar. Yet the idea of creating a modern national style, rooted in folk culture, that would embody moral and community values was nurtured by the home industries. In the context of domestic and international exhibitions, both types of craft were presented as part of a continuum. At the same time, in the context of domestic policy, the Arts and Crafts became associated with ambitious programs of social reform and urban development, generating a more sustained and stylistically coherent engagement with the international Arts and Crafts. This final phase of the movement resonated most strongly with developments in Finland (a country linguistically close to Hungary), and with the work of English architect-designers C. R. Ashbee and M. H. Baillie Scott. Akseli Gallen-Kallela and Eliel Saarinen were protagonists of the Finnish movement who employed design as an agent of national identity. These Finnish leaders and their British counterparts, Ashbee and Baillie Scott, were all in direct contact with leading figures of the Hungarian Arts and Crafts during this period.36Two centers of activity dominated—one at Gödölló, and the other around architect Károly Kós, the leading figure in a group known as the Fiatalok (Young Ones).37 The link between the two groups was their involvement in the recording and publication of folk arts, activities supported through Lippich. Many of the individuals were involved in the preparation of the monumental five-volume study A Magyar Nép Múvészete (The Art of the Hungarian People), published between 1907 and 1922. This experience brought a new understanding of the folk arts and how they might be used in contemporary architecture and design. With his design for the National Museum of Decorative Arts, Ödön Lechner had performed a major function by focusing attention on the issue of a "national language of form," but among his followers and admirers many were increasingly critical of the older architect's selective appropriation of vernacular motifs from published sources rather than from firsthand observation. The aim of this new generation was to transform the artistic power and spiritual intensity found in humble folk arts into "all walks of modern arts and life."38 In their ethnographic studies, scientific rigor was infused with a mystical sensibility that aestheticized the folk arts, projecting them into mythological time and space. The sense of entering a world of living legend was graphically communicated in the illustrated publications of Wigand and Kós that depicted figures of ![](images/f9feb755d82198546d47482eaa0c1e94a5c60f9e3a24388ae32715b6a365aa54.jpg) Hungary's distant past in humble peasant interiors; as, for example, in Wigand's drawing of the infancy of Attila's son Csaba (5.20). In Hungary in the late nineteenth century, there was a huge popular investment in the concept that the Hungarians were descended from hunters and horsemen from the East. This idea was fostered by ubiquitous imagery derived from mythic cycles that identified Attila and his family as the originators of the Hungarian people.39 Nostalgia for the unity and integrity of rural life inspired the establishment of the arts and crafts colony at Gödöllő. Aladár Körösfői-Kriesch had settled there in 1901, and he was joined over time by others in search of a fulfilling, philosophically rich lifestyle. These like-minded artists and their families included Leo Belmonte, who came to run the weaving workshops in 1905, Kriesch's sister Laura, and her husband, Sándor Nagy. Others like Ede Wigand, István Medgyaszay, and Mariska Undi spent periods of time at Gödölló without settling there permanently. In the spirit of Ruskin and Morris, and the heroic vision of peasant life celebrated by Leo Tolstoy, the group embraced social idealism as well as more conventional artistic aims.4º This was apparent in enlightened communal aspirations, and in the varied social, religious, and ethnic profile of the community. The Gödöllő artists tried their hands at a variety of media, producing stained glass (5.21), frescoes, ceramics, furniture, bookbinding, wallpaper design, embroidery, and most importantly, carpets and tapestries. Through immersion in such processes, and through exploration of Hungarian legends and baliads, they hoped to recapture the inner spirit of Hungary's medieval culture and folk arts. The quality of their oeuvre was inconsistent but, in common with the work of many Arts and Crafts groups, it was produced in a spirit of millenarian optimism that transcended mere aesthetics. The search for totality of expression and a belief in the socially transformative, spiritual power of art were rooted in the concepts of collaboration and the Gesamtkunstwerk, the total work of art. In particular, the artists' houses associated with the colony provided a model for the integration of home and work and, more generally, for the reform of domestic design and lifestyle. István Medgyaszay, the only Hungarian pupil of Otto Wagner, gravitated toward ![](images/8f6d7b7b81e15fcfc84c45a2950d3b642260d9a4fabe8a4f571b92961991a6df.jpg) Gödöllő, where between 1904 and 1906 he designed modest houses for Leo Belmonte and for Sándor and Laura Nagy in a modern idiom informed by his study of peasant dwellings (5.22). These houses were brought to public attention at the international exhibition in Milan in 1906 through exhibits that re-created "the home of an artist."Medgyaszay's architectural framework was elaborated with furniture by Nagy and Wigand, embroideries by Laura Kriesch, and a variety of other domestic objects designed by members of the colony. Likewise the artist-designers Mariska Undi and Ödön Moiret, inspired by Wigand, shaped their immediate surroundings with plank-style furniture and the symbolic use of color and ornament derived from nature and the traditional crafts (5.23 and 5.24).41 Through their influential connections—above all, their friend Lippich—the group was heavily promoted at international exhibitions and in the art press, and they were given opportunities to work on large public projects, such as the interior decoration of the Academy of Music in 1907. The community's wider educational role as a training center within the home-industries network was confirmed in 1907, when Lippich established Gödöllő as the weaving branch of the National School of Applied Arts. In economic terms, such support and government subsidies shielded the colony from the harsh realities of the marketplace. Only in this sheltered environment was the community able to pursue its quasi-mystical ideal of a life lived in harmony with nature and rooted in the traditions of the rural peasantry. Despite the radical tenor of their pronouncements on art and the unconventional aspects of their lifestyle (vegetarianism, reformed dress, sandal-wearing, nude bathing, and sleeping outdoors), the Gödöllő artists were never genuine outsiders to middle-class urban society. At the end of a suburban train line, and with a Baroque royal palace, the town of Gödöllő provided a picturesque destination for bourgeois daytrippers from Budapest."2 Ultimately, despite their left-wing sympathies, the artists did little to address rural poverty in the remoter regions of Hungary or the working conditions of the urban proletariat. While Wigand worked with Kriesch and Nagy, and was associated with their endeavors at Gödöllő, he never became part of the community. In 1907 he turned his back on Budapest to settle in Marosvásárhely (now Târgu Mureş, Romania), where he immersed himself in the study of local vernacular buildings. He was not formally trained as an architect and therefore could not execute buildings in Budapest; however, no such restrictions hampered him in Transylvania, where he designed an innovative range of multifunctional community buildings and modest houses.43Critics stressed the functional, logically constructed design of his buildings and his sensitivity to topography, which was described as comparable to that of designers like Baillie Scott."4 The apex of his work in Transylvania, and a project on which he collaborated with Róth, Nagy, and Kriesch, was the interior decoration of the Palace of Culture at Marosvásárhely (1912). Emil Tőry and Móric Pogány, Zsolnay Factory In Budapest the architectural scene was enlivened by the precocious group of architectdesigners known as the Fiatalok (Young Ones), who emerged from the Technical University in 1907 and 1908. At their center was Károly Kós, a native of Transylvania, who worked briefly in Maróti's studio and collaborated with Wigand in Marosvásárhely. The group took up many of the themes addressed at Gödöllő, not least the shared belief in the ideal of a modern national style based on the village architecture of Transylvania. The town of Körösfő (after which Kriesch had named himself Körösfői) assumed iconic significance for the Fiatalok. As the critic Elemér Czakó observed in 1908, the distinctive form of the Körösfő church was "engraved upon their spirits," as Mount Fujiyama was for Japanese artists." That year Lajos Kozma featured the church in his cover design for A Ház (The House), the new journal that served as the principal mouthpiece for the group's theories and designs (5.30). The magazine's coherent graphic identity, which indicated a clear debt to Wigand, emphasized the Fiatalok's adherence to ideals in book design that looked back to the Private Press movement initiated by William Morris's Kelmscott Press. In fact, Kós was experimenting with just this sort of activity alongside his architectural work, printing a series of hand-illuminated texts based on a combination of his ethnographic studies and Transylvanian folk tales. Kós eventually set up his own printing press and guild at Crow Castle in Transylvania, which consciously emulated British models. His study Erdély Kövei (The Stones of Transylvania) was a tribute to Ruskin's Stones of Venice. Fellowship, respect for the environment, simple living, and the integration of art into all aspects of life—these traditional values were being tested on a small scale at the Gödöllő colony, but they were now harnessed to a more substantial program of social and municipal projects, which was facilitated by the end of a depression in the building trades and a change in the political climate. One of the key figures in this story was István Bárczy, the mayor of Budapest, who aimed to confirm the capital as a world city run by an enlightened municipal authority, a centerpiece for the nation-state.48 In planning housing projects, schools, facilities for leisure or for the poor, the authorities looked to foreign models, but they adapted these models to suit Hungarian needs and commissioned some of the most talented artists and architects available. ![](images/5e4e4eae41ab71a3e8ad2a8f6ec604da31f6117fccbd2a3cce133d9ddb06be0f.jpg) ![](images/09959eb7fd159a8f5437dda4f1284555af7cbc515b261e3efc631659bb5633b1.jpg) In 1907, for example, the city launched a comprehensive redevelopment of the zoo. The commission for the seventeen pavilions went to Kós and Dezsö Zrumeczky, only two years after the completion of their studies. In the heart of stony Budapest, the two young architects set about creating a remarkable "village" for the animals, employing roughly hewn and carved timber above heavy rubble foundations, all based on vernacular prototypes (5.31). Further demonstration that this Transylvanian vernacular style was applicable to one of the most modern cities in Europe came the following year, when Kós won the commission to design housing for a new garden suburb for low-income families on the southeastern edge of Pest. The Wekerle Estate, developed between 1909 and 1926, would be the largest housing project built in the city during the first half of the twentieth century. In mixing city and country, the planners looked primarily to the British Garden City movement, but Kós based his actual designs for the main square on the German fortified towns of Transylvania, forming each of the main access points into covered walkways reminiscent of medieval gateways. Bárczy's most ambitious and lasting project was a huge school-building program, part of his campaign to improve standards of literacy and general education. A major issue in Hungarian politics since the Ausgleich, a national education system was seen as an important element in "nation building." Kozma, like other members of the Fiatalok and his mentor Lajta, had immersed himself in folk arts, but he increasingly blended his interest in vernacular sources with references to eighteenth-century traditions of English and Hungarian cabinetmaking (5.36). The distinctive "Folk Baroque" that resulted was well suited to the sophisticated and wealthy clientele he now began to cultivate.50 Compared to such high-end craft practices, relatively few attempts were made to engage with the industrial methods and character of the urban proletariat. Representations of work as a redemptive noble activity were invariably images of the solo woodworker, clad in an amalgam of peasant and medieval dress. In mural designs for a workers' club in Budapest, however, Maróti depicted groups of factory workers in modern clothing, universalized and dignified by allusions to Hungarian, classical, and Germanic myth (5.37). Largely funded by the family of Manfréd Weisz, a prominent industrial manufacturer, the project had been started in 1916, developed under the Bolshevik Republic of Councils in 1919 (after the war), and finally opened in 1920. For many involved in the shaping of a Hungarian national consciousness through the arts and crafts, identity was not so much a matter of ethnicity or religion as a sense of being bound together by the abstract ideal of an enlightened, multiethnic state. The same style could be as applicable to a workers' club as to an art lover's house, to a Calvinist prayer school or Roman Catholic church as to a Jewish synagogue or tomb. The intellectual process of conceptualizing nationhood was nowhere clearer than in the way designers changed their names, deliberately identifying with an associative geography of the imagination. Around 1905 Béla Lajta, for example, had changed his surname from Leitesdorfer to reference the river that formed part of the border between Austria and Hungary. Similarly, Géza Rintel added Maróti to his surname, associating himself with Aranyos-Marót in northern Hungary (now Slovakia). Lajta and Maróti, like Kozma, Tőry, Pogány, and many other design professionals involved with the Arts and Crafts in Hungary were all of Jewish extraction, exemplifying a significant facet of Budapest's intellectual and cultural life in the early twentieth century. For these individuals there was no conflict between their ethnic or religious identity and their intense patriotism. By contrast, the craft activities of those who did not identify with the overarching ideal of Magyarorszag were officially relegated to the background.5 Many Slovaks in Upper Hungary, for example, aligned themselves with the Pan-Slavic movement rather than with Greater Hungary. Although reported outside Hungary, manifestations of the Slavic Arts and Crafts revival within Hungary, headed by Dušan Jurkovič, were conspicuous by their absence from Budapest-centered publications, exhibitions, and collections.5²The simmering ethnic tensions that lay behind such omissions and exclusions were to erupt in the context of World War I. 1920 TREATY OF TRIANON—THE PARTING OF THE WAYS The outbreak of war in 1914 brought this period of heady activity in the Arts and Crafts to an abrupt halt, and in the process pitted Hungary against Britain and France, countries that had provided so much intellectual stimulus to the movement. The political momentum that had given the Hungarian Arts and Crafts such a clear trajectory was now fractured into different directions. In the aftermath of the conflict—the short-lived Bolshevik Republic of Councils in 1919 and the dismemberment of Austro-Hungary in the 1920 Treaty of Trianon—the territorial shape and cultural identity of Hungary were fun- ![](images/200c58ed7b4b7c48de8d45320ffa9bc71f976419cb13685448072e6000d5c419.jpg) damentally altered. The moody coloring and symbolism of Kriesch's last great tapestry design—Eagles over the Tomb of the Hero, which incorporated a Transylvanian church and wooden grave markers redolent of nationhood and ancient spirituality poignantly commented on both the human loss and the cultural schism that had taken place (5.39).53 For Kriesch, as for so many others, the folk arts of Transylvania had been central to the development of a national Hungarian style, yet under the terms of Trianon, this whole region was ceded to Romania. This and other losses (such as the ceding of the northwest regions to what became Czechoslovakia) triggered identity crises at the levels of the individual and the state. Several of the key figures involved in the Arts and Crafts died or sank ![](images/70dc49f786337803dbcddcf10a123148a58def2a61c0122f042f0b25c9a5b8b0.jpg) into obscurity. Others became economic or political migrants; Maróti, for example, spent five years in the United States, where for a while he found refuge at the Cranbrook Academy of Art outside Detroit with his Finnish friend Eliel Saarinen. Trianon highlighted the provisional nature of national boundaries and identities, but the concept of Greater Hungary remained an emotionally powerful idea that was difficult to dismantle. Arguments about culture and "national spirit" featured prominently in protests by the Hungarian government against the injustices of Trianon, and the Arts and Crafts ethos, which had become strongly entwined with Hungarian patriotism, continued to surface in propagandist images like that of the sower on the cover of the political text There Is No Czech Culture in Upper Hungary (1921), but now in support of a more insular and divisive nationalism (5.38). In the increasingly anti-Semitic climate of the years leading up to World War Il, the style that so many Jewish architects and designers had helped to forge in the early years of the twentieth century—in particular, its oriental streak—now became negatively associated with their Jewishness. A more positive legacy of Hungary's engagement with the Arts and Crafts is evident in the untarnished idealism and breadth of vision in projects such as Maróti's text and model reconstructing the lost city of Atlantis in 1937.s⁴ Similarly, in the relative obscurity of his native Transylvania, where he worked until his death in 1977, Kós continued to develop the forms that had proved so exciting before the war, unswerving in his commitment to the ideology of the British Arts and Crafts movement (5.40). In 1923 he had argued for the need to reassert the values of guild craftsmanship: 'Someone may ask: why are you telling us all this? Don't you realize it is 1923, we are in Transylvania, and we have neither a Ruskin, nor a Morris, nor an Ashbee, and our people are not English? Nor do we have anonymous patrons who are able to dedicate large sums of money to communal purposes."55Kós knew these things all too well, but for him the shining ideal of an all-embracing, socially committed, and spiritually driven art, an ideal that Walter Crane's visit to Hungary had highlighted in 1900, was still relevant and urgent.
