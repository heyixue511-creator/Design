William De Morgan, UK ## FOREWORD The Arts and Crafts Movement in Europe and America, 1880-1920: Design for the Modern World is a groundbreaking exhibition, the first to assess the influence of the Arts and Crafts movement internationally. At the turn of the last century, the Arts and Crafts movement transformed not only how objects looked but also how people looked at objects. It provided a framework for many essential issues still being debated today-the conflict between standardization and individuality, the question of whether a unique handcrafted object is superior to a mass-produced one, the problem of defining what kind of design most benefits society. The movement's ideals were appropriated by the avant-garde throughout Europe and the United States, who adapted them to suit their own national identities and economic needs amid rapid industrialization. The movement was a catalyst for the world that became modern, and LACMA is proud to be the first museum to interpret it in this context. At the heart of LACMA's project are the visionary collecting and unstinting generosity of Max Palevsky, whose gifts form the core of the museum's renowned collection of Arts and Crafts design. The Arts and Crafts Movement in Europe and America was made possible by his contribution of one of the largest donations to an exhibition by a private individual in LACMA's history, by his gifts of objects or the funds to purchase them, and by loans from his personal collection. The museum admires Mr. Palevsky's commitment to this remarkable movement in art, and we are tremendously grateful for the support that allowed us to make the first truly comprehensive presentation about the Arts and Crafts movement to the public. LACMA is also deeply grateful for the additional major support provided by the National Endowment for the Arts and the enthusiastic participation of some seventy-five institutional and individual lenders. The Arts and Crafts Movement in Europe and America was organized by Wendy Kaplan, department head and curator of decorative arts at LACMA. She is a foremost authority on the Arts and Crafts movement, and the exhibition is the culmination of two decades of research on the subject. This catalogue fully documents the exhibition, and with contributions from scholars representing many different countries, it contains the most current research on the Arts and Crafts movement. Charles Rennie Mackintosh and Margaret Macdonald Mackintosh, UK ## SPONSOR'S STATEMENT I have been collecting decorative arts from the Arts and Crafts movement since the early 1970s. Their simplicity and directness reminded me of the mid-century modern objects that had caught my attention in the 194Os. What began as an attraction to Arts and Crafts aesthetics soon became an admiration for its ideology. I was drawn to the movement's ambivalence toward the modern world of mechanization. While this may seem ironic coming from the founder of one of the earliest computer companies, Scientific Data Systems (established 1962), there are definite parallels between Arts and Crafts attitudes about industrialization and my own feelings about computers. With few exceptions, leaders of the movement did not reject the technology that would make life easier. William Morris declared that he was not against machinery per se, but "the great intangible machine of comrnercial tyranny, which oppresses the lives of all of us." Computers were originally intended to expedite work and solve serious problems, from space travel to record keeping. Unfortunately, they have also become passive entertainment devices—substitutes for interactions with the real world. Just as the Arts and Crafts movement took issue with the alienation of people from "pleasure in labour" and the resulting loss of human creativity, I, too, oppose the depersonalization that comes from the hypnotic quality of computer games, the substitution of a Google search for genuine inquiry, the instant messaging that has replaced social discourse. With time I've come to see the Arts and Crafts movement as considerably more complex than it had first appeared to me in the 1970s and 1980s. Since Wendy Kaplan was appointed head of the decorative arts department at LACMA four years ago, I've had the opportunity to broaden my perceptions to include other important influences on the movement, such as the Gothic Revival, vernacular traditions, Asian art, and the interactions between designers throughout Europe and the United States. Given Wendy's expertise and passion, it now seemed appropriate to construct an exhibition that analyzed the movement comprehensively, from an international perspective. I have come to realize that a culture cannot recognize the paradoxes it creates. The Arts and Crafts movement was both a return to craft traditions and an attempt to produce a new art. This exhibition explores a broad range of strategies that were developed to deal with the consequences of modernity; it aims to increase our understanding of the concerns that underlie the Arts and Crafts movement, concerns that are present in our society to this day. MAX PALEVSKY ## DESIGN FOR THE MODERN WORLD ![](images/e4d7b5c4e34e0078856b5dfc3ae4d901d1d7e43cd4ab0179aa312a3bdc27cb9b.jpg) he Arts and Crafts movement was a response to a century of unprecedented social and economic upheaval. Its name was coined in 1887, when a group of designers met in London to found an organization—the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society—where applied art would be valued as equal to fine art. Their first display, in 1888, was considered a major achievement, marking "the beginning of the end of the easel-painting despotism,"as George Bernard Shaw noted.' Today, however, the society can be seen as standing for something much larger—the establishment of a movement to provide alternative methods of production and consumption. Arts and Crafts leaders tried to ameliorate conditions at the end of a century characterized by a dramatically accelerated rate of change. It was a world of millennial fears and utopian dreams. As the most industrialized country in the Western world, Britain was also the first to attempt to counter the malevolent aspects of mass production—the cost in human creativity and quality of life. Many believed these ills could be remedied by a social and redemptive role for art. John Ruskin, William Morris, and their followers championed the moral and spiritual uplift that would come with the revival of making objects by hand. The improvement of working conditions, the integration of art into everyday life, the unity of all arts, and an aesthetic resulting from the use of indigenous materials and native traditions were also central to the movement's philosophy. The Arts and Crafts movement offered a variety of responses to the challenges of modernity, and by 1900 it had spread throughout Europe and North America. "Joy in labor," "the simple life," "truth to materials," "unity in design," "honesty in construction" "democratic design, "fidelity to place"—these ideas became part of the language of the movement, although, depending on the country, some resonated more than others. This book and the exhibition it accompanies examine this language of reform in all its variations and expressive possibilities. Neither focuses on demonstrating a particular aesthetic since Arts and Crafts was not so much a style as an approach to the making of objects. British precedent, however, did provide a visual as well as a verbal vocabulary to address the common issues of industrialization, urbanization, competition in an increasingly imperial and global economy, and the establishment (or reinforcement) of national identity. For more-mechanized "center"countries, the first two issues were more important; for less-industrialized "peripheral" countries that were trying to win their independence from "center" countries, the last two were paramount. The Arts and Crafts movement was an international one: it touched countries as far apart as Russia and Scotland, the United States and Australia. Here the discussion focuses on the thirteen most representative countries. By analyzing Arts and Crafts objects made in England, Scotland, Ireland, Germany, Austria, Hungary, Denmark, Norway, Sweden, Finland, Belgium, France, and the United States, the authors explore how the movement's ideals were disseminated and then transformed in response to specific economic, cultural, and political conditions. Three themes provide the leitmotifs of the book and exhibition: Art and Industry, Design and National Identity, and Art and Life. ART AND INOUSTRY The Arts and Crafts movement, in large part, was neither anti-industrial nor antimodern. While its adherents idealized the preindustrial past, they did not reject the present. Even in the 184Os, the Gothic Revival architect A. W. N. Pugin stated, "We do not want to arrest the course of inventions, but to confine these inventions to their legitimate uses." Later, this viewpoint would be echoed by Arts and Crafts leaders in Britain (with the notable exception of John Ruskin). They believed that machines were necessary but should be used only to relieve the tedium of mindless, repetitive tasks. As Alan Crawford discusses in his essay, a new alliance between the crafts and industry was an important aspect of the movement in Britain. William Morris ran a factory and while a ![](images/adf20f130ea40483f4bd2b64d3338349a432e6120ec4284c9d48a8df89bede14.jpg) textile such as Peacock and Dragon was handwoven and expensive, it was an oft-repeated pattern and one particularly popular in foreign markets (1.1).3 For decades, the moderately priced, rushseated Sussex chairs were the company's most successful line of furniture (1.2). Many other prominent Arts and Crafts designers, such as M. H. Baillie Scott, worked for companies that, although high-end and considered progressive, mass-manufactured their goods. At the same time that C. F. A. Voysey was receiving commissions to build and furnish unique houses (1.3), he continued to design wallpapers and fabrics for large companies. But Britain, at the very epicenter of the Industrial Revolution, was also the center for designers most opposed to the dehumanizing consequences of factory production. While Arts and Crafts leaders did not oppose the use of machinery, they were convinced that what mattered most was the process of making. Without joy in labor, production would have neither merit nor value. At the same time, they felt that objects should be affordable and useful. The conflict between these two beliefs, and the attempts to reconcile them, was the focus of design debates during the first two decades of the twentieth century. In Rüdiger Joppien's essay, Germany provides a case study of how ideas about accessible design were wedded to economic necessity, as the country successfully competed with Britain and France. Maschinenmöbel (machine-made furniture) and other designs by Richard Riemerschmid, and the work of Peter Behrens for the company AEG, show how Arts and Crafts principles of simplicity, integrity, and fitness for purpose were adapted to an emerging goal, Typisierung (standardization)—the concept that design should be objective, rational, and suited to endless repetition. Christian Witt-Dörring's examination of Vienna demonstrates a different aspect of the selective adaptation of British Arts and Crafts principles. In the Work Program of the Wiener Werkstätte published in 1905, cofounder Josef Hoffmann declared that "the joyful hum of arts and crafts [would] be welcome to anyone who professes faith in Ruskin and Morris," but he was not nearly as committed to social reform aspects of the movement.' While Hoffmann had successfully collaborated with manufacturers, and the company had often designed for multiple production, the Werkstätte is best known for its exclusive luxury goods. Hoffmann rationalized that "it is absolutely no longer possible to convert the masses.... It is all the more our duty to make happy those few who turn to us." Since Arts and Crafts proponents believed so deeply in the spiritual benefits of work done by hand, crafts practiced as a hobby were particularly important. This posed a problem for many professionals: glorifying a work simply because it was handmade, they contended, led to judging an object by intention rather than by quality, and thus to a decline in standards. Professionals also resented amateurs for the economic threat they posed: when the latter sold their work, the prices need not reflect the hours of labor that went into production. Ironically, many craftspeople marketed their skills to hobbyists, as was often the case with china painting. Susan Frackelton of Milwaukee, for example, was acclaimed in the United States as well as abroad for innovative experimentation with stoneware (1.4), but she made her living by developing odorless china colors for amateur china painters and by publishing the popular manual Tried by Fire.6 The Arts and Crafts movement is replete with accounts of people abandoning deskbound professions to find personal fulfillment in the crafts. T. J. Cobden-Sanderson had been a dissatisfied lawyer before he took up bookbinding and created a renowned private press (1.6). Robert Riddle Jarvie was a clerk in the Chicago city government who transformed a home hobby into a successful career as a metalsmith (1.5). Karl Kipp, head of the copper shop at the Roycroft community, had first been a banker.
