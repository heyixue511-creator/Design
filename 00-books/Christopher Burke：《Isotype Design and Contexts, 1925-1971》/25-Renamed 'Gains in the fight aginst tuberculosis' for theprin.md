This new year card for 194o proved uncannily prophetic. Of the emissary Isotype boats sent out from The Hague, one shows an elephant: Otto Neurath's personal signature was a cartoon elephant. When German troops reached The Hague in May 1940, Otto Neurath and Marie Reidemeister fled at the last moment, without any possessions, and were luckily able to board a motorized lifeboat, which set off in the direction of Britain. It was picked up by a British destroyer, and they were conveyed to England. They were sent to (separate) prison camps for 'enemy aliens' on the Isle of Man, until it could be established that they posed no threat.

8 The Isotype Institute, Oxford War, welfare, and post-war reconstruction 1941-8

After being released from internment by the British government in February 1941 – and then immediately getting married – Otto and Marie Neurath settled in Oxford, where a small amount of teaching had been arranged for Otto Neurath at the university. They began to work in visual education soon after their release due to the remarkable interest shown in employing their expertise for the British war effort and for explaining social policy. In June 1942 the Isotype Institute was officially established.

Apart from the collaboration with Paul Rotha in making documentary films (see chapter 9), a fruitful partnership developed from early in 1942 with the company Adprint. Established in 1937 by another Viennese émigré, Wolfgang Foges, Adprint was not a publisher but a book packager. The first projects coordinated by Adprint to involve Isotype were two booklets that visualized information about the budding welfare state: Social security and Social insurance. In this way, Isotype reconnected with the issues of social welfare and planning that had been integral to its development in Vienna. Otto and Marie Neurath were entrusted with secret documents in order to carry out this work, demonstrating the confidence shown in them by the British authorities.

Towards the end of the war, as a contribution to promoting mutual understanding between Britain and its principal allies, Adprint produced two series of books – 'America and Britain' and ‘The Soviets and ourselves' – which featured a mixture of text, Isotype charts, and photographs. After the war, the Isotype Institute contributed charts to the 'New Democracy' series of books examining issues of post-war social reconstruction.

In April 1948 the Isotype Institute moved to the offices of Adprint in London. There Marie Neurath continued work on a variety of projects (see chapters 10 and 11).

## America and Britain 1943-4

In this series of books, differences in ways of life were examined, with contrasts of geography, climate, population, and government depicted by a mixture of Isotype charts and photographs (not shown here).

Both this series and 'The Soviets and ourselves' (overleaf) were begun during wartime and, while certified to conform with the Book Production War Economy Standard, they were subsidized by the Ministry of Information. This explains the extensive colour printing afforded to the Isotype charts.

The series editor was Philip Sargant Florence, an American economist based in England.

## The Soviets and ourselves 1945-7

The editor of this series of books was John Macmurray, but during its preparation Otto Neurath also dealt directly with the head of the Russian division at the Ministry of Information, Peter Smollett. Originally named Hans Peter Smolka, Smollett was also an Austrian émigré, and was later exposed as a Soviet spy (after having received an oBE for services to the British Empire), The author of the second book in the series was historian Christopher Hill, under the pseudonym K. E. Holme. Hill was a Communist and served on the Russian desk at the British Foreign Office during the Second World War. It has been alleged that he used this position to promote Soviet policy. In his text Hill certainly took an idealized view of collectivization and the single-party system.
