If ETTORE SOTTSASS (also see under "Living with Postmodern Design") was the figurehead of Postmodernism, Charles Jencks (also see under "Living with Postmodern Design") was its chief historiographer. In a titanic output of books, articles, and lectures, Jencks—a writer and theorist of considerable skill as well as an architect and landscape designer—codified and clarified the philosophy of Postmodernism to an audience that found its aims as difficult to understand as its pluralist aesthetics. His writings became sourcebooks for the architecture and design communities, as well as ongoing references for historians and students Beginning with The Language of Post-Modern Architecture (ı977),33 which has been printed in ten languages and multiple editions, Jencks transposed the term from literature to architecture, providing a theoretical foundation for what began as simply a revolt against the International Style. He has published more than thirty books and edited several more. His best-known works include Adhocism: The Case for Improvisation (with Nathan Silver, 1972), What is Postmodernism? (i986), and The Story of Post-Modernism: Five Decades of the Ironic, Iconic and Critical in Architecture (2oı), whose cover illustrated some of architecture's most recognizable buildings, based on what Jencks defined as the five traditions of postmodern architecture.34 It was Jencks who posited a specific date for the death of Modernism: the 1972 destruction of the Pruitt-Igoe housing project in St. Louis, Missouri, an ill-fated example of the failure of the International Style to improve the lives of the thousands of residents who had moved into the complex when it opened in 1955, and saw it deteriorate into a slum. Jencks was born in Baltimore, Maryland, and studied English literature at Harvard, ![](images/06aae43907a01156a8e871b1ccbdd6571a21ba9d3588827cea66a3c3896a99aa.jpg) ![](images/dfaf142cd0f206b52b6b119322c8eb74bb02d113179748f0d80e29c489c959ef.jpg) receiving his BA in 196ı before changing course to attend Harvard's Graduate School of Design in 1965, where he studied under Sigfried Giedion and Reyner Banham, both of whom had published theoretical works that dealt with the history of Modernism and would certainly have influenced Jencks's inclination toward intellectual study over conventional practice. He moved to the United Kingdom in the mid-ı96os and attended University College London, where he received a PhD in architectural history in 1970. Despite his connection to Postmodernism, Jencks classified his own work as “symbolic,"35 in its allusion to cultural ideas and historical references. The Garagia Rotunda (i977) in Cape Cod, Massachusetts, illustrates his theory about adhocism, the assemblage of unrelated elements in an almost improvised fashion. His own Thematic House (i984) in London was a far more complex project, which he designed to represent symbolic architecture, an approach he identified as a “next stage" of postmodern architecture. Over five years, he remodeled (with the architect TERRY FARRELL, see under “Architecture") an 184os townhouse to incorporate symbols expressing the ideas of cosmic time (the passage of the Moon, Sun, and seasons) and cultural time (the progress of civilization). He sought to return meaning to ornament, feeling that in Postmodernism it had declined into kitschy and meaningless signs. Human forms can be seen in doors and windows, and the entrance hall has stenciled images of the dominant themes. Four rooms, one representing each season, are clustered around the central staircase, and Jencks also designed furniture relating to the themes, the best known of which are his Sun table and Sun chair (1984). In addition to his significance as writer and lecturer on the postmodern movement, Jencks has also been an influential landscape designer, beginning with the Garden of Cosmic Speculation (i989), an extraordinary 30-acre (ı2-ha) expanse in Scotland which celebrates the many aspects of nature by engaging both the mind and the senses (including, according to its designer, the sense of humor). In 1995, with his late wife Maggie Keswick, Jencks co-founded the Maggie's Centres, a group of drop-in centers that provide a caring environment for cancer patients and their families.
