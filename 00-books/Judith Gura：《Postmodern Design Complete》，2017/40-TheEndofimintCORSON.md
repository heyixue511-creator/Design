Iron Curtain nations like Poland, Czechoslovakia, and Hungary were hotbeds of innovative cultural, film, theater, and art posters that both echoed and diverged from Western European Modernism. Work by Polish designers like Henryk Tomaszewski and Roman Cieślewicz and the Czech Zdeněk Ziegler were respected by admiring Westerners through feature articles in the Zurich-based magazine Graphis, edited by Walter Herdeg. Postmodernism did not entirely supplant Modernism but coincided with and sometimes smashed into it—and, like atoms in a collider, produced another, sometimes explosive style. Postmodernism arose in the mid-i98os when Tibor Kalman, founder of New York-based M&Co., began designing with so-called vernacular or untutored forms (the tasteless stuff that drove modernists out of their skins), like Chinese menu design, as a means of protesting what he called an elitist modernistic professionalism. He did not call himself postmodern, but railed against corporate Modernism: "[Mly overall worry about the design business is whether as a group we are becoming overly influenced by money and professional success," he argued in 2oı1, "and whether that's impinging on our ability to criticize our clients and make an impact on the world and as a group influence culture."4 The corporate system that Modernism served was being critiqued by a new generation. This was one reason why, when Apple's 1984 television commercial announced that the Macintosh was the next major design tool, fearless designers went computer-crazy. The power of the computer implied power for designers and signaled new opportunities, postmodernizing design through primitive techniques like pixelating, overlapping, underprinting, and serendipitous digital flaws that soon became de rigueur. The new digital tools were boons that allowed postmodernists to play with illegibility and engage in other such typographical sins. Postmodernism, which even the curators of the 2oıl exhibition “Postmodernism: Style and Subversion, 1970-1990" at the Victoria and Albert Museum refused to categorically define, was incorporated into graphic design long before the term was coined. Setting aside for a moment the PoMo theory and pedagogy of the 198os and 199os, even in the late 1940s and 1950s modernists battled against renegades who rejected or ignored the changes in design form that modern designers had accomplished. But the battle lines were sometimes blurry. Paul Rand was a devoted form-follows-function modernist who made photocollage and cut-paper illustrations that might seem postmodern today by comparison with the more rigid work of his Swiss colleagues. Although his design was not as risqué as those of, for example, NEVILLE BRODY, APRIL GREIMAN, or David Carson\*, Rand did not always adhere strictló to minimalism. Dan Friedman\*, who studied at the Hochschule für Gestaltung Ulm—a hotbed of orthodox Modernism—and later at the Schule für Gestaltung Basel, with both the rebellious WOLFGANG WEINGART and modernist Armin Hofmann, called his later work "radical modernism"⁵ because it contained eccentric RIGHT April Greiman, business cards for Vertigo, 1979. visual traits and color play that later suggested a strain of Postmodernism. Even earlier, the late i96os rock-poster master Victor Moscoso, who studied with modernist guru Josef Albers at Yale, ignored all the modern rules of form and function in work that could arguably be called postmodern, but was referred to as psychedelic. And of course, Push Pin Studios, the first of the design and illustration studios of the 195os and 196os, proudly rejected the sterility of Swiss Modernism's limiting typo-pictorial guidelines. Milton Glaser and SEYMOUR CHWAST, Push Pin's co-founders, had proto-postmodernist leanings, and Glaser has admitted that they rejected "the strong authoritarianism of modernism."6 Then there was Herb Lubalin, who became known for smashed and overlapping typography that was resolutely contemporary but avoided modernist rigidity. His work was not postmodern, but today it might be considered so. Asked to define Modernism, Paul Rand said, “It means integrity; it means honesty; it means the absence of sentimentality and the absence of nostalgia; it means simplicity; it means clarity."7 Postmodernism, presumably, was the opposite of all that. By the late i98os and i99os the new generation of PoMo designers finally had a soapbox. Emigre magazine, the voice of new type and digital typography edited by Rudy VanderLans, was dedicated to showcasing a coterie of avid contra-modernists. In 1995 in a story for Emigre, Jeffery Keedy decried “Zombie Modernism" as a walking dead style. But whatever the next thing was, Postmodernism—or the New Wave, Newer Wave, and so on—was an enigma. In his review of the V&A exhibition in Eye magazine, design critic Rick Poynor wrote: “any attempt to reduce such an intricate set of cultural factors across so many kinds of art, design and media to a brief, all-encompassing definition would be a misrepresentation.' ″8 Perhaps PoMo is a bracket within which is anything that does not fit or is contrary to the definition of Modernism. Arguably, there is a lot of unintentionally un-modern design from the period spanning 1970-20oo. Punk, for instance, was a visceral approach that defied the rules of all proper design but was a slap in Modernism's face in particular. It is rarely put in the PoMo category; whereas what is sometimes called grunge, which is a lot like punk in its defacement of classical design, is considered postmodern by some. The work of ART CHANTRY, the celebrated punk and post-punk era poster designer, sometimes falls into PoMo because he employs vernacular type and imagery that routinely bust the grid, and uses vintage stock art. Stock cuts characterize the styles of Charles S. Anderson\*, who seized the monopoly on vintage commercial art clichés, which he refined and retrofitted. His work fits squarely into a retro style that signals a nostalgia anathema to orthodox Modernism and leans toward Postmodernism. Retro came into focus when Philip B. Meggs, the pioneering graphic design historian, wrote the article "The Women Who Saved New York."9 It featured four women, leaders of the retro "movement" PAULA SCHER, Louise Fili, CARIN GOLDBERG, and Lorraine Louie, who were each dipping into design history for inspiration. The story focused on the rise of women and the fact that men had dominated the modernist field; and it also supported the notion that retro fell chronologically and aesthetically under the postmodern umbrella. Consequently, other postmodern women designers began to get publicity in the trade press. Many, in fact, were modernist-trained designers who were making turns toward postmodern aesthetics; among the most visible was DEBORAH SUSSMAN, who studied at the New Bauhaus and worked at the Charles and Ray Eames Studio, and in i984 created a postmod ern graphic identity for the Los Angeles Olympics, in a style some critics dubbed California Modern. And, after working for Unimark (co-founded by modernist extraor dinaire Massimo Vignelli), KATHERINE MCCOY took over the graphic design department at Cranbrook Academy of Art, which was a wellspring of designers who helped defne postmodern-Deconstructivist style. It is important to realize that new technol ogy increased the power of women in design, and the number of postmodern women as well. April Greiman was one of postmodern graphic design's pioneers. A student of Wolfgang Weingart, she had been entrenched in Modernism until 1984, when the Macintosh was introduced. Greiman took the leap of creating digital collages that commingled video, still photography, and type. Her mixed media work was known as New Wave in the design press but it also fell under the PoMo banner: "She had been rocking the Modernist boat for a few years when she undertook a major assault upon the design community's sensibilities and preconceptions of what constitutes design in i986, in an issue of Design Quarterly." Greiman was the subject and designer of the Walker Art Center's Design Quarterly issue 133: an opportunity for Greiman not only to present her digital work “but to ask a larger question of the work and the medium: Does it make sense? Reading Wittgenstein on the topic, she identified with his conclusion: 'It makes sense if you give it sense.'"1² Her influence in multimedia design continues to have far-reaching implications. Digital typeface design was a postmodern outlier, and no one captured the essence of the 198os and 199os better than Zuzana Licko, the designer of such early digital fonts as the pixelated Lo-Res, Citizen, and the dot matrix-styled Matrix. “I'm sure that being free of preconceived notions regarding typeface design helped me in exploring this new medium to the fullest," Licko told Eye in 2oo2, "It's interesting how the gradual sophistication of my type design abilities has been matched by advances in the Apple Macintosh's capabilities, so it has continued to be the ideal tool for me."13 Dozens of her typefaces, both precise and grungy classical and novel, helped in some instances to pinpoint the moment of conception of postmodern graphic typography. Today the banners still wave, but modern tenets and postmodern theatrics coexist separately as well as together; Sinatra and The Beatles exist on the same playlist, so to speak, along with disco, punk, and hip hop—as well as cross-genre combinations and more. The modern versus postmodern discourse may occasionally prompt a critical or theoretical debate—like the difference between readability and legibility—but the polarization that spiced up the earlier discourse is over.
