# NIKOLAUS PEVSNER THE JUBILEE EDITION OF An Outline of European Architecture

Q27

Professor Nikolaus Pevsner's An Outline of European Architecture is by now a classic. It came out as a Pelican book in 1943 and has since grown in material and illustrations through six English editions and foreign editions in German, Dutch, Italian, and Spanish. The total sales of the book now exceed a quarter of a million. Thanks to continual revision it is as up to date now as when it first came out. This edition, however, is a completely new venture. For the first time the detailed text of Pevsner's book is as fully illustrated as it deserves. Moreover the author has added a completely new last chapter dealing historically, appreciatively, and critically with architectural developments from 1914 to the present day. There are 740 pages with over 600 plates and 135 plans and drawings.

'Quite the best short history of European architecture that has ever been published in this country, preserving . . . not merely a control of all the essential facts, but a balance of treatment of quite exceptional justness and at the same time a liveliness of approach and freshness of vision which are generally sacrificed in such efforts of condensation.'- Herbert Read in the Architects' Fournal

'It is Dr Pevsner's proud distinction to have produced a work which is always stimulating, occasionally provocative, and never dull, which nevertheless rests firmly on a foundation of unchallengeable scholarship.'- Osbert Lancaster in the Listener

Born in 1902, Professor Nikolaus Pevsner is married and has three children. He is Head of the Department ofHistory of Art at Birkbeck College (University of London), Editor of the Pelican History of Art and the King Penguin Books, an Editor of the Architectural Review, and one of the most learned and stimulating writers on art in England.

He studied the history of art and architecture in Germany; he was for five years on the staff of the Dresden Gallery and for four years lecturer at Göttingen University, where he specialized in the History of Art in Great Britain, and for six years Slade Professor  Fine Art at Cambridge.
