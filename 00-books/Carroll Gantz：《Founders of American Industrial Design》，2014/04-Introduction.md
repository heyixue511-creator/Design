# Introduction For those who are unfamiliar with the general subject matter of this book, it may be helpful to summarize the overall context of the time. The “Machine Age” is a generic term that refers not just to machines, which had been around in industry for a century before, but to the new way in which the public perceived them. In the middle of the 1920s, people suddenly realized that mechanical machines were not just for large-scale industrial purposes, but that they actually could purchase machines such as cars, washing machines, refrigerators and vacuum cleaners at affordable prices for personal use around the home. They began to regard machines as personal possessions, rather than abstract technology. At the same time, people also became aware of style, a quality previously considered only in decorative products for display in the home, but of little significance in functional, mechanical products, such as their new household appliances. Now that they were able to afford such products, and regarded them as personal possessions, however, they were extremely proud of them and wanted them to be seen by friends and neighbors as evidence of their increasing financial success. So they wanted them in the latest styles, just like some of the recent European designs being promoted by upscale department stores as fashionable. In addition to these new perceptions, people began to realize that they could influence the way these products looked and functioned by expressing their preference of competing products on the market through purchase choice, or through customer surveys, commonly used by advertising agencies. Such public demand soon caused some manufacturers of functional, mechanical products to pay attention to the appearance of their products by engaging artists to redesign them. Most, however, saw no such need, since business was booming, and changing tooling was costly. In the midst of these interrelated developments, the Great Depression hit in 1929, and manufacturers not only lost half of their sales volume, but faced going out of business completely, which many in fact did. They became desperate, and when they learned of industrial designers in 1934, they rushed to locate a design consultant, only to find that the few available were already over their heads with work. Manufacturers' demand for such designers was so intense that it attracted designers from numerous other fields to fill the need, and the industrywide transformation of industrial products into modern styles had begun. It has not stopped since. They were, and are, called industrial designers. Today there are more than 50,000 industrial designers in the United States. By the time the profession of industrial design first became well known, in 1935, there were only about 36 pioneers who were practicing in the field. They came from all walks of life and businesses, mostly in the creative arts, to address a national problem: the economy was as bad as it had ever been, unemployment was at 25 percent, and industry was on life support because consumers were not spending. America was far behind Europe in design, particularly in massproduced electromechanical consumer products, because manufacturers of these products relied solely on engineers for their design. Mechanical products were devoid of humanistic attributes such as attractive appearance, convenience of use, or the latest styles. The pioneer “machine designers," primarily commercial artists, stage designers, and craftsmen, changed all this within a decade, by working with engineers, salesmen, and the public to transform the man-made landscape into what we now call the modern world by redesigning mechanical products to become sleek, stylish, shiny, and attractive. Their creative work attracted consumers, who began spending the money they had been hoarding because of bad economic times. Increased sales reinvigorated industry, brought America out of the Depression, and led to the highest standard of living in the world after World War II. As will be described later, it was the first successful marriage of art and industry, a visionary objective sought by many European design educators since the beginning of the 20th century to provide well-designed products to the masses. Before that, 19th-century craftsmen had dreamed of providing ordinary people with handcrafted designs previously only affordable by the wealthy. Now, in the midst of the Great Depression, the machine designers accomplished this dream by founding a new profession, which created affordable and artistic designs available in mass-produced quantities. The original pioneer designers continued to work diligently, and by the end of the 1930s they dominated the rapidly growing field. After World War II, some headed huge consulting firms or corporate design departments to meet the enormous postwar consumer demand for new and better products. As the leaders of the profession, the pioneers confirmed the status of their activity as a legal profession, established professional organizations, recommended educational programs, implemented codes of ethics, and initiated national annual design awards programs. A few became international celebrities and led the formation of international design organizations, or assisted in the economic and design development of war-torn nations after World War II as design consultants abroad. By the time some of the pioneers approached retirement, they had established large, successful organizations that continue to this day. This is the story of those few dozen pioneers who founded modern industrial design in America and changed the landscape of consumer and industrial products within a decade, one product at a time. ## CHAPTER 1 ## The "Artless" and "Art" Industries The Machine Age (1918–1941), as it was known even at the time, not only included the Great Depression, but also the preceding decade after World War I when America led the world in industrialization, science, technology, engineering, and consumerism. Except for a brief postwar depression from 1920 to 1921, as President Warren Harding took office, the 1920s in America were a period of economic boom and prosperity, as American industry returned to peacetime production to meet consumer demand for the many new mechanical and electrical products then becoming available on the market for the first time in high quantities and at affordable prices. Among these were automobiles, led by Ford's Model T, which by 1918 comprised 50 percent of the auto market and which epitomized American mass production, also known then as Fordism. Many home appliances used electricity, which, although only half the country was electrified, mostly in urban areas, was becoming more widespread. Machines, large and small, were the leading edge of technology. Refrigerators were replacing iceboxes, ranges were replacing woodstoves, and vacuum cleaners were replacing carpet sweepers. There were electrical appliances such as mixers, fry pans, coffee pots, waffle irons, juicers, irons, roasters, and toasters. To many consumers, many of whom were women, these industrial products for the kitchen were perceived to be as magical a technology as personal home robots would seem to us today. Prices sound very reasonable today (\$260 for the Model T and \$52 for a vacuum cleaner in 1918), but in 2013 dollars, the Ford would cost \$4,020 and the vacuum cleaner \$804. Still, many households had good wages (\$2,000 per year in 1918, or \$30,700 in 2013 dollars), and since almost 50 percent of working-class wives worked for wages, many were two-income families. Credit buying was also available. Can you imagine paying 40 percent of your annual salary for a vacuum cleaner? Still, to many families, and with installment plans becoming quite common, it seemed like the American Dream coming true. After 50 years of invention and development of a massive industrial infrastructure, including gigantic steam engines, hydroelectric plants, electrified cities, factories, railroads, bridges, and paved roads, average citizens were finally seeing the products of mass production and industrialization in their own homes. Technology had finally reached their personal, daily lives. The production system had been refined, improved, and made more efficient by Philadelphia engineer Frederick Winslow Taylor (1856-1915), who in 1911 published The Principles of Scientific Management, a system of efficiency experts using stopwatches to measure every step of production in order to reduce time and labor. This process of industrial organization became known as Taylorism, and was known everywhere as the companion to Fordism, the twin jewels in the crown of mass production. All mass-produced, new, electrical, and mechanical consumer products were invented, designed, and built by engineers, the backbone and champions of 20th-century industrialization. Many were famous and known worldwide. Herbert Hoover, who would become U.S. president in 1928, was himself a wealthy mining engineer. Thomas Edison (1847–1931), Henry Ford (1863–1947), and Orville Wright (1871–1948) were still alive and being celebrated as American heroes as late as 1929. They had brought electric light, the phonograph, the automobile, and the airplane to the world. All engineers were highly respected and admired as heroes of the age. The number of engineers had increased exponentially since 1870, when there were only a hundred. By 1900 there were 45,000, and by 1930, half a million. They were building modern bridges, dams, roads, skyscrapers, airplanes, ships and trains, as well as consumer products such as automobiles, cash registers, telephones, appliances, industrial machinery, clocks, tractors, shovels and pitchforks. Engineers were trained in hundreds of technical schools and were familiar with a range of materials, stresses, structures, manufacturing processes, costs, and the many technologies, such as electricity, steam, and combustion engines, that were required to power the manufacturing equipment or the mechanical devices. The overriding objective of all engineers was to create a physical structure which, whether a suspension bridge, an airplane, a vacuum cleaner, or a toaster, was strictly functional, economical, and efficient. These were, after all, machines intended to provide humans with transportation, power, and labor saving, and these were the functions expected, highly admired and acclaimed by the public. Most consumers were delighted with any of the new mass-produced electromechanical goods being made available, despite the fact that they were generally rather mechanical looking and ungainly in appearance, and although for many they were still rather expensive. But their mechanistic look was initially a positive image of technology. The main thing was that they performed their functional objective, were available at reasonable prices, and offered a higher standard of everyday living. For those many new consumers who had never had a vacuum cleaner before, its function, not its appearance, was of primary interest and the greatest source of user satisfaction. Functional performance, power, and labor saving were the coins of the realm, and all the new appliance manufacturers competed successfully on that basis. Therefore, there were few “artists" needed or desired in the new mechanical product industries, other than those that applied a few surface decorations to the finished product, often just pinstripes or flourishes suggesting a token “human” or “artistic” touch. It is no surprise then that these products, which included autos, locomotives, cash registers, clocks, optical goods, sewing machines, washing machines, fountain pens, refrigerators, and electrical/mechanical machinery, were generally categorized at the time as the “artless industries," because they were generally designed and produced by engineers, not artists. When we talk about the Machine Age, it is this specific category of mass-produced products, the “artless industries," that is referred to, not handcrafted artistic products such as decorative objects or crafts with limited production. Not only did machines of mass production manufacture these mechanical products in the millions, but in individual function and character, the products themselves were in fact mechanical, or machinelike devices, often with multiple moving components, such as motors, mechanisms, or engines, and were often operated by electricity. When we talk about "design," we mean the formal definition: "a plan or drawing produced to show the look and function of an object before it is built or made." A designer, of course, is the person producing such a plan or drawing. Designers differ frorn artists in that they create work that has a purpose other than that of individual self-expression or esthetic beauty. Often the purpose is to meet the needs of an individual, a company, an audience, a functional operation, or all combined. When we talk about “machine designers," the primary subjects of this book, we are talking about a small, innovative group of artistic individuals in the 1930s, who accomplished what no other artists in history had done: they abandoned the artist's traditional personal freedom of self-expression and applied their professional, artistic, creative, and analytical skills to massproduced products that previously were designed solely by engineers, in order to make them more attractive to consumers, to make them more convenient and comfortable for users, to use more appropriate and economical materials, and yet to accommodate the normal business interests of cost, schedules, and manufacturing ease. To achieve this, these artists had not only needed to create attractive, hand drawn, concept sketches of their ideas to communicate them to others, but to produce mechanical drawings to instruct engineers in the specific dimensions and final shape to be manufactured by production tools. They had to work, not as an individual, but as a team member with other specialized industry disciplines, such as engineering, marketing, manufacturing, and sales. In short, they became multitalented artists-designers-engineers-businessmen-salesmen, inventing a brand new profession that as yet had no specific name. The term “artless industries" had evolved to distinguish certain mechanical products from the traditional “art industries," as defined by the Twelfth Exhibition of Contemporary American Industrial Art at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York, October 12 to November 22, 1931.' The “art industries” were the only industrial “art" supported, promoted, and exhibited by museums. These were industries producing decorative arts, which included ceramics, glassware, enamelwork, furniture, leatherwork, printing, jewelry, art metalwork, lighting fixtures, carpets, drapes, silverware, textiles, and wallpaper, or any product intended as a decorative object for the home. A number of these were in fact functional as well as decorative, so their designers were necessarily involved with form and function as well as surface decoration. By and large, these “decorative arts" industries grew out of the Arts and Crafts movement of the 19th century, where skilled craftspeople originally selected appropriate materials and made both decorative and functional objects individually by hand, shaping and/or decorating the surfaces manually or by hand-operated machines of the era. The craftsman was in control of all the physical and functional aspects of a product, as well as its final appearance. Even after machines were developed to produce such products in quantity, many were still designed before manufacture by craftspeople, who therefore still controlled both the functional form and its external decorative treatments. This personal involvement of a single individual craftsman in the total design of a particular product was common historically, and resulted in famous craftsmen names such as Paul Revere (silverware), Thomas Chippendale (furniture), Eli Terry (clocks), and Duncan Phyfe (furniture). Ever since Leonardo DaVinci (1452–1519), art and engineering had gone hand in hand. From the beginning of industrialization, art and industry were closely related. In the early 19th century, the “mechanic arts" or “useful arts,"² as they were called, included a wide range of technical skills, such as mechanical design, decorative design, mechanical drawing, materials analysis, crafts, machine design, production planning, art, factory design, physics, mathematics, etc. Over the century, as industrialization advanced and as educational institutions tried to teach such comprehensive technical skills, these all became individually named specialties and were separated from one other. Thus did art and engineering become separate disciplines, with specialized skills and differing motives. As 20th-century manufacturing produced more and more consumer products in greater and greater quantities, and as factories become more complex technologically, more impersonal, and more industrial in character, fewer and fewer American craftspeople or artists were interested in the artless industries dominated by engineers, dirty machinery, time clocks, and crass commercialism. Even the art industries held limited appeal to artists because such applied art generally went unrecognized and required working nine to fve in a factory environment that was less than clean, quiet, or dignified. So even though many educational institutions offered programs in “industrial art” or "applied art," most aspiring artists tended to prefer the “fine arts," such as painting or sculpture, where they could receive personal recognition and prestige as exhibitors in galleries or museums and could work in their own personal studios. This was the essential conclusion of a \$120,000 comprehensive study of industrial arts education from 1920 to 1922 by Charles R. Richards of the Cooper Union, funded by the General Education Board of the federal government.3 At the time, the terms “industrial art" and “applied art" were the most commonly used terms for art careers in industry, and as noted above, they were not popular choices for aspiring artists. But there was another, more obscure term used occasionally in the fine arts field since 1839: "industrial design," also referring to the use of artists for industrial purposes. This term had more recently been specifically defined for industrial purposes in 1913 by the U.S. commissioner of patents, Edward B. Moore, who urged modifications of existing regulations to protect the property of“industrial design, the distinguishing form of products that have marketable value" (italics in original). The existing regulations he referred to were design patents, which protected the exterior appearance of an object against copying since 1842, when they were first initiated by the patent office for "any new and original shape or configuration of any article of manufacture not known or used by others before."⁴Many museums encouraged the copying of European products by American manufacturers because they were not protected outside of the country of origin. Commissioner Moore was concerned that even U.S. design patents were not being enforced or upheld aggressively in America, thus discouraging innovative designs by Americans. America found itself in a conflicted situation in the 1920s. It commanded the most productive manufacturing industry in the world, as well as the most lucrative market for consumer products. Factories were making as many products as possible, and a stable economy enabled a mass market of middle-class consumers to buy as many products as industry could produce. Nevertheless, a few art museums and organizations, who were familiar with the European potential to dominate world markets, were warning that America was far behind Europe in applied arts and was in desperate need of more future designers in industry to compete globally. In 1918, Walter Sargent (1868–1927), director of the Department of Fine and Industrial Art at the University of Chicago, had written, The situation regarding industrial design in the United States is improving, but so far as we can estimate ... we shall need after the war about fifty thousand more industrial designers [author's italics] than are now available or in training, and probably few can be imported. Each country will need its own ... we shall have to depend on ourselves more than in the past, not only for designers, but also for styles of design.⁵ Meanwhile, during the same time, the situation in Europe was quite different. While America enjoyed the postwar benefits of mass production and a strong economy, Europeans were suffering from wartime devastation and inflation, with virtually no industrial production to restore economic health and recovery, and practically no consumer markets. America was fortunate in that it had no foreign competition. Europeans had seen the American industrial model as the key to the future for some years before the war. In 1909, Italian poet Filippo Tommaso Marinetti (1876–1944) founded a fine arts movement called Futurism, which promoted speed, technology, youth, automobiles, and airplanes, but also, sadly, fascism and war. Followers believed that the power of art and technology could change the social structures of the world and produce a dynamic, aggressive, and strong Italy, but political upheaval, scarce national resources, and World War II would prevent European countries from competing effectively with U.S. manufacturing for another 30 years. In Germany, the march of Nationalist Socialism would within ten years lead inexorably to Hitler and German rearmament, and in Russia, the Bolsheviks would transform the country into communism, where nationalized industry would support the government, not individuals. However, Europeans still led the world in modern art. In fact, the modernist movement had begun in the last part of the 19th century. France, for example, had introduced Impressionism and led Art Nouveau at the turn of the century. Prominent artists such as Pablo Picasso, Henri Matisse, Fernand Léger, Georges Braque, Marcel Duchamp, Francis Picabia, and André Derain led a parade of new interpretations of art. From 1907 to 1911, Picasso, Léger, and Braque introduced Cubism, an art movement in which objects are analyzed, broken up, and reassembled in an abstract form, often depicting objects from a multitude of viewpoints. Cubism has been considered the most influential art movement of the 20th century affecting art, sculpture, architecture, and music. The works of many of these European artists and a few Americans were first shown in America at the International Exhibition of Modern Art in New York in 1913, the so-called Armory Show, since it was held at the city's 69th Infantry Regiment Armory, at Lexington and 26th Street. Most American viewers, including President Theodore Roosevelt, rejected the new styles as obscene or primitive, certainly not art, and most American artists continued to paint or sculpt in highly traditional styles. America was not yet ready for modern art. In 1914, as World War I began, modern artist Duchamp (1887–1968) took a step that challenged the very definition of art. He took a common manufactured wire bottle rack, signed his name to it, and declared that it was art because he said it was art. In 1915, he and Francis Picabia (1879–1953) both visited New York and were publicized as prominent French artists who for the first time were, as they said, "seeking America in matters of art." Picabia praised what he saw: I adore New York. There is much about it, which is like the Paris of the old days.... I have been profoundly impressed by the vast mechanical development in America. The machine has become more than a mere adjunct of human life.... I intend to work on until I attain the pinnacle of mechanical symbolism.6 Americans were flattered to be recognized and admired by famous French artists, and Picabia was true to his word. He soon produced a number of drawings inspired by spark plugs, meshing gears, pistons, and cylinders that were published by Alfred Steiglitz's art journal, 291. Picabia's work clearly assumed that mechanical objects were worthy as works of art, a concept that at the time most Americans failed to share. The unconventional work of European artists had no effect whatsoever upon the 1915 Panama-Pacific International Exposition in San Francisco, which had been planned since 1911, primarily to celebrate the completion of the Panama Canal, but also to herald the recovery of San Francisco from the disastrous effects of its 1906 earthquake. The architecture, sculpture, and murals featured were clearly in the traditions of the 19th century, with highly realistic, classical, but allegorical statues and murals, mostly nudes, suggesting abstract ideas of abundance, progress, conquest, creation, power, etc., and historical depictions of conquistadors, pirates, and miners. The art at the exposition was a public display of America's imitation of European art as it was in the 1870s, not an appropriate expression of America's 20th-century leadership in technology, science, or manufacturing. It was art of the past. A more effective global advertisement of America's obsolescence in art and design could not be imagined. Yet despite criticism from traditionalists, modern art marched forward. In 1917 Duchamp submitted a mass-produced ceramic urinal, titled Fountain, to the Independent Artists Exhibition in New York as “art," which aroused outraged protests but nevertheless established a fundamental principle of modern art: art is whatever the artist chooses to define as art. In this case, it also suggested that common utilitarian objects could be viewed as art, which may have been a surprise to the manufacturer of the urinal, or any company making similar utilitarian products. Other European countries evolved independent styles of modern art. In 1917, Piet Mondrian (1872–1944) and Theo van Doesburg (1893-1931) initiated the Dutch movement of de Stijl (the Style), which featured primary colors arranged asymmetrically in rectangular paintings, or abstract furniture. In Russia after the 1917 Revolution, Alexandr Rodchenko (1891–1956) and Varvara Stepanova (1894–1958) founded an artistic movement called Constructivism in 1921, and in 1922 published their manifesto to “set itself the task of finding the communistic expression of material structures."7 Leon Trotsky (1879–1940) inspired Communist Party encouragement of avant-garde art. He rejected fine art but promoted the more “productive" and “constructive" fields of architecture and mass production. So artists were asked to “go into the factories, where the real body of life is made."8 Rodchenko and Stepanova inspired the founding of an avant-garde art and design school called Vkhutemas (an abbreviation ofits formal title: Higher State Artistic and Technical Workshops). After the war, Europe also led in architecture and design, which was often inspired by the American model of mass production. In the particular category of consumer product design, the primary focus of this book, the English Arts and Crafts movement dominated the latter 19th century. As the 20th century began, Arts and Crafts leadership broadened to other European countries, as France dominated Art Nouveau. In Austria in 1903, the Wiener Werkstätte (Viennese Workshops) were formed by architect Josef Hoffmann (1870–1956) and Koloman Moser (1868–1918) who, like most Arts and Crafts leaders, were opposed to mass production and rejected the evils of what they saw as cheap and shoddy products produced under abhorrent conditions. They wanted to counteract this machine-made trend by making handcrafted products that were both innovative in design and superior in quality and workmanship. Arts and Crafts workshops in America followed this same philosophy, which drew a sharp line between handmade crafts and industrial mass production, segregating art from industry on the basis of morality and artistic superiority. The core belief of the Arts and Crafts movement was that production by cold machines and unskilled labor (often under poor working conditions), even though inexpensive, had no humanistic value, and in fact was a form of slavery. However, acceptance of mass production was growing. In 1906, a member of the Dresden (Germany) Workshops, Richard Riemerschmid (1868–1957), designed a line of Machinenmöbel (machine furniture), where individual pieces were precut and assembled in a production process, rather than in the one-at-a-time tradition of craftsmen. Still, most Arts and Crafts workshops continued to oppose mass production as a threat to their livelihood, while innovative German applied arts educators radically sought a final solution (sorry—not the best term) to the problem by literally marrying art and industry. In 1907, German applied arts educator Hermann Muthesius (1861-1927) formed the Deutscher Werkbund (German Union of Work), a trade association. It consisted of 12 industrialists and 12 artists/architects, dedicated to dominating global product markets for Germany. That same year, to consummate this intended marriage of art and industry, Werkbund architect member Peter Behrens (1868-1940) was appointed as “artistic consultant" to Allgemeine Elekricitäts Gesellschaft (AEG), the German equivalent of America's General Electric, where he designed corporate graphics, products, and buildings, the first such collaboration of art and industry in practice. His designs were well in advance, designwise, of comparable products in the United States. It would be 50 years before an American corporation engaged a similar “artistic consultant” to integrate and control a single corporation's entire visual “identity' (IBM, 1956). Muthesius in 1907 also proposed the development of a new Machinenstil (Machine Style) esthetic of product forms dictated by the purpose they serve, and as the “visible expression of inner driving force of the age,9 that is, to generate new types of forms that visually communicated their intended function, as well as their manufacture by machine processes. The significant purpose of this proposal was to restore the craftsman's role in shaping mass-produced consumer products by an artistic hand and eye, a role that had been lost at the beginning of the century when engineers took over the entire process of mass production. This had ended the Arts and Crafts era and relegated artistic contribution in manufacturing to mere surface decoration then known as applied art. The innovative concept of a Machine Style would soon be advanced by one of Germany's leading applied arts schools. In 1908, Bruno Paul (1874– 1968), one of the 12 artist/architects who founded the German Werkbund, developed a Typenmöbel (standard furniture), which permitted an unlimited number of combinations of standardized, machine-made elements. After the war in 1919, Walter Gropius (1883–1969), an architect, member of the Deutscher Werkbund, and former employee and protégé of Peter Behrens in Berlin, returned from military service to take over as director of the Weimar Kunstgewerbeschule (Weimar Arts and Crafts School), founded in 1902 by Belgian architect Henry Van de Velde (1863–1957), an Arts and Crafts leader who had become a member of the Werkbund, but as a foreign national was forced to leave the country and abandon the school during the war. Gropius renamed the school Das Staatliches Bauhaus (State School of Building), commonly known only as the Bauhaus. Gropius was an avid admirer of American Fordism and Taylorism, and of American grain silos for their architectural simplicity and monumentality. He shared the belief of many in postwar Germany that setting new standards of industrial art would secure German global design leadership in the economic recovery. There at the Bauhaus, over the next decade, an avant-garde art and design faculty and their students developed principles for the design of modern architecture and products that featured simple, undecorated, geometrical modern forms, which were exquisitely crafted in quality materials. Although staffed by many of Europe's most renowned modern artists, the school in Weimar was patterned after medieval craft guild workshops. Professors were known as masters, and students progressed from Lehrlinge (apprentices) to Gesellen (journeymen) to Jungenmeister (young masters). Gropius wanted to elevate Arts and Crafts to the level of fine art. In 1923, Làszló Moholy-Nagy (1895–1946) joined the faculty as master of the metal workshop. He was born in Hungary, studied law in Budapest, and later served in World War I. Convalescing from a war wound, he took up painting and worked his way across Germany as a letterer and sign painter, arriving in Berlin in 1921, where he took up painting and experimental photography. In 1922 he participated in the Dada and Constructivist conference organized by Theo van Doesburg in Weimar. In general, many of these student designs met the idealistic standard of Muthesius's Machinenstil, and a few would be manufactured in limited quantities for the commercial market. Although the school was plagued by financial and political difficulties, it would flourish until 1924, when political demonstrations and Weimar budget cuts forced Gropius to relocate the school to Dessau, where it would come under increasing pressure by the Nazis, who were determined to rid the country of forward-looking (avant-garde) intellectuals. Another former employee and protégé of Peter Behrens, Swiss-born architect Charles Edouard Jenneret Gris (1887–1965), was also intrigued by American Fordism and Taylorism, only he used it in reverse. Designing a slaughterhouse in Paris in 1916, he included a conveyor system to “disassemble" the animals. This practice, used in the United States since 1867, was often claimed by Ford to be the inspiration of his assembly line for automobiles. In 1917 Gris established a small brick-making factory, and by 1920 he considered himself an industrialist as well as an architect. It was then that he adopted his pseudonym, “Le Corbusier" (the Raven), under which he began publishing L'Esprit Nouveau (The New Spirit), a periodical with themes of order, rationality, engineering, and technology. Twenty-eight issues were published over the next five years. As an architect, Corbusier dreamed of producing houses the way Ford produced automobiles. In 1921 he published sketches of a house called Maison Citrohan (Citrohan House), using the analogy of France's mass-produced car based on Ford's assembly line, the Citroën. His concept, he declared, could complete houses in three days made of artificial materials such as concrete. Over the next few years, Corbusier designed a number of private homes in Paris based on his Citrohan House concept. They featured stark interior white walls, furniture made of tubular steel, and single bare bulbs in light fixtures. The effect was pure, simple, modernist design that followed the German Bauhaus style and which he called “a machine for living in." In 1922, Corbusier developed plans for a “Contemporary City" for three million inhabitants that he hoped would reorganize society along lines of American industrial models. In 1923, he published Vers une Architecture (Toward an Architecture), a book summarizing his philosophy of a future world functioning like a machine. Most Americans, enjoying the first fruits of mass production in a new consumer culture, had no idea of the extensive European design or art theories that had evolved on the continent either before or after the war. Modern art was simply not part of American culture. Art, to most Americans, was pretty landscape or portrait paintings on museum walls; Greek sculptures in gardens; or expensive, handcrafted, decorative home furnishings in traditional styles imported from Europe. The concept of combining functional, mechanical products and art was literally unimaginable. By 1925, Europe had not yet recovered economically, but Paris was not only still at the leading edge of modern art, attracting scores of aspiring artists from around the world, including young Americans, but had also become the center of fashion, both in style and entertainment. African American entertainer Josephine Baker (1906–1975) became a stage sensation for her exotic dancing at the Folies Bergére cabaret and would later popularize a dance step called the Charleston. Jazz was the most popular music of the time, but the term “jazz” also meant anything exciting and fun. George Gershwin's composition of 1928, "An American in Paris," evoked the sights and sounds of Paris in the 1920s, and constituted most Americans' sole impression of Paris. Gabrielle "Coco" Chanel (1883–1971) had opened her boutique in Paris in 1919, setting global fashion trends in women's clothing with her "little black dress" and borrowed men's clothing accessories, such as suits, ties, and shirt collars, for feminine fashions in the mid-1920s. She introduced Chanel No. 5 in 1924, the first perfume to feature a designer's name. She epitomized Paris of the 1920s, where women tasted new freedoms to wear what they liked, to cut ("bob") their hair short, to wear short skirts (above the knees), to wear straight dresses without waistlines, to wear makeup and perfume, to expose bare legs, to wear high heels, to wear "cloche" (helmet-type) felt hats, to drive automobiles, or to smoke in public. Whatever happened in Paris soon became fashionable around the world. Young American women of the "Roaring Twenties," who had only recently been granted the right to vote (19th Amendment to the Constitution, May 19, 1919), shortly after being deprived of the legal right to buy alcohol (Prohibition: 18th Amendment to the Constitution, January 19, 1919), were influenced by these new Paris fashions. Many who followed the new styles were called “flappers," and their behavior was perceived pejoratively by many during Prohibition as outlandish, irresponsible, frivolous, sexually provocative, and in general as flouting social and sexual "norms." Retrospectively, of course, women were merely expressing their freedom from residual Victorian restraints against women, and at the same time establishing "fashion” as a driving social force of the 20th century, particularly for women as they became consumers of the new household appliances now becoming available. Such appliances were featured in France in 1923 at the Salon des Arts Ménages (Salon of Domestic Science). French manufacturers collaborated with domestic designers by paying them to develop new styles for presentation, and also by paying them royalties for each reproduction of their design, thus sharing risks as well as rewards. Many of the new designs were based on the basic geometric shapes of Cubist modern art. By and large, such products were not produced in large quantities, as manufacturing in Europe had not yet recovered from the war, nor had it evolved to the level required for significant competition with America. But Europe still retained its historical reputation as the center of art and design, and in fact Europeans have a number of legitimate historical claims for initiating what we now call industrial design because of the many Arts and Crafts groups that advanced the quality of design esthetically in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. However, what we call industrial design today is more broadly defined than as simply designing consumer products in a modern style, but as the process of designing products for mass production by machines in collaboration with engineering, marketing, and manufacturing, for the purpose of consumer acceptance and affordability, as well as for corporate profit, as it is practiced today. It is the Machine Age in America, in the 1920s and 1930s, that defined, pioneered, and implemented this modern process in reality, rather than in European theoretical prototypes that were designed without any manufacturing, cost, or sales considerations, and were therefore only of academic, or esthetic value. Before that, as we have seen, the only major industries in the United States involving artist/designers were the “art industries" in general, and of course there were many designers in these fields for decades before. Some of these designers made the transition to the new field of industrial design before others and could claim, as some did, to be the first “industrial designers" in the United States, long before it became well known as a specific profession. A number of them were veterans of World War I. One of the art industries was the manufacture of decorative lamps, and in about 1920, 24-year-old Chauncey Eugene (Chick) Waltman (1896–1962) was already well known for his lamp designs for the Art Lamp Company on Wabash Avenue in Chicago, where he served as designer, salesman, and advertising manager. The most successful lamp he designed at about this time was the Almco lamp. Waltman was born near Columbus, Indiana, the son of a minister. The family moved to Cincinnati, Ohio, and lived near the famed Rookwood Pottery, where Waltman, as a boy, was inspired by artists he saw working there. The family moved several times around Michigan, ending up in Grand Rapids, where Waltman was at the top of his art class in high school. While still in school, he worked in the decorating department of the Berkery and Guy Furniture Company, and by 1912 he started a decorating department at the Imperial Furniture Company. After high school he took a job in Chicago with John A. Colby and studied decorating and furniture design at night school, but after the start of World War I in 1917, he joined the Air Force and was trained at Eastman Kodak, in Rochester, New York, to prepare maps with photos and hand sketching from airplanes. In 1918, during the flu epidemic, he was sent to Cornell University for further training and to earn a commission as second lieutenant. But suddenly the war was over and he returned to Chicago and resumed night school. Also among the art industries was the furniture industry, which almost exclusively produced traditional and historical styles (many still do). Among designers in this field was Kem Weber (1889–1963) who began in 1921 at age 32 to work in the design of furniture for the Barker Brothers store in Los Angeles, then the largest furniture retailer in the United States. Weber had been born in Germany as Karl Emanuel Martin (K.E.M.) Weber, apprenticed as a cabinetmaker and had graduated from the Berlin Kunstgewerbemuseum (Berlin Academy of Fine Arts) in 1912, studying under the well-known Bruno Paul (1874–1968) and after graduation working for Paul. In 1914 Paul sent him as a designer for Germany's exhibit at the 1915 Panama-Pacific Exposition in San Francisco, but he was stranded there by the start of World War I. At first he opened a modern design studio in Berkeley. But after the sinking of the Lusitania in May 1915, American sentiment turned against Germany, and as a German, he had trouble finding clients, so he worked as a lumberjack in the High Sierra. Toward the end of the war he operated a chicken farm in Los Gatos with his young wife Erika. In 1918 they settled in Santa Barbara, California, where Weber taught art classes and opened a small factory in an old church to produce modern and period furniture, but he met with only modest success. When Barker Brothers offered him a position in its design department in 1921, he jumped at the chance and moved to Los Angeles." Other artists were in the advertising business, which promoted mass-produced artless industry consumer products such as automobiles, appliances, furniture, machinery, tools, and other functional products designed by engineers. Gradually, as the demand for more attractive products grew, these artists suggested ideas for product improvement to their advertising clients. One such individual was New Zealander Joseph “Jo” Sinel, who in 1923 at age 34 was working for the Lennon Mitchell advertising agency in New York, and who designed graphics and products for clients, although specific product designs by him are not known until 1929. Another artist who would become legendary in industrial design, John Vassos (1893–1985), designed a face lotion bottle in 1924 that became popular (with the lotion removed) when used as a hip flask during Prohibition, and sales increased dramatically by 700 percent. Vassos was born John Plato Vassacopoulos in Romania. His family moved to Constantinople where his father became a Greek publisher and diplomat. As a teenager, young Vassos drew a cartoon in his father's newspaper that offended the Turkish senate, and he was forced to leave the country in 1909 at age 16. He fled on a British ship to Palestine. During World War I, he served as a cabin steward on a Belgian steamer, which was torpedoed. He was rescued by the U.S. Navy and landed in London in 1915. He obtained papers and traveled to Boston in 1919 where his older brother Alex lived. There, he studied at the Fenway Art School; studied art and illustration with John Singer Sargent (1856–1925), the leading portrait artist of his generation; and assisted with stage designs for the Boston Opera Company. He came to New York in 1924, became an American citizen, changed his name to Vassos, opened a commercial studio called the New York Display Company, studied at the Art Students League, worked as an illustrator for Harper's and New Yorker magazines, and illustrated and published books. Furniture designers became prominent in the emerging field of industrial design as more modern designs were demanded. Among these was Leo Jiranek (1900–1990), who in 1924 took over his father's furniture design business at age 24 and became one of the industry's first freelancers. In fact, he was considered by many to be the "Dean of Furniture Designers." Jiranek, known as Jerry, was born in Grand Rapids, Michigan, and after prep school at Western Military Academy, he spent two years at Norwich University, then transferred to Princeton University in 1920, where he graduated as an engineer in 1922. Over the years, he would contribute to more furniture companies than any other designer. But in 1925, there was one furniture designer who abandoned all tradition by introducing a style intended to represent the vitality of modern America, inspired by the new steel towers rising all over Manhattan. Generally, his pieces were bookcase-cabinet combinations or desks, which featured unornamented block forms that rose in terraced levels and provided various functional storage boxes and shelves, often lacquered red, blue, or green. Typical were his Skyscraper cabinet and Skyscraper bookcase. Pieces were tall, but with a narrow footprint, perfect for small Manhattan apartments. The designer was 38-year-old Paul T. Frankl (1886- 1958), whose new Skyscraper furniture immediately became popular and was probably the first evidence of a modernistic style originating in America. Frankl was a furniture designer and maker, architect, painter, and writer born in Vienna, Austria, the son of a wealthy real estate dealer. He had been a member of the Wiener Sezession (Vienna Secession) that had early in the 20th century sought to create a new style that owed nothing to historical influence. After completing his architectural studies at the Berlin Polytechnic, he spent time in Berlin and Copenhagen before arriving in the United States in 1914 and settling in New York. He brought an outsider's perspective and a desire to create a uniquely American design esthetic. By 1922 he had established Frankl Galleries on 48th Street in New York, and by 1924 he had a showroom on Madison Avenue, where he sold furniture of his own design as well as imported modern textiles and wallpapers, calling his company Skyscraper Furniture.
