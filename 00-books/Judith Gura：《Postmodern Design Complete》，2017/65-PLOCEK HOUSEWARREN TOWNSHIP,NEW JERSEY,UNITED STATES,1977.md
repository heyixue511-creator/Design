# PLOCEK HOUSE WARREN TOWNSHIP, NEW JERSEY, UNITED STATES, 1977 MICHAEL GRAVES A POSTMODERN PALAZZO, WITH CLASSICAL ELEMENTS INCORPORATED INTO EXPANSIVE MODERN INTERIORS. ABOVE RIGHT The palazzolike residence, set on a sloping hillside, is entered from the second level. RIGHT The driveway leads to a side entrance and auto court. OPPOSITE The domed atrium is framed by modern torchères. Located on a heavily wooded, steeply sloping site in southern New Jersey, the much-published Plocek House is a classic example of the interpretation by the firm of MICHAEL GRAVES (also see under “Masters of Postmodern Design") of classicism in a postmodern vernacular. With the imposing air of an Italian palazzo, the residence (which occupies an area of 4,ooo sq. ft/37o sq. m), completed in i977, was meticulously restored in 2oil to return it to its original elegance. The rusticated base, piano nobile, and attic follow the traditional tripartite form of classical architecture, adapted to an unusual site. The main entrance, facing the street, is actually on the lower level, while the second entrance is off the first floor, accessing the parking area. Inside, a series of layered spaces are organized around a central stair column, which rises to roof height and is crowned with a skylight-topped capital that brings light into the center of the house. On the second floor, metal railings edge the bridges that wrap around the stair column. Clerestory window openings overlook the two-story living room, with its marble-faced fireplace surmounted by a mural that is an abstracted reflection of the landscape outside. RIGHT The entrance is a layered composition of geometric elements. OPPOSITE A prefabricated garage was transformed into a unique country cottage using off-the-shelf components. This quirkily appealing house, in an isolated site, surrounded by sand dunes, ponds, and occasional trees, was both a logistic and an intellectual challenge for CHARLES JENCKS (also see under “Masters of Postmodern Design"), who was at the time crystallizing his concept of Postmodernism. In planning the house, which he called his “first steps to symbolism," Jencks made unrestricted use of ornament, mixing classical and vernacular elements and introducing touches of anthropomorphism. Beginning with the form of a prefabricated garage, Jencks applied his principles of adhocism, building the house entirely of readymade components, purchased as inexpensively as possible; the total cost was a mere \$5,50o. The resulting building reprises a vernacular Cape Cod cottage, embellished with aspects of Queen Anne design: a widow's walk on the roof, a pediment over the front door, classical touches in the ornament. In the absence of luxury materials, the house gets its designworthy credentials from color: no fewer than ten shades of blue were used on the exterior and ![](images/9fb0fc1cf2baf1f85e4e48123bf4f9ff7f011fcf852dab4a7b5ff22c6da1210a.jpg) ![](images/b9f475c3fa33d3f29287e89f858612f033f247775d3cc5c31fce419d13f8126a.jpg) ![](images/77860bdbfb60c453e6fdb743c80c806d33442a52a9ec790cd87b3cf5a24c1c34.jpg) interior to create eye appeal as well as intriguing dimensional effects. Reflecting the variations in tones of blue in the Cape Cod sky and water, the colors take on different intensities as the natural light changes throughout the day. The interior is a sequence of spaces framed by posts and beams, leading to the central living area, which is defined by a cross-axis on the floor. Wall studs are painted in graduating shades of blue, making them decorative as well as functional. The furniture, all by Jencks, is also painted in various shades of blue. The anthropomorphic element is introduced with images of the face or body— a Medusa head above the entrance, the suggestion of a face in the form of a rear bay window.
