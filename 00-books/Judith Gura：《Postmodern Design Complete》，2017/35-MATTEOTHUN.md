# MATTEO THUN b.1952 THIS ITALIAN'S FUNCTIONAL OBJECTS ARE DISPLAY-WORTHY DECORATIVE ACCESSORIES. BELOW Stillight light sculptures, plastic, produced by Bieffeplast, 1985. Perhaps his initial training in sculpture at Oskar Kokoschka's Schule des Sehens (School of Seeing) in Salzburg, Austria, accounts for the individualistic forms of Matteo Thun's objects. Or do they stem from his earlier schooling from his mother, who worked with clay? Perhaps it is simply his desire to make design a sensory as well as visual language; his ceramic objects demand that they be touched as well as looked at. And though he became most celebrated for his inventive ceramics, he has ventured into other areas with equally happy results. Thun was born in Bolzano, Italy, and studied architecture in Florence, graduating in i975 from the University of Florence. He then moved to Milan at the recommendation of ceramicist MARCO ZANINI in order to work for ETTORI SOTTSASS (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design” and “Living with Postmodern Design") at Sottsass Associati. This placed Thun in an ideal position to become one of the founding members of MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") in i98o, along with his co-workers ALDO CIBIC and Zanini. The collaborative's congenial environment was conducive to developing Thun's particular aesthetic, which in his designs for Memphis led to quirky pots and vases that cheekily flouted the conventions of good, modern design. Some had zoomorphic silhouettes with jagged edges or angular forms, clearly prioritizing looks over function and seeming better suited to a display shelf than the dining table. For the first Memphis collection Thun contributed the Nefertiti tea set, an assemblage of classical pyramid forms, and his ceramic pieces for subsequent offerings included the striking Danubio and Volga vases of i983. Anxious to pursue his own path, Thun left Sottsass Associati and Memphis in i984 and established his own Milan practice, turning his talent to designing for a variety of clients. The most highly visible of these was Swatch, for whom he was creative director from 199o to 1993, introducing a series of eye-catching watches that helped transform an inexpensive accessory into a collectible fashion icon. For CLETO MUNARI (see under “Edited Design") he designed an appealing leaning pitcher, a shape he reprised in “walking" coffee pots for WMF (i985); his other product designs included tableware, carpets, flatware, and jewelry for ALESSI (see under “Edited Design"), Anthologie Quartett\*, Arteluce\*, Kartell\*, and Vorwerk. Indulging his humorous side in a i989 project for Thonet, Thun playfully dressed the company's classic bentwood chair in skirts and slipcover tops to suit each season of the year. Despite the sometimes unorthodox nature of his designs, they were favorably received by critics; Thun is a three-time winner of the prestigious Compasso d'Oro award for good design. He shared his ideas with students at the University of Applied Arts Vienna, joining the faculty in ı983 and remaining there for almost two decades, teaching ceramics and product design. For a broader audience, Thun produced the tongue-in-cheek “Baroque Bauhaus" manifesto in 1985, exhorting contemporary designers to learn from the work of the past. In recent years, Thun has broadened his scope to include interiors as well as products, and he has designed several luxury hotels as well as store interiors.
