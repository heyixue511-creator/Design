Those who imitated Isotype in the 193os apparently saw it as little more than a system governed by rules that were easily mastered, and on this basis they produced work that was superficially similar. Neurath's criticism of such imitations emphasized that Isotype was not merely made up of pictograms configured according to a method determined by definite rules, but was instead distinguished by the work of transformation. He had already made this point in his reflections on work directed by Modley for the Museum of Science and Industry:

Public opinion has recognized that the achievement of the Gesellschaftsund Wirtschaftsmuseum in Wien – the 'Vienna Method' – does not only consist of following rules, but far more in the particular cooperation, through years of joint work, of a well-attuned group of people. ...

If imitations of the 'Vienna Method' have hitherto failed so badly, it is because 'transformation' – the cooperation between science and graphic design on the basis of a group tradition – cannot be secured by following rules.¹³2

Although transformation' was defined as a process of converting the bare factual data and the main idea into the basic layout',1ªª Neurath always considered it as a process that was difficult to explain precisely:

There are many transformation rules, some hundreds of them. Since the application of the rules cannot become standardized, but each new picture needs, as it were, a somewhat new invention of combinations, There is no possibility to transfer the rules in a simple way, one has to become acquainted with the whole structure of rules and to learn how to weigh them from case to case, i.e. transformation needs rules plus much routine.

Not only, therefore, were there many rules of transformation to learn, they should also be applied flexibly, as circumstances demanded and not automatically. To do so required experience, but in doing so a greater variety of work resulted. In this respect, the efforts of those who sought to capitalize on Isotype 'without taking the trouble to master the more fundamental principles' (Kleinschmidt) frequently lacked variety and interest, as well as rigour and clarity.

That Modley himself placed relatively little emphasis on the role of transformation is indicated both by Neurath's criticisms of his work (at the Museum of Science and Industry and subsequently) and by Modley's failure to mention transformation in How to use pictorial statistics.
