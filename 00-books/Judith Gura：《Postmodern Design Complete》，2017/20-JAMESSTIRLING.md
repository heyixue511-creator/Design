Among those structures cited as the “first" example of postmodern architecture is the Engineering Building at the University of Leicester (i963), a cluster of brick-faced geometric solids that pre-dates even the iconic Vanna Venturi House. Though visually more akin to Brutalist structures than postmodernist ones, it reveals a commitment to move beyond the limits of International Style on the part of James Stirling, who designed it with his then partner James Gowan. One of the most forward-thinking British architects of the postwar years, Stirling resisted being labeled a postmodernist, rejecting the style's undue focus on surface. In an impressive career marked by major public and institutional commissions, he was responsible for some of the United Kingdom's most significant late twentieth-century works. Though many of them incorporated elements of the postmodern vocabulary, he remained uncommitted to any specific style. Despite his significance, Stirling's name is far less known than those of many of his contemporaries Stirling was born in Glasgow, Scotland, and interrupted his schooling to serve in the armed forces during the Second World War, returning to study architecture at the University of Liverpool (95o). where he was schooled in the traditional Beaux Arts aesthetic. Moving to London in 1956, he began a successful partnership,with Gowan that lasted seven years and was influential in reawakening interest in concrete and brick as modern building materials. During the next eight years Stirling worked independently, producing muscular, Brutalist-influenced structures in concrete and terracotta tile, including buildings at Cambridge and Oxford that were critically praised despite, in the latter case, delays and problems in construction. After that, Stirling did little work in the United Kingdom until 1971, when he formed a partnership with Michael Wilford\*, who had worked with him since 1956, and began to win commissions for major projects both there and abroad. In two decades, the firm James Stirling Michael Wilford produced an impressive body of work that included major museums, galleries, libraries, and theaters. The most admired among them include the Arthur M. Sackler Museum at Harvard University (i985) and the Clore Gallery (i987) addition to London's Tate Britain, which adopted symbolic elements drawn from the classical vocabulary. Stirling's last completed work, the office building No i Poultry (designed 1985, completed i997, and in 2o16 became the youngest Grade Il\* listed building in the country), is most distinctive for its trapezoidal shape, encased in pink-and-yellow striped limestone. Revealing a postmodern penchant for color, it reprises the multihued exterior of the Neue Staatsgalerie. Stirling taught for many years at Yale University and at several schools in Europe. In ı99ı he was awarded the Pritzker Prize, and after his death the United Kingdom's most prestigious annual architecture award was renamed for him, In 2oi, two exhibitions at Yale—one in the School of Architecture and one at the Yale Center for British Art—featured lecture notes, models, and drawings from his archive, and traveled to London, Stuttgart, and Montreal, opening a window onto his working methodology and igniting interest in an exceptional talent.
