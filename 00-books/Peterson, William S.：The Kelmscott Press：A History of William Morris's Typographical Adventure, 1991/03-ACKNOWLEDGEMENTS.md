# ACKNOWLEDGEMENTS ![](images/225eed7d9cf00cd13bc5714ccb0c3d8799c6db018b57810d25ab616e5cd019ff.jpg) HIS book began alnost accidentally in 1978 when I devoted a summer at the Library of Congress to poring over nineteenth-century English printers' trade magazines, trying to discover what they had to say about bookdesign. My quest was largely fruitless, because Victorian printers seemed inarticulate on that subject, but as I turned the pages and reflected upon the typography of the last century, I frequently stumbled upon the name of William Morris. At first I resisted the lure of Morris, with his enormous reputation among bibliophiles, since it appeared selfevident that there could be nothing new to say about the Kelmscott Press, for I was aware that it had been discussed by almost every English-speaking printing historian of this century. Eventually, however, I realized that there was no avoiding Morris — who seems to many of us the most significant figure in modern book typography - and I then discovered, to my great pleasure, that much fresh evidence about the Kelmscott Press, unavailable to earlier scholars, was now to be found in public and private collections. Only gradually did I grasp the larger cultural significance of Morris's little typographical adventure' and understand that the meaning of this episode extended far beyond the boundaries of printing history. That moment of illumination came to me a decade ago, and since then I have had the pleasure of examining the handsome books of the Kelnıscott Press in many British and American libraries, of studying a good deal of manuscript material relating to the history of the Press, and of receiving innumerable courtesies from libraries and collectors. I am keenly aware of the inadequacy of merely supplying simple, undifferentiated lists of institutions and persons who have given me assistance along the way, yet I could not record here all the nuances of generosity and friendship without writing a memoir of that decade. I feel confident, however, that many of the persons mentioned will recognize the warm memories that lie behind this litany of names. Without several fellowships and travel grants, it would have been impossible for me to complete my research. They were supplied by the American Philosophical Society, the Division of Arts and Hunanities of the University of Maryland, the John Simon Guggenheim Menorial Foundation, and the National Endowment for the Humanities. Those who have given me permission to quote unpublished documents and to reproduce illustrations are cited elsewhere in this book, but I must register my special indebtedness to the Society of Antiquaries (London), the owner of the William Morris copyright. John Dreyfus, Joseph R. Dunlap, and Julia Markus read drafts of the book and offered stimulating criticisms, from which I hope I benefited. My wife Eileen, my daughter Heather, and my son Glenn have cheerfully tolerated my prolonged preoccupation with the Kelmscott Press and have given me much love and support. At an early stage in the research Heather also helped in tracking down books at the Library of Congress.
