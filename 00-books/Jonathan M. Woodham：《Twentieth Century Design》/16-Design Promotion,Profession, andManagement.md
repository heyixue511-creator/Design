# Design Promotion, Profession, and Management The nineteenth and early twentieth centuries had seen the proliferation of many societies which sought to promote design as a significant activity, whether from a social, cultural, political, or economic point of view [114]. These included such diverse bodies as the Swedish Society ofIndustrial Design (Svenska Slödförinengen), which had been established in 1845to encourage and promote handicraft in industry, the Finnish Society of Crafts and Design of 1879, the Art Workers' Guild and the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society, founded in Britain in 1884 and 1888 respectively, the Hungarian Decorative Art Society (Magyar Iparmüvészeti Társulat) of 1885, the Polish Applied Arts Society of 19o1, the Netherlands Association for Crafts and Industrial Art (Nederlandsche Vereeniging voor Ambachts en Nijverheidskunst), the Danish Society of Arts and Crafts and Industrial Design of 1907, and the National Alliance of Art and Industry in the United States of 1912. The more significant impact inGermany of the activities of the Deutscher Werkbund in the twenty-five years following its foundation in 1907 has already been discussed in Chapters 1 and 2. Like other similar less influential bodies formed in its wake, such as the British Design and Industries Association (DIA) founded in 1915, it sought to achieve recognition of the economic significance of design in manufacturing industry and thus indirectly helped to enhance the professional status of the designer. The long-term difficulties inherent in efforts to achieve recognition of the design profession in manufacturing industry can be seen to reside partially in the conflict between notions of the individual artistic personality and the economic and technological realities of manufacturing industry. Indeed, for much of the twentieth century there has been considerable reservation about designers in manufacturing circles. Several instances of such suspicion early in the century can be detected in the debates about design and standardization in industry. But there was also considerable disagreement amongst designers and their propagandists themselves, centering on opposition of individuality and standardization, subjectivity and objectivity, and artistic licence and social responsibility. Although these rifts have been accorded a high historicalprofile on account of the dominance of personality-centred design-historical writing, resonances of such concerns were felt in many countries in the boardrooms of everyday manufacturing industry, where the spectre of the artistic personality, oblivious of the realities of production technology, consumer preferences and market strategies, haunted the thoughts of many industrialists and businessmen. In Britain, for example, the Council for Art and Industry's 1937 report on Design and the Designer in British Industry stated that a key ‘difficulty which has to be overcome is the·existence of a mutual distrust between artists and manufacturers'.1 Design-historical writing has often obscured much of the wider picture of the professional status of the designer and the role of design in industry through its generalfocus on successful partnerships between designers and industry: attention is drawn to Sweden, for example, whereWilhelm Kåge worked as Art Director for the ceramics manufacturer Gustavsberg, and Simon Gate and Edward Hald helped to establish the artistic reputation of the glass manufacturer Orrefors; to Italy, where Marcello Nizzoli designed for Olivetti and Gio Ponti for the ceramics manufacturer Richard-Ginori and the furniture maker Fontana; and to the United States, where the first generation of industrial designers such as Raymond Loewy, Henry Dreyfuss, and Walter Dorwin Teague worked for large, well-known corporate clients. However, the widely-felt uncertainty of the connotations of terms commonly usedin the interwar years such ascommercial art' or graphic design',‘industrial art'or‘industrial design'reflected the inability of designers to establish a clearcut professional identity or status. Indeed, the use of such terms can lend insights into the changing politics of professional validation. For example, the Chartered Society of Designers (CSD) in London, the leading professional organization for designers in Britain, began life in 193o as the Society of Industrial Artists (SIA) and despite a radical reorganization at the end of the Second World War, did not actually incorporate the word design' into its title until 1965, when it became the Societyof IndustrialArtists and Designers. The last change of title, to Chartered Society of Designers, reflected the continuing drive for more widespread professional recognition by the business and manufacturing community: the omission of the word 'Artists', together with any notion of the idiosyncracies that such a term might still have been thought to imply, was perhaps as significant as the word Chartered', with all of its professional connotations. ## The design profession in interwar Britain The Society of Artists (SIA) was established with two distinct aims, the first of which was to establish thè profession of the Designer on a sounder basis by forming a controlling authority to advance and protect the interests of all engaged in the production of Design for Industry, Publishing and Advertising. The second purpose is to assemble the resources of Design as a vital factor in British Industry and so to assist the advancement of British Trade, both at home and abroad.² The founding of the SIA appeared to presage a flurry of official design activity in Britain in succeeding years: a Board of Trade Committee under the Chairmanship of Lord Gorell was appointed in the following year, 1931, to report on Art and Industry;³ late in 1933 the Council for Art and Industry was established under the auspices of the Board of Trade, making its mark through the publication of a number of designcentred reports (including Design and the Designer of 1937), the organization of exhibitions, liaison with industry, and involvement in design education; in 1936 a National Register of Industrial Art Designers was established, and the Royal Society of Arts appointed ten members to act as a new select faculty of Designers for Industry (upgraded by the Home Office to Royal Designers for Industry (RDI) in the following year). It was also a decade which heralded a number of BBCradio programmes devoted to design matters, saw the publication of Herbert Read's celebrated pro-modernist text, Art and Industry (1934),4 and Nikolaus Pevsner's Pioneers of the Modern Movement (1936)⁵ and An Enquiry into Industrial Art in England (1937),6 as well as the mounting of a number of widely reported exhibitions7 including the large-scale British Art in Industry'show at the Royal Academy, with 2,223 exhibits on show. The Design and Industries Association (DIA) was also active at the time in its promotion of the design cause, publishing its own magazines, newsletters, books, and other design propaganda, as wellas organizing public exhibitions. But, as has been suggested in Britain and Modernism' in Chapter 2, such apparently clear-cut evidence, though cited by many historians of British design,8 can be misleading in its portrayal of a real sea change in terms of recognition of the designer in industry. The long history of British design has often embraced the establishment of many official design-related committees, particularly in times of economic difficulty, when the government or its agencies wish to be seen to be working towards improving the position. An early instance of this was the Select Committee on Art and Manufactures which was appointed in 1836 in the light of worries about increasingly strong competition from foreign manufacturers. Almost 15o years later, in not dissimilar circumstances, and indicating in some ways how little the picture had really changed, the Department of Education and Science and the Design Council commissioned a report on The Industrial Design Requirements of Industry which came to the conclusion that foreign companies generally value more highly the contribution made by design to market success, they spend more money on industrial design and give the designer considerably more say in the final selection of the design for manufacture. As a result they appear to attract and motivate designers who are generally very competent and highly creative.9 The DIA has left a considerable legacy for historians in its Yearbooks, magazines, and the prolific writings of its key members. None the less, its influence should not be overestimated: its attitude to industrial design in the 192os was ambivalent, its membership was small by comparison with the Deutscher Werkbund(in 193o Werkbund membership stood at 3,ooo and the DIA's at 82o), and its magazines had an extremely difficult time in the difficult economic climate of the 193os. Design in Industry launched in 1932 lasted for only two issues, the lifespan of Trend in Everyday Things of 1936 was equally brief, with only Design for Today lasting for any length of time, between 1933 and 1935. Similarly, although the state-funded Council for Art and Industry, and its predecessor the British Institute ofIndustrial Art (founded in 192o), may be researched easily through their published reports and the papers lodged in the Public Record Office at Kew, their contemporary impact is more difficult to evaluate. The Design and the Designer in Industry report of 1937, claimed by one British historian to be ‘by far the most influential'10of the Council for Art and Industry's publications, may well have been read in official circles, but the publisher, His Majesty's Stationery Office, was concerned a year after issue that only 1,594 of its 5,000 print run had been purchased. However, the interwar years saw the emergence of a small number of significant advertising agencies, such as S.H. Benson (established in 1893) and W. S. Crawford (established in 1914). Ashley Havinden joined Crawfords in the early 192os, becoming director of art and design in 1929, and did much to gain recognition for the firm as one of the leaders of the field in Britain. Multidisciplinary design consultancies along the lines of the American model, as discussed in Chapter 3, were slow to emerge in Britain. Early instances included the Bassett-Gray Group of Artists and Writers which became the Industrial Design Partnership (1935-40). The founders were Milner Gray, Misha Black, and Walter Landor: Gray and Black were significantly involved in the establishment of the Design Research Unit (DRU) in 1943, a consultancy aimed at providing the necessary design expertise for postwar industry; Landor founded the internationally renowned consultancy, Landor Associates, in San Francisco in 1941 after emigrating to the United States in the previous year. None the less the American model of industrial design consultancy did emerge on the British stage with the opening of the London office of Raymond Loewy.This was launched in London in 1936 under the management of Carl Otto, who had worked at. General Motors and with Bel Geddes, and employed Beresford Evans and Douglas Scott as designers. Before its closure in 1939 its clients includedAllied Ironfounders, Electrolux, General ElectricCompany, and Rootes, the motor manufacturers. ## The design profession: fresh opportunities after 1945 The post-1945 period saw many significant shifts in design practice and approach: fresh attitudes were engendered by the implications of changing technologies, which were in turn stimulated by widespread European and American involvement in the Second World War; by an increasing reconsideration of the extent to which the methods and approaches adopted by designers in the interwar years were still appropriate for postwar needs; and by the growing perception indesign circles that there was a fresh opportunity to establish in business circles a clearer perception of the importance, or professional status, of the designer than hadgenerally been the case in the 192os and 193os.11 There was a clear move away from faith in the primacy of intuition and individuality towards the recognition that the increasingly complex problems of an increasingly technologically-dependent society could only be solved by a coalition of a range of skills and expertise. Ironically, the individual celebrity status which a number of the emergent breed of industrial designers in the United States had sought to cultivate in the 193os, inorder to gain both public recognition and commercial advantage, tended to reinforce doubts in business circles about individual designers'abilities to grasp the wider manufacturing, marketing, advertising, retailing, social, and economic implications of product development. The principles of teamwork upon which the larger American practices were in fact organized pointed towards the basis upon which many postwar consultancies were to be organized. This notion of teamwork had been strengthened by the wartime experience of many designers who worked alongside engineers and scientists in the war effort, gaining a more complete understanding ofnew materials and processes. Examples of this multidisciplinary approach may be seen in consultancies such as the Japanese GK Industrial Design Associates which, from its modest beginnings in 1957, has grown to embrace product design, communication design, architectural and environmental design, research, planning and development, and design studies; Pentagram, founded in London in 1972 from a range of different types of expertise capable of catering for the complex demands of large corporations which were placing an increasing premium on design, was similarly able to deal with a broad range of commissions. Other examples include Total Design, founded in the Netherlands in 1963, Minale Tattersfield, founded in Britain in 1964, and Plan Créatif, founded in Paris in 1985. ## State initiatives in design promotion The potential of design as a means of gaining a market edge was acknowledged by a number of governments in their establishment of official design bodies to promote its value to industry and the public. The most significant of these was the Council of Industrial Design (COID) established in London in 1944 on an initial grant of £55,ooo, ready to assist in the anticipatedpostwar export drive. Its specific aims were to promote good design in industry; to disseminate design advice and information to manufacturers, government departments, and others; to organize design exhibitions and advise on design education and training; and to educate the public in the importance of good design. Amongst its most important early initiatives was the organization of the 1946 Britain Can Make It Exhibition. Providing a sweeping vista ofthe benefits that design could provide for the home, work, and leisure this large-scale exhibition in theVictoria and Albert Museum attracted 1,432,369 visitors and included a display designed by Misha Black of the DRU entitled ‘What Industrial Design Means'. Centred on the problem of designing an egg-cup for mass-production, Black showed how the industrial designer consulted with management, engineers, and salesmen in order to determine such matters as proportion, practicality, colour, and decoration, and how he needed to have an understanding of materials and mass-production technology. As well as advising industry through its industrial officers and library and information service, the COID organized regional conferences and exhibitions, furnished show houses, commissioned reports, and published books and, from 1949 onwards, its own magazine, Design. It has undergone many changes of fortune since then, often due to wider economic problems which at some times have threatened its grant-in-aid from government and at others have enhanced its profile, aswith the widely publicized design initiatives of the Conservative government under Mrs Thatcher in the 198os. The COID established a Design Centrein central London in 1956, an information centre and showroomfor exhibitions of well-designed goods. During the 196os its centre of gravity shifted away from consumer goods towards engineering, and in 1972 it was renamed the Design Council in recognition of its aim‘to wed engineering and industrial design in one body dedicated to improving design throughout all sections of British industry'. In 1994 the Design Council was dramatically cut in size, resulting in the closure of the Design Centre and all regionaloffices, this leading to a complete re-appraisal of its role.12 Similar state-funded organizations were established in a number of other countries in the postwar years. In Poland the Buiro Nadzoru Estetyki Producji (BNEP/Office for the Supervision of Aesthetic Production) was established in 1947 within the Ministry of Culture and Art under the directorship of Wanda Telakowska. Its aim was to invigorate the design profession in the immediate postwar years, to improve standards of design in industry through the provision of models and prototypes, and to promote greater aesthetic awareness in everyday life. However, its ambitions were soon thwarted by the Stalinist reorientation of the State of the late 194os.13 Later, in 196o, the Council of Design and Aesthetics ofIndustrial Production was established to promote industrial design activity although its effective impact was very limited. Elsewhere in eastern Europe, state initiatives were equally problematic. In the USSR, VNIITE(the All-Union Scientific Research Institute for Technical Aesthetics) was set up under the directorship of Yuri Soloviev in 1962, but its subsequent achievements were considerably constrained by high levels of official bureaucracy and planning.14 Its greatest strengths lay in ergonomics and engineering design. In West Germany, the German Parliament voted in 1951 to establish a Rat für Formgebung (Design Council) which was inaugurated in 1953 to further the understanding of design in manufacture. Although it received attention in the Milan Triennali it was not highly effective in the 196os and 197os, but with support from the Ministry of Economic Affairs it has played a continuing role in promoting greater awareness of design in German industry, a task of considerably greater complexity in the wake of the unification of the FDR and GDR. In France, the picture of state support for design has been more ambivalent, although the state has provided architects and designers with many high-profile commissions which during the 198os included furniture at the Elysée Palace, furnishing of the French Embassy in Washington, the interior design of the Musée d'Orsay, and redecoration work at the Louvre and the Ministry of Finance. The Centre de Création Industrielle (CCI), first established in 1969, was funded by the Ministry of Culture from 1973 in order to promote the cultural dimensions of French design. In 1975 the Ministry of Industrial and Scientific Development set up the Conseil Superieur de la Création Esthétique Industrielle, to complement in economic terms theCCI's cultural role, but following widespread lack of interest and support, as well as antipathy to any notions of intervention from the industrial sector, was soon wound up. Other European initiatives include the commissioning in 196o by the Irish Government's Exports Board of a panel of Scandinavian design experts to report on potential design strategies that the Republic might adopt.15 Their deliberations may be seen to have resulted in the Government's establishment of the Kilkenny Design Workshops (KDW) in1965. In Singapore, the Singapore Trade Development Board has included design promotion amongst its activities since 1984 and, following the British Design Council's model of the early 198os, paid for up to 75 per cent of a design consultancy's fees for firms using such expertise for the first time. ## Professional design organizations From the 194os onwards there has been a significant increase in the number of design organizations, reflecting the growing number of professionals in the field as well as their perceived need to campaign collectively for greater recognition of their value to business, commerce, and society. In the United States the Society of Industrial Designers was established in New York in 1944, initially with fourteen members (including Loewy, Bel Geddes, Teague, Deskey, Van Doren, and Russel Wright) but growing to almost 1oo members by 1949. It aimed to improve the vocation of industrial design, by providing the mechanism through which the members of the profession can agree and take action on every type of common problem' as well as to promote better standards of industrial design education and toimprove public and industrial understanding of their work. In order to provide a showcase for many examples of their members' work from 1949 onwards it produced an annual publication entitled US Industrial Design. In 1955 the organization changed its name to the American Society of Industrial Designers, merging in 1965 with two other bodies to become the Industrial Designers Society of America (IDSA). According to an article in the American business magazine Fortune,16 by the late 195os there were about 3oo design consultancies in the United States, a fact that also reflected the growing number of in-house design departments in manufacturing industry. Many of them increasingly moved into the broader arena of design management rather than dealing with products or graphic design in isolation. Many of these changes can be followed in the pages of Industrial Design which, founded in 1954, was the leading magazine for design professionals in the United States; in 198o it was retitled ID Magazine of Industrial Design. In Italy in the 195os the names of designers were important selling tools, but their role in industry was generally much more concerned with product aesthetics than with the wider concerns occupying American and, to an increasing extent, British consultancies. In 1956 the Italian Association for Industrial Designers (ADI) was founded and many of their concernscan be discerned in the magazine Stile Industria which was launched in 1954 and edited by Alberto Roselli. Published in Italian and English, it was an important vehicle for growing international interest in the theory and practice of Italian design, although it closed in the difficult economic climate of 1963. In 1951 the Japan Advertising Artists Club was founded, followed by the Japanese Industrial Designers Association (JIDA) which was founded in the following year with 25 members. It has grown considerably since then, in tandem with the growing recognition of design in manufacturing industry discussed in Chapter 5, with 6or full members in 1985 (284 being employed by companies, 258 being freelance, and 59 drawn from education). It also published its own magazine Industrial Design, which by 1985 had about 3,ooo subscribers, although there were only about twenty subscribing from abroad. Throughout the 195os and 196os many other national design associations were formed¹7 and there was an increased awareness of the wider international climate, stimulated by the growing number of professional design magazines and the holding of international conferences, as at Aspen, Colorado, from the early 195os. The International Council of Societies of Industrial Design (ICSID) was established in 1957, mounting conferences biennially at various locations around the world, followed in 1963 by the International Council of Graphic Design Associations (ICOGRADA). Designers of many nationalities increasingly shared many concerns, and in 1981 ICOGRADA, ICSID and IFI (the International Federation of Interior Designers) held a joint conference in Helsinki, examining design issues on a world scale.
