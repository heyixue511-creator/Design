Her work is the outcome of a lifelong study of the Russian avant-garde. Architectural historians in both Moscow and St Petersburg have been very aware of the author's activities for at least twenty-five years. She has been regularly present at the main events connected with the investigation of twentiethcentury architecture – conferences, exhibitions and seminars. During her frequent journeys to Russia, Catherine Cooke has always been on the look-out for the latest publications, gathering materials from state and private archives, and in pursuit of books, journals, leaflets and everything that appeared in architectural publications during the 192os and 193os. Catherine Cooke's research began in the 196os, at much the same time as a revival of interest in constructivist architecture was taking place in Russia. Though many of the famous architects and architectural theorists of the 192os were still alive, things were not easy. The destruction of the avant-garde that took place during the Stalinist reform of art and led to the establishment of socalled socialist-realist aesthetics had been total and profound. Moreover Stalinist neo-classicism was based on the traditions of nineteenth-century historicism, still strong in Russia at that time. Between the late 193os and the mid 195os a totally different architectural culture emerged that was very largely opposed to avant-garde ways of thinking. This kind of retardataire neo-classical mentality not only obstructed any real revival of constructivism, but also barred the way to research on the avant-garde for several generations of architectural historians in Moscow and St Petersburg. That is why the efforts of foreign scholars were so important, especially when they were working, as Cooke was, with Russians who were trying to preserve the memory of the 192os. During the 197os and 198os attitudes towards the avant-garde changed somewhat slowly in Russia. Nevertheless, work of great importance was done, notably the collecting of designs and manifesto texts by famous architects. S. O. Khan- Magomedov and V. F. Khazanova played the leading role in this. The re-publication of documents of architectural theory also began. It happened very modestly at first (during the Soviet years some were marked secret’ or for office use only’), but later on a wide scale, during the greater freedom of the late Brezhnev period and then the Gorbachev years. After perestroika it might be supposed that the climate in Russia would favour the study of the avant-garde, but that wasn't and still isn't the case. Writing about constructivism is of course 'allowed'; and people can publish or exhibit what they like about the subject. But the economic crises of the past few years have caused a serious decline in scholarship, notably in such disciplines as architectural history. The rate of publication has greatly diminished. Yet this is not the main reason for the slow-down in research into the Russian avant-garde. This is just the point that Catherine Cooke underlines in her book, by translating many important texts by the leading figures of Russian architecture in the 192os. The author correctly says that her work is a book about ideas – ideas that came out of a specific period and from specific people, but which still have extraordinary relevance seventy years later’. She cites original texts in order to try and recreate the voices of the artists and architects', their manifestos and commentaries on their own activities. Mostly she does so chronologically and progressively, step by step, so that we get a good, solidly based and detailed history. Where necessary, she cuts through the material thematically, so that the issues emerge with greater clarity and in a broader context. Catherine Cooke begins her study with the precursors of the Modern Movement, discussing nineteenth-century Russian rationalism and its links with Europe, especially France. She next stresses the importance of the Art Nouveau period for what was to happen in the 192os, and then turns her attention to the new art institutions founded in St Petersburg and Moscow just before the 1917 Revolution. Reasonably enough, the author then moves to the question of choice between the avant-garde and tradition that typified the early Bolshevik years. Then comes the problem of what Soviet architecture the architecture of the New People's State', ought to be. The main groupings and associations of the avant-garde are all described in detail, along with their manifestos and important designs; the many, well-chosen illustrations are a great help here. They are organized either according to the various, like-minded architectural groupings, or according to outstanding individuals like Moisei Ginsburg, Alexander Nikolsky (a less-known but brilliant Leningrad architect), Andrei Burov, Ivan Nicolaev (famous for the dom-kommuna or 'house commune' and for modern-style factories), and then such giants as Konstantin Melnikov, Ilya Golosov (authors of the most interesting workers' clubs) or the Vesnin brothers, the main figures in constructivist ideology. Architects who began their work before the Revolution as historicists, then became involved in the Modern Movement, and later became leaders in Stalinist neo-classicism are naturally not forgotten, like Alexei Shchushev or Ivan Zholtovsky. Anthony Jackson's Reconstructing Architecture presents itself as an enquiry into the architect's world. His central theme is that architecture today is élitist and out of touch with those for whom it is made. Their education and practice is designed to perpetuate a set of myths and a culture which are based on an art historical perspective more concerned with schools and styles than with everyday values.
