The origins of Victor Hasselblad AB can be traced back to an earlier wholesale and distribution company, which was established in 1841 byVictor Hasselblad's(1906-1978)grandfather. This firm sold all kinds of products – musical instruments, knives, perfume, toys, oil lamps, buttons, soap, crinolines, pens, and water closets – and by the 1870s was retailing lanternes magiques imported from France and various photographic and stereoscopic pictures. Later, the company began importing photographic equipment and in 1893 it issued the first Swedish camera catalogue, which not only included the Kodak No. 1 and No. 5 cameras, but also a camera marketed under the name of HasselbladSwedish Express. The company eventually became the official agent for Kodak in Sweden and its founder's son, Victor, became a keen amateur photographer. In turn, his son Victor also developed an interest in photography, which he combined with his love of ornithology to become a pioneer bird photographer. In 1925, he undertook a period of study at Kodak in Rochester, New York, and returned with a somewhat cumbersome Graflex, which was then the best camera available for bird photography. Although he later used a more compact 35mm Leica, he was still not entirely satisfied with the results. In 1933, he published a book entitled, Flyttfågelstråk (The Passage of Migratory Birds) that was illustrated with his own photographs, but by this time he was already thinking about creating an entirely new camera. During the SecondWorld War, a German bomber containing a reconnaissance camera was shot down over Swedish territory. When theSwedish Air Force took the camera to Victor Hasselblad to see if he could produce a replica, he reputedly said, “No, but I can make a better one!" The resulting handheld military aerial camera, the HK7, was the first single-lens reflex camera in 6x6 format with exchangeable film cartridges, and was manufactured for the Swedish Air Force from 1941 to 1945. This undertaking proved an invaluable experience, particularly in terms of what Hasselblad learnt about the construction of shutters for use in very cold temperatures, the benefits of motorized camera operation and interchangeable cassette systems, and the value of high-resolution optics that could be speedily interchanged. Even before the hostilities of war had ended, Victor Hasselblad began adapting the HK7 for civilian use. To this end he had the foresight to recruit the leading Swedish industrial designer Sixten Sason to work on the project. Using wooden prototypes, Sason developed a highly compact housing for the camera that had a simplified and easy-to-use arrangement of operational buttons. The resulting 1600F, which shared many of the features of the earlier HK7 camera, was launched in New York in 1948 where it met with huge enthusiasm – U.S. Camera magazine commented, “With few reservations we have found this to be the dream camera." The 1600F was an ergonomic masterpiece and became the blueprint for all subsequent Hassel blad cameras. In 1962, when NASA engineers were sketching what they thought would be the ideal space-camera, the astronaut Walter Schirra remarked, “That looks just like my Hasselblad!" A Hasselblad 500C was duly purchased and modified by NASA engineers to fit their exacting specifications. Throughout Scandinavia, he is best known for his thousands of epigrammatic poems, which he referred to as “grooks” and published under the pseudonym, “Kumbel”. He also wrote extensively on the relationship between the arts and sciences. While working as a consultant on a town-planning project in 1959 that encompassed the design of Sergels Square in Stockholm, he devised the concept of the “superellipse” as a way of solving traffic problems. Halfway between an oval and a rectangle, the “superellipse” form was repeated in his product, lighting and furniture designs, such asthe Superegg box (1966) for Georg Jensen and theSuperellipse table (1964), which he co-designed with Bruno Mathsson for Fritz Hansen. For his achievements as an inventor and his work in the sciences Hein was awarded the Alexander Graham Bell Prize in 1968, and an honorary doctorate from Yale University in 1972. His pioneering work as a designer was also recognized when he was awarded the prestigious ID Prize and the German “Die gute Industrieform” Award in 1971. Hein's product designs can be seen as an outcome of the continuous search for the “ideal” form – a defining characteristic of much Scandinavian design. Unlike most other practitioners, however, Hein used mathematics as the basis of his solutions rather than historic precedent. He was a wry commentator on design, who summarized his definition of good design in a poem entitled Form and Content: “Most design is sheer disaster/hiding what one doesn't master.
