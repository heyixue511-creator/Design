After completing her studies, she began working at the Orrefors glassworks in 1947. Around this time the older and more established glass designers at Orrefors were pioneering various new techniques, such as Sven Palmqvist's Ravenna technique and Edvin Öhrström's Arie/ technique, which brought a new kind of artistry to Swedish glass production. Lundin rejected this new aesthetic and technical approach, preferring instead to use both simple forms and techniques. Her resulting designs, such as the Timglas(Hour-glass) vase(1954) and the well-known Äpple (Apple) vase (1955), were remarkable for their pure forms and informal elegance. The Äpple vase is essentially a large bubble of glass that emphasizes the inherent qualities of the material – transparency, lightness and fragility. In 1954 Lundin was awarded the prestigious Lunning Prize and the following year exhibited her glassware at the landmark “H55” exhibition in Helsingborg. The 1950s were a period of relative prosperitythat could financially support the proliferation of objects of high-quality craftsmanship. As a result, designers such as Lundin became widely acclaimed for their one-offstudio pieces. Her work was often engraved with abstract “jottings” that provided a sense of informality and artistry. During the 1960s, however, Lundin and other designers workingoutside the industrial process suffered from the economic downturn, as manufacturers looked for ways ofreducingcosts. As a decorative artist rather than an industrial designer, Lundin eventually left Orrefors in 1971 – times had moved on and studio craftsmanship was no longer considered as important as it had once been. Ironically, however, Lundin was a master of simplicity – a crucial aspect of design for industrial production – but she never abandoned her production of studio pieces in favour of objects for mass manufacture. Following the completion of his studies in 1937,he worked as a freelance designer and displayed his first glassware products at the1942“Danske Kunsthåndværk”(Danish Arts & Crafts) exhibition heldin Stockholm. This early work was distinguished by the use of simple classical forms and colourless glass. In 1946, Lütken succeeded Jacob Bang as art director and chief designer at the Holmegaard and Kastrup glassworks, and initially he perpetuated the classical modern aesthetic of his predecessor. After visiting the (X) Milan Triennale exhibition in 1954 on a study tour of Italy, however, Lütken pioneered a new aesthetic direction for both companies by introducing soft colours and free-flowing forms into his work. The resulting designs, such as his well-known Provence bowls (c. 1956), Beak vases (1951)and Martini jug (1957), epitomized the fluid and gentle organic forms that came to dominate Scandinavian Modern design during the post-war years. Throughout the early 1960s, Lütken experimented with clear and coloured glass in an effort to achieve dramatic swirling colour combinations. In 1969, he introduced his innovative Lava glassware, which was blown into wet clay moulds in such a way that the impurities were picked up and retained so as to produce a then-fashionable rustic finish. During the 1970s, Lütken's work became increasingly experimental, as his multi-coloured egg-shaped glass sculpture Form (1970) demonstrated. Later in 1978, he introduced his Vintergæk series, which was characterized by the combination of colourless glass flecked with green and white opaline glass. Lütken stated that glassmaking demanded an “intimate, imperative and exciting collaboration based on mutual acquaintanceship between designer and glassmaker” and believed that “the more the finished piece of glass tells us about this process and bears witness to it through the vigour of its form, the better”. During his long career at Holmegaard, Lütken redefined the aesthetic of the company's output and produced a number of unique experimental pieces that had much influence on glassware intended for serial production.
