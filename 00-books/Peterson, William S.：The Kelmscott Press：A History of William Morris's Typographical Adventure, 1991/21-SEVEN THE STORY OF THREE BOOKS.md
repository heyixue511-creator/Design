# SEVEN THE STORY OF THREE BOOKS ![](images/93f206298027292bc38148b57da853ba1bade9c143092f5b636a2edc7b5bc18c.jpg) HE Kelmscott Press books, as we have seen already, come in a variety of sizes and forms: some are long (Chaucer, 564 folio pages), some short (Epistola de contemptu mundi, sixteen pages); a few are heavy with ornaments (Godefrey of Boloyne), while others are decorated only with a few small initials and a border on the first text page; a minority are typographically elaborate (Atalanta in Calydon (1894) has three typefaces on the title-page), yet many of the books are almost austerely simple in design. Despite Morris's preference for medieval texts, there is also literary diversity in the Kelmscott books, which reprint works written over a span of more than a thousand years. For these reasons it is difficult, indeed dangerous, to offer broad generalizations about how the books of the Kelmscott Press were produced. The process by which Morris designed and printed the Epistola, for example, was very different from that followed in manufacturing The Well at the World's End, which was in production from 1 892 to 1896 (the Interminable', Morris called it). If we wish to move beyond glib truisms, therefore, we must look carefully at particular instances of Morris the printer at work and try to understand the ordinary, day-to-day problens with which he struggled at the Press. In this chapter we will examine the production history of three Kelmscott books: Morris's Poemns by the Way (1891), Wilfrid Scawen Blunt's The Love-Lyrics & Songs of Proteus (1892), and The Golden Legend (1 892). Because they are all early works of the Press (the second, third, and seventh books issued, respectively), there is an unusually large amount of documentary evidence available, and though unfortunately the Press's ledger does not cover the period when they were printed, it is possible to reconstruct financial information from other sources. These particular books also possess the merit of representing the three major types of literary texts that Morris printed: his own books, the writings of other Englishmen (mainly poets), and medieval works. ## Even though it was the last of these three books to be published, The Golden Legend deserves to be considered first since it was the earliest book planned for the Kelmscott Press. The following notice appeared in the Literary Gossip' column of the Athenaeum on 13 September 189o: Students of English mediaeval literature will be gratified to learn that an exact reprint of“The Golden Legend" as originally set forth by William Caxton is in preparation, under the joint editorship of Mr. William Morris and Mr. F. S. Ellis. If it is found practicable to obtain the use of a copy of the first edition it is proposed to reproduce that text with scrupulous accuracy, supplementing it with a glossary and index. In place of the black letter, to the use of which there are manifold objections, a fount of types newly designed by Mr. Morris after the fashion of those employed by Nicholas Jenson will be substituted. . . . Some time must necessarily elapse . . . before the work can go to press, though it will be proceeded with immediately. The editors have agreed to give their labours gratuitously in consideration of Mr. Quaritch bearing all expenses of production. As the impression will be a limited one, subscribers would do well to send their names to the publisher forthwith.'This announcement, which came from a remarkably well-informed source (probably F. J. Furnivall), constitutes in fact the earliest reference in print to any of the Kelmscott Press books. During the summer of 1 89o Morris, as he was designing his Roman type, pondered the possibility of reprinting one of two Caxton books - either The Golden Legend (1483) or The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye (1474) – which would be published by Bernard Quaritch. With this in mind, he purchased on 21 July 189o a copy of a slightly later edition of The Golden Legend (Wynkyn de Worde, 1 527), and, when Cockerell saw Morris on 14 August, he found that ‘he thinks of reprinting [it] when his new type is all cast' (Cockerell diary). To Ellis, who had offered to edit the book, Morris wrote on 29 August: I did want to weigh between the Golden Legend and the Troy book for reprinting: now I have borrowed a Recueil of the Histories of Troy (the Wynkyn de Worde of course) from Quaritch, and have no doubt that the G.L. is by far the most important book of the two...." (Eventually Morris printed the Troye as well.) What was the nature of The Golden Legend's appeal to Morris? A collection of saints’ lives compiled by Jacobus de Voragine (or Varagine) and originally published under the title Legenda aurea sanctorum, sive Loinbardiea historia (Strasburg, c.1472-4), it struck some Victorian readers who did not share Morris's antiquarian enthusiasms as an improbable choice for the first big book of the Kelnscott Press. In his Menoranda, Bibliographical & Explanatory' which appeared at the end of the third volume of the Kelmscott edition, Ellis offered this justification for reprinting it: Among the books which serve to illustrate the religious life and mode of thought that prevailed in the middle ages, none holds a more important place than the "Legenda Aurea." Compiled and put into form in the latter half of the 13th century, the welcome with which it was received by the clergy & the lettered world is attested by the great number of manuscripts of it which are still extant, while its ready reception by the people generally is evidenced by the fact that within a short time of its appearance it was translated into the vulgar tongue of most of the nations of Europe, frequently with the hagiological preferences of the people of the country for which it was designed' (iii. 1282). Quaritch, always the sly businessman, saw this venture as a means of snaring Morris. You are no doubt aware of the high standing, as a poet, of William Morris,' he remarked to his son on 5 June 1 89o; Ī have sold to him lately several early printed books. I also told hin I should like to publish some future book of his. I am in a fair way of getting such a new book from him; - and this may lead much further afterwards." In early September 189o letters were flying back and forth among Morris, Ellis, and Quaritch as they negotiated a contract for the book. Quaritch was surprised to discover that the only modern reprint of The Golden Legend was an incomplete facsimile published by the Holbein Society in 1878 (a book that Morris owned). Furnivall's Early English Text Society, however, had printed several other Caxton translations during the 187os and 188os, and David Nutt, a London publisher, issued a limited edition of Caxton's Le Morte Darthur between 1889 and 1891; the latter was, like the Kelmscott Golden Legend, an exact reprint, but its typography was undistinguished and two of the three volumes were devoted to learned analyses and bibliographical studies ofthe text. Quaritch, who did not yet fully grasp Morris's new preoccupation with book-design, inquired innocently whether he perhaps intended to make The Golden Legend uniform with Nutt's book.³ My interest in the book is double,' wrote Quaritch to Ellis, first I think every man of culture should be acquainted with the alleged Lives of Saints, and second I am aware of the philological value of all books printed by Caxton.' The odd phrasing of the last clause remind us that Morris at this stage still had only a very indistinct notion of the future Kelmscott Press and was undecided whether to ask another printer to produce the book (with Morris's types, of course); significantly, Quaritch assumed that as publisher he would furnish the paper. By the end of 189o it was clear that Morris would print the book hinself. In selecting the edition he would reprint, Morris displayed less hesitation — it would be Caxton's first version, not the Wynkyn de Worde edition that he owned — but this meant finding a copy that could be borrowed. The Cambridge University Library agreed to lend its Caxton Golden Legend, heavily insured, to Ellis, whose daughter Phyllis (later Mrs Payne) then laboriously transcribed the entire book, so that the volume itself would not have to be touched by the inky hands of Morris's printers. It was an heroic undertaking, for which she never received the public recognition she deserved, and the few surviving scraps of her nanuscript confirm W. H. Bowden's testimony that not one word ofit was illegible. Later her father read the Kelmscott proofs directly against the book. Though the contract with Quaritch referred to Morris and Ellis as co-editors, Morris was only nominally involved in editorial decisions, thus setting the pattern for many later Kelmscott books. Ellis established the text, directing the occasional query to Morris; Morris agreed or disagreed with Ellis's proposals. That is how it always went between them, with much joking on Ellis's side and some blustering on Morris's. But in his concluding Memoranda’ Ellis rather exaggerated Morris's participation: ‘That I express myselfin the first person throughout this notice, and sign it, is only because I am responsible for the statements as to matters of fact which it contains, & which I could not ask Mr. Morris to guarantee with his name, but it should be understood that he has gone carefully over the whole book, and nothing of any importance in the editing has been done without his advice and sanction’ (iii. 285-6). The colophon is less equivocal, listing Ellis as the editor and Morris as the printer. As usual, Ellis worried a good deal about bibliographical and textual matters while the book was in production. He also struggled to persuade Morris to include a glossary and a bibliography of English editions of the book; the glossary appeared in truncated form as A List of Some Obsolete or Little Used Words’, but Morris declined to use the bibliography prepared by Edward Gordon Duff. In September 1892 Ellis wrote to Duff, The difficulty is that he has not the necessary types or the small fount which it would be desirable to use. He said he would get it printed by Whittingham [i.e. the Chiswick Press] as a separate paper to be given with the Golden Legend but whether he will think of it again or not I cannot say – but seeing the many irons he has in the fire I am fearful of its getting forgotten & do not like to bother him further on the subject."7 The real reason for its omission, however, was, as Ellis had admitted in an earlier letter, that the ‘author-printer kicks much against introductions'.8 The colophon of the Kelmscott Press Golden Legend describes it as a reprint ‘in which there is no change from the original, except for the correction of errors of the press, & some few other amendments thought necessary for the understanding of the text'. Ellis's textual alterations in this and other Caxton books he edited for Morris have displeased some twentieth-century specialists, but to assess his work fairly we have to see it within the tradition of energetic amateur scholarship which flourished in Victorian England, particularly among the members of the societies founded by Furnivall. Ellis, though infinitely more scrupulous and careful in his treatment of documents than Furnivall himself was, lived at a time when the editing of historical and literary documents was not yet solely the preserve of professionals as it mainly is today. Ellis's aim in preparing the text of The Golden Legend for the Kelmscott Press was not only to achieve verbal accuracy but also to anticipate the needs of non-specialist readers. Although much has been written about the typography and design of Kelmscott books, historians of the Press have failed to observe that Morris and Ellis were also engaged in a pioneering attempt at intelligent popularization ofliterary works drawn, in some instances at least, from obscure or remote sources. Measured by that standard, Ellis's achievements as an editor are impressive. While Ellis concentrated on the text, Morris began to think about the physical shape of The Golden Legend. Ellis was eager to reproduce the general appearance of Caxton's book, but Morris had misgivings about the wisdom of this, because he recognized that the Roman type he would be using was different in nature from Caxton's Gothic, and perhaps also because he had learned from The Story of Gunnlaug the futility of such mock-quaintness. Of course I should like the reprint to be of the same form as the original if the Roman type can do it, which I doubt, as black letter takes up less room: in any case some kind of folio it will have to be,' he reported to Ellis in September 189o. In one thing I think I differ from you a little, i.e., about the joined letters or queer signs: since our book is to be a reprint, not a fac-simile, I do not think we need reproduce these: indeed I should extend the abbreviations in order to make the book more readable." By December Morris's ideas about the dissimilarities between Roman and Gothic types had been taken a step further: One thing may disappoint you — to wit, that we cannot make a double-column page of it, the page will not be wide enough. For my part, I don't regret it: double column seems to me chiefly fit for black letter, which prints up so close. Jenson did not print even his Pliny in double column. But it is a case of a fortiori in modern printing: because we have no contractions, few tied letters, and we cannot break a word with the same frankness as they could: I mean we can't put whi on one side and ch on the other. This makes the spacing difficult, and a wide page desirable."º When the first delivery of paper arrived from Batchelor early in the following year, Morris concluded that it did not have sufficient sizing and reported to Ellis (11 February 1891) that, since it is only a little lot (9 reams)', he intended to print ‘a little edition of the Glittering Plain on it'. Meanwhile, he said, he would set up a signature of The Golden Legend as an experiment until more paper was available. But I can sympathize with my pardner's anxiety,'Morris commented; ‘and accordingly send him a [trial] page of the G[littering] P[lain], of course full of defects, but on the paper and with the types. I don't know what you will think of it; but I think it precious good." It would be gratifying to know exactly how Morris went about designing a book, but, although he wrote at length about the broad principles of book-design and left us a legacy of fifty-two books at the Kelmscott Press, there is surprisingly little documentation of his actual procedures and techniques. Unlike some designers of our century — Bruce Rogers or Jan Tschichold, say – he seems not to have nade elaborate, precise drawings of the pages he envisioned. Designs of ornaments, types, and lettering for frontispieces we have in abundance from Morris's hand, but not one drawing to show the layout of a Kelmscott page. (There is, in the Bodleian Library, a rough sketch by Morris of the title-page of the Reeves and Turner edition (1 894) of The Well at the World's End,'² but it is not very instructive and is merely patterned after the title-page of the Reeves and Turner Poems by the Way.) Probably this dearth of evidence reflects the way in which Morris worked: he always distrusted excessively detailed designs by an artist, because he felt that they inhibited the freedom of the craftsman who would execute then. No doubt the instructions he gave his compositors at the Kelmscott Press were generally delivered orally, with Morris often at their elbow, and he never hesitated to demand that they produce one trial page after another until he was satisfied. Trial pages of The Golden Legend, which survive in particular abundance,"3 reveal that by the time they were printed (in March 1892) Morris had settled on an approximate page size, $\mathbf { I } \mathbf { I } _ { 4 } ^ { 1 } \times 8 \frac { 1 } { 1 6 }$ inches, and the length of the line of type, 5 inches (though he continued to experiment with the latter), but these dimensions were largely determined by the paper size. Morris was much more preoccupied with the proportions of the margins, on which he made countless small adjustments. Once the general appearance of a page was established and the margins were deemed satisfactory, Morris's compositors began setting the text, usually providing him with galley proofs, though sometimes, as in volumes of poetry, page proofs were pulled immediately. (This was probably unwise, since last-minute alterations frequently shifted lines on to following pages.) Spaces for initials were left blank, and in the proofs Morris specified which initials and borders were to be inserted by writing in their numbers from his ‘bloomer book'. The Golden Legend was by far the largest book issued by the Kelmscott Press in the first two years ofits existence — more than 1,3oo pages printed on a ton and a half of paper¹⁴ — and progress was correspondingly slow. Morris had begun designing initials for it even before the first supply of type arrived in January 1891, and he hoped to ornament the book profusely: I think I shall make some scratch of a border to each life or section,' he wrote in April. 'I want to make it grand." Such a scheme was far beyond the resources of the Press at this early stage, but Morris was able to design four full borders (the first one drawn at Folkestone in April), corner borders, and a large number of six- and ten-line decorated initials. A book of this size strained the capabilities of Morris's small group of printers; to make matters worse, in late April a slab holding fifty pages in galleys collapsed, with the type scattering in terrible confusion on the floor.
