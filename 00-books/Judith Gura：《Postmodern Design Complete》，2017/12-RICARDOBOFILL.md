# RICARDO BOFILL b.1939 BARCELONA ARCHITECT WHO USES CLASSICAL FORMS IN TIMELESS STRUCTURES THAT RECALL ANCIENT WORLDS. RIGHT The Palacio, Espaces d'Abraxas, Marne-la-Vallée, Paris, 1978–83. Some of Postmodernism's most dramatic structures were designed by this Spanish architect, who practiced independently of the movement's mainstream. His work ensured the acceptance of classical iconography as major elements in buildings that also considered contemporary context and incorporated modern materials. Born in Barcelona, Bofill grew up surrounded by the diversions of maverick architect Antoni Gaudí, yet his work is altogether different, drawing instead from a classical vocabulary. Indeed, some of his postmodern era works have the appearance of timeless structures that, save for their modern surroundings, might have been recovered from ancient worlds. He attended architecture school in Barcelona and graduated from the Geneva School of Architecture (i96o). In i963, he established his practice in Barcelona, gathering architects, engineers, planners, a sociologist, writers, film-makers, and philosophers into what became the Taller de Arquitectura. This unusual combination of talents has since been dedicated to a specific challenge: finding new ways to deal with the problems of modern urban space. Over several decades, Bofill's work has developed an increasingly sophisticated personal language, first adopting elements of traditional Catalan architecture, then gradually marrying classical forms and ornamentation with elements of twentieth-century Spain into distinctive forms that are postmodern in their classical references but very spare in ornamentation. In addition to large residential projects, Bofill's office completed urban design and master-planning commissions around the world. His City in the Space, a conceptual housing project, explored his ideas for a livable city, with half the space reserved for community use, circulation, and gardens, and the buildings themselves designed according to a modular system of cubic forms. Walden 7 (i975), outside Barcelona, further implemented his ideas in a fortress-like apartment complex with eighteen buildings clustered around seven courtyards, linked by a system of connecting bridges and balconies that creates an appealing variety of spatial experiences, as well as interior and exterior vistas. Though the exterior is a uniform deep red, the courtyards are relieved with a variety of bright hues. Walden 7 brought Bofill international attention, and further commissions. Beginning in the late 197os, Bofill carried out most of his architectural work in France, designing building projects to meet the country's need to house its growing migrant population in new towns being developed in the aftermath of the Second World War. He became celebrated for large-scale projects like Les Espaces d'Abraxas (i983), outside Paris, a monumental environment with classical details like arches, columns, and pilasters for an effect that was at once historicist and futuristic. Rich in classical detailing, they have the massive appearance of ancient stone structures but were executed with modern technology, in precast concrete—considerably less expensive than the stone they replicated. His approach has been referred to as “radical classicism."i6 Bofll has since translated his distinctive vocabulary with great success to other countries, adapting his classical aesthetic to harmonize with different local cultures. Some designers never have the opportunity to implement their architectural concepts for their own use, but Bofill did just that in perhaps his most widely published project, the transformation of a nineteenth-century cement factory into an extraordinary residence and workplace. La Fábrica proves that even purpose-built architecture can be renovated and transformed for another use rather than destroyed. Purchased in 1973, the eight-building complex (all that remained of thirty original silos) was sensitively restored and revived into an undeniably modern space that retains vestiges of its past in its use of simple industrial materials. The result is a project that reveals its inspiration, but subtly. RIGHT Sketch of residence in Origlio, Switzerland, c. 1982. Mario Botta became an architect by accident. He never liked attending classes, and left secondary school at the age of fifteen to work in an architect's office, where, happily, he discovered the drawing skills that ultimately led him to the profession that has made him internationally famous. Though he is considered a postmodernist, Botta's work in no way resembles that of others given that designation. Said to combine native Swiss reticence with the exuberance of Italy, it avoids explicit historical references, conspicuously declines to play with color, and has none of the provocative elements associated with postmodern design. His buildings are notable for their use of traditional masonry, reflecting the influence of his early mentors Le Corbusier and Louis Kahn. They incorporate muscular, geometric forms, bold black-and-white pattern, and the ingenious manipulation of light and shadow. Yet his designs are clearly rooted in history; his buildings have the presence of classical structures, interpreted with a lightness that is altogether contemporary. Botta was born in the town of Mendrisio in the canton of Ticino, an Italian-speaking region of Switzerland, and most of his architectural projects are in and around nearby Lugano, where he has his office. It is also the location of the architectural studio he joined as a teenager; he trained there for three years, first as draftsman and then apprentice, and completed his first design project, a local parish house. He left in ı96ı to attend the Liceo Artistico art college in Milan, Italy, where he reportedly skipped many of the classes, though he did take the examinations required to graduate. After deciding to commit to a career in architecture, he enrolled in the Istituto Universitario di Architettura (IUA) in Venice. There he had the opportunity to learn from three of the century's most important modernists: he seized the chance to work in Le Corbusier's studio during his first year of study, had Carlo Scarpa as a teacher and thesis adviser, and in his final vear, assisted Louis Kahn on an exhibition and plans for the new Palazzo dei Congressi—although Botta spoke no English and Kahn spoke no Italian. Returning to Switzerland after graduating from IUA, he worked briefly in a Lugano architecture studio and opened his own practice there in 197o. His designs included many single-family houses that explored the relation between man and nature, as well as several important churches that brought him international attention. All Botta's designs begin with rough sketches, which he refines and reworks into their final form. Most of his buildings follow an axial plan—that is, with the elements organized along a given axis, as opposed to being arranged around a central point—and many are enclosed by a circular frame. Yet the spare vocabulary allows for many different variations; his work has a consistency, but no two buildings look alike. Critic Paul Goldberger has called them “self-assured essays in geometry."17 In 1982 Botta began to design furniture and lighting, primarily for Italian firms Alias\* and Artemide\*. His product design is clearly kin to his architecture, using a similar vocabulary. Breaking ranks with the top tier of late twentiethcentury English architects, Terry Farrell became something of a pioneer when he designed the first postmodern building in the United Kingdom. While contemporaries like Richard Rogers, Norman Foster, and Nicholas Grimshaw continued their allegiance to modernist principles, Farrell changed direction to move along the less confining paths charted by early proselytizers of the new style. In his role as a champion of urban planning, he has also been influential in helping to shape government policy on development and planning in London. After graduating from Newcastle University in England in 96o. Farrell received a Master's in urban planning from the University of Pennsylvania, where he met ROBERT VENTURI and DENISE SCOTT BROWN (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") and was taken by their belief in an approach to architecture that embraced both historicism and popular culture. He returned home to partner with Grimshaw in an architectural practice, but left in i98o when he became disenchanted with the established modernist style. Opening his own firm he sought to move from designing housing projects to working with clients willing to pursue a more adventurous path. His earliest success, and one of his most attention-getting commissions, was the TV-am building in London's Camden Town (i982). In addition to its then radical postmodern design, the building marked another milestone—the inauguration of morning television broadcasting in the United Kingdom. The impossible-to-miss structure, converted from an industrial garage, became an ideal example of the “double coding" prescribed by Venturi and CHARLES JENCKS (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design” and “Living with Postmodern Design"). The two-faced exterior had a distinctive striped, serpentine front and contrasting rear facade topped with a row of eleven huge eggcups as architectural ornament— a Pop art fillip suggesting its link to breakfast-time. The interior announced its links to historicism in colorful areas that evoked exotic locales such as Japan, Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Africa. (The building, popularly known as Eggcup House, was recently controversially renovated into far more conventional form.) They reflect the openness to personal expression that Farrell considers a key legacy of Postmodernism.
