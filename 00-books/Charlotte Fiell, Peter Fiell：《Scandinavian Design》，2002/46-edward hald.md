![](images/c3071833fc0da2a7e29434d789db55d0f4c97d30f732a85895da2fa6ca011d77.jpg) ▶Edward Hald, Bollspelande Flickor (Girls Playing Ball) vessel for Orrefors, 1919 # edward hald ## (b. 1883 Stockholm – d. 1980Stockholm, Sweden) ![](images/809e4dc1246db4f3596e83edaeb9322dfb902ec3deb6f876e8620980411304c6.jpg) The Swedish glass and ceramic designer Edward Hald trained in England and Germany from 1903 to 1904 before furthering his studies at the Technische Akademie (Polytechnic) in Dresden from 1904 to 1906. At this latter institute Hald was undoubtedly inspired by the reforming activities of the Dresdner Werkstätten für Handwerkskunst (Dresden Workshops for Arts and Crafts, founded in 1898) and its advocacy of well-designed products for everyday use. In 1907, Hald went on to study painting at the Artists' Studio School in Copenhagen, and from 1908 to 1912 trained under Henri Matisse(1869–1954), whose Fauvist style had a major influence on the decoration of his later designs. Between 1917and 1927 Hald designed a number of ceramic products for Rörstrand, including the earthenware Halda service(1919)with blue rustic-style transferprinted decoration. This tableware, which epitomized the elegant “Swedish Grace” style, was illustrated in Gregor Paulsson's influential 1919 publication, Vackrare Vardagsvara (Good Everyday Goods). In 1917, Hald also began designing modernistic glassware for Orrefors. Like his contemporary, Simon Gate, Hald executed a number of engraved narrative pieces for Orrefors, even if his style was influenced more by trends in contemporary fine art than by historic classical styles. His Bollspelande Flickor (Girls Playing Ball) vessel of 1919, for example, was adorned with simplified expressionistic motifs that drew stylistic references from Henri Matisse's art. In response to the Svenska Slöjdföreningen's (Swedish Society of Craft and Industrial Design) rallying cry for beautiful yet functional wares for everyday living, Hald also designed more utilitarian glassware, which was shown to much acclaim at the 1917 “Home Exhibition” held at the Liljevalchs galleryin Stockholm and was produced by Sandvik (which became an Orrefors subsidiary in 1918) from1920 to 1930. These moulded products made of muted coloured soda glass were notable for their softened traditional forms and simple ribbed or fluted decoration. In the late 1920s, Hald began devising even more rational pressed glass designs that were suitable for large-scale mass production. It was his virtuoso engraved “art” glass pieces, however, such as his exuberant Fyrverkeriskålen (Fireworks) bowl of 1921, that brought Hald the most recognition at the time and led to him being awarded a Grand Prix at the 1925 “Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes” in Paris. While continuing to design for Orrefors and Rörstrand, Hald was also the artistic director of the Karlskrona porcelainfactory from 1924 to 1933. He became managing director of Orrefors in 1933, and although the glassworks experienced cuts in exports and shortages of raw materials during the Second World War, it continued to undertake both technical and artistic experimentation. During this period, Hald developed the Cut Graal and Fish Graa/ cased-glass techniques, while the forms he adopted became softer and simpler. In 1945, Hald was awarded the Prince Eugen Medal for his glasswaredesigns, but two years later he decided to resume painting, while simultaneously remaining a freelance designer for Orrefors until the late 1970s. As one of the leading proponents of Modernism in Sweden, Hald's great skill lay in his ability to impart the high-object integrity of his exclusive craft-based pieces to product designs that were intended for industrial mass production. ![](images/d2161d8dfaed6e73d13d0cd504ed01ba9802f912a9b6ae848ed44d12220bca22.jpg) △ Edward Hald, Fish Graal vases for Orrefors, 1937 Frida Hansen, design for Poppy tapestry, c. 1898 ![](images/f6085d491bfb52b9de746a898922b495494cfa592ab69def6612154ad821bd0c.jpg) ▶ Frida Hansen, Summer Night's Dream tapestry hanging, c. 1899 ## frida hansen ## (b. 1855 Hillevåg-by-Stavanger – d. 1931 Oslo, Norway) The Norwegian textile designer Frida Hansen studied painting and then later established an embroideryworkshop inStavanger in 1882. During this period she became increasingly familiar with the Norwegian folk tradition of tapestry weaving – since the 18th century “peasant” weavers had produced tapestries depicting relatively primitive biblical scenes. These textiles, whose powerful artistic expression made up for all that they lacked in technical finesse, were generally viewed in Norway as national art treasures. Inspired by these antecedents, Hansen began learning weaving techniques on a standing loom at Kjerstina Hauglum in Sogn, and in 1889 completed her first woven tapestry. A year later she established her own textile studio, and then in 1892 moved to Oslo (then known as Christiania) where in 1899 she founded a weaving company under the name of Det Norske Billedvæveri (DNB). Initially she drew motifs from the ancient Norwegian sagas, but after a trip to Paris around 1885 she embraced the Art Nouveau style. From this period onwards, her tapestries were often adorned with “Mackintosh-style” roses and other highly stylized flowers with strong saturated colours and bold swirling outlines. At the DNB workshop, Hansen not only revived the use of natural dyes and old weaving techniques, but also pioneered special new techniques including her famous “transparent weaving”, which brought her widespread acclaim. This innovative process involved leaving some areas of the warp exposed so as to create a transparent background that contrasted dramatically with the richly coloured woven motifs. During the 1890s, Hansen collaborated with fellow Norwegian designer Gerhard Munthe (who designed several tapestries for DNB), and in 1899 she wrote a book entitled Husflid og Kunstindustri i Norge. She exhibited her work at the 1893 “World's Columbian Exposition” in Chicago and also at the 1900 Paris “Exposition Universelle et Internationale”, where she was awarded a gold medal. By the turn of the century, Frida Hansen was not only the most important Norwegian weaver, she had also become one of thegreatest advocates of the naturalistic Art Nouveau style in Scandinavia. ![](images/878ca14b0bcf6de84309217edb3244302cca57c96f8581e0693fb94ed64fab7c.jpg)
