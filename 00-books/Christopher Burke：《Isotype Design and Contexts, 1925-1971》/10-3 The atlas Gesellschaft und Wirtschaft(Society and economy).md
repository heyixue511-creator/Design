# 3 The atlas Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft (Society and economy) 1930

Christopher Burke

Otto Neurath described Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft as merely the first part of his grand project for an illustrated encyclopedia or ‘visual thesaurus'. The project had germinated around 192o with his idea for a series of indexed pamphlets, and Neurath received encouragement from Albert Einstein, who wrote to him that 'such a well-planned collection would serve the same function for the masses today as did the French Encyclopedia for the intellectual groups in France during the eighteenth century.'¹

Neurath recognized an intent similar to his own behind Paul Otlet's plan for a 6o-volume ‘Atlas of civilization', and the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum formed a partnership with Otlet's Palais Mondial (Brussels) in order to realize it. However, Neurath lamented that ‘it was not possible to accomplish any concrete work, and the idea of a new Orbis Pictus, of a Visual Thesaurus remained, and still remains an idea, a plan for the future'.²

Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft was initially unconnected to the collaboration with Otlet; the commission came instead from the Bibliographisches Institut of Leipzig, publisher of the well-known encyclopedia, Meyers Konversationslexikon, as well as of Duden's standard dictionary of German orthography. Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft was the most extensive publishing project undertaken at the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in Wien. Despite the name of the Museum being reflected in its title, the publication was not financed by the Vienna municipality and its scope extended beyond the themes of previous exhibition work. The team in Vienna was allowed great freedom in determining the content of the atlas as well as in designing it.' Nevertheless, the pictorial plates in Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft were produced in the Museum's customary way, as hand-crafted exhibition charts, and these were used as artwork for reproduction in the atlas at half the original dimensions. They were printed lithographically: some are monochrome but others feature up to eight separate colours of ink. The original charts were exhibited at the Museum's Mundaneum' branch to coincide with publication.

Certain themes and modes of presentation were developed from Die bunte Welt but Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft displays a significant advance in the creation of a consistent visual style: in particular, the design of pictograms benefited from the presence of Arntz as a full-time employee at the Museum and from the appointment of Peter Alma and Augustin Tschinkel to assist him. Valuable contributions were also made by members of the so-called academy' of specialist advisers assembled for compiling the publication. Among them was the cartographer Karl Peucker, a German who taught at the Hochschule für Welthandel (College of world trade) in Vienna. He was a prolific theoretician of mapmaking: the use of colour on maps was of particular concern to him, and Neurath wrote with approval about Peucker's favouring ofphysiological colours’.8 Much attention was also given to the variety of projections used for the maps that were integral to many charts in Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft (see overleaf). A note on the atlas's cartographic principles explained that equal-area projections were preferred to Mercator's famous projection, which distorts the relative size of land masses. Neurath took inspiration from accuracy in map-making for the design of Isotype in general, and the aim of minimizing distortion in two-dimensional, geographical representation can be aligned with the basic rules of the Vienna Method (non-perspective depiction and consistent pictogram size).

## Contemporary reactions to Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft

Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft provided the first opportunity for comprehensive results of the Vienna Method to be seen widely outside of Austria, due to its distribution by a major German publisher. In one of the earliest articles published in English about Isotype, Jan Tschichold praised the admirably-drawn' plates of the atlas and the method behind it." The German journalist & satirist Kurt Tucholsky welcomed the publication in his regular book-review column for Die Weltbühne as ‘a masterpiece of educational statistics', which should be acquired by popular libraries and educational institutions (although he reserved suspicion, 'not towards the producers of this atlas, who honestly give their sources, but towards statistics itself, that sweet form of lies'.)1² Serious criticism was made by Vladimir Woytinsky, a veteran of two Russian revolutions and former editor of Izvestia, who was then working in Germany as a political economist. His own, multi-volume work of statistics, Die Welt in Zahlen (The world in figures, 1925–8), had been criticized by Friedrich Bauermeister of the Vienna Museum for its unsystematic attempts at visualizing statistics. 13

Woytinsky made fundamental objections to the Vienna Method, and even questioned the legitimacy of calling it a ‘method':

I need not address here the question of whether a technical approach [Handgriff] to statistical visualization should be described as a method'. The Vienna Museum has certainly created a style; what harm can it do to name this style a method'! Its misfortune, however, is that the Museum direction believes it can apply one technical approach in a unified way to reproduce a complete range of statistics. This is an error: the search for a universal philosopher's stone' of pictorial statistics contradicts the nature of statistical method. A universal means of visualization would perhaps be possible if, as the Vienna Museum management seem to believe, statistics always concern a greater or lesser number of objects. Statistics operate with all kinds of quantities.14

Neurath recognized that the style of the Vienna Method was to a great extent determined by objects' – this was a principle behind its iconicity.¹⁵ He described the Vienna Museum's statistical charts as 'representations of objects from the sociological collection'. And he would not have seen it as a fault that this partly determined the choice of subject matter that could be addressed, as Woytinsky did. Neurath admitted: Not every body of figures is suitable for visualization.'¹⁶ Towards the end of his life he conceded: Some correlations are even distorted and falsified by being pictorially represented.'¹7

In addition to the repetition of pictograms to indicate quantity, Woytinsky identified as principles of the Vienna Method that it created ‘statistics without figures' and that it exercised a great degree of rounding off on the original data. Again, these were features readily recognized by Neurath: ‘Any vivid representation, even if it is defective, attracts the attention more and stays better in the memory than a table of figures.'¹⁸ In September 1931, some six months after Woytinsky's review was published, an essay by Neurath titled Bildstatistik nach Wiener Methode' (Pictorial statistics by the Vienna Method) appeared in the educational journal, Die Volksschule, in which he seems to specifically address Woytinsky's objections. Woytinsky considered this approach unsuitable for modern, popular statistics: Figures are the language and soul of statistics. ... Whoever feels that figures are something abominable, and is ashamed to write them down, must seek an occupation outside statistics.'²º Although the team at the Vienna Museum included statisticians (most notably Alois Fischer), its central task was to transform statistics into pictorial education', as Neurath began to call it from 193o.2¹ The key concept of transformation' began to be clarified in writings by Neurath and Marie Reidemeister from 1932, perhaps partly in response to criticism such as Woytinsky's. (See also pp. 63-4 above.)

Some of Woytinsky's points were consciously taken up by Benedikt Kautsky in his critical review of Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft for the Berlin journal Die Gesellschaft. Kautsky's comments came from closer to home: he was secretary of the Wiener Arbeiterkammer, which had given financial support to the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum from its beginning.²² In Kautsky's opinion, Neurath's assertion of 'educational reasons' for unorthodox rounding of figures was unjustifed and turned the common methods of arithmetic learned in school ‘upside down'. He suggested that the nature of the Vienna Method forced this approach: the specific value assigned to a pictogram was completely unelastic', and the use of halved or quartered symbols was a violation of the Method's own principles. (While Kautsky realized that Isotype pictograms were not naturalistic pictures, he was troubled when human figures placed on a world map spilled off a land mass into the ocean.)

Kautsky believed there to be a wider public mistrust of statistics than Neurath realized, and he warned that Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft could give more cause for such mistrust. He considered pictures to be a diversion that provided only a vague retinal residue' of the actual statistics:

But statistics are not to be separated from numbers, and graphic presentation should do nothing else than facilitate our way to them; Neurath, however, wants to replace numbers with pictures and this puts him on the wrong track.

While Kautsky agreed with Neurath that statistics were the basis for 'clear-eyed politics' and planned economy, he did not share Neurath's faith in pictorial statistics as a tool of socialist education: 'We Marxists - who strive to explain the diversity of the present environment and the past – have so much more cause not to be satisfied with a few examples of coarsely rounded-off data. That would amount to proclaiming the triumph of the slogan.'²³

Another contemporary critic of Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft was the influential French historian Lucien Febvre. He wrote a short review in the journal Annales d’histoire economique et sociale, which he had co-founded and which gave its name to the school of French historical thought. Febvre, who went on to co-write the standard text on the history of the book L’apparition du livre, was naturally sensitive to the artefactual qualities of Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft:

Now, the work is seductive at first glance; it is even luxurious, with its beautiful plates painstakingly printed in several colours, the minute care brought to the details, the incontestable resources of a material execution that is almost without fault. And certain plates testify to an evident ingenuity. But when, after having examined each in turn, one puts them back in their case, it is difficult not to feel a question come to one's lips: all this great effort, all this care, all this luxury, are they really justified?

Febvre recognized that some charts (such as no. 37, overleaf) required a significant degree of reading and calculation to appreciate their full meaning, and he found this difficult to balance with the eloquent figurative depiction [figuration parlante]'. He wondered whether the graphic means could not be 'economized'. Also, he felt that the charts 'in the atlas of Mr Neurath sing a resounding hymn – a bit too resounding – to quantity':
