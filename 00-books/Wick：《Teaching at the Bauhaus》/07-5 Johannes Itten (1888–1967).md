The institution of the preliminary course as a step in artists' training was not an invention of the Bauhaus or of Johannes Itten in particular, but rather goes back, as we have seen, to the nineteenth century, and it was expressly encouraged in the early twentieth century by numerous art school reformers (see chapter 4, "Bauhaus Pedagogy in the Context of Art School Reform"). The reason that the preliminary course at the Bauhaus, in contrast to similar courses at other art or arts and crafts schools, achieved the legendary fame that it has today is, however, closely linked with the personality of Johannes Itten, who, thanks to his dual qualifications both as a trained teacher with progressive views on education and as an avant-garde artist, succeeded in giving the pedagogy at the Bauhaus an unmistakable profile during the founding years of the institution.

## Formative Influences

Born in 1888 in the Bernese Oberland in Switzerland, Johannes Itten attended the teachers' seminar in Bern from 1904 to 1908 to become a primary school teacher. Looking back, he would write:

When I began my career as aprimary school teacher in a Bernese village in 1908, I tried to avoid anything that coulddisturb the naive unself-consciousness of the children. Almost instinctively I recognized that any criticism or correction has an offensive and destructive effect on self-confidence, that praise and appreciation of work well done encourages personal growth.⁴

Another feature of his teaching method, which was highly unusual for its time, was that Itten never corrected the mistakes in his students workbooks, because he considered corrections to be an inappropriate incursion into their personal development, so instead he would discuss errors generally with the entire class. This principle of unconditional respect for the individuality of the student—a principle of reform pedagogy that he encountered at the teachers' seminar in Bern (on the influence of reform pedagogy, see "The Background of Itten's Pedagogy" in this chapter)—is one to which Itten remained true when later teaching at the Bauhaus and other art schools.

In October 1909 he began to study art at the Academy of Art in Geneva, but he was so disappointed by the academic teaching method, which was diametrically opposed to his own conceptions of pedagogy, that he returned to Bern one semester later for training as a secondary schoolteacher in the subjects of mathematics, physics, and chemistry from 1910 to 1912. After completing his teaching exams in 1912, under the influence of the art of the Blue Rider and cubism, which he had encountered on extended journeys through Germany, Holland, and France (including the Sonderbund exhibition in Cologne and a Kandinsky exhibition in Munich) he decided to

give up teaching and become a painter. I now believed I knew what I wanted to learn, and went back to Geneva. In a course run by Prof. Gilliard I learned something very important: he introduced me to the geometrical elements of form and their contrasts.⁵

Elsewhere Itten states more specifically that these "studies in contrasts using the elementary forms of the square, the circle, and the triangle represented an introduction into [his] nonobjective designs of the years 1915 to 1919."⁶ In light of his position on the question of whether art could be taught, which would later be discussed with some degree of controversy at the Bauhaus, it is interesting to sée a letter from Itten to Gilliard from 1914 in which he writes that he considers the Geneva professor's teaching method outstanding because it "offers something fundamental that every artist should know, because you [Gilliard] present the elements, the basis, for all artistic work."7

As Willy Rotzler has shown persuasively, the prehistory of Itten's preliminary course can be traced back one important step through Gilliard. Rotzler writes:

His teacher Eugène Gilliardset Itten ona trail that would later become important for the art teacher: the study of the means for design. From Gilliard, who was himself working to revitalize the teaching of drawing, Itten learned about the textbook on the fundamentals of design written by Eugène Grasset—which even today is not sufficiently appreciated. When this methodologically innovative textbook for composition in drawing is stripped of its obligatory flourishes, with its debt to the Art Nouveau design sensibility, it represents the first cornerstone of the theoretical framework on which Itten would later build his basic teachings on design, the preliminary course.8

This 1905 textbook by Eugène Grasset (1845–1917), Méthode de composition ornementale,9 represents a significant moment within the history of the French tradition of academic training in drawing in the late nineteenth and early twentieth century in that—in keeping with a trend of the time—it entirely abolished the "dessin géométrical" that was then part of the standard training for artists. From his fundamental thesis that "the laws of matter are those of geometry,"Grasset deduces that it is necessary "to study the rules that are dependent on geometry, because they contain the essence of all the others." He continues: “The return to the primitive sources of simple geometry ... is a certain guarantee of the soundness of our method. Every crystal, every flower, every leaf is a well-founded geometrical construction, and the law that forms these things is the same one on which we ourselves depend. Geometry is assuredly an absolute truth for our world."1º In Grasset's view, it is not necessary (in ornamentation) to invent new forms, but rather the new, according to his thesis, would emerge from the possibility of infinite variation of known reserves of forms: “To innovate is to preserve by modifying"11—a principle that was widely accepted at the Bauhaus as well.1² We cannot cite Grasset at length here; we need only mention that he constructed his theory of ornament on, among other things, the following basic geometrical forms: point, line, triangle, square, circle, arc, S-curve, spiral—that is, on elementary forms that would later become important in Itten's design theory (as well as in Klee's and Kandinsky's). The examples that Grasset gives in his book reveal a highly rationally controlled, deterministic design procedure, and a glance at Itten's early diaries shows studies in ornament that are very similar to Grasset's in their reduction to elements.

It is surely not wrong to view Grasset's book as the expression of a highly sculptural rationalism; Itten encountered another sculptural rationalist when he went to Stuttgart in 1913 to study at the Academy there with Adolf Hölzel (1853-1934). Itten was not, however, immediately accepted by the admissions committee, and so—because Hölzel, as a professor at the Academy, was not permitted to accept private students—he had to be content with studying with one of Hölzel's students, Ida Kerkovius, from whom he learned the basics of Hölzel's theory. Soon, however, the teacher-student relationship would be reversed when Ida Kerkovius, at the age of forty, took part in Itten's preliminary instruction at the Bauhaus. There is no question that Hölzel's theory had a decisive impact on Itten, both artistically as well as pedagogically. Itten took from Hölzel, this "moderate avantgardist"13whose abstract compositions made him a pioneer in twentieth-century art (who is still insufficiently appreciated), fundamental insights into the process of artistic creation, which he understood as the application of objective pictorial rules. We cannot treat Hölzel's theory in depth here, so we will have to be satisfied with a few hints with an eye toward Itten.

In the book Farben und Formen als lebendige Kräfte (Colors and forms as living forces), whose third part contains "impressions derived from Hölzel's teachings," Carry van Biema writes: "It was . . . Goethe who said that 'painting lacks a figured bass like that of music' and 'the way Kant wrote in The Critique of Pure Reason is how a capable person would have . . . to write a critique of the senses and human reason now. Hölzel's theory presents a kind of figured bass of painting and especially a critique of the sense of sight."14 In this view, the laws of design can be derived from the laws of vision. Even so, the application of these laws is no guarantee of high art, and it is never an end in itself but rather at most a preliminary stage for the freedom of artistic creativity. "The highest artworks, emotion and law combine and interpenetrate. The tolerance in Hölzel's attitude toward the laws of art can be seen in remarks like this one: 'The laws are there merely to work deliberately with them or deliberately against them; all that is necessary is to know them.' Goingeven further, he remarks, 'every work is in fact a deviation from the law.'"15 Of course, this is not acknowledged in his theory, where Hölzel asserts that the first task is to become familiar with the “eternal laws of art,"16 to appropriate them rationally, and consciously apply them, because “anyone who does not know the ABC's of art will always have to fake it in a painting."17 Thus it is only logical that Hölzel should attribute extraordinary significance to a knowledge of the elementary means of design: "I can turn it this way or that, however I wish, it is always the same: 'Art is in the means.' All we have to express something artistically are these means. And the spiritual in the work of art can only be produced with these means."18 In Hölzel, Itten found confirmation for what he had already experienced .in Geneva; namely, that "simple forms"—by which he meant the triangle, the square, and the circle—are “the best and most certain basis for artistic exploration"19; moreover, that “the strength of great artistic personalities lies in the invention of new combinations and possibilities that can be traced back to the elements."20 The proximity of these ideas to Grasset—whose book Hölzel knew just as well as the theoretical writings of Leonardo, Dürer, John Burnet, Owen Jones, Delacroix, andothers—is obvious.

The object of Hölzel's theory of design, which does not exist as a self-contained theoretical edifice but has been handed down only in aphoristic form and in the notes of individual students (including Itten), is to experiment with the picture plane and frame; with the elementary pictorial means of the point and the line (also and especially in relation to the picture plane); with the geometrical forms of the triangle, the square, and the circle; with the rules for proportion (especially the Golden Section); with fundamental principles of composition (horizontal, vertical, diagonal, circular); and with contrasts. The contrast of light and dark held the highest significance for Hölzel, and it was considered central by Itten as well. It is certainly not wrong to view Hölzel's theory—and consequently Itten's theory of design— as essentially a theory of contrasts. Just as in music “in counterpoint one sets 'punctus contra punctum'—that is, note against note—so it is in composing a painting with line and counterline, form and counterform, light and dark, color and countercolor, number and measure."21 The goal of artistic design, according to Adolf Hölzel, is harmony, understood as a balance of contrasts. This idea is based on, among other things, a polar way of thinking inspired by Goethe but also founded in the psychology of perception: "The more, and the more strongly, that opposites are concentrated in one place, the more the viewer's glance will be directed there."22 Again and again, we find allusions in Hölzel to laws of vision from which the laws of design and even harmony are derived. "In painting the eye is our dictator. What it takes to be good and pleasant becomes our law. For this reason alone, Hölzel's laws of harmony are not arbitrarily defined." The hand "must execute what the eye demands. The latter obeys the laws of vision. It is far more objective and impersonal than the hand."23 This overlooks, of course, the fact that vision (and thus design) is always a historical process that is determined by a series of cultural andsocial factors, so that Hölzel's—and consequently Itten's—efforts to formulate laws of vision and design of timeless andcrosscultural validity should be approached with caution.

Hölzel's significance for Itten can be stated concretely as follows:

(1) Itten's study of color theory "by Goethe, Bezold, Chevreul, and Hölzel as well as the color circles they developed";24 Itten concerned himself most especially with Hölzel's theory of color contrast, which in turn goes back to Goethe.

(2)The great influence of Hölzel's explorations of the problem of chiaroscuro as well as his analyses of old masters, which were intended to give students insight into the constructions and laws on which older works of art were based.

(3) The period between 1913 and 1916—that is, the very period Itten spent in Stuttgart—was for Hölzel himself a phase of intense experimentation. He made abstract collages of torn paper and shreds of fabric,25 which in turn motivated Itten to try similar experiments. He remarked on this in his diary in 1914: "On gluing. Using paper I paint (glue) a reality on a surface. In such a way that the distribution of masses corresponds to our sensibility. . . . To reproduce this glued object using the means available to the painter—i.e., paint—gives a higher reality, because it is something harmonious. Prof. Hölzel copies all of the details of the glued painting and achieves a vividness that is astonishing."26 As we will see, these sorts of montages of materials, as well as exercises in transforming them into drawings and/or paintings, would later play a large role in Itten's teachings.

(4) The gymnastic exercises that Hölzel regularly performed²7 seem to have been equally influential, and Itten introduced them at the Bauhaus in order to relax the students, develop their motor skills, and put them in the mood for the rhythmic studies that would follow. We should also mote that Hölzel encouraged automatic writing. Itten reports that once during a life-drawing session Hölzel had the students "'draw a nude with a single stroke the way Rodin did it.' That a professor at the academy at the time had his students draw based on a rhythmic automatism was unheard of, and his colleagues were outraged."28

This covers several of the important premises—though by no means all of them—of Itten's later preliminary training course at the Bauhaus.

At Hölzel's suggestion, Itten began giving private lessons while still in Stuttgart, then in 1916 he was invited by one of his female students to move to Vienna, where he opened a private school. There he had an opportunity to take what he had learned from Gilliard and Hölzel about the interplay of the teacher's own artistic activity and pedagogical practice and put it to the test, modify it, and expand on it:

Geometric and rhythmical forms, problems of proportion and of expressive pictorial composition were studied in depth. Tasks involving textures and the evolution of subjective forms were new. Besides the theory and practice of polar contrast the exercises in relaxation and concentration required of the students proved amazingly successful. I recognized creative automatism as one of the most important factors of artistic activity. My own work focused on geometrical-abstract subjects based on careful pictorial composition.29

The Vienna years represented a time of maturation for Itten as both an artist and a teacher; at the end of this period his pedagogical concept was developed and fixed to such an extent that he was able to transfer it seamlessly to the Bauhaus in 1919. Information about his time in Vienna is sparse. Itten himself remarked that he was "completely isolated" in Vienna, and he had no contact even with "other painters with similar aims," though he did have contact with the composers Arnold Schoenberg, Alban Berg, and Josef Matthias Hauer—allof whom were writing twelve-tone compositions—as well as with the architect Adolf Loos.30

His contact with Josef Matthias Hauer (1883– 1959) was especially important, as both Itten and Hauer were interested in exploring the relationships between music and color (a problem that also occupied Kandinsky extensively), and in doing so Hauer alluded to Itten's twelve-part “circle of expression."31 (Itten's own color theory, which was first published in 1961 under the title Kunst der Farbe: Subjektives Erleben und objektives Erkennen als Wege zur Kunst [translated that year as The Art of Color: The Subjective Experience and Objective Rationale of Color], is in turn full of musical analogies and allusions.)

Adolf Loos (1870–1933), the author of the famous essay “Ornament und Verbrechen" (Ornament and crime)—whose influence on the Bauhaus still needs to be examined more closely—not only organized an Itten exhibition in 1919 (which Itten claimed was the first exhibition of nonobjective art to be shown in the Austrian capital)³2 but also mentioned Itten by name in his “Richtlinien für ein Kunstamt" (Guidelines for a department of art), which was concerned with the reform of the arts in the wake of the collapse of the Austrian-Hungarian Empire. In the latter he calls for a new form of arts education: “training in drawing must serve merely as a means to an end. The end is the sharpening of the senses. The type established by the painter Itten shouldbe the basis."33 This is an indication of the high esteem Itten already enjoyed as an art teacher, at least within informed circles.

It is interesting that Itten's name appears in these guidelines but not that of Franz Cizek (1865–1946). The art course for children that Cizek had established in Vienna in 1897 as aprivate school (integrated in 1904 into the School for Arts and Crafts) had met with great response. “Do not teach, do not learn! Allow things to grow from their own roots!"34 and “deschooling of the school"35were the sorts of pedagogical maxims with which he attracted attention and gained a reputation in London in 1908, at Arts Education Day in Dresden in 1912, in Cologne in 1914, and elsewhere later. Cizek's teaching methods seemed new and revolutionary to many educators at the time, because he did not have students draw from dead objects in an academic and sterile manner but rather encouraged children to “paint spontaneously,"36 to make paper collages, and to experiment with a wide range of materials, but especially because he never intervened in the process, or even in the finished product, to make corrections. In addition to his art courses for children, Cizek also gave courses in “ornamental form theory"37 at the School of Arts and Crafts, in which he transferred these pedagogical principles to his work with “a more mature group of students."38 In addition to applied works like wallpaper designs, architectural models, andornamental relief sculptures, they also did free studies in rhythm and expression—all of this was an interesting amalgam of trends in cubist, expressionist, futurist, and constructivist forms. A review from 1921 remarked:

The formal and craft aspects are developed not from appearance but from inner experience; the student learns to design independently abstract feelings and indefinable sensations like those of colors, noises, and so on. What is at first—once it is roused—freely and unrestrictedly pursued as a, personal expression is then purified according to the pictorial laws of painting or sculpture and elevated to a planar or cubic form. In addition, the school's true concern is the painterly or sculptural formation of movements that have been experienced(kinetism)" (emphasis added).39

The reviewer believed the educational significance of Cizek's class to be that “it encourages self-sufficient experience and creativity; craft and form are not seen as something ossified, something transferable, but rather as something that has to be conquered anew each time and that recognizes that something new is valid for every age and for every individual."40

The question arises, What does all this have to do with Itten and his preliminary course at the Bauhaus? In the relevant literature it is frequently asserted that Itten's preliminary course was decisively based on what happened in Cizek's course in Vienna. Wingler, for example, says that "the nucleus of [ltten's] basic course, inspired by Cizek, consisted of studies on materials,"41 and Sibylle Zrost even goes as far as to say that Itten carried on “the ideas for arts pedagogy of his former teacher Professor Cizek."42 In fact, Itten was never Cizek's student, and“Itten himself expressly deniedhe hadever come in close contact with Cizek."43Itten's remark that Cizek's former students felt “at home"44in his ownprivate art school at most points to shared principles of educationbasedon reform pedagogy; however, to imagine on the basis of formal similarities between the tasks both teachers gave their students that Itten must have been dependent on Cizek seems extraordinarily audacious. Because all the works we know from Cizek's course that show certain formal correspondences to student works from Itten's teachings in Vienna and Weimar (rhythmic studies, montages of materials, and so on) appear to have begun in the years between 1920 and 192445—that is to say, after Itten had left Vienna. For his part, Itten had exhibited samples of his students' work in Vienna in 1919, so hypothetically it is even possible that Cizek was somewhat dependent on Itten (which would explain why Loos mentions Itten but not Cizek in his guidelines). Without being able to answer these difficult questions definitively, we can nevertheless sum things up, against the usual reading, that understanding the principles of the preliminary course at the Bauhaus does not require that we see Cizek as a connecting link or transitional figure, especially if we take into account the defining experiences Itten took away from Hölzel and from his own independent artistic development—which went beyond Hölzel and was completely independent of Cizek—inthe years before he was called to the Bauhaus.46

## Early Work

Before we examine Itten's preliminary course at the Bauhaus more closely, we should look at the artist's early work in order to mark the horizon against which his artistic theory developed. The point is not to provide a seamless chronological depiction of the development of his oeuvre or to provide analyses of individual works but rather to summarize several of the basic pictorial problems explored in Itten's early work. This work oscillates—as in fact does his entire artistic oeuvre between objective and constructivist, nonobjective tendencies.

The objective paintings of the period before 1921, which recall Hodler to some extent, can be disregarded here. In 1913, when Itten came to Hölzel in Stuttgart, he had “in his artistic baggage . . . already some essential items along: first, his overcoming of Ferdinand Hodler's symbolic and expressionist art, then the decisive experience with early cubism which became the real initiation into painting for him and his colleagues [especially for another Hölzel student who later became an instructor at the Bauhaus, Oskar Schlemmer—R.W.],"47 as, for example, the painting House with Staircase (1912, CW 21) shows.48

Toward the end of his student days in Stuttgart he created his first purely geometrical and abstract compositions, whichare distinguished by their vivid palette from the gray and brown hues derived from analytical cubism that had previously dominated his work and represent his immediate reaction to the occupation with color theory that Hölzel had inspired (e.g., Horizontal-Vertical [1915, CW 97]; The Encounter [1916, CW 101]). In his essay “Fragmentarisches" (Fragmentary remarks), written in 1916 for the small publication Hölzel und sein Kreis (Hölzel and his circle), Itten noted:

The form most easily grasped and defined is geometrical, andits basic elements are the circle, the square, and the triangle. These form elements contain the seed of every possible form. Visible to those who can see; invisible to those who cannot.

Form is also color. Without color no form, without form no color. Form and color are one. The colors of the spectrum are the most easily grasped. They contain the seed of every possible color. Visible to those who can see, invisible to those who cannot. . . .

The geometrical forms and the colors of the spectrum are the simplest and most sensitive and thus the most powerful and delicate means to represent an expressive example of form.49

This occupation with the pictorial possibilities of basic geometrical forms and the colors of the spectrum is found continuously through Itten's lifework. It is particularly characteristic of the artist's early and late work, whereas his middle period is more concerned with landscapes and problems of style relating to objective painting.50

In parallel with his first purely abstract, rhythmically subdivided compositions (which were exhibitedby Herwarth Walden in the gallery Der Sturm in Berlin in 1916), and to some extent even later, Itten created a series of works that remained objective. They reveal a cubism expressive in color and form (insofar as such schematic characterizations are capable of saying anything at all), such as the Bach Singer of 1915 (CW 65), the Red Tower of 1918 (CW 121), or the Last Watch also of 1918 (CW 137). Willy Rotzler has expressly noted that this development occurred independently of Hölzel. Of the Bach Singer he remarked: "The compositional principles are original—namely, a richly contrastive, dialectical use of rectangular and circular forms with an emphatic rhythm; original, too, is the use of contrasting color effects, like simultaneous vibrations."51 Summing up, Rotzler characterizes the phase between 1915 and 1918 as follows:

"The common feature is a rhythmical pictorial structure in which diagonals and arcs produce a dynamic whole. The color is . .. richly orchestrated and produces strong three-dimensional effects. The expressive, vitalistic character of all these paintings leads them to be called 'futurocubist.'... In Itten's work a rhythmical vibration permeates the prismatic, circular, spiral forms. Opposing movements of form are often juxtaposed contrapuntally."52

Clearly inspired by Hölzel—and years before the surrealists placed "psychological automatism" on their agenda—Itten gained insight into the meaning of "creative automatism" as one of the most significant "factors of artistic activity."53 This can be seen above all in the artist's early drawings, which, in contrast to the relatively stringently constructed studies in composition, have something of the character of psychograms, of spontaneous "jottings of impressions."54 In view of Itten's procedure in the preliminary course at the Bauhaus, this aspect seems especially worthy of mention.

Nearly all of the artist's works from 1915 to 1919-20—paintings, drawings, sculptures—share a strong dynamic, a distinct emphasis on movement. The artist was not, however, concerned with the futurist model of depicting a course of superficial motion but rather—much like Klee (see “Movement" in chapter 10)—with the pictorial manifestation of a principle of life and of design that was central for him. Thus he wrote in 1921:

Everything living reveals itself to us by means of movement. Everything living reveals itself in forms. So all form is movement and all movement is revealed in form. The forms are vessels of movement; and movements are the essence of form. . . .

Every point, every line, every plane, every shadow, every light, and every color is a form born of movement that in turn gives birth to movement. . . . If 1 want to experience a line, then I have to move my hand in a way corresponding to the line, or I must follow the line with my senses, that is to say, be moved spiritually. Finally, if i can imagine a line mentally—see it—then I am moved mentally.

These are just three different levels of being moved. IfI draw a line with my hand, then I am moved physically—that is the first, the physical level of being moved. If I move my senses along a line, then Iam movedin the second, the spiritual levelof being moved. When I imagine the line mentally, that is the third, the mental level of being moved. . . .

Before me stands a thistle. My motoric nerves sense a conflicted, jumpy movement. My senses of touch and sight perceive the sharp pointedness of the movement of its form, and my mind sees its essence. . . .

It requires no explanation to understand that the drawn form of the thistle can only be correct if the movement of my hand, of my eye, of my mind corresponds precisely to the acutely pointed, prickly, painfulformof the thistle. That means:

Character of motion equals character of form. This is a basic proposition of our entire exploration."55

Thus we findourselves at the heart of Johannes Itten's artistic philosophy and pedagogy: the connectionbetween movement andform conceived as an identity; the overall view of body, spirit, and mind; the emphasison the emotionalover the intellectual—allof these things are fundamentalideas for thepreliminary course that Itten offered.

## Fundamental Pedagogical Intentions

Itten began his activity at the Bauhaus at a time when the traditional moral and social norms had lost their validity following the war and the postwar events, a time “of terrible discord,"56 a time of a general loss of orientation, in which the students at the Bauhaus were more inclined to discuss and debate, even to be active politically, than to work as artists or artisans, in which, as Schlemmer remarked, “the students apparently ha[d] little enthusiasm for practicalcraftsmanship."57 In this time of extreme difficulties Itten managed by means of a great deal of personal involvement, pedagogical skill, and strict discipline to motivate the newly arrivedstudents at the Bauhaus (including more than twenty of his Viennese students, who followed him to Weimar) to confront in useful ways problems of form and color but especially to confront themselves. (In February 1921 Schlemmer wrote that “the pillar of calm amidst the student chaos" were“the teachers," and that Itten was working to overcome this chaos "with dictatorship, exhibiting . . . ruthlessness and school masterly pedantry”;58 by mid-1922, however, he acknowledges him as “pedagogically... more skilled than the rest" and as having a “decided talent for leadership."59

By his own account, Itten set himself three tasks for the preliminary course:

(1) To liberate the creative forces and thereby the artistic talents of the students. Their own experiences and perceptions were to result in genuine work. Gradually, the students were to rid themselves of all the dead wood of convention andacquire the courage to create their own work.

(2) To make the students' choice of career easier. Here, exercises with materials and textures were a valuable aid. Each student quickly found the material with which he felt the closest affinity; it might have been wood, metal, glass, stone, clay, or textiles that inspired him most to create work. . . .

(3) To present the principles of creative composition to the students for their future careers as artists. The laws of form and color opened up to them the world of objectivity. As the work progressed it became possible for the subjective and objective problems of form and color to interact in many different ways.

. Iconsidereditessential,in teachingthe means of artistic representation, to evoke an individual response in students of various temperaments and talents. This was the only way to generate the creative atmosphere conducive to original work. The work was to be “genuine."60

In the citations from 1930 that follow, Itten's pedagogicalintentions are more clear than in the citations above:

From the very beginning, my teaching was not directed toward any particular fixed, external goal. The human being itself, as a creature capable of improvement and development, seemed to me to be the task of my pedagogical efforts. Developing the senses, increasing the ability to think and experience spiritually, relaxing and developing the bodily organs and functions—these are the means and the paths available to the teacher concerned about education.61

These sentences clearly reveal that Itten's ambitions for arts education were comprehensive, integral, and went beyond simple training to qualify students for a career in the arts. Consequently, the preliminary course, too, was not to be understood as a way of transmitting basic drawing abilities like that of the traditional training at the academies, where drawing was acquired in three stages: first, by copying drawings, then drawing plaster casts, and finally working with a live model. Instead, it was to be seen as a program that viewed the "entire human being" as a physical, spiritual, and intellectual whole—that is, in the sense of a "theory of totality," to borrow a term from Till Neu's analysis of Itten's pedagogy.62

## The Course

A reliable reconstruction of Itten's course at the Bauhaus is made more difficult by a series of problems, just two of which will be mentioned here:

(1) Although the book Staatliches Bauhaus Weimar, 1919–1923, which Walter Gropius edited for the Bauhaus exhibition in the summer of 1923, contains numerous illustrations from Itten's course, his name is shamelessly underrepresented in this publication. Johannes Itten, amember of the teaching staff for many years and acritical force during the founding years of the institute, is mentioned in only one place, as the master responsible for "the teaching of form."63 The captions in the chapter on the teaching of form are limited to the general remark "From the preliminary course"64 (it is telling that only illustrations from works not made at the Bauhaus are characterized by the additional remark "from Itten's course in Vienna"), and the works that were created in the workshops under Itten's direction are not marked as such but appear under the names of other masters of form who were assigned to succeed Itten in the direction of the workshops (e.g., Schlemmer or Moholy-Nagy). The background of this sort of practice is no doubt the conflict between Gropius and Itten mentioned earlier65 (see “The Founding Phase" in chapter 3), which obviously led to the director of the Bauhaus putting some distance between himself and Itten, even publicly, in the first substantial and elaborate presentation of his institute. This was made that much easier, as Itten hadannounced his resignation already in 1922 and had left the Bauhaus at Easter 1923. There can be no question that this book publication obscured for many years the crucial contribution Itten made to the pedagogy of the Bauhaus.66

(2) Itten himself, in his book Mein Vorkurs am Bauhaus (1963), did not really contribute to an authentic reconstruction of what had happened in Weimar. For a significant portion of this book, he illustrated and commented on student works that were done from 1926 on at his private art school in Berlin and later at the Higher Technical School for the Textile Arts in Krefeld and the School for Arts and Crafts and the Technical School for Textiles in Zurich. This means that Mein Vorkurs am Bauhaus contains an abundance of material that is not genuinely derived from the Bauhaus and is really closer to Kandinsky's sense of form and other constructivist design concepts from the twenties than to the distinctly expressive tendencies (with some traces of dadaism) that clearly predominatedin Itten's teachings at the Bauhaus.67 In what follows, then, we will limit ourselves to examining the period up to the end of 1922 and bracket out all later developments, however much they might continue the same practices.

This seemingly restrictive method, which is reminiscent of the timed drawing and rapiddrawing practiced in the late nineteenth century (based on the American timed-writing method), should not be understood to mean that Itten was merely interestedin preparing the students in the sense of a giving them a skill (as was the case with the so-called dictation drawingtaught in schools). The intention was the reverse: the students were to act out with their motor skills in order to free themselves up, to relax, andalso to experience movement and rhythm, directly and physically, as theprimordialexistentialprinciple andas the fundamental principle ofpictorial organization. Oneusualfeatureof these exerciseswas that Itten had them write rhythmic forms withboth hands in order to train the coordination of motor skills in a unified way and to reduce the deficit of dexterity in the weaker hand.

There were also free improvisations, which immediately call to mind East Asian calligraphy, but in fact differ fundamentally from it in that they are spontaneous jottings of psychological states and not artificially fixed, codified symbols. It is striking how closely many of these rhythm studies, in which the "line [is] freed of the obligation to represent a thing, and instead itself functions as a thing,"70 resemble the pictorial forms of abstract expressionism that would come two or three decades later.

The real task for the semester consisted of a sequence of varied exercises in improvisation and construction, beginningwith two-dimensional studies and moving to three-dimensional essays in composition using diverse materials. Exercises in the light-dark contrast, one of the fundamental design methods andin Itten's view the most expressive one, took up a great deal of the course. Before “exercises to be solved in an intellectually constructive manner [were] set," Itten had the students work on themes that could“be mastered on the basis of free perception and imagination."74 In general, charcoal was used for this, as it was sensitive and well suited to produce chiaroscuro. Because the students generally found it difficult at first to recognize differences in light and dark with sufficient differentiation and to realize them in their drawings, Itten had scales of shades made that moved in equal gradations over the range from the lightest gray to the darkest black. In order to heighten the student's senses, both optically andhaptically, Itten carried out studies with materials and textures in his course that were taken over, in modified form, by his successor László Moholy-Nagy. Itten remarked on these:

At the Bauhaus, I had long chromatic series of material samples made for the tactile assessment of the various textures. The students had to feel these sequences of textures with their fingertips and their eyes closed. After a little while their sense of touch improved dramatically. I then asked them to make texture montages of contrasting materials. Fantastic structures were produced and their effects were completely novel at the time.76

These two- andthree-dimensional texture montages are an interesting example of avant-garde design principles that were then common in cubism and dadaism being introducedinto the teaching canon of artists' training. Already in his Stuttgart period, aswe have seen, Itten made glue paintings of paper, but the collages did not interest him as“autonomous designs" but rather as a “means to discover new compositions of color and form."77 Shortly after moving to the Austrian capital, he wrote in his diary on 20 October 1916 something that suggests this field of experimentation had been greatly expanded:

Experiment withmaterials. Attach pieces of straw, wood, iron, and cloth to a wooden board and then give all of them a coat of paint, go wild. ... I am looking for a formative principle based on the knowledge of the form that determines the connections."78

This note and a sheet of studies from 1916 entitled On Materials (CW 115) not only demonstrate Itten's great interest in researching the materiality of things, their sensory qualities, but also seem appropriate to further undercut the speculations discussedearlier (in“Formative Influences") that Cizek influencedItten. For it is clear that Itten was following a line of development that had already begun while studying with Hölzel, and that this was done independently of Cizek and his circle, about whom he may have known nothing at all at this early date. A diary entry from 1918 shows that Itten's “material studies"79 were imbeddedwithin the overarching context of his theory of contrasts:

The study of nature is above all a study of the purely material. Contrasts: loose-solid, softhard, fluffy-smooth, rough-smooth, pointedblunt, shiny-matte, fibrous-consistent, tightknit-holey, cloudy/airy-rigid.

Veil, wool, silk, knits, weaves, crocheted, hammered, filed, sawed, marbled, fur, glass, brush, metallic, leather, wood, flesh, grid, wheel hair, stone.

As early as 1917, Itten had introduced “material studies” into his teachings: “So I hope that in this way the stroke, the line, will also be felt as something material, that the love of the line will grow from the love of silk."81

These exercises withmaterials and textures were not an aesthetic end in themselves for Itten, but rather they occupied a precise place within his pedagogical process. Through a playful approach to various materials, they wereintended to qive those starting their studies assistance in choosing from among the subsequent teachings offered in the Bauhaus workshops. At the same time, the haptic, optic, and even emotional experiences acquiredwhile putting together and “copying" the“material compositions,"82 along with theelementary light-dark exercises (scales and ribbons) would serve as a prerequisite for the study of nature, to which Itten devoted particular importance in his teachings, and which wouldremain at the center of his own work during the Bauhaus years and long after.

In 1918 Itten described the importance of studying nature for his course as follows:

In order to train their observational abilities to be sharp and exact, beginners must make precise,photographicallyaccuratedrawings—in color as well—from nature. I want to train the eye and the hand, and memory as well. That is to say, to learn to see by heart. [And indeed in the preliminary course students drew things from memory that captured the materialcharacter of the objects with deceptive accuracy.— R.W.] First I train the physical body, the hand, arm, shoulder, and senses. That is the training of the external existing human being. The education of the mind follows step by step: clear, simple, thoughtful observation of that which can be perceived by the senses.83

Thus the study of nature is for Itten not l’art pour l’art, mere training in dexterity, or a simple coordination exercise, but rather it serves to sharpen the ability of the senses to recognize and to expand“concrete thinking," a connection that Itten once expressed in the concise formula, which he did not further explain: “sharp senses = exact, real thinking."84

Feeling and thinking, intuition and intellect, expression and construction are the poles that define not just Itten's artistic oeuvre but also his practice as a teacher of art. (They also mark the domain within which Bauhaus pedagogy as a whole operates. This needs to be emphasized separately, because the Bauhaus has all too often been reduced to the merely functional and its pedagogy to the merely rational.)

This relationship of tensions also characterizes Itten's theories of form and of color. With respect to the Bauhaus period, both are documented only in fragments. As a result of the selfimposed burden of teaching obligations that resulted from his commitment to the task at hand, not only did his own artistic production receive short shrift in the period from 1919 to 1922–23, but Itten also found no time to conceive his theory of forms and of colors as the sort of self-contained whole that both Kandinsky and Klee managed to achieve at the Bauhaus. Only in 1930 was Itten's theory of form published, in a private printing entitled Tagebuch von Johannes Itten: Beiträge zu einem Kontrapunkt der bildenden Kunst (Diary of Johannes Itten: Essays on a counterpoint of the visual arts; a reprint was published in 1980 as Elemente der bildenden Kunst),96 and then another thirty odd years passed until his color theory was published (Kunst der Farbe, 1961). Representations of simple bodies: sphere (infinite central movement), cube (horizontal, vertical, diagonal), pyramid, cylinder, cone. . . . Nevertheless, there can be no doubt that Itten's intense occupation with the phenomenon of color—we need only point to his diary entries on the subject, to his collaboration with Hauer, to his Tower of Fire (1920, CW 215–17), and to his planimetric sketch of color spheres at seven light levels and with twelve shades (supplement to Utopia [1921, CW 228])—surely transferred to his teachings at the Bauhaus, even if the sources that could permit a confident historical reconstruction of this aspect of his teaching are considerably less numerous than those for the theory of form, and very few color studies by his students survive from this period.111 As we cannot give an account on Itten's color theory here, it should at least be emphasized that it—like the artist's pedagogical practice in general—aimed for an arts education based on "subjective experience and objective rationale" (to cite the subtitle of his book The Art of Color).

## The Background of Itten's Pedagogy

With his teachings at the Bauhaus, as well as those later in Berlin, Krefeld, and Zurich, Johannes Itten had a lasting influence on both the training of artists and arts education in the schools, and he is widely celebratedas the founder of a new form of arts pedagogy. Nevertheless, it should not be overlooked that his pedagogy was not a fundamentally new approach but had models and precursors that can be concretely defined. Itten's real contribution to arts pedagogy consisted in being one of the first to combine these trends into an effective and productive form for arts education.112 It is obvious that his pedagogy was not nourished on the spirit of the academic training for artists or on the sources of orthodox drawing but rather stood in the tradition of the liberal pedagogical reform movement of Rousseau, Pestalozzi,Fröbel, Montessori, and others.

In Itten's diary for July 1916 one finds the following remark: "Vision without an idea is blind. H. Pestalozzi—An idea without vision is dead. J.-J. Rousseau."113 It is quite evident that Itten is aligning with Pestalozzi's position that "vision is the absolute fundament of all knowledge, in other words, that every piece of knowledge must derive from vision and must be led back to it."114 Itten may have borrowed other things from Pestalozzi as well, in particular the idea of a uniform training of the head, heart, and hand—the credo of ail experiential educators. (Pestalozzi's pedagogy for drawing, which aimed to overcome the unproductive method based on copying by introducing a method, then still new, for reducing drawing to elements, must be criticized in one respect in that it ultimately ossified into a rigid schematism, from which arts education in the schools did not begin to free itself until very late, specifically, the late nineteenth century).

Generally speaking, the efforts of the teachers named above can be characterized by saying that they aimed to develop abilities that are latent or dormant in a child or young adult by means of a process of a playful acquisition of reality and independent learning. This means that education and training are not understood in the sense of the old school of rote learning and cramming, in which a stock of supposedly unchanging facts, abilities, and skills are transmitted according to the model of the Nuremberg funnel. Itten compares the "true teacher" to a gardener:

He prepares the ground and sows. The seed germinates out of sight in the dark bosom of the earth; after the appropriate amount of time, it pushes upward; and finally it appears as a young plant. . . . It gets everything it needs from the earth and atmosphere around it. Its own essence develops out of itself into a flower and fruit. . . . The clever gardener cautiously allocates the care he gives it. He knows that his help is limited, but the strength and power of nature is enormous.115

This quotation reveals Itten's pedagogical naturalism, which is reminiscent of Rousseau. Itten's pedagogical reflections begin with the child. The artist shares with Goethe the conviction that there would be nothing but geniuses “if human beings were to keep developing in the direction they point as children." In Itten's view, the child acquires for itself "in a complete totality of thinking, feeling, and acting . . . its world by playing in a creative way." Only on entering school, with the foreign influence of a teaching plan, the personality of the teacher, and the regulation of the school system, is the natural growth of the child threatened. With the help of new methods of education, it is possible to counteract these dangers in order to "preserve the growth period of child genius" and thus to give “a completely different expression to the face of our culture."116 In his pedagogical thinking, then, Itten is not simply fixed on the individual but considers the overall cultural context, though not in a way that the contours of this "other" face are recognizable— that is, in the sense of a utopian plan. In formulating his pedagogical goals, Itten goes back to Plato's division of education into three areas: gymnastic, artistic, and mathematical. For Itten, education is consequently a liberation, strengthening, and differentiation of the physical, spiritual, and intellectual functions. Because these three areas are developed only in very different ways in our industrial society—Itten criticizes the overemphasis “of an uncreative intellectualism"117 to the detriment of artistic education—he calls for people to be educated as artists:

We must, on the one hand, see to it that all young people are educated in a way that they can develop in an original, characteristic manner, so they remain creative; on the other hand, we must make them familiar with all the rules governing the means of artistic representation that they must master in order to be able to give shape to their new and original ideas.118

It is evident, then, that Itten viewed the knowledge of the means and rules of design as a conditio sine qua non of artists' training, but also that another aspect is at least as decisive: namely, the creative self-development of the active subject in the design. His respect for the individual and his or her creative abilities can be seen in that for him the highest principle was the effect of teaching on the individuality of each student, that on principle he would not correct the students'work, and that he was also suspicious of students' taking after the manner of their master. At the same time, one cannot overlook the fact that he influenced the students who followed him like disciples more strongly—because of his forceful per: sonality, his natural charisma, and his outstanding pedagogical principles—than the maxims of his emancipationist pedagogy would lead one to expect. It should be noted as an aside, however, that other members of the Bauhaus decidedly turned away from him and sought their salvation in the private courses of the de Stijl artist Theo van Doesburg, for example.

The idea of an education that points the students at the center of its concerns, that starts with their temperament, their talent, and their abilities, that builds up from the basis of an informal student-teacher relationship, that gives priority to action over purely cognitive learning is one that stands within a large context of tradition with a highly complex nature. I am referring to the tradition of reform pedagogy. Itten had encountered the progressive ideas of reform pedagogy as part of his training as a teacher at the teachers' seminar in Bern. In his autobiographical fragment, Aus meinem Leben (From my life), he writes:

The advanced seminar was led by the twentyeight-year-old revolutionary Dr.Ernst Schneider. . . . The problems of the latest school reform were discussed around1905-6, the Sonnenschule of Vienna, Scharrelmann's reform ideas from Hamburg, all of these efforts found ardent partisans in us. There was a revolutionary air in the house. . . .119

Ernst Schneider (1878-1957) studied pedagogy with Wilhelm Rein (1847–1929), among others, in Jena at the turn of the century and practiced at the training school of the pedagogical department of the university there; he had also encountered the Educational Residence at Castle Biberstein in the Röhn that had been founded by Hermann Lietz (1868–1919), a student of Rein, before—as a committed supporter of reform pedagogy—he was appointed director of the teachers' seminar in Bern in 1905.120 Once there, however, he had to defend himself from the beginning against the criticisms and attacks of his conserva tive enemies, who finally forced his dismissal in 1916. The young Schneider was fond of experiment and fervently admired by the students in his seminar, but his critics considered him eccentric and disconnected from actual practice. He was accused of siding too much with the students and too rarely with the teachers, of not communicating enough of the material that future teachers would need, and of placing too much weight on psychoanalysis in the teachers' seminar, which was out of place and even harmful.121 With the fervor of a missionary, Schneider tried to put his convictions about reform pedagogy into practice at the seminar in Bern. This can be seen most clearly in his concept-—common in progressive pedagogy of the day—of the "free composition." Thus he refused to consider writing when grading essays and advocated "liberation instead of the coercive norms, imagination instead of grammatical correctness, dialogue between teacher and student instead of testing spelling skills."122 As mentioned, Itten practiced these principles with conviction, not only as a primary school teacher but also later as an instructor in the arts:

How can I permit myself, as an educator and teacher, to cross out the ideas of the children, to show all their mistakes andweakness with red ink?. .. The teacher as a constant corrector becomes the gravedigger of the initial childlike thinking. ... Don't cripple the students inside by making corrections but overlook their mistakes with praise and approval. This increases self-confidence, their faith in themselves. And every time the results are astonishingly good.123

Schneider's maxims of reform pedagogy were not limited to rejecting the memorization drills of the old school, to favoring teaching by experience, or to advocating the Work School (Arbeitschule) that was being promoted by Georg Kerschensteiner at the time; rather, the director of the seminar in Bern recognized the extraordinary importance of art and arts education for school reform. Here, again, he came up against Kerschensteiner, whose wide-ranging empirical study Die Entwicklung der zeichnerischen Begabung des Kindes (The development of drawing talent in children; 1905) had impressed him just as much as the artists' training movement had in general (see below for more on Kerschensteiner).

In his lecture "Die Grundlagen der Schulreform" (The principles of school reform), Schneider argued as follows:

The whole movement demands an intensive education of emotion, which the traditional, purely intellectually oriented school allowed to atrophy. In solving the question of education for the arts through the arts, we must necessarily demand an education from the inside out, in contrast to mere training, to education from the outside in. The experience of art should become a factor in education in lieu of mere learning.

He formulates the general goal of education as follows: "Education of the entire person from the inside out by means of personal achievement (work) within the community and for the community."124

Given the background of these brief remarks, there can be no doubt that Itten owed his élan for reform pedagogy above all to the teachers' seminar in Bern, and that it was probably Schneider who introduced him to the goals and methods of the arts education movement.

The arts education movement125 originated at the end of the nineteenth century and reached its high point at the First Arts Education Day in Dresden in 1901. This movement for the reform of arts pedagogy was too heterogeneous to be explored in depth here, but it can be said that it was directed above all “against intellectualism andschematism in education"andwatched "with disconcertment the lack of artistic sense" as it was “articulate in the stifling of artistic creation, of its own creative design."126 The child was discovered andcelebrated as a creator, inventor, and artist, as Carl Götze's book Das Kind als Künstler (The child as artist; 1898) shows. Götze characterized "the goal of education as the ability to control life with one's own creative forces in order to achieve good andbeautiful things. The constructive principles of life can best emerge if the child can freely develop its organic abilities, in order to be turned from its gradually crystallizing talent toward its appropriate paths. By encouraging the child to act on its own, in its interactions with other people and with things, its creative forcesare awakenedandexercised."127

In the small book Das Malen und Zeichnen (Painting and drawing) by the reform educator Heinrich Scharrelmann (1871–1940), Itten found remarks that may have confirmed for him his own pedagogical views: “The straight line, the triangle, the rectangle, the circle, and at most the oval and ellipse—these are the basic mathematical forms of all drawing."128And somewhat later: "As long as .. . training in drawing does not connect directly to the child's experience, it willalways remain unchildlike"(my emphasis— R.W.).129

Another strandwithin reform pedagogy should also be mentioned: namely, that of John Dewey (1859–1952) and the American pragmatism that he founded, whose pedagogical essence can be expressed in the concise formula “learning by doing" (for more on this, see “Learning by Doing" in chapter 7).

Dewey's pedagogical views found their way into the German Work School movement by way of Georg Kerschensteiner (1854–1932). As early as

1908, at the first Congress of the German Werkbund, Kerschensteiner called for an end to the constricting arts education in primary schools, to stop loadingchildren up with bits of knowledge—as was the practice—and instead to see to it that each child discovered its own soul and produced that which was hidden dormant within it. Like other educators of the time, Kerschensteiner was concerned about the education of the "complete human being," which definitely included developing creative abilities. For Kerschensteiner, however, the demand for creativity was not a universal principle of education but was confined to a specific period of development that ended around the age of ten. After that, in his view, children should begin to practice precise vision and manual dexterity.130

The opposition to the "decapitation" of the schools, and specially to the reduction of the artistic aspect to a cognitive one, was a common one at the time. For example, Hermann Muthesius (1861–1927), from whom we have already heard in another context (see “Hermann Muthesius and the German Werkbund" in chapter 2), also took a critical position with regard to arts education in the schools, in his capacity as adviser on arts and crafts schools in the State Department for Arts and Crafts in Berlin:

Our schools are positively teeming with scientif; ic approaches. The German schoolteacher still views art as a remote specialized subject that has nothing to do with him and for which the school has no time. . . .

We Germans have a strange tendency to allow our natural artistic abilities, which are by no means slight, to be pushed against the wall by our stronger characteristic: a scientific approach. Though we do introduce training in drawing into our schools, we organize it scientifically, so that it becomes mathematics or optics or physical exercise or who knows what. But we remain indifferent to the one thing it really should be: a means to artistic education. . . . This sort of drawing instruction is worse than none at all; for most people it destroys any pleasure in drawing for the rest of their lives. . . .

In the primary schools in London, training in drawing is done at a level that is astonishing to us; many schools devote four hours a week to drawing . . . and in most schools it is done on a genuinely artistic level. They do not laboriously copy models but follow nature. . . ."131

At the time, most of the suggestions for improving the state of arts education remained fixed at the primary school level. The area of professional training for artists was, as a rule, ignored. One exception was Joseph August Lux (1871–1947), who in his book Das neue Kunstgewerbe in Deutschland (The new arts and crafts in Germany; 1908) called for the ideas for reforming arts pedagogy to be carried out on all levels of artistic education, from the elementary level to the art academies. Lux's maxims on education can be characterized with the modern slogan "student-centered." For example, the teaching is supposed to start from the creative possibilities of the students and not from the teacher's notions of what they shouldbe doing; that is to say, the student, not the teacher, is the main issue in the teaching and learning process. The teacher is merely the hand that should leadgently from a certain distance.132

Alongside the framework of reform pedagogy, which was one factor in the development of Johannes Itten's art pedagogy, his thoughts and actions and also his theory were heavily influenced by certain philosophical, religious, and esoteric writings. Though we cannot discuss these influences in detail here, it can be said that the bulk of the authors that the artist was reading during the Vienna years and the Bauhaus period in Weimar were those "who spoke of a ... unified view of the world that rejected positivist and materialistic thinking and in doing so pointed to ancient religious or esoteric traditions."133In this context, Itten's interest in theosophy played an important role, as is clear from extended excerpts from a popular book from the turn of the century, Die grossen Eingeweihten: Skizze einer Geheimlehre der Religionen (The great initiated: Sketches of a secret doctrine of religions) by Édouard Schuré (1841–1929), that appear in his diary aroundNovember 1916. In the subsequent period, too, the diary contains indications that he was reading other important theosophical treatises, such as Die uralte Weisheit: Eine kurzgefasste Darstellungder Lehren der Theosophie (The age-old wisdom: A brief portrayal of the teachings of theosophy) and Das Denkvermögen (The ability to think) by Annie Besant (1847–1933; Besant was world president of the Theosophical Society from 1907 on) and to the essay Das Christentum als mystische Tatsache (Christianity as mystical fact) and Theosophie by Rudolf Steiner (1861–1925). In Vienna, through the circle around Alma Mahler, whom Walter Gropius had married in 1915. Itten came into contact with theosophical and—its successor—anthroposophical ideas. This was a way of thinking that is also extremely important as a key to understanding Kandinsky's work, as Sixten Ringbom has emphatically demonstrated.134A diary entry from 6 July 1918 reads:

Already for some time, and now once again when I was with Frau Mahler, the characteristic forms and colors have come to mind again and again; often they are only perceptible around people. I was always comparing and wanted to arrive at a set of characteristics of forms and colors as the expression of human ability and desires. Yesterday, almost by coincidence, I passed the theosophicalbookstore, ... and suddenlyI asked for books with illustrations, and I was given Thought-Forms by C. W. Leadbeater and A. Besant and Man Visible and Invisible by C. W. Leadbeater. Both are perhaps exactly the books that I need at the moment. After some comparison with many paintings, I have confirmed that their colors accurately characterize the things depicted.135

This quotation shows that Itten's study of esoteric texts not only corresponded to his striving for religious and spiritual involvement but also stood in direct relation to his artistic practice, with his search for the "spiritual in art" (to use the now classic title of Kandinsky's book). In this view, every color, and every form, is the expression or equivalent of a hidden spiritual element.

It was precisely the knowledge of this "spiritual element" and its laws—that is, what lies behind what is manifestly perceptible—that interested the artist and educator Johannes Itten, who in this context usually spoke of the "essence of things" or of "essences." Walter Hess has pointed out that this is a way of thinking that is related to the main movements within modern art:"The hope was to discover the laws that were as much laws of the world, of its raison d’être, as laws of the work of art."136

This strivingfor insight into “essences," for universal knowledge of art and the world, also led Itten to take an interest in Indian mysticism, in the teachings of Buddha (ca. 540–ca. 480 B.c.), and in the works of Chinese philosophers. Particularly worthy of emphasis in this context are the aphorisms of Lao-tzu (604–520 B.c.) collected in the Tao-te ching, especially the eleventh aphorism, which the artist noted in his Vienna diary several times in slightly different forms and with which he opened a 1918 exhibition of works by his Viennese students:137

Thirty spokes meet at the hub, but the empty space between them establishes the essence of the wheel; pots are made from clay, but the emptiness in them establishes the essence of the pot; walls with windows and doors form the house, but the emptiness in them establishes the essence of the house; Fundamentally: The material conceals utility; The immaterial establishes essence.138

There is an entry in Itten's diary on 27 May 1918 along similar lines: "The essence of the material is its effect of space, the immaterial. Space is the soul of the material. Space is the material of the immaterial."139And in March 1919, half a year before he moved to the State Bauhaus in Weimar,

In particular, Itten saw numerous paralleis between the Taoist philosophy of Lao-tzu and the ideals of education of Itten's own day, which were based on the individual finding him- or herself and on trusting in intuitive processes. Also of great significance for Itten was the syncretistic Mazdaism that Otoman Z. Hanisch (1844-1936) founded on the basis of the ideas of the ancient Persian religion of Zoroaster (Zarathustra):

[One of] those exclusive substitute religions which sprang up after World War I to avert the despair of the Europeans that were faced with the irretrievably sundered order by promising them inner tranquillity by turning to Oriental forms of belief. The belief is based, in short, on a duo-deism. The creation of light. .. is engagedin a constant struggle for the rule of the worldagainst the forces of darkness. . . . It is man's task to contribute towards the victory of the creation of light. This he achieves through his own uplifting as he liberates himself from the downfall of the influence of the counter-creation. He must refine his own being by expelling the forces of the material from his body, for instance, by fasting, through vegetarian food and a special diet, purges, meditation, and breathing exercises, which restore his inner peace and free him from the rat-race of civilized society.144

According to Mazdaism, the body is the "temple of the living god," and consequently the individual is obliged to bring intellectual, spiritual, and physical forces into harmonious balance. The relaxation exercises that Itten introducedat the Bauhaus, inspired by Hölzel but given an increased ideological significance by Mazdaism, should be seen against the backgroundof this quasi-religious philosophy of life.

## Between Art and Design

Naturally these were practices that sometimes divided the teachers and students into two camps (as mentioned, Itten's opponents among the students sometimes sought refuge with Theo van Doesburg), and they played a role in the conflict between Itten and the director that would weigh heavily on the Bauhaus for years, though they were certainly not the decisive factor. Two diametrically opposed conceptions were the cause behind that. Itten remarked in retrospect: "My conception for the Bauhaus was, in the end, that of a school for the arts based on new principles of education. Gropius, by contrast, wanted a program for a school of architecture, and he considered my plans—which were, admittedly, then still somewhat vague—a fantasy."145

Although Gropius's plans certainly did not include reducing the idea of the Bauhaus to an academy for architecture—or he wouldnever have appointed so many visual artists—Itten was rather remote from the director's interest in addressing problems of applied design (that is, design solutions in the way that term is used today). Consequently, he never viewed the preliminary course as merely a preliminary stage to prepare the students for their future activities as architects and designers but rather as a platform for an integral education for "complete human beings."

In fact, there were narrow limits set on the transfer of what took place under Itten in the preliminary course to the area of applied design. Marcel Franciscono notes that most of the studies produced in the preliminary course hardly addressed formal and technical problems, especially of industrial production. These studies were at best suited to guard the students against conventional, run-of-the-mill solutions that had been used a thousand times; at worst, however, the represented a serious impediment to the students' willingness to subject themselves to the formal and technical discipline that is necessary for applied design. 146

Although Itten's diaries from the years in Vienna and Weimar frequently address problems of furniture design and architecture, and at the Bauhaus he even had furniture made from his own designs (see CW 313–15), in the end he seems to have considered visual art and applied design to be incompatible, as the following statement makes clear:

Wall painting—architecture. Only in one tiny aspect do the two come in harmonious contact; namely, where architecture becomes an end in itself, as pure art. Only then is the fundamental attitude a pure one. Everything else is a compromise. Architecture is made “useful." Functional architecture is an absolutely inartistic goal. There is only one art.147

In light of this view of the problem, it is easy to understand why Itten would necessarily have fallen into a dilemma about his role as a master of form in several of the Bauhaus workshops, where the point was to put aside the role of the artist or to mediate between artistry and goal-oriented design. That this was something of a tightrope walk for Itten is demonstrated by the student works from the artists' workshops that Itten ran in parallel with the preliminary course. (As a reminder, these included the following workshops: stone sculpture until 1922, in conjunction with Schlemmer for the year 1921–22; metal workshop until 1923, from 1921 on, together with Schlemmer; workshop for wall painting until 1922, in 1921-22 in alternation with Schlemmer; glass painting until 1922; cabinetmaking until 1921; weaving in 1921).

Johannes Itten was not prepared to take part in this change of course or share in the responsibility for it. His letter of resignation is dated 4 October 1922: "Dear Mr. Gropius: Because my view of how to solve the task before the Bauhaus differs in essential points from the way you, as director of the institute, have attempted to solve it, I find myself unable to take responsibility for contributing to a successful solution. This compels me to ask you to release me from the teaching staff of the Bauhaus at the end of the winter semester 1922–23."150

In the spring of 1923 Itten left the Bauhaus. The new man was László Moholy-Nagy. The radical changes that would result for both the preliminary course and the workshops will be discussed in the following chapter. First, however, it seems worthwhile to give a brief sketch of Itten's reception after 1945 in arts pedagogy for schools.

## Itten's Reception in Arts Pedagogy for Schools after 1945

Although Itten, at the latest following the appearance of his books Kunst der Farbe (1961) and Mein Vorkurs am Bauhaus (1963), had a widespread influence on arts training in schools—even without the express approval of the makers of teaching plans and the school authorities—the reception of his theory in the professional literature on arts pedagogy in recent decades swings between devout adoration and sharp rejection. It follows a pattern that was already typical in evaluations of Itten's pedagogical practice at the Bauhaus itself, where Itten's disciples and opponents fought sectarian battles.

As we have seen, Itten's express goal was to develop the students' abilities for artistic design by means of “subjective experience and objective rationale." Only this could establish "wholeness" andachieve the goal of the "complete human being."151

We already see this goal unmistakably reversed for the first time among Itten's successors after 1945withKurt Schwerdtfeger (1897– 1966),152 who studied with Itten, among others, at the Bauhaus from 1920 to 1924. On superficial examination, his book Bildende Kunst und Schule (Visual arts and school; 1957) is entirely in debt to the pedagogical maxims of his teacher.

Compelled by the necessity to translate teaching practices and materials that were actually developed for art students to arts education in schools, and thus take into account the vertical division according to grade level, Itten's call for totality is lost in Schwerdtfeger's didactic model, in which“subjective experience" is meant to dominate at the primary schoollevel whereas “objective rationale" should dominate for older children and youths. This means that “in gradeschool years the task is a ceaseless stream from the source of the arts."153 Expressive drawing, painting, and modeling as well as handicrafts are the focus of the training. "With puberty, this takes a turn. The lingering in the realm of dreams and fantasy comes to an end and with it creation from primal things."154 Without in principle abandoning the framework of the artistic (musisch), which was Schwerdtfeger's substitute for Itten's principle of totality (Itten himself later spoke more frequently of the artistic), with older children and youths he is concerned with “education to pictorial form,"155 with visual experience of pictorial means (borrowing much from the theory of design of Itten and other Bauhaus teachers). "Here lies, even if Schwerdtfeger is not aware of it, the beginning of a rational concept of art didacticism, . . . in which art too can . .. claim its due,"156 an approach that found broad acceptance in the seventies under the rubric “formal art instruction." Thus among Itten's immediate successors, Schwerdtfeger plays a curious double role. By embodying Itten's principle of totality within his concept of “artistic education", he fulfills, on the one hand, the “expectations of the irrational group"157 in aesthetic education, while, on the other hand, his didactic model contains a decisive impulse to founda rationally conceived arts training that aims at knowledge of pictorial laws.158

Gunter Otto rightly pointed out how problematic it is to dissociate in this way Itten's theory into a subjective and an objective side, because it would ossify either into a sermon on pure subjectivism or a training based on studies of form. "Isolating these two aspects from each other is precisely what exposes the lack of any social connection, the 'ahistorical' approach in the objective domain."159Nevertheless, Otto's criticism of Itten is balanced. On the one hand, he admits to uncertainty whether it would be possible to "integrate into pictorial constellations" the "material, structural, color, or other such stimuli" that are "practiced in isolation" for reasons of the training. He also criticizes Itten's analyses of old masters as overgrown with "subjective assumptions and attempts at symbolist interpretation,"160 though, on the other hand, he credits Itten for rightly pointing to the "importance of motor activity" in the artistic process and for having contributed"to the systematization of artistic forms." His summary: "It is beyond question that Itten's concept is important for the specialist, and much of his formulation of the problem can be applied to pedagogical situations [by which Otto clearly means arts training in theschools— R.W.]."161

Hans Giffhorn, whom we shall use as a representative of Itten's opponents, comes to an entirely contrary assessment. In his book Kritik der Kunstpädagogik (Critique of arts pedagogy) of 1979, he considers Itten entirely from the no doubt one-sided perspective of whether and to what extent his pedagogy is appropriate to help students "correctly recognize their own interests, and those of the society as a whole, and to fulfill them optimally."162 This concept of society, in which Giffhorn provides the measure for what is correct and what is not, has been diluted with vulgar Marxism. The focus of Giffhorn's criticism is that Itten in general left social reality out of consideration, that he understood aesthetic practice as “self-expression," and that his conception of talent was “static” (because it insisted on innate ifferences among individuals).163 In his view, Itten's principle of knowledge is a form of “art pedagogical irrationalism,"164 which manifests itself, among other places, in that it “operates" (emphasis in the original) with a false claim of objectivity “as if there were objective good and bad pictorial means and structures, correct or false principles of artistic design."165 Giffhorn sees the danger for the students in that they are placed in the "ghetto situation of the artistic," rather than being taught political responsibility and the ability to communicate.166

Given the introduction of politics into arts pedagogy that leftist prophets advocated in the seventies, such objections were quite popular. They did nothing, however, to assess Itten's teachings in their proper context—namely, that of Bauhaus pedagogy. It is beyond question, moreover, that any attempt to set the current debates among specialists as the benchmark for a historical phenomenon raises the suspicion that it is ahistorical and inappropriate to the matter at hand.
