# The Politics of Postmodern Architecture Sean Griffiths The aesthetic of MICHAEL GRAVES (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design")—a pop-inflected, taste-challenging form of appliqué classicism—was the dominant form of architectural expression in the West during the i98os. FRANK GEHRY called it "the architecture of Ronald Reagan." Graves was a New York liberal, a member of the New York Five, a group of architects dedicated to the reinvigoration of the Corbusian tradition of purist Modernism. They became Tom Wolfe's villainous “Whites" in From Bauhaus to Our House, his i98ı polemic against modern architecture. Wolfe's goodies were the “Grays," the proselytizers of Postmodernism. By the i97os, however, Graves had transformed into a full-blown postmodernist. The architectural theorist CHARLES JENCKS (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") located the death of Modernism as July 15, 1972, the date of the demolition of Minoru Yamasaki's Pruitt-Igoe public housing scheme in St. Louis. But the critical attack on Modernism had begun a decade earlier, with the i96ı publication of Jane Jacobs's concerted attack on modernist city planning, The Death and Life of Great American Cities. Ebenezer Howard and Le Corbusier, whose urban ideas were relentlessly speared in the book, had been highly influential in advocating the rationalization of cities by compartmentalizing their functions into separate zones. But the outcomes of these experiments had been controversial, as superblocks of housing, often lacking amenities such as shops, community facilities, and employment, replaced the close grain of traditional cities. Jacobs decried top-down solutions to urban issues as the work of dangerous bureaucrats who failed to recognize people's ability to make “plans of their own." For acobs, acts of comprehensive redevelopment had only made problems of poverty, crime, and desolation worse, Instead, she championed the residents of Boston's North End who, using bartered skills and small loans, transformed slum neighborhoods into rejuvenated, vibrant places. For Jacobs, examples like this exemplified a community's innate understanding of the city as a form of organized complexity, which compared favorably to the simplistic rational ism of the city planners. In his book Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture (i966) ROBERT VENTURI (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") brought this kind of thinking to the appreciation of individual buildings. Attacking the reductive aesthetics of modern architecture and anticipating Tom Wolfe, Venturi made a claim for the ambiguous over the simplistic in architecture, stating his preference for “both-and" over “either-or” and for "gray" over “black or white." Venturi's argument was structured around concepts of complexity versus simplicity, embracing convention as well as invention and promoting adaptability against rigorous function alism, all of which was to be skillfully resolved as a "difficult whole." Among works by famous architects from history, Venturi also celebrated ordinary build ings that had gained new meanings through adaption. Both these texts were of great importance in the development of postmodernist architecture. Both rejected the rationalist, expert-led imposition of architectural and urban solutions in favor of a people-centered approach to architecture and city planning. At the same time, neither was arguing from an explicitly ideological position. Jacobs's and Venturi's books coincided with the appearance of the first cracks in the social democratic ideology that had prevailed after the Second World War. Planning projects were the ultimate physical embodiment of “big government," whereby benevolent experts implemented what was good for people. At the time of the New Deal in the United States, big government had been popular and had provided employment, renewal, and optimism at a time of economic hardship. By the early 196os, however, the negative effects of powerful government, not least in the area of city planning, were beginning to be felt and resisted. The revolt against “expertise" manifested itself in the development of grass-roots political actions, such as the BELOW Robert Venturi, drawings of homes with classical elements, 1970s. anti-Vietnam War and Civil Rights movements in the United States. The security and prosperity that social democracy enabled had generated a new consumerist individualism, leading to diverse effects like the explosion of youth culture and the rise of the American homeowner associations. New forms of libertarianism were emerging on the Left and the Right, but the ideas exemplified by postmodern architecture, originating in resistance to the technocratic overreach of government were, as it turned out, especially ripe for appropriation by the New Right, and the result was the i98o election of Ronald Reagan. Postmodernist pluralism was a form of resistance to the universalism of Modernism. But it was also a matter of cultural assertion, and the emergent culture wars of the i96os were given further impetus in the world of architecture by the publication in i972 of Robert Venturi's second book, Learning from Las Vegas. Written with his wife, the socially oriented planner ![](images/a045b745008fbddb181199a43b23b449b702e4d519dd850ef0db5020a86e3aa1.jpg) DENISE SCOTT BROWN (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design"), and colleague Steven Izenour, the book tapped into a new pop consciousness and suggested that architects might learn something from the tastes of the ordinary people upon whom they imposed their expertise. The book caused outrage among modernists. "Ideology in its purest form," shouted the critic Kenneth Frampton. For architects and other liberals, Las Vegas was the ultimate expression of vulgar capitalism. But Learning from Las Vegas was not strictly “about" the values of Las Vegas at all. Indeed, the authors implored their readers to suspend moral judgment, and to some extent the book was actually a product of the counterculture. As a study of the new automobile-driven urbanism of the Strip and as the promotion of a methodology for revealing the order that under lay the apparent visual chaos of this new kind of city, its thinking (and the graphic styling) paral leled that of more acceptably avant-garde figures such as Buckminster Fuller and Marshall McLuhan. However, whereas Venturi's earlier Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture offered a subtle and challenging route toward an architecture of "the difficult whole," in Learning from Las Vegas the authors introduced a concept that significantly influenced the architecture of the i98os. They called it "the decorated shed." The idea denoted an architecture of simple structures, decorated with signs, electronics, and ornamentation. Though perhaps a valid typology in the context of Las Vegas, it nevertheless gave architects license to splash skin-deep decorations over otherwise simple building types regardless of function or context. This approach was exemplified by Michael Graves's Portland Building of 1982 in Oregon. This building, which graced the cover of the seminal The Language of Post-Modern Architecture by Charles Jencks, was essentially a modernist steelframe building clad in a marble skin of flattened, pop-styled classicism. It became the model for many commercial developments over the next ten years, its decorative approach bringing some relief and gaiety after years of austere Miesian minimalism. But perhaps more than that, it constituted a relatively cost-effective way for developers to give otherwise mundane buildings a bit of razzamatazz. Deregulation of financial services by the Thatcher and Reagan administrations in the i98os led to an explosion in demand for new office space in global financial centers. This in turn encouraged the development of speedy and cost-efficient design and construction methodologies. One such system, imported from the United States into the United Kingdom, was called fast-track construction. Architects were now hired to do concept designs, while the in-house design teams of developers/construction companies carried out the detailing. The decorated shed concept lent itself only too well to these procedures, and the architect's input was increasingly reduced to the design of appliqué cladding. This was the method used in i988 to design the controversial Broadgate project in the City of London, a huge development of office space at a scale never before seen in the city. Later schemes in London, such as the first phases of the Canary Wharf development in the abandoned Docklands of the east, utilized the same techniques, much to the dissatisfaction of architects, whose control and level of input into the design of buildings diminished as the power of project and cost managers grew. While a sophisticated form of postmodernist architecture flourished in cultural buildings, such as HANS HOLLEIN's and JAMES STIRLING's respective museums in Mönchengladbach and Stuttgart, Germany, mainstream architecture became associated with the superficiality of a brash, vulgar, and consumerist culture. This architecture not only represented the culture of the new economics but was also a manifestation of the techniques that were deployed to finance, design, construct, and market buildings. It was in this sense that Postmodernism came to be thought of as the architecture of Ronald Reagan. Despite the successes in the idiom by architects like Hollein and Stirling that it claimed as its own, the profession was never entirely comfortable with the new populist, skin-deep architecture. The modernists suffered bitter defeats, exemplified by the row over the design of the extension to the National Gallery in London. An infamous interven tion by Charles, Prince of Wales—who described the modernist design by Ahrends, Burton & Koralek as a "monstrous carbuncle on the face of a much-loved and elegant friend"—caused the scheme to be aban doned. Review as “picturesque, mediocre slime," added insult to injury. It was a moment that marked the apotheosis of postmodern architecture in London but also signaled its decline. Thanks to Prince Charles, Postmodernism, despite its beginnings as a liberating movement against the excesses of top-down expertise, became aligned in the minds of the profession and the public with reactionary politics. In its embrace of activism, participation, and popular culture, architects had seen in Postmodernism a threat to their ability to exert some control in the built environment. The failures of Modernism had been especially painful, since the brave new world it had heralded had been inspired by the best of intentions. Never before had architects thought of themselves as so socially engaged. At the same time, they had also been reluctant to acknowledge the capture of Modernism by powerful corporatism. It was as if, in an echo of their own critiques of Postmodernism as a merely skin-deep form of architecture, they had thought that the application of the modernist “look" could clothe capitalism in an acceptable signification of social worth. Nonetheless, Postmodernism's days as the dominant architectural philosophy were over, and an architecture drawing on a retroactive embrace of neo-Constructivist aesthetics emerged in an equal and opposite reaction to the rise of Postmodernism. Perhaps Postmodernism's greatest legacy is its implicit acknowledgment in contrast to the modernist quest for timelessness, of the idea of architectural fashion. Its embrace of the skin-deep created a template for new strands of architectural Postmodernism to develop, facilitated by ever-faster new technologies. And indeed, in recent years a new kind of “cutand-paste" Postmodernism has emerged among a younger generation of architects capitalizing on the ability to manipulate images digitally and to utilize the Internet for the dissemination of ideas. The political character of this new Postmodernism is still emerging, but the grounds on which its battles are being fought are those of taste and politics. In challenging the current mainstream of “iconic" and "tasteful" Modernism, younger architects are tapping into the issues of class, inclusion, and culture that have become the dominant political issues of the present. It is also clear that the new Postmodernism, in its embrace of the image, in its engagement with social activism, and in its attempts to develop new, entrepreneurial approaches to practice, is a reflection of its origins in an internet culture—which itself is keeping the ideas of seminal figures like Jane Jacobs, Denise Scott Brown, and Robert Venturi very much alive and relevant. ![](images/79a29e5cfc0eefee8287a0774f4e756311c14c8be5eb5c584a93e7d87bf16255.jpg) ## ARQUITECTONICA Bernardo Fort-Brescia, b. 1951 Laurinda Spear, b. 1950 MIAMI'S TROPICAL PALETTE AND ADVENTUROUS FORMS MARK THE DISTINCTIVE STYLE OF THIS PARTNERSHIP. Some practitioners, in their mature years, prefer to downplay their youthful exploits. For Miami-based Arquitectonica, that may be understandable, since the firm's highly telegenic buildings were used as backdrops for a popular i98os television show depicting the city as young and hip, and perhaps more frivolous than serious. Though the show has long ended, Arquitectonica has grown in stature (along with their home city), and one of those early works has become an icon of Postmodernism. Established in i977 by Bernardo Fort-Brescia and Laurinda Spear, along with Andrés Duany\*, Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk\* and Hervin Romney (the only partner who then had an architect's license8), Arquitectonica developed a highly individual style inspired by the tropical colors of their Miami environment. After three of the original partners left,9 Spear and Fort-Brescia, by then married, became partners in a firm that would gain international recognition. Spear is a Minnesotan who grew up in Miami, studied art at Brown University, and received her MArch from Columbia University's Graduate School of Architecture in 1975. Before Arquitectonica, she worked briefly in urban planning, and has since studied landscape architecture. Fort-Brescia is a native of Lima, Peru, and was educated there and in Europe, coming to America to attend Princeton and the Harvard Graduate School of Design (i975). He then moved to Miami to teach in the University of Miami's architecture program. Arquitectonica's first project, in ı977, was a house in Miami Shores for Spear's parents and, like the house that ROBERT VENTURI (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") designed for his mother, it drew instant attention to the new firm. Called the Pink House, with facades in multiple hues of that color, it was a shocking departure from conventionally bland modern buildings and prefigured the palette of bright pastels that would distinguish much of Arquitectonica's work.'o Looking to make their mark, the young firm accepted commissions that many architects avoided, including a trio of high-rise residential buildings in downtown Miami (apartment houses were not considered prestigious projects), and turned them into tour de force design statements. The Atlantis (i982) was a waterfront condominium whose monolithic form was punctuated by a “sky court," complete with palm tree, spiral staircase, and blue whirlpool, and topped with a bright red triangular roof ornament. It was photographed and published around the country and across the world, appearing in the opening credits for the television show Miami Vice, with other color-saturated Arquitectonica projects often used as background scenery. The result was an elevated profile for the newly stylish city—and the architects. Arquitectonica also fearlessly took on shopping centers, making pedestrian locales like the Sawgrass Mills (i987) discount mall appealing for their design as well as for bargains. Arquitectonica's contributions to postmodern product design include a table for MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") and jewelry for ACME (see under “Edited Design"), as well as textiles and wallcoverings. Spear has also designed laminates for FORMICA (see under "Edited Design") and ceramics for SWID POWELL (see essay under “Edited Design"). Though they have worked around the world, Arquitectonica's roots, and much of their most important work, is in South Florida and, in the tradition of Miami-based midcentury architect Morris Lapidus, tends to be extroverted, almost exhibitionistic. They often deploy gravity-defying forms with unexpected curves and angles, and in the 199os designed three Disney All-Star resorts that dressed up motel-type buildings in Pop art and DISNEY (see under “Edited Design") iconography. Beeby was one of the Chicago Seven\* architects, a group that came to national attention in i976 when they protested the emphasis on Miesian Modernism in an exhibition at the city's Museum of Contemporary Art by organizing a counterexhibition suggesting a new approach to the city's architecture. These early supporters of what would soon be labeled Postmodernism staged what became the notorious “Exquisite Corpse" show, proposing alternative styles that referenced history. By reviving interest in traditional architecture and urban design, the group helped to shift the direction of Chicago's development Following the same principles, Beeby has been an influential voice in the New Urbanism movement, which seeks to improve the urban experience by reintroducing diversity and human-scale buildings. After graduating from Cornell University (i964) and the Yale School of Architecture (1965), Beeby worked for C. F. Murphy Associates before forming Hammond Beeby & Associates with Jim Hammond, where he was chief designer for more than four decades. He is currently Chairman Emeritus of the firm (now HBRA Architects) Beeby's career has combined architectural practice with teaching, first at Illinois Institute of Technology (ı973-79), where he was one of the first instructors who had not attended that school, then as director of the School of Architecture at the University of Illinois, Chicago (i98o-85), and finally as Dean of Yale School of Architecture (i985–92). He has continued to teach and lecture there as well as at a variety of prestigious museums and institutions. Beeby's buildings have been strongly influenced by his respect for classicism, and his best-known works include the Rice Building addition to the Art Institute of Chicago (i988), an understated, historicist structure that was praised for avoiding the “self-conscious prettiness" of many traditional buildings.'² For the Harold Washington Library Center (i99ı), on the other hand, Beeby designed a trafficstopping downtown landmark for what was then the country's largest circulating library (it is still one of the largest). Beeby's building was criticized for its classical-inspired postmodern design,'3 which won the commission in i987 over proposals favoring a more modern structure. The architect defended it as a hybrid of modern and nineteenthcentury ideals, inverting Miesian architecture by putting the building's core on the exterior rather than the interior.'4 Like many postmodern buildings, it references historical precedents, in this case those by Chicago masters; Beeby has noted the allusions in the window system to H. H. Richardson's Marshall Field Warehouse, and in the classical ornament, brickwork, and rusticated base to Daniel Burnham's Rookery. For Paul Newman's Hole in the Wall Gang Camp (ı988), Beeby designed a whimsical row of facades that suggest an old-time Western film set, again following his characteristic blending of modern and traditional—in this case the vernacular elements revisited by the postmodernists.
