![](images/ac893a8006be7b5d5d91643441f9d2fa04aabdd537e02dbf14af256779ca11f4.jpg) Georg Jensen, Model no. 263 silver bowl for Georg Jensen, 1918 # georg jensen ## (b. 1866 Rådvad – d. 1935 Hellerup, Denmark) ![](images/c7e522e2e6241ab67f88f64bfadaea74f07abb55a5b1a37b9685594fde0af1d5.jpg) ![](images/d0ea8bae36ec2508bbf59023cc33049728e3d4a44412d6ee52d2f3435d35ba18.jpg) The Georg Jensen name refers not only to a man and his firm, but to a style that throughout the 20th century has epitomized Scandinavian design. Georg Jensen, the designer, was born on the outskirts of Copenhagen in the small industrial town of Rådvad, which had both a steelworks and a water-driven grindery where his father worked as a blacksmith. As a young child he worked in the foundry and became an apprentice in the braziers' workshop. At the age of fourteen, his parents recognized his metalworking ability and decided to move to Copenhagen where he served an apprenticeship as a goldsmith. From 1884 he worked as a journeyman, but he was not content with the prospect of remaining just a highly-skilled craftsman. Consequently from c. 1895 to 1901 he trained as a sculptor at the Kongelige Danske Kunstakademi (Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts) where he was influenced by the designer C.C. Peters (1822-1899), who worked in the Neo-Classical style. During this period, Jensen produced sculptural ceramics in collaboration with the painter Joachim Petersen(1870-1943)for theMogens Ballin workshop, which wasjust outside of Copenhagen. After completing his studies, and unable to earn a living as a sculptor, Jensen turned his attentions to design. He began making ceramics with Christian Joachim for the Aluminia pottery and later became a designer at the Bing & Grøndahl porcelain factory in Copenhagen. In 1904, he established his own silversmithy and, rebuking the contemporary taste for historicist metalware, he began designing Arts & Crafts style jewellery and silverware that was influenced by natural forms, such as berries, leaves and swirling tendrils. Jensen's most notable designs were the Blossom(c. 1904-1905) and Grape (c. 1918) ranges, which comprised a variety of pieces from tableware to jewellery. Around 1906, he persuaded the artist Johan Rohde (1856– 1935) to produce a number of designs for the workshop and these included the well-known Acorn pattern(1915) that epitomized the transition from the Arts & Crafts style to Art Deco – from naturalistic ornamentation to a more stylized and abstracted form of decoration. During the 1920s, Jensen recruited other avant-garde designers, such as Harald Nielsen, Gundorph Albertus(1887– 1970), and Sigvard Bernadotte to produce designs for the company. At the same time, Rohde introduced unornamented modern forms to the firm's line of silverware and in so doing heralded a new direction for the silversmithy. Upon Jensen's death in 1935, the running of the company was taken over by his son Jørgen Jensen(1895-1966), who perpetuated itsforward-looking ethos. After the Second World War, the company began producing supremely elegant Modern handcrafted silverware designed by Henning Koppel and Tias Eckhoff, which exemplified the functional and aesthetic purity of Scandinavian Modernism. During the post-war years, the firm also began manufacturing glassware by Finn Juhl and stainless steel products by Arne Jacobsen, such as his flatware of 1957 (original manufactured by A. Michelsen). Throughout the 1960s and 1970s, the company produced a number of beautiful and sculptural jewellery and wristwatch designs by Nanna Ditzel and Vivianna Bülow-Hübe that were in tune with the more casual lifestyle of the period. In 1973, Royal Copenhagenpurchased the Georg Jensen brand and since the late 1970s the silversmithy has produced gold and silver jewellery, elegant watches, and stainless steel and silver tableware, which either incorporate the company's “trademark” undulating organic forms or express a classical pared-down aesthetic. In 1997 Georg Jensen became part of the Royal Scandinavia group, which also owns Royal Copenhagen, Holmegaard, Orrefors, Kosta Boda, Boda Nova, and Höganäs Keramik brands. Today, the Georg Jensen company continues to uphold its long-held commitment to innovative design, superlative craftsmanship, and high-quality manufacturing standards – attributes that have ensured that its name has remained utterly synonymous with Danish design excellence. ![](images/49135645ca195f9381cda751f04599a2093a61d4b97e10bebebd1241c57664f6.jpg)
