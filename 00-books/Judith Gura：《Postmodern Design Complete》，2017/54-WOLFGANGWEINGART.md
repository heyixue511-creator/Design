# WOLFGANG WEINGART b.1941 GERMAN DESIGNER AND INFLUENTIAL TEACHER WHO PIONEERED NEW WAVE IN GRAPHIC DESIGN. In the middle years of the twentieth century, Swiss style—a grid-based form founded on linearity, sans serif type, and elements drawn from Bauhaus-aesthetic typography—dominated the practice of graphic design. A serious and academic approach, it focused on content, rather than distracting decoration. Wolfgang Weingart is credited with changing that narrow view, opening up new possibilities for type to be regarded a design tool, graphic design an art form, and trade schools to be considered design institutions. Discarding the constrictions of right-angle layouts, he rearranged formats, resurrected traditional typefaces that had been abandoned by the modern movement, and moved away from manual typesetting and the letterpress to using halftone film, collage, and offset printing as well as the new Xerox photocopier and, later, even the Macintosh. His work introduced what came to be called Swiss Punk, and foreshadowed the New Wave in graphic design. His advanced class in graphics at the Allgemeine Künstgewerbeschule Basel (now Schule für Gestaltung Basel) was an intensive, interdisciplinary program that encouraged creative expression and took an intellectual approach to what had been treated as a somewhat mechanical exercise. Type weights varied, typefaces were mixed and set at irregular angles or in curved configurations, and the type shop was treated as a laboratory for experimentation. ![](images/11161c9f38a8094ddf567cf35421e0c4352ce87023bd8217bbd938d74de14390.jpg) Weingart was born in southern Germany and attended the Merz Akademie in Stuttgart, learning linocut and woodcut techniques. Apprenticing as a typesetter, he was trained in the Swiss Style, but rebelled against the limitations of the strict regimen and began to seek a more creative direction. He became interested in design, and traveled to Basel to study at its prestigious school of applied arts.3º In ı968, still virtually unknown, Weingart was put in charge of the typography class in a new postgraduate program in graphic design for professionals, established by Emil Ruder and Armin Hofmann. He continued to teach the course for thirty-seven years. Weingart's revolutionary approach drew students from thirty-five countries to a program that was sometimes called “anti-Swiss." and trained next-generation students including NEVILLE BRODY, David Carson\*, and APRIL GREIMAN, all of whom went on to pursue their own, innovative paths. He also ran a summer program in graphic design for Yale and lectured internationally.
