Initially the factory produced designs that were hand-painted with simple floral patterns but by the late 1830s the company had adopted various new techniques for ceramics manufacture from England, including transfer printing. At this stage Gustavsberg was also producing bone china using imported clay from Cornwall, and employed British workers skilled in china making. During this period, the patterns used often closely followed those of British wares. Reputedly, one of the owners of the factory had a double-walled suitcase in which he smuggled copper-plates between England and Sweden. Nevertheless, the factory soon became celebrated for its wares and achieved commercial success when its Parian figurines (also based on British antecedents) were awarded a gold medal at the 1867 Paris “Exposition Universelle”. In the mid-1860s, Gustavsberg broadened its production of both “art” wares and tableware, but duringthe 1870s it came under increasing attack for the “bad taste” of its products. The management responded to this criticism by issuing ceramics that were truly Swedish in nature, and by commissioning August Malmström(1829-1901) and Magnus Isäus (1841–1890) to produce designs in the Viking style. By 1900, under the directorship of Wilhelm Odelberg, the factory had 1,000 employees and was manufacturing delicately painted ceramics designed by Gunnar Wennerberg in the ArtNouveau style. The factory's output later came under the influence of the Svenska Slöjdföreningen (Swedish Societyof Craft and Industrial Design), founded in 1845, an organization that was later inspired by the Deutscher Werkbundand simi larly promoted an alliance between art and industry. Its rallying cry for the production of Vackrare Vardagsvara (MoreBeautiful Everyday Objects) was taken up byWilhelm Kåge, who had been appointed the artistic director of Gustavsbergin 1917. Under Kåge's leadership during the 1920s and 1930s, the factory's output became increasingly influenced by the Modern Movement. Kåge's stackable open-stock Praktika ware (1933), for example, was utterly utilitarian and epitomized the functionalist approach to design that was being promoted by the Continental avant-garde. The same year, Gustavsberg was sold to the Kooperativa Förbundet (Swedish Co-Operative Union and Wholesale Society) and the factory was modernized. Two years later, it began production of sanitary wares, and in 1945started manufacturing products in plastics. In 1949, Stig Lindberg took over from Wilhelm Kåge as artistic director. During his tenure, Gustavsberg produced both industrial products and more limited artistic wares. During the1970s, Sven-Eric Juhlin (b. 1940) – who founded Ergonomi Design Gruppen – and Jan Landqvist designed sanitary wares and plastic products for the company that were primarily intended for institutional use. In 1980, Karin Björquist (b. 1927) succeeded Lindberg as the factory's artistic director and in 1987 the company was sold to the Finnish company Wärtsilä. Later, Gustavsberg was divided into three small companies that continued to produce ceramics in its old factory. This wood was lumbered from forests owned by the company and processed in its sawmills. When Johan Friedrich Hackman died in 1807 his widow consolidated the company. At the same time, his son introduced a number of industriallyproduced products that were made and retailed by the company. By the early 1860s, Hackman was using steam-driven saws to increase its productivity, and the company's subsequent expansion led to the acquisition of the Havi Candle Factory in 1866 and the founding of a cutlery factory, close to Viipuri, in 1876. This new, larger enterprise undertook the manufacture of a wide range of wares, including kitchen knives, hunting knives, scissors, skates and forks. Hackman later exhibited at the 1878 Paris “Exposition Universelle", where it was awarded a gold medal for its kitchenware and cutlery designs, In 1891, the company's cutlery factory was relocated close to its sawmill in Sorsakoski. After a fire at the sawmill in 1898, the factory expanded its product-line to include handcrafted tools, a variety of household utensils, and a number of products made of sheet metal, In 1902, Hackman began using a nickel-steel alloy for its knife and fork handles – a new material that had been developed in France in 1885 and was remarkable for its strength. Later in 1923, Hackman began manufacturing products in another newly developed material – stainless steel. Like other Scandinavian manufacturers, Hackman was highly instrumental in the promotion of this revolutionary material through its production of elegant yet popular designs. During the early 1950s, Hackman introduced its first stainless steel saucepans (which were initially made by hand) and started commissioning well-known designers such as Kaj Franck to contribute to its product-line. The company subsequently received widespread recognition when it launched Bertel Gardberg's Canton cooking pan and Carelia cutlery in 1957 – designs that exemplified Hackman's pursuit of design and manufacturing excellence, Over the following years, Hackman continued to specialize in the production of stainless steel household wares, which on occasion featured handles made of exotic hard woods or plastics. One of the most iconic designs produced by Hackman was Tapio Wirkkala's ## stainless steel and nylon-handled Puukko knife (1961), which was inspired by traditional Sami hunting knives. During the 1960s and 1970s, Nanny Still McKinney and Ilmari Tapiovaara also produced designs for the company. In 1969, Hackman & Co became part of a joint stock company and later in 1989, joined an amalgamated trust group known as Designor Oy (which today also owns the Arabia, littala and Rörstrand brands). Having subsequently become the leading design house in Finland, the company reaffirmed its commitment to avant-garde design by holding a large design competition in 1990, with the winning entries being put into production. During the late 1990s and early 2000s, Hackman commissioned a number of leading designers, including Björn Dahlström, Harri Koskinen, Carina Seth-Andersson (b. 1965), Antonio Citterio (b. 1950) and Ross Lovegrove (b. 1958), to create a new and exciting range of cutlery, utensils and cookware known as the Tools collection. The manufacture of functional innovative and simple products such as these has ensured Hackman's position at the forefront of a major international revival of interest in Finnish design.
