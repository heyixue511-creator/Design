# Prehistoric Prelude to Graphic Design 35,000-2700 Bce Basic graphic concepts such as figure/ground distinctions, scale relations, organization of images, and effects of color were developed in prehistoric sign and image making. The processes of early image production imply forethought and preparation— of pigments, surfaces, and tools—that distinguish conscious design from incidental traces. Graphic communication has always depended upon the design of symbols and images that are recognized according to the conventions of a community. The design of prehistoric symbolic forms suggests that early humans did not simply record their needs and activities but placed great value on the representation of ideas. 0.1 Stenciled hand, Pech Merle, France, 23,000–18,000 Bce. The urge to make marks is a fundamental human impulse. Creating external symbols gives back an image of the self while leaving a sign in the world. The stenciled hand shown here is thousands of years old, but similar signs can be found throughout history. Graphic design arises from the relation between that basic impulse and the system of communication that connects individuals within a culture. The impulse toward personal expression and compliance with convention are the twin engines of all graphic design. Connecting contemporary graphic design to visual art in the earliest periods of human civilization might seem like a stretch. But, in fact, Stone Age artists established the basic conventions essential to graphic design as early as 35,000 years ago. These foundations are now so familiar that they are taken for granted. Precisely because they are so fundamental to later visual communication, the remarkable achievements of prehistoric artists deserve attention. Prehistoric artists organized surfaces to support figure and ground distinctions, establishing the basis on which signs and images could be produced and read. 'They prepared specialized materials for works of art, demonstrating that graphic expression was a deliberate process, not simply the result of impulsive acts. Finally, they shared a common visual vocabulary, indicating that they worked within a system of socially recognized conventions. Each of these characteristics is a uniquely human phenomenon. We are the only species that has a developed sign system abstracted from literal meaning and immediate circumstances. Human signs have a dual identity as representations and communications. As representations, human signs stand in for or refer to things that are absent, such as ideas, objects, feelings, or events. As communications, they enable social exchanges among people and across time and space. We can point to prehistoric art as the origin of graphic design because it presents clear evidence of conscious decisions about form. Stone Age sculptures and cave paintings exhibit deliberate intention and a high level of craft. They are designed objects that often feature decorative images and marks. Their striking shapes, styles, and imagery carry information about other social and ritual practices and thus have been the focus of anthropological study. The material record of this early period has much to tell us about the technological and cultural conditions of image and object production. Evolutionary foundations of communication The history of graphic design begins with the emergence of a distinct human culture. In Africa, between three and four million years ago, the earliest hominids (proto-humans) began to walk upright, distinguishing themselves from other apes. This posture is considered the precondition for the evolution of modern humans. Standing upright freed our hands to make tools. Anthropologists suggest that this physical change also produced new kinds of behaviors, including increased socialization and use of language. Hands play a primary role in the creation of symbolic forms. The ability to make marks and signs depends upon motor skills and conceptual thought. Eye, hand, and mind are all coordinated in the human designer whose capacity to devise symbolic systems is the direct result of these earlier physical and social developments (Fig. 0.2). Language and design Archaeological evidence suggests that spoken language preceded writing. As early as 150,000–200,000 years ago, human beings apparently used language to organize complex social activities such as hunts. Such objects provide very early evidence of design as a mode of intentional form-giving. In making these tools, human designers understood the idea of shapes for use—independent of their embodiment by any particular object. Early humans could generalize the design of a form to suit a purpose and repeat it across the production of many individual objects. They had a model of the design in mind to guide tool making (Fig. 0.3). The general category of a tool, such as "something to poke the earth with," can be fulfilled by any number of objects. This capacity to distinguish an abstract idea, or model, from a specific realization is a basic component of both design and language. Many creatures use signs to exchange information, but only humans have the capacity to represent absent and abstract phenomena in symbolic form. Animal sign systems are based on direct references to things that are present and physical. Their methods of communicating are often closely related to the messages they carry, as in chemical trails (ants), flight patterns (bees), markings (territorial animals), display (mating rituals), and cries and sounds (mating calls, danger signals). Animals have no capacity for abstraction and no developed sense of history or time, at least according to current scientific study. By contrast, human language and graphic systems use signs that are divorced from physical evidence or concrete references. Capable of representing things, ideas, actions, and abstractions, the signs of human language are socially produced. With these conventionalized visual and verbal gestures and forms, any information, emotion, or idea can be translated. Early graphic forms The earliest works of visual communication were produced in three separate periods before the invention of writing (in about 3000 BCE). Art from the earliest of these, the Stone Age, or Paleolithic period, dates from about 35,000 BCE. Although visual design, art, and tool making are closely linked at this stage, all the founding principles of graphic communication can be distinguished in the formal expression and conventional signs of the carved figures, decorated tools, and cave paintings that remain from this period (Fig. 0.4). Stone Age artists in Western Europe were part of Cro-Magnon culture. Collective hunting was one of the first organized activities and was recorded in images. Cro-Magnon artists approached their subjects realistically. When the cave paintings at Altamira, Spain, were discovered in the late 1870s, they created a sensation. Far from being primitive expressions by unskilled artists, these paintings were sophisticated at every level of conception and execution. Because they were recognized as finely made, they challenged concepts of progress in art. The figures are clear and distinct, and the ground on which they appear is a wellprepared and organized surface. These paintings are not only powerful aesthetic expressions, they also document a long history. Successive waves of climate change, for example, were recorded in cave art. Images of reindeer, painted on earlier layers, were replaced by those of ibexes and other animals as the ice sheets receded in later miliennia. Although drawn from memory, the images are naturalistic. What is remarkable, however, is how much similarity exists among cave paintings. This striking fact shows that a distinct set of visual conventions had been established for producing artworks. Although it would be a stretch to call the world of prehistoric cave painters a design community, rules governing what was formally possible and acceptable were followed by these artists. The artists did not simply invent visual solutions at random in response to circumstances or impulses. They produced highly organized works of art, designed according to formal and cultural conventions. Communicating ideas and beliefs The traditional interpretation of cave art is that it was a form of sympathetic hunting magic. More recent theories propose that these paintings had an intellectual purpose. According to this interpretation, these images followed from the notion that something that was "good to eat" might also be pleasurable to think about. 'The paintings not only responded to needs (or fulfilled a superstitious function) but also corresponded to ideas. These graphic forms are now considered to be the symbolic expression of a worldview rather than simply an attempt to gain power over the uncertain forces of nature. The realization that prehistoric graphic signs embody values and express beliefs places them on a continuum that includes contemporary visual design. We have very little sense of the identity of Stone Age artists. The cave painter might have been a shaman possessed of spiritual power, a loner sent off to a far corner of the cave, an idle doodler taking pleasure in aesthetic expression, a craftsperson especially capable of fine work, or some combination of these. In any case, evidence of advanced material preparation implies a pause between conception and execution that allows for critical reflection upon the act of making. The gap between thinking and making is crucial to all forms of design. ![](images/f2587f0948c1580d7bd3bd2b3779dc36e9e353521353f99539955cfc61bca109.jpg) 0.6 Clay seal, Mesopotamia, 3000 BCE. Clay seals dating back as early as the fourth millennium Bce have been found in archaeological sites of the ancient city of Uruk, present-day Iraq. These seals provide evidence of a flourishing agricultural and urban economy. In the act of creating individual patterns that served as signs of ownership, ancient scribes established basic design conditions and conventions. For instance, a pattern had to be distinctive if it was to function as a signature. A surface or material support had to be prepared and unified so that it defined the space of a message. Signs and marks had to be consistent—or intentionally varied—in scale, proximity, orientation, and groupings. Pigments and colors, where used, had to be refined and deliberately applied. In other words, basic elements of composition and display were developed in the graphic codes of these early impressions made by seals rolled across soft clay. The invention of proto-writing The Mesolithic period (or Middle Stone Age)—about 10,000-5000 BCE—is characterized by the development of agriculture, leading to the rise of cities and civic organization in the ancient Middle East. It is also marked by a huge leap in graphic communication: the appearance of proto-writing. This development is linked to increased population and changes in the social organization of human settlements. City structures took social and cultural form within clearly defined boundaries (urban design in the ancient city of Jericho can be traced to its earliest walls, which were 13 feet high by 8000 BCE). The invention of proto-writing was a direct consequence of successful cultivation of hard grains (which were capable of being stored without rotting) at the beginning of the Neolithic period (or New Stone Age)—about 5000-2000 BCE—in the ancient Mesopotamian region. When more of this grain could be grown in a season than was needed for immediate or local use, accounting systems became necessary to help monitor ownership, distribution, and storage. These systems relied, for the first time, on symbols designed to signify numerical value and specific objects in the world. After about 8000 BCE, the numeric and pictographic signs of this proto-writing were used on tokens and clay tablets to mark ownership and quantities of goods. These cultural changes took place much later among settlements in Northern Europe, Africa, and Asia—and even later in the Americas (Fig. 0.6). Conclusion Graphic design depends on symbolic systems that are visually stable and shared by convention. The early evolution of human language and imagery laid crucial foundations for later developments in the design of all material objects, including graphic communications. Even in cave art, technologies of production—the care given to preparation of materials and tools—attest to an awareness of the creative process. The idea that graphic forms not only give shape to needs and desires but also express a view of the world is critical to all phases of human history. Distinctions of figure and ground that are fundamental to the organization of visual perception were made in cave art, and other aesthetic properties of composition, color, and imagery were developed. Later distinctions among different types of signs and symbol systems allowed the representation of numbers, words, and things to exist side by side without confusion. Graphic conventions for arranging and reading marks and signs on a surface were already in place at the end of the prehistoric era. These elements remain essential to visual communication and provide a basis that connects current design practices to this prehistoric prelude.
