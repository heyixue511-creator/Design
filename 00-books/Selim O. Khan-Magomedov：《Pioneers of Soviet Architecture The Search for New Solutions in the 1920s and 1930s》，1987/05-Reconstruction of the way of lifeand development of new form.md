The grouped garden-city theory also continued to exert a marked influence on the formulation of projects for the reconstruction of Moscow in the early 1930s. An outline scheme for replanning Moscow was prepared at the end of 1930 under Semenov's direction. It suggested that the city required radical reconstruction and represented an ill-defined urban form. Semenov described it as the Moscow blob . . . a historically generated conglomeration of factories, buildings, streets and green spaces’. It needed to be deliberately broken up into a system of city complexes’ arranged around the old city centre in accordance with a predetermined plan. Each city complex was to be given a significant degree of autonomy in planning and administrative matters and immersed in a protective and healthgiving green zone. In an outline plan produced in 1930, Le Corbusier proposed that the area of Moscow should be cut back and filled with greenery by redeveloping it with high-rise buildings, while preserving the best historical settings, substituting a rectangular grid pattern for its radial-concentric plan and drastically reconstructing the bulk of existing buildings. A closed competition to find suggestions for the reconstruction of Moscow was carried out in 1932. Seven teams took part. In his competition design, as in his article published in 1930, Ladovsky advocated the application of a parabola-shaped planning scheme so as to gradually transform the radial-concentric lay-out of Moscow into a dynamic structure developing in a particular direction. The design submitted by Vladimir Kratyuk's team also proposed the development of the city beyond the historically conditioned radial-concentric lay-out, but – as distinct from Ladovsky's proposal – the growth ofits area was planned in several directions at once with differing degrees of scale and intensity. The remaining designs combined the preservation of the city's historical nucleus with the decentralization of the entire structure of Moscow, ranging from the articulation of the city into districts to its conversion into a set of independet settlements. They formed a single conurbation, with Moscow, the big city, still retaining its importance. In Ernst May's design, the compact big city was in fact replaced for all intents and purposes by a system of satellite towns linked with a common centre. The discussion early in the 1930s about the further development of Moscow, and the designs submitted for its replanning, stimulated additional work on the preparation of a general plan for the city's reconstruction. All the aspects and every stage of the development of the new society are reflected in this search for an appropriate form of housing. Soviet architects closely followed the social changes taking place around them as they worked on their new designs. They studied the proposals of utopian socialists, such as More, Campanella, Veiras d’Allais, Morellet, Dézamy, Fourier, Owen and Saint-Simon, as well as those of the Marxist-Leninist classic writers. The latter treated the housing problem and its solutions under socialism as part of the much wider problem of socialist resettlement, in connection with the resolution of the conflict between town and country and the involvement of women in the social production process. Engels wrote that the liberation of Woman, the achievement by her of equality with men is impossible . . . so long as Woman is kept away from socially productive labour and confined to domestic work’.' Lenin also devoted much attention in his writings to the reconstruction of the way of life in which he saw the opportunity for a genuinely socialist position on the status of women. He wrote in March 1917, as part of an analysis of various aspects of the structure of life undersocialism:...without drawing women into social service . . . and political life, without drawing them out of their stultifying domestic and kitchen situation, it is impossible to ensure genuine freedom, it is impossible to build even democracy, let alone socialism’.² After the October Revolution, Lenin repeatedly returned to the problem of the socialist reconstruction of the way of life. He regarded practical measures in this field as imperativeand urged that all embryonic manifestations of such restructuring should be sought out in everyday life, to be fostered and propagated. At the First All-Russian Congress of Working and Peasant Women called by the Central Committee of the Communist Party in November 1918, Lenin said: Woman is crushed by her domestic work, and only socialism can save her from this situation.'³ After a thorough examination of the problems involved in women building a new society, the Congress adopted a resolution which stated that: In the place of kitchen pots and wash troughs we must put, in town as in thecountry, communal kitchens, communal eating places, central laundries, workshops for mending clothes, collectives for the cleaning of linen and dwellings,etc."4 ‘Woman,’ wrote Lenin in 1919, remains a domestic slave despite all liberating legislation, because menial domesticity presses on her, strangles her, renders her mindless and humiliates her by fettering her to kitchen and nursery, squandering her labour in anunbelievably unproductive, mean, enervating and depressing way.The genuine liberation of women begins only at that place and time at which the struggleagainst such demeaning domesticity is undertaken, or more accurately, the mass reconstruction into large-scale socialist management begins.’ On 8 March 1920, in its special edition devoted to International Woman's Day, Pravda published an article in which Lenin yet again stressed the close connection between the genuine liberation of women and the radical reconstruction of domestic life: To involve woman in social-productive labour, to extract her from “domestic slavery", to liberate her from the subjection which dulls and humiliates her in the ceaseless and unremitting environment of kitchen and nursery – that is the main task. This is a long struggle which requires a radical reshaping of both social practice and ethics. ## The growth of communal living: a new social brief for housing The actual changes which the Soviet way of life was undergoing at that time exerted a strong influence of their own on the search for a new type of dwelling. On 20 August 1918, the Presidium of the All-Russian Central ExecutiveCommittee issued adecree Concerning the Abolition of Private Real Estate in Cities’. All the more valuable dwellings were transferred to the local councils. A mass resettlement of workers began, moving them out of shacks and cellars into the houses confiscated from the bourgeoisie. In 1918–24, nearly 500,000 people were moved into comfortable apartments in Moscow, and some 300,000 were likewise moved in Petrograd. Housing Communes sprang up of their own accord for social, political or simply domestic purposes, as part of the massed move of workers into bourgeois apartments. The victorious proletariat was intent on converting the expropriated houses into springs of communist culture. The properties originally built for investment and rental were renamed Housing Communes - workers' houses or communal houses – and treated as new forms of workers’ dwellings, the domestic arrangements and organization of which would help to develop collective habits among the population and educate its communist consciousness. During 1918–20, before the introduction of the New Economic Policy, the workers paid no rent and each house set up an administration ofits own which would not only manage the building, but also organize collective arrangements, such as communal canteens and kitchens,kindergartens, crèches, rest rooms, library reading rooms, laundries, and so on. All these, as well as the cleaning and maintenance of communal premises, were carried out collectively. The Housing Communes which spontaneously developed in 1918 were officially recognized by local councils from 1919 as an approved method for the use of dwellings by a workers’ collective. Such communal self-management of housing by the workers was very widespread in the early Soviet period. There were 865 Housing Communes in Moscow at the end of 1921, and 242 were registered in Kharkov during the years 1922–25. Housing Communes played an important part in the upkeep of the nationalized building stock that had been handed over rent-free to the workers. They did much to improve living conditions and to consolidate new political principles in the way of life of the labouring classes. The very term Housing Commune’ described a new social focus and a new relationship among the inhabitants of a building. The domestic communes formed in the early post-Revolutionary years were voluntary consumer associations of workers with a set of rules to regulate the lives of all their members. Fully fledged communes were governed by a general assembly and a Communal Council. The domestic communes varied widely in size. In Petrograd, for instance, there was in1919 a commune consisting of thirty-two people sharing their everyday existence, while in Kiev, in 1922, the workers of the Arsenal factory set up a commune in an old house in which each of the eighty flats housed two or three families, and in which a club, reading room, bakery, baths, laundry and storeroom had been set up. When the Housing Commune movement gathered momentum, the conversion of communal houses into centres of the new communist culture, the creation of domestic collectives and the transformation of the way of life through self-administered communal institutions were among the tasks set for it. But even when this movement was at its peak, communal ways of life developed very slowly in nationalized houses. It was thought at the time that the main reason for this lag was that old houses were ill-suited to the new way of life and that the provision of specially designed communal dwellings, complete with the requisite social services, would lead to a more rapid movement in this direction. There was, however, no agreement about what architectural form the new dwellings should take. Three basic concepts were in competition. The first was linked to the garden-city theory and aimed at a communal settlement consisting of individual houses, together with public buildings. The second mainly stressed the role of communal housing complexes, and a socialization of the way of life in which not only the domestic economy but the family itself would be radically reconstructed. The supporters of the third concept believed that buildings specially designed for fully developed Housing Communes could not be built immediately, but that a transitional type of dwelling the collective house – must be designed so as to assist with the gradual integration of new habits into the existing way of life. Such a house would continue to offer accommodation for individual families, but would incorporate collective institutions, such as communal kitchens and dining rooms, crèches, laundries, and so on. The Housing Communes that arose spontaneously in nationalized accommodation served as a basis for the formulation of the new social dwelling requirement. They were regarded as an important advance in the field of housing, representing prototypes for the dwellings of the future and actual embodiments of the new way of life.The experience they offered was studied and the indications about a new way of life arising from them were spotlighted as models. This new way of organizing the life of the workers had the support of the Party, the trade unions and all the other social organizations in the country. The programme formulated by Lenin and adopted at the Eighth Party Congress stated that: 'Without confining itself to the formal statement of women's equal rights, the Party is intent upon liberating them from the material ties of an outdated domestic economy through the provision of communal canteens, central laundries, crèches and the like."7 The movement for reshaping the way of life arose among the workers themselves. When they took over the homes and real estate investments of the bourgeoisie they rejected the entire social system developed by the old society. Thus self-management by the residents replaced private ownership of housing. There was rent-free accommodation for workers, instead of high payments for living space; an explicitly collective approach to everyday life, instead of the isolation and segregation of individual families; broad mutual co-operation, instead of the friction and rivalry aroused by the pursuit of prestige in, for instance, the cult of material things’, and voluntary self-help, instead of the employment of servants and maintenance staff. The Housing Communes served as a kind of test-bed for trying out new ways of organizing life. It was here that the systems of communal social services which were to develop later first arose spontaneously and manifested themselves in a variety of ways. This principally involved aspects of communal and cultural activities with a bearing on such crucial socio-political tasks as the liberation of women from domestic work, so as to bring them into the field of production and social life, with the help of canteens, communal kitchens, laundries, kindergartens etc, and the launching of a cultural reyolution through inauguration of such facilities as reading rooms and Red Corners. ## Communal houses The experience acquired in Housing Communes led to the formulation of a programme for the development of new dwellings suitable for this purpose, and the appearance of corresponding experimental and competition designs during the first half of the 1920s. One of the earliest projects of this sort was put forward by Ladovsky and Krinskyin 1920 atZhivskulptarkh. This involved multi-storey buildings of complex design in which accommodation of various kinds was grouped around a covered courtyard. A competition was launched at the end of 1921 for the design of a standard collective dwelling for use in the development of the Petrograd suburban area. The specification involved the construction of a transitional type of dwelling for thirty families combining the normal domestic amenities with some communal facilities. The first prize went to David Buryshkin and Lev Tverskoy for a building of complex design the central part of which was to house a canteen and kitchen, a library reading room and a crèche, while four wings with cottage-type' living quarters (but no cooking facilities) radiated from the centre. A competition held at the end of1922 for model workers' houses in two Moscow residential districts also specified a transitional type of dwelling. Requirements for family and single living quarters were clearly defined: the two- and three-room family flats were to be provided with a full set of domestic offices, including kitchens; adjoining communal facilities included a kindergarten, crèche, library reading room, storage rooms, baths and showers. Childless couples and single people were to have individual rooms of appropriate size, with wash basins. The accompanying communal accommodation included reception rooms,library reading rooms, canteens, kitchens, storage rooms, baths, showers, lavatories etc. The district was also to be provided with a surgery, garage, laundry, housing management office and meeting hall. In the majority of competition entries, the family accommodation consisted of three-storey buildings where standard dwelling units were repeated around staircases, as in the projects by Leonid Vesnin, Sergei Chernyshev, Ilya and Panteleimon Golosov, Edgar Norvert and others. In many cases, the district institutions were planned either as separate buildings or grouped functionally. The project submitted by Melnikov was of outstanding importance. It provided separate blocks for families, while combining all communal services in a single building of complex design, linked by covered walkways at second-storey level with four-storey blocks intended for childless couples and single people. It was the prototype of a communal house, with its distinguishing features already present: a well-developed communal sector with provision for eating, rest and culture, education and domestic requirements; dwelling blocks for single people, without kitchens or other facilities; covered walkways carried on piers to connect the dwellings withthe communal block, and a modern design radically different in aspect from the old tenements. From 1923,students in Ladovsky's Vkhutemas studio worked on the design of‘The Communal House for Workers’. The task set by Ladovsky was to create a dwelling which would combine the advantages of individual housing with those of a well-developed communal building. He occasionally varied the requirements and in a number of projects, for instance, demanded access from each dwelling unit both to the communal facilities and direct to the street, at no increased cost. Turkus provided a brilliant solution with the design for a three-storey house in which all the living units for childless couples and individuals communicated directly with the outside world and with inner corridors. Lamtsov suggested a novel way of linking individual and communal accommodation by routing all communications through the front of the house. Silchenkov provided horizontal communication along low corridors carried in overhanging outer galleries. He later elaborated this scheme and patented it in 1930. He also designed a two-storey unit of two rooms, a component for a communal house with a corridor serving two storeys at once. An All-Union competition was held in 1924–25 for a communal house in Kharkov. Its specification required a new type of dwelling incorporating a fully developed communal component. In the outcome, Trotsenko's design was implemented. In 1925–26, the Moscow City Council held an All-Union competition for the design of a workers' dwelling, which specified a type of house suitable both for single workers and working families with no housekeeping facilities of their own’. The design submitted by Georgy Volfenson, Samuil Aizikovich and E. Volkov, which secured a second prize, involved a building on a complex ground plan, consisting of living quarters sited along corridors and abutting on each other at either side of an open courtyard with a communal block at its far end. This project was carried out with some modifications on Khavsko-Shablovsky Lane. The dwelling blocks consisted of flats and single rooms, while the communal part contained a canteen, a club with a reading room, a crèche and a kindergarten. Communal houses were also designed – and some built – for other cities in the mid-1920s. An acute shortage of living space, however, meant that the use for which they had been planned was disregarded. Functionally designed communal houses could ensure comfort for their inhabitants if all the communal amenities worked well and if they were used for housing the kind of families for which they had been designed. Any disregard of these conditions – such as service failures, the use of communal accommodation for dwelling purposes, or the use of blocks intended for childless couples and individuals by families with children – made any objective assessment of the advantages and drawbacks of such experimental communal dwellings impossible. Worse still, it inflicted discomfort on the inhabitants and so made them sharply critical of communal houses as such. The failure to establish the communal house as a model for mass workers’ housing was also attributable to other causes. Thus the conditions and forces that had brought the domestic communes into being during the early years, the reasons for their evolution and gradual disintegration, had not been adequately studied before new design briefs were formulated. During the period of War Communism it had been thought, for example, that the buying and selling of goods would be replaced by barter through a highly developed network of consumer collectives or communes. The exchange of goods and labour would underpin not only the distribution of industrial products and foodstuffs, but the provision of public services and housing as well. In the barter system that arose under War Communism, with its appropriation of surpluses, its distribution points and its ban on private trade the commune became the basic economic unit of the distributive system for goods and services. All this was radically changed by the New Economic Policy. Moreover, the creation of Workers’ Housing Communes had also been promoted by the proletariat's assertion ofits political role in previously hostile residential districts. This fostered the organization of domestic communes which became the mainstays of Soviet power among the population of cities, helped to bring sympathizers together, instilled a new attitude to material possessions among the people at large and played an undoubted part in setting a new socialist ethical and communal standard. In many ways, therefore, the first Workers’ Housing Communes were not, as later critics wrote, an untimely move’, but genuinely reflected the circumstances of their day. Attempts to preserve them and secure their future failed because economic, social and political circumstances changed rapidly as socialism was being built. Some features of everyday life faded away and others emerged. This was as true of Housing Communes as of everything else. This process already began to show itself in the first half of the 1920s, when the switch to the New Economic Policy and to the principle of rent-maintained urban housing substantially changed the very basis of the Workers’ Housing Commune economy, founded as it was on a totally free use of each building and complete self-service. A new approach to co-operative housing took its place, under which members contributed financially to the construction and running of a building. ## The housing associations The co-operative housing associations took over from the Housing Communes of the War Communism period and introduced a new set of collective principles. A resolution of the First Congress of Housing Co-operation, held in December 1923, stated that the tasks of housing associations included both the maintenance of buildings and the provision of various domestic amenities for general use, such as study rooms, reading rooms, canteens, bathrooms and facilities for children. The housing co-operative buildings planned and erected in the mid- and late 1920s often included, alongside family flats, a number of cultural and general domestic amenities. As a stage in the collectivization of the way of life, they approximated more closely to transitional type dwellings than to fully developed communal houses. Typical buildings of this kind include the Dukstroi co-operative house in Moscow, built by Alexander Fufaev in 1927–28; the co-operative house in Zachatevsky Lane in Moscow, designed by Samoilov in 1925; the design for the Novkombyt (New Communist Way of Life) building, by Ilya Golosov in 1928, consisting of family flats, single rooms, and communal accommodation; and the co-operative house built on the Tverskaya in Moscow (today's Gorky Street) by Ladovsky in 1930. Co-operative housing, along with most other new types of buildings developed during the 1920s, was a subject of study in Vkhutemas, where Alexei Zaltsman designed a building for a housing association in Alexander Vesnin's studio. As a rule, housing associations were connected with a specific working community. Efforts were made during the mid- 1920s to accommodate within a single building both the living quarters and offices of the organization to which the members of the housing co-operative belonged. The Dom Kozhsindikata (the Leather Syndicate building) in Moscow, for instance, designed by Golubev in 1925, housed offices on the three lower storeys and flats for employees on the four upper ones. In the same year, the Elektro Co-operative commissioned from Ilya Golosov a combined dwelling and office building in which the first three storeys were reserved for offices and the top four for living quarters, while the basement housed the club accommodation. ## Experiments in the use of traditional dwelling types The interest displayed in early Soviet days in the fully developed communal house stemmed partly from the sharp contrast drawn between it and private housing in the course of polemics, since the single-family house with its plot ofland had been treated as the model for mass workers' dwellings by the supporters of the garden-city concept. Small houses for workers were first designed immediately after the October Revolution. In 1918, Belogrud produced a project for a proletarian dwelling' consisting of a single-family house arranged on three levels. In 1921, Markovnikov designed an experimental two-family, split-level, brick house. His designs were used in 1923 for a settlement by the Sokol housing association, comprising a variety of small self-contained houses for single families, terraced houses and small blocks containing three dwellings. Early in the 1920s, architects were trying to keep down the cost of small houses while preserving their cottage character through inclusion of direct outside access from each housing unit and a garden plot for each family. This led them to design a variety of houses, both free-standing, with two, four or eight flats each, and in terraces. Some of these designs displayed great inventiveness in the search for rational planning of the accommodation and spatial organization of the flats. In competition entries for a dwelling complex in Moscow in 1922–23, for instance, Belogrud and Melnikov designed family flats in terraced houses set out in rows on a saw-toothedground plan, with the smaller flats on the ground floor and the larger on the floor above, while the attic floor was provided with outside stairs at the back of the blocks. Early in the1920s, small houses became the usual type of workers’ dwellings in towns themselves, as well as in outlying settlements. In Moscow during the first half of the decade the dwelling complexes that were built consisted chiefly of small houses, such as the workers’ settlements of the AMO factories, comprising two-storey terraced houses by Zholtovsky in 1923; the Krasny Bogatyr settlement in 1924–25; and the Duks settlement, with two-storey four-, six- or eight-flat houses, by Venderov in 1924–25. The Stepan Razin settlement at Apsheron, by Samoilov, the first sector of which was occupied in 1925, consisted of single- as well as some two-storey houses, containing one, two, four or eight flats. The first workers' settlement in 1924–26 at Ivanovo-Voznesensk consisted of standard small houses. It became obvious by the middle of the decade, however, that neither small houses nor communal ones could provide the basic model for mass housing. The construction ofsmall houses, especially with a plot attached, proved uneconomic in urban conditions. Communal houses were still only in the early stages of their evolution as a form of dwelling. Doubts and disputes arose about many aspects of life within them, while the inclusion of a complete communal component greatly increased their initial cost. The increasingly acute shortage of housing necessitated a switch to mass building methods for workers' dwellings and to an economically viable type of building. The use of standard dwelling units around staircases provided the answer, and this type became known as ‘sectional' housing. A major factor in this development was that local councils had become the main commissioning authorities for housing by the mid-1920s, while the volume of private commissions, as well as those from trade enterprises and co-operatives, shrank sharply. The growing part played by local councils in commissioning urban housing influenced both the type of dwellings built and the method of allocating living space. Private building aimed at individual dwellings. Co-operatives represented pre-selected groups of shareholders, and therefore often commissioned well laid-out garden settlements, or houses with elaborate communal elements operated on a self-service basis. A local council, on the other hand, did not usually have any particular group in view for housing purposes and supplied accommodation to those workers’ families which needed it most. As Alexei Meshkov, an architect actively involved in the planning of housing on behalf of the Moscow City Council, wrote in 1925, the slogan here was: Maximum living space at minimum cost’.8 In housing commissioned by the Moscow Council, public services were provided at district and area level. The closed system of the communal house was being replaced by large dwelling complexes made up of sectional housing, shops, children’s facilities, canteens etc. In Moscow, Leningrad, Baku and other towns, the first dwelling complexes consisting of sectional housing were built with specially designed units of accommodation for each project. The introduction of standardized and mass-produced components, however, made it necessary to devise standard solutions. The first standard sectional units began to appear in the mid-1920s. These underwent substantial changes in design during the years that followed, and this in turn affected the type of occupancy of the newly built housing. Thus in Moscow, for instance, in 1925–26, two-room flats predominated in the first sectional units of four flats each, with only rudimentary domestic facilities, thereby setting a limit to the number of occupants per room. By 1927–28, the standard module contained two flats, usually of three rooms each. The standard of amenities in the flats also substantially improved – with bathrooms, through ventilation and no rooms without an independent exit – and the total area of each flat increased. On the other hand, the general tendency during the second half of the decade to build flats with larger numbers of rooms, combined with relatively little new construction and an acute shortage of housing, affected the allocation of living space. As a rule, new housing came to be occupied room by room. The transition in cities, during the mid-1920s, to dwelling complexes consisting of sectionally designed housing required the architects to produce new standard plan elements suitable for adequately compact dwelling complexes, yet capable of ensuring a varied spatial organization in city districts and allowing for plenty of fresh air and vegetation. With this end in view, the widely used linear, abutting, angled,T-shaped and cruciform sectional units were supplemented by new types arranged as thrée-pronged elements or elements set at an obtuse angle. The three-pronged standard sectional unit was first used in a design by Ladovsky in 1924 for a small residential area consisting of three-storey masonry houses composed of three types of elements – linear, three-pronged and square – offset in relation to each other. In 1925, Ladovsky and Lissitzky submitted a joint entry in a competition for the design of dwelling complexes at Ivanovo-Voznesensk.They used wide-angle sections in the design of family housing laid out in strips on curved or undulating ground plans. The rooms were all rectangular and the wide angle was used to fit in stairwells on a trapezoidal plan. By altering the orientation of the latter, the component sections could be made to point in a variety of directions. ## Osa's internal competition ## for the design of communal dwellings The switch in the mid-1920s to sectional construction of mass housing for the urban working population made communal dwellings a suitable subject for experimentation, especially in so far as the design of a new type of housing was concerned. Much work was done in this field by the Constructivist architects' group Osa, which put forward the slogan ‘Modern architecture must crystallize the new socialist way of life' in the first number ofits periodical SA in 1926. The periodical then went on to announce an internal competition for the outline design of a new workers’ dwelling', Practically all the designs submitted in 1927 for this competition represented transitional forms of housing, comprising both family dwelling units and a fully developed communal component.The designers were particularly concerned to establish the cheapest way of providing these units and communications to the communal facilities – suchas corridors, gangways and stairs. Ginzburg designed a communal house consisting of two sixstorey dwelling blocks linked at the top by the communal component, with a canteen, library reading room, club and assembly hall. The ground floor of each dwelling block was flanked near the stairs by small annexes for children – a kindergarten and crèche.Corridors provided internal communications and each served two floors, since all flats were built on two levels. In two of the designs, the architects – Sobolev and Andrei Ol – tried to save space horizontally by making a single corridor serve no less than three floors of a dwelling with split-level flats. Such flats were also used in designs with external galleries encircling the buildings, by Nina Vorotyntseva and Raisa Polyak, and in sectional buildings, by Vladimirov. In virtually all the entries featuring split-level flats the main living rooms rose to the full height of both levels, while small bedrooms and ancillary accommodation were kept to half that height and fitted in either at the level of the living room or on half-landings. The five-storey sectional houses in Vegman's entry were linked at the top by covered gangways, and the top floor of every seventh block was reserved for communal purposes. In Pasternak's project all the ground floors of each six-storey block held communal accommodation, so that corridors could be combined with a sectional lay-out. The internal Osa competition supplied a wealth of new ideas about the spatial organization of dwelling units, the combination of residential andcommunal elements within a single building and the rational use of floor space. Two projects of similar date by Leningrad students were closely related to the Osa internal competition entries. The first of these, by Mikhail Krestin, involved five four-storey dwelling blocks, consisting of split-level flats with a kitchen, bathroom and lavatory, each linked with two communal blocks. In the secondproject, by Konstantin Ivanov, Petr Smolin and Fedor Terekhin, the dwelling and communal elements were combined in a single sevenstorey block on a trefoil ground plan, with the canteen, kindergarten, crèche and reading room located on the ground floor. ## Transitional housing designs ## incorporating new uses of space Work on the rationalization of housing and the development of a transitional design for communal dwellings was carried out in 1928 in the Typological Section of the Committee for Construction, RSFSR (Russian Republic), by a group of architects, including Barshch, Vladimirov, Pasternak and Grigory Sum-Shik, directed by Ginzburg. Problems involving a scientific approach to the organization of the way oflife were tackled here at government level virtually for the first time. Housing units were to be developed so that a separate flat could be allocated to every family. After a thorough study of the practical possibilities then available, the architects in the Typological Section decided that the only viable solution to the housing problem lay in the production of a standard, small(initially oneroomed) flat intended for an individual family. Part II/Social tasks of architecture Ways of reducing the cost of flats in sectional housing were thoroughly investigated. Particular attention was paid to their rational planning and, more especially, to the reduction of ancillary floor space. The housewife's work in the kitchen was subjected to close time-and-motion analysis and a planned arrangement of the equipment involved made it possible to save some otherwise wasted space. Several versions of a rationally planned sectional flat were produced which the architects called Type A, as well as a fresh spatial lay-out for a sectional flat termed Type B. The designers succeeded in reducing the height of ancillary accommodation by alternating the accommodation within a building from side to side,in other words, by following a mirror-image plan from one floor to the next. Apart from this rationalization of the lay-outof sectional flats, the Typological Section developed various versions of the four ways of arranging housing units along a corridor serving a single floor to produce their Type C, or on two floors in Types D and F and three floors in Type E. A comparative analysis of the economic efficiency of the various types of housing units showed that Type F was most cost-effective for purposes of mass resettlement of single families in small flats. In spatial terms, a building composed of Type F units required a corridor. As distinct from Types C, D and E, however, such corridors did not reduce the area available for dwellings. Each corridor served two floors and fitted into the space saved by reducing the height of the ancillary accommodation and sleeping alcoves in the flats. Moreover, such corridors secured daylight, while every flat could be naturally ventilated. Several models were worked out in the Typology Section for single-room Type F flats with living areas of 27 m², 30 m² and 31 m². Each of these had a small entrance hall, from which stairs led up or down to a main living room 3.50 m or 3.20 m high, including, behind a folding partition, a kitchen recess containing standard units – range, sink etc. Accommodation with lower ceilings at 2.25 m or 2.15 m included a sleeping alcove and adjoining toilet facilities containing a bath, shower or wash basin. A number of designs for transitional communal housing were prepared by the Typology Section in which various types of dwelling units were combined in different ways. The Section's work in 1928–29 resulted in the formulation, published in 1929, of ‘model designs and assemblies for the construction of dwellings recommended for use in 1930’, as well as the construction in Moscow, Sverdlovsk and Saratov of six experimental communal houses of transitional design. Various designs of housing units, methods of linking the dwelling and communal components to each other, new structures and materials, and methods of organizing building work were tried out in the course of their construction. The most interesting of these houses is that designed by Ginzburg and Milinis, with Prokhorov acting as engineer, built in 1928–30 on the Novinsky Boulevard (now Tchaikovsky Street) in Moscow, with the inclusion of dwelling, communal and shopping elements. The dwelling part consists of a six-storey block with two corridors, one on the first, the other on the fifth floor, and two staircases. The ground level is open save for pilotis. Three types of flat make up the design: thirty-two reduced Type F units, several doubled 2F units, eight units for larger families approximating to Type D, and hostel accommodation with rooms for one or two people. The flat roof carries a sun terrace and a garden. At first-floor level, a covered gangway leads from the dwelling block to a separate building housing a kitchen and canteen (midday meals were taken home) and kindergarten. New structures, materials and building methods were tried out in the Novinsky Boulevard house, as well as new dwelling units. These included weight-bearing ferro-concrete frames, thermo-insulated outer walls, fire-doors,sliding windows, standardized prefabricated structural elements, such as pillars, girders, panels, windows and doors, and their assembly on site. Other experiments were also carried out: in spatial organization, to find a human scale for the rooms, a maximum permissible limitation of space etc; in lighting,to establish the relationship between the dimensions of rooms and the lighting required for them, and to create the apparent enlargement of interiors by the rationalized distribution of window space; and in colour, by the differential shading of walls and ceilings to stimulate the perception of interior space, and by the functional use of colours for purposes of orientation within the buildings. The second of the experimental houses was built in Moscow on the Gogol Boulevard by Barshch, Vladimirov, Milinis, Orlovsky, Pasternak,and Lyubov Slavina. Some of these designs were carried out, mostly on thestrength of commissions from housing associations, in Kiev, Kharkov, Baku, Tashkent, Smolensk and elsewhere. ## The family and communal accommodation: the debate about transforming the way of life Work on the design of new towns and dwelling complexes to serve the industrial plants being built under the First Five Year Plan focused the attention of architects on the problem of mass housing, as well as on resettlement. A keen debate developed on this subject both in the press and in specialized publications. All aspects of life were debated: the future of the family, relations between parents and children, the nature of everyday social contacts, the collectivization of domestic life and consumption, and many other such problems. A diversity of social models were put foreward, each of which involved its own special new requirements inhousing. The charaçter of marital and family relationships, including their implications for the planning of new dwellings, came under particularly close scrutiny at that time. The difficulties of everyday life immediately after the Revolution had further aggravated a series of moral problems arising from the rejection of the traditional view of the family, while its new interpretation had not yet taken shape. The new Soviet concept of the family emerged among the disturbed circumstances of Civil War, economic chaos and the popular upsurge of War Communism and was therefore often prevented from functioning normally. Day-to-day existence lost much of its stability as a result of the Civil War, the new family was often altogether unable to take root in it, and marriage itself frequently became impermanent in character. Although such instability had been caused by the complex circumstances of those early Soviet days, it was nevertheless often regarded as the expression of a new socialist ethic. And so, when the stabilization of economic life during the first half of the 1920s restored to the family its role as the basic economic and consumer unit in society, the young revolutionary generation sawin this a threatened rebirth of conformism, the jeopardizing of the collective spirit and an attempt to replace it by petit-bourgeois individualism. Stormy discussions raged in the press and at youth meetings throughout that decade concerning a new sexual morality and alternative models of family relationships. Theories gained currency which linked a new way of life not merely with the wholesale collectivization of domestic existence, but with the rejection of the family as the basic unit of society. In the new collective way of life the existence of every member of society would be subjected to minute regulation. This approach to a new way of life was most coherently stated in the theoretical writings of Nikolai Kuzmin and found its architectural expression in 1928–29 in his design for a communal house. Kuzmin completely discarded family flats. Instead, he divided the residents into age groups and designed separate accommodation for each of these. The rooms provided in special blocks were reserved for sleeping, and the remainder of free time had to be spent by everyone in a cultural centre. The family as such did not exist. Children were brought up collectively, in separate age groups. Meals were taken communally. Life was strictly regulated, and the programmes prescribed for each age group were reckoned out to the minute. The ‘Theses on Housing' put forward by Nikolai Kuzmin at the First Osa Congress in 1929, which declared flats to be a material manifestation of petit-bourgeois ideology unacceptable in the new society, were adopted by the gathering as a whole. The fully developed communal house, involving the utmost collectivization of all aspects of life, was erected into the model of new socialist housing. The communal house designed in 1929 by Barshch and Vladimirov reflected the Osa concepts as embodied in these Theses’. A complex consisting of two sets of buildings intersecting each other at right angles was divided into three interconnected basic blocks: a six-storey block, housing 360 people, for children of pre-school age; a five-storey block, housing 320, for school children;and a ten-storey block for 1,000 adults. Each block contained all the facilities required by the relevant age group. Designs by Alexander and Leonid Vesnin, Ilya Golosov and others, for actual dwelling combines, regarded as the structural units of a new town in the Sotsgorod planning concept, were undoubtedly influenced by such proposals for a fully developed communal house catering for a completely collectivized way of life.Sabsovich, the proponent of the Sotsgorod theory, favoured the wholesale collectivization of the way of life and the rejection of the family. He even opposed the provision of shared rooms in communal houses for married couples: rooms, in his opinion, were primarily there for sleeping in, as well as for individual rest and study. Sabsovich believed that the family would disappear as the basic unit of society and be replaced by communities founded on other principles. As he saw it, in a socialist society the problem of parents and children would be replaced by that of adults and children, or society and children, and resolved thereby. ## Youth collectives and communal dwellings involving wholly shared living The theories advocating a wholesale collectivization of life and the liquidation of the family, as well as the architectural designs derived from them, claimed to reflect a current social need. Their promoters pointed to individual examples of Housing Communes with wholly shared living and voluntary elimination of the family. Such communes did in fact exist, but were usually no more than a temporary form of domestic organization by groups of young people. The young, owing to their position in the family, at work or in training, are very inclined to associate with their contemporaries and to form independent groups with values and an outlook of their own. They need close contact with their own age group as they develop from dependent children into fully fledged adults. This urge gradually fades, new family ties develop and individuals become absorbed into working communities. Hence the objective circumstances which favour the development of youth groups, but hence also their temporary nature, their instability and their inevitable decay. This tendency among the young to form groups of contemporaries was intensified during the 1920s by special circumstances. The young often saw the struggle for a new way of life as a challenge to family ties: these gradually weakened in any case and were replaced by comradeship among contemporaries. The creation of communes among the young was also greatly stimulated by the fact that large numbers of them were directed to workers' colleges and institutes of higher education, or employed in the construction of new cities. These were the circumstances in which domestic communes for the young came into being. Lonely students or building workers, a long way from home and their childhood peer groups and communities, as yet without new families of their own, naturally chose company over solitude and preferred to live communally. Many aspects of the new way of life were exemplified in the everyday existence of young workers and students during the 1920s. Certain sociologists and architects belonging to that decade allowed themselves undue scope, however, in their interpretation of specific life styles, ethics and relationships when they studied the Housing Communes of the young. In practice, many designs of communal houses catering for a fully collective way of life and the rejection of the family were no more than attempts to provide an architectural framework for youth hostels and to rationalize the way of life appropriate to them. The subsequent history of communal houses built to serve such communities is indicative in this respect. Those designed for student communes survived successfully for many years as wellequipped hostels, because their inhabitants consistently met the age and family requirements for which the buildings were initially conceived. On the other hand, communal houses designed for young workers' communes gradually developed into unsuitable dwellings as their inhabitants acquired families of their own and the occupants no longer bore any resemblance to the youth communes for which such buildings had been designed.It must be stressed, however, that youth communes came into being before any communal houses specifically intended for a fully collectivized way of life were designed and built. In other words, these houses met a genuine social requirement, even if its scope has been grossly exaggerated. In the mid-1920s, for instance, the students of the Leningrad Electro-Technical Faculty formed a domestic commune in which housing, basic expenditure, everyday activities, food and study were collectivized. Total self-service was introduced. All parts of the hostel premises were reallocated and provided with new functions – bedrooms, access to which was completely forbidden in daytime, a dining room, a rest room and studies. All books were nationalized’ and incorporated in a selfservice library. A self-service shop was organized. The creation of a commune was helped by the age and common social origin of the students, all of whom were former workers, their joint interest in study and their equal incomes. The movement for the establishment of domestic communes among young workers entering higher educational establishments, and the spontaneous emergence of such communes, strongly influenced the design and construction of student hostels towards the end of the 1920s. An experimental communal student house was built in Moscow in 1929–30 for 2,000 residents, to designs by Nikolaev. A vast eight-storey block contained small double bedrooms measuring 6 m². This block communicated with a three-storey communal building containing a sports hall, an assembly hall seating 1,000, a dining room, a reading room seating 150, a study room accommodating 300, and numerous individual study cubicles. The design also included a laundry, a repair workshop, a crèche accommodating 100, a surgery, sun terrace, club rooms and showers. Other well-equipped student hostels in Moscow, contemporary with Nikolaev's design, variously reflected the research into communal housing. A student hostel built on the Kotelnicheskaya Embankment in 1930 consisted of two dwelling blocks linked to a building inserted between them and housing a dining and dayroom, as well as a self-service kitchen, on each floor. Similar hostels were also built in other cities.The hostel of the Zoological Veterinary Institute designed by Kochar in 1931 in Erevan, for instance, consisted of three buildings – two dwelling blocks and a communal block lying between them, connected with each other by gangways. An All-Union Intercollegiate Competition for the design of a communal students' house in Leningrad, with accommodation for 1,000 residents, was held in 1929–30,when the commune movement was at its height among students. There was a marked difference in the spatial composition of the communal building between the projects submitted from the Leningrad Institute for Communal-Construction Engineers (Liiks) and those from Vkhutein in Moscow. The Leningrad students adopted an approach which had already become traditional for this purpose: a multi-storey block or blocks linked to a communal building or buildings. The designs produced by the Vkhutein students under Leonidov’s direction, on the other hand, attempted to reflect the inner structure of the life of commune members. Most of the designs split the communes into groups of ten, fifty or a hundred students. Each group was architecturally provided for by a building complex with individual rooms intended for sleep, rest and study, together with communal and ancillary accommodation. In addition, there was a common cultural centre. In Ivan Kuzmin's design, each such basic community was allocated a separate circular two-storey building on pilotis. In the design by Leonid and Nikolai Pavlov, it was part of a low complex with individual small inner courtyards. For Grigory Pyankov, it was a single standard ‘section' in a tower block, and for Viktor Kalinin, as for Alexander Maximov and Zanvil Korsunsky, it was part of a multi-storey block. Leonidov's theories about the reconstruction of the way of life, which had influenced the Vkhutein students' designs, were best illustrated by his design for a socialist settlement attached to the Magnitogorsk Industrial Combine. The dwellings in it represented a form of hostel for individuals and childless couples, in which each section consisted of sixteen small singleroom flats for two people. These sections could be used as separate dwellings or incorporated in a multi-storey tower block. At the centre of each section, a hall provided with a double light was intended for use as a common rest room, cultural meeting place, morning gymnasium and dining room. Leonidov believed that the new dwellings should not recall hotels, where thousands lived along noisy corridors far removed from nature. Instead, they should provide a framework for small communities in which the individual would not be submerged, but be able to develop to the fulland communicate with others by graduating from small groups to increasingly larger ones. In Leonidov's design, the dwellings were surrounded by gardens, sports grounds and fountains. Each dwelling complex or district would house 250 people, and a free-standing building for children's institutions would be sited between every two complexes. Only the most elementary forms of public accommodation and communal services were located in the immediate vicinity of the dwelling units. All other communal institutions in Leonidov's design were located in public buildings beyond the confines of the residential districts. In 1930, the Leningrad City Council launched an All-Union open competition for a communal house –a collective dwelling combine' – to house 2,000 workers. The bulk of the entries offered dwelling combines comprising housing and communal blocks linked by gangways. The design by Simonov, Notes and Rusakov was ultimately selected and provided a communal dwelling for 1,750 people. It was a complex design consisting of interconnected housing blocks, each with its strictly defined purpose – for individuals and childless couples, families, school boarders – and a communal centre containing a theatre, sports facilities, a dining room, and so on. The communal house designed by Ol and built in Leningrad on Rubinstein Street in 1930 was commissioned by an association of young engineers and writers who themselves decided at their general meetings the degree of collectivization to be applied in the building. All domestic work was to be communalized, and leisure was to be collective. None of the flats in the building, whether larger, with three to four rooms; small, with two rooms; or hostel accommodation for single people, included a kitchen. Tħe ground floor housed a dining hall, reading room, children's rooms, rest room and other public premises. However, difficulties arose in the life of this domestic commune after the first flush of collective enthusiasm. New families developed, and the house which had been planned for a community of young people proved to be completely unsuitable for ordinary domestic purposes. This fate also overtook a whole series of other communal houses built for the working youth, In contrast to communal houses for students, the family circumstances of the inmates changed as the years went by and with them the entire life style. ## Individual personal requirements, collective living and the economy: ideal projects and actual circumstances In their model for the future way of life, the disurbanizers scorned designs for communal houses involving total collectivization where an individual would constantly be surrounded by other people. They advocated a combination ofindividual freedom with a provision for everyday needs through a centralized supply system, as opposed to the communalist concept of a minutely regulated way of life. In his settlement scheme, Okhitovich assumed that people would always eat, dress, and so on – in other words, engage in consumption - as individuals, and that individual demands for consumption would grow as society evolved. The disurbanizers’ attitude to the socialization of everyday life stressed the need to socialize the more labour intensive domestic tasks within the house and outside it, but denied that consumption itself must be collectivized. They did not set out to create permanent social contracts between fellow occupants of a dwelling, but stressed instead the autonomy of each individual's existence in every possible way. The disurbanizers designed small individual standard units which could either be free-standing in natural surroundings; linked in continuous ribbons, as in the Barshch and Ginzburg Green City design; or included in housing association and hostel projects, such as those drafted for various types of dwellings in the Socialist Settlement Section. However, even though the disurbanizers rejected extremist attitudes towards the socialization of domestic life and the collectivization of consumption, their approach to the design of a basic housing unit was much the same as that of the supporters of communal housing involving a fully socialized way of life. Okhitovich believed that the unit of housing should be designed for a single person, since wives and husbands would live apart in the future, while children would be brought up collectively. As he saw it, the family hearth’ as such would vanish in time. The debate at the end of the 1920s about restructuring the way of life unfolded at a time when resources for new housing had to be allocated most sparingly in order to meet all other demands for the rapid industrialization of the country. Any betterment of the workers’ living conditions required an improvement of the housing as such – rationalized planning, modern equipment, increases in the per capita quotas of floor space etc – as well as the development of a network of communal facilities. These twobasic requirements, however, proved conflicting when a decision had to be made as to which of them should be given priority in funding. A majority gave clear preference to the socialized amenities, since the restructuring of life was linked, as they thought, not only with a gradual loss of the family’s role as the basic economic unit, but also with its decay as the primary element of society. The family was not rejected outright, but merely regarded as an unstable form of association between individuals. This made it necessary to adjust the design of housing units to the mobile’ nature of the family, in order to take account of both joint and individual occupancy. Miliutin, for instance, asserted in his theoretical statements and in a design for a dwelling block that living quarters in building called upon to reflect the mobile character of the family in a socialist society must be planned flexibly. Most of those concerned with restructuring the way of life believed that the main need was for the design of radically new types of housing, concentrating on the search for the correct planning and spatial organization of the dwelling of the future. The development of a new way of life was intimately linked in their minds with the provision of certain forms of housing, and all old’ types of dwellings were usually rejected as being socially unacceptable. Ladovsky saw the connection between the way of life and housing rather differently. He thought it important to shift the emphasis in pursuit of the new way of life on to the design of districts and entire cities, and away from the preoccupation with individual dwellings. He considered it wrong to identify particular types of residential building with specific social purposes, since the social identity of any dwelling – be it a flat, say, or a hostel embodying one measure of socialization or another – may be successfully accommodated in every known type of building, including a skyscraper. The distinctions betweensuch buildings were simply a matter of minor differences in everyday practice and of the way in which a dwelling fitted into the network of communal services, rather than any point of social principle. This attitude was reflected in Ladovsky's own designs and those of his students in Vkhutemas-Vkhutein. The designs originating from the latter included dwellings that might be conceived sectionally, or in galley form, in blocks of towers, along corridors or in some other form, but always firmly as part of the new social organization of a dwelling complex or a city. This approach to the connection between housing and a new way of life proved justified by experience. In terms of everyday life towards the end of the 1920s, districts consisting of sectional housing and free-standing communal buildings did not, in fact, differ greatly from the intermediate type of dwellings with communal accommodation built for the housing associations. The total socialization of life and the withering away of the family were advocated in radical theories. Meanwhile, the communal houses and dwelling combines that were designed, and especially those that were actually built, increasingly proved to be intermediate dwellings, hotel-like buildings and groups of sectional dwellings and communal blocks, either adjacent or linked by gangways. The advocates of communal houses catering for a wholly socialized way of life were searching for a new guiding principle for housing and a new primary component of society to replace the traditional family. The latter had developed within its own form ofaccommodation. This was now to be replaced by a fresh spatial design to fit the new everyday personal relationships within a new primary community. The actual course of events soon made it plain, however, that two of the basic features of the proposed ideal' housing communities, which would need to take root and spread before the family flat could be replaced by a different housing component, were unrealistic. For one thing, the family proved more lasting as the basic unit of society than many had imagined in early Soviet years. For another, the Housing Commune as a consumer collective based on completely voluntary self-help by its members, operating in a socialist economy on the principle From each according to his ability, to each according to his labour’, proved to be mere utopia. It took no account of the real economic relations between people, such as differential earnings among the members of the community and the resulting material inequalities in opportunities and needs, or the impossibility of assessing individual contributions to the self-service system as a whole , and the resulting artificiality of egalitarianism. The Housing Commune, therefore, simply did not take root as a new basic unit of society. However, the demand for a special type of dwelling – the communal house – which arose during the early Soviet period in connection with the spontaneous emergence of Housing Communes, acquired an impetus of its own. It formed part of the search for a restructured way of life and came to be regarded as an ideal model, however unattainable it might be in the actual circumstances prevailing at the time. Many sociologists and architects treated it as a matter for the future and believed that at some stage it would be necessary to forgo the ideal communal house as a model and temporarily resort instead, for purposes of mass accommodation, to a transitional tópe of building. Yet even the latter did not achieve widespread use. In real life, the burden falling on the housewife was eased by taking some of the heavier domestic tasks out of the home – by such means as ready-made foods, for instance – and by streamlining work within the home through new equipment. Hopes that the majority of domestic chores could rapidly be eliminated proved illusory, however, and the family continued to provide not only the basic component of society but also its primary economic unit. Furthermore, experience failed to justify the expectation, which had seemed so incontrovertible in early Soviet days, that everyday consumption itself would gradually become collectivized. Finally, the growth of increasingly complex social relationships outside working life put an end to the hope that neighbourliness would become an important factor among residents and lead to the gradual evolution of a living community within a house. In the outcome, not only did transitional housing fail to provide a basis for a gradual evolution of communities to the higher’ level of Housing Communes, but even such elements of self-service as they incorporated failed to develop and often vanished after a few years. A house would then either simply shed its communal accommodation, or become part of a dwelling complex with public services of its own. Alternatively, it might survive as a hotel-like dwelling offering a high level of service, rather than encouraging self-help, for the benefit of residents who for one reason or another had no domestic facilities of their own. A large number of houses and complexes designed and built towards the end of the 1920s included features variously taken from communal houses, dwelling combines, houses of a transitional type, hotel-like establishments and districts with their own networks of public service, and often extravagantly combined. Among these were a house on the Petrograd Embankment in Leningrad, designed by Simonov and others in the early 1930s, consisting of blocks internally connected with each other and composing an entrance hall, foyer, auditorium and dining room on the ground floor; a complex of family hostels in Pirogovskaya Street in Moscow by Rukhlyadev and Osipov in 1928–30 and consisting of eight interconnected blocks: one for single residents, six with two-room flats for families and one with larger flats, together with communal kitchens, toilet facilities and laundry rooms on every floor at the butt-ends of the buildings; a dwelling complex by Boris Iofan, in 1928–30, on the Bersenevskaya Embankment in Moscow, in which the public rooms – film theatre, kindergarten, crèche, dining room and shop – adjoin the dwellings, but do not communicate with them internally; a housing complex in Kiev, in Revolyutsiya Street, built in 1929–30 by Anichkin as architect and Zholtus as engineer, which consists of a five-storey building with a complex ground plan, having public rooms on the ground floor and a laundry in the cellar; and a dwelling on Karpovka in Leningrad by Evgeny Levinson and Igor Fomin in 1931–34. These and many other such examples of housing designed towards the end of the 1920s clearly show that the mass urban dwelling was then still only in its experimental stage. Architects were no longer content with sectional houses consisting of large flats with single-room occupancy, or communal houses made up of dwelling cubicles lacking ancillary facilities. A kind of convergence was taking place between these two types of dwelling which involved a search for a family housing unit and ways of linking a dwelling with public amenities. In May 1930, the Central Committee of the All-Union Communist Party adopted a resolution ConcerningWork on the Reconstruction of the Way of Life’ which stressed the importance of shaping a new socialist way of life and pointed to errors committed so far in the course of this process. The development of a socially new form of dwelling displayed many contradictory features during the period under review. Yet the intensive investigation of creative opportunities existing in this field greatly influenced the evolution of Soviet architecture. The reconstruction of the way of life was debated and tested in numerous designs and buildings. New approaches to the planning and spatial organization of dwellings and housing complexes were worked out. In order to plan and equip dwelling units in a rationalway, new scientifically based methods were introduced into the design work. The set of communal institutions required within a dwelling complex was carefully studied, the structure of the public services involved was elaborated and the various public buildings required could thus be provided. ## Manifesting the new collective way of life in the appearance of housing When architects designed workers' dwellings, they tried not only to promote a new way of life among the inhabitants, but also to impart a new look to the buildings, so as to make them visibly different from the inheritance of the past. The visual stereotype for the new communal dwelling – a complex composition, consisting of blocks of different sizes linked by gangways, which gradually achieved almost universal currency – did not establish itself immediately. Earlier on, the appearance of the dwelling of the future might be marked by its palatial character, as in Tverskoy’s 1920 competition design of a Dwelling for a Proletarian in Petrograd; or by its emphatically dynamic composition, as in Ladovsky’s forceful design for a communal house; or by contrasting outward austerity with the pompousness of a bourgeois investment property, as in Malevich's Planits of 1923–24 and Khidekel’s communal housing designs of 1927-31; or by the way in which the workers' dwelling was seen to be thrown open to sun and fresh air, as in Leonidov's glazed prismatic houses designed for Magnitogorsk. Nevertheless, an emphasis in the appearance of the dwelling on the collective nature of the new life within it, and the provision for general social contact within housing units, became the essential elements of the spatial composition of new dwellings in the 1920s. This approach was first exemplified clearly in Melnikov's competition design for a dwelling complex in Moscow, of 1922–23. Covered gangways – on pilotis or in spans – between dwelling blocks and communal buildings were widely used during the mid- and late 1920s in designs for new types of housing. The use of gangways to connect buildings led to the introduction of new spatial solutions. A new town-planning dimension developed from the design of residential areas. Many fresh ideas were incorporated in designs for communal houses, transitional buildings and dwelling combines, where the externalevidence of the connection between the various blocks created a new architectural image for them and for the new life itself. The fully developed communal house or dwelling combine was usually designed on a near-square ground plan and took up an entire city district or block. In practice, the intricacy of the spatial composition could not be fully displayed if the component buildings were set parallel or at right angles to the sides of the site. The spectator would then only have a partial view of them, and would perhaps fail to take in the collective nature of the new urban element. In an attempt to impart greater external impact to communal houses or dwelling complexes, their architects sometimes set the component blocks diagonally, or strung them out with their connecting gangways along the street, instead of laying them out in depth within the site. Two designs by Ilya Golosov are typical in this respect: the UMS communal house, drawn up in conjunction with Boris Mitelman in 1931, and the socalled Collective House for the First Workers’ Settlement in Magnitogorsk in 1929–31. Experiments in the spatial compositionof dwellings were not confined to the creation of a new image for a socially novel type of dwelling. They also involved the design of new types of sections, new forms of spatial organization for dwelling units, the formulation of spatial compositions for dwelling complexes and the use of colour. The Moscow City Council approached Asnova in 1927 with a request to design and build an experimental residential district on the Shabolovka. At that time, the construction of dwelling complexes based on standard component sections and designs embodying the latter was in full swing in the city; their appearance satisfied neither those who had commissioned them nor their prospective users. This forced the City Council in 1926, first to launch a competition for the design of façades for dwellings, and then to resort to Asnova as a creative organization with a reputation for research into artistic form. Asnova carried out an internal competition and selected Travin's design, which was used as the basis for the design actually carried out by an Asnova team. Spatial organization of the district by buildings sited diagonally and the application of colour by a conjunction of brick and coloured rendering were principally stressed. As a result of this original artistic presentation, the district built on Shabolovka in 1930 was pleasingly different from other dwelling complexes built by means of the very same component sections. ## Experiments with curved forms During the 1920s, Soviet architects resorted to semicircular and circular ground plans for buildings in their search for rational planning and expressive spatial composition. Krutikov designed a competition entry for a three-storey hostel on a semicircular plan in 1924; a standard semicircular plan in 1924; a standard semicircular housing unit in 1926; and in 1927, as partof a scheme for an academic community designed in Ladovsky's studio at Vkhutemas, he used a semicircular section for a two-storey individual house, for a housing blockcomposed of two sections, and for a seven- and eightstorey multi-section student hostel In 1928, both Mazmanyan and Kochar, also working in Ladovsky's studio at Vkhutemas, designed two-storey and multistorey dwellings on a circular ground plan.Mazmanyan designed a two-storey domed house, while Kochar's design was for a nine-storey hostel-type house on a circular plan, either single, or twinned with a shared stairwell. Towards the end.of the 1920s, Melnikov evolved a method of combining a set of intersecting cylinders. He used this in a number of designs for housing blocks and public buildings, as well as in the house he designed for himself in Moscow. Here, the curvilinear plan and the different ways of articulating the various elements generated a set of complex interior effects. Two rooms identical in their shape and dimensions, for instance, produced wholly different effects, because one was provided with a large plate-glass screen while the other was lit by a combination of forty hexagonal windows combined in a complex pattern. The paraboloid’ communal house for northern districts, by Bunin in 1930, provided another instance of originality in spatial organization. It consisted of a six-storey dwelling for communal use in the form of anupward-pointing paraboloid. It was set on pilotis with a central stairwell, from which circular corridors led on each floor to the doors of the individual rooms, as well as to a reading room, hall and open terrace. On the top floor, a sports hall was roofed over by a transparent dome. ## Equal opportunities for the consumer: ## a new approach to rationalizing accommodation The tendency prevailing towards the end of the 1920s to design communal houses for a fully socialized way of life, on the one hand, and the provision by local councils of sectional housing with flats intended for single-room occupancy,on the other, undoubtedly delayed the development of a rationally planned standard town flat which took maximum account of functional familyneeds and the efficient organization of everyday life. However, the new social circumstances and the living conditions which they imposed demanded the development of such a standard unit. The introduction ofa single norm for the allocation of housing space required a fundamentally different approach to the design of flats: the consumer considerations governing living space no longer involved satisfaction of the needs of variouslayers of the urban population in accordance with their levels ofincome. They were determined instead by scientifically based standards of public health and other parameters, due attention being paid to the economic circumstances of the country as a whole. Equality of opportunity in securing accommodation was in practice, if not in theory, the decisive factor in the shaping of a new way of life, and radically altered the entire approach to the design and construction of urban housing. The rationalized planning and equipment of flats became a matter ofintensive study towards the end of the 1920s, especially by the team of architects which Ginzburg headed in 1928–29, first in the Typology Section of the Committee for Construction, RSFSR, and later in the Socialist Settlement Section. The flat with its basic and ancillary accommodation was treated as a single spatial entity in which all the main processes of family life unfolded. These processes were closely studied so that they might be rationally organized within a restricted living space. Built-in equipment, its design and efficient distribution, was given high priority as a means of improving comfort in a confined space. The economic circumstances of the time did not allow the allocation to every family of a flat with purpose-built rooms, so that the latter had to serve a number of purposes. In an effort to meet this need, adaptable equipment and furniture were designed, as well as flexible-purpose flats. The Typology Section team devised an economical flat of limited area calculated for a single family. They carried out extensive research into scientifically based standards of housing; studied the rationalized planning of housing units in the light of various basic features of everyday life; and took into account individual perceptions of housing in terms of size, lighting and colouring. Side by side with this work by the Typology Section, Lissitzky at Vkhutein was directing a programme for the development of modern built-infurniture,adaptablefurniture, sectional furniture and individual components of standard furniture, many of which were designed to fit the housing units evolved in the Committee for Construction. The complexity of such an approach to the rationalized design of mass type flats and standard equipment and furniture for them not only reflected a highly professional approach to housing problems, but was also dictated by the new social conditions. Work done in the Soviet Union during the 1920s on the rationalization of urban flat dwelling was not directed, as elsewhere, towards the provision of cheap housing for low-income earners, but towards the design of standard flats for mass use. This was a fundamentally different approach to the design of a housing unit and was linked, in particular, with the establishment of a standard for the allocation of living space. ## Prefabricated accommodation and mobile dwellings Suggestions for the use of standard elements in prefabricated buildings and for various forms of mobile dwellings crop up early in Soviet times. Thus Lavinsky assumed in his City on Springs design of 1921 that standard prefabricated components made of materials such as steel, aluminium, glass and asbestos would be used in construction. A group of architects who had learned to use new timber structures when building the pavilions of the Moscow Agricultural and Handicraft Exhibition of 1923 worked in the design office of the Standard Joint-Stock Company in 1924–25. The company therefore organized the prefabricationby wood-processing plants of standard two-storey houses forworkers’settlements, such as those used in 1925–26 at Ivanovo-Voznesensk. Building materials were primarily reserved for industrial purposes towards the end of the 1920s, and were in very short supply. The construction process itself was converted as far as possible into the continuous industrial production of standard components for subsequent assembly. Both these features stimulated research and design work on the construction of prefabricated housing from local materials and industrial waste. The first dwelling built of small breeze blocks was designed by two engineers, Krasin and Loleit, and erected in Moscow in 1927. Burov and Blokhin began a long run of designing breezeblock housing at the end of the 1920s. Research into the use of large building blocks was launched in 1929 at the Kharkov Structural Institute under the direction of the engineer Andrei Vatsenko. These investigations resulted in the construction of experimental city districtsconsisting of three-storey houses built with large breeze and concrete blocks in 1929; a trial sixstorey dwelling built of large blocks in 1930 in Kharkov, and a settlement consisting of similarly built houses at Kramatorsk in 1933. Simultaneously with the construction of increasingly tall dwellings using large building blocks, a team headed by Ginzburg investigated the use of factory-made standard components for the assembly of low-rise timber houses. The scientific study and experimental design of standard prefabricated wooden houses was carried out initially at the Socialist Settlement Section of Gosplan, the State Planning Commission of the USSR, and later by the Group for Prefabricated House Building and Planning of Giprogor, the State Institute for Urban Design, in 1929–31. Designs for various kinds of dwellings built oflocal materials were produced. Provision was made in a number of these for variations of the basic lay-out by means of movable andcollapsible partitions. It was proposed to set up special organizations for the construction of two-storey standardized housing with local materials. Building work was to be fully industrialized by the production of lightweightcomponents in factories, for assembly on site with the help of small cranes. The basic structural components were to consist of prefabricated timber frames and fibrolite panelling. Designs incorporating prefabricated housing units began to appear at the turn of the decade. Varentsov's New City diploma project already included multi-storey dwellings with housing units suspended on brackets from a central pillar. In 1930, Ladovsky suggested in his Green City competition entry that a fully equipped housing unit or cabin, produced in two alternative designs, should be usedas the basic standard element, rather than walled boxes or structural members. Such units would be factory-produced and brought on site for assembly in a wide variety of patterns ranging from individual houses to multi-storey buildings, offering bothdwelling space and communal accommodation for general or specialized purposes. The arrangement of the standard components of the framework allowed a choice of spatial compositions for such dwellings, while communal accommodation could be located anywhere within the framework and allowed as much space as necessary. A little book called My i doma (Ourselves and Houses) had been written by the Futurist poet Velemir Khlebnikov in 1914–15, but was only published in 1930. It contained many stimulating ideas about future housing, which only obtained wider currency during the second half of the twentieth century. Khlebnikov wrote that in times to come the housing unit or cabin would not only provide a fixed residence, but would move about freely with its occupant, who could thus carry on his or her usual existence wherever he or she went. Khlebnikov suggested that all dwelling cabins should have strictly standard dimensions. All cities would be provided with metal cellular structures, or Frame Houses’, with mooring bays for the cabins. All means of passenger transport, whether by road, rail or water, would be designed to carry these standard cabins. The dwelling cabin could be fitted into a Frame House bay in every city by special means of propulsion and lifting gear. Similar Frame Hotels’ would be erected at leisure centres, where the bays for dwellingcabins would alternate with public accommodation, such as dining rooms, living rooms etc. Khlebnikovconsidered it essential to vary the spatial composition of Frame Houses, so as to diversify the visual appearance of a city. He suggested several basic types of Frame House for this purpose, all of which could be assembled out of standard dwelling cabins. These included a Poplar House, a communications tower to which circular tiers of dwelling cabins could be attached; a Tape House designed for 1,000–2,000 cabins and representing a single or double row of cabins strung out along a vertical partition between two towers; a Bridge House consisting of towers and spans connecting them above ground or water, with bays for dwelling cabins; a Funnel House, which represented a circular frame with a large inner courtyard; a Book House formed by two vertical planes (with bays), set at an angle to each other and resembling an open book; a Whisker House consisting of one, two or three vertical sets of dwelling cabins supported by a central metal rod; a Bowl House, in which several cabins were arranged under the cover of a glass dome along a high metal pylon; underwater houses; floating houses; pyramidal houses; housing complexes with a random distribution of dwelling cabins in natural surroundings; moving cabins; Chess Houses, with bays occupied by cabins in a chequerboard pattern; Cradle Houses; Field Houses, and so on. The quest for a new form of housing during the period under review was marked by the boldness of its approach and the broad, clear and courageous statement of the problems. The new type of housing was most closely connected with the social problems of that period and intimately touched upon the restructuring of the way of life. But other questions – concerning function, aesthetics and structure – were also raised by many designs for new housing. In fact, during those years Soviet architects intensively studied all aspects of the opportunities they encountered, such as novel methods of spatial organization, the alternatives available in combining housing and public accommodation, spatial models of housing units, rationalization in planning and equipping, new types of housing – single flats, blocks, single- and multi-sectional houses, prefabricated and mobile dwellings – and much else. ![](images/895f4cc161963a71cf5bb0fbcc21c77d8df20fd1c52b5228c273af194665466c.jpg) Special social requirements in the immediate post-Revolutionary period The bitter political struggle whichthe working class had to undertake in order to establish a new system of government and radically reorganize the social and administrative systems was reflected in the elaboration of new kinds of public buildings. During the early post-Revolutionary years, trade unions and Housing Communes played an important part in the evolution of a network of cultural and civic institutions. These proletarian organizations, in conjunction with the local councils, supplied the basic constituents of a new urban life and brought into existence a whole set of new mass institutions and, consequently, of new types of public building. The Housing Communes which had sprung up spontaneously in the first years after the Revolution, and the communal houses for workers which they called into being, demanded new kinds of communal service accommodation,such as canteens, public laundries and children's facilities. Multi-purpose buildings characteristic of those early years – such as Palaces of Labour, Workers’ Palaces, Palaces of Nations etc – played a leading part in the creation of a new kind of mass cultural establishment. In the circumstances of the times, when the proletariat had only just set up the new structure of social organizations and mass cultural services, these multi-purpose buildings became the workers' social and cultural centres. They were accommodated in former private houses, palaces, Council Halls of the Nobility and other nationalized buildings. The range of activities for which they catered made it possible to determine the design specifications for new buildings of this kind. These covered a wide spectrum and included the provision of accommodation for social organizations, educational work, a theatre, club, museum, library reading room, and so on. The very varied purposes fulfilled by such public buildings reflected a particular stage in the development of the network of new social organizations and institutions, as well as the functionally undifferentiated nature of the developing system of cultural and civic provision for the workers. The determination to bring together as many institutions and organizations as possiblewithin a single publicbuilding was, however, also strongly influenced by a wish to make the new proletarian public building conspicuously huge and majestic, to be seen as the unique centre of the workers’ political and cultural life. The upsurge of those early years shed a glow of revolutionary romanticism over the new requirements for public architecture. The workers were determined that the evidence for a bright and joyful future should be embodied from the very start in splendidly majestic buildings, which must essentially bePalaces': Workers’ Palaces, Palaces of Nations, Palaces of Labour, Palaces of Culture, and so on. These were seen as symbols of the new power. They were also visible representations of a free working people's future city, often described in those days by such adjectives as huge’, ‘gigantic', bright', radiant', stupendous','luxurious', marvellous', 'majestic', 'wellshaped', beauteous'. The worker had become used to being in company during those stormy times, at meetings, assemblies and demonstrations. Having achieved power, the proletariat sought to express its monolithic unity and vastness in its public buildings. All these considerations influenced the architecture of public buildings, representing as they did a set of social requirements arising out of actual circumstances. Architecture provided a spatial embodiment to the truly revolutionary way of life of those heroic years and created a framework for the activity of the revolutionary masses. The individual was seen as a member of a team, in constant motion – in processions, at meetings or marching in the ranks. This also accounts for certain characteristic features of early designs for public venues: vast open spaces for demonstrations, grand halls for meetings, and monumental flights of stairs. ## Palaces of Labour Palaces of Labour were officially the venue of trade union organizations. In early Soviet years, however, they became typical of public buildings serving a wide range of purposes, many of themwell outside the range of trade union activities. A competition was launched in 1922 for the design of a Palace of Labour in Moscow, intended as a symbol of the new society. The list of functions specified for the building provides the clearest illustration of the complex uses to which these institutions were then put; it included: a large hall holding 8,000 people; a number of smaller halls for a variety of purposes, such as meetings, lectures, concerts, performances and films, for audiences of 300, 500 and 1,000 people; sets of offices for the Moscow City Council and the Moscow Party Committee, including conference rooms and suites; a Museum of Social Sciences; a dining hall seating 1,500 people, and more. The upper levels of the building were intended to house a radio station and an observatory, among other things, while the possibility of laying an aircraft landing strip there was also to be investigated. A variety of approaches were used to convey the spirit of the social changes then in progress in some fifty designs, submitted, among others, bythe Vesnins, Trotsky, Ilya Golosov, Lyudvig, Melnikov, Belogrud, Ginzburg with Grinberg, and Sergei Toropov with Alexander Kuznetsov. The requirements set in the Moscow Palace of Labour competition served as a model for a whole set of competitions for similar official buildings during the years 1923–26. Thus the Rostov-on-Don Palace of Labour competition required the inclusion in a single building of a theatre seating 3,500; two concert halls, seating 500 and 1,000; a dining room for 1,000 people; a library reading room; rooms for group activities; and other accommodation. Designs for this were submitted by, among other, Boris Korshunov, Ilya Golosov with Ulinich, Sergei Ovsyannikov, Grinberg, Ginzburg and Aleshin. Much the same requirements were set in the competition for the Palace of Labour at Ekaterinoslavl (now Dnepropetrovsk) with entries submitted, among others, by Sergei Krestovnikov with Vasily Voinov, Grinberg, Grigory Barkhin, David Kogan, Raikh with Fridman, and Ginzburg with Korshunov. All the prizewinning designs provided for a Palace of Labour building consisting of functionally different components welded into a single complex composition. Such Palaces of Labour represented centres of mass cultural activity with clearly differentiated sectors reserved for theatrical, club, sports, library, lecture and other purposes. Many public buildings serving a variety of functions first took shape within these Palaces of Labour complexes and then increasingly developed a separate existence during the second half of the decade, as clubs, libraries, sports buildings, theatres, Houses of Soviets, communal dining rooms or museums. A competition was held in 1932 for a Moscow District Trade Union Palace of Labour. Among the most interesting designs submitted were those by Kornfeld and Milinis, with the main building opening in a semicircle on to the main road; Egorychev, Karra and Lamtsov, with a main hall formed by rhythmically arranged semi-cylinders of glass; and Kalmykov and Osipov, in whose design the wings of a thirty-storey building descended to street level in steps forming a series of terraces. ## Houses of Soviets During early post-Revolutionary years, local Soviets, or Councils, were housed in existing buildings adapted for their use while the organization of the new authorities was being worked out and specifications were devised for specially designed buildings. Experimental designs at that early stage usually sought to provide a visual identity for buildings embodying the newly acquired workers' power, such as those by Rodchenko and Shevchenko for a Sovdep in 1920, and by Nikolsky for a rural district Soviet in 1921. The evelopment of a building type for a House of Soviets began in real earnest towards the mid-1920s, when theconstruction of buildings to house the new People's Authorities began in the Republics and administrative areas. Competitions for the design of Houses of Soviets exerted a considerable influence in this field. One of the first of these, launched in 1924, was for a House of Soviets in Bryansk. Grinberg's design was implemented in 1926. Ilya and Panteleimon Golosov, Fomin, Rukhlyadev and Krinsky were also among those taking part. According to the specification, this House of Soviets was required to accommodate the Provincial Soviet, as well as Party, trade union, and Young Communist institutions and organizations, together with a group of public premises: a small and a large hall, seating 200 and 1,000–1,200 respectively, both for House of Soviets purposes, such as congresses and conferences, and for club activities, such as shows and film performances; together with a library and section offices. In most of the competition designs, the architectural image established by this building was mainly developed from its cultural and social component, since this offered greater scope for spatial composition than the strictly official part of the premises. Two fundamentally different approaches to the design of a new type of official building came to light in the 1926 competition for the Dagestan Republic's House of Soviets at Makhachkala. The clearest examples of the contrastingtrends were Zholtovsky's design, which was adopted, offering an impregnable castle with a closed inner courtyard, and that by Ginzburg, who attempted to stress through his spatial composition the democratic character and freedom of access promoted by the new kind of official premises, and the accessibility of the new authorities to the broad layers of the population. Ginzburg further developed this approach in his competition design of 1927 for the Government House of the Kazakh Republic at Alma-Ata, which was adopted and carried out in 1929–31. It consisted of several blocks linked by corridors and a covered gangway. In his organization of interior space, the architect took special trouble over the reception areas. The interior composition is centred on a high entrance foyer, lit by paired windows from either side, whichacts as aconnecting link between all the main parts of the building.This foyer is laid out as an indoor patio, complete with a fountain and plants. Its glass partitions could be slid open in summerto let open air into the building. Other interesting Government House designs can be found at Elista in Kalmykia, by Ilya Golosov, at Minsk in Belorussia, by Langbard, and at Tashkent in Uzbekistan, by Polupanov. Houses of Soviets were also built during this period in Khabarovsk, by Ilya Golosov and Ulinich; in Gorky, by Grinberg; in Novosibirsk, by Gordeev, Turgenev and Andrei Kryachkov, while a new block was added to the Moscow Soviet building by Ivan Fomin. The Krasnaya Presnya district of Moscow was among the first to acquire a local Concil or Soviet building of its own, built in 1928–29 from a design by Golubev and Nikolai Shcherbakov. Leningrad architects made a major contribution to the design of District Houses of Soviets at the end of the 1920s and beginning of the 1930s. Great importance was attached to the actual siting of these buildings within the general lay-out of the district communal centres then being built. The Leningrad Narva District Soviet, located ina large open area, was designed by Trotsky and built as a flattened volume, with a contrasting high-rise component which provided the dominant accent in the entire district centre complex. Closely related to this was the design by Valerian Daugul andViktor Tvelkmeier, awarded a first prize in the competition for a Vyborg District Soviet building in 1931. A different conception was used for the external elevationsof Leningrad's Moscow District Soviet, by Igor Fomin, in which the main element of the spatial composition was a block, built on a circular ground plan with an inner courtyard, set against two counterbalancing three- and fourstorey blocks. The whole building was erected during the first half of the 1930s. ## The search for an aesthetic image for the country's ‘Supreme Building' The designing and building of palaces’ of every kind in early Soviet times reflected the proletariat's determination to assert its position as the new ruling class. It was also proposed to create a grandiose monument to the Great Revolution. This urge was further manifested in the search for a national Supreme Building' which would become the symbol of the Revolution and the new society. It was envisaged that this ‘Supreme Building' would act as a focus for social life and provide a landmark of international significance. Various institutional buildings were suggested at that early stage as suitable political symbols for the country as a whole, because, whatever its primary designation, the Supreme Building' was intended to provide for an assortment of functions. A candidate for the part was, for instance, the Palace of Nations in Moscow, the foundation stone of which had been ceremonially laid in the autumn of 1918, and which was conceived as a grandiose edifice housing assembly halls, theatres and a wealth of accommodation for a variety of purposes. Another was the building forVesenkha, the Supreme Council for the National Economy, for the design of which a competition was also decreed that autumn. To mark the contrast with the past, the Supreme Building’ had to present a totally new artistic image. This largely accounts for the widespread support secured by Tatlin's Monument to the Third International and its emphatically novel conception. The First Congress of Soviets of the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics met on 30 December 1922and adopted A Resolution and Treaty for the Creation of the USSR’. Sergei Kirov moved a proposal that this occasion should be marked by undertaking in the very near future the construction of a monument in which the representatives of labour could meet'. It is oftensaid of us,’ he declared, that we wiped the palaces of Part II/Social tasks of architecture bankers, landowners and tsars off the face of the earth with the speed of lightning. That is correct. Let us now erect in their place the new palace of workers and labouring peasants." It was such a palace of workers and labouring peasants’ that architects set out to design when they took part in 1922–23 in the competition for a Palace of Labour in Moscow, then intended to become the ‘Supreme Building'. Great activity was devoted in Vkhutemas-Vkhutein to the design of a Supreme Building’. Three buildings vied for this role: the Central Palace of Labour – with designs by Kozhin and Sobolev in Alexander Vesnin's studio, and by Leonid Teplitsky in Golosov's studio – in 1926; the House of Congresses of the USSR – with designs by Rashel Smolenskaya, Glushchenko and Travin in Ladovsky's studio – in 1928–29; and the Comintern building – with designs by Komarova in Alexander Vesnin’s studio, and by Kochar in Fridman’s studio – in 1929. ## The competition for the Palace of Soviets, 1931–33 A competition for the design of a Palace of Soviets in Moscow was carried out during 1931–33 in four successive rounds. Its significance was outstanding in terms of the development of a new type of government building, the establishment of an artistic model for the country's Supreme Building’ and the further evolution of Soviet architecture as a whole. In the first, preliminary, round ofthe competition, which served to clarify the requirements, many architects treated the Palace of Soviets as a People’s Forum, a place for mass collective occasions, such as demonstrations, meetings, political festivals, and both military and sports displays. The business part of the Palace, including the smaller of two halls, was accommodated in a separate set of premises subordinated in compositional terms to the main block of the building incorporating a large hall. The ARU, Vopra, Asnova and Sass teams' entries were typical in this respect The ARU design, by Nikolai Beseda, Georgy Krutikov, Vitaly Lavrov and Valentin Popov, clearly distinguished between two sets of premises in the Palace, one reserved for official use, the other for mass purposes, whichcommunicated through the outdoor area left open for demonstrations. The main component of the accommodation was a large rectangular hall capable of holding 15,000 people and arranged so that it could open on to the demonstratíon area or provide access to it. In the Vopra design, by Alabyan, Karra, Mordvinov, Revyakin and Simbirtsev, the main hall which represented the pivot of the composition was sited and organized so that all the congress delegates' seats faced the main square, from which columns of demonstrators could move towards them when the glass front partition was opened. In the Asnova design, by Balikhin, Budo, Prokhorova, Turkus, Romuald Iodko and F. Sevortyan, the main element of the building, which housed the large hall, represented a cube intersected byan inclined plane. This formed an amphitheatre, within the building and continuing outside it, in the open air, so as to enable the delegates to greet the demonstrators. The large hall in the Sass team's project, by Leonid Pavlov and Mikhail Kuznetsov, was a glass and metal structure provided with walls that could be raised and a roof that could be lowered. Some interesting individual elements for the Palace were also submitted by Ladovsky, in the form of a hemispherical dome with sloping tiers of external balconies; by Nikolsky, with a tent-shaped roof; and by Fidman, with a building drawn out lengthwise and composed of overhanging tiers. The second round attracted 160 entries, including twentyfour from abroad. Spatial and stylistic treatments of the subject varied greatly in the designs submitted. There were simple monumental forms, by Boris Iofan and Hector Hamilton; symbolic compositions by Lyudvig, Vlasov, Kurovsky, Lamtsov and Igor Yavein; the application of traditional methods and forms by Zholtovsky, Golts, Sobolev and Parusnikov; and compositions following the forms of the new architecture by Le Corbusier, as well as by Alabyan and Simbirtsev. In the statement by the Council for the Construction of the Palace ofSoviets concerning the results of the open competition, which awarded top prizes to the designs by Zholtovsky, Hamilton and Iofan, guidelines laid down for further work on the projects stressed ‘monumental quality, simplicity, integrity and elegance in thearchitectural presentation', and the application ‘both of new methods and the best employed in Classical architecture'. The variety of approaches displayed in the open competition became even more marked in the third, closed round involving thirteen entries. Three different conceptions stood out: a nationwide forum designed in the forms of the new architecture, as in the entries of Ginzburg, Gassenpflug and Lisagor, Ladovsky, and the Vesnins; a monument governed by simplicity of line, as in designs by Alabyan, Kochar and others, Golosov, and Iofan; and a set of closed compositions relying heavily on traditional forms, as in the designs by Zholtovsky, Dmitry Chechulin, and Shchusev. Five teams competed in the fourth, closed round, and they all treated the Palace of Soviets as a single monument set on a compact groundplan, regardless of the external stylistic features adopted in each case, whether these were the forms of the new architecture by the Vesnins; simplified forms in the spirit of monumental Neo-Classicism by Iofan, Alabyan, Yakov Doditsa and others; or a generous use of traditional forms by Shchuko and Gelfreikh, and Shchusev. The economic life of the country was reviving, a network of official and administrative institutions was being established, and trade relations were developing with many countries. All this required the construction of modern office premisesfor the newly created industrial, sales and foreign trade organizations. During this period, competitions were held for the design of offices for the Arkos organization in Moscow and for Gosprom, the State Industry bureau, in Kharkov. The Vesnins' design of 1924 for the Arkos building was awarded the first prize, and stood out among the rest of the entries by its rational approach to the solution of functional requirements by structural means and its uncompromisingly modern look. It set a standard for further research into the design of office buildings and influenced many competition entries for the House of Textiles in 1925, for the Rusgertorg (Russo-German Industrial and Trade Commission), Orgametal (Trust for theRationalization of Manufacture in Machine Building and in the Metal Processing Industry) and Elektrobank buildings in 1926. The latest construction techniques such as ferro-concrete frames were used in these buildings, and this to a large extent determined the treatment of their façades. When designing such office buildings, architects were closely tied by explicit requirements and largely deprived of any opportunity for functional experiments. They therefore devoted much attention to formal and aesthetic aspects, the more so because the commissioning agency as a rule insisted on buildings with a distinctive look. This led to the production towards the middle of the decade, largely by Constructivists, of some very strikingoffice building designs. Horizontal strips of glazing were combined with normal windows; solid areas of glass on the lower floors contrasted with blank walls in the upper storeys; colour was applied; and the lettering of signs and advertisements was used for decorative purposes in Russian and foreign-language inscriptions. Office buildings in the mid-1920s also frequently fulfilled various additional functions. The specification for the House of Textiles, for instance, included, apart from office space, a restaurant, general store and hotel, while a shop, café, film theatre and lodgings were provided in the Rusgertorg building. One such variant of a multi-purpose office block was designed by Alabyan and Zalesskaya in Ladovsky's Vkhutemas studio, with shops at ground level, offices on the next two floors and a hotel on the top three. Rationalist designers also essayed high-rise structures. As early as 1922–23, Krinsky took up a suggestion by Ladovsky and produced an experimental design for a Vesenkha skyscraper. This work was carried further in Ladovsky's Vkhutemas studio with designs by Volodko, Glushchenko, Vitaly Lavrov and others for a Vesenkha building of between forty and sixty storeys. In 1929, Ladovsky designed a 56-storey skyscraper on a cruciform ground plan for the Columbus Monument competition. In the mid-1920s, there emerged office building designs which attempted to combine the Constructivist and Rationalist approaches. They were divided for purposes of spatial composition into two elements, one lower, on the lines of Arkos, the other a high-rise tower akin to the Rationalist concept. The outstanding examples of this approach were designs for the buildings of Izvestiya, by Grigory Barkhin, and of Gostorg, the State Trading Office for Import and Export, by Boris Velikovsky, both of 1925–27. In both cases, ferro-concrete frame buildings were to combine a main six-storey block with a central component of twelve to thirteen storeys. However, a regulation issued in Moscow in 1926 banned the construction of buildings more than six storeys high within the Sadovoe or Garden Ringroad, although some structures rising to ten storeys had previously been erected there, such as a dwelling block by David Kogan. This made it necessary to stop high-rise construction while building work was already in progress and accounts for the fact that not a single high-rise building was constructed in central Moscow during these years. Such buildings nevertheless continued to be designed, in particular for publishing houses. Examples from Vkhutemas included designs byLeonidov, in Ladovsky's studio, and by Alexander Kurovsky, in Shchusev's, in 1926. Three- to five-storey buildings took their place, such as the offices put up in 1930 for Mosproekt, the Moscow Organization for Architectural Projects, on the Leningrad Highway, and for Transtroi, the Construction Organization for Refrigeration Works, on the Rozhdestvenka. A competition was held in 1930 for a Pravda Publishing House building, for which Golosov and Kurovsky designed a structure with a high component at the angle of the building, while Lissitzky's entry, for instance, contained no high-rise elements. Yet the project adopted and carried out in the 1930s was that by Golosov, though shorn of its high-rise component. The competition held in 1928 for the design of the Tsentrosoyuz headquarters set a trend in the design of office buildings and Leonidov's entry attracted particular attention. He offered a functionally well-conceived building comprising an entrance hall between the two parallel streets on either side, a rationally laid out working area, and a sober but nevertheless striking spatial composition. The Tsentrosoyuz building was finally built in accordance with a design by Le Corbusier, though only after he had considerably altered it. During the period under review, appropriate People's Commissariats – or ministries – had not yet been set up, and industry was directed by republican and local Economic Councils called Sovnarkhozy, for which Houses of Industry and State Industry, or Gosprom, buildings were erected. The Kharkov Gosprom building, designed and built by Serafimov, Felger and Kravets in 1925–28, represented an important landmark in the development of this form of official administrative centre. Some twenty-five individual institutions – a Higher Council for the People's Economy,an Industrial Bank and various other industrial institutions – were housed in the vast Gosprom building consisting of three blocks linked by gangways. Each block had its own entrance hall, but they communicated internally and shared such facilities as a main conference hall, a dining room and a library. The House of Industry at Sverdlovsk, the industrial headquarters for the Urals, closely resembled the Gosprom building in Kharkov. The competition for its design was carried out in two rounds: the first, in 1927, attracted entries from Alexander and Leonid Vesnin; Andrei Burov, Sinyavsky and Barshch; and Ivan Fomin, among others. The second, in 1931, received designs from Afanasev, Kornfeld and Milinis; and Ilya Golosov, among others. In general, research into the creation of new types of administrative and office buildings pursued two main aims: to create favourable circumstances of work and leisure for those employed there, such as special accommodation for rest, eating and social work; and to stress the democratic nature of Soviet institutions both in the organization ofinterior space, in well-lit passages, reception rooms, exhibitions in corridors etc, and in the exteriors of the buildings, so as to create an atmosphere favouring close contacts between visitors and those employed in the various institutions concerned. A large number of administrative and office buildings were erected during the period under review. These often differed sharply from each other, not only in their functional lay-out, but also in their external appearance. Thus, for example, the People's Commissariat for Agriculture in Erevan was built by Tamanyan in 1928 in the Neo-Armenian style, while its Moscow equivalent, by Shchusev in 1929–33, was conceived in the spirit of the new architecture. The People's Commissariat for Communications in Moscow of 1930, by Ivan Fomin, was a Neo-Classicist building. There was an equally marked difference in appearance between the virtually contemporaneousNeo-Renaissance Gosbank in Moscow,by Zholtovsky in 1927–29, and the Constructivist Bank in Ivanovo-Vosnesensk, by Viktor Vesnin in 1927–28. A competition was held in 1934 for the design of a headquarters for Narkomtyazhprom, the People's Commissariat for Heavy Industry, which was the first of the industrial ministries. This was regarded as far more than an office building. It was to be a symbol of the country's successful industrialization, and the proposed site for it was in Moscow's Red Square. This competition could be said to have been the last creative contest between the innovative architects – represented here by Alexander and Viktor Vesnin, Leonidov, Melnikov, Ginzburg and Lisagor – and the traditionalists, who now established themselves ever more firmly. ![](images/7ec6cc4eb16432e8a76cc37caf8400fb5e355b79a8c5d51d91d8058168115f21.jpg) Workers' Clubs as centres of a new socialist culture ## Types of Workers' Clubs From the very first years of Soviet power, a great deal of attention had been devoted to the cultural dimension of the Revolution, which was seen as an integral component of the plan to build socialism. The Workers' and Village Clubs – otherwise known as People’s Houses’ – which sprang up in those early days provided the main breeding ground for the dissemination points of the new socialist culture. These clubs ranked as outstandingly important centres for mass agitation, for the improvement of cultural standards among workers and for the organization of leisure throughout the population. The programme adopted by the VIIIth Party Congress in 1919 included the task of organizing the most fruitful possible network of People’s Houses’.¹ No less than 7,000 People's Houses – or clubs – came into being in the first two years of Soviet rule. The XIIth Party Congress stressed that clubs must become centres for mass propaganda and the development of creativity among the working class'.2 As Workers' Clubs proliferated in former palaces and private residences, four distinct types developed from the very start, depending on the kind of community each of them was intended to serve. There were domestic ones, linked with Housing Communes; industrial ones, attached to production plants; vocational ones, which were offshoots of trade unions, and territorial clubs, run by district or city councils. These last formed the most closely planned and numerically important category. They originated from the design of communal cultural complexes such as the first Palaces of Labour and Workers' Palaces. Typically, the competition specification for the design of a Workers' Palace in Petrograd in 1919 stated that this was a new requirement, first called into being by life itself, for the creation of a district cultural educational centre’. It was to include a large hall for meetings and theatrical performances, accommodating 3,000–4,000 people, a small lecture hall for 300, separate study areas, a two-year People's University course with a lecture hall seating 1,200 and lecture rooms for 120 students each, an art school, a school of music, an educational cultural club comprising a library, reading room and scientific department, accommodation for leisure activities and special interest groups, a hall for chamber music and amateur dramatics, a self-service restaurant, a gymnasium, an open-air stadium and the provision of fields and courts for various sporting activities. The specification for the first district clubs or People's Houses was close to these Workers’ Palace requirements. For instance, the accommodation required at the Lenin House of the People at Ivanovo-Voznesensk, for the design of which a competition was held in 1924, comprised a theatre with 1,200 seats, an assembly hall for 400 people, a Lenin Museum, a central town library, a sports hall and club premises. Entries were submitted by Grigory Barkhin, the Vesnins, Panteleimon Golosov and Parusnikov, Ilya Golosov, Rukhlyadev and Krinsky, among others. A series of competitions for the design of Workers’ Clubs or Palaces of Culture was held in 1924–25. Among the first to be built was the Moscow-Narva district House of Culture in Leningrad, by Gegello and Krichevsky in 1925–27. It included a theatre and concert hall seating 1,900, cinema for 400, a library, a lecture room, several dozen rooms for club activities, a sports hall etc. All these were gathered into a compact symmetrical spatial composition. The front elevation was made up of massive volumes aligned along a smooth convex curve. A glass screen, divided at rhythmic intervals by mullions with a triangular section, provided the centrepiece. This was flanked on either side by tall piers housing the stairs, backed by neutrally conceived lateral blocks with plain walls pierced by windows. An All-Union Competition was held in 1927 for the design of the House of the Worker in Kharkov, later built in accordance with Dmitriev's plans, which had secured the first prize. This building represented an elaborate cultural and educational complex midway between the early conception of the Palace of Labour and a district House of Culture. The central part of the Palace's front elevation curvedoutwards. It was generously glazed, modulatedby a fluted surface, andstood higher than the lateral elements of the building which descended from the centre in three separate steps. ## Melnikov's clubs Workers’ Clubs serving the labour force of large industrial enterprises or the members ofindividual trade unions were much in demand during the second half of the 1920s. Melnikov made a major contribution to the planning of the buildings required with designs for five such clubs built in Moscow – the Rusakov, Gorky, Kauchuk, Frunze and Burevestnik Clubs – and for one at nearby Dulevo. As an architect, he set great store by a functional and rational organization of the premises, but he nonetheless also took much trouble to produce an expressive exterior and to link the external composition of the building with the innovative organization of its internal space. He was at pains to provide for the greatest possible variety of uses of the building, and worked out a series of ingenious ways of transforming the halls and putting them to a number of different purposes. In the Rusakov Club, for instance, each of the three balcony elements with their back rows suspended on projecting brackets could be segregated from the rest of the main hall, which accommodated a maximum audience of 1,500 people, thereby turning them into self-contained individual auditoria with a capacity of 180 each. In the Gorky Club, the hallcould be divided into two equal parts with a capacity of 500 people each, by means of movable partitions lodged in recesses in the side walls. A swimming pool was also designed under the floor of this hall, but was not eventually built. The large viewing hall in the Burevestnik Club held tiers of seats along its sides, as well as in the stalls, for use at sports events in the hall, which was lit from above. The stall seats were then removed and the hall was combined with the adjoining sports hall into a single interior unit by moving a retractable partition. The semicircular, three-tiered hall of the Kauchuk Club was transformed into superimposed units by means of a horizontal platform which could be raised to the level of the first or second tier from the stalls, thereby separating the upper from the lower part of the hall. The small Frunze Club had a hall with 370 seats which could be divided into three longitudinal sections by partitions raised from recesses in the floor. The imagination and inventiveness displayed by Melnikov in composing these spatial organizations are also remarkable. The Rusakov Club has a trumpet-like shape with three projecting brackets. The Burevestnik Club represents a four-storey tower built on a cinquefoil ground plan. The Gorky Club has a barrel-shaped auditorium wedged between two tall rectangular end blocks. The ground plan of the Kauchuk Club represents a sector of a circle. The smaller Frunze Club has a sweeping outline and its façade overhangs an open terrace. And Melnikov's design for the Zuev Club, which was not carried out, consists of a number of intersecting vertical cylinders. Chapter 4/Workers' Clubs as centres of a new socialist culture ## The search for an aesthetic image of the Workers' Club A number of different conceptions of the requisite image for a Workers' Club were current during the 1920s. Melnikov treated the club building as a single large volume with a striking, usually symmetrical shape. Ilya Golosov, on the other hand, did not try to collect the various elements of the building into a unified and clearly outlined shape. He placed the main external accent of the building on one or other of its component elements, deliberately designed on broad but simple lines, while scaling down and complicating all the other components so as to subordinate them visually to the main element. In the Moscow Zuev Club, designed and built by him in 1927–28, the glazed cylindrical staircase cage intersects the horizontal parallelepiped formed by the upper storey and thereby becomes the main element in a complex and highly articulated composition. The means used to single out one strongly designed component and subordinate the remainder of the composition to it are even more explicit in Ilya Golosov's design for a Palace of Culture in Stalingrad produced in conjunction with Mitelman in 1928. The huge windowless cylinder of the main hall is contrasted with the rectangular volumes of various sizes and shapes which intersect it horizontally and vertically. The followers of the Leningrad school ofSuprematist Constructivism’ approached the image for a Workers' Club quite differently. They constructed complex compositions consisting of rectangular – usually flattened – volumes scored by horizontal runs of windows, often contrasted with the vertical rectangular mass of the theatre, as in Khidekel's designs for a Workers' Club in 1926 and the Dubrovka power station club in 1930–31, as well as designs emanating from Nikolsky's studio in 1926–27. Strict Functional Constructivists’, on the other hand, preferred to use a pavilion lay-out in their designs for clubs, with no special emphasis on any particular component. Andrei Burov, who designed a number of clubs for members of the food industry trade unions in Moscow and Tver during 1927–28, introduced many novel features, such as complex compositions in which the club and theatre were housed in separate, but interconnected blocks. Outwardly, Burov's clubs were characterized by a proliferation of galleries, balconies, loggias, openair terraces and pergolas. The functional organization of interior space is even more emphatically reflected in the exterior composition of clubs designed by Ginzburg and the Vesnins, such as the standard Railwaymen’s Clubs by Ginzburg in 1926, and the project by Leonid Vesnin for theTextile Workers’ Union in 1927. The Workers' Clubs built in Baku and surrounding oil workers' settlements at the turn of the decade after designs by Alexander and Leonid Vesnin adopted a pavilion lay-out. The individual buildings communicated with each other in accordance with their functional role, and their outer appearance neatly reflected the proportions and shape of the individualinteriors. The Vesnins, unlike Melnikov, made no attempt to impart a well-defined and memorable image to club exteriors. Their designs were, on the contrary, asymmetrical and complex in composition, as though they were intent on fragmenting volumes and shrinking their scale. This approach is well illustrated in the House of the Association of Pre-Revolutionary Political Deportees, built in 1931–35 after their entry for a competition which produced a number of other interesting designs, such as those by Ilya Golosov, as well as Barshch, Vladimirov and Zundblat. Miliutin's design in 1931 for a club and restaurant laid out on a slope also conformed to Constructivist methods and compositional concepts. Lamtsov, on the other hand, a dedicated Rationalist, treated the same task quite differently in 1926 by designing a cylindrical structure from which sectors were sliced out at various levels. ## The large-scale club building programme and attendant debates The design and contruction of Workers' Clubs commissioned by large industrial enterprises and craft trade unions got into full swing during the second half of the 1920s. These included: in Moscow, the Proletariat Metalworkers' Club, by Vladimirov in 1927–28; the Red Textile Workers' Club, by Rozanov in 1928; the Dzerzhinsky Building Workers’ Club in 1928; later, the Rotfront Printing Workers' Club, by Pen in 1930; and the Hammer and Sickle, by Milinis in 1929–30; in Erevan, the Construction Workers' Club, by Alabyan, Kochar and Mazmanyan in 1929–31; in Leningrad, the House of Culture of the Leatherworkers' Union, by Mikhail Reizman in 1929–31; in Kramatorsk, the Workers' Theatre Club, by Dmitriev and Grad in 1928–31; and in Kharkov, the Club of the Construction Workers’ Union, by Malozemov, Milinis and Shteinberg in 1927–28. Work on the construction of clubs during the second half of the 1920s greatly exceeded that involving other types of mass cultural institutions, mainly because clubs had taken over and incorporated the functions of a varietyof educational establishments and places of entertainment. The Workers’ Club started in early Soviet times as a centre for agitational and cultural educational work among the labouring masses. It had been an important instrument of the cultural revolution and the political education of the workers, but by the end of the 1920s it had also accumulated a multiplicity of other functions and organizational arrangements.The workers' developing intellectual needs and the ever-growing complexity of urban life outside working hours required a diversification of club functions. If the entire range of needs arising among the members of the particular industrial community that a club was intended to serve was to be satisfied, the architect would have to provide for every purpose in his design and introduce various methods of transforming the premises to meet the various needs.Obviously, once the limit of such adaptability had been reached, any further extension of the workers’ interests could only be achieved by physically enlarging the premises. In practice, therefore, either the purposes to which a club could be put would have to be restricted, or greater diversification would have- to be introduced and specialized accommodation made available for general use, rather than for the benefit of a particular industrial community. Thesecontradictions – and many others – in the requirements set for Workers' Clubs came to light when mass building work had already begun, during the second half of the 1920s. An embittered debate broke out in periodicals in 1928 concerning the future of the Workers' Club which by then was undergoing a marked crisis of identity as a form of cultural workers’ organization. This further intensified arguments then current about the architecture of the clubs. A series of suggestions about design came up in discussion, such as those tabled by Milinis in 1928. These included a radical change in the nature of social demand, as part of the establishment of a new society; the unsolved problems of socialist settlement and a new way of life; the conflict between various theoretical conceptions – and attendant models of spatial organization – of urban life in the future. All this left its mark on the development of new types of building and, in particular, brought undue attention to questions of ‘social contacts’ out of working hours. It was the determination to collectivize both consumption and leisure that caused the creation of such new building types during this period as Housing Communes, clubs for workers, mass kitchens, mass theatres, and public baths. The maximalist demand for the collectivization of every worker's entire leisure time, implicit to a greater or lesser extentin the specifications for all such buildings, played a part in plunging architecture into a state of crisis towards the end of this period. Serious contradictions began to emerge betweenthe demands put to the architect and the actual, rapidly changing living conditions. This was mostly the result of a certain disregard in experimental work during the 1920s of the fact that the worker – and his requirements – had switched in the briefest possible interval of time from the old capitalist circumstances to the newsocialist ones. New needs constantly arose, and many designs which their authors had regarded as pointers to the future were rapidly put out of date for modern purposes, despite the genuine virtuosity of their functional and artistic treatment. This represents one of the essential contradictions in Soviet architecture during the period when it was becoming established. Leonidov’s designs were of special interest in this context, because designing for the future in his terms was not merely a matter of boldly tackling functional and technical problems but, first and foremost, of providing for changing human needs. ## Leonidov's clubs In 1926, Leonidov had submitted competitiondesigns for standard Workers'Club buildings to accommodate 500 and 1,000 people, which differed little in the disposition and allocation of their premises, their lay-out and spatial composition, from what was customary at that time. By 1928, however, when a lively debate developed about the future of Workers' Clubs, Leonidov produced two alternative versions of an experimental design for a Club of a New Social Type’, which he used as illustrations for his paper on the new type of club at the First Osa Congress in 1929. These designs involved large complexes comprising a number of separate, but interconnected buildings, some of which were reserved for specialized purposes, while the rest were intended for unrestricted use. Leonidov treated a club complex as a kind of social cultural centre, with a winter garden, a general-purpose hall for lectures, cinema, demonstrations, meetings, use as a planetarium etc; a library; a laboratory; an open ground for glider competitions, motor racing, war games, tourism etc; a sports hall; a playroom with playpens and a pool; and a park. In architectural terms, the ‘Club of a New Social Type’ represented a broadly conceived and loosely organized park-like composition with, as its centrepiece, the great hall roofed by a parabolic vault-like covering. In addition to the Club of a New Social Type’, Leonidov worked out a Scheme for an Area Cultural Organization, which graphically described the provision of a cultural service for the population as a whole, involving both clubs and cultural activities at work and at home. Although Leonidov did not think it essential to concentrate all cultural contacts within a single venue, he identified as a main strand within the general system of culture and leisure the sharing of mutual interests within a fully developed area club complex. The principles stated in the Scheme for an Area Cultural Organization were most fully implemented in his design for a Palace of Culture in the Moscow Proletarsky district. ## The competition for the Proletarsky District Palace of Culture, Moscow, 1930 A competition was held in 1930 for the design of a Palace of Culture in the Proletarsky district of Moscow, to be built on the site of the former Simonov Monastery. There was much interest among the public in this competition, which was regarded in architectural circles as marking animportant stage in the creative rivalry between the various trends and schools. Teams from all the principal architectural associations took part. The Mao team, consisting of Golts, Parusnikov, and Sobolev, Part II/Social tasks of architecture suggested a stylistic link with the old monastery, while those of ARU, comprising Krutikov, Vitaly Lavrov, Sergei Lopatin and Valentin Popov; of Asnova, with Bykova, Korzhev, Andrei Korobov, Spassky and Turkus; and of Vopra, with Simbirtsev and Alexander Mashinsky, all offered a modern treatment. Leonidov's design, on behalf of Osa, attracted the greatest attention, although he had substantially departed from the specifications and conditions. He used a large site on which he proposed to create a cultural complex forming an oasis of greenery amid throbbing modern urban life and, shielded from its hubbub, where one might find spiritual relaxation after a day at work. The wide area occupied by the Palace of Culture was divided into four square sectors – one reserved for scientific research, another for group activities, the third for physical culture and the fourth for staging demonstrations. Each sector had a lay-out and main building ofits own: an extended threestorey building in the scientific research sector; a pyramidshaped gymnasium, flooded with light from above, in the physical training centre; and an all-purpose auditorium on a circular ground plan roofed by a hemispherical glass dome, for group activities. This auditorium could be split up into individual sections and the seats rotated or, if so required, sunk into the floor, so as to clear the entire area. Several stage aprons could be raised from a circular pit located at the centre of the hall and moved to any part of it, so as to provide either a rostrum or a platform. Leonidov's entry set off an animated debate embracing not only problems of club design, but the future development of Soviet architecture as a whole. The design finally adopted for the Proletarsky District Palace of Culture was provided by the Vesnins in 1931. It consisted of three main blocks, one for club purposes, the other two serving as theatres, of which one was small and calculated for an audience of 1,000 spectators, while the other – which was never built – was to have held 4,000. The club block was built on a T-shaped ground plan, with an auditorium, library, winter garden, observatory, restaurant etc. At first-floor level, the club block communicated with the small theatre's foyer, lit from two sides by a bay window, through the exhibition hall, also lit from both sides, and led into a winter garden projecting on a semicircular plan over the steep bank of the Moscow River. The design and construction of Palaces of Culture speeded up considerably inthe early 1930s. Grandiose cultural complexes were planned, among others, at Voronezh, by Kornfeld, Zaltsman and Pavel Blokhin; at Zlatoust, by Arkady Arkin and Mashinsky; and at Samara, by Arkin. There was the Vasily Ostrov Palace of Culture in Leningrad, by Trotsky and Solomon Kozak, and the Gorbunov Palace of Culture in Moscow, by Kornfeld. Mass spectacles staged in connection with public holidays and popular celebrations had an important part to play in the workers’ lives amongst the many forms of political agitation. Mass activities – ceremonialmeetings, festival demonstrations, manifestations and open-air festivals – were regarded as characteristic features of the new society, which in many ways set a course for creative experimentation, both within the theatre and in the design of new buildings for it. The diversity and constantly changing pattern of the mass acitivitiesspontaneously generated by the workers required the creation of a physical framework for them: speakers' rostrums were set up in the squares and streets of cities, backgrounds created for funeral processions, vast halls designed for assemblies and meetings, arrangements made for theatres to open into the street so that columns of demonstrators could march through them, and so on. One of the first attempts to develop an architectural framework for the new forms of mass activity was the design in Zhivskulptarkh in 1919 of a Temple of Communion Between Nations. This was designed as a grandiose building with a platform for events’, or as an assembly of structures provided with a platform stage, or as an open-air court laid out for large meetings, flanked by a relatively small, enclosed hall. Mass staged spectacles to celebrate May Day and the anniversary of the October Revolution became widespread. One of the most splendid of them was to be the celebration on the Khodynskoe Field in honour of the Third Congress of the Comintern in 1921. The presentation of the event was arranged jointly by Meyerhold as stage director, AlexanderVesnin as architect and Popova as painter. Elaborate three-dimensional decorations were prepared and agitational placards were suspended at various heights from balloons. Mass events also left their mark on stage presentation. Directors and designers tried to narrow the gap between actor and spectator by removing the set from the box stage and imparting to it a three-dimensional function of its own, so that it became a machine tool' for the artist's performance. This autonomy of the stage set, enabling it to be viewed from all sides and to be erected in all circumstances, even in the open, especially when the company was on tour, was then seen not merely as a new approach to stage presentation, but as a means of democratizing the theatre and bringing it closer to a mass audience. The development of mobile vehicles of propaganda was a matter of special concern in early Soviet days, when Agit trains and boats were much in use. Large theatres were fitted out inside special Agit barges, as in Osipov's design of 1921. There were also motorized Agit theatres, such as the mobile model created by Babichev in 1922, with a sectional factory-made metal frame which could be assembled in a couple of hours in a square, or even a field. This structure was intended for theatrical and variety performances, but could also be converted into a circus ring, a sports court, a cinema, a lecture rostrum or a small platform suitable for debates etc. Soon after the Revolution, a large network of amateur Proletarian Theatres developed within the Proletkult system, to counterbalance theold' professional theatres, often thought of then as specially serving the displaced former ruling classes. The workers also countered traditional variety performances with the new form of show provided by the Blue Blouse Agit teams. ## Meyerhold: development of the mass action theatre The Proletarian Theatres, which mainly used Palaces of Labour and Workers’ Clubs, influenced the lay-out of the auditoria in such buildings. Social segregation into stalls, boxes, circles, tiers and galleries, so characteristic of the past, was done away with, and there was no discrimination in the seating arrangements provided in the new kind of hall. Performances were no longer addressed to an elite audience, as in the past, but to a broad layer of the working population. Hence the requirement for halls accommodating very large numbers of spectators. The need for close contact between the audience of workers and the amateur actors in the Proletarian Theatres or the members of the Blue Blouse teams, as well as a wish for audience participation, set a new standard for ease of mutual access and closer physical proximity between spectators and performers than in the traditional theatre.This new approach to the spatial organization of the auditorium was already apparent during the first half of the 1920s, in the treatment, for instance, of the large hall in many entries for the MoscowPalace of Labour competition in 1922–23 and the Moscow-Narva District House of Culture competition in Leningrad in 1925. Requirements reflecting the democratization of the theatre were supplemented in the design of the auditorium by demands arising from current experiments in the professional theatre, such as maximal mechanization, mainly to effect rapid changes of scene, and the introduction of projection facilities. New basic specifications for theatre buildings were worked out in the second half of the 1920s on the strength of experience acquired in the staging of mass events, the creation of amateur theatres, and the experiments of innovative directors such as Meyerhold, Eisenstein, Tairov and Vakhtangov. Meyerhold deeply influenced the architectural requirements set for the new type of mass action theatre. He stimulated the creation of an extensive network of amateur proletarian companies when he was in charge of the Theatre Section of the People's Commissariat for Education during the early years of Soviet rule. He saw the revolutionary theatre as a mass popular spectacle – a spectacular meeting acted out on stage among a crowd of workers, soldiers and peasants, as demonstrated by his highly effective first political production, Mayakovsky's Mystery-Bouffe. In his writings under the general title of October in the Theatre, Meyerhold stated his belief that the Revolution would bring forth a public theatre of popular action. He introduced a series of new methodsaimed at creating a direct contact between actor and audience, and converting a performance into a spectacular meeting. These methods, which affected both production and the organization of scenic space, involved discarding footlights, keeping houselights up throughout a performance, transferringspeech and action into the audience, effecting a lack of formality between actors and spectators – often by use of improvisation – erecting and dressing the set in full view of the audience,and soon, Meyerhold, together with Mayakovsky, created a new form of Agit spectacle which transformed a theatrical performance into a political statement. This approach to a theatrical performance as a mass event posed new problems in the spatial organization of productions and the ways ofintegrating the interior of a theatre with the life of the squares and streets. In his lecture ‘The New Theatre' in 1927, Meyerhold asserted that new theatre buildings specially adapted to the new kinds of performance must be erected.He then published a detailed specification for such a mass action building in 1929.This includeda whole series of specific requirements concerning the spatial organization and equipment of the new theatre – maximum mechanization and projection facilities in the hall, a very great increase in its seating capacity, the abandonment of balconies and circles and the abolition’ of the proscenium arch stage. Meyerhold believed that the development of a new form of theatre required close collaboration between director and architect. His work with Lissitzky in 1928–29 in the production ofTretyakov's I Want a Child marked an important stage in the formulation of new principles governing the interior spatial organization of a theatre. Director and architect jointly decided, in the course of their experiments with the production, radically to reshape the interior of the old theatre and to set the stage in the middle of the hall with the audience fanned out in an amphitheatre round it. Meyerhold developed this idea further with the architects Mikhail Barkhin and Sergei Vakhtangov, first in the adaptation of an old theatre in 1930–31, then in the design for a new theatre in 1931–32 based on his Principles for the Construction of a New Theatre’. These involved: (1) merging the auditorium and the scenic space, in other words doing away with the proscenium arch; (2) promoting an axonometric view of the action by seating the audience in an amphitheatre; (3) providing a three-dimensional perception of the action by the use of an arena stage; (4)including within the lay-out of the hall all essential services such as dressing rooms, technical equipment and orchestra; (5) arranging for access to the stage by motor vehicles and demonstrations. The whole building later became a concert hall. ## Competition for new types of theatre design, 1930–33 The basic specifications for the new type of building devoted to the presentation of public spectacles – the mass action theatre – had been fully formulated by the end of the 1920s. It was to become, at the start of the next decade, the chief edifice in town for every sort of purpose, from theatre to sports competitions, meetings, lectures, gatherings, and so on. The demand for adaptability, which was fundamental in the mass action theatre, in fact helped to convert it into an all-purpose hall. Competitions were held in a number of large cities for the design of a theatre destined to be the most important building there, serving not only cultural display functions, but also used for the organization of mass politicaloccasions. The first such competition was for the design of an opera and drama theatre at Rostov-on-Don in 1930. Its specification provided for the combination in a single building of an auditorium accommodating 2,500 people, a concert hall for 800, exhibition galleries, a theatre museum, and premises for children. The design carried out in 1930–35 was by Shchuko and Gelfreikh. The building was compact and rectangular. The hall itself was designed as an amphitheatre with a wide proscenium arch and a stage projecting well forward. The concert hall was placed above the main foyer. The exterior effect derived from the contrast between the twin, free-standing tall glazed staircase cages linked to the upper storeys of the theatre, at concert-hall level, by the spans of gangways, and the vast, blind, white marblelined screen overhanging the glazed-in foyer. The international competition to design a Massed Musical Performance Theatre in Kharkov in 1930–31attracted 142 entries, including ninety-one from abroad – the USA, France, Germany, Italy, Japan and elswhere. According to the specifications, the stage and a hall accommodating 4,000 spectators were to form part of a single spatial unit and provide, in addition to theatrical performances, for national festivals, meetings, sporting events and competitions, circus shows, dramatized Agit team events, demonstration marches, and so on. Amongthe large number of original ideas and spatial inventions produced by this competition,the Vesnins’ design secured the top award. It included a horseshoe-shaped hall in which the single amphitheatre and semicircular stage formed part of a combined ovoid volume, the spectator capacity of which could be varied from 2,000 to 6,000 places. The exterior of the building reflected its inner structure and was treated in large simple volumes. The oval hall, surrounded by a gallery foyer, was roofed over by a flattened dome and contrasted by its smooth curved outline and generous glazing with the blind rectangular screening wall of the box stage. A competition was held in1931 for the design of a mass action theatre at Ivanovo-Voznesensk. The specificationswere similar to those of the Kharkov project and required a multipurpose building. The most interesting entry, that by Ilya Golosov, offered in effect a general municipal hall with 2,750 seats. Amphitheatre and stage were designed for massed performances of various kinds and arranged so as to bring actors and spectators into close contact. Mass demonstrations, army detachments and motor vehicles were able to cross the stage. A large open space fronted the theatre, and several speaker's rostrums overlooked it from the main façade so that mass open-air meetings could be conducted from them. Vlasov's design was eventually adopted and carried out. It involved a trapezoidal hall with a capacity of 2,500 seats. A wide proscenium arch helped to unite the auditorium and the stage, and a concert hall with 500 seats was fitted into the ground floor. A synthetic panorama and planetarium' theatre, with a hall to hold an audience of 3,000, was designed by Grinberg in Novosibirsk in 1931. Stage and auditorium were completely fused into one: a stage which reached far out into the hall was surrounded on three sides, as in the Meyerhold Theatre, by a raised fan of seats. The design incorporated a high degree of mechanization both on stage and in theauditorium: a peripheral proscenium was arranged on a movable ring along the perimeter of the auditorium; the stalls could be converted into a circus ring, with some of the audience seated on the proscenium; the vast dome was to be used for the projection of films; and so on. The construction of the theatre suffered a number of long delays during which the original design was repeatedly modified by different architects, mainly as regarded its exterior. A competition for the design of a synthetic theatre’ in Sverdlovsk was launched in 1932. The building was to be the mass cultural and educational centre for the capital of the Urals industrial area. It was intended to accommodate all types of theatrical performance – drama, opera and ballet – concerts and variety shows, as well as political meetings. The specification required a theatre with 4,000 seats, which could be adapted for mass meetings with up to 8,000 participants; a cinema seating 1,000 spectators; a group of study and class rooms; and service accommodation. Ginzburg’s design was placed first. Its most interesting feature was the main hall laid out on a trapezoid ground plan. A well-judged organization of the space available enabled the architect to restrict the dimensions of the hall and fan of seats to a minimum, while locating the spectators at the right distance from the stage. The acoustic treatment was particularly successful owing to the skilled design of the lining of the dome spanning the hall.The circular portion of the stalls together with the proscenium could be rotated through 180° in order to create an arena in the middle of the hall. Ilva Golosov's and Ladovsky's entries for the Sverdlovsk competition were also of considerable interest. In order to make the hall as adaptable as possible for a variety of purposes, Golosov designed a large stage, with a middle section consisting of a mechanically operated catwalk reaching out into the rectangular area of the stalls. Ladovsky, for his part, designed the hall as a vast roofed stadium with a large central rectangular arena flanked by three fans of seats, two of them at either end and the third facing a longer side. Competitions were held in 1932–33 for the design of the theatre building for Mosps, the Moscow District Soviet of Trade Unions, requiring a hall with 2,500 seats, all-purpose use, full convertibility and mechanization; and for the Nemirovich-Danchenko Theatre, also in Moscow, with a capacity of 1,600 seats. These competitions provided a culmination in the quest for a new type of theatre. In the first, designs by Ladovsky and, in particular, by Melnikov, proved of the greatest interest. The latter proposed a mechanically rotated, amphitheatre-shaped auditorium to take advantage for production purposes of three separate stages, located on the perimeter of the hall, differing in size and šhape and provided with a variety of transformation equipment. Ginzburg produced the most original entry for the second competition with a building comprising two components – one, on a trapezium-shaped plan, containing the auditorium, foyer and stage, inserted into another, on a ring-shaped plan, housing the dressing rooms. The four-year period, from 1930 to 1933, devoted to the elaboration of a new type of building designed to accommodate the mass action theatre represents a unique development in twentieth-century architecture, both in the intensity of the innovative work undertaken at that time and the multiplicity of original ideas bearing on the spatial organization and transformation facilities planned into an all-purpose auditorium. Very little of all this, however, was carried out. The theatre at Rostov-on-Don was the only one to be built in accordance with its initial design. The remainder of the successful entries were either abandoned or radically altered in the course of construction. This was partly due to the complex situation affecting creative endeavour at that time in Soviet architecture as a whole, but also to doubts about the very concept of mass action, which led during the first half of the 1930s to a substantial change in the specifications for theatrical buildings. This included, among much else, a reduction in the required capacity of halls, re-establishment of the separation between stage and auditorium, the renewed use of tiers, balconies and stalls and of theatres designed purely for purposes of dramatic performance. The circus, one of the most democratic forms of mass spectacle with its open arena surrounded by seats, also contributed to the design of the standard mass action theatre during the 1920s. Separate circus buildings were planned at this time – as in Ilya Golosov’s design in 1922 for a model circus – or were inserted into a club complex – the round circus hall, for instance, in Golosov's design for the Palace of Culture in Stalingrad in 1928. In some cases, an all-purpose hall for circus and stage performances was proposed, such as the circus building at Ivanovo-Voznesensk built in 1931–33, with Sergei Minofev as architect and Boris Lopatin as engineer, which could also be used as a cinema and a music hall. It contained an arena and a platform stage, used alternately as performing and spectator areas depending on the type of show. The hall had a capacity of 3,000 seats and an unusual ceiling, consisting of a hemispherical dome, fifty metres in diameter, supported by a vaulted frame of thirty-two semicircular wooden arches. ## The planetarium: a scientific display The traditional concept of the theatre was challenged during the 1920s not only by various forms of mass action spectacle with a politically agitational flavour, but also by scientific educational displays, such as the planetarium. The first planetarium was built in Moscow in 1927–29 to designs by Barshch and Sinyavsky. The auditorium held 1,440 seats on a circular ground plan. Its ferro-concrete parabolic dome, lined by a hemispherical screen, provided the main structural element in the building's spatial composition. Most of the service accommodation was housed on the ground floor, below the auditorium. A few outbuildings, on a small scale in relation to the inner cylindrical space contained under the dome, were provided for special purposes. These small annexes with their imaginatively designed details – a winding staircase in a glazed cylindrical cage, a curved ferro-concrete canopy over the entrance, round and elongated ferro-concrete pillars – further underlined the massive proportions of the main building. The quest for a new type of scientific theatre’ – the planetarium – went beyond its general architectural organization, which had been functionally perfected abroad.The hemispherical screen made it possible to display the whole sky, but at some discomfort to the spectator, who was kept facing in a single direction. Attempts were made to resolve this difficulty by experimenting with the shape of the auditorium and the dome. One such experimental design was produced by Bunin in Ladovsky's Vkhutemas studio in 1927, with a nearly-square hall containing parallel rows of seats and roofed by a cylindrical vault which ended in a half-dome at the front of the hall on to which the image of the starry sky was projected. ## Experimental cinema designs From the earliest days after the Revolution, tremendous importance was attached to the cinema as a new form of mass art. A brilliant constellation of innovative directors – Kuleshov, Dziga Vertov, Eisenstein, Pudovkin and Lovzhenko – gave Soviet films a leading position in the world. Much attention was paid to providing the film industry with modern equipment. A competition was held in 1927 for the design of a complex of film studios, a so-called film factory’, in Moscow, which attracted entries from Leonidov and Ilya Golosov among others. The film factory or film village as a new type of modern structure was also studied during the 1920s in Vkhutemas by Zaltsman and Simbirtsev among others. Few cinemas were built during the 1920s, since Workers' Clubs were adequate for the purpose. Nevertheless, the standard form for a film theatre was well studied, and certain improvements were made in the architectural specifications for its design. The most interesting of such designs produced during the 1920s came from Ladovsky's Vkhutemas studio. He set his students tasks such as that of fitting a film theatre into an awkwardly shaped plot within a heavily built-up area – a theme developed in designs by Zalesskaya and Oganes Balyan.
