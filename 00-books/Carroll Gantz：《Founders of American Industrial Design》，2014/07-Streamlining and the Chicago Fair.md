# Streamlining and the Chicago Fair The German Bauhaus, much admired by MoMA, was in serious trouble in Dessau, Germany. Faced with political and financial pressure, Gropius and Moholy-Nagy had resigned from the Bauhaus in 1928 and went to England, while architect Hannes Meyer, a committed Marxist who attempted to link the school to a vision of social and political transformation, assumed leadership. Some students were communists, others Nazis, both escalating political tensions. Architect Ludwig Mies van der Rohe (1886–1969) had become director of the Bauhaus in 1930, replacing Meyer, who had been forced by Nazis to resign at the same time that communist students were expelled. In the month before the MoMA exhibition, the Dessau city council, pressured by the powerful National Socialist (Nazi) German Workers Party under Adolph Hitler, who was not yet in full power, dissolved the Bauhaus in that city, due to a “Jewish-Marxist art manifestation," a travesty that may have inspired MoMA to elevate the legacy of the Bauhaus to near martyrdom. As a last-ditch effort to save the school, Mies van der Rohe, the last director of the Bauhaus, would move the school to Berlin, but within a year, Hitler would close the school in April 1933, and its faculty and students would scatter around the world, many coming to the United States later in the decade. There were other similar schools in Germany that were much larger in scope and number of students than the Bauhaus, such as the Industrial Art School in Berlin, with distinguished graduates such as Bruno Paul, Adolph Meyer, Kem Weber, and Mies van der Rohe himself, but such schools were not shut down by Hitler and thus were essentially forgotten in the United States. In July 1932, Belle Kogan (1902–2000) opened her own design office, Belle Kogan Associates, at 185 Madison Avenue in New York City with a retainer from the Quaker Silver Company. The company had hired her in 1929 and sponsored her training in glass and silver manufacturing. Kogan was born in Ilyashevka, Russia, and had emigrated to the United States in 1906 with her parents, who settled in Allentown, Pennsylvania. She was the first of eight children, and she showed interest in art at an early age. Her Bethlehem High School art teacher encouraged her to study mechanical drawing, where she was the only girl in the class. After graduation, she chose to attend the Pratt Institute in Brooklyn while teaching her high school class in mechanical drawing. But in 1920, she was forced to leave during her first semester at Pratt to manage her father's jewelry store. During this period, she attended the Art Students League of New York as time permitted. After Quaker hired her, the company sent her to train as a silver designer at the Rhode Island School of Design, and in 1930 at the German Art School in Pforzheim, Germany. The first female industrial designer was now in business for herself. Other industrial designers were already making names for themselves. Both Raymond Loewy and Henry Dreyfuss appeared on major magazine covers. Dreyfuss was interviewed by Popular Mechanics magazine and pictured in the October issue working on his Toperator washing machine. In it, 28-year-old Dreyfuss described the process followed by industrial designers as more than simply adding beauty, but requiring research and incorporating practical considerations of cost, function, and convenience. Gilbert Rohde had designed furniture for the Heywood Wakefield Company, including a side chair in 1931, 250,000 of which would be sold over the next eight years. He had established a relationship with D.J. DePree, president of the Herman Miller Furniture Company, in 1929. Rohde convinced DePree that the company should manufacture modern furniture. In 1932, DePree appointed him as director of design to shift the company's orientation from traditional to modern furniture, and instituted the policy of putting Rohde's name on each product. Rohde's designs of 1933 and 1934, including the clock he designed in 1933 for the Herman Miller Clock Company, would be equally strikingly modern, spare, and simple as many later designs of the 1950s. A Design for Machine exhibit held in Philadelphia in 1932 featured furniture by Gilbert Rohde and designs by Russel Wright in a breakfast room that included lamps, tables, flatware, and accessories in aluminum, a material that had been used previously only in cookware. Since 1930, Wright and his wife had been designing and making decorative aluminum pieces in attractive, modern styles, some of which were cooking utensils attractive enough to be carried directly from the stove to the serving table. Wright had also designed an early table radio model along with his aluminum housewares. More manufacturers were seeking designers. Jo Sinel in 1932 designed an Accousticon hearing aid for Dictaphone, a Radio-Victrola for RCA, Toastmaster tray accessories, and a Folmer-Graflex camera. Joseph B. Platt (1895–1968) in 1932 was engaged by the Parker Pen Company to design its Vacumatic fountain pen and its famous arrow-shaped ![](images/1ce8b67d84542c856feb02ecfc172bd1d0b1ad80f9bedfa785e3a8adc0c1cd4f.jpg) Parker Pen clip by Platt, 1932 (U.S. Patent Office, D88,821). pocket clip, which became a Parker trademark on all its pens to this day. Platt in 1919 had married June Evans (1898–1977), the daughter of Rudolph Evans, the sculptor who would create the 1,000-pound bronze statue of Thomas Jefferson that would be added to the Jefferson Memorial in Washington in 1947. In the 1920s, Platt and his new wife had relocated to the French commune of Senlis, an expatriate American community north of Paris, where Platt worked as a correspondent, editor, and illustrator for Vanity Fair, Vogue, and House & Garden magazines. He illustrated magazine covers from about 1923 to 1925, and probably attended the Paris Art Deco Exposition. He and his wife hoped to become community leaders at Senlis, but this did not work out. Returning to the United States in about 1929, the Platts lived in New York City and in Little Compton, Rhode Island, a beach resort. Their apartment in New York was in the same building as that of Raymond Loewy and his new wife, Jean Thompson, and the couples became good friends. Loewy was probably responsible for getting Platt the job at Parker Pen. Platt's wife, June, was also, according to Loewy, “an incomparable and creative cook," and starting in 1936 with June Platt's Party Cookbook, she used Loewy's name as an endorsement on the book jacket. She would write a number of popular cookbooks well into the 1950s, including the June Platt Cook Book in 1958. During the Depression, Platt and his wife collaborated in painting murals, probably WPA projects, one of which wrapped the dining room of the Country Club of Detroit in Grosse Pointe Farms, Michigan. In 1932, another major manufacturer, the B.F. Goodrich Company in Akron, Ohio, engaged H. Creston Doner (1902–1991), who had just opened his design office in Akron, Ohio, to design the company's rubber products. Doner was born in Nevada, Ohio, and was raised on farms in Nebraska and Pennsylvania. After high school he installed radios and worked on oil wells in Oklahoma. Doner was flamboyant in both appearance and behavior. In 1929 he had designed a stylish English sport jacket in Oklahoma and had it tailored to his own specifications and patented. He always dressed fashionably and regularly wore an ascot with a jacket. In 1954, he would be selected as one of the 25 men to become a charter member of the American Society of Beau Brummels (honoring those welldressed men in the spirit of George Bryan Brummell, the leader of English fashion at the beginning of the 19th century). Alfons Bach (1904–1999) opened his own design firm in 1932, Alfons Bach Associates, in New York. Clients included Heywood-Wakefield, its subsidiary, Lloyd Manufacturing Company (Bach was a colleague of Kem Weber, a fellow German, who also worked for Lloyd); International Silver; Philco; and Trans World Airlines (TWA). Bach was born in Magdeburg, Germany, grew up in Munich; and studied in Berlin. He worked in set design and studied architecture at the Reimann School and the University of Berlin. He knew people at the Bauhaus but immigrated to the United States in 1926 and became a freelance designer. Adolph Daniel “Al" Lambach, Sr. (1912–2008) arrived in New York in 1932 to work for the Progressive Table Company designing dinette furniture. Guild, born in Syracuse, New York, attended Syracuse University where he studied painting and 17th- and 18th-century design and crafting methods. He also did some freelance design work. After graduation, he and his wife relocated to New York City. Using his training in art and mechanical drawing, Guild worked in art industries, mainly home furnishings and the decorative arts. In 1924, Guild started his own business, Lurelle Guild Associates, which, with a staff of eight, made models and worked on product development. He also started Dale Decorators, a doorto-door decorating service, which employed 100 women who marketed wallpapers, curtains, and carpeting. He scoured the country for antiques and sold them. He placed samples of his products in retail shops and surveyed potential customers. When he invented a new product, he patented it and then assigned the patent to the manufacturer, charging a fee plus royalties. His early interest in crafts led him to study Chauncey Jerome (1793–1868), a New Haven clockmaker who was one of the fathers of mass production. Fascinated with history, Guild moved an entire early American village from New Hampshire to Darien, Connecticut, where he lived. Guild designed the permanent museum at Alcoa's New York offices, which displayed individual pieces as works of art, utilizing modern display tables of extruded aluminum and Z-shaped window treatments that eliminated glare and allowed daylight to enter the room at various angles. For Kensington, Guild also designed the Kensington showroom in Rockefeller Center in New York City. Indirect lighting was used from below display shelves. The walls were painted so that they graduated from dark blue-green to white, and the floors were colormatched to them. What attracted these early designers to enter a strange world of machinery and mass production? Of course, as mentioned earlier, industrial design had suddenly become a financially lucrative field, as manufacturers scrambled to find talent to meet intense public demand. But aside from that, there is something very satisfying to a creative person to know that his or her design would not just be printed in a magazine, exhibited in an art gallery, win an award, or sold in small quantities as a decorative craft, but would be mass produced, sold by the thousands, and distributed throughout the country to a large audience of the general public who would use and interact with his or her design on a daily basis. It was a feeling, not only of public recognition and acceptance, but one of a personal contribution to society, making the world a better place to live. This was a powerful incentive. New York, of course, was the American center of culture, business, and style, and many of the early leading designers were working there. John D. Rockefeller, Jr. (1974–1960), had developed Rockefeller Center, a 12-acre complex in Midtown Manhattan, beginning in 1929. In 1930, Donald Deskey had designed home interiors for Rockefeller, a client connection that helped him win the competition for the interior design of Radio City Music Hall in Rockefeller Center, an extravagant Art Deco landmark that opened in December 1932 with a lavish stage show featuring Ray Bolger and Martha Graham. Deskey would work on the interior until 1934. The Music Hall would be restored to its original condition in 999. But the biggest attractions in New York were its famous skyscrapers. New York skyscrapers were then capturing the imagination of the world. The Chrysler Building in New York City had been completed in May 1930, becoming the tallest building in the world (71 stories, 1,046 feet). Started in 1928, it was a classic Art Deco design by architect William Van Allen (1883– 1954) for Walter P. Chrysler (1875–1940), president of the Chrysler Corporation he had formed in 1924 to manufacture Chrysler automobiles. Chrysler had been Time magazine's Man of the Year in 1928. Eleven months after the Chrysler building was completed, in April 1931, the 102-story Empire State Building was completed to capture the title of the world's tallest, at 1,454 feet. These skyscrapers had been the inspiration of Paul Frankl for his stylish Skyscraper furniture in 1925, and he had written several books to publicize his business and to promote the new modern styles. In 1928 he had published New Dimensions: The Decorative Arts of Today in Words and Pictures, and in 1930, Form and Re-Form: Practical Handbook of Modern Interiors. Now, in 1932, he published Machine Made Leisure, capitalizing on the popular term “machine," which to the public was shorthand for the wonder of industrialization and mass production, just as “digital” or “computer” would be shorthand for the “Information Age” of the 1980s and 1990s. But the skyscraper styles popularized by Frankl would soon be replaced in popularity by “streamlining," a style based on the public fascination with high speed, as celebrated in the new age of aircraft and racing cars, such as the Supermarine S.6B British seaplane which set the world speed record of 340 mph at the Schneider trophy air race of 1931 (and which would be developed into the famous British World War II fighter plane, the Spitfire), and Malcolm Campbell's Blue Bird racing car of 1931 (245.733 mph). The sleek, streamlined forms of shining metal embodied in these vehicles symbolized speed and were used by Norman Bel Geddes to envision the future in his 1932 book, Horizons. Bel Geddes based his designs on the concept of speed, as he said in his book, “speed is the cry of our era, and greater speed one of the goals of tomorrow."1 Bel Geddes did not invent the term “streamlining," although he popularized it in America. The term was originally used by English biologist/mathematician D’Arcy Wentworth Thompson (1860–1948) in his influential 1917 book, On Growth and Form. He used the term to describe organic structures that offer the least resistance when they are in motion, and how a fluid medium tends to impress its “stream lines" on a deformable body, like snowdrifts and sand dunes. He concluded that the same principle occurred in the evolution of the bodies of fish and birds. German Count Ferdinand von Zeppelin incorporated the same concept into the design of zeppelins, rigid airships named after him that he developed at the start of the 20th century, and which were popularized in the early 1930s by transatlantic flights from Germany to the United States. Early aircraft and racing cars emulated similar streamlined forms. A pioneer was Austro-Hungarian Paul Jaray (1889–1974), an employee of the Zeppelin Company in Germany, who observed that automobiles roll along the ground, but they also move through the air. He developed scale-model cars to test in the Zeppelin wind tunnel to determine the best shape to lower air resistance. The ideal shape was a teardrop with rounded windscreen and enclosed bottom. In 1922, he had a full-scale prototype, the Ley T6, built by the German carmaker Rudolph Ley AG in Armstadt. Jaray was soon consulting with a number of European carmakers, and his principles were soon incorporated into racing cars by Maybach, Audi, and Mercedes-Benz, as well as aircraft designs. In 1922, Romanian engineer Aurel Persu (1890–1977) also applied aerodynamics to auto design, based on the ideal shape of a falling drop of water. In 1923, he built a prototype in Berlin, which reached high speeds, and patented it in the United States in 1927. Detroit had picked up on the streamlining concept in 1931, when Amos Northup, then the chief body designer of the Murray Corporation, first used streamlining principles, although modestly, in the design of the Reo Royale Eight, the first mass-produced car to display the streamlined esthetic by smoothing the traditional sharp angles of car bodies into soft curves A rounded, peaked shell surrounded a vertical V-shaped radiator, and the headlights were mounted in elongated pods. Front fenders swept rearward almost to the middle of the car. A curved panel provided a tapered rear end and covered the fuel tank. Murray claimed the curved fenders increased strength and that the shape reduced wind resistance, although this was negligible under 60 mph. The design was an immediate public sensation, proving that streamlining could sell cars and encouraging Murray and Northup to design a much bolder streamlined body for the 1932 Graham Blue Streak, which had the irst chromed, tilted back, V-shaped grille concealing the functional radiator, the first fender skirts to hide the “spatter zones” behind the wheels, and a trailing “beaver tail” that concealed the gas tank. This design made all previous automotive design obsolete and began a progression of automotive streamlining that continued well into the 1950s and beyond. By 1934, there would be no U.S. car without front, and sometimes rear, fender skirts. A few early American industrial designers, besides Harley Earl of General Motors, were attracted to automotive design, which was, as it is today, a romantic and popular icon oflifestyle, speed, and freedom, for example, Walter Dorwin Teague and his son, Walter Dorwin Teague, Jr. (1910–2004, later called W. Dorwin Teague). Then a 19-year-old engineering student at MIT, the son had teamed up in 1929 with his father to design the Marmon 16 for the Marmon Motor Company, headed by Howard C. Marmon. Both father and son later claimed individual credit for the design. The car was introduced in 1932 and was a classic and impressive design, but it could not be described as streamlined. Four hundred would be produced before the Depression put the company out of business. Raymond Loewy patented his first car design in 1930, and in 1932 designed a “cycle fender" Hupmobile for the Hupp Motor Car Company, which was in a fairly traditional style. He would design a more radical, more streamlined Hupmobile in 1934 called the Aerodynamic Hupmobile. Because executives were skeptical of his proposal, he decided the only way to get the job was to build a car by hand, using an existing chassis, to show them he was right. It took him several months, but when the executives saw the innovations in three dimensions, they agreed, and he got the job. The exercise cost Loewy over \$20,000, in 1932! (In 2013 dollars: \$341,000.) But he said it was worth it. He was to have been paid for the design as a percentage of all Aerodynamics sold over 10,000, but Hupp sold fewer than that, so he never received a dime. Later in his career, Loewy would become the most renowned non-Detroit auto designer. Fascinated with transportation in general, Loewy even ventured into ship design by designing the Princess Anne ferryboat in a streamlined style for the Pennsylvania Railroad Company in 1933. It was the year he took an office on the 54th floor of a skyscraper at 500 Fifth Avenue. Bel Geddes, as we know, had entered the industrial design field in 1928 designing cars and was well aware of these streamlining developments in automotive design. He apparently concluded that transportation with streamlining was the wave of the future. His book, Horizons, not only astounded the design world with futuristic designs of transportation that bordered on science fiction, but jolted the transportation industry into action. The book was filled with advanced concept designs that were never built, but seemed realistic, detailed, and easily achievable in the foreseeable future, because they included not only dramatic artist renderings of the external appearance, but detailed cross sections, mechanical specifications, and plans for interiors. ![](images/b261535db3ac79c781ebde4422a0ae61f4d4787a41317f80dce9d579f9639b13.jpg) 1934 Aerodynamic Hupmobile by Loewy. His Motor Car Number 5 sedan with a teardrop shape had a huge rear fin to stabilize it at high speed, which anticipated General Motors' car fins of the 1950s by several decades. His double-decker Motor Coach Number 2 had a similar teardrop shape and fin but seated 53 people and included a lavatory and buffet service. Bel Geddes's Locomotive Number 1 resembled and anticipated 1960s aluminum “bullet trains" with coaches and locomotive having nearcylindrical cross sections, as well as a rear lounge observation car, also bullet shaped, with generous glass windows. Horizon concepts included designs for airports, such as a floating rotary airport at the tip of Manhattan, and an incredible Air Liner Number 4, a swept-wing, giant, amphibian, tailless, flying wing. The wingspan was 528 feet, four times that of the 1958 Boeing 707, and on top of the wing were mounted 20 engines, producing a maximum speed of 150 mph. The wing itself, which varied in thickness from 10 feet at the tip to 22 feet at the center, provided adequate space for 451 passengers (with sleeping facilities) and a crew of 155. Bel Geddes's Ocean Liner in Horizons was a 1,088-foot-long behemoth of 70,000 tons that looked more like a torpedo than a ship, with streamlined funnels and 10 decks. The stern deck was enclosed in glass, which opened in good weather, and the stern itself tapered to a point, completing the teardrop appearance. The liner included an aircraft in a hanger, a tennis court, a sand beach, swimming pool, gymnasium, cinema, and three restaurants, with 2,000 first-class passengers and a crew of 900. Also in the book were concepts and plans for private homes, theaters, auditoriums, openair cabarets, and a 256-foot-diameter rotating aerial restaurant which seated 1,500 at the top of a 32-foot-diameter, 27-story-high central tower. Bel Geddes intended the design for the 1933–1934 exposition being planned in Chicago. Another was a design of a glass-walled factory and machine shop for the Toledo Scale Company, as well as product concepts for counter and island scales, but the latter were never produced. The book also included a few more mundane commercial designs by Bel Geddes, including his Franklin Simon windows (1929), his car design for Graham-Paige (1928), his counter and island scales for Toledo Scale (1929), his steel beds for Simmons (1928–1929), his famed 1931 ![](images/6c7900bc3855825cc60841281bf6435986734391899e1838e2b69be4b34894fa.jpg) C. Stowe Myers, ca. 1950s (courtesy Industrial Designers Society of America). gas stove for the Standard Gas Equipment Corporation (SGE), and his radio for RCA (1931). But it was his incredible visions of the future of transportation in Horizons, and his groundbreaking, streamlining style that would earn him the title “the P.T. Barnum of Industrial Design" by some. Ironically, the noncredited renderings of many of the spectacular designs in Bel Geddes's book were actually drawn by a young recent graduate in architecture from the University of Pennsylvania, C. Stowe Myers (1906- 1995). Myers was born in Altoona, Pennsylvania, and received his degree in architecture in 1930. He would go on to work for Walter Dorwin Teague in 1934. But the future visions of Horizons were by Bel Geddes alone. Later in life, Raymond Loewy admitted that he and most other pioneers had little idea of what the future would bring. They were just making a living by improving products with good appearance: Looking back to the early days ... I can say that we proceeded with considerable naïveté. We didn't realize what the repercussions might be decades later ... that a new profession was beginning around us!² But Bel Geddes did. He recognized full well that he and others were transforming the visual landscape of America. On page 1 of his book, he states, quite presciently, In the perspective of fifty years hence [1982], the historian will detect in the decade of 1930–1940 a period of tremendous significance. He will see it as a period of criticism, unrest, and dissatisfaction to the point of disillusion—when new aims were being sought and new beginnings were astir. Doubtless he will ponder that in the midst of a world-wide melancholy owing to an economic depression, a new age dawned with invigorating conceptions and the horizon lifted.³ He was absolutely right. These were the men and women who pioneered in the new field of industrial design before it became well known by either the business community or by the public. Compared to many other artists practicing in the art industries, they were boldly entering the business and industrial world of mass-produced mechanical products, where artists normally feared to tread. They embraced the change that modern design offered to a nation mired in traditional, historical, and antiquated design habits. They committed their careers to advancing that change and sought to make America a leader in international design, rather than a follower. ![](images/c81a283f4c1a2e36040929c394591880bc93f2589db9c1a8d8f3be8e3a1c7250.jpg) Ocean liner by Bel Geddes (Patent No. D91,579, U.S. Patent Office). By January 1933, when President Franklin D. Roosevelt initiated his administration, America became focused on several burning issues. The Depression was affecting not only the many individuals who had lost their jobs (25 percent) and/or investments (millions). Farm commodities were only at 40 percent of what they had been in 1929 as a severe drought ravaged the agricultural heartland. Seven billion dollars in deposits had been frozen in failed banks after the March Emergency Banking Act (called the “Bank Holiday"), which closed all banks for four days and allowed only Federal Reserve-approved banks to operate. The day after the Bank Holiday ended, the stock market rose from 53.84 to 62.10 points, a gain of 15.34 percent, the largest single-day percentage gain to this day. When you are at the bottom, there's nowhere to go but up. Nevertheless, American exports had declined from \$5.2 billion in 1929 to \$1.7 billion in 1933. Manufacturing was struggling to survive in an environment where consumers had little means or incentive to buy products. The lowest point of industrial production in the Great Depression had been reached, which was at 50 percent of what it had been in 1929 before the crash, as were retail sales. Auto production was at a quarter of its pre-Depression levels. The Depression had never been darker, despite the sudden rise in the stock market. A boost in national morale was needed, and several events in 1933 contributed to raising spirits. First, Roosevelt repealed Prohibition, the policy that had made public use of alcohol against the law since 1920, but which had done little to curb the illegal activities of rumrunning gangs or speakeasies that provided liquor to continued public demand. The second was the opening of the Century of Progress International Exposition, a World's Fair, held in Chicago to celebrate the city's centennial. The fair had been in the planning stages since January 1928 and had been slowed by the start of the Depression in October 1929. Now, on May 27, 1933, the fair opened on 427 acres along the Lake Michigan shoreline between 12th and 39th Streets in Near South Side, Chicago. The Chicago Century of Progress World's Fair opening in May 1933 was a showcase of American technology, transportation, architecture, and modern design. The Fair's motto, “Science Finds, Industry Applies, Man Conforms," emphasized the belief that “man” needed to, as we say today, “get with the program." The program was the future. Buildings for the exposition were multicolored, creating a “Rainbow City" as opposed to the "White City” of the 1893 Chicago Fair (the World's Columbian Exposition) that introduced electric lighting. The 1933 fair opened when light from the star Arcturus was detected by photoelectric cells in a series of astronomical observatories and was transformed into electrical energy that was transmitted to Chicago. The star was chosen because its light had started its journey to earth 40 years before, at the same time as the previous Chicago Fair of 1893. Unlike the 1893 fair, which featured neoclassical architecture and themes, this one generally followed “modern" architecture, and its symbol was the Sky Ride, a transporter suspension bridge perpendicular to the shore of the lake on which one could ride from one side of the fair to the other in “rocket cars," or ride elevators up the two 628-foot towers for a spectacular view. The midway was filled with nightclubs and the re-creation of important scenes from Chicago history. Future stars such as Judy Garland and the Andrews Sisters performed at the nightclubs, along with fan dancer Sally Rand. The first Major League Baseball All-Star Game was held at Comiskey Park in conjunction with the fair, and yes, Babe Ruth hit the first All-Star home run. A 1933 Homes of Tomorrow Exhibition featured 12 modern homes demonstrating modern conveniences and practical new building materials. A 12-sided, glass-and-steel-framed House of Tomorrow designed by architect Fred Keck had an all-electric kitchen, plastic wall partitions, air conditioning, fixed window panes, and, incredibly, an airplane hangar. The Briggs Company exhibited a Kitchen of Tomorrow designed by John Tjaarda, which included a cylindrical electric range center island lit by a lamp that rose from the center like a periscope. Entry to the exposition was through “Passimeters," modern turnstiles designed for the Perey Company by John Vassos, who, since his entry into the design field in 1924, had written, illustrated, and published a number of fiction books: Contempo in 1929, Ultima in 1930, and Phobia in 1931. Now, in 1933, he established an internal industrial design department for the Radio Corporation of America (RCA) and would remain as RCA design consultant until 1964, while continuing to practice independently. In 1934, he would design RCA's first plastic tabletop radio cabinet, the International, replacing what Vassos called the “wooden tombstone” type radios of the 1920s that preceded it, and he also would design the first living room console radios without furniture type “legs." What Vassos called “wooden tombstones" were Philco's tabletop radios shaped in a Gothic arch, not unlike a tombstone. In 1932, there were a lot of them, including Models 36B, 71B, and 91 B, all designed by Clyde F. Schuler (1892–1969), including a later Model 66MB. Shuler was born in Pottstown, Pennsylvania, and educated at the Philadelphia Museum and School of Industrial Art. He earned a certificate in industrial drawing in 191l, a second for interior decoration in 1913, and in 1921, a diploma in interior decoration. He received his architectural registration for the state of Pennsylvania in January 1930 and opened an office with a partner, Schuler & Gurney, practicing domestic architecture and product work, such as for Philco. But by 1933 he had joined the faculty at the Philadelphia College of Art, where he would remain until 1952. Major manufacturers mounted numerous pavilions or exhibits at the Chicago Exposition, many of which provided opportunities for designers. Alfonso Iannelli, the Chicago sculptor/ industrial designer mentioned earlier, designed pavilions, rides, entrance gates, and ornaments for the fair, including the Havoline Thermometer building. Norman Bel Geddes had been appointed “designer of illumination" for all buildings and grounds in 1929, and he also designed the Aquarium restaurant, the Seats-Surrounded by Stage Theater, the Water Pageant Theater, the Island Dance restaurant, the Arial restaurant, the Divine Comedy Theater, the Open Air Cabaret, and the Temple of Music.⁴ Walter Dorwin Teague designed the Ford Rotunda, which featured the “Briggs Dream Car,” a rear-engine prototype aimed at Ford's need for a “small Lincoln," designed by John Tjaarda (1897– 1962), who had been hired as chief of body design in 1932 by the Briggs Manufacturing Company, Detroit's largest body supplier, which supplied body designs to Ford. He had worked for Harley Earl's Art and Colour Section at General Motors in 1927, for Duesenberg from 1928 to 1930, and had returned to Harley Earl from 1930 to 1932. The Briggs car, although much sleeker, more streamlined, and more refined, bore a striking resemblance to the 1932 prototype of the Type 32 Kleinauto, designed by Dr. Ferdinand Porsche (1875-1951) in Germany, which would later in the decade become the famed Volkswagen (People's Car) subsidized by Hitler. Other auto manufacturers displayed concept cars at the Chicago Exposition, such as Cadillac's V-16 limousine and Pierce-Arrow's Silver Arrow, for which it used the byline, “Suddenly it's 1940!" But Packard won the best of show. Another unusual car design appeared and was demonstrated at the exposition by R. Buckminster Fuller (1895–1983). It was a prototype of his teardrop-shaped Dymaxion Transport Unit, which looked a bit like an aircraft without wings. It had a rear engine that drove two front wheels, with a third wheel in the rear that was controlled by the driver with the steering wheel to change directions. Three such prototypes were built, but a fatal accident with one in 1935 would doom the project. By 1928 he was living in Greenwich Village in New York, decorating Romany Marie's popular café in exchange for meals, and displaying concept designs for his mass-produced house and bathroom, which he called Dymaxion House and Dymaxion Bathroom, respectively. He coined the term Dymaxion from the words “dynamic," “maximum," and “tension." At the café, he met Japanese-American artist and landscape architect Isamu Noguchi (1904–1988), and they teamed up to model the Dymaxion car based on the recent work of Aurel Persu, the Romanian aeronautical engineer mentioned earlier. Major electrical equipment manufacturers were also represented at the exposition. Donald Dohner, then the Westinghouse director of art, painted huge murals for the company's pavilion. One of the exhibit designers for the General Electric (GE) pavilion was Raymond E. Patten (1897–1948), who had joined the Edison GE Company in Chicago in 1928, and who supervised appearance design. In 1931 he had designed an Art Deco, tilt-type, Hotpoint Gazelle toaster for the company, as well as waffle irons, clocks, and kitchen ranges. In 1933, GE established an internal design group at its headquarters in Bridgeport, Connecticut, and named Patten as its head. Patten was born in Malden, Massachusetts; attended Harvard; and studied engineering at MIT. His first position was with the Hume Body Corporation in Boston. Other exhibit designers on the design staff at the fair would become prominent in the industrial design field. One was David Chapman (1909–1978), a 1932 architectural graduate of the Armour Institute of Technology (which later became the Illinois Institute of'Technology) in Chicago, who, at the same time he was working on fair exhibits, joined Montgomery Ward as an architect. Ann Swainson, head of Ward's Bureau of Design, appointed him head of product design. Chapman was born in Gilman, Illinois. Another exhibit designer at the fair was Jean Otis Reinecke (1909–1987). Reinecke was born in Bourbon County, Kansas, and started his own sign-painting company at age 17 in 1926. He attended Kansas State Teachers College, then moved to St. Louis and attended Washington University's Art School. He began his career as a display artist at General Display Studios in St. Louis, and a designer of automated convention and World's Fair displays, where he soon became a partner and art director. He opened his own Chicago general display office in 1930 specifically to work on the fair. In 1934, he would establish a Chicago industrial design firm, Barnes & Reinecke, with partner James Barnes, with Reinecke providing the design talent and Barnes in charge of sales. In the closing days of the Chicago Fair, on October 26, 1933, one of the final highlights was the arrival of the 776-foot German airship, the Graf Zeppelin, which circled Lake Michigan for two hours near the exposition site before Commander Hugo Eckener landed at the nearby Curtiss-Wright Airport in Glenview at 1:00 p.m. It remained on the ground for 25 minutes and then took off ahead of an approaching weather front, bound for Akron, Ohio. For some folks, it was not a welcome sight, since, with a huge swastika on its side, it was a grim reminder of the rise of Adolph Hitler to power in Germany earlier that year. In fact, German theoretical physicist Albert Einstein had just arrived in the United States nine days before, on October 17, a refugee from Nazi Germany. There would soon be many more. Nevertheless, the fair, which closed on November 12, was so successful that it was decided to reopen it again to run from May to October the following year, 1934. Over the previous 170 days, it had already attracted well over 22 million paid admissions. Before the end of 1933, Robert Davol Budlong (1902– 1955) had joined the growing ranks of industrial designers and started his career with the Hammond Clock Company. He would begin a relationship with Zenith in 1934 as a radio designer and consultant that would last until his death. Budlong was born in Denver, Colorado, studied art at the Cummings School of Art in Des Moines, Iowa, and graduated from Grinnell College, Iowa, in 1921. He then studied art at the Chicago Academy of Fine Art and opened an advertising office in Chicago. Around the same time, there were several start-up firms in New York: Cushing & Nevell in 1933, and Jim Nash Associates in 1934, both of which would survive into the 1960s. ![](images/83e073dfe1b01863705096efffa7bf54d816abac65726bc7cfd35a4ebfc0b7fe.jpg) Robert D. He also designed a medallion to commemorate the silver anniversary of the General Motors Corporation that year. Aside from his normal business routine, the rumblings of potential war in Europe inspired him to renew his interest in realistic games, and he developed what he called the War Game, a scale model simulation of a 20,000-square-kilometer battlefield on a 24 × 4 foot table, representing the coastline of two imaginary countries, complete with 9,000 cities and towns, mountains, valleys, tunnels, and bridges. It was much larger and more complex than his Nutshell Jockey Club of the 1920s. Thousands of pins of various colors, sizes, and shapes stood in for 40-plus types of military units, including armies and navies of 28 belligerents. A complicated rulebook determined chesslike moves by a general staff regarding speed of movement and degree of destruction by attacks. The game ran once a week during winter months, with an evening of play commensurate with three and a half days of war. Players, consisting of brokers, architects, shipping magnates, and rare book dealers, took the game seriously, often sacrificing vacations, business, or family commitments. By 1935, Bel Geddes would put the game into storage because of time constraints, but his skills of miniaturization and three-dimensional landscaping would be valuable in a number of his future professional assignments. But the real war against the Great Depression was still raging. Industry was still stalled, and most companies were still unaware that one of the reasons was the obsolete design of their products. By and large, they were also unaware of what to do about it. This was about to change dramatically.
