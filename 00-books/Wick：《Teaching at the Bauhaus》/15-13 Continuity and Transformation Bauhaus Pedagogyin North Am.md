## Introduction

In contrast to other countries offering exile, North America had leading figures of the Bauhaus living and working there who in their various professions as teachers, designers, painters, architects, exhibition organizers, typographers, and photographers represented all of the main fields of activity at the Bauhaus, including Bauhaus pedagogy. Despite obvious changes in the teaching activities of the Bauhaus members in North America compared to their time spent at the Bauhaus in Germany, academic research to this day presents many of the Bauhaus members' educational concepts in the light of continuity and further development. In this context, institutions in the United States where former Bauhaus members taught are often described as offshoots of the Bauhaus.

The numerous publications on the subject of the Bauhaus to date have contributed little to a reconstruction of the intellectual and sociohistorical processes to which the emigrants were exposed during their North American exile. From a German or Western European point of view, the famous Bauhaus masters tend rather to be characterized as atypical emigrants. Exile in North America is not regarded as having been imposed upon them, because they emigrated as a consequence of their natural vocations and not owing to political persecution.² Within the secondary literature, a compromise version of events states that it was not the Bauhaus members but the Bauhaus idea that went into exile.³ This view of the reception of the Bauhaus should, however, be encountered with some skepticism, as it plays down and levels out the challenges that Bauhaus members, in particular the less well known among them, faced in North American culture. It was not Bauhaus pedagogy as a general concept, but rather individual Bauhaus members who enjoyed an enthusiastic reception as teachers at various North American institutions. Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, Moholy-Nagy, and Albers were the Bauhaus "giants," while Breuer, Feininger, Hilberseimer, AnniAlbers, Schawinsky, andPeterhans were at least awarded the status of minor accompanying figures. With respect to their educational activities within North American culture, however, Aliner, Beckmann, Bredendieck, Drewes, and Wieghardt, to name only a few, are hardly ever mentioned.

Until now, attention has mainly focused on the influence brought to bear by the Bauhaus members on American art and culture. All the Bauhaus members, whether they were truly emigrants or not, were obliged to adapt the experiences they had had in Europe to the circumstances in the United States if they wanted to work with any degree of success. It is my thesis that the better they achieved this process of adaptation, the more successful they became. Of the fifty or so former Bauhaus teachers and students who stayed in North America for a lengthy period,4 however, this applied only to the more eminent Bauhaus figures, because the decision to assume or reject the legacy of the Bauhaus was only in the hands of those members who performed leadership functions in the United States from the outset. For many Bauhaus students—for instance, a woman such as Marguerite Friedlaender-Wildenhain, who with her Pond Farm Pottery in California, felt no less committed to the legacy of the Bauhaus than Walter Gropius or Josef Albers—the continuation of Bauhaus principles was no guarantee of recognition or success. For an architect, it was simple to declare even the tiniest ceramic object as significant—this relationship did not work the other way around. In the Bauhaus succession, hierarchical and gender-specific ranking, which had existed at the historical Bauhaus itself, was reinforced

In my estimation, the notion that gaining an overview of the dissemination of Bauhaus ideas, principles, and methods in North America is virtually impossible is highly exaggerated. It is only recently that the emphasis in research on the subject of emigration has begun to shift.⁵ As mutually interdependent factors, the works created in exile, the significance of the creative efforts within the personal development of the artists concerned, and the reception awarded to these by the host country's culture are increasingly taking center stage within the debate.

While in the Germany of the Weimar Republic, the fundamental Bauhaus concept of crossing the boundaries between artistic genres and exploring the various disciplines was tied to everyday teaching practice at the school; in North America, there was no attempt to adopt the Bauhaus model as a whole. With the exception of László Moholy-Nagy, none of the Bauhaus members who emigrated to America contemplated repeating the zero hour by founding the Bauhaus anew. Robbed of the Bauhaus as a school, or more precisely the school, the former Bauhaus members were forced to concentrate on their individual abilities and their artistic independence. This meant that they had to elaborate upon what had distinguished them individually from their joint work at the Bauhaus. Regardless of the criticism they had leveled at the elitist academic world, the former Bauhaus teachers now had to ask themselves how they could become integrated into their new domains within the existing educational system.

The Director of the Museum of Modern Art, Alfred H. Barr, Jr., even succeeded in portraying the closed system of the historical Bauhaus as a hindrance to productivity. His words contain the promise that with the end of the Bauhaus as an institution, the inevitable process of obsolescence of its material and intellectual productions could be slowed down or even completely brought to a halt. In 1938 he formulated the question as follows:

[W]hat have we in America today to learn from the Bauhaus? Times change and ideas of what constitutes modern art or architecture or education shift with bewildering rapidity. Many Bauhaus designs which were once five years ahead of their time seem now, ten years afterward, to have tken on the character of periodpieces. And some of its ideas are no longer so useful as they once were. But this inevitable process of obsolescence was even more active in the Bauhaus while it still existed as an institution for, as Gropius has often insisted, the idea ofa Bauhaus style or a Bauhaus dogma as something fixed and permanent was at all times merely the inaccurate conclusion of superficial observers.⁶

In this statement, to be found in the catalog of the exhibition that was so important for the North American Bauhaussuccession, Bauhaus, 1919– 1928 at the New York Museum of Modern Art in 1938, lies the legitimation for relinquishing an educational practice that might have been comparable or even identical to that which had existed in Germany.

At the historicalBauhaus, the difficult alliance of independent personalities under a single roof had been successfullyaccomplished toa great extent, while life in North America and the free market caused the Bauhaus members to become competitors. This realization, which is supported by an analysis of the emigrated Bauhaus members' correspondence, ultimately also has consequences for a present-day assessment of the staff structure at the historical Bauhaus itself. Accordingly, in his 1996 book Teachíng Art, Carl Goldstein writes of the educational program:

And the Bauhaus curriculum invariably has been reconstructed in terms of the contributions of remarkable individuals. Such have been the arrangements of important Bauhaus exhibitions, with sections devoted to Klee's course, Kandinsky's course, Albers's course, and so on. Because of the stature of these figures within the history of modern art it has seemed vital, too, to grasp their individual ideas about teaching in order better to understand their own art. And these ideas, in the form of lecture notes andclassroomassignments, havebeenpublished in great detail.7

From the previously mentioned major Bauhaus exhibition of 1938 in New York on, the historical achievements of the Bauhaus gained as much prominence in North America as did the famous artistic personalities associated with the school.

Concentrating on the Bauhaus's past modified the view of the present. Accordingly, the specialist literature has long shown no interest in how students might put what they had learned into their professional artistic practice following their training, or whether they were even willing to uphold the Bauhaus heritage in any form whatsoever.

With regard to the reception of Bauhaus pedagogy in North America, one characteristic, which Walter Gropiusascribed to himself and which was also attributed to him by others, is particularly crucial. It concerns equating his person with the original Bauhaus, the consequence of which was a far-reaching identification of his teaching methodwith that of the preliminary course at the early Bauhaus. In America, Helmut von Erffa, Hannes Beckmann, Hin Bredendieck, and other former students at the Bauhaus had on several occasions reported about the teaching methods and had particularly emphasized the combination of subjective experience and objective observation.

In 1943 Helmut von Erffa had written about the connection between the "feeling of phenomena" and“the structure of the world of realities" in the work of Gertrud Grunow, and had shown how Gropius and Itten broke with tradition.8 He documented the teaching methods at the Bauhaus even more vividly in an article entitled "Bauhaus: First Phase" of 1957. Here he described the training as a "programme of learning-by-doing" and explained Itten's pedagogical approach as follows: "Intuition! The Unconscious! These were the master keys of Itten's teaching... . The teaching methods that Itten used in those early days, and which were partly his invention, are now widely used in the United States—though with local modifications. He made us feel and experience whatever we drew."9Erffa also made clear that these principles of the preliminary course continued under Albers andKandinsky, even after Gropius had steered the character of the Bauhaus in another direction owing to the programmatic demands for the unity of art and technology. Intuitive design and the subjective receptivity to experiences on the part of the students appeared as a prerequisite, not only for the creation of “art," but also for the elaboration of new industrial forms. Combining art, science, and technology was an achievement of the Bauhaus and consequently an achievement made by Gropius and his teaching method, which henceforth became a main journalistic focus.1º Comparable comments were also made by Hannes Beckmann. At a lecture in Boston in 1976 he stated:

Yet the Bauhaus remains topical: we continue to discuss it and to write books about it. I have asked myself why. I suppose that what intrigues us—besides the fact that so much of what we call "modern design" originated there—is the incredible spirit of confidence in a better and brighter future, where art, science and technology will really work together for a more livable environment. And the idea that it is possible, through new teaching methods, to activate the student's latent creative abilities, to combine eyesight with insight. It was Gropius's final aim to promote what he called“the improvement of life."11

One year later, Beckmann wrote: "the architect Walter Gropius founded the Bauhaus .. . a totally new kind of school, where students would learn to make the arts and crafts, science and technology, work together to improve the quality of life."12

The reception of Bauhaus pedagogy in North America concentrated mainly on the teaching method, which was subjected to diverse adaptations as many North American educational institutions made efforts to put it into practice. This text from Cooper Union Art School, where Hannes Beckmann taught, offers an exemplary selection of such formulations, which describe the method: “interaction and interrelationship of faculty and students," "experimental exploration of the arts," "one of the many methods by which the processes of learning become in reality an exciting adventure and exploration," "School, which believes wholly in 'foundation training' rather than 'specialization'" or “emphasis on the 'ability to think' and 'the power to act' and believes that professional competence cannot be secured without these special attributes."13 The transposition of Bauhaus pedagogy across the Atlantic ultimately led to discussions concerning methodology during the sixties, which concerned questions relating equally to both design and training methods. After 1960 simultaneous affirmative and negative voices commenting on Gropius's method, his style of training, and his teachings became distinctly audible.

As Margaretta Jean Darnall14 made clear in 1975, Gropius's teaching paved the way for at least two trends in the history of North American architecture and design, both of which took very literally what Gropius himself had emphasized as the most important goal of his teachings: "It is not so much a ready-madedogma that I want to teach, but an attitude toward the problems of our generation which is unbiased, original and elastic. . . . What I do want is to make young people realize how inexhaustible the means of creation are if they make use of the innumerable modern products of our age, and to encourage these young people in finding their own solutions."15

The one trend can be regarded as a reaction to Gropius's teaching and should be judged in connection with the establishment of personal styles. The work of some Harvard graduates, such as for instance leoh Ming Pei, Edward Larrabee Barnes, and John Johansen, all students of Gropius, display a very self-aware mode of stylistic expression, an individual architectural language, which is brought to bear on the prevailing formal synthesis of an overall building program. In 1937, shortly after taking up his activities at Harvard and again in a speech held on the occasion of his seventieth birthday in 1953 in Chicago, Gropius stressed that: “It would be an absolute horror for me if my appointment would result in the multiplication of a fixed idea of 'Gropius architecture.'"16 In order to prevent anything of this kind, Gropius introduced his method of approach: “I want him [the young architect] independently to create true genuine forms out of the technical, economic, and social conditions in which he finds himself, instead of imposing a learned formula onto surroundings which may call for an entirely different solution."17

The other trend, established principally in the fieldofpractical training, adoptedprecisely Gropius's teachingapproach based on a design process that begins"from scratch" ("starting from scratch" hadalready been a common figure of speechat the historical Bauhaus). This was intuitive design, quided by feeling, which—in contrast to John Dewey's definition of intuition—canbecome the product of contemporary social, economic, andtechnological forces without the experience or knowledge of historical or traditional forms. In North America this theory was represented by the movement of the so-called Design Methodologists, whose supporters published their views concerning a "tabula rasa" form of design in the Design Methods Group Journal.18 The Design Methodologists followed Gropius in his conviction that “the initial task of a design teacher should be to free the student from his intellectual frustration by encouraginghim to trust his own subconscious reactions more than his intellectual ones, and to try to restore the unprejudiced receptivity of his childhood."19 The significant thing here was that, above all, "sensation comes from us, not from the object which we see."20 Both trends in the history of North American architecture and design, for which Gropius's teaching had paved the way, were associated with questions of the evolution of modern aesthetic forms in particular, and with the manner of dealing with history in general.

During the sixties, subjective receptivity to experiences and the objective viewing of facts stood just as much in opposition to each other as intuition and method or academicism and antiacademicism. It is hardly a coincidence therefore that in 1967 an exhibition at the Carpenter Center for the Visual Arts at Harvard University in Cambridge was entitled “Bauhaus: A Teaching Idea." In the catalog of the same name, the center's director, Eduard F. Sekler, wrote:

The Bauhaus was founded in 1919 by Walter Gropius as a teaching institution that would initiate a new direction in the teaching of the visual arts and of design in a world dramatically changing after a war that had marked the end of an historic epoch. . . . Throughout the comparatively short period of its existence, the Bauhaus was characterized by change and by willingness to experiment. What never changed was the emphasis on an education toward personal creative development and towardaunifiedvision of man at home in and in harmony with a technologically oriented and controlled world.21

Sekler's words should be read in the context of a nascent criticism of functionalism. It was, in fact, increasingly being defined as a naive and immature aesthetic by virtue of its being limited to design concerns and its overlooking the consequences arising from the use or the expression of an object. As Sorkin has pointed out, this interpretation of functionalism was endorsed by a national consensus in North America—the result of a successful implementation of a needs economy during World War II. After 1945 the functionalist forms of design became increasingly dubious: "Emptied, the objects of war became mere insignia. For consumer culture, the result was an avalanche of metonymy, of things that looked like things. In the art world, functionalist efficiency devolved into minimalism, a last-gasp run on the proprietorship of meaning, an attempt to keep the faith with the idea of pure, unassailable expressiveness, by a deliberate evisceration of all scope for nuance, the optimism of pessimism."22

It was not only at Harvard University and at the Illinois Institute of Technology, where the architecture departments were linked to the Bauhaus succession by virtue of their respective directors, Walter Gropius and Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, but also at those schools providing artistic training in the narrower sense that the typical characteristics of the activities of former Bauhaus members and the aftereffects of their approaches to teaching can be observed in the United States. The directions taken by Albers and Moholy-Nagy were, of course, of  different nature than in the field of architecture, which makes it clear that on the North American continent the holistic Bauhaus conception—including pedagogy—was abandoned in favor of the autonomy of the disciplines of architecture, art, and design.

The fact that the generalist conception of the Bauhaus preliminary course was used as a model by a wide variety of educational institutions in the United States was answered by an increased specialization during the main course of study. Both the division into separate departments and an increased focus on the intellect or on science within of the training program as could later be observed at the New Bauhaus (i.e., at the institute in Chicago under Moholy-Nagy) demonstrate that objectifiable facts and theories were insisted upon. On the other hand, visual artists were apparently more able to identify with Albers's model of dynamic imagination.

## Walter Gropius at Harvard

If one scrutinizes the reports and items in the North American specialist press from 1940 on, what is striking is that precisely in contexts where mention of Gropius's experiences with the organization and coordination of a training program would seem appropriate, the main topic is his person as a “well-known architect and professor of architecture at Harvard University."23 The item quoted here, from the Magazine of Art of 1940, reports on a conference at the University of Michigan on the subject of how the training of architects and industrial and advertising designers could be coordinated. Gropius, who was invited as a speaker, was not presented in his double function as an architect and former director of a school at which the main focus was on questions concerning training and the coordination of teaching programs but only as an architect.

The manner in which Gropius's employment at Harvard came about has been described by Margret Kentgens-Craig. The author relates that at the start of his American career, Gropius hit upon the detailed curriculum already formulated by the director of the architecture department, Joseph Hudnut, who needed the first Bauhaus director for the realization of his own plans and ambitions. "Hudnut's intentions in restructuring the Harvard program," she writes, "were pragmatic. This meant that Gropius's philosophical position could not be the basis of his teaching, as it had been in Dessau and Weimar. One of the most distinguished characteristics of his earlier teaching was thus lost. For this reason alone, it would be wrong to speak of a 'transplantation' of the Bauhaus to America, as has often been done."24

Although there surely can be no mention of a transplantation, Hudnut was not free of an ideological foundation in modern architecture. The remarkably similar theoretical formulations, aims, or declarations of principle made by Gropius and Hudnut—the latter referred to John Dewey's philosophy of pragmatism—particularly in their articles appearing between 1937 and1945, make it difficult, however, to clearly discern Hudnut's intentions. On the contrary, the two architects' predominantly identical themes and choice of wording suggest the idea of a comparable point of departure at the foundation of the Bauhaus in Weimar and the School of Design in Cambridge. In his biography of Gropius, Reginald R. Isaacs actually draws a parallel between the spirit of renewal prevalent among German youth following 1918 and the young generation, shaped by the effects of the world economic crisis, living in the United

States in the midthirties, who under the influence of the New Deal demanded reforms.25

When Hudnut was appointed dean in1935, he merged the schools ofarchitecture, regional planning, andlandscape design, which had previously been separate; they now became departments of the School of Design. This organizational change in the system of training was Hudnut's attempt to finda new position from which to overcome the stagnant academicism that existed in architecture and the training of architects, a position he characterized as follows: “The habit of a renewedcomradeshipwith those professions which were once parts of the profession of architecture and which still share its essential purposes and ways of working; I mean, of course, city planning, civilengineering, landscape and industrial design."26 According to Hudnut's views, modern architecture couldnot be realizedwithout joining these departments. The ideas he elaborated on this subject are immediately reminiscent of Gropius's statements concerning architecture as a coordinating function. In The New Architecture and the Bauhaus, for which Hudnut wrote the introduction in 1936 to the American edition, Gropius wrote: "My idea of the architect as a oordinator—whose business it is to unify the various formal, technical, social and economic problems that arise in connection with buildings—inevitably led me on step by step from study of the function of the house to that of the street; from the street to the town; and finally to the still vaster implications of regional and national planning."27 In 1945 he was to reiterate these words with reference to the training at Harvard.28

The architect Richard Neutra's memoirs suggest that Hudnut, although he only decided to take on Gropius as a teacher at Harvard in 1936, was familiar with the Bauhaus system of teaching and used it as a source to restructure the curriculum at Harvard. Neutra claims to have introduced Hudnut to Gropius's ideas and the Bauhaus principles. "I talked a great deal of Gropius," he wrote, "and after each lecture, a little man came forward to talk to me. He asked me many questions about Gropius and the Bauhaus and its system of teaching, and showed a great interest to learn about it. On the third lecture, I learned that his name was Hudnut. . .. [H]e was the man who wrote that letter of invitation to Gropius. And Gropius came to Harvard through this mediation which I half unknowingly had instigated. It wasn't a 100% success, I must say. I know that he sooner or later had problems with Mr. Hudnut."29 Indeed, Hudnut's ideas about modern architecture and up-to-date training practices for architects diverged considerably from those of the Bauhaus's founder, despite the misleading dependence on Gropius's choice of words and themes.

Hudnut implemented the architecture program—and thus aspects of Gropius's conception of teaching—at the Harvard Graduate School of Design in the spirit of American transcendentalism. In this, he made reference to Ralph Waldo Emerson, the leading figure of American transcendentalism, when in 1941 he wrote, "The philosophy which governs the teaching of architecture in the Graduate School of Design at Harvard is deeply rooted in the culture of New England and is admirably expressed by Ralph Waldo Emerson in his essay on Self-Reliance."30 Citing Emerson and pointing to the deep roots of Emerson's concept of training within the culture of New England were the linguistic means Hudnut employed to prevent any question of a bias toward the

European tradition at Harvard from arising. He thus made use of a current prevailing image of a rising North American culture. In evolutionary terms, the idea of common aims and universal values, to be either served or drawn from, marked the change to the optimistic vision of an egalitarian culture in which differences gradually become indistinguishable. This represents a tendency generally discernible in various areas of social, economic, and political life at the time of World War Il, and which finally, after 1945, found expression in the North American hegemonic claims as a world power, sustained by its reestablished prosperity. In accordance with the forward-looking optimism of transcendentalism, Hudnut emphasized the aspect of development. To legitimize his philosophy of education, he elected to use biologicalistic terminology, that is, terms relating to growth, development, and phenomena; “process [is] biological in character."31

The biological interaction model is indebted to John Dewey's theories. Influenced by William James's belief in "life in action,"32 for Dewey the nature of experience was determined by the fundamental evolutionary conditions of life. Dewey's central idea is that of an organic order: “Life itself consists of phases in which the organism falls out of step with the march of surrounding things and then recovers unison with it—either through effort or by some happy chance. And, in a growing life, the recovery is never mere return to a prior state, for it is enriched by the state of disparity and resistance through which it has successfully passed. If the gap between organism and environment is too wide, the creature dies. If its activity is not enhanced by the temporary alienation, it merely subsists. Life grows when a temporary falling out is a transition to a more extensive balance of the energies of the organism with those of the conditions under which it lives. These biological commonplaces are something more than that; they reach to the roots of the esthetic in experience."33 Consequently, overcoming resistance and conflicts becomes a significant life task. What is taking place here is “adaptation by expansion rather than contraction. At the core, a condition of balance and harmony is achieved by means of rhythm. This kind of balance represents form, it consists of a duration and an order."34

According to Hudnut, the variety of and changes in architecture possess a quality that is associated with the universe and can be understood as an organicism of nature.35 Accordingly, the Bauhaus and the Bauhaus members were integrated into this system. Hudnut's outlook for the future in 1938 was:

The modern architect will find his opportunities. . . . Still more congenial will be those vast new themes which the evolution of the social order promises the future architect: the regulation of housing by the State and the controlled development, for the general welfare, of cities and of regions. Resolute to use his science not for the comfort of individuals merely but for the communal good, the modern architect, as a necessary part of those processes by which space is organized and materials assembled for his useful end, will illumine in new orderings of space and objective form that responsible and compassionate impulse which guides these heroic enterprises.36

The heroic enterprise that this architectural vision represents must be placed in the hands of those people who are able to translate the qualities and expression of the time into new forms of construction.37

In Germany, Gropius had already proven this ability in performing this task. His hope that better architecture and art might bring forth a better human being, that characteristic properties of modern architecture must influence every individual, whether consciously or unconsciously,3⁸ was reversed by Hudnut insofar as he assumed that only the development of an egalitarian social order supported by government construction programs would make the spread of modern architecture possible. This reversal can be explained by taking a look at Dewey's criticism of static or only pseudo-dynamic models: “All the educational theories criticized by him [Dewey] have in common that they consider the educational goal as having been accomplished in principle, and only see dynamism in the fact that either the material determines the form, the world determines the human mind, or the form, the spirit, the material."39 According to Dewey, however, the relationship between the mind and the world is not only unilaterally dynamic in the sense of a fixed goal, but also reciprocally, without one of the two factors "human being" and “world" being overemphasized at the expense of the other. This notion of Dewey's does not coincide with the educational model devised by Gropius, as the latter had definite objectives aimed at a new type of human being as well as a "true national art, pulsating through every branch of human activity" and at a“new type of worker who is able to combine the qualities of an artist, a technician and a businessman."40

Nevertheless, for Hudnut, it was a fairly simple matter to propagate Gropius's and Dewey's universal educational goal as one and the same. Their demand to place technical knowledge at the service of the social good and the superficial similarity between Gropius's emphasis on the integration of art and everyday activities and. Dewey's famous maxim of learning by doing, as.well as the associated suspension of the difference between service and independent occupations, techne and science, was a reason why the Bauhaus founder's ideas never seemed completely alien in North America. “Making and doing will be the cardinal activities of the courses,"41 Hudnut had written in 1942. In the attempt to establish a common ground between Gropius and Dewey, howevèr, two important differences were ignored. The first has already been alluded to above and concerns two quantities, the human mind and the social environment, which form the basis of any educational model. Unlike Dewey, Gropius assumed educational goals that lay outside the educational process and to which he considered education as being subordinate. Dewey, however, sought goals that might be located within the process itself.42

The second difference concerns their respective definitions of experience. Dewey's conception of experience was twofold: namely, as a simultaneous experience in nature and of nature.43 Dewey maintained that the "past is carried into the present so as to expand and deepen the content of the latter" and that “great original artists take a tradition into themselves."44 According to Dewey's understanding, experience thus also appears within a historical context. On this subject Martin Suhr has remarked: "The meaning of an experience is enhanced by the fact that its relationships or contents are brought into consciousness. Thus, geography and history are only different aspects of a single whole: the relationship between human being and nature"; and the more people “know about human beings within history,.about cultures, the greater . . . [their] ability will be to see relationships."45 Consequently, subject and object are maintained, undivided, within experience. In this double sense, experience contains life and history at the same time.4⁶Gropius's conception of experience, however, was oriented toward the derivation of theoretical, objective knowledge, which went beyond historical contexts. His ideas were more committed to a kind of structural view reminiscent of linguistics and the structural anthropology of Claude Lévi-Strauss.

The consequence of Gropius's renunciation of historical contemplation—historical knowledge lost some of its significance for him because he believed it would diminish his student's creativity—was a search for ahistorical basic structures. Rather than starting out from genetic or evolutionary historical premises, Gropius insisted on the analysis of synchronous, temporally unified conditions in order to furnish evidence of the universal structures active within social relations.

In 1946, in an article entitled "Living Architecture or 'International Style'?" he wrote:

So far, we have been exceedingly successful in working out ways of acquainting our young with the achievements of the past by a reproductive and imitative approach, but we are not so successful in stimulating them to come forth with their own creative ideas. We have made them study art history so hard that they have found no time to express their own feelings. By the time they have grown up, they have developed such fixed ideas about what art and architecture are that they have ceased to think of them as something to be freely approached and created by themselves. They have lost the joyful, playful urge of their early youth to shape things into new forms, and they have become instead selfconscious onlookers who all too often withdraw into some remote period of art history because their educational guidance was built up on the mentality of aesthetes rather than creators.47

To concentrate on the achievements of the past, according to Gropius, means to hamper the intellectual productivity of youth, which is realistic, organic, and“from the earth."48 He considered modern architecture to be ġoverned by other forces: “Filled with the delight of a new beauty, of a new aspect toward life, modern architects have rediscovered that man must be the focus, that animation by simple means derived from the natural environment is needed—not aesthetic stunts. A new set of standards in architecture has been born which draws its life from the climate, from the soil, and from the habits of the people."49

The human being and his habits, as well as the surrounding climatic and geographical conditions, are the focus from which the standards of modern architecture rnust be developed and evaluated. For Gropius, the purpose of psychological observations of social interactions was to investigate these aspects of life and of the human community.50 It is only then that “passion and inspiration as well as scientific thinking" form the basis for the development of a living and contemporary architecture.51

The move away from art historical thinking patterns to those of the social sciences was one of the innovations introduced by Hudnut at Harvard, and one that went weli with the trust Gropius placed in scientific objectivity. However, it is important to point out that Hudnut neither considered art history superfluous to the curriculum nor was interested in an overevaluation of the social sciences, even if this is often maintained with reference to Hudnut's appointments of the sociologist Talcott Parsons to the Regional Planning Department and the political economist Charles Merriam to the visiting committee.5² Hudnut was in agreement with Gropius in rejecting architectonic imitations: "Not one of the neo-classic buildings of our time, for example, possesses, in any convincing degree, that command over our spirit without which there can be no genuine architecture."53 He went on to argue that the imitation of classical architecture represents an attempt to suffocate the creative impulse with erudition and to replace it with inherited and codified knowledge. At the same time, Hudnut followed Dewey in the notion that the reflexive analysis of primary experiences could not be oriented toward the laws and structures of the present alone, but also had to guarantee historical continuity.54 Dewey described intuition as a “meeting of the old and the new,"55 and Hudnut seldom refrained from using historical examples—the architecture of the Egyptians, Greeks, and Romans—in order to make clear what mattered to him in modern architecture and in itspreliminary trainingpractices: “unity of process."56Hudnut thus understood the social sciences as “empirical and direct knowledge of the thing to be done. .. . The student is to visit an actual site, learn for himself the form and movement which life is to assume there, discover and formalize its economic basis, and from that knowledge work out a pattern of ideas to be later translated into a pattern of structures.' "57

In 1960, when James Marston Fitch's publication on Gropius appeared in the Masters of World Architecture series, it contained numerous passages that served the purpose of refuting the reproach that Gropius had created a style. These comments, which can be found in many other publications and served to vindicate Gropius,5⁸ enable us to draw conclusions about the manner in which the theories of the Bauhaus founder were overwhelmingly received in America. In his monograph Fitch wrote: "Two charges, both of them esthetic, are brought against the Bauhaus by its critics today. The first is that it established a style; the second is that that style is 'bad.' These charges, though related, are not at all the same thing. Gropius has always denied that he had any ambition to establish a style; he has always maintained that, on contrary, it was a basic methodology of design which he sought."59 A few lines later, Fitch quotes Gropius, “I realize that I am a figure covered with many labels. .. 'Bauhaus Style,' 'International Style,' 'Functional Style'—these have almost succeeded in hiding the human core behind it all."60 And finally, he also states that: “The other charge against the Bauhaus runs thus: it created a style which was 'bad' because it was 'cold,' 'inhuman,' 'narrowly functional,''mechanistic,' etc. These are the judgments ofa generation which took no part in the great esthetic battles of the twenties and which is consequently in no position to understand the terms of that revolutionary struggle."61 The secondof the three main chapters in Fitch's monograph—“Gropius, the Educator," “Gropius, the Architect and Designer"

and "Gropius, the Social Critic"—principally discusses the works in Germany, and the author is mainly concerned with reaffirming Gropius's role as primus inter pares. During the sixties Gropius himself aired his views on several occasions concerning the misunderstandings and misinterpretations with which he saw himself confronted. The tenor he struck can be summarized in an exemplary manner by a statement from 1961: "The present generation is inclined to think of it as a rigid stylistic dogma of yesterday whose usefulness has come to an end, because its ideological and technical premises are now outdated. This view confuses a method of approach with the practical results obtained by it at a particular period of its application."62

With respect to Gropius's reception as a teacher in North America, it must be taken into consideration—even though this cannot be discussed in detail here—that at least three further significant aspects not immediately related to his teaching activities at Harvard, bore some influence. The first aspect concerns the already mentioned fact that it was not Gropius's abilities as the director of a school, but his competencies as a teacher of architecture that were praised. For this reason, he also experienced difficulties as a practicing architect. Although he emphasized again and again that "there is nothing more deadening to the vitality of a design school than when its teachers are compelled, year in and year out, to devote the whole of their time to teaching, "63 it is the perception of many experts that Gropius remained a teacher and theoretician in the United States:

I remember an experience I had myself years ago when, on the occasion of my seventieth birthday, Time magazine commented on my career. After coming to this country, they said, I had been "content to teach only," as if this were, in itself, a minor occupation as compared to that of a practicing architect. Apart from the fact that the paper was misinformed—I had never given up my practice—it brought home to me again therealizationthat theprofessionof the teacher is looked upon in this country as a kind of refuge for those visionaries who cannot hold their own in the world of action and reality.64

Despite his successes as an architect, Gropius was above all regarded as an important representative of an educational theory. In 1969, in his review of Gropius's book Apollo in the Democracy, Peter Collins wrote unequivocally, "It is to his personal prestige as an inspiring teacher that this book is a memorial, and the poetic qualities of its contents can only justly be appreciated by reading these speeches with constant awareness of the festivities that call them forth."65

A second aspect that contributed to the effect Gropius's teachings had was the presence of Ludwig Mies van der Rohe in the United States. The activities of both architects in the United States offered the possibility of comparing their respective curricula in Cambridge and Chicago, as well as their buildings. For Mies van der Rohe, who was virtually given a free hand in the conception of a new curriculum at what was to become the Illinois Institute of Technology (IIT), the Bauhaus teaching methods associated with the name Gropius were virtually irrelevant. Instead, Walter Peterhans's "Visual Training" became an important part of the training at IIT and Peterhans completely dissociated himself from the preliminary course method in his teaching practice. The different orientations of Gropius and Mies van der Rohe in the field of architectural training had already been recognized among specialist circles around 1950. Ten years later, these differences even led to a quarrel carried out in Journal of the American Institute of Architects between Howard Dearstyne, who also taught at IIT in Chicago, and Gropius.66 The latter endeavored to counter Dearstyne's criticism of his teaching method. Seeking to underscore the uniqueness of his teaching method, Gropius compared his pedagogical concept with those of van de Velde in Weimar and of Frank Lloyd Wright in the United States. He claimed that, contrary to both of these, he had established an objective teaching method. In comparison, the works of the students of van de Velde and Wright, displayed no independence from the designs of their mentors to Gropius; they were consequently mere imitators with no creativity of their own. From this he concluded that a great artist need not automatically also be a great educator. Gropius himself, on the other hand, saw himself both as an artist and an educator.67

Finally, a third aspect that came to bear on the reception of Bauhaus pedagogy is worthy of mention here, namely Gropius's own efforts to create a Bauhaus archive. His concern, on the one hand, was to maintain the purity of the original Bauhaus, and on the other, to ensure that his achievements were not diminished by the consequences of history. His efforts to safeguard this, however, prevented any modification of the Bauhaus philosophy, which in order to achieve greater effectiveness and sustainment, would have needed to orient itself more to the conditions and requirements of American culture. The incipient criticism of Gropius's teachings in North America, however, expressed less displeasure about the fact that Gropius's implementation of his teachings and architectural theory were based on experiences made in Europe. It was much rather that the voices grew, which interpreted Gropius's approach during the twenties in itself as already being flawed by an insurmountable contradiction: "[R]easonable men might well conclude that architectural theory today is as far away from solutions to society's problems as it was in the time of Walter Gropius, and . . . that between Gropius's call for a spiritual awakening on one hand and his faith in scientific objectivity on the other, there existed an antagonism as deep and ultimately as disabling as in the theory of his great precursors."68

## Josef Albers at Black Mountain College and Yale University

At Black Mountain College Josef Albers initially entertained a more experimentally oriented notion of art from the outset, which was later granted academic recognition owing to his subsequent position at Yale University. Albers had never left any doubt that the purpose of his educational goal was not the practice of secondary knowledge. "Knowledge and its application is not our aim," he wrote in 1963, "instead, flexible imagination, discovery, invention—taste."69

A comparison between the Bauhaus and Black Mountain College seems thoroughly justifiable in view of the experimental work performed at both schools. Whereas the Bauhaus was“experimental work on the testing ground,"70 Black Mountain was, as Mary Emma Harrisdescribed it, a“unique combination of liberalarts school, summer camp, farm school, pioneering village, refugee centre and religious retreat. ... There was little money and the facilities were simple. ... [W]hen the college moved to its own property in 1941, the faculty and students constructed buildings and remodelled existing structures. Crucial to the founders' vision was that the college remain open to new ideas in a truly experimental spirit: the community changed from year to year, driven by the ideas and personalities of its faculty and students."71 The college was just as unable to establish courses specifically for advanced students inpainting, graphics, sculpture, and other visual arts disciplines, as it was to offer well-equipped workshops. The financial means did not permit setting up a that which wouldhave guaranteed full professional study and academic degrees: "Black Mountain College does not train youth to become professional architects."72

Although under Gropius's directorship, no architecture class had existed either in Weimar or initially in Dessau architecture played a central conceptual role at the Bauhaus and also a real one from 1927 on. Consequently, the fact that no architects were trained at Black Mountain College represented a certain restriction in comparison with the Bauhaus. Nevertheless, the descriptions of work and life at the school reveal a series of tendencies which refer back to the Bauhaus. In American art history, the college finally became stylized as “a symbol of academic freedom and the experimental spirit,"73 underscoring the rò- mantic appeal of an antiacademic atmosphere which contributed to the institute's myth.74 John Cage's observation the "you were just as much in [Black Mountain] when you left as when you were there"75 demonstrates the lasting influence the college had on anyone who came under its spell. Cage, who was impressed, according to some commentators, by the historical Bauhaus and felt drawn to some of Klee's and Kandinsky's works, regardedBlack Mountainas an exceptional community in contrast to the other schools in the United States.76

Philip Johnson acted as a mediator in establishing contact with thecollege,77 and Josef Albers arrivedin North Carolina with his wife, Anni, in November 1933, only two months following the founding of the institution. At that time, the college was built at the foot of the Great Craggy Mountainsnear Asheville during the heightof the Depression. The small group of teachers and educators there were committed to moving away from the rigid educational rules and the customary "grade-and-credit" system that predominated at American colleges. As some of the Bauhaus memberscongregatedat Black Mountain during the summer courses at which they taught, it was soon judged to be a center for conveying the theories and ideas of the historical Bauhaus. It must, however, be taken into account that this teaching was limited mainly to general or preliminary studies.

Walter Gropius taught architecture there on three occasions during the summer sessions (in 1944, 1945, and 1946); Dearstyne was also employed there as guest instructor for architecture from 1941 to 1942. Lyonel Feininger taught painting at the summer session in 1945; Marguerite Friedlaender-Wildenhain taught ceramics there in October 1952. It was Josef and Anni Albers who worked there for the longest period of time; from 1933 to 1949, Anni Albers was instructor for weaving and textiles, thus initially maintaining a role established at the Bauhaus, according to which women were in charge of the weaving class. In 1963, Anni Albers began to devote herself to the art of printing. Her first actual print work was made around 1964, when she visited the Tamarind Lithography Workshop in Los Angeles thanks to a research scholarship. In 1970, with the move to Orange, Connecticut, she finally completed the professional move from weaving to printing.

Josef Albers taught art from 1933 to 1949, while Xanti Schawinsky was responsible for art and the stage from 1936 to 1938. Albers continued with the teaching activities he had begun at the Bauhaus, while at the same time also enriching them with new impetus in keeping with the changed conditions, the different continent and the different times. Thus, it was important for him to work against what he considered to be “the American idealization of, and subservience to, the cultures of Europe"78 by drawing the students' attention to their own culture: “to contemporary architecture, to bridges, to photography, to commercial typography and advertising, to abstract art andtoearlyAmerican crafts."79At Black Mountain College, the fundamental principles of the Bauhaus were adopted, but there was no specialization in particular disciplines, and questions of a style did not necessarily arise. It was much rather the experiences students made while experimenting which were intended to enable them to find their own artistic expression. This was a decisive factor for the success of both the college and for Aibers. Immediately following his arrival in New York, Albers was questioned on his training methods. He answered that what he had in mind was more a “pedagogy of learning" than a "pedagogy of teaching."80 As in his lessons at the Bauhaus, he didnot preach any doctrine but rather emphasizedcreative concerns. His educational concept assured individuality, and this coincided with the needs of the time.

Unlike the western European avant-garde of the twenties, American artists did not wish to create an internationally comprehensible art for all mankind. There was no longer a belief in the design of a universally validmodern language of form. The loss of any idealistic, sociocritical claims meant that, for a while, an art exclusively focused on itself was able to develop without catering to any target group. In 1935 Grace Alexandra Young wrote, “The courses in art which Professor Josef Albers teaches at Black Mountain College are, surprisingly, not for artists but for people. Art becomes a discipline and focusing of personal powers, and a release for original creation."81 In addition, Louis Adamic stated in 1936, "To attend his [Albers's] classes is very definitely to experience art as a process which is also life. ‘To us,' he [Albers] said, 'the act of drawing is more important than the graphic product; a color correctly seen and understood is more important than a mediocre stilllife.'"82 This is what distinguished the training at Black Mountain Coliege from that at the New Bauhaus in Chicago. There, during the first year of educational activity, the Association of Arts and Industries acted as financial backer and consequently as patron. As a result, from the foundation of the school on the terms of reference and objectives were partly determined from outside, which was never the case at Black Mountain College. The few funds available to the college were therefore, revealingly termed “free money"83 by Adamic.

When Albers left Black Mountain in 1949, his name was closely associated with the college. A comment made by the Feiningers clearly points to the fact that this was also how the former Bauhaus members saw the matter: “The news about your leaving Black Mountain College has quite startled us. We've been thinking that your name and that college was a thing inseparable. It certainly was in our minds a‘Begriff'—so even Begriffe can undergo changes."84

Between 1936 and 1940, invited by Gropius, Albers provided training similar to that of the preliminary course at the Graduate School of Design at Harvard University and gave numerous lectures there. In 1941 he taught fundamentals of design and color theory and in 1950 taught there again as a visiting professor. In addition to his activities at Harvard, he was a member of the Advisory Council of Arts at Yale University, in New Haven, from 1948 to 1950. In 1950 he worked as a visiting critic at the Yale University School of Art and was appointed to the position of director of the Department of Design at Yale University.

At Yale, Albers began to elaborate a systematic program of classes for artists and “made Yale the most important art school in America, as he had done previously at Black Mountain."85 In 1951 Albers handed over the first of altogether eight written annual reports as Chairman of the Department of Design to A. Whitney Griswold, president of Yale University, and at the same time presentedhim a four-year plan. To Griswold, he wrote:“The aim of the new four-year plan of study is to provide, first, an elementary general art training, and second, a specialized professional study in one of the three fields of Painting, Sculpture or Graphic Arts. The addition of a section in Product Design is under consideration."

With the intention of explaining the effectiveness of the new course of study, he went on to state:

The general courses of the first year are Basic Design, Elementary Drawing, and Color; and of the second year, the newly introduced courses in Lettering and Drafting—besides the introductory courses in Painting and Sculpture, respectively. The objectives of these courses are: a) To study principles underlying all the arts and which establish their relationship with each other. b) To encourage the student to explore his personal inclinations and potentialities before making a definitive choice of his profession. c) To develop a visual idiom related to requirements of a contemporary civilization.

## Albers then described the curriculum as follows:

The visual and manual training of the basic laboratory courses is to be considered as part of general education, as a balance and supplement to intellectual training, available to students from all fields of learning. ... The specialized studies of the third and fourth year aim at individual projects within the chosen professional field. Here the objectives are: a) Discrimination as to choice and craftsmanship in the application of the means. b) Ability to visualize and to interpret the meaning of a formulation chosen. . . . In summary, we seek an understanding of the proper manufacture (execution or production) of a'design or composition, as well as of itspsychologicalandphilosophical implications.86

The third report to Griswold for the academic year 1952–53 contains an interesting comment on the future professional orientation of the students. It is remarkable that, right up until the fifties, it was taken for granted by the educational system that painting be treated as an independent discipline that could not be associated with applied art or design. At the same time, it became apparent that Albers hadrecognized this and that he wanted to take action to prevent the removal of painting from the curriculum he had devised. In the report he stated: “Although the dominating trend among them is still painting, the painters are now getting ready to think more of practical work—such as graphic and industrial design—and less of teaching as the only way of making a living.' "87

Albers hadmade a name for himself as a teacher in the United States since 1933, thereby securing a livelihoodfor himself andhis wife. There was no shortage of opportunities to teach at colleges añd universities, as the many inquires regarding such activities highlight. In 1949 he was appointed guest professor at the Art Academy in Cincinnati and at the Pratt Institute in Brooklyn, New York, where he taught color theory. Between 1953 and1954 he lectured in the department of architecture at the Universidad Catolica in Santiago and at the Escuela Nacional de Ingenieros del Peru in Lima. In 1957 he earned an honorary doctorate from the University of Hartford. Teaching activities followed at the University of Minnesota, the Kansas City Art Institute, the Art Institute of Chicago, the Department of Architecture at Princeton University, and at Trinity College in Hartford, Connecticut.88 Around1958, however, his desire to dedicate himself to his own artistic work became stronger and that same year, he resigned as chairman of the Department of Design at Yale University, although he continued to regularly teach there as a visitingprofessor until1960. In

1959 Anni Albers wrote to Ise Gropius on the subject of her husband's teaching activities: "But one course will be enough from now on as far as teaching goes—only the question of subsistence still remains a question."89

In many diverse articles about the artist to this day, constant mention is made of the antiacademicism at Black Mountain College, of Albers's metaphysical theory of art, andof his classes founded upon epistemology. His conviction that western culture overemphasizes the idea of the individual artist to the detriment of communal work became a topos of Albers's reception. It has been said that he always maintained a belief “in the humanistic implications of form" and that he felt that “figure-ground equivalence implied 'a very valuable socialphilosophy,namely real democracy: every part serves and at the same time is served.'»90 Albers's belief in democracy and his orientation toward an assimilation of epistemology and social philosophy are the expression of a specifically American understanding of sociobiologicalprocesses. In1936Adamic asserted that Black Mountain College “challenges the existing chaos and the methods of fascism and communism. It goes beyond all three and has the method to get there. It has a chance of becoming deeply attractive to millions of Americans who are sick of themselves, their own corruption, the corruption about them, and the stench of dissolution now filling the world; and who, weary of individualism, wish to lose themselves in, or identify themselves with, something bigger and better than themselves."91 It was always that which can be described as the wealth of individually or cooperatively acquired experiences that Albers exploited to gain general insight. “Insight gained from an attempt, not with prejudice, but with curiosity and expectation: a kindof divinatory pragmatism . . . experience based on trial and error."92

Albers's art was received against the backdrop of a Puritan tradition, for which art's interactions with the world took place on the level of quality definitions exclusively determined at the level of the artistic means employed and their analysis. As far back as 1935 Grace Alexandra Young had stated in an article on Albers's theory, “His methods as a 'research artist’ are as precise as the experimental chemist's."93 Subsequently, in 1995, Michael Craig-Martin, who studied at Yale, affirmed that, "Perhaps even more important, we learned to make and value work that 'worked,' without needing to like it, and that this was a quality of the work separate from our own feelings. .. . We were trying to explore each material purely for its visual potential."94 The great diversity of analyses, elaborated communally including sociological, psychological, and other scientific factors, and taking into account industrial processes, formed the basis of all the courses at Black Mountain and at Yale. In 1946 Alvin Lustig reported about a graphic design course at the Black Mountain Summer Institute:

Graphic design, like architecture, is an industrial process and demands complete mastery of all the technicalconditions.... Specifically, the course began by demonstrating its relationship to the basic design classes of Albers, and attempted to show methods of extending and developingthese discoveries into the graphic field.... This was followed by an explanation of “approach" to a graphic problem and the psychological factors involved in “selling" not only [to] the public, but [to] the man who is paying for the work. . .. Considerable time was spent in discussing methods by which the designer can learn to translate and synthesize the formal discoveries of experimental paintings as well as the new visual frontiers opened up by science, the electron microscope, stroboscopic andX-ray photography. It is in the maturity and richness of this synthesizing task that the ultimate measure of the designer can be found.95

These kinds of descriptions of the courses seldom fail to mention the fact that these analyses opened up an arena within which it became possible for students to find their individual artistic avenues. This is what has made out the significance that Black Mountain College has achieved today in the history of the development of modern art: The college has been cast in the role of a catalyst in the discovery of a genuinely American style. According to Mary Emma Harris, the influence of Black Mountain College on American art made itself felt everywhere. She remarks that it was Albers who transposed the ideas of European modernism to American culture. This process of integration created "an environment for the generation and realization of experimental activities and events in all areas of the arts. As an outspoken advocate of Geometric Abstraction, Albers was instrumental in creating in America an atmosphere receptive to experimental art forms."96

Both the success of his classes andof his teachings had a determining influence on the Bauhaus's reception in the UnitedStates, as his training methods were always associated with the preliminary course at the Bauhaus. Albers's position as a teacher, which had connected him to the Bauhaus for eleven years, was already known to the specialist public. Albers had, after all, clearly outlined and made public his teaching method as a process of assimilation basedon experimentation.97

The orientation of classes toward"experiment in training" became just as much a trademark of Albers's as the“intuitive search for and discovery of form and, on the other hand, the knowledge and application of the fundamental laws of form."98 As was true of Gropius, in Albers's case, too, combining intuition with fundamental principles proved to be far-sighted.99 The reprinting of excerpts of Albers's article “Art as Experience" in the Architectural Record in 1936, which had originally appeared in Progressive Education in 1935, and which by no coincidence shared the same title as John Dewey's book published a year earlier, ensured that his ideas became publicized beyond specialist circles.100 With the publication of Search versus Re-Search,101 Albers once again gave expression to his artistic ideals in a programmatic manner. Albers described his notion of a search, which takes the place of research, as a transition from an urge for activity to an urge for creation.102 In order to characterize this transition, he initially referred to Dewey; later he quoted Kandinsky, "What counts is not the what but the how."103

During his time at Black Mountain College, Albers defined artistic training in its broadest possible sense as a comprehensive education in creativity, and in 1935, in "Art as Experience," he wrote: "I believe that it is now time to make a change of method in our art teachingthat we move from looking at art as a part of historical science to an understanding of art as a part of life.

Under the term ’art' I include all fields of artistic purposes.»104 Albers also pursued this concern at Yale University. Hannes Beckmann reported that after Albers had become chairman of the Fine Art Department there, he endeavored to rename it. "The first thing he wanted was the word 'fine' cut out, arguing that it should be taken for granted that art is fine. He didn't get away with it."105

Albers's universal approach paradoxically led to the situation that, although the aspiring artists used all the theoretical and practical means of design, they did not want to see their work identified with design in the general sense. This meant that, following their training, most famous artists who studied under Albers were not inclined to produce anything other than art in the strict sense of “fine art." This corresponded to a position, prevalent in the New York art scene that can be traced back to the way artists saw themselves, and was basedon fine art's claim to autonomy. They balked at letting their works be placed at the serviceof consumerismandover aperiodof decades, they leveledcriticismat thefact that “imagination itself has been reduced to a commodity."106 In 1957, for example, Ad Reinhardt, in his twelve-point program that became famous as the "Twelve Rules for a New Academy” and “The Twelve TechnicalRules (or How to Achieve the Twelve Things to Avoid)" stated as Rule No. 5, “No design. 'Design is everywhere.'“107 In his interpretationof this statement, Goldstein makes one thing very clear: “Reinhardt would have agreed with the Bauhaus andAlbers about one thing— namely, that art cannot be taught—but he also had no interest in design. . . or in anything that was not art, namely craft."108

In view of Albers's comparatively high degree of fame, which resulted in numerous articles in the specialist press,109 Hannes Beckmann's comments in 1976 do not seem exaggerated:

Until his retirement in 1960 Yale had been one of the liveliest art departments in the country. While at Yale, Albers wrote: “A school of design is not a place where one learns to express oneself—it is an educational institutionwhere teaching is systematic and learning takes place step by step." Of course such a teaching philosophy was not agreeable to everyone; some found it too rigid, even Teutonic, others feared it would hamper spontaneity and freedom of expression. But Albers was convinced that art— the creative process as such—cannot be taught, that “instant art courses“ are nonsense, and that every student, even the most gifted one, must learn to see and think in visual situations. Only then is he prepared to discover his own creative potential. In short, he was convinced that rehearsal should precede performance.110

Beckmann's detailed description reveals the sort of reception Albers's education theory was awarded. The criticism of the methods that Beckmann mentions was not generally aimed at the manner of training, but stood in connection with Harold Rosenberg's rejection of this form of education at universities. In his recent book Teaching Art, Goldstein drew attention to this historical academic dispute in the history of North American painting. His critical analysis refers to Rosenberg's 1973

The significance of the book, which was published for the first time by Yale University in 1963, can hardly be overstated with regard to Albers's reception as a teacher. Interaction of Color is not a color theory, which derives the particular from the general. Albers does not order and systematize the world of color, but confines himself to the observation of color effects as visual phenomena, the interrelations of which provide basic visual experiences.

## László Moholy-Nagy's Institute in Chicago

Following his teaching activities at the Bauhaus, from 1928 on, Moholy-Nagy worked, independently of any institution, as an artist and designer in Berlin. In 1934 he went to Amsterdam and from May 1935 to July 1937, he lived in London. From 1937 until his death in 1946, he worked in Chicago, where he was the founding director of the New Bauhaus. György Kepes, who had previously worked with Moholy-Nagy in Berlin and London, joined him as a staff member there. Kepes left the school in 1944 after the Chicago Institute had been forced to close for the second time. Other teachers at the New Bauhaus were the former Bauhaus member Hin Bredendieck, as well as Alexander Archipenko, Henry Holmes Smith, and David Dushkin. Charles Morris brought two scientists into the school who were members of the Unity of Science movement:114 Ralph Gerard, to teach biology, and Carl Ekart, to teach physics.

The Chicago Institute—the New Bauhaus became the School of Design in 1939 and the Institute of Design (ID) in 1944, which has been part of IIT since 1949-was the one successor institution of the Bauhaus in the United States on which Gropius was able to exert his influence. On the one hand, this can be seen by virtue of the fact that in the exhibition Bauhaus, 1919–1928 at the Museum of Modern Art in New York, he also presented works by New Bauhaus graduates, and on the other, in his function as a pedagogical adviser, which he held until 1955. Gropius's active participation at the Chicago Institute, as well as correspondence with individuals involved in setting up the school, is often mentioned and examined in the literature, based on the premise that initially, Gropius was cited in the function of “adviser" at the New Bauhaus. When the School of Design was being established. he was a member of the patrons' committee; when the Institute of Design was reorganized, he was appointed to the administrative board; and when the ID was merged with IIT he became a member of the ID's administrative board. In fact, the New Bauhaus was the much longed for child of Moholy's that Gropius only acknowledged because his name was constantly identified with it. He always affirmed that of his own accord, he would never have had the idea of founding a new Bauhaus. Reminded of his duty by the ambitious Moholy, he finally hesitantly acquiesced to the project and agreed to be on the board, more in name than in person.116

Moholy's Chicago Institute endeavored to keep up with the market's demand for professionally qualified staff and to build up a systematic course of professional training. One consequence of this was a watering-down of the integration of all artistic disciplines, a feature of the Bauhaus curricula. Gradually, in the official pamphlets and study programs advertising for the New Bauhaus, the School of Design, and later the Institute of Design, the visionary promise of a cultural renewal was dropped, replaced by a focus on vocational training. James Sloan Allen has already pointed out that in the "Presentation of the Institute of Design" of April 1944 the idea of integrating all the artistic fields of activity was only the last goal in a long series of teaching and learning obiectives. The texts of the Chicago school's catalogs reveal, according to Allen, a significant shift in teaching and learning goals compared with those at the Bauhaus. "The school catalogues, once works of art boldly illustrated and resounding with Moholy's cultural and metaphysical credo, were also recast, becoming conventional pamphlets describing the course of study, listing classes, and leaving the idea of integration a mere shadow of its former self, implicit in the interrelation of subjects."117 This restructuring, which led to a more easily comprehensible curriculum and more modest educational goals, was a concession to the criticism that had been leveled at the school. The new professional image of the Institute brought about by these measures should not lead to the false assumption that Moholy rejectèd his ideals of a comprehensive and broadly based training in favor of a fast, specialized form of professional study.118

For new students, the Chicago curriculum continued to hold the preliminary course, which was subsequently redubbed“Foundation Course" and "Intellectual Integration." The final course of study, however, was divided into four specialized departments: industrial design, advertising arts, textile design and photography. The introduction of scientific study, ranging from physics to humanities and social sciences in the Chicago curriculum, and the expansion of the art courses by the addition of classes in photography, film, music, and literature119 served to fulfill Moholy's aspirations toward the interdisciplinary training of future designers. This change alone would not necessarily have represented a shift from the traditional Bauhaus idea, with its goal of cultural regeneration, to a commercial and vocational level. The reputation of Moholy-Nagy's Chicago Institute as a leading school for "industrial and interior design"120 was initially acquiredmore thanks to the Association of Arts and Industries, which assumed the responsibility for the founding of the New Bauhaus. This association endeavored to improve the quality of the product design of goods manufactured in the Chicago region. Previously, this had been the task of the School of Industrial Art, which, however, only consisted of one specialized department with one course of study within the Art Institute.121 The Association's committee responsible for the New Bauhaus, of which Norma K. Stahle, Walter Paepcke, and George Fred Keck, among others, were members, wanted a school modeled on the Bauhaus. The Bauhaus had become an international model for training in modern industrial and graphic design. Accordingly, the call for a Bauhaus in Chicago primarily reflected a desire for a training institute for modern aesthetics and design. Little or nothing, however, was known in Chicago about the significance of architecture at the Bauhaus in Germany, as the discipline uniting all branches of art, about any metaphysical dimension or about a Bauhaus philosophy.122 Neither was any consideration given to the status of the degrees awarded by the New Bauhaus in the sense of a government-approved final qualification.

Furthermore, hardly any funds were available to the Chicago Institute, and the teachers who had been employed by the new school in the early part of 1939 were teaching there without receiving any payment. In 1940 Walter Paepcke was the only board member of the former Association of Arts and Industries who began to support the school with cash resources. This financial assistance provided by Paepcke represented a valuable contribution toward the Institute's ability to make a name for itself as a place of training for product design. Paepcke was the owner of the Container Corporation of America (CCA). This company, which he established in 1926, manufactured all types of packaging for consumer goods, from shipping containers down to cardboard boxes. Elizabeth Paepcke's knowledge about the developments in modern art and the influence she had on her husband in this respect, ensured that, particularly after 1935, when the designer Egbert Jacobsen became art director there, the company was identified with commercial modernism. Jacobsen created the corporate identity of the Container Corporation, which subsequently became the first North American company to systematically and continuously commission modern, contemporary artists for advertising campaigns. Artists such as Herbert Bayer, Fernand Léger, Man Ray, Jean Hélion, Henry Moore, Willem de Kooning, and Xanti Schawinsky designed the CCA's advertising material. In April 1945 the company made available its collection of more than one hundred works for an exhibition at the Art Institute in Chicago. The show was entitled "Modern Art in Advertising" and, together with the annual international design conference in Aspen, Colorado, held for the first time in 1951 by Paepcke and Jacobsen under the title "Design as a Function of Management," underpinned the company's image as a link between designers and industrial managers.123 Paepcke's early public support, therefore, had a noteworthy influence on Moholy-Nagy's school. The fact that “Moholy virtually banished the term art from the school, supplanting it with Mies's detested design,"124 bears witness to this fact.

By setting up vocational training in the disciplines of graphic design and photography, the Institute adapted itself to the demands of North American consumer culture. This development had already been heralded before Moholy-Nagy's death, when the New Bauhaus became the Institute of Design (ID) and, as a result, the curriculum was restructured: it was intensified by the takeover of ID by IIT in 1949 and the appointment of Jay Doblin to the position of Director at the Institute in 1954. On the one hand, this development led to a situation in which supporters of Moholy's pedagogical principles—first and foremost Sibyl Moholy-Nagy in her book Moholy-Nagy: Experiment in Totality (1950)—felt dutybound to once again draw attention to Moholy's passionate experimental spirit and cúltural visions. On the other hand, given the growing acceptance of modern design by the world of commerce and business, together with factors suchas market economy profit-seeking, advertising, and consumer taste, as well as the demands for a more vocational type of design training, it is easy to see why Gropius held so relentlessly to the original Bauhaus idea. The deveiopment associated with mass technology and consumer culture was nat only inconsistent with the utopian character of the Bauhaus and Moholy's subversive activism, but it also cast doubt on the topical relevance of the Bauhaus principles. "Having embraced the aesthetic and economic benefits of modern design," Allen concludes, "what need had consumer commerce of the modernists' metaphysics and cultural anthropology? What need had it even of the Bauhaus's elementary art education for professional designers? 'Who needs to go to a Bauhaus any more,' asked prominent Chicago architect Harry Weese some years later, 'to learn about space and volume by waving your hand around in a paper bag? Kids do that in first grade.'"125

The problems that appeared between the thirties and the fifties in attempting to define the term "design" were caused by the fact that it was no longer used only by artists to describe an impulse, based in theory, to improve all areas of life. A variety of other sectors of business, and consequently consumers, also pursued an interest in design. Museum administrators in North America, in particular at the Museum of Modern Art in New York, promotedthis development. That representatives from other fields, for instance businesspeople with no interest in the arts, shared in shaping the notion of what design should be and participated in the formulation of divergent definitions thereof, represented a conflict of interests.

The Chicago Institute found itself caught in the crossfire of these divergent interests. Moholy-Nagy himself held on to his claim in favor of universal design and, in contrast to the Bauhaus curriculum, wanted additionally to assure the completion of the Institute's curriculum with subjects such as history, sociology, literature, mathematics, and physics. At the same time, in order to do justice to the expectation of his financial backers and patrons, he had to attend to the provision of professional and practical study. Although he had no influence on the organization of “Industrial Design as a New Profession"—a conference held at the New York Museum of Modern Art in November 1946—he did make his position clear that this new profession could only be learned by

## Conclusion

All the former Bauhaus members pursued their teaching activities in their respective highly specialized fields. Here are some examples: Gropius, initially together with Marcel Breuer, taught architecture at the Graduate School of Design at Harvard University in Cambridge, and Mies and Hilberseimer at the architecture department of the Armour Institute and the Illinois Institute of Technology in Chicago, respectively. Albers taught painting, drawing, and graphic arts at the Yale University Art School in New Haven, among other places; Werner Drewes at Brooklyn College in New York City, at the Chicago Institute of Design in Chicago, and at the School of Fine Arts, Washington University in St. Louis, and elsewhere; Hannes Beckmann worked as a professor for color and design at the Cooper Union Art School in New York City, and so on. Following the period he spent working as an assistant teacher for painting at Black Mountain College, Schawinsky lectured at New York University and at City College in New York City (later CUNY). The departments of design and commercial art were shared between Moholy-Nagy in Chicago and Hin Bredendieck, who was, among other things, a professor and head of the Department of Industrial Design at the Georgia Institute of Technology in Atlanta, and so on. Anni Albers taught weaving and textile design at Black Mountain College, which Marli Ehrmann taught in Chicago.

What is striking in this respect is that those art courses teaching fundamentals or preparing for a particular discipline, that is, color theory or figure drawing, were often taken on by former Bauhaus students, particularly from the thirties to the fifties. Thése teaching activities of the second generation of Bauhaus members mainly took place at colleges, institutes such as night schools, or in the context of workshops. This can be documented in an exemplary manner in the cases of Beckmann, Bredendieck, Dearstyne, Drewes, and Lux Feininger, who initially worked at Sarah Lawrence College, in Bronxville, outside of New York City. Around 1935 Lila Koppelman held a position as professor at Bennington College in Vermont. Paul Wieghardt began his career in North America in charge of the painting department at the summer school, Cummington Playhouse in the Hills, in Cummington, Massachusetts. And after her emigration, Marguerite Friedlaender-Wildenhain was in charge of the pottery/ceramics workshop at the College of Arts and Crafts in Oakland, California. These preparatory or basic courses provide evidence for the fact that in part, the educational ideals of the Bauhaus were carried on.

The essential aspects relating to the reception of Bauhaus pedagogy in North America can be recognized by examining the examples of Gropius, Albers, and Moholy in their roles as teachers in the United States. Notably, the principle of interdisciplinary design only gained general acceptance at North American educational institutions as classes in basic or general courses of study. These courses, on the level of a general education, were completely in keeping with the Bauhaus's preliminary course idea. Nevertheless, whether they were, andare still today, called "preliminary course," "introductory design," or "basic design," they merely formed the basis for a course of study within a particular field. Furtherreaching courses, which wouldhave taken on a Bauhaus tradition combining all the artistic disciplines, would have been an almost impossible notion within the very strict and goal directed curricula at American colleges. Consequently, even Gropius, who had wholly identified the principle of his teaching method with the marriage of subjective training and objective observation, contented himself with the inclusion of his idea of the preliminary course. Later, Albers and Moholy satisfied themselves with the same; Albers owing to his professional reorientation at Yale University, Moholy due to the financial difficulties at the Chicago Institute. Their personal evaluation of the significance that should be awarded to the preliminary course varied nevertheless. Albers's fouryear plan at Yale appeared convincing by virtue of the equal relationship between “elementary general art training" and "specialized professional study." Under Moholy's direction, the curriculum in Chicago maintained the preliminary course at the beginning of study; the subsequent course of training, however, was organized into four specializeddepartments. The intentionalmodification that the idea of the preliminary course underwent can be summarized as follows: In North America, it no longer served as a preparation to an interdisciplinary form of design, but rather as the preparation for a specific discipline. With this shift, the educational goal of the former Bauhaus members doubtlessly altered, for now, it finally became fixed, that is, aimed toward a particular ultimate purpose.

## Bibliography

The literature on the Bauhaus has grown immensely in recent years, and it is scarcely possible to get a handle on it any longer. Andreas Haus (Berlin: Reimer, 1994), alone includes 4104 titles. Where more than one title by the same author or editor is listed, they are arranged chronologically by date of first publication.

In comprehensive individual analyses, the author Rainer K Wick presents the varied pedagogical approaches of the Bauhaus teachers Walter Gropius, Hannes Meyer, Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, Johannes Itten, László Moholy-Nagy, Josef Albers, Wassily Kandinsky, Paul Klee, Oskar Schlemmer, and Joost Schmidt, who effected a profound revolution in art education. The author also sheds light on the historical circumstances behind the development of the Bauhaus, identifies its important precursors, and provides a brief sketch of how Bauhaus pedagogy continued to have an effect long after the end of World War II. This sketch is supplemented by an essay by Gabriele Diana Grawe on the reception of Bauhaus ideas in North America, where many of the former Bauhaus teachers and students emigrated.

Rainer K. Wick is professor of art and cultural pedagogy at the Universität-Gesamthochschule in Wuppertal.
