I hate f\*cking racist Chicago.” ## The Modern City and Joining Universal Humankind The modern city of Chicago is also one of the most racially segregated in North America. It has a segregation dissimilarity index of 80, with 100 being absolute segregation, and this is by design.<sup>1</sup> According to urban historians, Chicago invented modern segregation. In an interview in Chicago magazine, historian Elaine Lewinnek is quoted as stating: “Chicago's real estate industry was a national leader [in the 1900s]. The first texts of real-estate appraisal were written by Chicagoans. The first realestate lobby, which is one of the most powerful lobbies in America, was formed in Chicago. These ideas that we need single-race, single-use spaces in order to have our property values go up—these are actually new ideas that can be traced back to Chicago in the 1910s.” <sup>2</sup> Chicago shows that the second theme of the modernist project in design is a lie: the promise that if you give up your national and ethnic identities, you might join universal humankind. In the eyes of this potential landlord, it was only my partner's and my advanced education that allowed her to see us as potentially human. Before that, we were a cluster of Black stereotypes for her, threatening to bring loudness, drugs, and drama into her home. The promise of giving up your national and ethnic identities to join universal humankind was open only to white Europeans. In this chapter, I will demonstrate how this was a failed project in Europe, and how it succeeded in North America and places like Australia only because of white supremacy. ## The Utopian Vision of European Unity In 2007, I traveled to Germany for a design competition, which took place in the medieval city of Ulm, founded in 1181 CE. I wrote down my reflections on the city in a blog post, “Reflections on Ulm: When Does Cultural Method Become Style?” (September 28, 2007): One of the cool things about being in Ulm, Germany, last week was to see why many of the decisions around the modern, International methods of design make sense. For example, German words are very long, so condensed typefaces make sense. The post-WWII relations between Germany and the rest of Europe were dismal, so it made sense to develop typefaces without national markers as German, Italian, French, especially in Switzerland where all “national” groups reside. So the erasure of ethnic markers was important to the development of Europe. You can see [that] the order of the farms, the towns, and the desire to distance oneself from a medieval past and the economy of space in the hyperdensity of Europe make more necessary the rationality of the grid. So to be in Ulm where the reason for this is clear and transparent makes me wonder [about] the appropriateness of this method in an American context. In an American context, does it just become “style” because the culturalhistorical meaning is not apparent nor relevant? Due to the 100th-anniversary celebration of the Bauhaus in 2019, there has been deep reflection in various publications on the legacy of the Bauhaus and the previous omissions of its sexism, racism, anti-Semitism, and collusions with Nazi Germany. Here the book Bauhaus Futures, published in 2019 and edited by Laura Forlano, Molly Wright Steenson, and Mike Ananny is instructive. I am indebted to Ramia Maze and her feminist read of the Bauhaus as well as Daniela K. Rosner and Alex Taylor's deep discussion of Anni Albers as a central Bauhaus figure. Elizabeth Chin shares my critique of the framing of the practices of those making outside of the Bauhaus as “people without design history.” V. Mitch McEwan puts DuBois and Anni Albers in dialogue in ways that expand the principles of the Bauhaus and decenter its whiteness. This myth busting is important because the Bauhaus histories have helped perpetuate the harms done to diverse designers and the communities from which they come. In his essay “Getting Over the Bauhaus,” Emory University Associate Professor of Art History Todd Cronan outlines the biological determinism “at the core of Bauhaus thinking and practice” and its unvarnished white supremacy: The bio-determinist outlook of the Bauhaus cut across all styles, periods, and individual differences and carried over into the work done in the United States. Not surprisingly, those “timeless biological elements” included race. Johannes Itten—the defining character of the first phase of the Bauhaus—was explicitly racist. In 1921 he designed his plan for the House of the White Man and he and his colleagues published essays celebrating the fact that “the white race represented the highest level of civilization.” … Is Itten's instinctive racism any different from Gropius’ and Moholy's “scientifically” inflected account of race? After all, at the same moment Itten was removed, Gropius and his colleagues were busy courting racist industrialists for jobs by appealing to their racism. Here is the collective Bauhaus letter to Henry Ford and William Randolph Hearst of 1923: “We make our appeal to yourself, who have the privilege of living in the Land whose population today is in the act of taking the reins of Leadership of the White Race into its grasp.” <sup>3</sup> The Bauhaus's “universal man” for whom they were designing was the working white man. They sought to protect his war-traumatized body and spirit through the economizing of space and the meaningfulness of satisfactory and productive work. Design practices that draw their origin stories from the modernist project thus perpetuate the biases of the supremacy of white bodies and cultural values. But the Bauhaus was just drawing upon earlier foundations of the modernist project in design. Looking to the aspirations expressed in industrial world's fairs and expositions, Prince Albert gave a speech upon the closing of the 1851 Great Exhibition of the World's Industry that is utopian in its call for harmony among “eminent men”: I cannot refrain from remarking, with heartfelt pleasure, the singular harmony which has prevailed amongst the eminent men representing so many national interests—a harmony which cannot end with the event which produced it. Let us receive it as an auspicious omen for the future; and while we return our humble and hearty thanks to Almighty God for the blessing he has vouchsafed to our labours, let us all earnestly pray that Divine Providence, which has so benignantly watched over and shielded the illustration of nature's productions, conceived by human intellect, and fashioned by human skill, may still protect us, and may grant that the interchange of knowledge, resulting from the meeting of enlightened people in friendly rivalry, may be dispersed far and wide over distant lands; and thus by showing our mutual dependence upon each other, be a happy means of promoting unity among nations, and peace and good will among the various races of mankind.<sup>4</sup> Of course, the event was not an auspicious omen, as Europe plunged into the two world wars and colonization continued. Drawing from something as basic as a Wikipedia list of European wars, from 1790 to 1950 there were over 208 wars and rebellions fought between European nations, including Turkey as part of the Ottoman Empire. During World War II, the rhetoric of universalism confronted the harsh and deadly realities of fascism, concentration camps, and the genocide of European Jews, homosexuals, the Roma, and mixed-raced Germans. After World War II, Europe continued to be mired in the Cold War and the breakup of central Europe. The fraying of the European Union in the 2010s and 2020s exposed the conflicts between European nations, which may have shifted into economic and border policies instead of bullets and bombs. (Although as of spring 2022, Ukraine and Russia are in full-scale war with bullets and bombs.) The nationalist impulses toward intra-European violence expose themselves in the wars of the former Yugoslavia, the Brexit stories of the Polish plumber taking British jobs, or the responses to the 2007–2008 economic crisis in Greece. Thus, the modernist story of universal humankind falls apart when we realize that in spite of all the efforts of the European Union, it has not been achieved in Europe. ## Design Racism and the Great Chain of Being The universal humankind imagined in the modernist project is white, cismale, Anglo-Saxon, Christian, heterosexual, affluent, and of able body and mind. Anyone who deviates from this model is asked to assimilate to becoming that figure. Here I give a trigger warning for the racist violence in the descriptions of the exhibitions from the “Aboriginal States” in the 1851 Great Exhibition of the World's Industry, which included British Guiana, the Sioux of North America, Western Africa, Egypt and Tunis, and the Maltese: the productions of those who are commonly called Aborigines, or the less civilized races—are substantially the inferior fruits of human industry. Yet they illustrate the primitive elements, out of which the most advanced nations have elaborated their gorgeous and graceful, their eminently useful productions. The most polished nations may in them trace their own perfection backward to its source. Then, these aboriginal productions suggest, in their rude aptitude of purpose, sometimes in their skillfulness, irresistible arguments to the more refined, to look with greater indulgence upon their struggling fellows, by who such interesting productions are made. The highly civilized man, rendered by science familiar with the works of uncivilized people, will subdue his own prejudices in regard to their incapacity, and soon come practically to aid them to acquire the superior qualifications that shall rightfully place them on his level. China and India have so much in common with us, in their manufactures, their arts, and their agriculture, and they have made so much progress already in many respects, that purely aboriginal products are comparatively few in those countries.<sup>5</sup> These descriptions of the exhibition present a racist hierarchy of human production with Europeans at the top, East and South Asians in the middle, and everyone else at the bottom. Given that the modernist project and the colonizing project were one and the same, they also reflected the political structure of transporting South Asians to the Caribbean and Africa, especially after the abolition of slavery, to serve as a buffer population between Black populations and white settlers. This same hierarchy informs narratives of design excellence. If you look at the 2021 QS Top Universities Rankings for Art and Design, only European and Euro-American design schools make up the top ten. The first Asian design school in the list, Tongji University in China, is ranked at number 13 with four more Asian design schools in the 20s and two in the 30s. Euro-Australian design schools appear in the 20s and in the 40s. The first design school that is not European, Asian, or Australian is ranked 48, Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México (UNAM). Only two Middle Eastern schools and no African schools appear on a list of over 200 global design schools. To be clear, the sentiment in Europe that universal humanity could be manifested through design forms, in which the ethnic and national markers that reified national differences were absent, failed. But it was effectively realized through European colonization, in which Indigenous, Black, and other non-European practices of making were deemed inferior, outright prohibited, or systemically erased in order to maintain a culture of white supremacy. ## White Supremacy and White Supremacy Culture Until May 2018, I had not known that the invention of white supremacy had a time and place. Like most Black folks, I had assumed white people's racism was from time immemorial. But at a keynote lecture by Dr. Jacqueline Battalora as part of Toronto Metropolitan's White Privilege Conference, I learned that the time was 1681 and the place was Maryland. I was shocked (and a little bit angry) to hear this because, in addition to the wisdom of Black folks, I had as an anthropologist come across deep anti-Black and anti-Indigenous racism expressed by Europeans much earlier than 1681. And so, I asked Dr. Battalora about it. She replied that racism existed but it would be the racism of a British Christian person, a Spanish Christian person, or a Dutch Christian person. As she describes in an article transcribing a keynote about her book, Birth of a White Nation: The Invention of White People and Its Relevance Today: “The first appearance of this designation [that is, being white] was in an amendment by the Colonial Assembly of Maryland to what can best be described as the precursor to antimiscegenation laws. These were laws that made it illegal for ‘White’ people to marry various categories of ‘non-Whites.’ Prior to 1681 those who became ‘White’ were referred to first as ‘British and other Christians’ then ‘British and other freeborns.’” <sup>6</sup> The context for the appearance of the term ‘White’ was Nathaniel Bacon's Rebellion, which took place from 1676 to 1677 in Virginia. During the rebellion, white indentured servants, enslaved Africans, and freeborns of African descent “united in a fight against native tribes, unpaid labor, the plantation elite, and those governing the colony.” <sup>7</sup> To drive a wedge between white indentured servants and Black and Indigenous Peoples, Maryland and other southern colonies began passing series of racist and antimiscegenation laws with the intent to achieve social division not by elevating poor white people's socioeconomic status, but by lowering the statuses of Black and Indigenous Peoples. Key aspects of the laws were the prohibitions of ► White people marrying a person of African descent or a member of a native tribe, ► Setting enslaved African peoples free, ► Free Blacks holding public office, ► The beating or whipping of a white Christian servant naked without special order of the justice of the peace, ► Persons of African descent testifying against a white person, ► Free Blacks from possessing any weapon, and ► Voting by persons of African descent and members of a native tribe. The effects of the laws, which are still built into North American institutions, were that white Christian bodies, white voices, and white choices were protected by law and given higher value than those of Black, Indigenous, and non-Christian peoples. White supremacy became the law of the land of the colonial settler states, such as Canada, the United States, and Australia. And while European nations continued to jockey for supremacy over each other in colonial proxy wars, they were generally united in their sense of superiority over Black, Indigenous, and non-Christian peoples. It is important to note that not all of the people who are considered white today were included in this power positioning. Jewish people, as non-Christians, were structurally excluded from white status until white Christian guilt allowed them into the club after the exposure of the Nazi concentration camps and genocide. As a target of white supremacist hate groups, Jewish peoples still have a different positionality in relationship to white supremacy itself, although those with greater proximity to whiteness are provided some privileges compared to other groups. The Irish, because of their Catholic opposition to the British, became white only by outoppressing the British oppressors in their relationships with Black, Indigenous, and Asian folks. A must-read account of this history is How the Irish Became White by Noel Ignatiev. Southern European groups, such as Italians and Greeks, were also only deemed white in the United States after their mass immigrations of the 1900s when they served as a buffer against growing Black and Indigenous populations. White superiority manifests itself in two ways: first, in the protection of fragile white Christian bodies and, second, in the normalized set of values that white supremacy propagates. The Colonial Assembly's laws set in motion the legal protection of the white Christian body by prohibiting its beating in public and thus protecting its fragility, exempting it from legal prosecution for infractions against Black and Indigenous Peoples, and shielding it from the fear of death at the hands of Black free persons by prohibiting their gun ownership. In his book My Grandmother's Hands: Racialized Trauma and the Pathway to Mending Our Hearts and Bodies, Resmaa Menakem argues that the unprocessed trauma of Europeans over the brutality of the feudal regimes going back to the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries still resides in the bodies of white Europeans and their diaspora. We understand this white-on-white brutality when we watch Game of Thrones, which is a fictionalization of European dynastic history, or read Charles Dickens's descriptions of how poor British people were treated in the countryside as well as in urban workhouses and orphanages in the 1800s. What Menakem makes clear is that Europeans learned their colonial violence against Black, Indigenous, Asian, Middle Eastern, and other non-Christian peoples from centuries of practice on each other: “Throughout the United States’ history as a nation, white bodies have colonized, oppressed, brutalized, and murdered Black and Native ones. But well before the United States began, powerful white bodies colonized, oppressed, brutalized, and murdered other, less powerful white ones. The carnage perpetuated on Blacks and Native Americans in the New World began, on the same soil, as an adaptation of longstanding white-on-white practices. This brutalization created trauma that has yet to be healed among white bodies today.” 8 Menakem also discusses the effects of white body supremacy on police bodies, which is the reason I first read the book: to help me process the events surrounding the murder of George Floyd by Minnesota police officer Derek Chauvin, as well as the long list of Black, Indigenous, Asian, and Latinx peoples killed by the police. The murder was one of many contemporary manifestations of this pattern of violence in which white bodies are protected, and Black (and Indigenous) bodies are seen as a threat to be controlled, now mostly through the prison system, and police bodies, regardless of race, carry the trauma-inducing fear of protecting fragile white bodies by destroying Black and Indigenous bodies. The trial of Derek Chauvin was one of the rare times when the defensive narrative of white fear for its own safety to justify the killing of a Black person failed to produce the acquittal of a white man. Some stories tell us that it found its pinnacle of expression in the Bauhaus and its diaspora. But the story of design is older and more diverse than that lie. In 2010, Dr. Norm Sheehan (Wiradjuri), an associate professor at Swinburne University of Technology, and I laid out the structure of the Fundamental Design Studio course's first module: 2D Design and Indigenous Ways of Knowing. The double-weighted course module would be organized around the Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander principles of Respect, Know, Care, and Share, which Dr. Sheehan contributed to the dialogue. It would focus as much on oral storytelling as on visual sketching and drawing, with video oral presentations weighted equally in the assessments. Any student whose outcome mimicked Aboriginal dot paintings would get a failing grade for cultural misappropriation. Indigenous ways of knowing would be put into dialogue with Bauhaus principles of design. While my formal degrees are not in design, my colleagues, especially at the University of Illinois at Chicago (UIC), had schooled me in the principles following their own trainings in Basel from Wolfgang Weingart, at Yale from Armin Hoffman, and then at UIC through Philip Burton. I selected the Bauhaus movement because it had such a strong influence on design pedagogy, even in Australia. It was important that its biases be made explicit, so that students could select how its principles mattered to them. The assignment briefs asked students to focus on a place that had significant emotional and cultural meaning for them. Twice a week they would sketch the place with the constraints of using the Bauhaus principle that was the focus of the lesson. For example, in lesson one, they had two minutes, five minutes, and ten minutes each to sketch compositions using only point, then only lines, and then only planes. The homework task required that they complete a sketch using point, line, and plane together. For every sketch, they had to tell an oral story of the place, noticing how they might focus on light more in the point composition or focus more on large structures in the plane composition. The stories changed as the sketches changed, with each student learning more about one another through their relationship to the place. No one ever produced an Indigenous dot painting. In its final two years, the module was fully taught by all-Aboriginal faculty, including the Elder of the local Boon Wurrung peoples, Arweet Carolyn Briggs. The students always felt unsure if they were learning the Indigenous principles because the principles reflected internal explorations of the students’ relationships to the respect, knowledge, and care of place and how they share those relationships with others. When I ran into a former student in Sharjah, UAE, she told me that the course had changed her life and she had posted her original drawings on social media. I see this course as a possibility model for decolonizing design for a few reasons. First, it extracted what was useful rather than harmful in the Bauhaus pedagogy, which was its methodological breakdown of different elements and principles of design so that new designers could enter a practice of designing. Rather than “Throw the Bauhaus Under the Bus” as my friends Silas Munro and Ramon Tejada semi-jokingly suggest, the course sought what was redeemable from the design context of Europe in the 1920s and 1930s. Even the Indigenous instructors found the grounding and experimentation of elements useful to talk about, for example, how Aboriginal communities such as the Papunya Tula in the western desert use dots based on the way light is refracted in their environment, while those on the coast, such as the Gunnai of Southeastern Victoria, use lines, represented on their wooden shields. Second, our design course shifted the context of the utopian promise of the modernist design project by undergirding the ethos of the course with Aboriginal values of Respect, Know, Care, and Share. These Aboriginal values are the antidote to the values of white supremacy culture. They are directly connected to the land and the original peoples of the land, not imported European values which have harmed the land and its original peoples. Finally, the course put the two cultural domains in direct dialogue to further mutual understanding and respect. Readings from the Bauhaus and readings and visual examples from Aboriginals and Torres Strait Islanders were assigned each week. In its intentions, the course aligned itself with the five dimensions of reconciliation as outlined by Reconciliation Australia, including: 1. Race Relations: All Australians understand and value Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander and non-Indigenous cultures, rights, and experiences, which results in stronger relationships based on trust and respect and that are free of racism. 2. Equity and Equality: Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples participate equally in a range of life opportunities, and the unique rights of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples are recognised and upheld. 3.
