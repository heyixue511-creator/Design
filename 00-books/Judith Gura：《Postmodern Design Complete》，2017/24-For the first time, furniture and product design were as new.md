In Germany, the Neue Deutsche Welle (New German Wave) of the late 197os and early ı98os awakened a parallel interest in the social possibilities of design, as a new generation challenged establishment ideas and the dominance of Modernism. A group of designers in Berlin and Cologne, though influenced by their Italian counterparts, forged their own creative path to Postmodernism, with minimalist, rectilinear forms that were visually arresting but refreshingly unpretentious, using contrasting tone and texture and unexpected embellishments for decorative interest. These experimenters in furniture and industrial design worked with detritus and demolished materials like cardboard, glass, and perforated metal. In Prague, meanwhile, a Memphis-like cohort explored the use of traditional materials in non-industrial production.3 As some of the best (and costliest) postmodern objects became status symbols for the design elite, many American producers, anxious to keep pace with fashion, introduced designs that were more wannabes than true examples of the style. Translated for mass-production, most of these pieces were pastiches that merely grafted classical columns and other elements onto conventional forms, decorated with postmodern motifs and colors.
