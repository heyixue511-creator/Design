# DEAN POND HOUSE PUTNAM COUNTY, NEW YORK, UNITED STATES, 1988 JAMES EVANSON A CITY-POLISHED-MEETS-COUNTRY-CASUAL DESIGN, WITH A CONSISTENT USE OF CONTRASTING TONES AND TEXTURES. Pursuing parallel careers as both an architect/ builder and designer/craftsman, JAMES EVANSON (see under “Design") has often used one to explore ideas for the other. Such was the case with the Dean Pond House, built on speculation in the bucolic Kent township. Though Evanson refers to it as a "natural house," made with natural materials and neutral colors that suit the quiet landscape, the effect is more city-polished than country-casual. The exterior of colored natural cedar interlocks with a massive stucco base to anchor the house neatly onto the site. Both the angular form and the tripartite facade suggest a postmodern take on Palladian architecture. In the stripped-down interior, oversized rectangular skylights flood every room with light, bringing out the tones and texture of the natural wood on the floor, upper walls, and ceiling. A stone freplace, subtly echoing the columnar form of the main entrance, divides the main space into living and dining areas. The almost stark background is a perfect foil for Evanson's quirky furniture designs, which combine provocative forms with fine materials and craftsmanship. Instead of color, the contrasting textures create decorative interest: a terracotta tiled floor and lacquered cabinets in the kitchen, polished and unfinished wood against stucco walls, and nubby or glazed cotton fabrics. Although Evanson's furniture is very much at home here, so would be almost any other collection of contemporary pieces—which, of course, was precisely his intention.
