## Introduction Both are based on the practices of designers, but one (the original formulation of ‘design thinking’) is focused firmly within design practice whereas the other (a more recent but perhaps transient formulation) is focused outside the normal design domain, notably in business and the management of innovation. The latter formulation has been attractive within businesses that have realized the financial and competitive benefits of design-led technology. It has also been applied in a diversity of other fields, such as in education and more broadly in social innovation. This wider recognition of the relevance and value of design thinking is welcome. However, whilst the wider formulation draws upon some aspects of design practice, such as creative thinking, it is often applied through a simplified version of design methods adapted by and for consultants who mainly do not work within the design domain. These two different formulations and applications have led to some confusion because they both use the term ‘design thinking’. This book follows the original formulation of design thinking, which grew from the study of design processes, design reasoning and design cognition. Every experienced designer has learned, practised and honed their designerly ways of knowing, acting and thinking. Designers know about the technical aspects of their professions, about the materials and manufacturing processes of their field, about the habits and preferences of the users of their products and systems, about organizing and managing projects and teams, and about precedents and exemplars within their field. Designers act in ways that are creative but constrained by the problem requirements set by their clients, guided by the goals and abilities of the potential users of their products and systems, and facilitated and enhanced by their own powers and processes of imagining, modelling and evaluating ideas for partial or interim solutions. Designers think strategically within and around the problems they are given and the solutions they are creating, developing together both the problem and the possibilities for its solution to reach a satisfactory, matching resolution of both, using patterns and types of constructive reasoning. These designerly ways of knowing, acting and thinking are not something that can be gained adequately just from a seminar, a one-day workshop or even a four-day ‘bootcamp’ in design thinking – or just reading a book. This book is about design thinking in terms of the practical, strategical and cognitive skills actually used by designers in creating proposals for new products and systems. This is not a methods or ‘how-to’ book but a book that provides explanation, commentary and advice, rather than instruction (although becoming more self-aware of one’s thinking and acting can help in the development of skills and abilities). It is a book that reveals what has been learned from research into many different aspects of designerly thinking and is intended to sit within that culture of research and education. Its primary purpose is expressed in its subtitle: understanding how designers think and work. In writing this book, my goal has been to help anyone interested in design to develop their understanding of how designers think and work. Anyone so interested might well be a design student, teacher or researcher, perhaps a practising designer who still finds their own processes mysterious or difficult, other professionals who want to develop their understanding of design thinking or indeed anyone interested in the mysteries of designing, because anyone can be a designer, even in everyday activities. The focus of the book is on revealing what designers do during the activity of designing and on building an appreciation of the nature of design ability and expertise. Readers should gain from the book some insight into what it means to be a designer, how designers employ their practical, strategical and cognitive skills, both individually and collaboratively, and what is known about the nature of design ability and its development from novice student to expert professional. My own background includes architectural and industrial design, but primarily I am a design educator and researcher with an interest in the common aspects of designing that recur across different professional fields of practice. My approach to trying to understand how designers think and work is research-based: I look for and report evidence that comes from observation, experiment, analysis and reflection. My aim is to reveal and articulate the apparently mysterious (and sometimes mystified) cognitive and creative abilities of designers, that are common across many design fields. I take an interdisciplinary approach to design, so throughout the book observations are made, and comparisons are drawn, across various professional fields such as architectural, product, engineering and automotive design. Other professional design fields, such as software and interaction design, furniture design and communication design, are also mentioned. But because I take a research-based approach to understanding design thinking, some fields get less coverage in the book simply because less research has been conducted in them (or I am less aware of it). Nevertheless, I believe that many aspects of design thinking are common across the different fields of application, and so I trust that my observations and comments will have some validity across them all. ## The design of this book Just about everything that we have around us has been designed. Anything that isn’t a genuine, untouched-by-human-hands piece of nature has been designed by someone, including this book. Many people have been involved in the design of the book, including editors, typesetters and graphic designers, and the external reviewers. I am responsible for the choice of the words and illustrations, the selection and presentation of the evidence and the construction of the arguments, but I have also designed the overall structure of the book in a way that I hope helps the reader to use it and navigate through it. The structure is based upon a repeated pattern of double case studies followed by a commentary that summarizes and discusses what can be learned from the case studies in more general terms, and brings in and builds relationships with a wider field of literature in studies of design thinking. The focus of the first pair of case studies and the following discussion is more on ‘how designers think’ and the focus of the second pair is more on ‘how designers work’. These case studies are based on my own research; each of them is treated in depth and forms a complete chapter. They provide a focused resource for the study of high-quality, expert design thinking. The commentary and overview chapters that follow them provide discussion and reflection that I hope lead the reader into a broader and deeper understanding of the nature of design thinking. These central elements of case studies plus discussion are bounded by chapters at the beginning and the end of the book on design ability and design expertise. The first two case studies (Chapters 2 and 3) are interviewbased and draw upon the work of highly successful, outstanding contemporary designers: one an automotive designer, Gordon Murray, and the other an industrial designer, Kenneth Grange. The other two case studies (Chapters 5 and 6) are experiment-based research studies, featuring an expert engineering designer, Victor Scheinman, and a team of experienced product designers from the IDEO design consultancy. Both Victor and the IDEO team were asked to tackle the same small design project in a recorded, laboratory-like situation. In addition to observations to be made, and lessons to be drawn from these four particular case studies, I draw widely upon the research literature in design thinking in order to amplify and extend from the particular to the general in the four other chapters. Each of those chapters concludes with a short summary of its main points and the sources from which they are drawn. Occasional words or phrases that might perhaps be novel for readers, or have particular meanings or connotations within research in design thinking, are highlighted in bold type and are defined or expanded upon in a Glossary included at the end of the book.
