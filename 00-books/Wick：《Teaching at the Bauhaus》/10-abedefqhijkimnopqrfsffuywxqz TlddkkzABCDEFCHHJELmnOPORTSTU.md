## Criticism of the School

One of these ideas was a critique of the traditional school—a criticism that began early and remained a central theme for decades. Albers expressed it with acuity in 1924 in his essay “Historisch oder jetzig" (Historical or contemporary).

At its heart it has a double nature: it first addresses the fruitless process of the school's circular reproduction of historical teaching matter; and second, the ideology of individualism the school was propagating. In his view, the school had lost

its original goal; it is a teaching institute, and it revolves around a center, its highest figure, the professor. He passes on so-called “established" facts: knowledge, methods, rules, to enable historical thinking. . . . The old school seeks, in addition to its main goal of popular education, to pass on abilities but only a few essential ones: language, writing, arithmetic. Today people want knowledge and so they want scientific departments. There, people are teaching, writing things up and writing things down, reading things aloud and looking them up, finding snacks everywhere but never eating their fill The highest students are called auditors, they take many books and turn them into a single one, after which they are called doctor and they call up their own auditors: the teaching moves in circles. Today, passing something along without increasing its value is called wangling. So the school produces wanglers rather than creators. Rather than having the students design, it has them take notes. . . . That is a way to make managers, not designers. . .. Today's youth notes the wrong direction: that... historical knowledge hinders production. And that hearing teaching without being allowed to forget is like taking a meal without a stool to follow, and that the substitute for the latter—regurgitating in exams— is unhealthy. . . . A lot of history leaves little room for work. The reverse—little history and much work—is our task.24

What Albers is articulating here in terms of critical discontent matches point for point the criticism expressed by the reform pedagogues²⁵ of the old tutorial and book schooling, a criticism that came to the fore at the end of the nineteenth and the first decade of the twentieth centuries, with vehemence and a determined will to change it. In the course of his own training as a teacher and subsequent practical experience in schools, Albers would naturally have encountered this critique. It is a critique of the teaching material, of dead book knowledge, of false scientization and superficial knowledge of everything ("finding snacks everywhere but never eating their fill“), of the figure of the dogmatic, authoritarian teacher, but especially of the excess of historically oriented instruction, which rejects the present and any productive approach to it—since Nietzsche, this has been a common theme in the critique of education.26

A second aspect of Albers's critique of the school concerns the "cultivation of the individual." The task of the school as a public institution is, in Albers's view, to “integrate" the individual into the community or society.27 The development of personality is an “extracurricular task," for “the development of personal goals can only be achieved by the individual alone."28 And elsewhere:

Individualism is not primarily a concern of the school, because individualism emphasizes separateness; the school is concerned with integrating into the events of time, into society (state, career, economy). The cultivation of indīviduality is the individual's own task and not that of collective enterprises like the school. The school should cultivate individuals passively, i.e., by not interfering with personal development. . . . Sociological economy must reject the cult of personality of existing pedagogy: productive individuality asserts itself without and despite education.29

This put Albers, who continued to take a position against "forced," “exalted," or even "lawless" individualism³0 (and whose own artistic practice was anything but free, individual self-expression), in sharp contrast to the reform pedagogues (e.g., Berthold Otto), who sought to redesign the school explicitly to promote the individuality of the child or youth. Albers should rather be seen in the context of approaches to reform pedagogy that aimed to connect the individual more closely to society, which were promoted under slogans like “living community schools" or—with an eye to the overall social and political context—“education for the citizen of the state" (Foerster, Kerschensteiner). It is clear that Albers's dismissal of "the arrogance of the individual" and the “cult of personality" (which naturally flowers most fully in the cult of genius in the arts) was part and parcel with the ideals of community formulated in the Bauhaus program and went together with the guiding social ideas that were influential there. It is equally clear that this view found its most visible expression in the anonymity of the student works (and admittedly also in their interchangeability) produced in the preliminary course.

## Learning by Doing

Albers countered this negative idea—criticism of the existing school—with his own conception of education, which can be expressed in the succinct formula "learning by doing."

We need not examine in detail the assertion that the old tutorial and book schooling barely offeredan opportunity to develop and encourage the creative potential of the students. The appearance ofthearts education movement within the reform pedagogy movement resulted from the realization of the necessity of creative education as a corrective to one-sided cognitive schooling andmechanical acquisitionof traditional content and methods.

In light of the goal at the Bauhaus to educate students to be “human beings who design,″³1 Albers also focused on the creative aspect in his pedagogical approach, which he characterized in the pithy slogan “Experimenting takes priority over studying."32 He objected that the teaching of knowledge and skills based on tradition (both in the old learning school and in an apprenticeship in the crafts based on the principle of imitatio) “hinders creation and invention":

But invention—and re-invention too—is the essence of creativity. . . .

Learned working methods and their application develop insight and skill but not creative energies.

The ability to invent through construction and to discover through observation is developed—at least at first—by undisturbed, uninfluenced, and unprejudiced experiment that is (at first) a playful tinkering with concrete goal, that is to say unprofessional (i.e., not burdened with instruction) experimental work.

It often happens that what were thought to be innovations in the application or treatment turn out afterward to have existed before.

But the result is experienced and thus a possession because it is learned and not taught.

Learning is better, because it is more intensive, than teaching: the more that is taught, the less can be learned.

Sometimes the results of these experiments represent innovations in the application or treatment of material. But even when we evolve methods which are already in use, we have arrived at them independently, through direct experience and they are our own because they have been re-discovered rather than taught. Learning is better, because it is more intensive, than teaching: the more that is taught, the less can be learned.

We know that this instruction by learning takes longer paths, even detours and false paths. But no beginningis straightforward. Andmistakes that are recognized encourage progress. Conscious detours and controlled false paths willsharpen criticism, will make those once burnt twice shy, and will produce a desire to find the right paths.33

"Learning and not teaching," the principle of trial and error as the condition for the creative process, “learning by discovering" as a necessary element of teaching creativity—with these maxims Albers demonstrated that he was an educator who took the progressive educational ideals of the pedagogical reform movement of the period around and after 1900 and productively applied them to the field of artists' training. The pedagogical ideas of the American John Dewey (1859–1952) seem to have had an especially lasting influence on Albers. Dewey's writings on pedagogy—for example, The School and Society and Democracy and Education—were published during the first two decades of the twentieth century in German translation and were extraordinarily stimulating for the reform pedagogy movement, especially for Kerschensteiner. Dewey's pedagogical pragmatism, which he himself characterized as "instrumentalism," can be summarized very briefly as follows: The school should prepare students for practical life; consequently, dead book knowledge (ancient languages, to some extent mathematics as well) should be abandoned. "Knowledge is obtained not through abstract insight but through action itself. ... The experiences gained through action benefit further action."34 "Learning by doing" is thus the fundamental pedagogical principle. As a result, among the reforms that Dewey called for, "conscious development and exercise of manual dexterity. . . is of primary importance"35 (see “The Background of Itten's Pedagogy" in chapter 5).

In Germany this goal of training in manual dexterity was addressed by Georg Kerschensteiner (1854–1932) in his conception of the work school. He encouraged the student's own activity; the acquisition of knowledge through experience, an emphasis on objectivity (meaning the ability to let the ego withdraw and subsume one's own subjectivity to the task), training in fundamental qualities for work—these are some of the main goals of the work school that also played a central role in Albers's concept of education. Albers saw his own view of practical instruction in deliberate contrast to the practical instruction of the work school, which was focused only on craft skills: "Where there is a bit of cabinetmaker's work, a little bookbinding, a bit of tailoring. Then sawing and planing (the most difficult of the cabinetmaker's tasks), filing and driving, pasting and gluing all remain unproductive. Because they satisfy only the need to remain occupied, not the creative urge."36 Although Albers appealed to the handicrafts, he tried to expand the dimension of the creative, which he considered essential. In order to activate the creative energies in the students, in his "Werklicher Formunterricht" (Instruction in form through work), which was anything but a theoretical course in form and design, he rigorously limited the use of tools at first in order to encourage “thinking and fiddling about on one's own"37 and to counteract a tendency to fall into actions guidedby tradition. Part of his pedagogical strategy forbade everything that is known and familiar, and thus does not need to be invented:

For example, out there (in the handicrafts and industry) paper is usually used lying down, flat, and glued, which means that one side of the paper usually loses its expression, and the edge is almost never used. That gives us an opportunity to use paper standing, uneven, sculpturally vivid, with both sides and an emphasis on the edges. ... Thus the material is intentionally treated differently than it is outside but not on principle. Not in order to do it differently (that usually means considering the norm) but in order not to make it like the others do (whereby the emphasis is on the method). That means not to imitate but to search on one's own and to learn to find on one's own—constructive thinking.38

Albers himself characterized his method of instruction as inductive.39 That means that he did not confront his students with an elaborately worked-out theory that was abstract and often remote from the student's range of experience (at the Bauhaus this contrasting approach was especially true of Kandinsky's design theory), but rather he let the students—freed from all theoretical ballast—accumulate primary experiences with the simplest materials of the sort that had traditionally been considered unworthy of art: paper, cardboard, wire, glass, straw, rubber, cellophane, matchboxes, razor blades, phonograph needles, and so on. Uninhibited experimentation with these materials, with an eye to their technical and aesthetic possibilities, led directly to experience with elementary forms that—in the classic manner of reform pedagogy—were reflected on in the process of self-control and collective discussion of the results (for, according to Kerschensteiner, there is no purely manual activity, it always implies an intellectual activity as well). In this way, the students inductively achieve through "precise observation and new vision"40not only a basic technological understanding but also knowledge of universal formal principles like harmony, rhythm, scale, proportion, and symmetry.

In contrast to the official opinion at the Bauhaus (as stated in the founding manifesto) that art cannot be taught, Albers took a somewhat modified standpoint on the issue. In his view, although art “cannot be taught directly," it can be “learned."41 This linguistic nuance contains that which distinguishes the principles of Albers's concept of education from the practical training of the old school, as manifested in the activities of the art academies. The question that remain are: According to what principles does this learning happen concretely? To which of the premises that the teacher establishes is it related? To which fields of sensory knowledge does it relate?

## Exercises with Matière and Materials

A minute reconstruction of Josef Albers's teachings between 1923 and 1933 willnot be undertaken here. It is possible, however, to distinguish three phases clearly. A first phase from1923, after Itten's departure, to the end of the Weimar Bauhaus in 1925, during which Albers, an apprentice at the Bauhaus, was responsible for the socalled work studies that formed part of the preliminary course, then still just one semester, that was officially led by Moholy-Nagy. In 1925 he became a regular member of the teaching faculty as a junior master and until 1928 took over responsibility for teaching the first semester of the now two-semester preliminary course; Moholy taught the second semester. That marked the second phase; and the third began in 1928 with Moholy's departure from the Bauhaus and ended with the final closing of the school in 1933. During this last phase Albers had sole responsibility for the entire preliminary course.42

Substantial knowledge of Albers's pedagogical activities in Weimar is as sketchy now as it ever was. Without going into the Gropius-Itten conflict again, we need only recall that Johannes Itten's teachings were increasingly in the cross fire of the Bauhaus director's criticism, as can be seen clearly from a passage in a circular letter of 13 February 1923 to the council of masters:

The failure of the students . .. seems to me to have been partially caused by the way the preliminary instruction was handled. I have the impression that the goals set for the preliminary instruction—to educate individuals to creative work—would certainly be affirmed by all of us but that the way that it is carried out is not right and that it produces in many of the students the exact opposite of what was sought. The fog of the artist that we wanted to clear grew even more dense. The free speculative work of the first semester and the many intellectual offerings clearly overfed their brains and led some of the young and dependent students to misjudge themselves and become vain.43

In order to establish contact with the workshops as early as possible—the question of the organic continuation of the work of the preliminary course in the workshops and the seamless transition from the preliminary course to the practical instruction was one of the problems of the founding phase of the Bauhaus that ultimately remained unsolved—in the spring of 1923 Gropius proposed introducing the work studies as an obligatory part of the preliminary course: "Albers, who has sufficient technical and pedagogical abilities, will oversee everything."44 It soon became evident, however, that this newly conceived preliminary instruction required adjustments to the organization: "There are so many sides to the preliminary course that for most of the participants half a year is too short. For that reason, starting in the fall, the preliminary instruction will last one year."45 The intended date for this change was 1 October 1924.

The first question is what this work study, which from 1923 on was an integral part of the preliminary course and required for all new students, looked like. The work study [Werkarbeit] should not be confused with the practical instruction [Werklehre] that followed the preliminary course. A text that Albers wrote from the great Bauhaus retrospective in New York in 1938 makes clear that this workshop course continued to be oriented around the particulars of the specific crafts and did not yet have the "abstract character"46 of the later basic course in Dessau (which is evident from the objects produced at the time: simple containers, children's toys, toy furniture, and so on):

The work with materials in this course was planned to prepare the first semester students for later craft-studies in the various Bauhaus workshops. The students were introduced to a simple and elementary, but appropriate use of the most important craft materials, such as wood, metal, glass, stone, textiles and paint, and to an understanding of their relationships as well as the differences between them. In this way we tried, without anticipating later workshop practice, and without workshop equipment, to develop an understanding of the fundamental principles of materials and the principles of construction.

To this end we analyzed typical treatments and combinations of materials, and worked them out with our hands. For instance, we visited the workshops of box, chair and basketmakers, of carpenters and cabinet-makers, of coopers and cartwrights, in order to learn the different uses of wood, the different characteristics of flat grain and quarter-sawing, split, bent and laminated wood, and to learn the various methods of joining: glueing, nailing, pegging and screwing.

We tried to apply our knowledge to the making of useful objects. . . . Thus, at first, we studied material more or less on a traditional handicraft basis.47

In Dessau the contours of our image of the course begin to be defined more clearly, aided by the large numbers of photographs by Erich Consemüller and other Bauhaus students that document the student works. The method of an eclectic mix of specific techniques taken from various crafts was abandoned by Albers as pedagogically inadequate (“finding snacks everywhere but never eating their fill"). This was not only because theemphasis was laidtoo much on imitative drilling of traditional techniques that hampered innovation and set strict limits on the creative explorationof new possibilities of workingwith materials and tools, but also because Albers recognized that this kind of use of specific crafts to prepare for the experimental work in the teaching workshops was still too dependent on specialization andthat theopportunities for transferring experiences from one area to another were too limited.

Consequently, Albers thoroughly eradicated the residuals of the specialized training in the crafts from his version of the preliminary course and built up his instruction upon two pillars: on the exercises with matière [Materie]andmaterials [Materia/]—two terms that, because of their similarity, have naturally been confusedagain and again.

The exercises with matière show that Albers was building on the work of Itten and Moholy-Nagy. With respect to terminology, he was closer to Moholy. The assemblage character of the individual works that resulted, however, is more reminiscent of Itten.

## Educational Methodology

Hannes Beckmann, a student at the Bauhaus from 1928 to 1931, described his first impressions of Albers's preliminary course as follows:

Josef Albers entered the room, carrying with him a bunch of newspapers, which were distributed among the students. He then addressed us, saying something like this: "Ladies and gentlemen, we are poor, not rich. We can't afford to waste materials or time. We have to make the most out of the least. All art starts with a material, and therefore we have first to investigate what our material can do. So, at the beginning we will experiment without aiming at making a product. At the moment we prefer cleverness to beauty. Economy of form depends on the material we are working with. Notice that often you will have more by doing less.

Our studies shouldleadto constructive thinking. . . . I want you now to take the newspapers you got and try to make something out of them that is more than you have now. I want you to respect the material and use it in a way that makes sense—preserve its inherent characteristics. If you can do without tools like knives and scissors, and without glue, the better. . . .“ And with these words he left the room, leaving us quite flabbergasted. He returned hours later and asked us to put the result of our efforts on the floor.

There were masks, boats, castles, airplanes, animals. . . little figurines. He referred to all this as kindergarten products, which could have been made better in other materials. He pointed then at a study of extreme simplicity, made by a young Hungarian architect. He simply had taken the newspaper and folded it lengthwise so that it was standing up like a folding screen. Josef Albers explained to us how well the material was understood and utilized—how the folding process was natural to paper, because it resulted in making a pliable material stiff. . . . He further pointed out to us that a newspaper lying on the table would be hidden. Now that the paper was standing up, both sides had become visually active. The paper had lost its tired look— its lazy appearance. After a while we caught on to his way of seeing and thinking. Fascinating studies in all kinds of materials, like paper, corrugated cardboard, kitchen matches, wire, metal, were produced [my emphasis—-R.W.].59

This report from a former student is cited here at such length because it clearly reveals the working principles that characterizedAlbers's teaching. These working principles—which, as Albers emphasized, were basednot on a methodology of "fabrication" but of“education"60—are economy of materials and economy of labor.

Economy of materials and economy of labor are directly related in Albers's “methodology of education, In his teachings economy of labor was usually practiced by placing a limit of a single work process and, initially at least, by a radical limitation of the use of tools (only at a more advanced stage is the arsenal of tools expanded). A good example ofeconomy of materials in conjunction with economy of means is a sculptural material study in sheet metal by Alphons Frieling, about whichAlbers commented:“Emphasis of material economy (created from a rectangle without any waste, tested as strong as possible, made as tall as possible) and of economy of means (one tool: metal shears, . .. one process: cutting with the shears, without turning back at all)."67

Restricting the students to simple materials and requiring them to use methods based on economy of materials andlabor could be interpretedas pedagogical rigor, which would seem to be incompatible with the goal of developing creativity. In fact, however, the works created in Albers's preliminary course reveal a variety that demonstrate the contrary, namely, that for creative people, despite external restrictions—which are, of course, primarily pedagogically motivated here, although we should not ignore the economic difficulties of the time as part of the general context—the number of possibie solutions is almost unlimited. Moreover, Albers, who on principle offered no model solutions and was wary of correcting his students' work (another ideal from reform pedagogy that Albers shared with Johannes Itten, who was born the same year), believed there was no ideal solution. Rather, in his view there were a number of different solutions, with different advantages, all of which were equally justified as long as the criteria he had set were strictly followed: "relation of effort to effect is the measure for judging the result. One element plus one element must at least exceed their sum by producing an interesting relationship."68

## .Criticism and Reception

Under the directorship of Hannes Meyer, the preliminary course was increasingly in the cross fire of criticism from the left. As was demonstrated in "The Bauhaus Directors as Educators" in chapter 4, the Bauhaus at the time was seeking to move away from being"the art school of modernism" and transform itself into a architectural university with associated design departments. The value placed on artistic training was rigorously limited, and as early as 1929 Ernst Kállai, the editor of bauhaus magazine, wrote in the catalog for a Bauhaus exhibition at the Arts and Crafts Museum in Basel that the preliminary course was an "opportunity to work off artists' complexes," and anyone entering the workshops at the Bauhaus has "no more urgent task than to forget the preliminary course."69It was only logical that at time when political forces within the Bauhaus— as in society in general—were becoming more and more polarized between right and left, a group of Communist students would step in with a hectographed appeal that calied for the elimination of the preliminary course. The targets of the critique were Albers's "Werklicher Formunterricht" and Kandinsky's “Analytisches Zeichnen" (Analytical drawing) (for more on this, see "Critical Objections" in chapter 8). The critique of Albers's preliminary course read as follows: "With its practical instruction in form problems and with its experiments in materials, the preliminary course (allegedly) serves to develop a knowledge of these materials and an understanding of their uses. . . . We question the contention that this can be achieved by abstract, hence nonfunctional, use of corrugated cardboard, chicken wire, etc. Contrasting different kinds of material by no means demonstrates their practical value, but is instead a formal 'thing in itself.' . . ."70 Although this criticism was not entirely far-fetched, it was selective and tendentious. It was directed only at the matière exercises and did not address the material studies, which treated the constructional, functional, and economic possibilities of the materials. And though these material studies might seem at first glance to have been formalistic, this appearance is deceptive. They were—or at least were intended to be—exercises that served to train constructional thought and economic action. They were in no case purely formal exercises in which, as Heimo Kuchling asşerts, "the aesthetic refinement overshadows the practical tendencies."71

The desire on the part of several of the students to eliminate the preliminary course was accompanied by the odd notion of establishing a first semester workshop in its place. Workshop instruction in the very first semester would have meant a relapse to positions that Albers himself had overcome in a long clarification process in his pedagogy at the beginning of the Dessau period, namely, to positions that would necessarily have led to amateurish handicrafts (“’a bit of' cabinetmaker's work, ’a bit of' tailoring, ‘a bit of' bookbinding") and would have been incompatible with the need for an elementary education in creativity for future architects and designers (not fine artists, who did not officially exist at the Bauhaus).

Albers's influence on aesthetic education (meaning not his groundbreaking didactic work on color, The Interaction of Color, but rather the material exercises he introduced at the Bauhaus) has been insufficiently explored. Already during the Weimar Republic, Alfred Ehrhardt's Gestaltungslehre (Design theory) of 1932 tried to take Albers's approach and transplant it into arts education in the schools (see "Weimar Republic" in chapter 12), and several art schools of the time also took note of Albers's work.7² In the education of artists and designers after World War Il it was above all the former members of the Bauhaus themselves—Hin Bredendieck (Chicago), Hannes Neuner and Maximilian Debus (Stuttgart), Kurt Kranz (Hamburg), Hanns Hoffmann-Lederer (Darmstadt), and Iwao Yamawaki (Tokyo)7³who propagated the methods that Albers practiced (see "After 1945: The Federal Republic of Germany" in chapter 12). Nor should we overlook the fact that Albers's materialexercises found their way intoartseducation in schools after 1945, even though they were robbedof their genuine intentions, lockedinto formalism, and degraded into a “game with artistic means" (Röttger), serving more the “urge to entertain" than the "urge to design" (Albers). This would indeed be a place for a critique, a critique directed less at Albers than at the fellow travelers and followers who, for lack of ideas or simply to make things easy, 'chose to follow well-trodden paths.
