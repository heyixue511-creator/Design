The link established by Buffalo Books and the Isotype Institute to the Western Regional Government came during a unique phase of Nigerian history. Over the course of the 195os, Nigeria made increasing gains in self-determination until, in 196o, it achieved independence from Britain. During the 195os, much of Nigeria's growing autonomy was exercised regionally, and of its several regions the governance of the Western Region was the most politically and socially progressive. The decade was punctuated by a series of conferences convened by the British government that were intended to strengthen and resolve Nigeria's constitutional arrangements, leading up to its anticipated independence. The conference held in London in July and August 1953 attracted delegates from all regions of Nigeria; the most prominent figure among the Western Region's delegates was Obafemi Awolowo, then Leader of the Party in Power, the Action Group.²⁴

In the months that followed, further discussions with the Western Regional Government took place, leading in early 1954 to a memorandum drawn up by Marie Neurath setting out in general terms a programme of work to be undertaken by the Isotype Institute in the Western Region. The object of the work would, in effect, be public relations, to illustrate, explain, and bring home to the people of the Western Region what the aims of the Government are; everybody should understand, be interested and take part in the affairs which are their own.'³º The memorandum described the Isotype method for producing visual work and Marie Neurath's expertise in transformation, which was especially relevant to the requirements of the Western Regional Government.³¹ The proposed sum of activities would encompass research and transformation leading to visual work, as well as – and importantly – the establishment of a local workshop to carry out similar work on a permanent basis by trained Nigerians.

## In Africa

Having reached agreement with the Western Regional Government on a programme of work, Marie Neurath was invited to the Western Region. She would be based in the capital Ibadan, and would be given the title 'Visual Aids Expert'. A first trip would take place from June to September 1954, with a second planned for the following year.³² Although she declined an initial proposal of a single six-month stay, the two trips would nevertheless keep her away from London and the Isotype Institute for lengthy periods. That she accepted such arrangements as necessary suggests the importance she placed on local immersion. This would give her direct and regular contact with ministers and civil servants in the Western Region, and allow her to experience conditions on the ground. She could observe how, where, and by whom visual aids were used before resolving on how Isotype work might be best integrated into the local scene.

Among the first items on Marie Neurath's itinerary after arriving in the Western Region were visits to schools in and around Ibadan. She hoped to observe classroom teaching and learning that involved visual aids ‘with a view to improving her own work in visual education'.³ª She encountered a range of visual aids made by pupils that presented topics such as water supply, imports and exports, geography, economics, health, agriculture, and counting methods. She was impressed by the work being done and commended the progress, drive, and optimism

of all those she met. Her visits were reported in at least three regional newspapers.³⁴

## White Paper booklets

Together with her visits to local schools, it seems that Marie Neurath's encounter with life in Ibadan also had important and immediate consequences.

Already in London I had been given a white paper' on education to read ... . From this I had designed a large summary chart; it went straight into the wastepaper basket after I had walked through the streets of Ibadan; why should these people have to struggle with my chart? I made a booklet of 16 pages out of it; problems, tasks, solutions were shown step by step. This format was retained for all the other subjects.'³⁵

The booklet to which Marie Neurath refers, Education for all in the Western Region, was one in a series that would first depict existing circumstances in the Western Region, then show how the government's programme of development would lead to improvements in the lives of all. Other booklets in the series would address agriculture, health, and fiscal policy under the titles Better farming for better living in the Western Region, Health for all in the Western Region, and Paying for progress in the Western Region. Each was commissioned by a government minister in connection with his portfolio and collectively became known as White Paper booklets, since they summarized policy in these areas.³⁶ Marie Neurath recalled how the commissioning happened:

I had a discussion about my work in the house of Awolowo, the prime minister; I first had to design a booklet about schools – general education was just being introduced – together with the minister of Education, Awokoya. During the discussions, the minister of agriculture, Akinloye, said that he wanted to have a book too. The minister of health, Ighodaro, soon introduced himself as well; Chief Akran gave me access every time I needed any help; I probably spoke with him most when I was preparing the more general booklet, Paying for progress'.³7

The aim of the booklets was not to instruct but to show and explain 'problems, tasks, solutions' simply and directly. For each booklet, work was first directed towards identifying the basic message to be communicated and devising a narrative strategy and structure to convey it. A sequence of sixteen pages then evolved from small pencil sketches through to full-size colour visualizations;³⁸ text was composed in parallel.³9 By the end of the process, the completed 'rough' was sufficiently detailed to enable ministers to approve the designs, which would then guide the making of art-work back in London. Throughout, ministers, civil servants, and British colonial administrators collaborated in shaping content, reviewing designs, and resolving details.⁴º

Apart from their subject-specific content, Marie Neurath pursued a broadly similar visual strategy for the booklets as a whole, in particular by balancing general and local depictions. General depictions consisted of graphic arrangements showing processes, relationships, and comparisons typical of Isotype. Local depictions included the flora, fauna, equipment, buildings (materials and structures), village and town scenes, and work roles typical of the Western Region, which Marie Neurath observed herself or had brought to her attention by government officials and specialists. With few exceptions, depictions of humans were also localized in their naturalistic features and in their cultural and social identities, conveyed mainly through attire. The latter consisted of Yoruba robes, wraps, and headwear, while professional or managerial roles were indicated by specialist work clothing or Western dress.4¹ All graphic depictions were then carefully structured and integrated with the text using the well-practised 'picture-text-style' common to other Isotype work. The result was a mutually reinforcing narrative in which text would frame graphics or be woven through them, but always working in parallel to the order of looking.ª²

Another project begun by Marie Neurath during her first stay in the Western Region was a series of 'poster-leaflets' on health topics. They would result from an extensive process of research and consultation both in the Western Region and in London.4³ The poster-leaflet idea came about on a trip Marie Neurath made to Benin province in early August 1954 to learn more about health care provision. While there she visited various health facilities including the General Hospital in Benin City, the Leprosy Centre at Ossiomo, several medical field units, and a doctor's surgery.⁴⁴ At the surgery, she saw a waiting room crammed full' of patients who (she was told) returned again and again with the same complaints. So she suggested producing educational pictures with which medical assistants could explain to patients, while they waited, the nature of their condition and how it could be prevented.They would then perhaps grasp what they were doing wrong and how they should do it better.'⁴Discussions with doctors and nurses at the General Hospital revealed their preference for illustrated wall charts that patients could also take home to study. Together with the resident, Dr Ogunlesi, Marie Neurath identified a group of health care issues in need of clear communication. They included the importance of good food and water, and the diagnosis and treatment of tuberculosis and leprosy.

On her return to Ibadan, having secured the agreement of the Minister of Health, Ighodaro, Marie Neurath set to work. She consulted with Dr Lengauer, who she had met at Ossiomo. Lengauer offered advice on the visual representation of leprosy, its mild and bad forms, its infectiousness, and its mutilations, which most people found frightening. 46 Back in London, she corresponded with V. W. Hetreed, a tuberculosis specialist connected with the Tuberculosis Survey in Ibadan, from whom she hoped to get feedback on her evolving designs.4 Initially, Hetreed expressed doubts about the value of visual methods, which he had already encountered in the Isotype booklet prepared for the National Tuberculosis Association some fifteen years earlier: 'I am however not at all certain that it [the NTA booklet] can be a successful method for the illiterate or semi literate, who does not readily relate diagrams, magnified pictures &c to the object they represent.'48

After receiving Marie Neurath's rough, Hetreed offered feedback that was mostly encouraging.4 He began, however, with the general observation that a poster-leaflet predicated on patients visiting a doctor was unrealistic when, in Ibadan, for a population of nearly half a million, there was only one doctor assigned to treating tuberculosis and then only half-time; in Lagos, with a population of 28o,ooo, again only one doctor treated TB (though here full-time); elsewhere in the Western Region there were apparently no other doctors dedicated to TB treatment. Hetreed then commented on several of the visual representations, including one showing bad breath, which he predicted would be read both literally and as a sign of TB transmission, though not in the sense of germs or tiny creatures'. He also offered thoughts on the use of colour: yellow, in his view, had been correctly deployed to designate a healthy person; red, by contrast, should represent tuberculosis because of its Yoruba associations with danger and misfortune; blue, suggesting happiness, was appropriate for depictions of treatment and protection, and for doctors.⁵º

By late 1954, designs for the poster-leaflets were finalized. As planned, each would function as a wall chart that could also be folded up and taken away. Like the White Paper booklets, the poster-leaflets placed locally recognizable figures and scenes into graphic arrangements typical of Isotype; in a few instances, general and local pictograms worked side-by-side.

## School visits

With White Paper booklets in hand, Marie Neurath was eager to try them out on their intended audiences, and in mid-March she began a week-long tour of schools in the Ibadan area. An upbeat report written by a government publicity officer, probably for circulation among ministries that had commissioned a booklet, provides a record of the visits.⁵³ They were planned to give teachers an opportunity to present and discuss the booklets with their students, while allowing Marie Neurath to observe how the booklets were received. For younger pupils, this happened first through looking, then by reading:

In the lower classes it was intended to discover how much the pupils could recognise in the books certain objects familiar to them and to guess why they are shown there side by side with one another. That is to say, to build up a story by connecting a number of objects used together in the daily activities of the town. The teacher was then required to hold a reading lesson with them in the course of which he explains those objects in relation to the activities of the Regional Government. 54

Older pupils read the texts on their own, then took part in a questionand-answer session with the teachers and Marie Neurath. At one school where Education for all was distributed to several classes, its message was apparently received enthusiastically:

the children expressed unanimous opinion that education for all was better than that for a few, as the former would give equal opportunities in life for all. In the upper classes, where the book is better appreciated, the pupils held that the introduction of free primary education in the West was a bold step towards the abolition of illiteracy and ignorance in the Region.

While the report was in no doubt about the positive impression the booklets had made on the pupils, this was also confirmed by notes taken by teachers in classes at the Momo Mohude Memorial (Sabo)
