Modernism might be characterized as a movement to reject architectural conventions, to impose new rules. In the 1920s, there were competing strands of modernity and, in any history of interior design, the different intentions of rival factions are important because they created divergent architectural cultures to which interior design was obliged to respond. In the beginning, the most aggressive of these factions was identified by a number of labels, which implied a new, unemotional response to the act of designing. In German it was Neue Sachlichkeit (New Objectivity), in French L’Esprit Nouveau (The New Spirit), in English it was Functionalism. It was fortunate in the end to be modernism, which gave it custody of the word labeled “modern” and the progressive virtues deemed to accompany it. ## PIONEERS OF MODERNISM One perception of the emergence of architectural modernism is that it was defined and driven by four men: Frank Lloyd Wright in the United States, the Swiss Le Corbusier in France, and Walter Gropius and Ludwig Mies van der Rohe in Germany. Throughout his career, Wright’s projects, with the exception of the Prairie houses, were too diverse and too personal to constitute a definable style but he did have an early impact on the European trio. All three were working in the Berlin office of Peter Behrens (1868–1940) when a copy of the Wasmuth portfolio arrived and their excitement at its content is recorded. It was Le Corbusier who set out rules for a new architecture in his Vers une Architecture (Toward an Architecture) in 1927. He proposed it would eliminate the social and political shortcomings that all previous architectural styles had embodied and perpetuated. He argued against established architectural conventions and, most vehemently, against ornament on—or in—buildings. Over the course of his career his own architecture strayed from his first precise strictures. Mies coined aphorisms, most memorably “less is more” and “God is in the details.” He issued no manifestoes, but his buildings set out his arguments for an architecture of steel and glass. He refined that simple formula throughout his career, particularly in the United States when he immigrated from Nazi Germany in 1938. Gropius established the Bauhaus school in Weimar, Germany, which rejected the evolutionary progression of European design culture across all disciplines in favor of a revolutionary rejection of all precedents. The Bauhaus became the model for art and architecture schools in Europe and North America but imitation diluted and distorted its message. ## Le Corbusier and His Five Rules Between 1920 and 1925, Le Corbusier, French painter Amédée Ozenfant (1886–1966), and Belgian writer Paul Dermée (1886–1951) produced the magazine L’Esprit Nouveau (The New Spirit). In it, Le Corbusier set out his five rules for a modern house, which should have: Pilotis Columns to raise main living areas one story above ground. Free plan Internal floors that are free of structural internal walls. Free façade Exterior walls that are non-structural. Horizontal ribbon windows Continuous horizontal strips of windows to light rooms evenly. Roof garden Flat roofs with garden terraces. In theory, the free plan offered opportunities for interior designers. However, proscription of decoration and prescription of plain flat surfaces limited their opportunities. Le Corbusier’s five rules became standard theory in schools of architecture and were absorbed by new waves of architects and designers but the new architecture never found favor with the laity on whom it was foisted. Elimination of applied decoration and enthusiasm for raw concrete had a Calvinistic rigor. Yet it was revolutionary, and in the spirit of a time when the Soviet Union was establishing itself and National Socialism was picking up momentum in Germany. Modern architecture, as defined by Le Corbusier, announced itself to be philanthropic but its instincts were totalitarian. It was not for the frivolous. Le Corbusier declared: “Decorative art, as opposed to the machine phenomenon, is that final twitch of the old manual mode and is a dying thing . . . [the new architecture] will contain only standard things created by industry in factories and mass produced, truly the objects of today.” In 1924 Le Corbusier designed a small house for his parents at Vevey on Lake Geneva in 1924. It was an opportunity to demonstrate his newly formulated principles and he achieved three of his five points: the free plan, the ribbon window, and the roof terrace. Concerns his parents might have about inhabiting a “machine for living in” were ameliorated by traditional wooden furniture. ## Early Work In Paris, in 1925, Le Corbusier, supported by his cousin, Pierre Jeanneret (1896–1967), designed the Pavillon de l’Esprit Nouveau for the Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes (International Exhibition of Decorative Arts and Modern Industries). Sponsored by the French government, it was a showcase for the exhibition of interior design, objects, and rooms in the new post-World War I style. Le Corbusier, however, designed the pavilion to demonstrate his architectural response to the “machine age” that was to last from approximately 1918 to 1939 and during which the new machines, cars, airplanes, radios, and cinemas reshaped public and private lives, and held a romantic fascination for modern artists in all disciplines. Ironically, a lot of the pavilion’s success depended on an existing tree on the plot, which the cousins were required to retain. They built around it and it emerged through a hole in the floor of the terrace, which was contained within the pavilion’s cuboid volume, and protruded through a circle cut in its roof (17.3). ![](images/5b7e62db06c5c016ab9c44434ed1dce0d607d54c393f2463a29ace6722d501cf.jpg) Le Corbusier, Pavillon de l’Esprit Nouveau, Paris, 1925. At the beginning of his career, Le Corbusier had ambitions to be a painter and, in the prototype dwelling that was the pavilion, the artist manqué, attempted to incorporate the glamor of an artist’s studio into a family home: the living area was a double-height space with a fully glazed external wall, the kitchen and dining area were beneath but open to the bedroom, which looked over a balcony to the living area below. The furniture included Thonet chairs (see here) and simple bourgeois armchairs. Metal-framed furniture, with painted and glass panels, replaced traditional free-standing wooden pieces. Purist paintings hung on plain white walls, rugs were hand-crafted Berber weavings from North Africa, laboratory glassware served as vases, and stones and shells, mass-produced by nature, were the only ornamental accessories (17.2). ![](images/71447c8e22c58e914f9df520386bd339cf8f567258453da06b25018049a07600.jpg) Le Corbusier and Pierre Jeanneret, Pavillon de l’Esprit Nouveau, Paris, 1925. The double-height living area with the kitchen and dining area, behind a built-in storage unit and below the bedroom. The pavilion was decidedly out of step with both the intentions of the exhibition organizers and popular taste. The public preferred the materials and techniques displayed in the highly crafted decorative pieces in what was an identifiable emergent style that the exhibition was intended to showcase. For Le Corbusier, rooms needed only simple factory-made chairs and tables, created by scientific analysis of how the human body, free from outmoded social conventions, would disport itself. They would be made with the precision that only metalworking processes could achieve. Objectivity fell victim to Le Corbusier’s aesthetic instincts. His chaise longue of 1929 is elegant but its form determines that a user must bend their knee lying on one’s back is possible, lying on one’s side is impossible (17.4). The flat surface of the Victorian chaise offered more options. Le Corbusier’s Grand Confort armchair (1928–30), which he created with Charlotte Perriand (see here), is visually sumptuous but the aesthetic that required backrest and arms to be level did not allow the languid sprawling of the club armchair that had inspired it. In pursuit of anthropometric perfection, Le Corbusier later created his Modular Man for whom data was determined by a male figure, based on the heroes of the English crime novels Le Corbusier enjoyed who were invariably 6 feet (183 cm) tall. In 1950, the average Frenchman was 5 feet 7 inches (170 cm). ![](images/7b439df475f9a0df6a1a93f8289b193b1a392b8e562ac041959fe249c0c08002.jpg) 17.4 Le Corbusier and Charlotte Perriand, chaise longue, 1929. This modernist version has an upper structure that slides on the base to permit the seating surface to be placed at an angle to suit the user. Further evidence of Le Corbusier’s interiors may be deduced from his Villa Savoye in Poissy, near Paris (1929–31), which is generally accepted as the best and most influential of his early houses and a clear application of his five rules. The ground floor, containing an entrance hall, a garage, and service rooms is set back behind pilotis and painted dark green to minimize its presence (17.6). A ramp and a spiral stair lead up to a living and dining area that stretches the length of the building with a floor-to-ceiling window looking on to an open terrace (17.7). The link between the interior and the exterior is crucial. An unglazed ribbon window on the terrace offers views of the surrounding country. The flat roof garden is punctuated by curved concrete walls (17.5). In keeping with his Purism, Le Corbusier used blocks of flat color in the interiors. Walls were bright blue and orange, the floor was square yellow tiles laid on the diagonal. There was no door between the master bedroom and bathroom, which has a blue–gray mosaic tiled sunken tub and a built-in chaise longue. ![](images/33e5306a8e6cddbcac39e1cc6d90a56dfef474fea11dfe61199382b04a804fe8.jpg) 17.5 and 17.6 Le Corbusier and Pierre Jeanneret, Villa Savoye, Poissy, France, 1929–31. Neither are the most efficient or functional way of moving between floors, and are perhaps evidence of Le Corbusier’s need to break away from the orthogonal emphasis in modernist work. ## INSIGHTS ## Le Corbusier and Color It is often assumed that Le Corbusier’s interiors were white in the name of modernist reductivism. In reality, he used color extensively and daringly, presumably drawing on his painterly instincts. Even his exteriors were not always plain white boxes, a misinterpretation derived from their publication in black and white photographs. He was commissioned to design workers’ houses in 1926 in Pessac, Bordeaux. In keeping with his Purism, they were painted yellow, blue, pale green, or dark maroon to underline the elements of their composition. He used similar colors in his interiors, where often one wall would be painted to contrast with other lighter, often white, walls. In fact, modernists were not against color and accepted rules for its “correct” use. Le Corbusier and Amédée Ozenfant prescribed a Purist palette of yellow ocher, red, brown, white, black, ultramarine, and mixes of those basics. Le Corbusier, always happy to set down rules, produced two editions of Polychromie Architecturale (Architectural Polychromy) in 1931 and 1959 for the Swiss wallpaper manufacturer Salubra. The first edition comprised forty-three shades in fourteen series of solid colors and gradated tones, all of which were gentle and derived from nature. The second added twenty more intense colors. He considered that different hues and combinations of hues could affect perceptions of rooms and the moods of those in them. Alfred Roth, a Swiss architect who joined Le Corbusier’s studio in 1929 (see here), talked about how painting the walls of a room could unify it or define areas within it. ## Perriand As his reputation quickly grew, many young and radical architects worked in Le Corbusier’s studio. Yet—apart from his cousin Pierre Jeanneret—Charlotte Perriand (1903–99) was arguably Le Corbusier’s most important collaborator perhaps because she had skills and an imagination that complemented his. Perriand was born in Paris and studied furniture design from 1920 to 1925 at the Ecole de l’Union Centrale des Arts Décoratifs (The School of the Central Union of Decorative Arts) with Henri Rapin (1873–1939) and Maurice Dufrêne, (1876–1955), both of whom were interior designers and early exponents of Style Moderne (see here). In 1927, for the Salon d’Automne (Fall Salon) exhibition in Paris, she created the Bar sous le Toit (Bar under the Roof) using generous amounts of aluminum and nickel-plated steel, leather cushions, and glass shelves. She was interested in machine production and low-cost housing and, inspired by Le Corbusier’s writings, applied to work with him in 1927. He turned her down, explaining that his studio did not embroider cushions. Yet when he visited Bar sous le Toit he was impressed and, soon after, offered her a job designing furniture. She was also responsible for interiors and exhibitions to promote Le Corbusier’s ideas. In 1928, when Le Corbusier proposed three chairs—the B301 sling-back armchair for conversation, the LC2 Grand Confort armchair for relaxation, and the B306 chaise longue for sleeping—Perriand developed all three with tubular steel frames. They became the quintessence of modernist seating and, at the time, were exclusively attributed to Le Corbusier. Perriand said that she and Le Corbusier worked harmoniously, and in 1934 she designed furniture and fixtures for his new apartment in Paris. She left the Le Corbusier/Jeanneret studio to work with the painter Fernand Léger (1881– 1955) on a pavilion for the Exposition Internationale (Universal Exhibition) in Paris in 1937 and a ski resort in the Savoie region of France where her grandparents lived and to which she often retreated. She became involved with leftist groups and decided to work for a less elitist audience, switching from expensive chromed steel and machine production to wood and hand production. Her designs were often inspired by Savoyard vernacular models. In 1939 she returned to Paris to design prefabricated buildings with Jean Prouvé (1901–84). When Germany occupied France in 1940, Perriand moved to Japan as a government advisor on industrial design. When Japan attacked the United States in 1941, she tried to get back to France but was marooned in the French colony of Vietnam. There, she experimented with woodwork and weaving, absorbing Asian ideas about design. She returned to France and established her independent career while continuing to collaborate with Prouvé. In 1950 she designed a prototypical kitchen for Le Corbusier’s Unité d’Habitation, a social-housing block in Marseilles. She worked independently on a succession of interior projects, notably tourism offices for the French government, and offices for Air France in London, Paris, and Tokyo (17.8). In 1962 she designed a series of large ski lodges at Les Arcs in Savoie, all incorporating her ideas about prefabrication and standardization. When Perriand returned to France after World War II, she had gone some way to becoming a national institution and was a natural choice for a prestige project with the national airline. ## Gropius and the Bauhaus Modernist iconoclasm acquired its own academy with the foundation of the Bauhaus (Building House) in Weimar, Germany, in 1919 under the leadership of Walter Gropius (1883–1969). It rejected boundaries between art and design and treated design as a single discipline, supposedly common to all materials and techniques. Its regard for machine production distanced it from the Arts and Crafts movement but shared its commitment to Gesamtkunstwerk, the total work of art —the idea that a building, its interior and all the elements in it should have aesthetic coherence— to be successful as a single object. Gropius’ directorial responsibilities embraced both architecture and the decorative arts, and he was obliged to think beyond architectural polemics. In his book Neue Arbeiten der Bauhauswerkstätten (1925), published in English as The New Architecture and the Bauhaus, he suggested that the first stage of modern architecture should be a “purifying process” with the ultimate goal of creating a “composite but inseparable work of art— the great building—in which the old dividing line between monumental and decorative elements will have disappeared forever.” The disciplinary net of the Bauhaus net was cast wide and included photography and film. Learning began with a year devoted to two- and three-dimensional abstract design, studies of materials, textures and colors, all intended to be the basis for specialist study. Gropius recruited an impressive staff that included Paul Klee (1879–1940) who was Swiss, Wassily Kandinsky (1866– 1944) a Russian, Josef Albers (1888–1976) a German, László Moholy-Nagy (1895–1946) and Marcel Breuer (1902–81) who were both Hungarian. Female staff members were few. It was Gropius opinion that men thought in three dimensions while women could manage only two and, even then, they were not encouraged to pursue the lofty practice of painting but were steered toward activities associated with domesticity, such as printmaking and weaving. Anni Albers (1899– 1994), married to Josef, worked in textiles and printmaking, crossing the lines between art and craft. Gertrud Arndt (1903–2000)was married to Alfred Arndt (1896–1976), who briefly taught interior design, geometric drawing, and perspective, but made her own reputation as a photographer. Metalsmith Marianne Brandt (1893–1983) became head of the metal workshop in 1927 although she specialized in domestic objects. In 2007 one of her teapots achieved the highest ever auction price for a Bauhaus object. The small and sculptural teapot is unconventional and intended to distil a concentrated extract, which, when combined with hot water in the cup, can produce tea of any desired strength. Gropius’ office was a study in rectilinear geometry and typical of a Bauhausian interior (17.9). Furniture, textiles, and light fixtures were designed by Bauhaus teachers and students. An armchair, by Gropius, is shaped more by geometry than anthropometrics. The color scheme was white and gray, with primary accents suggesting the influence of De Stijl. Blocks of color were applied to walls and ceiling in unexpected places. A textile, the height and width of a door, rose from floor level. ![](images/c6181b548b751c09386ae96bb32630a14b13cae64ce9f735bf30c0fd49005afb.jpg) Walter Gropius, Bauhaus, Weimar, Germany, 1923. Gropius designed his own office. The rug, tapestry, light fixture, and some furniture are by Bauhaus faculty or students. Gropius designed the armchair and desk. Economic and political problems in 1925 led to the closure of the school in Weimar and its relocation to the industrial city of Dessau, to be housed in buildings designed by Gropius and conforming to modernist principles. A workshop block with a glass curtain wall allowed students to produce prototypes. A bridge over a public street contained a library and offices, with a link to the classroom block and studio bedrooms for advanced students, each with a tiny balcony to enliven the otherwise flat façade. The school attracted political and artistic opposition from the growing Nazi Party. In 1928 Gropius resigned and was succeeded by Swiss architect Hannes Meyer (1889–1954), whose communist sympathies proved to be unacceptable and in 1930 he was sacked. The school was closed in 1933 but Ludwig Mies van der Rohe relocated the institution to Berlin, transformed it into a private school, and banned followers of Meyer from teaching or studying there. He had invested his own money in the bowdlerized institution and in 1934 was the only member of staff to sign a letter supporting the Nazis. Mies left Germany in 1938 when it became clear that Adolf Hitler preferred the faithful replication of architectural classicism of German architect Albert Speer (1905–81) to Mies’s own abstracted re-interpretation. Mies settled in the United States as head of the architecture at Chicago’s Armour Institute of Technology, which later became the Illinois Institute of Technology. Gropius was already professor of architecture at Harvard University, and thus European modernism took control of American architectural education. Socialist idealists among Bauhaus staff went east to the Soviet Union where they found that Joseph Stalin was no more appreciative of their ideas than Hitler. ## Mies van der Rohe Ludwig Mies van der Rohe (1886–1969) was born in Aachen, Germany. He moved to Berlin to work with Bruno Paul (1874–1968), a German architect, furniture and interior designer who principally designed ocean liners. Paul was a founder member of the Deutscher Werkbund (German Work Federation) association of artists, architects, designers, and industrialists, which was created in 1907 to promote German design abroad. Mies next joined Behrens’s office in Berlin in 1908 before leaving in 1912 to set up his own practice. His early work, particularly an unbuilt proposal for a museum in the Netherlands, suggests the influence of German Neoclassicism with something of Wright’s Prairie houses in its long low, asymmetrical massing. Its flat roofs may be early explorations of that modernist essential. After World War I, he experimented with options for cladding multistory buildings with glass curtain walls or horizontal bands of concrete and glass. These became templates for his office buildings of the 1950s and 1960s in the United States and Europe. While Le Corbusier offered prototypes for social housing, Mies speculated about the future of office building. The pavilion is an archetype of Miesian open-plan design. The roof is supported by steel columns in place of conventional load-bearing walls. Non-load-bearing walls of glass and marble establish discrete areas without formal enclosures. ![](images/ad974c0a38b81e18f7962cf98f26cd9f59cda47b02f15b24fc094b1cdaa83d85.jpg) Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, German Pavilion, International Exhibition, Barcelona, 1929. Classical restraint would be clear in two buildings that made Mies’ reputation. The first, the German Pavilion (also known as the Barcelona Pavilion) at the International Exhibition in Barcelona in 1929, made Germany’s case for being the pacesetter of modernism (17.1 and 17.10). Cantilevers of thin roof slab, supported on free-standing chromed steel columns, partly cover walls of marble and glass that sit on a travertine limestone platform in which two pools reflect the green and red marble walls and a scarlet curtain (17.11). The structure has no definitive enclosure but implies functional areas, in which chairs and stools, designed by Mies and Lilly Reich, suggest how it might serve as a dwelling. ![](images/025085d5cc2c8c714acc6c0bad55e2312eb7f5c9747dd544cf6b5fcfbaa1b0ee.jpg) 17.11 Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, German Pavilion, International Exhibition, Barcelona, 1929. The open space, which had no identified rooms but screen walls of glass and marble to define spaces, has been a key influence on modern ideas of interior planning. Floor-to-ceiling glass walls subdivide the floor and slim cruciform columns support the roof. Materials include green and red marbles, travertine, and green glass The Tugendhat House, completed in 1930 in Brno in what is now the Czech Republic, takes the German Pavilion formula of isolated walls and columns loosely defining functional areas (17.12). Mies made his reputation in the United States in rural Illinois with the Farnsworth House (1945–51) in Plano, Illinois (17.15). Dr. Edith Farnsworth wanted a weekend retreat on a wooded site of 9 acres (3.6 ha) by the Fox River and Mies chose to locate it on the flood plain. There is an argument for riparian sites but raising the floor 5 feet (1.5 m) above ground level was perhaps more to justify a concept in which the floor and roof slabs, separated by walls of floor-to-ceiling glass on all four sides, appear to float. Both slabs, of identical dimensions, are supported on eight steel columns. A third of the floor area is outdoor deck. The columns and edges of roof and floor are painted white. The interior is subdivided only by a central utilities block, with the living room at the deck end, the bedroom at the other. There are two identical bathrooms at either end of the utilities block, the result perhaps of Mies’s love of symmetry. Furniture (all designed by Mies and Reich) was deployed sparingly. Mies had completed the design by 1947, in time for an exhibition of his work at the Museum of Modern Art in New York. Philip Johnson (1906–2005), the museum’s director of architecture and a devotee of Mies, cloned the essence of the Farnsworth for his own home in rura Connecticut—and stole a march by completing it in 1949 (17.14). ![](images/fac404c3bad5fab99540348deb80841b2b215491b3d022ce5b6b952525c37186.jpg) 17.14 Philip Johnson, Glass House, New Canaan, Connecticut, 1949. Johnson went on to work with Mies, supervising construction of Mies’s tower, the Seagram Building in New York. Completed in 1958 it is considered to be the quintessence of the modern office block. Johnson also designed the interiors of the Four Seasons restaurant in the building, which consists of two rooms, each 60 × 90 feet (18 × 27.5 m) and 20 feet (6 m) high and free of structural columns. Both had glass walls in bronze frames. Curtains of metal chains, designed by Marie Nichols, wafted in the breeze from concealed ventilating ducts. Travertine marble walls and floors matched those in the Seagram’s lobby and were augmented by walnut, rawhide and carpet paneling. In the Pool Room, tables were arranged around a 20-feet (1.8-m) square white marble pool, with fig trees in enormous planters at each corner (17.16). Off-white aluminum panels, with recessed lighting sources suggested a coffered ceiling. In the Grill Room, the bar and lounge were separated by a walnut desk, and are overlooked by mezzanine dining rooms. He embraced the North American brand of postmodernism to deliver the AT&T headquarters building in New York in 1984 with its notorious “broken pediment” top. ## Reich Mies’s frequent collaborator Lilly Reich (1885–1947) was born in Berlin. She began as a designer of textiles and women’s clothes. In 1908 she worked in Josef Hoffmann’s studio in Vienna but, in 1911, returned to Berlin to design furniture and clothes, supplementing her income by decorating store windows. She joined the Deutscher Werkbund. In 1913 her prototype for a working-class apartment in the Berlin Gewerkschaftshaus (Trade Union House) was widely praised for its efficiency and she went on to exhibit at the Werkbund exhibition in Cologne the following year. In 1920 she became the first woman to be elected to the Werkbund board and, in 1921, organized two exhibitions at the Newark Museum in New Jersey on its behalf; the first promoted women’s fashion from Germany and the second, with more than 1,600 objects, displayed the range and quality of German industrial production. From 1924 to 1928 she worked in Frankfurt, organizing and designing trade fairs. There she met Mies, who was then vice president of the Werkbund, and they collaborated to design furniture and, it is thought, became lovers. In 1929 she was appointed artistic director for the German contribution to the International Exhibition in Barcelona. They collaborated to design the Barcelona chair (17.17), which became ubiquitous in corporate office reception areas throughout the twentieth century. In 1932 Mies asked her to teach interior and furniture design at the Bauhaus, a short-lived appointment since the Nazis closed it a year later. In 1937 her installation at the Paris World’s Fair was included in Speer’s Nazi Pavilion. ![](images/f2c8858ee1cc357e91919188b7c99d727b0f0971885395db406a1bdd03e080a9.jpg) ## 17.17 Ludwig Mies van der Rohe and Lilly Reich, Barcelona chair, 1929. Albert Pfeiffer, former vice president of design and management of the Knoll furniture company, which manufactures the chairs credited to Mies, has said that Mies did not fully develop any contemporary furniture successfully before or after his collaboration with Reich. ## Skeptics There were those who questioned the integrity of early modernism. In 1928 Ozenfant, Mies’s confederate in the cause of Purism, published Fondations de L’Art Moderne (Foundations of Modern Art) in which he criticized architects, saying, “Many, and not the least, invent feigned utilities for the pleasure of solving the problems thus raised, as witness those balconies for urban haranguings.” He may have had his old collaborator in mind—an early Le Corbusier sketch showed inhabitants of an apartment block addressing their neighbors from a little balcony. The architectural instinct is to aestheticize, and balconies create patterns of light and shade on flat façades—as did traditional ornament on a more delicate scale. Not all architects were in thrall to Le Corbusier’s work. Auguste Perret (1974–54) criticized Le Corbusier’s buildings for impracticality, pointing out that external walls needed projections to prevent staining and erosion and that ribbon windows allowed too much light into rooms and reduced interior-design options. Traditional tall thin windows, he said, gave options for making an interior. He suggested that modernists treated the whole building as a single ornamental object but, because there was no applied decoration, it was presumed to be rational. Perret was a pioneer in the use of reinforced concrete. He left his course at the Ecole des Beaux-Arts in Paris prematurely to establish Perret Frères with his brother Gustave (1876–1952) and they became the leading exponents of designing in concrete. Le Corbusier worked in the Perrets’ office in Paris, during 1908 and 1909, to learn the rudiments of using concrete, which helped shape the theory of his Domino House. Between 1911 and 1913 the Perrets amended and built Henri van de Velde’s design for the Théâtre des Champs-Elysées in Paris. Van de Velde recognized that they knew more than he about concrete and stepped aside. Auguste composed formal orthogonal elevations with inset bas-relief panels. The building lost van de Velde’s Art Nouveau elements but is considered the first example of Art Deco in the city. It is easy to believe that Perret retained some of his Beaux-Arts training as he edited those principles to suit concrete. The same compositional formality is clear in the elevation of the Salle Cortot concert hall (1928–9) and the grand stairs of the Palais d’Iéna (1934) where moldings show Perret’s willingness to work with conventional, albeit simplified, decorative devices. His church of Notre Dame at Le Raincy (1922–4) has the height, the columns, the vaulted roof, and the stained glass of a medieval cathedral but is clearly modern (17.18). The thin, concrete columns allow the non-load-bearing external walls to be devoted entirely to stained glass, held in thin, concrete screens rather than bulky stone traceries. The impact of the church at Raincy brought the Perrets a succession of ecclesiastical projects and they completed three small chapels experimenting with different aggregates and color dyes added to the concrete mix. Chapelle Perret (1927–9), was built for a girls’ orphanage on a tiny budget in Arcueil in the suburbs of Paris. The plan and elevations are symmetrical. An exposed concrete frame supports infill walls of hollow, unplastered red blocks. The concrete columns and beams have a blueish tint that may be the result of dye or daylight shining through blue glass in the concrete screens. A small concrete boss hangs at the center of each beam suggesting a refusal to discard traditional decorative devices. Chapelle de la Colombière, for a school in Chalon-sur-Saône (1928–9; 17.20) in eastern France, is again defined by thin, concrete columns that separate a nave from side aisles but here rise to create a clerestory. Huré designed the stained glass and his brother Gustave designed the furniture. Chapelle des Capucins in Tours, completed in 1930 is a compendium of their interior experiments: the striated columns support a shallow vaulted roof, blue glass is set in geometric concrete screens, and the same geometry is used in the timber screens that separate secondary rooms. The wooden-parquet flooring pattern is in tune with that of internal brickwork. ![](images/d90dd743776069ab617fa331bcaeebb2d7928cebd75184f48d8a7065e361702f.jpg) 17.20 Auguste Perret, Chapelle de la Colombière, Chalon-sur-Saône, France, 1928–9. Medieval masonry technology could build spires high but their surfaces were necessarily smooth to repel water. The tower of St. Joseph was heavily modeled externally but even more so internally and concrete technology allowed the spectacle of its height and construction to be visible from within the church. French designers who chose not to follow Le Corbusier’s directions were producing furniture, lamps, textiles, and accessories with clear stylistic similarities. They used lacquer, glass, aluminum, sharply angled cubistic forms, and zigzag motifs that were seen to be symbols of the contemporary world, inspired by new technological and social phenomena. Such items were for the rich but they inspired mass manufacturers, who supplied affordable versions to the wider public. Rich and poor seemed to share tastes. If many of the techniques were traditional, the imagery was not; it was of its time, modern, contemporary and had no reforming agenda, other than an aesthetic one. In 1925 Paul Follot (1877–1941), the French designer of luxury furniture and objets d’art, said: “We know that man is never content with the indispensable and that the superfluous is always needed . . . if not, we would have to get rid of music, flowers, and perfumes.”
