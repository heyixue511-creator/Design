# hong kong

a history of manhua Wendy Siuyi Wong

Enter the world of manhua and enter an exotic world of political satire, kung-fu fighters, and sexy teenage animé. With more than 1,000 illustrations, Hong Kong Comics is the first overview of the colorful, immediate, and always-captivating universe of Chinese manhua and Japanese manga—broad terms that include cartoons, comics, and picture story books. The growing interest in these fascinating illustrations comes both from a desire for increasingly exotic forms of entertainment and from an ever-increasing curiosity about the artistic and cultural traditions of their originating countries. In this lavishly illustrated tome, Wendy Siuyi Wong traces the art form of manhua from its beginnings in the early twentieth century to its most influential contemporary practitioners and, in the process, reveals the development of a unique Hong Kong style. She examines a wide variety of comics (each with an English annotation), from satirical political cartoons to lighthearted funny papers, from children's stories to action-filled martial art works, bringing to the reader—whether illustrator or comic book fan—an encyclopedic look at this undiscovered and rich world of graphic art.

After the Second World War, comic books became one of the most popular reading materials in Hong Kong. Comic books were considered low-brow entertainment for the enjoyment of the working class and children, and its art was thought to be insignificant. However, as comic books increased in popularity and commercial value, they became an important money-making industry. Although the Hong Kong comics market is primarily influenced by Japanese manga and animation, it has developed into its own, individual style. Hong Kong comics has a distinct drawing style, storytelling format, and cultural identity.

There are more than forty locally produced titles (excluding Japanese comics) published on a regular basis and sold to the six million people of Hong Kong. Manhua, the local term for “comics," comes in many forms: cartoons, caricatures, political and editorial satire, comics, graphic novels, and lianhuantu (Chinese-style action comics). It reflects the social and cultural changes of Hong Kong life and is an expression of Hong Kong identity. This book, the first of its kind on Chinese comics, aims to facilitate Western culture's understanding of contemporary Chinese popular culture in Hong Kong. Most of the images displayed have never been published in the West, in presenting them here, the hope is to promote Hong Kong comics to the English-speaking world.

The history of Chinese conic arts has not yet been fully explored, and if the visual language of comic arts, cartoons, illustration, and animation is universal, this book enhances the complete history of these art forms. Throughout the comics found in this book, an intermingling of influences— European, American, Japanese—combine with Chinese heritage to produce the Hong Kong-style manhua. Although Hong Kong manhua preserved a unique cultural identity, readers may find visual images that are familiar. Yet they will also be introduced to subjects that are not found in the Western social and cultural setting.

This book was originally published in Chinese for a Hong Kong audience. Funding was provided by the Hong Kong Arts Development Council. This version contains three sections: Introduction, Annotated Captions, and Appendices. The introductory essay is a survey of the development of Hong Kong comics through the decades and includes a brief background of the history of Chinese comics. Four genres are represented in the annotated captions: satirical and political manhua, comical manhua, action manhua, and children's manhua. They include approximately

800 illustrations from Hong Kong as well as titles taken from the history of Chinese comics. The appendices include selected artist biographies, reference sources, and a translation conversion index.

Because no definitive work on Hong Kong comics has been done in English, the title translations are primarily the author's but include the established version given by the existing title. The Chinese name convention, in which the family name precedes the given name, is used throughout the book based on the romanization of Cantonese, the Chinese dialect spoken in Hong Kong. The English names of the artists are used when they have already been established. The translation conversion index at the end of the book provides a reference for readers with knowledge of both English and Chinese.
