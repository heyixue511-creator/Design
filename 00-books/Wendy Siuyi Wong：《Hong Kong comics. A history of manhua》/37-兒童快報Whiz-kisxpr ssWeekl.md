## Children's Daily, 1989

Children’s Daily was the first tabloid-size daily newspaper for children. Its eight full-color pages included news, comics, and stories on sports, entertainment, science, and literature. The newspaper had great content in early issues and quickly achieved a circulation of 20,000 to 30,000. However, after a series of personnel changes, including the hiring of a new editor, the content and illustrations became less sophisticated, and the paper became less popular. It ceased publication in 1991.

## Little Ming Pao Weekly, 1989

Since this publication was a supplement to Ming Pao Weekly, an entertainment magazine that came out every Sunday, it did not have to be as commercial as standalone comics. As a result, it tried out new artistic and editorial approaches, which were well received by its readers. Illustrators included recent design school graduates, who explored new techniques in their weekly stories. These young artists included Kwok Siuming, Mak Ga-bik, Siu Shek, and Wong Ging-tsai. The weekly’s most popular character was the adorable pig McMug, drawn by Mak Ga-bik with a script by Tse Lap-man. McMug later moved to the magazine Yellow Bus.

## City Children's Weekly, 1990

The cover and contents of this weekly comic for children were drawn from Japanese manga. Appearances by the popular Hong Kong film character Shum Kinfan gave the title a celebrity boost. Each issue also included an educational supplement provided by a schoolbook publisher.

## Whiz-Kids Express Weekly, 1993

This magazine began as a Sunday supplement to Express Daily and later became an individual magazine. The cover used Japanese manga to attract readers, although the contents included Hong Kong comics as well as educational stories.

Yellow Bus, 1996

![](images/8b5a7f1b41ad7975fee417cf78d34cc9fab26ff4256fc56e16c6dfa6d634e7f5.jpg)

Yellow Bus first appeared as a supplement to Sunday Weekly, but when that newspaper folded, the illustrated magazine's contents were adapted to a new audience. The new target audience was children accustomed to watching TV and playing video games, although the lively content and computer-generated illustrations also attracted young adult readers. Yellow Bus had other attractions, too. Readers could send in their photos and they would be morphed into fantastic images with animals or flowers. The magazine also often included stickers, badges, notebooks, erasers, and red packets for Chinese New Year.
