## The Journal of Current Pictorial, 1905

## Art: Various artists

First published in Guangzhou, this magazine was part of the first generation of Chinese cartoon periodicals and thus played an influential role in the development of manhua. Published three times a month, it contained simple line drawings accompanied by text. (It also published poetry, short stories, and commentaries on current events.) Editorially, the publication was aimed at informing the general public of events and politics in China and promoting revolutionary anti-Qing ideas. The staff and many of its artists, including Pan Da-wei, Gao Jian-fu, He Jian-shi, Xie Ying-bo, and Chen Heng, belonged to the revolutionary group Chinese Alliance, which criticized Chinese feudal society, exposed the corruption of the Qing government, and decried China's weakness in the face of foreign powers. The Qing government banned the magazine in 1907; production resumed in Hong Kong for a few months the next year, but it was banned there, too, because Qing officials pressured the British colonial government. The artists then returned to Guangzhou, where they used pseudonyms to produce critical work such as Current Jokes Pictorial (Shi-xie Hua-bao) and People Pictorial (Ping-wen Hua-bao).

## The True Record, 1912

Art: Various artists

This illustrated magazine was published in Guangzhou and distributed in Shanghai. Under the editorial guidance of Gao Qi-feng, The True Record used pictures and text to monitor the policies and politics of the government of the Republic of China, which had been established in 1911. It also reported current events and local news. The magazine's political drawings focused particularly on the power struggles and bureaucratic ineffectiveness of the Republic. In The Satirical Drawing, for example, the artist Zheng Nuquan implied that the new government, as led by provisional president Yuan Shi-kai, was incompetent. In another example, The Reasons for the Weakness of China, Ma Xing-tsi depicts China as a weak bureaucracy. In 1913, the government banned The True Record, and the artists and editor were forced to flee the country.
