Bertel Gardberg, Canton stainless steel and teak pan for Hackman, 1957 Bertel Gardberg, silver and teak chocolate pot (handmade), 1955 ![](images/a293477d0693aa8dff156cac59559fdb55482af388435101826a595c42b25e67.jpg) # bertel gardberg(Fiih b,118) ![](images/1404af1caaec1db071ffe9d9e7a91c5a2a6b2269986f9550f576c8dfcffb627e.jpg) The Finnish metalware and jewellery designer Bertel Gardberg studied at the Goldsmith's School, Helsinki, before training at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu (Institute of Industrial Arts), Helsinki from 1938 to 1941. In1949 he established his own studio in Helsinki and subsequently designed and produced his own silverware products, such as the cuboid teak and silver tea caddy (1955) and his silver and palisander Mocha service (1957). He also designed objects for industrial production that were manufactured by Fiskars and Hackman, among others. For the latter, his product designs include the stainless steelCarelia cutlery (1957)and the Canton cooking pan (1957) – both of which possess a remarkable aesthetic refinement that testifies to Gardberg's careful attention todetail andskilful handling of materials. The perfectionism and timelessness of products such as these earned Gardberg widespread recognition and numerous awards, including gold and silver medals at the Milan Triennale of 1954 (X), 1957(XI)and 1960(XII), andthe Lunning Prize in 1961. Between 1966 and 1971 Gardberg lived in Dublin, where he was artistic director of the Kilkenny Design Workshop(1966-1968) and technical director of the Rionor company in Kilkenny (1968-1971). During this period, he attempted to revitalize Ireland's decorative arts by promoting the role of craftsmanship in local workshops. After returning to his native Finland, Gardberg established a silver and woodworking studio in Pohja in 1973. There he continued to produce exquisite jewellery and small sculptures set with a combination of precious and semi-precious stones, as well as ecclesiastical silver that was produced by Noormarkun Kotiteollisuus. Gardberg also designed other domestic products for industrial production such as his pewter tobacco jars, which were produced by Hopeatehdas. Known as the father of Finnish Arts & Crafts, Gardberg believed that, “It's not just brains that think, as every craftsman knows. The hands think, too, when they work with various materials. And between the hands and the brain lies the human heart, and love for the work. You have to respect the qualities of the material." Like many other Scandinavian designers, both past and present, Gardberg elegantly demonstrated through the aesthetic and functional integrity of his work that the “crafts are the imagination of industry”. ![](images/0910a5465f896eef12619ad47a64ca428d1c29d3086c6268bd8ccca78e78c9c4.jpg)
