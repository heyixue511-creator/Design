## Casa C Apartment

Location Milan, Italy

Remodelled 2010

Designer Francesco Librizzi

![](images/a369bae94b516ec22ab79dc8f5b590d39ddcb8522f89a89ca672387f2573cca1.jpg)

One of the key components of movement in interior space is the staircase. Its carefully considered balance of proportion and geometry, combined with stringent regulatory requirements and codes, is often the result of design and the combination of four key elements: risers and treads, stringers, and a balustrade. Their combination will lend the space, as well as the user, a particular interior rhythm. Whether off-the-peg or bespoke, lightweight or monumentally heavy, it is an element of interior space that has fascinated designers for a long time. Hence its appearance can undertake myriad forms even when constructed from a few simple elements.

Experiments with this type of vertical movement through a space have often revolved around the complex mix of imagination with the harsh realities of the relevant building regulations. This combination of poetry and pragmatics can vary, depending on the user, and whether in a public or a private space. For instance, the design of stairs in a domestic space can take on a different trajectory as their public duties are relinquished, leaving them freer of some of the more burdensome regulatory requirements of particular measurements of riser, tread and balustrade. Therefore domestic space stairs can sometimes prove to be the elements of movement in the interior where experimentation and imagination can really take fl ight.

Figure 3.89 As the user descends from the mezzanine to the lower level they are neatly framed within the slender lightweight steel stair sculpture.

In a small, 45 square metre apartment in Milan, Francesco Librizzi has experimented with a stair that gives its user access to an upper level mezzanine sleeping space. The intimate proportions of the apartment were contrasted with the high ceilings of the space. A full height slot in the wall, separating the two main rooms of the space, suggested an opportunity to utilise the upper heights to gain more space for the occupant. Therefore a mezzanine, providing a sleeping room, was placed above the kitchen. Access to it provided the next challenge.

The designer developed a slender lightweight vertical steel stair, linking the lower fl oor and the new sleeping level. The proportions and geometry of the stair refl ected the simple confi guration of the space of the apartment. Librizzi pared back the basic elements of the stair; (tread, riser, balustrade, and stringer) to become as economical as was possible in their barest essentials. He then reordered them into a tall and slender steel frame, constructed with a 14mm solid section steel bar. The thin treads were welded to a stringer, one that also diagonally braced the box, connecting to a landing and affording the slender construction some stability as it connected back to the wall. Each tread connected to a side bar that reinforced the other side of the frame. The side bracing carried onto the top of the frame, reinforcing the solidity of the construction and lending some stability, as well as something more reassuring to grab onto, as the user ascends the stairs to the mezzanine level and to the bed.

The stair was fi xed to the fl oor and to the ceiling. This afforded the object some structural stability as it transfered its user from the landing to the mezzanine. When in use, the dynamism of the occupant of the apartment is palpable as they carefully and deliberately ascend or descend the stair. The slender, deliberate and economically rational stair neatly frames the space and the user in a static, thin frame of this adroitly considered sculptural construction.

The practice’s brilliance is to analyse a particular sense of place, sift these traditions, then synthesise a solution that draws on diversity but hangs together naturally. This is possible only with a thorough knowledge of the basic grammar that allows complete architectural sentences to be structured and goes beyond the decorative to the vital circulation decisions that restore coherence to the experience of the building.

– Robert Bevan, Building Design.<sup>46</sup>

Over time, buildings can become illegible. Additions and changes to a place’s working life will often include adjustments with regards to how it is used. These amendments will alter its patterns of occupation – particularly when circulation systems such as stairs, ramps and lifts are added to increase accessibility and the use of an interior space. Other alterations will facilitate updates on the existing, pragmatic concerns such as addressing out of date services, or making buildings more accessible and bringing them into line with changes to building regulations. However alterations change a building, their impact is always upon the overall comprehension of a building. Over a long period of time these changes will impact on the building to such a degree that a series of robust interventions will need to be carried out in order to re-clarify the space, and ensure the building is fi t for purpose. The design and construction of new circulation, including elements that facilitate movement, will often form an intrinsic part of this type of new remodelling.

Caruso St John were appointed as architects for Tate Britain in 2007. The long-term commission involved the development of a masterplan for the whole of the Tate’s Millbank site. The masterplan was conceived as a two-fold approach: it envisaged a major new extension on the adjacent Queen Alexandra’s Military Hospital site, to accommodate a larger proportion of the Tate’s historic, modern and contemporary collection of British art, and it advocated a continuing and robust refurbishment and improvement plan for the existing building.

## Hankai House

Location Japan

Remodelled 2010

Designer Katsuhiro Miyamoto & Associates

![](images/359c84caa6f16de58031fe3df631cba5660350ccb29bcf746bd1ea2df1ca3763.jpg)

The ramp is an instant creator of scenography. It strives towards architectural infi nity. It generates mythical proportion…it directs the view skywards, leaving unknown what is at the end of the road.

– Koolhaas, Elements of Architecture.<sup>47</sup>

Figure 3.92 The junction between the new addition and the old building was left brutally exposed, enhancing the juxtaposition between them.

The Japanese word Hankai can be translated into English in a number of ways. It can be used to describe a partial understanding of something or it is used to depict something that is ‘semi-civilised’. In this project Katsuhiro Miyamoto used the word to illustrate the ‘partial destruction’ of a building, utilising it in order to give the semi-demolished building on the site a title that encapsulated its own demise, whilst at the same time signifying its survival and a possible future new role after the disaster that had befallen it.

The existing building was a traditional 300-year-old wooden gatehouse that was partially destroyed in the Great Hanshin earthquake of 1995. Its ruined status ensured that any changes to the building needed to be robust and at the same time needed to restructure the building in order to halt its collapse and demise. As well as saving the building from collapse the new additions needed to ensure its future survival by protecting it against any further earthquakes. To achieve this, Miyamoto fi rst of all stripped away the extraneous elements that had grown up around the original square building. He did this in order to reinstate the original orthogonal and traditional four-room home.

Figure 3.93 Views from the ramp frame the old building.  
![](images/f163dbb50a9d6df4526c78e6915803085c9762c4fe0d73bd22e94710acd7ede9.jpg)

In order to stabilise the house, and then more importantly to ensure it would survive any future seismic activities, Miyamoto wrapped the old building with a new volume. The construction acted like a ‘collar’, or a beam that rings the old building and reinforces and protects it from any lateral or vertical movement. Whilst acting as a structural device, the new insertion also solved some of the access and servicing requirements that the old building lacked. The new element contained new circulation and services such as bathrooms and storage. On the top level, where it was separated from the original house, it provided a terrace and a private room. Both overlooked the street below. The new wing followed the exterior boundary of the old property and created a new gatehouse, reinstating the original function of the ancient building by creating a new boundary threshold.

Whilst the memories of the Hanshin earthquake may fade, the house contained the effects of the disaster in its reconstructed self. In this project the utilisation of a ramp that wrapped the building, as well as stabilising it, helped to restore a sense of domestic protection, whilst at the same time ensuring that the occupant was aware of the volatile nature of the environment in which they were housed. The new home was once again made habitable by a new structural device containing circulation and services that were nonetheless haunting reminders that a rapid destruction of the spaces in which they feature could take place at any time.

## BAYSIXTY6

Location London, UK

Remodelled 2012

Designer Brinkworth

![](images/4c00905623d6e3cdf76e76780bcb946e6f7242a3eca04775bc93f550dcc67fd7.jpg)

The interior is often designed from the point of view of the occupant, as they traverse its spaces. But movement in the interior does not always have to be considered from the perspective of an occupant being on foot.

Figure 3.95 The cavernous skatepark underneath the huge expanse of the Westway above.

The legendary BAYSIXTY6 Skate Park has occupied a unique location under the Westway fl yover in London since the late 1990s. But whilst the world of skating and the design of parks has since moved on and become more complex and sophisticated in their demands, BAYSIXTY6 did not. In order to ensure that the much-loved park continued to inspire future riders, it needed to change. For Adam Brinkworth, a keen skateboarder, it was a dream job.

Appointed by brand/event agency Urb-Orbis on behalf of Nike, Brinkworth were presented with a unique opportunity to help resurrect one of London’s most iconic skate parks: updating it for a new generation of skaters. Challenged to develop a scheme that encouraged a spirit of inclusion and community, Brinkworth reworked the complete layout of the site to offer the best possible skateable area, whilst improving the amenities offered to enhance the experience for both skaters and nonskaters alike.

Consolidating the new ramps and obstacles to a single area of the park ensured that the expanse of the site could be celebrated, championing the park’s core activity – skating. Importantly, lines of sight were designed to be uninterrupted across the entire park, save for the impressive structure of the Westway supports, maximising the visibility for spectators during competitions and demos and at the same time also ensuring safety for the participants.

Brinkworth worked closely with one of the world’s best timber skate park designers and builders, Skatepark Amsterdam (NDSM), who were tasked by Urb-Orbis to design, develop and build a set of ramps and obstacles. To achieve this, a number of unique features were specifi cally produced, catering for a broad range of skaters across all levels of skill and ability. A hydraulic box allowed the height of one to be adjusted at the push of a button – meaning it could be set to suit any level. Another was a new kicker ramp, directly in front of a viewing window, offering spectators the chance to catch a glimpse of boarders as they fl ew past. And an impressive bowl featuring innovative hexagonal corner panels offered the smoothest and most fl uid ride possible.

To complement the vast improvements in the skateable areas, Brinkworth developed the facilities to include a shop and a canteen that doubled up as an educational space, housed within brightly painted green shipping containers. These were positioned to back onto the ramps with a window framing sightlines down the spine of the skate park making it an ideal shelter for spectators in any weather conditions.

These new facilities were strategically positioned around a central courtyard – a communal zone clad in recycled decking and containing bench seating, for visitors to congregate to socialise or unite as part of organised events. The clean and utilitarian space was intended to be a fl exible venue, and a blank canvas to host events such as premier skate fi lms or exhibitions.

The shop, developed by Brinkworth specifi cally for BAYSIXTY6, mixed concrete wall panels and walnut shelves to offer a sophisticated pallette, not regularly associated with skate shops, further refl ecting Nike’s commitment to elevating the overall perception of the park.

One of the most iconic parts of Brinkworth’s scheme was a graphic approach to lighting throughout the site. The creative yet functional application of fl uorescent tubes followed the contours of the Westway’s dramatic concrete underbelly overhead.

BAYSIXTY6 was transformed into an inclusive and stimulating skate park, animated by the numerous riders who use it daily – ensuring its legacy will continue to inspire generations and attract more and more skaters to the ramps and facilities.

# National Archaeological Museum

Location Siena, Italy

Remodelled 2003

Designer Guido Canali

![](images/1be339f03af5e814437d36513de0dd5aa006c6cbdf07dd03ac3b1afa2ae5b381.jpg)

Movement through interior space can be arranged in order to articulate a particular narrative of a series of objects and spaces. This can often be the case in the design of exhibitions, where storytelling regarding the objects, and sometimes the space they are in, needs to be communicated to the visitor in a very particular manner.

Figure 3.96 Roughhewn, unfi nished scaffold boards were used to articulate the new route through the subterranean spaces.

In the centre of the city of Siena, directly opposite the cathedral, is the Santa Maria Della Scala. It was one of the fi rst hospitals in Europe and until recently one of the three original hospital buildings still caring for the sick and poor. According to legend it was founded in 898 AD, however the fi rst recorded documents mentioning it were in 1090. Since medieval times the building has been a hospital for pilgrims. Over time the complex of buildings has grown, and over the many centuries it has existed it has absorbed adjacent buildings, overfl owing into neighbouring courtyards and interiors. In the past, as it colonised this part of the city, it was even extended underground, carving out tunnels deep into the tufa rock on which the city was built, forming an underground citadel with its own internal street.

In 1992 Canali won the competition to create a new museum of archaeology .The daunting task of raising the fi nances to undertake such a demanding project, and to unpick the labyrinth of the extant building, meant that work did not commence on the project until 1998. The work also involved moving the hospital,

which was still in operation, and developing a particular conservation strategy in line with the installation of the new archaeological museum, art gallery, temporary exhibition spaces, archive, and services such as catering and offi ces that the new museum would require.

Fittingly, the new archaeological museum was arranged amongst the labyrinthine spaces of the underground chambers, tunnels and rooms of the hospital. Canali relied on the heavy, atmospheric qualities of the rooms and tunnels, leaving them more or less untouched. To form the backdrop for the placement of the museum objects he installed subtle yet distinctive lighting. This was in order to ensure that the walls physically conveyed the weight and density of the rock and earth excavated to forge the new subterranean spaces. Canali left them as found, and more importantly, as a testament to the endeavours over the years that the hospital had existed. In amongst the subterranean spaces Canali inserted a fl oating fl oor.

Constructed from simple scaffold boards, rough-hewn and unfi nished, the boards matched the intensity of the surrounding walls, yet they clearly delineate a new sense of purpose for the visitor to proceed through the interior. This clever tactic also acted as a device to house wiring and services, ensuring that the walls were as free as possible from services and able to direct the journeys of the museum-goer as the stories of the objects and the spaces unfolded before them.

## FRAC

Location Dunkirk, France

Former function Prefabrication workshop

New function FRAC (Fonds régionaux d’art contemporain)

Original date 1945   
Remodelled 2013

Designer Lacaton & Vassa

![](images/2be8938621cc407eed1926f87aa8b37de4ac6ef4653f251f6db5af87a30f7fa4.jpg)

Figure 4.2 The ‘twins’’ role as the   
accommodator of the support services of the institution meant that the original AP2 could become a huge fl exible and reprogrammable art space.

FRAC is a network of regional cultural institutions that were formed to collect and display contemporary art from across France. Between them, the 23 regions have acquired over 25,000 works of art that they loan to museums and schools. In recent years an ambitious scheme to develop purpose built facilities across France has resulted in numerous exemplary buildings. The Nord-Pas-de-Calais FRAC in Dunkirk, by Lacaton & Vassal, is the latest project to be realised in this series.

In the UK, Dunkirk, in northern France, is lastingly associated with the evacuation of hundreds of thousands of troops in the early stages of the Second World War. In France it has always been synonymous with shipbuilding, and the port of the town was the centre of the production of, initially, liners and warships, and then oil tankers and car ferries throughout most of the 20th century. This was until 1988 when many of the shipyards were closed. Like so many industrial landscapes, made obsolete by economic change and industrial decline, the port was slowly abandoned, creating a huge 150-hectare derelict site, fi lled with many empty and abandoned industrial buildings. One, the AP2 (the Atelier De Prefabrication No 2), a ship and boat repair and making workshop lovingly referred to by locals as ‘the cathedral’, was an epically proportioned concrete frame shed. As with numerous industrial landscapes made redundant by changes in industrial production throughout the last century, including shipbuilding, the buildings’ locations often proved to be their saviour. The attractive port location ensured two successive regeneration masterplans were developed in order to regenerate the area. The second, a proposal that is currently being realised, ensured that the local council allocated a €15 million budget to an architectural competition with which to develop a space into a new FRAC. The competition, won by Lacaton & Vassal, proposed to reuse the AP2 to accommodate a new contemporary art centre, one that would become the centre of the regeneration and initiate the slow return to prosperity for the area, a situation not seen since the industrious shipbuilding days.

## Analysis

We’re always very curious about what we are going to fi nd. We think that there is a lot of potential in what already exists. Every existing situation has its own special quality, and you have to take your time and be curious in order to understand it…I think its very important today to take the existing as a starting point – this includes existing buildings and the existing atmosphere.

– Interview with Lacaton & Vassal by Mathieu Wellner. <sup>48</sup>

The designers analysed the physical elements of the building as well as understanding and giving equal importance to the atmospheric conditions of the extant building spaces. The existing AP2 that the designers found on the site was a huge open volume, with an inside space with dimensions of 75 by 25 metres and over 30 metres high, a volume in all totalling approximately 11,200 square metres. The hall itself was almost exactly half of the volume of the Tate Modern Turbine Hall: the impressive reuse of an existing industrial building across the channel in London that this project may draw comparison with (see rereadings volume 1). Rather than the heavy brick carapace of the old London power station, here the designers were gifted a large concrete frame to work with: a fl exible form and structure, one that provided a space that allowed large sections of boats to be constructed, as well as a space made more fl exible by the absence of any internal columns in the large unbroken volume of the hall.

![](images/816f220dbbcdc1a8f81445c31732641bb53ee72ecd30e0d0cbd536b510e6d900.jpg)

The light and acoustic properties of the huge hall were also considered impressive and tangible elements to be reused in the design processes of reworking the existing building. The impression of the qualities of the existing spaces upon the designers was exceptional, and it shaped their proposal, which, amongst the fi ve competition entries submitted, was the only one that did not place a new structure within the existing hall. Instead, through an analysis of the qualities of the existing space, the designers decided to leave the shed untouched, and use it as a space that could be interpreted in a number of ways: primarily through its use as an epically proportioned arts space. Instead of potentially diminishing the space through fi lling it in, the designers proposed a new building, created in adjacency to the shed that would contain the support spaces that FRAC required, such as offi ces, cafés and storage, as well as new gallery spaces. This strategy would then allow the main shed to remain relatively untouched and maintain its open and fl exible volumes to display art. This proposal was costed at a price of €1 million, signifi cantly cheaper than the budget that was set for the competition. It was their analysis of the existing form and structure of the building, derived from its form use as a ship-fabrication space that ensured the designers not only wanted to retain the form of the host, but to replicate it. Through the development of an adjacent doppelgänger shed, they created a large, open, fl exible arts space, and one that could become a signifi cant cultural destination and provide an opportunity to regenerate the area.

## Strategy Insertion

To achieve this concept, the project creates a double of AP2 (Atelier de Prefabrication), of the same dimensions, attached to the existing building, on the side which faces the sea, which contains the program of FRAC. The new building juxtaposes delicately without competing or fading. The duplication is an attentive response to the identity of the old building.

– Lacaton & Vassal <sup>49</sup>

To copy something is both a type of compliment as well as a tacit form of approval for the qualities of something already existing. An approach based on any form of imitation will of course involve the translation of the material of the existing, or original, to be replicated. But fi rst, it requires a thorough analysis of the qualities and matter of the exemplar from which the new is to be formed. From their analysis of the existing building, and through a thorough understanding of the new programme that was to be developed in the space, the designers inserted a new building directly adjacent to the existing shed. This new-build, placed alongside AP2, adapted the exact form and measurements of the existing creating a clone of the original space. This doppelgänger approach to constructing the new museum allowed the designers to utilise effi cient and economic construction technologies, to create a new perfectly formed copy of the concrete frame exemplar, whilst leaving the host relatively untouched. This approach created a huge monumental un-programmed space, within which a diverse range of exhibitions and events could take place. For the new AP2, a concrete frame structure was erected, clad with a lightweight polycarbonate sheet, akin to a greenhouse structure. This structure

## Tactics

The robust nature of the existing AP2 ensured that the designers needed to choose a particular set of tactics to create a powerful visual language for the new building. These were required in order to ensure that it did not disappear against its ‘tougher’, more established and resilient neighbour. Also the designers – who had to work hard to convince the clients that the insertion of a new-build project alongside the old was, as proposed, economically achievable – needed to fi nd construction as well as material solutions that were costeffective and feasible. This led to a consistent ‘light’ touch approach, combined with an off-the-peg aesthetic, where already existing technologies could be utilised for the rehabilitation of the old and the fabrication of the new.

## Surface

The monumental scale of the original AP2 was complemented by a straightforward approach to the existing surfaces of the space. The drama of the raw space was maintained by leaving the surfaces of the room untouched. The concrete frame of the shed was left untouched, the fl oor cleaned and repaired in places but also left, and any scratches, marks and abrasions were retained in order to communicate the

previous life of the building. Whereas the muscular language of the AP2 was all about the interior and exterior frame, in order to counterpoint this raw aesthetic the ‘twin’ building was sheathed in a lightweight plastic skin: a move that created a ghostly counterpart to its more solid looking neighbour. The rough and ready workmanlike language provided the perfect backdrop to the exhibition of contemporary art as well as a challenge to curators and artists to compete with the tough, robust host space.

## Light

The north-facing location of the building ensured that an energy and budgeteffi cient cladding system could be deployed across the new building. The polycarbonate skin was complemented by a roof constructed of ETFE (Ethylene tetrafl uoroethylene) sheeting, ensuring a higher level of insulation and also creating a large open space in the new-build. This space was enthusiastically described as the ‘Belvedere’ space, a hanging space for exhibitions and meetings. On this level the agricultural-standard construction materials enclosed the view through the translucent skin, to the harbour beyond. Canvas shades were added that could be deployed across the roof if the occupants of the space decided they needed shielding from the hot rays of the summer sun.

## Movement

The traditional mechanism of the architectural promenade, a device often deployed in galleries in order to ensure a logical tour through the space, was rendered vertically in FRAC, and undertaken via lifts and stairs placed in a strip of circulation located at the entrance of the building. The space between the AP2 and its counterpart was left as a fl oor-toroof void, a prelude to the epic grandiosity

of the adjacent hall access on the ground fl oor of the corridor.

## Opening

The epic scale of the front door of the AP2 was retained and where once large over-scaled fragments of boats and raw materials would have passed through them, now artworks are trucked into the large hall and distributed around the building.

The richness of this insertion lies in the resonance of the new building when set directly against its existing adjoining ‘twin’. This is a design process and subsequently a construction technique that relies on reproducing the epic and monumental qualities of its adjacent neighbour. Insertions can derive their scale and form from the host building that they are placed into, around, alongside or upon. When the existing building or space is as resonant and as powerful as something like the AP2, its echo provides an oscillation, one that the new insertion can match, and ultimately become equal to.

Nationalgalerie followed in 1876. Ernst von Ihne, Alfred Messel and Ludwig Hoffmann completed the Bode and Pergamon Museums in 1904 and 1930 respectively. The latter was designed to house monumental antiquities such as the Pergamon Altar, and the Market Gate of Miletus, appropriated from archaeological excavations in Turkey and brought back to be housed in large and open spaces of the museum in which to communicate their grandeur to an awe-inspired public. The Altes Museum was designed to house ancient Greek, Roman and Etruscan antiquities. The Alte Nationalgalerie was designed to house paintings and

sculptures, whilst the Neues contained Egyptian and pre-history collections, including the iconic bust of the Egyptian Queen Nefertiti. Together this unique collection of museums provided a focal point for the cultural and intellectual life of the city of Berlin. Occupying a peninsula surrounded by the River Spree, the group of cultural attractions came to be known as Museum Island.

Unfortunately, the island provided a signifi cant target in the Second World War, and the buildings sustained considerable damage. The Neues Museum received the most sustained destruction, all from continued aerial bombardment. At the end of hostilities, the island was severely damaged, and the Neues Museum was left in ruins, with entire sections of the building missing and other areas severely fi redamaged. The destruction was at its greatest in the staircase hall, lost during a bombing raid in November 1943. Also damaged was the southeast cupola, the northwest wing, and the Egyptian courtyard.

All were signifi cantly damaged in air raids in November 1943 and February 1945. After the war, few attempts were made to repair the damage and consolidate the ruins. But, with more destruction occurring as the building was left roofl ess and open to the elements, it fell further into disrepair and was ultimately in danger of complete

Flatland was initiated and produced with a view to exploring the analysis of an intense, time-based function of a very particular space. The idea that six people were to co-exist in this project, in a public gallery, non-stop, and on full show for three weeks (apart from an enclosed bathroom unit, housing a toilet and shower), with food ordered online and delivered to the installation, ensured that the designers needed to carefully consider what would be needed to survive the duration of the installation. The constraints of the four storey, 18 square metre space, 7 metres wide and just 0.6 metres deep, were carefully detailed in order to allow the space to function for its occupants. As well as the analysis of the proposed function of the space, it needed to stand up and also be safe. So its form and its structure were carefully considered and detailed in order to ensure that it remained upright and structurally sound in the gallery space. As members of the public walked around it, and watched the everyday lives of the occupants unfold throughout the exhibition period, their safety needed to be carefully considered as well as ensuring that the spectacle before their eyes was permanently available for close scrutiny.

During the duration of the project, three people left Flatland. Over the fi rst week, the group had split into two groups, those that favoured disordered environments, and were comfortable with some clutter and disruption, and those that preferred a much more orderly and tidy environment. Those that generated the disordered environment soon found the space too constraining, and because of their disorganised tendencies were chastised by the group favouring the ordered space, particularly as the disorder encroached on both groups’ use of the space and had a signifi cant impact on the environment. In this type of situation, a small space with signifi cant restrictions, even the smallest disruption, such as some discarded rubbish or a misplaced object such as a book, had an enormous impact upon each of the participants. Every centimetre of space became contested as it needed to be utilised and so every scrap of space was scrutinised for its value in the everyday life of the participants. After ten

When the space and the relationships between the people in it become coterminous, and both become attuned to certain sensibilities of how environments can be utilised and adapted for shared use, there is a requirement for a tacit agreement between participants as to how each other’s behaviours and actions impacts upon every inhabitant of the space. ## Strategy

Installation is a strategy where the placement of an object within a certain building will heighten awareness of the existing without compromising or overtly affecting the container it is positioned within. In other words, the installation will not necessarily be without any relationship to the host building, but it is an unwritten rule that the existing space becomes the backdrop to the installed element within it. Often the installation will be arranged or positioned in the space in order to give maximum impact to the elements, but it is generally assumed that the host space will need few, if any, minor modifi cations or changes to the space or building in order to successfully host the installation. This

approach ensures that it is the main spectacle within the environment in which it is contained: the host plays a subsidiary role in the importance of the project.

Flatland was designed and conceived to host the six occupants for three weeks in such a way as to make them safe, and for the installation to be structurally secure, yet once the exhibition was over, it could be removed with little or no effect on the existing building. For the duration of the project, the Sculpture Centre environment became little more than a backdrop or stage for the performance of the installation. Flatland was a performance piece. It was a piece of interior architecture that was very constraining for the occupants, and a space where every move had to be carefully considered because of the impact it would have upon each of the other inhabitants. Because of these tight constraints, the four storey element became diffi cult to modify or adapt, as each change had to be agreed with the other occupants of the space. These negotiations became diffi cult to achieve for two reasons. Because of the narrowness of the installation, it was impossible to arrange a meeting because nobody could physically do anything other than stand in a line within the half a metre wide structure.

## Tactics

## Movement

In this project movement and its negotiation became of utmost importance. The occupants moved vertically between the four levels of the building on ladders placed internally in the structure of the space. This required subtle negotiation, not just between the participants moving between fl oors and agreeing how each can use the ladders (for instance, who could come down and who could move up) but also between all of the inhabitants of the structure as it physically moved as people traversed the object and moved around it. For instance, if one of the occupants was undertaking something that required focus, then a moving building was not conducive to an activity such as reading.

## Openings

The open structure of the space allowed all of the occupants, as well as the elements of the installation, to be viewed from all sides of the gallery. The only enclosed space was the bathroom, itself plumbed in with hot and cold running water. Whilst the bathroom was enclosed, if anybody was using it their absence would be known, through their disappearance from view.

The lack of signifi cant acoustic separation in the bathroom space would probably also reveal who was in the bathroom. Therefore the performance aspect of Flatland was permanently ongoing. As visitors entered and moved through the gallery spaces, they could view everything occurring in the structure through the open framework: a spectacle of an exhibition of everyday life.

## Object

Carefully placed objects in space can provide new and innovative ways with which to view space. The four storey structure was a dominating and powerful element placed centrally within the gallery. The only relationship it had to the existing building was that it could fi t within the tall existing space. The open structure of Flatland, exposed all of the different uses of the space, and how each occupant had ordered their own private, yet very public, spaces.

When I saw Inujima for the fi rst time, I was shocked. I grew up nearby, in a place that was covered in green landscape, an environment full of nature and what I would call natural ‘heritage’. When I visited Inujima it felt despoiled. I was shocked at how the island had been radically altered and changed by quarrying and refi ning. I felt I had to expose and show both these good and bad elements of the island in the new project.

– Interview with architect, 25th August 2016, Hiroshima, Japan. <sup>64</sup>

Located in the Seto Inland Sea, amongst a number of large and small islands, Inujima is currently home to a very small population of around 50 residents. This wasn’t always the case for the coastal area. Although designated as a national park in 1934, for over a century the inland sea and the natural beauty of the islands was the location for the industrialisation of its natural resources. This gathered pace in the late 20th century with large petro-chemical plants being developed along the coast, oil refi neries located in places such as Mizushima and quarries and copper

refi neries in Naoshima and Inujima. Along with all of the associated infrastructural changes such as transport networks, roads and the requirements for energy, the population of the area substantially increased as workers came to operate the industries. Unfortunately all of these changes rapidly escalated the contamination of the area and in particular the pollution of the inland sea.

As well as oil and chemicals, industries such as shipbuilding, cotton spinning and cement production were dotted along the shores. The area was rich in copper, and was mined successfully for many years. In the 19th century, several inland copper refi neries were designated ‘problem facilities’, due to the pollution and environmental damage they were causing. In response to this they were moved and relocated to the islands in the sea.

In ‘The Seto Inland Sea: An Asian Archipelago’, Shunya Yoshimi describes how the relocation of Mitsubishi’s copper refi nery to Naoshima affected the island:

Not long after, nearly all of the trees on the Northern half of the island fell victim to the smoke pollution, and the mountains became denuded. – Insular insight. <sup>65</sup>

![](images/e7632a4a49cde91ccc71c312cd985f08d65e59ed06a0bc081355f4c655781f1c.jpg)

In its entirety, the industrialisation of the area was impacting in a detrimental manner upon the sea, the islands, its coast and residents.

Inujima island had already been signifi cantly disfi gured by the quarrying of its own natural stone. Its granite was of good quality and highly prized. As a result of this, its western side had been completely altered through its deep extraction of stone (sometimes up to depths of 40 metres below sea level). This industry declined in the early 20th century, leaving the island irrevocably altered.

In 1909, a ‘problem facility’ copper refi nery, located in the suburbs of Okayama, was relocated to Inujima. Once built, the island refi nery was very successful, initially producing 300 tonnes of copper a month. At fi rst, it had over 2,000 workers, many of them living on the island; this led to a substantial increase in the requirement for housing and also entertainment. The copper plant was sold in 1913, and due to a complete collapse in copper prices it was closed down in 1919. The refi nery was abandoned, and with no work to sustain the rapid rise in the population, many of the workers left the island. At the peak of the refi nery operations in 1910, there were 6,000 residents on the island in both the quarrying and refi nery industries. In almost 100 years since its closure the refi nery was left to ruin, slowly becoming overrun with nature. Currently there are now just 57 registered residents, with an average age of 75.

We must shift from this civilisation of ‘scrap and build’ to a civilisation of sustainable growth. If not the inheritance and development of culture will be impossible, and the things that we create will inevitably be obliterated for future generations. I became convinced that for the revitalisation of this region and Japan, our ideal should be to ‘use what exists to create what is to be.

– Soichiro Fukutake. <sup>66</sup>

Soichiro Fukutake initiated the development of the regeneration of the island as well as the Seto inland sea. He had known the island since the early 1990s, and acquired the land and the refi nery in 2001. As he took over the initiating of a project by his father’s business, he started to redevelop the area through the initiation of various art projects and buildings. This started with the Benesse Art Site Naoshima, with Benesse House, by Tadao Ando, the fi rst building on that island, followed by numerous others. Hiroshi Sambuichi was entrusted with the Inujima project in 2004, aptly named Seirensho (refi nery) Art Museum, designed to inhabit the ruined copper refi nery on Inujima. Since Inujima, other projects have included Teshima Art Museum by Ryue Nishizawa, leading to a transformation in the perception of the islands.

## Analysis

In Inujima, I researched this place for two years, observing wind, water, light and the sun, in all of the seasons, in the cold and in the warmth, in order to understand how to capture them and make them components of the design, and making them what I consider to be the ‘moving materials’ of the site, elements to be used to design the building. – Interview. <sup>67</sup>

After careful study of the island and the location and context of the existing building, Sambuichi realised that the

The refi ning process needed lots of air in order to power its smelters and also to remove their smoke. They also need to be protected from excessive winds from the monsoons. Therefore, somewhat ironically, this large industrial complex of buildings which, when working, housed a polluting set of processes, was positioned specifi cally in order to capture one thing: the movement of fresh and natural air. ## Strategy

I don’t think that a building should be independent from its site. I considered the building and the landscape both as one. For me they were totally integrated. In this sense the moving materials then become the humans, the visitors as they interact with the site and the project. <sup>68</sup>

The new museum was organised around the existing chimneystack and took the form of a series of interconnected spaces, each of which was derived from the enhancement of the installation of a particular ‘moving material’. The air-drawing power of the chimney was enhanced by the placement of a glass-covered canopy at its base. Therefore as the air heated up, its density decreased and the fl ow of outside air, up through the chimney was facilitated. This provided the ‘engine’ of one of the ‘moving materials’. This entrance was located at the beginning of the earth gallery: a corridor that faced east and which cooled the air. The dark underground circulation corridor was lit with one major single source of natural light, with the sky refl ected through the space by a series of

carefully placed mirrors. The corridor was used to aid the movement of the visitors as well as cool down the air. The energy hall was located before the chimney hall, and then the sun gallery provided the route out to the café and courtyard, beyond which was the ruin of the refi nery set into the landscape. In the museum the fl ow of air and the movement of people was combined in order to create a continuous fl ow of materials through the museum. In the winter this fl ow of air is revered and instead it is warmed up in the sun gallery, into the energy hall and then out through the smokestack.

Various elements of the installation Hero Dry Cell by Yukinori Yanagi were located in each space. Composed of various actual elements of the Japanese author Yukio Mishima’s house, the energy hall contained Solar Rock, a suspended installation of fragments of his domestic space in Shoto. This comprised a space the size of three tatami mats, windows and door frames, and a single slab of Inujima granite that weighed 44 tonnes. Selected views from this room allow the sight of Icarus Tower hanging windows, doors and light fi ttings as well as a staircase and balustrade positioned on the ground. To complete the domestic mise-en-scène, a urinal and washbasin were placed upon the immaculately raked gravelled fl oor. The sun gallery was open to the elements and housed a timber-framed pavilion with pottery and domestic objects from the house where Mishima transcribed his last manifesto and was called Solar Note. Combined together, all of these spaces surmised the distributed contents of Mishima’s dwelling. Through utilising the strategy of installation the building became a container for the natural ‘moving materials’ of the site. And as each natural element grew in intensity, the suspended elements of the house of Yukio Mishima moved, re-animating the domestic space of the controversial author who had taken his own life at the age of 45.

## Tactics

It is important to use moving materials, solid materials such as concrete and steel, as well as more ephemeral matter, as these will control and infl uence more moving materials. For example, the combination of glass and the Karami bricks take the heat from the sun and heat air. Heat changes into the energy which moves the air.

– Sambuichi. <sup>69</sup>

## Objects

The fi rst time that Sambuichi visited the refi nery he became aware of the site being populated by an enormous amount of black bricks, strewn across the site. The bricks were the by-products of the smelting process and were considered waste and thus scattered all around the refi nery. After some analysis, Sambuichi realised that the bricks had excellent thermal properties. The 17,000 that were collected were reused in order to become the walls and fl oors of the new museum, and work as effective instruments, in combination with the planning of the museum and its spaces, for capturing and retaining the light and heat of the sun, as well as cooling the space in the summer. The environmental impact of the refi nery was minimised by utilising the bricks, and their thermal properties were such that the whole museum was designed without any air-conditioning or artifi cial heating systems. This reiterated the designer’s commitment to the environmental impact of the building on the island and expressing how its economic value has risen and fallen in tune with the fortune of the island.

## Movement

As well as the movement of ephemera materials such as light and air, the appropriation of the ruins was based on the sequential movement of visitors to the space. The 80 metre long dark subterranean entrance and corridor of the earth gallery, set two metres below ground level, directed visitors through the space, whilst around them the undulating walls extracted heat from the airfl ow. The darkness of the corridor was relieved by the energy hall, which was counterpointed with external views into the chimney hall and then the sun gallery. All of the interior spaces were contrasted with a less directed amble across the semi-ruined site of the almost derelict refi nery.

## Opening

In the energy and chimney hall, Yukinori Yanagi’s installation Hero Dry Cell was composed of suspended windows and doors extracted from the domestic space of Yukio Mishima. The aperture symbolised the critical role that openings and apertures play in the organisation of the building and the way that visitors view it as they pass through and around it.

After almost 100 years of redundancy, the site had decontaminated itself as nature slowly took over and outgrew the buildings of the refi nery. Sambuichi utilised the raw materials of the site, and the context, in order to install a museum that would touch the

earth as lightly as it could in order to leave a lasting legacy for the island and the people of Inujima, and a space that would aid the regeneration of the island.
