# ONE THE WORLD OF VICTORIAN PRINTING ![](images/b876e6a38d8df1aa33d7398c058f01c629ae3b52efe7eb5d7d2d6dfb2f549991.jpg) HE history of the printed book may seem, at first glance, to be a perfect confirmation of Morris's view that the older crafts had steadily deteriorated in quality since the Middle Ages. Printing', as Richard Durnford, Bishop of Chichester, put it trenchantly, is an art which beginning perfect, ever afterwards declined." And wno, comparing the typical massproduced volume of the nineteenth century - nastily printed in greyish ink on shoddy, acidic paper - with the Mainz Psalter or Gutenberg's Bible, could doubt that Morris and the Bishop were right? Yet the story of the decay of printing is a good deal more complex than Morris ever acknowledged. For him, it was all quite simple: the earliest printed books were still the best because they were produced at a time when the spirit of medieval craftsmanship was strong, but, with the advent of false historicism and a weakness for pseudo-classical ornament in the sixteenth century, printing, like architecture, began to decay. Only in the second half of the eighteenth century, according to Morris, were the consequences of this typographical dry rot fully revealed in the sweltering hideousness of the Bodoni letter, the most illegible type that was ever cut'. Bodoni and Didot, even more relentlessly than Baskerville (whose achievements Morris certainly underestimated), carried the development of the modern' typeface to its logical conclusion. It was reserved for the founders of the later eighteenth century', wrote Morris, to produce letters which are positively ugly, and which, it may be added, are dazzling and unpleasant to the eye owing to the clumsy thickening and vulgar thinning of the lines. . . ." The nineteenth century's further contributions to this melancholy tale were increased mechanization and cheaper paper, ink, and binding materials. When he reflected upon the contemporary printing scene, Morris found a small source of optimism only in the growing popularity of Caslon's old-face type and its imitations. Because of the wide influence of Morris's typographical writings, this sweeping indictment of nineteenth-century printing became accepted dogma for several decades following his death in 1896. There came in time the inevitable reaction against his oversimplifications and his doctrinaire hatred of the modern world. Writing in an American typographical journal, the Dolphin, in 1938, Peter Beilenson remarked with obvious irony that ‘there had developed the general opinion that between the early great classical period of Didot, Bodoni, and Bulmer, and the late arts-and-crafts period of Morris, the printing of the century was one continuous sacrifice on the altars of Machinery and Mammon'. In fact, he argued, many nineteenth-century printers had been preoccupied with the problem of quality, and technically bookmaking averaged at least the level of preceding centuries (except that papermaking and binding sought and found lower commercial levels) and . . . artistically the century offered a greater variety of good things, which on the whole unfortunately suit our present taste less than those of any preceding century'.3 Recently a less cautious revisionist, Ruari McLean, has insisted that ‘more exciting things happened in book design between 1837 and 189o than in any other comparable period in the history of the world's printing; and most of them happened in London'.4 (In fairness to McLean, it must be added that he is referring to the most sumptuous Victorian books; he elsewhere acknowledges that the ordinary commercially produced book of the last century was a very dismal thing indeed.) Not surprisingly, nineteenth-century printers themselves did not share Morris's low estimate of their achievements. The trade journals of the time were filled with hymns of self-praise. ‘What wondrous strides have been made in almost every department of the Art!' the Printers' Register (6 July 1881) declared. ‘Hand-presses have become almost exterminated, pushed out by power-driven machines; typefaces . . . may now be counted by hundreds in their varieties; the uniformity of black ink, relieved on rare occasions by red initials, now gives way to mosaics ofevery imaginable colour, combined with gold, silver, and many-shaded bronzes; while borders, ornaments, picturetypes, engravings, and lithographs are made use of to an extent never before conceived.' The naïve enthusiasm for machinery — and the implication that technological advances always produce aesthetic triumphs - in this representative pronouncement may make us reluctant to attend very seriously to the testimony of Morris's contemporaries in the printing trade; but at least it should be observed that they rejected his assessment of their craftsmanship, for Victorian printers believed, or professed to believe, that nineteenth-century bookwork was as fine as that of any preceding era. New technology — which Morris was to reject so dramatically at the Kelmscott Press — had completely changed the character of the printing trade in his day. Before the third Earl Stanhope perfected an iroņ press in about 1 8oo, all printing — from Gutenberg's day onwards — had been done on a wooden press which was so simple in structure that it was frequently built by the local carpenter or joiner. The earliest detailed pictorial representations of the ‘Common Press' (as it came to be called) appeared in the early decades of the sixteenth century, and they show the same unsophisticated mechanism that was still being used nearly three hundred years later. If Morris had really been motivated solely by the antiquarian impulses that sometimes are attributed to him, he presumably would have reverted to the wooden press; there were still a few of them around in late-Victorian England, and they were occasionally seen in operation on special occasions such as the Caxton Exhibition in 1877. But Morris recognized that the older presses were actually inferior to the iron hand-press of the nineteenth century. One of the great drawbacks to wood-block printing in those times [the fifteenth century] was the weakness of the presses,' he wrote. 'Their only resource was to print with the paper very wet, and with very soft packing, so that the block went well into the paper; but many books . . . have suffered much from this cause.' Lord Stanhope's press, despite some enhancements, was in fact only a slightly improved version of the medieval press. More dramatic technological developments appeared in the printing trade within two decades. Friedrich Koenig invented a steam-driven cylinder press which was first used to print The Times on 29 November 1814, and for the rest of the century the presses that were employed by newspapers and mass-circulation magazines grew ever more elaborate and imposing. By the 184os the machine-minders who tended these clanking monsters had come to resemble flies crawling over the skin of an enormous elephant. In the latter half of the century the handpress (always iron by now) was used much less frequently even in book-printing; when Morris founded the Kelmscott Press in 1891 such presses were still readily available but were regarded as useful mainly for the printing of posters and the taking of proofs. The growing mechanization of printing led to a reduction in the cost of books and periodicals. Some observers at the time were overjoyed by this means of spreading literacy. Charles Knight (a printer and publisher as well as a writer), in The Old Printer and the Modern Press (1854), argued fervently that the new technological developments were a heaven-sent gift that would lead to the wider distribution of books through book clubs and public libraries. The Society for the Diffusion of Useful Knowledge, of which Knight was an active leader, was symptomatic of the energetic, cheerful Victorian response to the ever-faster spinning cylinders of the steam press. It is also significant that a number of the edifying examples cited in Charles Babbage's On the Economy of Machinery and Manufactures (fourth edition, 1835) were drawn from the world of printing. ![](images/17096a26f221c53b32b4f308348a944ed2d552dcecfe9b061574ae310fde2dce.jpg) Figure 3 By the middle of the last century, this sort of massive, clanking press was commonly used for the printing of newspapers and magazines; even books were produced on somewhat smaller steam-driven cylinder presses. Morris's adoption of the hand-press was a direct reaction to such technological developments. Of course there were other voices, sometimes from within the trade, warning that the growing acceleration of the printing process was leading to a debasement of traditional standards in typography and materials. T. C. Hansard, in his Typographia (1825), a technical manual for printers, gave an example of the problems that grew out of the new preoccupation with speed: The system of hurrying works through the press, lately adopted by some booksellers, by giving them among a variety of houses, is destructive of uniformity. . . . It is not unusual to discover, in volumes, so divided, one to contain more lines in a page than another, or, even if the number of lines are the same, the page differs in length; or, should the type agree in depth, it is very probable that it varies in thickness; and still more probable that it varies in the quantity of work it has done; — all of which produce a glaring want of that uniformity which constitutes the beauty of the typographic art' (p. 394). At the same time there was a noticeable cheapening of inks and papers. Until the third decade of the century ink continued to be made from the usual ingredients — lampblack and linseed oil — according to the traditional recipes (one of which can be found in Moxon's Mechanick Exercises on the Whole Art of Printing, first published in 1683—4). Soap began to find its way into ink, because ink thus diluted gave a sharper impression, and soon the chemists were able to provide a whole range of artificial substitutes for the old ingredients. When Morris in the 189os insisted upon using an ink similar to that employed by printers of earlier centuries, it was seen as a revolutionary gesture. Developments in paper-manufacturing were even more alarming. With the supply of good white rags declining, paper mills began, in the final decades of the eighteenth century, to bleach inferior rags and to introduce gypsum and alum into the fibre. The disastrous consequences were evident long before the Kelmscott Press came on the scene. John Murray (not related to the publisher), a popular writer on scientific subjects, in 1823 contributed to the Gentleman's Magazine an article which was reprinted as a book the following year under the title Observatious and Experiments on the Bad Composition of Modern Paper. After describing the disintegration of particular books, Murray suggested that ‘the chief causes of destruction consist in the employment of sulphate of lime, &c. in the pulp, and bleaching the rags previously, or the paper subsequently, with oxymuriatic acid gas. (Chlorine.)' (p. 8). Worse was to come. In the latter half of the century book papers were more frequently made fron various rag substitutes, the poorest of which was mechanical woodpulp. As a general rule (with naturally a few exceptions), the older a book, the more likely its paper is to be in good condition today; librarians around the world are overwhelmed by the prospect of the steady disintegration of nearly all books printed since the mid-nineteenth century. To Morris, the most insidious aspect of modern book papers was that they frequently masqueraded as something better, with fake wire and chain lines (to suggest that they were handmade) and adulturants (to add weight and shininess). In certain kinds of books and periodicals, he suggested, cheap paper was acceptable, but only if it frankly acknowledged its own cheapness and made no effort to disguise itself. Probably the ordinary reader a century ago paid no more attention to such matters than readers nowadays do; the most appalling woodpulp paper, when it is new, may seem decent enough to the unpractised eye. But changes in type-styles were sufficiently drastic to be obvious to even casual observers. Everywhere around him — in posters, advertisements, shop signs, sometimes even in books — the nineteenth-century Englishman witnessed an unprecedented efflorescence of new type-designs. Egyptians, grotesques, Gothics, Tuscans: their very names were mysterious and exotic, their shapes heavy and insistent, perfectly suited to the frenetic commercial atmosphere of the times. Book-typography, meanwhile, had moved in the opposite direction, with letterforns that grew ever more delicate and fragile. Historians of printing usually classify serifed typefaces designed primarily for bookwork into three major categories. Except to specialists, however, the first two groups – Venetian and old face - look very much alike; on the other hand, the differences between old face and modern face are obvious to all. Until the eighteenth century, despite a slow evolution in the shape of the Roman letter, typefaces used in books changed remarkably little. But in the generation after Caslon a ne w style of type-design was introduced throughout Europe, subsequently known as‘modern face', which was to have an immense influence on the appearance of books in Morris's century. Although a Frenchman, Philippe Grandjean, is usually regarded as the progenitor of the modern face, it was left to Giambattista Bodoni, an Italian designer, and the French foundry of Firmin Didot to carry the new style to its logical conclusion. In England, at about the same time, John Baskerville produced a transitional version of the modern face that seemed better suited to conservative tastes. What all these varied type-designs had in common was vertical rather than oblique shading, unbracketed (i.e. unsupported) serifs, and greater contrast between thick and thin strokes. The effect was — as Morris always said disparagingly — dazzling to the eye. Whereas old-face types had grown directly out of the letterforms used by Renaissance scribes and Roman stone-cutters, the newly fashionable letters were geometrically precise and brilliant in their effect. Old-face designs convey a mellow charm, like that of an ancient building; modern-face types suggest the smooth regularity of a well-made machine. It is not difficult to understand why Morris repudiated modern face. Though they are not to everyone's taste, the books of Bodoni and Baskerville are extremely handsome. (Morris, needless to say, would never have admitted that.) Yet the eighteenth-century type-designers must be judged not only by their own types and books but also by the mainly unwholesome effect they had on their successors. As Daniel Berkeley Updike remarked, Grandjean, Baskerville, Bodoni, and the Didots had a mischievous influence on type-forms; for the derivations from types that their work made popular culminated in a kind ofletter which was capable of greater vulgarity and degradation than was ever the case with old fonts. The ordinary English, French, or Italian book printed between 1830 and 185o was very often a cheap and mean-looking production. Perhaps Bodoni and other great persons were not wrong in their day; but they put type-forms on the wrong track." The modern-face types for books that evolved in the nineteenth century from the Didot-Bodoni models were bland, undistinguished, and boring, and they changed very little as the century went on, except to become progressively lighter. It is also important to bear in mind that the evolution of type-designs is influenced by developments in technology as well as shifts in fashion. However, the progressively lighter typefaces, though they may have had their origin in technical decisions, exactly suited the taste oflate-Victorian readers and printers, and there was much boasting in the trade journals of the time that the growing delicacy of types was a sign of increased refinement': ‘in the present day we see the effect of an artistic taste, and appreciation for delicacy of outline and freedom of ornamentation developed in the excessively light types and floral embellishments now so popular with the best printers,' according to a contributor to the Printers' Register (6 April 1885). Even the heaviest styles were materially toned down to meet the current refinement of taste.' By contrast, older typefaces were condemned as heavy, clumsy, and crude. It is in the context of this widespread preference for delicacy and lightness that Morris's almost oppressively dark types must be seen: discarding what seemed to him the attenuation and weakness of nineteenth-century typography, Morris reacted (over-reacted, some would say) by designing faces that were solid, black, and aggressively self-assertive. But Morris was to argue on more than one occasion that typefaces alone did not determine the beauty and harmony of the printed page: even with mediocre type, if certain other principles of design were observed, it was possible to produce a satisfactory page. Morris had profoundly held views on margins, for example, and nothing infuriated him more than the popular practice of placing the text virtually in the centre of a page. That Morris was fighting a firmly entrenched eneny is illustrated by a series of articles written by De Vinne, the most skilful and best-informed printer in America, on Book Margins which appeared in the Printers' Circular (Philadelphia) between May and September 1871. De Vinne, who certainly ought to have known better, offered the unconvincing argument that ‘the text or printed page is the picture, and the margin is the frame; all the proprieties require that the picture, when framed, shall be in the centre of the frame'(June 1871). In fact, he was merely endorsing what printers on both sides of the Atlantic already did. Similarly, there was a broad consensus - which was not to be seriously questioned until Morris and Emery Walker attacked it in the 189os — that generous spaces between words and lines of type improved legibility. The combination of lighter typefaces and ample spaces produced a page in which the text appeared to be, at best, greyish. ## Yet such a damning summary of Victorian printing practices fails to take account that printers of the last century were sometimes, when not subjected to the usual commercial pressures, capable of producing very impressive work. All the printers' manuals of the day discussed what was variously known as fine press work', fine work', or (later in the century) simply fine printing'. As the terms suggest, the printer who did this sort of thing earned his additional wages (double or more the usual rate) by taking greater care in the presswork, especially the make-ready; there appears to have been no distinction made between ordinary' and fine' composition. The greatest achievements of printing during the last century undoubtedly are to be found in the striking technical skill with which illustrations were produced. In the eighteenth century woodcuts had disappeared almost completely from books, which were illustrated instead with copperplate engravings, often quite elegant in themselves but bearing no relationship to the typography. The engravings and the text pages were printed by entirely separate processes and were joined together only when the book was bound. Morris regarded this as a pernicious development, because it destroyed that harmony which seemed to him the supreme attribute of a well-printed book. The cautionary examples which Morris, Walker, and others in their circle frequently cited were Samuel Rogers's Italy (183o) and Poems (1834), both handsomely illustrated with steel engravings designed by Turner. Albert L. Cotton summarized the Morrisian view when he wrote in the Contemporary Review (August 1898): Beautiful as pictures, they bear no apparent relation to the volumes which they illustrate; their charm is independent and extraneous; the artist clearly did not concern himself to harmonise them with the text or with each other; taken apart entirely from the books, they would lose nothing of their force. They are steel engravings, pure and simple, which might just as well have been issued separately in a portfolio.' With less justice, Morris brought the same accusation against Thomas Bewick,º who had revolutionized English book-illustration at the beginning of the century by perfecting the wood-engraving, which, unlike the woodcut, is done on the end grain of boxwood with graver's tools and is therefore capable of rendering very subtle effects. Morris did not especially admire subtlety in art; he preferred the bold, simple outlines of medieval woodcuts to the intricate delicacy of Bewick's vignettes of English rural life. Probably Morris also harboured an irrational prejudice against Bewick because the brilliant technique he had created degenerated very quickly into the facile, characterless style of wood-engraving that was to be found in so many English books and magazines for the remainder of the century. Within a generation Bewick's successors were producing, on an unprecedented scale, illustrations that were both technically dazzling and aesthetically empty. The facsimile' engraving reigned triumphantly in Victorian England. Its supremacy was challenged finally in the 188os by a new mechanico-chemical process then known as zincography, which had been invented by Eberhard, a Bavarian. This technology bypassed the human engraver entirely, since the artist's design was transferred photographically on to a zinc plate, which was then engraved by immersion in a chemical bath. An illustration that previously had been engraved by hand over several days could now be prepared for the press in a few hours. By the time the Kelmscott Press came into existence, wood-engraving had disappeared almost overnight, and process engravings were to be seen everywhere, even in the most expensively produced books. Morris held zincography in contempt; it is one of the minor ironies of the Kelmscott Press that his chief adviser was Emery Walker, by profession a process engraver, who probably knew more about the latest developments in techniques ofillustration than anyone else in England. In the end Walker was able to wring one small concession to modern technology from Morris: the borders and initials used at the Kelmscott Press were all printed from electrotypes, another new means of duplicating engravings. Chromolithography was used less frequently in books, but it was capable of producing really splendid effects, as in some of the folios by Henry Noel Humphreys. Morris, curiously enough, seems to have been intrigued by the process: in 1894 he and Walker spent a day at the Bodleian Library, Oxford, to investigate the possibility of reproducing a beautifully ornamented fourteenth-century manuscript known as the Douce Apocalypse, no doubt under the Kelmscott Press imprint. Nothing came of their plans, but it is certainly significant that Morris regarded such an undertaking seriously. (Morris probably had chromolithography in mind on this occasion, though he may have intended to call into service some form of photographic reproduction, such as the newly invented halftone process.) In a letter written earlier, in 1882, Morris suggested that an Indian carpet recently presented to the Boston Museum of Fine Arts might be reproduced by the same means: some of the chromo-lithographers are so good nowadays that a reproduction of a good & careful drawing of it would be of great service to those who are interested in such matters.'o Despite these occasional flickers of interest in the latest methods of facsimile illustration, the one subject that most deeply preoccupied Morris was type-design, and it was there, he felt, that the contemporary book displayed its gravest weakness. Morris was not alone in his dissatisfaction with the modern-face type. There had been several efforts earlier in the century, both in England and on the Continent, to revive old-face designs," but the one attempt which had the most far-reaching consequences for English printers, including Morris, was the reintroduction by the Chiswick Press of Caslon's old-face Roman in the 184os. Notwithstanding an earlier debate among historians as to which books first appeared in Caslon type, recent scholarship has confirmed that it was The Diary of Lady Willoughby, a novel published by Longmans in the spring of 1844." At about the same time, however, the Chiswick Press was also setting up two other books entirely in Caslon: George Herbert's The Temple, which was issued later in 1844, and Juvenal's Satires, which appeared the following year.
