Peter Opsvik, Balans Variable sitting tool for Stokke, 1979 ![](images/26a2f61f40fc409fda08416a4e8504fe96d210e4718253dca4a4406eab79f681.jpg) # peter opsvik Srda, Noway) ![](images/a860252331c4158dee83ae8530116ecf24e3944466aa399cf245f62140d70aa5.jpg) The leading Norwegian furniture designer Peter Opsvik studied at Bergen College of Applied Art from 1959 to 1963 and the State College of Applied Art in Oslo from 1963to 1964, prior to working as an industrial designer for the Tandberg Radio Factory in Oslo from 1965 to 1970. In 1967, Opsvik won a design scholarship from the Norwegian Credit Bank and two years later was awarded another scholarship from the Norwegian Design Centre. He began working as a freelance furniture designer in 1970 and designed the innovative Mini Max desk and chair, which could be height adjusted to meet the needs of both adults and children and was manufactured by Stokke. Using the same revolutionary concept of Generationenstuhl (a chair that grows with the child), Opsvik later designed the best-selling Tripp Trapp high-chair/chair in 1972. This remarkable design not only elevated the child to a normal table height, but with its foot-rest also ensured that the necessary support was provided so as to allow enough freedom of movement “to explore the world in comfort and safety”. In 1974 Opsvik designed his Model no. 2010 office chair for Håg and five years later, having collaborated with Hans Christian Mengshoel (b. 1946) on the development of better performing ergonomic seating solutions, introduced the revolutionary Balans Variable sitting tool. With its balanced rocking base and provision of an ergonomically beneficial kneeling position, the Balans Variable afforded the user a good upright posture while working at a desk or table. During the 1980s, Opsvik continued to produce ergonomic seating designs for both Stokke and Håg, including his two Post-Modern products – the Balans Supporter (1983), which provided support for a semi-seated position, and the Garden Seating Sculpture (1984), which was more of an aesthetic exercise than a particularly functional solution. Since the mid-1980s, Opsvik has produced both conventional style ergonomic seating, such as the Sitti child's chair of 1993 and the Actulum rocking chair of 1995, as well as more experimental and expressive furniture designs, such as those shown at the “Dimension in Wood – Sculpture or Furniture?" exhibition held at the Applied Art Museum in Oslo in 1986. Some of this latter type of furniture, which is distinguished by quirky forms and ambiguous function, has been put into limited production by Cylindra. By the late 19th century, the forge at Orrefors was in terminal decline and as a result the local economy was becoming increasingly reliant on timber. After the ironworks' eventual closure, the glassworks was established on the same site and began manufacturing medical and domestic glassware, such as glass bottles and jars. In 1913, the Gothenburg industrialist Consul Johan Ekman bought the Orrefors glassworks from Johan August Samuelsson and appointed Albert Ahlin its manager, thereby heralding a new era for the company. A year later, Orrefors began manufacturing crystal glassware, but it soon became apparent that there was a need for accomplished artists to design new forms and patterns. To this end, Simon Gate and Edward Hald were recruited in 1916 and 1917 respectively. Gate immediately instigated a thorough refinement of both overlaying and etching techniques. Working with the master blower Knut Bergkvist, he also developed a new technique that he christened Graal, in which glass blanks were decorated with cutting, etching or engraving, then reheated and coated with an outer layer of glass before being blown into their required final form. In 1917, both Gate and Hald began designing figurative patterns for glass engraving, that included a series of modestly draped nudes which were a complete departure from traditionally engraved motifs. By the mid-1920s, both designers had thoroughly perfected the art of engraving and were producing exquisitely decorated pieces, such as those designs shown by Orrefors at the 1925 Paris “Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes”. Gate and Hald had quite different approaches to decoration, however – the former being described as “a bounteously figurative artist” borrowing baroque motifs, while the latter was “a vigorous modernist” inspired by the art of his mentor, Henri Matisse(1869–1954). Apart from the artistic wares of this early period, Orrefors also produced domestic glassware by Hald and Gate in response to the Svenska Slöjdföreningen's (Swedish Society of Craft and Design) rallying call for the manufacture of beautiful and practical objects for everyday use. Around this time, the factory also began manufacturing glass light fittings for public buildings and later for domestic interiors, bothofwhich proved a commercial success. In1928, Vicke Lindstrand joined Orrefors and two years later made his debut at the 1930 Stockholm Exhibition with a series of thick-bodied Art Deco pieces that were modern in form and decorated with engraved figurativeand geometric motifs. These new designs by Lindstrand signalled a general shift towards Modernism as well as a new and more sculptural direction for Orrefors. During the 1930s, bothSven Palmqvist and Edvin Öhrström developed innovative techniques at Orrefors known asKraka and Ariel respectively – the former involved sandwiching a grid-like pattern of white or coloured glass between layers of clear glass, whilethe latter entailed sandblasting the surface of a blank which was then sheathed with a transparent exterior layer or layers of glass so as to produce pockets ofair bubbles between the different layers. During the Second World War, the glassworks experienced a severe shortage of materials, but its designers continued to experiment with new techniques such as Cut Graal and the popular Fish Graa/ with marine motifs. In 1947, Ingeborg Lundin joined the firm and the following year the glassworks showed new designs by her as well as a number by Nils Landberg and Edward Hald at the NK department store in Stockholm. These post-war designs were notable for their simple, soft forms and subtle use of beautiful colours. During the 1950s, Orrefors widely exhibited its new products throughout Europe, includingthe 1954 (X)Milan Triennale and the landmark 1955 Helsingborg “H55” exhibition, where Sven Palmqvist introduced his range of Fuga domestic glassware, which employed a new manufacturing technique that utilized centrifugal force. If the 1950sat Orrefors were exemplified by the soft, undulating organic forms of Ingeborg Lundin's Äpple vase, then the 1960s were epitomized by the bold, colourfully striped Pop glasses by Gunnar Cyrén. During the early 1970s, Orrefors began promoting a rustic aesthetic, while in the 1980s the revival of interest in limited edition studio pieces led the company to the decision in 1986 to no longer produce designs from its former catalogue and to concentrate instead on the production of new designs. During this period, Orrefors designers were on numerous occasions presented with a new design award known as Utmärkt Svenska Form for their stylistically diverse work. The 1990s saw a revival in simple yet elegant table glass, such as the designs produced by Gunnar Cyrén and Erika Lagerbielke. The mid-1990s also saw the arrival of new designers, including Lena Bergström (b. 1961), Martti Rytkönen (b. 1960) and Per B. Sundberg (b. 1964). These designers have produced highly sculptural and technically innovative work that pays tribute to Orrefors' artistic traditions, while pioneering new forms and techniques for art glass as well as objects for everyday use. In 1999, the glass artist and ceramicist Ingegerd Råman joined the company.
