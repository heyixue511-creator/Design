The beginnings of Kosta – Sweden's longest surviving glassworks – can, however, be traced back to 1742 when two county governors and generals in ·King Karl XIl's army, Anders Koskull (1677–1746)and Georg Bogislaus Staël von Holstein(1685-1763), joined forces and founded a glassworks on the boundary between their counties of Kronoberg and Kalmar in the forested Småland region – an area that is now sometimes referred to as the “Kingdom of Crystal”. The name of the factory and the village in which it was sited, Kosta, was derived from an acronym of their surnames. Attracting skilled glassblowers from Germany, the Kosta glassworks initially produced high-quality household items such as traditional German-style drinking vessels. The company also became renowned for its manufacture of chandeliers (in the English style) and architectural glass, and became the main supplier of window-panes to theRoyal Palace(completed in 1753), which was built byCarl Gustav Tessin in Stockholm. From 1756 Kosta was under the financial control of the Wickenberg family, but eventually it became a publicly-traded company in 1875. Around the turn-of-the-century, the factory began producing more artistic wares, especially Art Nouveau style cameo glass inspired by the work of Émile Gallé (1846–1904), which was adorned with naturalistic floral decoration and was designed by prominent Swedish designers such as Gunnar Wennerberg and Alf Wallander (1862– 1914). Following the establishment in 1915 of an agency to promote the employment of artists in industry,theSvenska Slöjdföreningen (Swedish Society of Craft and Design), and the society's appeal for Vackrare vardagsvara (better everyday goods), the Kosta glassworks employed a number of artists to revitalize its product line, including Edvin Ollers(1888-1959) - a member of the Optimists art group – and Edvard Strömberg. Ollers' designs for Kosta were distinguished by simple forms with decorative motifs, and were stylistically similar to the objects manufactured by the Daum glassworks in France during this period. Strömberg's designs were similarly distinguished by their restrained yet well-proportioned forms. As artistic director of the company, Bergh was responsible for the graceful yet modern approach to design that epitomized Kosta's product line during the inter-war years. This included the production of cut glass products with simple outlines that were mostly executed in clear glass and engraved with either stylized figurative or geometric motifs. In response to the increasing popularity of the Art Deco style during the late 1930s, Kosta's glassware became increasingly angular, chunky and deep cut – as exemplified by the designs of Skawonius and Tyra Lundgren (1897–1979) that were embellished with highly stylized fish and bird motifs. Although Kosta's output during the interwar period was of generally high quality, it did not match the artistic and technical innovation of the Orrefors glassworks. The Kosta glassworks were not creatively energized until 1950whenVicke Lindstrand was appointed its artistic director. At Kosta, Lindstrand produced some of the most sculptural vessels of his career. These were distinguished by asymmetrical forms, soft undulating lines, spiralling stripes of colour as well as cut and sandblasted abstract motifs. Under his direction the firm manufactured a diverse range of glassware, including Erik Höglund's bubbly rustic-style coloured soda glass vessels, Mona Morales-Schildt's polychromatic faceted Ventana series, and Ann (b. 1937) and Göran (b. 1933) Wärff's highly expressive art glass, which was frequently embellished with humorous cameo decoration. In 1972 the firm acquired the Johansfors glassworks and in 1976 it was renamed Kosta Boda. In 1990 the company was taken over by its long-time competitor, Orrefors, and since then it has built on its reputation for artistic and colourful glass. Today, the company has a stable of eight inhouse designers – Monica Backström (b. 1939) Anna Ehrner (b. 1948), Kjell Engman(b. 1946), Ulrica Hydman-Vallien (b. 1938), Bertil Vallien (b. 1938), Ann Wåhlström (b. 1957), Gunnel Sahlin (b. 1954) and Göran Wärff – who design new products and additions to existing collections that “communicate their artistic messages with warmth and light-heartedness". While Kosta Boda endeavours to connect users not only with its cultural roots but also with the “ever changing present”, its glassware products continue to be widely acclaimed for their inherent craftsmanship and artistry. ![](images/e8c1635f06cc4ba1ead9716bb9ceed8efabf6c3cdd28ce9b6af6007109bc1b2e.jpg)
