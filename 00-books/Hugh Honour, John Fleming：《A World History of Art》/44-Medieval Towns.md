# Medieval Towns # COMMERCIAL PROSPERITY AND CIVIC PRIDE With the break up of the Roman empire, the geography and social structures of Europe were radically altered: population was scattered, society was essentially rural. It was only in areas of dense Roman urbanization – such as north Italy – that a significant proportion of the population remained concentrated in towns, but these were now greatly reduced in size within their Roman walls and most had become the seats of dioceses – and subject to the temporal rule of the bishop. By the eleventh century, new settlements had appeared all over Europe, at trading or bridge points, on coasts (e.g. Venice), in the shelter of a castle (e.g. Prague) or at the gates of a monastery (e.g. Cluny, France). Charters of liberties granted by feudal lords on whose land these settlements arose attracted craftsmen and merchants, and in the twelfth century development was further boosted by population growth and the increase in commerce and long-distance trade, particularly along the routes between north Italy and the Low Countries. Prosperity was accompanied by a desire for autonomy, and the twelfth- and thirteenth-century histories of most towns are marked by the transition from feudal to popular rule. The increasing complexity of town government, the rise of the guilds and improvements in public amenities led to the development of new building types - town and guild halls, granaries and warehouses, market buildings and shops - while rivalries encouraged pride in the appearance of the city and the enlargement and embellishment of both secular and religious buildings. Most medieval towns grew up organically around a marketplace or along a major road or waterfront, their layout conditioned by the landscape and the presence of important institutional buildings - cathedral and bishop's palace, castle, abbey. Bruges, which grew up around a castle, has typical narrow, winding streets (9,4s). Planned medieval towns were usually founded with some specific military or commercial purpose in mind. Outstanding among them is Aigues-Mortes on the Mediterranean coast of France, founded by Louis IX in 1246 as a port for crusaders and trade with the Levant (9,46). Built on flat marshland, the town has an unusually regular grid of streets within a roughly rectangular circuit of walls. The scheme probably influenced the 24 new towns (bastides) founded in 1296 by Edward I in England, Gascony, and the frontier lands of Wales. Towns of Roman origin usually show some traces of their ancient past. The Roman walls often formed the basis of the medieval circuit, as at Trier and London, and Roman buildings were frequently converted to other uses – at Arles in Provence the amphitheatre was turned into a fortification to protect the little town that had grown up within it - or their materials reused. The Roman grid of streets influenced the medieval layout, and at Verona the marketplace was on the site of the old forum; equally, medieval streets might cut across the Roman network or preserve the outlines of a ruin, as at Lucca, where the amphitheatre formed the marketplace. At Merida in Spain the Roman plan, bridge over the river and several buildings survived Muslim occupation and Christian reconquest. Despite the lack of systematic planning in towns of organic growth, most municipal governments attempted to control construction, partly to improve sanitation and prevent fire – as in the London regulations of c. 12o0 which banned thatched roofs - and partly for aesthetic or political reasons. In mid-thirteenth-century Florence the lofty stone towers built by competing family clans (similar ones can be seen in nearby San Gimignano) had to be reduced in height, while the Sienese, always sensitive to the impact of their city on foreigners, issued a whole series of regulations covering fountains, streets, churches, and public buildings. Prosperous cities such as Florence had to enlarge their defences more than once. In 1172-5 the walls enclosing the original urban nucleus containing the cathedral were extended to protect expanding suburbs across the River Arno. In 1299 a new circuit was started that enclosed an area five times larger: settlements had arisen along the approach roads to the gates and around the great churches founded outside the twelfth-century walls by the Dominicans, Franciscans, and other mendicant orders. There was a similar pattern of development at Bruges, where ambitious new ramparts were built in the fourteenth century; but subsequent economic decline meant that the population never expanded to fill the area enclosed. Civic buildings were symbols of a city's independence and prosperity, their location a reflection of its history. In Italy, where the bishops had been overlords, early town halls were situated close to the cathedral complex and the market to assert the jurisdiction of the commune: an early example is the Broletto in Como, begun in 1215, which has an arcaded ground floor opening on to the market with a council room above. Later, distinct municipal centres developed in self-confident communes. The Palazzo Vecchio in Florence was begun in 1299 as part of the scheme that involved the enlargement of the walls, the rebuilding of the cathedral – the responsibility of the commune – and the formation of a wide street linking the two centres together. A tall, fortresslike building with machicolations and bell-tower, it stood as a warning to the commune's enemies. At the same time a coherent civic centre was taking shape in Florence's rival, Siena: the town hall the Palazzo Pubblico, formed the focus of a large fan-shaped square, the Campo, which was surrounded by houses with standardized windows and balconies, as laid out in regulations of 1297 (9,47). The building was decorated with frescoes illustrating civic themes including the consequences of good and bad government (9,86). At Bruges two large public squares – the Markt (market) and Burg (the site of the former castle) – are dominated respectively by the cloth hall with its great belfry, which housed the city's charters, and the town hall with rich Gothic façade, begun in 1376. Town and guild halls could be vast and complex, housing a variety of functions: paradoxically, the largest and showiest – such as the town hall of Leuven (1448) – were built during a period of economic decline. Religion was central to the life of the medieval town, but the building of the main church could be a focus of patriotism as well as devotion. In Germany, citizens of Ulm began the prodigious west tower of the Minster in 1392 as an assertion of their town's independence. The marble-clad buildings of the Piazza del Duomo in Pisa (9,12; 13). set out from the mid-eleventh century, symbolize the wealth and prestige of a city that had triumphed over its rivals. In 1331 the Florentines decided to enlarge the cathedral they had begun in 1296. In response, in 1339 the Sienese embarked on an ambitious scheme to turn the nave of their cathedral into the transept of a vast new building, which would be almost twice the height of the old. And it is the precarious balance between these two axes that gives the interior its vibrant life and almost elastic tension, held together visually by lines as taut as the strings of a welltuned musical instrument. ## Stained Glass and Flying Buttresses The interior of Amiens Cathedral beautifully exemplifies the lucidity of Gothic architecture, in which all the parts are related to each other and to the whole, both in form and proportions. This visual logic was originally raised to an even higher, transcendental plane by the unearthly light filtered through stained glass windows and suffusing the whole space with immaterial color. Church windows had often been filled with colored glass since the fourth century (no crystal-clear glass was made until much later) and early theologians had endowed it with Neoplatonic significance. At least as early as the eleventh century the window sometimes incorporated figures composed like translucent mosaics from pieces of variously colored glass overpainted in black to indicate details. It was at St-Denis that figurative stained glass windows (only fragments of which survive) were first given the importance they were to retain for some four centuries in northern Europe. Suger saw in them a double symbolism, that of their subject-matter irradiated by the lux nova, or new light, of the Gospel.'Miraculous' was the word he used for the light admitted by these most sacred windows'. Chartres Cathedral still preserves most of its original stained glass, conceived as the source of both spiritual and physical illumination. As Gothic architecture developed, masonry walls were gradually replaced by windows filling the spaces between ever more slender supports. Eventually, in such buildings as the Ste-Chapelle in Paris, the chapel of the kings of France, the interior becomes one single tall space with, except for a low dado zone, walls entirely of glass (9,so). The stonework dividing one panel of glass from another (still fairly prominent at Chartres) is reduced to a lacy mesh of tracery more conspicuous from the outside than from within. (Tracery was a Gothic invention and its development towards ever greater dissolution of the wall – from ‘bar tracery' toplate tracery' – can be followed stage by stage from Chartres to Reims and from Reims to Amiens and beyond.) The desire for light and immateriality complemented the Gothic quest for ever greater height. Clerestory windows grew steadily taller, pushing up the vaults to 12o feet (37m) at Chartres, 125 feet (38m) at Reims, 140 feet (43m) at Amiens and a dizzying 157 feet (48m) at Beauvais (1230-40). But at Beauvais the architect overreached himself. Only the apse survived the collapse of the choir in 1284 and, although the choir was rebuilt, the nave was never even begun. These lofty vaults required external support and this led to another Gothic invention, 'flying buttresses' as at Chartres, where they completely surround the apse (9,5).
