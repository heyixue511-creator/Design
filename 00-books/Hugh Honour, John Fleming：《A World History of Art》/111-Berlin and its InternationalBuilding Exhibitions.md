# Berlin and its International Building Exhibitions 22.51 Frankfurter-Allee, Berlin, completed 1960. ![](images/6fb0d6f280246e5476a70453d4ac4fc2ebc46a580a729ba3296ac929cdbc19cd.jpg) Between the early nineteenth century and the mid-twentieth, nearly all current systems of urban development were adopted in Berlin. An imposing group of museums was built near the royal palace and cathedral. Some of the first public parks were laid out. Terraces, squares and leafy suburbs provided residences for the middle classes. Factories, which include monuments of modern architecture (19.16) were kept to the outskirts with planned estates for workers nearby. Finally Hitler's architect Albert Speer (1905-1981) dreamed up a megalomaniac scheme only shortly before the whole city was devastated in the war. Destruction of some 50% of the housing then created the need and presented an occasion for vast schemes of reconstruction and Berlin became a laboratory of successive ideals of urbanism. The Communist government of the eastern sector began in 1949 by commissioning the reconstruction of the straight mile and a half long Frankfurter-Allee leading out of the city. It was widened to nearly 1oo yards (9om) flanked by apartment blocks of eight to ten stories punctuated at about one third of its length by twin towers capped with cupolas similar to those of eighteenth-century buildings in Berlin (a,s). Two identical high-rise buildings - derived from unexecuted Futurist designs of the 192os – were raised at the entrance. Claimed to be 'the first Socialist street', it was completed in 1960 as Stalin-Allee but renamed Karl-Marx-Allee in 1961 and reverted back to Frankfurter-Allee in late 1991. Partly in response to this essay in monumentality, of a type outmoded in western Europe and also strikingly different from the grids of drab tenements built for the masses throughout the Communist world, the administration of West Berlin sought to express contrary political and aesthetic ideals. In 1953 architects from fourteen countries, including Walter Gropius and Le Corbusier were invited to design apartment blocks to relieve the housing shortage and also to form a permanent international building exhibition, named the Berlin Interbau (zz,sa). A site on which only a few habitable buildings had survived, was cleared and laid out in accordance with Le Corbusier's notion of a city as a park with widely spaced high-rise buildings in it surrounded by roads for fast automobile traffic (see p. 837). Housing had priority, but on a bombed area where no-one wanted to live, near the notorious wall rushed up by East Berliners in 1961, three architecturally distinguished public buildings were erected to constitute a Kulturforum: the New National Gallery designed by Mies van der Rohe, a founder of the International Modernism who had emigrated to the USA after the suppression of the Bauhaus, and the Philharmonie concert hall and the State Library by Hans Scharoun (1893-1972), the creator of an individual Expressionist style who had been unable to practice under the Nazis. Neither the Interbau nor its successor of 1965, a high-rise garden suburb, was popular with the public. They seemed hardly less authoritarian than the Karl-Marx-Allee, prescribing how people were to live. Under increasing pressure from residents, the city's senate began to change its urban policy. In 1977 a new international building exhibition, IBA, was initiated for the development of sites smaller than the Interbau, more tightly planned and integrated with the remains of the inner city. At the same time a scheme was launched to fill gaps where small groups of buildings had been demolished and to rehabilitate damaged survivors. Commissions were given to members of the new postmodern generation of architects including Arata Isozaki, Rob Krier (b. 1948), Charles Moore and Aldo Rossi. The use of traditional materials and colours for exteriors was recommended and a limit set on height, sometimes flouted as by Zaha Hadid (b. 195o) whose commercial and low-cost apartment block enlivens the junction of two uninteresting streets (22,53). Many painters in the 198os and early 199os were caught within the postmodernist bind that if one made an abstract painting it would necessarily be read in relation to earlier modernist abstraction, and would therefore be seen in large part as a representation of them and not truly abstract at all. The two strands of Gerhard Richter's practice discussed above bear witness to his recognition of this dilemma. One could also see it in the ironic mid-198os use of an Op art style by New York painters Philip Taaffe (b. 1955), Ross Bleckner (b. 1949) and Peter Schuyff (b. 1958). The old modernist idea, associated with the critic Clement Greenberg, was that painting developed towards the selfexpression of its own essential qualities: color on a flat, rectangular surface. In the case of Taaffe, Bleckner and Schuyff, their exploitation of a tendency from the very end of that modernist trajectory, which seemed to emphasise visual effect at the expense of intellectual depth, could be taken as a defiant insistence upon continuing to work and to hope in the face of the appearance of AIDS. The wide variety of abstract elements in the canvases of Jonathan Lasker (b. 1948) could, equally, be understood to have arisen out of a meditation on what a painter could effectively do in a culture saturated with all kinds of imagery once Greenberg's idea had lost its meaning. The city of Vienna took the decision in the early 199os that, in acknowledgement of the part played by Austria in World War II, there should be a monument to the 65,o0o Austrian Jews who perished in the Holocaust. The monument was to be placed in Judenplatz, so named because the square was the site of a Synagogue destroyed in a pogrom in the fifteenth century, and in which many Jews had barricaded themselves to die rather than face the choice between forcible conversion to Christianity or execution. Whiteread was announced as the winner of the competition to choose a design early in 1996, but it was not until the autumn of 2000 that the monument was finally unveiled (z,60). It takes the form of a single rectangular concrete block that represents the interior space of a library. Rows of small indentations across the surface of the sculpture are the spines of all the books that are lost and cannot be read. Although a set of doors is visible at one end of the block, these, too, are concrete moulds and allow no access to the space which the work occupies. Around its base, the names of the Nazi concentration camps are inscribed in Hebrew, German and English. Set as it is in a largely Baroque square in the heart of Vienna, the square in which Mozart composed Cosi Fan Tutte, Whiteread's subdued monochrome concrete block appears as what the New York Times art critic Michael Kimmelman has described as an 'unsentimental rupture'. It is in the work's ability to break into the pleasant façade of an otherwise cosy existence that its power lies.'This monument shouldn't be beautiful, it must hurt,' said the Nazi-hunter Simon Wiesenthal during Original from UNIVERSITY OF VIRGINIA the opening ceremony. The discomfort felt and voiced by those who, for a variety of political, religious, historical and aesthetic reasons, did not want the monument built, the many conversations needed to answer and assuage these anxieties, and the lengthy negotiations over the exact positioning of the work and of how it would relate to the excavated remains of the old Synagogue, were thus part of the process of remembrance and remembering that the sculpture accomplishes. Whiteread has always worked by casting the space around objects rather than the objects themselves. Prior to embarking on the Vienna memorial, her most ambitious project had been House, in which the interior walls of a 3-story terraced house in the East End of London were sprayed with concrete (,6). The fabric of the house itself was then removed from around this concrete skin to leave a record not so much of the house as of its spaces and of the lives of all those who had occupied them. Since the original house was of a style familiar throughout London, the work spoke to countless thousands who had inhabited similar rooms. It was, though, like the Judenplatz memorial, inextricably linked to its specific location. Speaking of the Holocaust Memorial Whiteread said: 'I felt I was trying to describe a memory, but an ongoing memory. It's not just about one particular event; there is an awful history to this particular site. [The memorial] was made specifically for the Judenplatz. When I made House it was also absolutely specific to the site. When it was knocked down there were various offers to prolong its life; to put it on wheels and place it somewhere else. Strongly connected to the Modernist view which saw Western society as striving to progress towards the attainment of Utopian ideals, was the understanding that art, insofar as it was able to embody those ideals, had its part to play. Within the hugely extended reach of Capitalist markets since World War II, some critics, notably those associated with the art journal October, have recognised in much art an embrace of the values of what the French Situationist Guy Debord described as the 'society of the spectacle'. In other words, where the work of art once had a use value, it had now been reduced to something with mere exchange value. The pinnacle of this phenomenon was taken to be the Neo-Expressionism of the 198os. As a return to painting after the period of Minimal and Conceptual art it was seen as reactionary, and as a tendency whose success was greatly assisted by the ever-rising prices of works at auction and on the secondary art market, it was thought to be little more than a cynical way to make money. This, however, is a one-sided analysis, and one which sometimes appears pessimistic rather than judiciously critical. Jue Chang, which means something like 'the height of poetic perfection', consists of a heavy wooden structure which acts as the support for a large number of bedsteads, chairs and stools. Each piece of furniture has been used as a frame over which Chen Zhen has stretched animal skin to make an idiosyncratic collection of drums. Hooks have also been attached to the structure from which hang various wooden implements that can be taken down and used as drumsticks. Buddhist doctrine states that it is not possible to speak of one's own spiritual being, and that anyone wishing to pray to the Buddha on this subject should be beaten as a reminder that what is really true is ultimately inexpressible. The free beating of hides (the ideal substitute for the hides of our enemies and adversaries) serves to exorcise the obscure brutality and violence that is potentially present within all of us.' Born in Ouanzhou, Cai now lives and works in New York, and in his art he explores the dynamics of cultural exchange between his country of birth and the West. Following in Marco Polo's footsteps – the explorer had reached the city of Cai's birth on his travels before returning home - Cai visited Venice, sailing up the Grand Canal in a traditional Chinese fishing boat before mooring the vessel in the lagoon. Four years later, in 1999, Cai realised the project Rent Collection Courtyard (,). Using twelve tons of clay, Cai and his assistants produced a replica of a tableau, well known in China, that had originally been commissioned during the period of the Cultural Revolution. In the tradition of the terracotta army discovered in the tomb of Emperor Qiu Shihuangdi in 1974, the tableau shows groups of peasants paying their feudal tithes. As a piece of propaganda, the work was intended to show how much better conditions were under Communist rule than they had been during the period of feudalism. Versions of the original, which was subsequently expanded in line with cultural policy to include groups of workers, soldiers and students, were offered as gifts by Mao Zedong's government to the Communist states of North Korea, North Vietnam and Albania. Cai's new version, on which he was also helped by one of those who had made the original, remained incomplete. Taking over the intention behind the original to show how far conditions had changed over time and turning it to his own purposes, Cai's reworking of Rent Collecting Courtyard reveals once again just how much circumstances have altered in the intervening thirty or so years. Its deliberate state of noncompletion offers the prospect, the hope and the expectation that things will change again in the years to come. Difference itself can become the subject of spectacle unless we guard carefully against it. ## Spectacle The very fact of contemporary society's dependence upon spectacle - the importance of the media not simply in reporting, but more significantly in manufacturing those stories, events and personalities upon which it reports - has itself been the subject of much recent art. Where many have addressed this reality in critical terms, others have turned it to their advantage, not by cynically exploiting it for their own aggrandizement, but more in recognition of the fact that our ideas of what constitutes aesthetic experience at the turn of the millennium must necessarily take account of how the flow and control of information, the structures of work and play, and the manufacturing of excitement by the leisure industry shape every aspect of our lives. La Nona Ora (The Ninth Hour) is a life-like wax model of Pope John-Paul II (z,67). He lies slightly awkwardly on a rich expanse of red carpet, staff still in his hand, having been knocked over by a meteorite which lies heavily on top of him. A hole in the skylight above indicates where the rock crashed into the gallery, and the glass from the shattered panes lies strewn nearby. The ninth hour – the hour of Christ's death - might lead us to suppose that we are seeing the death of religion itself in this scene, but the Pope actually looks rather unconcerned about what has just happened.
