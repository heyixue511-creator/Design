Peter Bang's father, Camillo, was manager of the progressive Copenhagen department store A. C. Illum (founded 1891), which achieved remarkable success through an innovative sales strategy of offering middle-class customers a wide range of high-quality products by means of a knowledgeable and friendly staff. The store was managed by Camillo Bang in a way that saw the “customer as a person", and this no doubt had a formative influence on the way Peter Bang would later approach the design, manufacture and retail of his electronic products. Indeed, as a child he began experimenting with radio equipment, but found that the many batteries he required needed continual recharging, which involved the use of potentially hazardous acid. Frustrated by the shortcomings of these batteries, Bang eliminated the need for them altogether by building a mains powered radio receiver. Later, Bang trained as an engineer at the Àrhus Electrotechnical School, where he graduated in 1924. One of his fellow students was Svend Olufsen, who subsequently undertook radio experiments at his family's estate in Quistrup. While attempting to build a mains receiver, Olufsen turned to Bang for help. Bang had just returned from America, where he had been researching the latest developments in radio technology as pioneered by the Radio Corporation of America, which under the visionary leadership of David Sarnoff had commenced manufacture of its revolutionary Radio Music Box. In November 1925, the two friends officially founded Bang & Olufsen, which was committed to the idea of “power for the radio directly from the mains." To this end they began working on an aggregate to connect battery receivers to mains power supply which resulted in the introduction in 1926 of the firm's first marketed product, the B&O Eliminator. A year later, they developed their own mains powered radio known as the Five Lamper, which had a wooden cabinet inlaid with classical motifs and three Bakelite dials, Although B&O equipment was at the cutting-edge of radio technology and renowned for the clarity of its sound, it was wrapped up as period-style furniture. In 1933, ![](images/553173600442376b67b42be272e677a452a5b041b71abe4825dc6aa88a067c10.jpg) the Five Lamper radio and radio gramophone were lambasted for their old-fashioned aesthetic when the prominent architect and designer Ole Wanscher commented ironically, “To make the radio fit in nicely is easily achieved through Caucasian walnut and badger legs or Chinoiserie – like a theatre design conceived by the furniture dealer's youngest apprentice",. Taking this criticism to heart, Peter Bang and Svend Olufsen decided to embrace a Modern aesthetic that would be in keeping with the advanced technology they were pioneering. The same year Bang designed the sleek Hyperbo 5 RG Steel radio-gramophone, which was inspired by Marcel Breuer's (1902– 1981) tubular metal cantilevered chairs. This remarkably forward-looking design was sold alongside other radios and radio-gramophones that continued to be housed in Art Deco inspired wooden cabinets. The real breakthrough for the company came in 1938, however, when it developed a radio that was housed in a casing made entirely of the new “material of a thousand uses” – Bakelite. During the Second World War,B&O established an illegal radio service for communications between the British and the Danish Freedom Fighters. Because of the company's links with the resistance, B&O's factory was bombed in 1945. Shortly afterwards, the factory was rebuilt and production of radios resumed, butagain the company was criticized, this time by the celebrated designer Poul Henningsen, who said its products had the appearance of furniture-like pastiches. To justify the company's by-line – “The Danish Hallmark of Quality” – B&O began working with some of Denmark's leading architects and designers in the mid-1950s, includingSigvard Bernadotte and Acton Bjørn.These collaborations produced spectacular results – the company's new generation high-quality products were completely integrated, and their refined Modern forms communicated a sophisticated design rhetoric to a new and highly receptive design-conscious audience. In the 1960s, B&O pioneered the design of integrated stereorecord players, “for those who discusstaste and quality before price”. Having become a pioneer of audio technology, the company later launched several landmark designs by JacobJensen, which married superlative quality with sleek good looks. Jensen's “easy touch” layouts and “flush-designed” systems quite literally redefinedthe design vocabulary of hi-fi equipment. Throughout the 1960s and 1970s, Jensen's designs came to epitomize both the technical and aesthetic refinement of Danish design. During the 1980s, however,the company suffered badly from Asian competition and eventually formed an alliance with Philipsin order to survive. In the early 1990s, B&O changed its approach to manufacturing, moving strategically from mass-production to order-production. There was also an important shift in design terms, which was implemented by David Lewis (b. 1939), who had been Jensen's assistant. Lewis' revolutionary Beosystem 2500 (1990), with its all-in-one layout, set itself apart from the anonymous stacked black boxes produced by B&O's competitors. More recently David Lewis has designed Bang & Olufsen's first plasmascreen television, the Beo-Vision 5, which can either be hung on or propped up against a wall – an innovative, flexible and elegant solution thatwas inspired by paintings in a gallery that were being removed to make room for a new exhibition. Believing that aesthetics are as important to consumers as function and ease of use, Bang & Olufsen skilfully balances external design with internal technological considerations in order to create distinctive products that powerfully communicate the company's values and vision about technology and product concepts. Bang initially studied sculpture and later trained as an architect at the Kongelige Danske Kunstakademi (Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts) from 1916 to 1921. In 1924 he began working as an industrial designer for the Holmegaard glassworks and four years later was appointed artisticdirector. Having left Holmegaardin1942 Bang subsequently established his own studio and began working as a freelance designer. Between 1942and 1957 he designedceramics for the Nymølle faïence factory and metalware products for Pan Aluminium and the silversmith F. Hingelberg. In 1957 he was appointed artistic director of the Kastrup glassworks, which subsequently merged with Holmegaard in 1965. While at Kastrup, Bang designed no-nonsense glassware that corresponded with his guiding principle that designed products should be sensible, simple and pleasurable to use. As one of the leading functionalists in Denmark, Bang produced numerous glassware designs, such as his well-known Hogla wine glass and his extensive Primula range (both produced byHolmegaard), which were notable for their simplified rational forms. Although his work was influenced by the Modern Movement, Bang steered clear of the dogmatic functionalism emanating from Germany and produced designs that were softer and more human-focused. By refining his designs thoroughly until every detail had achieved near functional and aesthetic perfection, Bang was able to create some of the most beautiful and timeless pieces of glassware of the 20th century. ![](images/6a2ff0ed19ce607f4bf4dfa4a3f2dd8bdd1be2559d8dcb09515cb0c2953cd247.jpg)
