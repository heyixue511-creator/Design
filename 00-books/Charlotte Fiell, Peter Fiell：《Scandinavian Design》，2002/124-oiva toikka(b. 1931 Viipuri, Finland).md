Oiva Toikka, Kastehelmi (Dew) glassware for Nuutajärvi, 1964 – Although best known for his “art" glass pieces, Toikka has also designed for mass production, most notably this mouldedglass tableware range ![](images/42c2d7bd10fab780eefd33afebd5e8a9538ae479bf7dfef7baf35c5f9259a105.jpg) # oiva toikka (b. 1931 Viipuri, Finland) ![](images/0dd2555a6dae0cac18eeff5e2c36860d0e9b9cc5603d7e46bc202d570fbc82b2.jpg) One of Finland's most inventive glass designers, Oiva Toikka studied at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu (University of Art and Design) in Helsinki from 1953 to 1956. Followinghis training, he worked as a ceramics designer for Arabia from 1956 to 1959, and later taught at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu from 1959 to 1961. He began working at Nuutajärvi in 1963 and the same year designed his gigantic free-blown Kurkkupurkki (Pickle Jar) glass sculptures, which were among his first one-off pieces in glass. His subsequent studio art glass, which was distinguished by the use of bright colours and bold forms, had an abstracted pictorial quality that was inspired by both folk-art and contemporary design. In 1969 he produced his well-known Lollipop Isle glass sculptures for Nuutajärvi which epitomized the joyful and carefree Pop aesthetic of the time and embodied the growing confidence of the Finnish glass industry. The 1960s were the golden age of Finnish glass design, and as Toikka noted, “The sixties was in a way a wonderful time, because one was still carried along by the idea that Finnish was good. It was also a happy time in that people still had faith in glass and design generally ... it was fun ... the sky was the limit as far as the appreciation of all the applied arts was concerned." Like many other Finnish artist-designers, Toikka also produced a number of designs for industrial mass production, such as his well-known Kastehelmi (Dew)glassware(1964), which became one of Nuutajärvi's best-selling product lines. Embellished with concentric circles of pearl-like beads, the Kastehelmi range included plates, bowls and candlesticks, and was available in a range of colours – grey, brown, red, blue, green and colourless. Toikka's later Pioni production tableware similarly explored the expressive potential of moulded glass. Apart from his glassware, Toikka also produced fanciful costume and scenery designs for opera and theatre productions in Tampere and Savonlinna. In 1977 he designed the first of his annual Vuosikuutiot (Year Cube) cubic paperweightsfor Nuutajärvi, which can be stacked to create a miniature decorative glass wall. Often disregarding function in favour of fantastic form, Toikka's playful, individualistic and frequently inventive designs possess a poetic quality that conveys the almost alchemical nature of glass making. He has received numerous awards for his glassware, ![](images/645fcc6ee33e27a40a0bdfdc60e711b30f98301f86da1172f4a008f57d92a564.jpg) ![](images/85ca452d78c407d4770898d79576761a75c950def51591fe09fac6eeee8290b9.jpg) including the prestigious Lunning Prize in 1970 and the Pro Finlandia Medal in 1980. More recently, he has become best known for his distinctive glass bird figures that have been produced for over two decades, witha new “species” being added to the range each year. Speaking of these widely admired pieces, Toikka has said that, “Natureis an unending source of inspirationfor me – and glass, as a natural material, is its best interpreter”. Through the extraordinary range of his work, Toikka has blurred the boundaries of art and design while creating some of the most expressive pieces of studio and production glassware to have ever come out of Finland.
