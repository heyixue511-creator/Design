![](images/1a15e676cf670251d823162e8778fa9c5a7ddcac288bf8839ab237bc95d44fb7.jpg) Göran Hongell, Aarne glassware range for Karhula, 1949-1950 - now produced by littala # göran hongell (b. 1902 Helsinki, Finland – d. 1973) ![](images/43a36a759851526ac78fb8649115117b226c91923428fa0013f8baddd07f6843.jpg) The son of a master builder, Göran Hongell studied decorative painting at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu (University of Art and Design), Helsinki, from 1919 to 1922. At that point in time “decorative painting” studies were the forerunner of more formal industrial design courses and were intended to equip students with the necessary skills to become either craftsmen in their own right, artistic directors working in factories, workshops and studios, or art teachers.During this period in Finland there was a general concern to forge a new and distinctive national style (as a resultof the country's newly-found independence) and to improve the aesthetic quality of industrially-manufactured products. In response to this climate of reform in design, Hongell became a member of Ornamo (the Finnish Association of Decorative Artists) in 1924. Having undoubtedly read an article in the 1927 Ormano yearbook written by Arttu Brummer (1891–1951), which praised the Svenska Slöjdföreningen (Swedish Society of Craft and Industrial Design)and its promotion of “Beautiful Things for Everyday Use”, Hongell and the decorative painter Gunnar Forsström established a design practice that initially specialised in the design of murals and stained glass. Following this, in 1928 they submitted designs to the Riihimäki glassworks' competition for “useful and tasteful types of glass for everyday use” and won fourth prize. In 1932, Hongell was awarded third prize in a similar glassware competition held by the Karhula glassworks for his Congorilla vase, which was decorated with simple marine-lifemotifs, and the same year he exhibited a series of black moulded designs with silver enamelling at the Finnish Society of Craft and Design exhibition. Also in 1932 Hongell began working as a part-time artistic adviser to Karhulaand the factorysubsequently began manufacturing his unusual decorative black glass designs with classical motifs, which were exhibited at the 1933 (V)Milan Triennale. During the early and mid-1930s Hongell designed a number of cut and engraved glass pieces for Karhula that played off the optical qualities of the material. It was not until 1937, however, that he began designing pressed-glass products that were suited to large-scale industrial mass production. During the 1940s and 1950s, his glassware became increasingly utilitarian, with simplified forms and plain undecorated surfaces. ![](images/97de9ba7b1180932b07fea178b51439dedd783602546bcbd594b5d0e93207c83.jpg) Among his best-known designs from this period were his “sea-green”glass vases, which were designed specifically for export, and his Aarne glasses(1949–1950), which continue to be produced bylittala – having been described in Hufvudstadsbladet in 1951 as, “a compromise between the popular stemmed glasses and the cheaper unstemmed ones. They feel good in the hand and possess a 'festive' quality.”" In 1957, Hongell retired and a year after his death in 1973, a retrospective exhibition of his work was held at the Karhula Glass Museum. Although less wellknown than later designers such as Timo Sarpaneva or Tapio Wirkkala, Hongell was one of the great pioneers of industrial glass design in Finland and was one of the first Scandinavian designers to produce high-quality everyday glassware that was not only extremely functional but also possessed a real sense of style. ![](images/a22a1c05867f819ce9a7f52780360dc937d6437d61fd0d9a2597b4fb53919aae.jpg) △Göran Hongell, blue crystal vase for Karhula, 1930s 4American advertisement for Karhula showing a cut crystal bowl by Göran Hongell late 1930s A SELECTION OF OUTSTANDING VASES AND BOWLS, HAND BLOWN AND HAND ENGRAYED BY KARHULA'S WORLD FAMED CRAFTSMEN. Saara Hopea, Pantteri vases for Nuutajärvi, 1954 ![](images/d2b72a3ca6e527b8f0275280c65fc9b702502f44e402e40ccae36473f61378ef.jpg) ![](images/346f6127efab8053ed56ed3de54ef0b45ce783c7e50037e4f0a0aeaaefd197c2.jpg) ![](images/13d570544037580346b87d0c3526f52d997440adf60b8e50720df95ab7d4ea63.jpg) # saareae hopea (Finish 256 d.1984) ![](images/e5e4576556dde3ed2fc0a26b94280099fe39bc4a2139ded5e8c9173c8da6d1b3.jpg) The Finnish ceramicist, silverware and glass designer, Saara Hopea, trained as an interior designer at the Konstindustriella Läroverket (College of Industrial Art) in Helsinki. After her studies she worked as a freelance interior designer from 1946 to 1949. In 1952she joined the Arabia ceramics factory and for seven years worked as an assistant to Kaj Franck. She showed her work alongside Franck's in a special exhibition held in Helsinki in 1954, and also participated in a number of other landmark exhibitions including the 1955 Helsingborg “H55” exhibition. At Arabia she designed several unusual ceramic pieces, including the Astarte hors-d'oeuvres tray, which was named after the many-breasted Phoenician fertility goddess and made up of nine “joined” bowls. In 1954 (X) and 1957 (XI) she was awarded silver medals for her “practical pottery” at the Milan Triennale. During the 1950s, she also designed glassware for Nuutajärvi – some of which was suitable for industrial production, such as her moulded pitcher and stacking tumblers, which won a silver medal at the 1954(X) Milan Triennale. Her higher volume designs were generally distinguished by clean undecorated forms and a rainbowof muted colours (the latter being a renowned characteristic of her glassworks), while her other work, such as her exquisite spotted Pantteri vases, fell into the category of “art glass”. Hopea also designed silverware for her family's business, Ossian Hopea, in Porvoo. In 1960, she married the American Oppi Untracht, and the same year emigrated to the United States where she worked as a freelance silver designer for, among others, Potter and Mellen Inc. Her husband taught her enamelling techniques and she subsequently produced numerous copper bowls and plates adorned with sweeping geometric patterns made by overlapping coloured enamels. Returning to her native Finland in 1967, she continued to produce enamelled metalware designs until 1980. Saara Hopea's work not only reveals her unquestionable mastery of materials, form and manufacturing techniques, it also stands in testimony to the significant role women have played in the history of Scandinavian design – a contribution that has facilitated the development of a more humanistic approach to design, especially with those products intended for industrial mass production. ![](images/8ad66aecae75d32cfb8cb6c0d41070cbdb9f1af2d26c74561cba785631ba4052.jpg) ∇ Saara Hopea, Model no. 1718 glasses for Nuutajärvi, c. 1951 ∇∇Saara Hopea, Model no. 1618 pitcher with Model no. 1718 glasses for Nuutajärvi, c. 1951
