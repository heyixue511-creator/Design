## 19–24.

## World Famous Children's Book Series, 1951

This series of comic books borrowed heavily from Western children's stories, with titles such as Pinocchio, Dance Performance, and The Lone Ranger. In some cases the comics' English name was used while others were translated into Chinese, as in Tsam-gao Adventures. Pinocchio's English name was removed completely; the Chinese version was titled Muk-nau Kiyu Kee (Wooden Puppet Adventures). However, the story and drawings closely resemble the original.

## 25.

## Wah Kiu Daily Supplement, 1951

After the Communists took control of mainland China, a great wave of immigrants hit Hong Kong. In response to the needs of a growing population (and it's children), some daily newspapers added columns and supplements geared toward kids. This column appeared in Wah Kiu Daily as a Sunday supplement. It featured fairy tales, stories about science, quizzes, and comic strips.

## 26.

## Children's Healthy Series Literature, 1952

In the early 1950s there were few talented local comics artists, and many Hong Kong comic books were simply direct copies of American examples. Many were reprints of the original, with the text translated into Chinese and references changed to local names and places. In other cases, American comics were redrawn. This comic book, The Christmas Gift, features a redrawn version of Felix the Cat. Neither the reprinted nor the redrawn comics give credit to the original sources.

## 27.

The Children's Educational Picture Book Collection, 1952

While many manhua publishers used American cartoons as their source, this series instead copied illustrated Western fairy tales. The collection included The Phantom of the Opera, The Snowman's Winter, Little Paradise, and Little Penguin Travels to the South. Although based on Western sources, readers were seldom informed of the original English titles; sometimes, local artists or editors received credit. These kinds of comic books were priced low enough for kids to buy them with candy money.

## 28-29.

## The Blessed Young Children Magazine, 1952

This magazine for elementary-school children was put out by a Christian educational publisher. It mainly printed Christian stories, particularly from the Bible, but also included nonreligious stories intended to promote morality. The magazine contained more text than most comic books; the pictures functioned primarily to support the story, reflecting the editorial bias toward the benefits of reading.
