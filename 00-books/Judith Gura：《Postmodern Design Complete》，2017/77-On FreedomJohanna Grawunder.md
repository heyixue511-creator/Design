# On FreedomJohanna Grawunder In the fall of i986, Design Gallery Milano installed an exhibition of Murano hand-blown glass objects: thirty four vases designed by ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") for MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design"). I had been working at Sottsass Associati in Milan since the summer of i985 and had seen a few previous Memphis exhibitions. They were big events; Sottsass's international tribe came out in full force. Many participated, designing furniture and objects for the Memphis collections, and those are the famous names we all know: MICHELE DE LUCCHI, ANDREA BRANZI, NATALIE DU PASQIUER, GEORGE SOWDEN, MARCO ZANINI, ALDO CIBIC, MATTEO THUN (see all under “Design"), and PETER SHIRE (see under “Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design"), as well as MICHAEL GRAVES (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), SHIRO KURAMATA ![](images/fda54d29c1c3556d2b314ec33fd1d0bfc40fa5de4e24f1b82aa3f6b5d17358f2.jpg) (see under "Design"), HANS HOLLEIN (see under "Architecture"), and others. Eventually, younger designers entered the group, including James Irvine. MASSIMO IOSA GHINI (see under “Design"), Ferruccio Laviani, Beppe Caturegli, Giovannella Formica, and Shuji Hisada. In Milan around that time, designing a piece for Memphis meant serious street cred. This created a polarization between one school of thought and others, stimulating discussion, criticism, and backlash minimalism, forcing the design community to take positions—something all great revolutions do. On a tall, almost room-sized block of whitepainted MDF, Sottsass displayed his thirty-four shocking sculptures of translucent brightly colored glass. It was a Warriors of Xi'an effect (the celebrated Terracotta Army) but in geometrical, elegant glass, blown high and thin: some with dangling earrings tied to looping glass rings with thin gold threads, some with forms blown inside forms, and some heretically glued together (a no-no in traditional glass blowing) to form death-defying constructions. Madonna's “Like a Virgin" was pounding out of speakers. I was alone in this room for perhaps four minutes, but the experience revealed to me the emotional, transcendent, drug-like power of design in one dramatic moment. Here, illuminated simply with a few spotlights, was human achievement: the result of hundreds of man hours of Murano glass-blowers, each artisan a master of the ancient art, with thousands of hours of experi ence, failures, and successes, a continuing process of error-correction not only within the knowledge process of mastering the specific skill of glass-blowing, but also live, with each piece blown. Though Sottsass avoided the label "artist" most of his life, I think he began to enjoy the confusion and the opportunity for pure theoretical and formal speculation. Though all of these improbable glass objects were “vases"—they had small or large compartments where you could, in theory, put water and flowers—this was a construct to keep these objects in the realm of design: not to minimize their power or worth, but to enrich the discourse and challenges of design by bringing it towards a more theoretical art. Memphis created design-art. The cognitive disfluency that resulted in the difficulty of categorizing these artifacts as design or art, or within micro-genres and stylistic labels like neo-expressionist, Pop, minimalist, or postmodern, led to more thoughtful consideration and, ultimately, deeper underštanding. I think that Memphis, and for me this particular collection of glass objects, was a defining moment, in a long line of cultural transgressions, that allowed design a new and brighter freedom. Design is free. Unharnessed from the traditional limits of industry (costs, production, marketing, sales) through technology and materials science on the one hand, and the very specific market needs for innovation on the other, design-art is a recent nomenclature that covers what used to be called decorative art, but also gets into what we could call theoretical design. The idea is that design can be an idiosyncratic art form, not solely site-specific, and/or not necessarily based on function or the industrial prerogative. Some of us designers have a residual moralism that tells us it must be functional. That is why when I design, say, a chandelier, I spend a lot of my time and production research concerned with the quality of light. Most of us also have respect for production technique and artisan craft, and this translates into intense micro-education with every new piece produced, listening carefully to artisan expertise, and then, sometimes, trying to exalt or deny this craft. But content-wise (and design is a richly saturated content-based endeavor), we all bring something unique to the discourse. For example, I am attracted to that part of American cultural heritage defined by austerity and hard, strong gestures. This theoretical aspect of design, very personal, is an intangible quality that cannot be described so much as experienced. It can be part of a narrative used to place an object or a collection of objects into the contemporary landscape, but it is also an expression of freedom. Designers and designer-artists of my generation— Marc Newson, Marcel Wanders, Hella Jongerius, Arik Levy, Konstantin Grcic, Robert Stadler, Karim Rashid, Campana Brothers, to name just a few—are not at all from the same cultural lineage, but many of us cut our chops at some point in Milan, during or soon after Memphis. Later, others like Maarten Baas, Joris Laarman, Nacho Carbonell, and Studio Job unabashedly embraced a system that had been broken open to expressionistic artistic endeavor. The issues and obsessions each of us metabolizes into our work are idiosyncratic by definition. Our processes and objectives are different. And it is really, really hard to pin most of us down into a category such as “artist" or “designer" (we squirm). Contemporary culture is story-based. Though there are very deep currents flowing within it that tie together all aspects of culture, from politics to philosophy to music, art, and, yes, design, the popular consumption of "narrative" in contemporary culture often and oddly makes it seem like a neoteric tail-chase. Arguably, during the time of Memphis and, before that, of Radical Design and Superstudio\*, contemporary culture was more idea-based. There seemed to be common cause in reacting, one way or another, to the failures and successes of Modernism, and to proposing “solutions” (Superstudio's Continuous Monument comes to mind). Now design, I mean art-design, theoretical design, is fragmented. There are threads, like the return of materiality and technology as prime initiatives (a neo-neo-Modernism?) and an awareness of sustainability, but there is really no “movement" to speak of, beyond the meta-idea of the existence of the art-design market itself. Design-art can be found in galleries and at art fairs. This is new. It gets discussed, published, and collected. Sometimes it resonates in the larger cultural landscape—and sometimes it is slightly out of sync. Though the abstract nature of much art-design work today, including some of my own, might seem to be striving for a sort of timelessness, design is anything but timeless. The very personal act of establishing frameworks from which to work is empowering and subjective, but temporal. We (designers) live within our culture and time. We are not outsiders. We are storytellers. Our stories, even nostalgic ones, or futuristic ones, are based and realized in our specific, technological cultural, and (sometimes) market-based moment. Sottsass was a guerrilla. Even his earliest work was a shot across the bow of the business-minded cultural elite yacht that had reduced Modernism from an ideology to a stylish new fashion, losing much significance in the process. Memphis was urban guerrilla warfare on the pretensions of “good-taste" design. It questioned the concept of objective perfection and the tyranny of codified “correct" design solutions. And therefore, it exists comfortably in the intellectual worldview of postmodern philosophy. It offered an alternative, sometimes transcendent, revolutionary way to see and interact with the world. And, once you have seen Sottsass's thirty-four glass vases, you cannot unsee them.
