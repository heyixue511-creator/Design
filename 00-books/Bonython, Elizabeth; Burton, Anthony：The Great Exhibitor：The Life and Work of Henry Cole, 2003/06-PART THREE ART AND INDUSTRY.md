# PART THREE: ART AND INDUSTRY # CHAPTER 6 # Felix Summerly Henry Cole, after he had become a figure of authority in the art world, would quite often be attacked as an untrained ignoramus in artistic matters. Indeed, even one of the most zealous defenders of his posthumous reputation, Sir Henry Trueman Wood, said that ‘he had no aesthetic judgment and no artistic power'.' Did Cole really know nothing about art? How did he become involved in it? At least he began with some capacity for the co-ordination of hand and eye, as is witnessed by his calligraphic abilities at school. We have seen, too, that he took drawing lessons from David Cox and Charles Wild in the 182os, and was encouraged by Elizabeth Palgrave. We shall see that in due course he took up etching. He believed that his creative capacities included an artistic element, ‘some instinct which, although not sufficiently cultivated to make me an artist, I have always felt I possessed'.2 As to his capacity to appreciate art, we know that from early adulthood he visited art galleries.3 In the winter of 1827-8 he attended lectures on anatomy, perspective, painting and sculpture at the Royal Academy, and would have continued with this course the following year, but found that the lecturers just repeated themselves annually.4 By this time he had evidently developed architectural and antiquarian interests, because he tells us that the Dean of Westminster gave him permission to draw in the abbey ‘as early as 1827'. There is also some evidence of artistic sensibility in his early travel journals. He made a tour in Scotland in 1833, sending back to his fiancée, Marian, conscientious descriptions of the terrain, assessing it as it presented itself to 'the discerning eye of the Artist', and dropping in references to Richard Payne Knight, high priest of the cult of the picturesque. Townscape as well as landscape attracted his criticism, as when in Edinburgh he asserted that 'throughout the New Town . . . instances of bad taste are perpetually forcing themselves on the attention'.6 The following year he and Marian, now married, travelled to Cornwall. 'In respect of the Picturesqueness of the County .. .', commented Cole, 'I should not give it a very strong character .... The grandeur or quaintness or boldness of every scene pleases but they do not furnish sufficiency for the pencil to work upon.' Nonetheless, he and Marian did make a number of sketches.7 In Yorkshire in 1835, Cole was assiduous in architecture-spotting, assigning columns and tracery to their correct periods. He also continued to lay down the law on taste. Of Beverley Minster he said: 'This relique of the catholic Church is preserved in good repair . . . and excepting the abominable whitewash, which has been daubed throughout the interior, the reparations and attentions of modern times are not altogether in bad taste.’ After admiring a little medieval church at Hemingborough, he disparaged modern churches: 'They are erected upon a lathe & Plaster sentiment of hypocrisy - wanting firmness, consistency & feeling - and the physical materials are of corresponding flimsiness and rottenness.'8 From his journals, then, one can see that Cole had some knowledge of the history of art and architecture, and cultivated aesthetic responses, albeit at a fairly commonplace level. As a tyro journalist, Cole wrote as a critic of music and drama, but not, it seems, of art, until early in 1838 he embarked on a substantial article for the London and Westminster Review on contemporary wood engraving - a well-researched survey that is still informative to present-day scholars. Wood engraving, a more subtly expressive technique than traditional woodcut because it used finer tools on harder wood, was pioneered by Thomas Bewick $\left( \mathbb { I } 7 5 3 \mathrm { - } \mathbb { I } 8 2 8 \right)$ . While Bewick worked for most of his life in his native Northumberland, the new technique of wood engraving necessarily moved to London, the centre of publishing. Here Robert Branston $\left( \mathrm { I } 7 7 8 \mathrm { - } \mathrm { I } 8 2 7 \right)$ stood at the head of a 'London school'of wood engravers. It was his disciples whom Cole studied, still quite a small group of craftsmen (and some women). In May 1838 and through the summer, Cole went to see engravers such as John Jackson, Orrin Smith and John Thompson $\left( \mathrm { I } 7 8 \varsigma \mathrm { - } \mathrm { I } 8 6 6 \right)$ , the final name being generally regarded as the leading exponent of wood engraving in the 183os. By the autumn, Cole had become firm friends with Thompson, and with his family. Thompson was 53 in 1838, and had two sons somewhat younger than Cole: Charles Thurston, born in 1816 and Richard Anthony, born in 1819. Thurston was to marry Marian Cole's sister Charlotte in 1857, and, moving over from wood engraving, in which he had been trained, into photography, joined Cole's South Kensington Museum as its principal photographer. Richard also joined the museum when it opened in South Kensington in 1857, and ended his working life there as an assistant director. John Thompson lived in Kensington, partly because it was convenient for Chiswick, where the printer with whom he mostly dealt, Charles Whittingham, had his press. Whittingham also was soon to become one of Cole's important collaborators. As the Coles lived in Kensington at various addresses, the Thompsons were near enough to be neighbours, and the families constantly visited each other. While friendship with Thompson brought Cole to the fringes of the artistic world, his professional contacts with artists seem to have begun in the following year, when, implementing the penny post under Rowland Hill, he had to commission designs for stamps and envelopes. Naturally he went to the top first, consulting the president of the Royal Academy, but eventually arrived at William Mulready. 'On the 13th December, 1839, I made my first visit to Mr. Mulready, and began an acquaintance which lasted till his death in 1863."9 Mulready, who was of Irish birth though he had lived in London from the age of six, was a near contemporary of John Thompson, having been born in $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 7 8 6$ . He had a wild streak in his nature, which was exercised, perhaps relatively harmlessly, in his enthusiasm for prize-fighting, but which had, more damagingly, led him into an injudiciously young marriage and an acrimonious break-up. He remained responsible for his children, as a lone parent, and his family relations were not without violence. He was a superb draughtsman, and a rather slow painter. His paintings, mostly scenes of ordinary life, had (surprisingly for such a rough man) an exquisitely elegant balance and finish, and vibrant colouring. He lived in Linden Grove, Kensington Gravel Pits (now Linden Gardens, Notting Hill Gate), so he too was near enough, as a neighbour to the Coles, for frequent socialising. Another local contact in artistic circles was Sir Augustus Callcott (1779-1844), who was a very successful landscape painter and Royal Academician, although inevitably somewhat overshadowed by Turner. Cole got to know him through his physician, Dr Merriman, in February 1841. Callcott also lived in Kensington Gravel Pits, and it was largely through the influence of the Callcotts that a group of artists settled in this suburb of London'.10 The gravel pits that gave the area its name had been worked, it seems, since the Middle Ages. They were found to the north and south of the present Bayswater Road, around Notting Hill Gate. The area was thought to be particularly healthy on account of its dry soil and bracing air',11 and a village of houses, embowered in trees, had casually grown up around the pits. Callcott introduced Cole to his wife Maria, who became an important influence during the short time Cole knew her before her death. A travel writer and art historian, she had been bedridden for some years, but presided over an artistic salon, in a room in which she had created an exotic setting for herself. 'Vines had been trained as natural blinds, and living arabesques were made among the shoots and branches by the mice and birds, as they came half-tamed, to take the meals that Lady Callcott daily placed for them.' In the room ‘prints, choice and rare as works of art, or associated with loved objects, covered the walls, unless otherwise occupied by paintings or sculptures, memorials of Wilkie and Chantrey and others'.12 Cole took to her immediately. 'She is a sharp witted woman, fitful as it seemed, rather trying after effect & talking blue & off hand.'13 Another time he said of her: 'I like the fine hearty sense of truth in her, & the absence of all humbug: she said no taste was bad taste but an exclusive taste.'14 Maria Callcott loved children, and always welcomed Laetitia Cole when her father brought her to visit, once giving her a book and a doll.15 She wrote stories for children, the most famous being Little Arthur's History of England (1835). Cole helped her by doing the legwork involved in putting her last children's book, The Little Brackenburners and Little Mary's Four Saturdays, through the press. Augustus Callcott ‘had close links with many continental artists. His interests, which included German and Italian primitives as well as the German Nazarenes, proved a formative influence on younger friends, including William Dyce'.16 Through the Callcott circle, Cole enlarged his acquaintance with artists. His contacts with Dyce, who was two years older, were not close, though the schools of design were an arena in which both men worked. Dyce was soon to take a leading part in the ‘fresco revival', the project keenly advocated by Prince Albert, to decorate the principal rooms of the new Houses of Parliament with frescos depicting scenes of British history. Other collaborators in this scheme became known to Cole. One of these was John Callcott Horsley (1817-19o3), a nephew of Augustus Callcott, who also lived in the Gravel Pits village. Another was J. R. Herbert (1 81o-9o), a friend of Pugin and a colleague of Dyce at the School of Design. He lived in Gloucester Road, a little way south of Kensington. H. J. Townsend (181o-9o), also involved in the fresco revival and a colleague of Dyce at the School of Design, lived in Old Brompton, not far away. Yet another fresco painter was Charles West Cope (1811-9o), who lived in Hyde Park Gate, just south of Kensington Gore. In an adjoining house lived his close friend Richard Redgrave (1 8o4–88). Redgrave was a painter, who provided a cartoon for a Westminster fresco but did not execute it; he turned to art administration and became Cole's closest, lifelong associate. Cope stood as godfather to Cole's son Alan. Other Kensington painters, outside the fresco group, were Callcott's nephew Thomas Creswick (1811-69), a landscape painter, and the genre painter Thomas Webster (18oo-86). Both lived in the Gravel Pits village. Not far away, in Victoria Road (running south from Kensington High Street), lived the sculptor John Bell $\left( \vec { : } \mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 \mathrm { ~ I ~ I ~ } { - } 9 5 \right)$ .17 Cole got to know all these artists in the early 184os, and socialised with them frequently. Webster took him to a meeting of the Graphic Society, which Cole called 'a sort of artists conversazione', reputedly the best of such groups.18 It was here (for he attended quite frequently) that Cole was introduced to Creswick and Redgrave. It will be noted that, while Callcott and Mulready were of an older generation - and so was John Linnell (1792-r882), resident in Porchester Terrace, Bayswater, to whom Mulready introduced Cole - the others were almost all of an age with Cole. Quite suddenly, he found himself part of an artistic peer group, and a group that looked to Prince Albert as its leader. Cole's first formal engagement with art was in a literary mode: he wrote guidebooks to historic and artistic sights in or near London. The first was to Hampton Court, published in 1841 (fig.4). The Queen had allowed the public to visit Hampton Court since 1838 and Cole already knew it well, having studied the building during holidays spent nearby at Shepperton and Weybridge. He decided that the pictures on display there had already been well described by other authors, so he concentrated on the building, and the restorations being done at that time by Thomas Willement, who was the best restoration architect of the day, famous as an expert on heraldry, and the first to design stained-glass windows in a believable medieval style. Cole became friendly with him, and was invited to Willement's house in London where he admired his collection of medieval artefacts, or, as Cole was still heedless enough to call them, ‘knick knackeries'. Later on he visited Davington Priory, Willement's house in Kent, soon after he bought it.19 Although the two men did not become close friends, acquaintance with Willement provided Cole not only with a link to architectural history but also, as will be seen, with useful contacts in other directions. Cole's Handbook was declared to be 'By Felix Summerly Esq. With embellishments engraved on wood by ladies'. The nom de plume was adopted because convention precluded a public servant from publishing under his own name. No one knows why he chose Felix Summerly', but it suggests happiness in the summertime and is typical of Cole and his ebullient and cheerful personality. The 'ladies' who made the illustrations were not just females, but ladies of rank: Lady Callcott and Lady Palgrave. Cole also called on other female friends and relations to help out with illustrations, and he drew some himself, as did David Cox junior. Hampton Court (which had been published by H. Cunningham, with whom Cole fell out)2" was followed in the next year by Westminster Abbey (published by George Bell, who went on to publish the rest of the series). The abbey was another building that Cole knew exceptionally well, having worked in the Chapter House, and made drawings of the tombs in the r82os. He claimed to be the first to notice the artistic merits of the monuments, saying in his autobiography that the building ‘is a perfect museum of illustrations of the Technical Arts, from the days of Edward the Confessor to the present time, of sculptures, brasses, mural paintings, glass, mosaics bronzes, &c'.21 Again Lady Callcott and Lady Palgrave drew some of the illustrations, but David Cox junior did a couple as well, as did Cole himself. The engraving was done by Marian's sisters Laura and Charlotte Bond, and by John Thompson's sister Eliza and his daughters Augusta and Isabel. The first two guidebooks were very successful. Westminster Abbey, for instance, was hailed as ‘a well arranged and correct description, neither too popular nor too pedantic'.22 Both books were translated into French, and ran into many editions. Cole then wrote a series of handbooks, to the National Gallery, the Soane Museum, the Society of Arts, and other galleries that were open to the public free of charge. These could only function as aides-mémoire, or reminiscences' as he called them,23 but the provision of images after Old Master paintings in a guidebook was most unusual at the time, and is another sign of Cole's enterprising originality.24 Among Cole's guidebooks was a slim pamphlet on the Temple Church (fig.5), where restorations by Thomas Willement had just been completed. For the floors of the church, Willement had bought tiles from Herbert Minton, the most innovative and successful china manufacturer in the world, based at Stoke-on-Trent. These tiles, a re-creation of a medieval art form, 'were made after the models of the ancient and most interesting pavement in the Chapter House of Westminster Abbey'.25 Willement introduced Cole to Minton (plate 2 1), and their collaboration and friendship persisted throughout Cole's career. Their first joint project was to bring out an educational toy, consisting of small coloured tiles, made by Minton, using a process recently invented by Richard Prosser for which Minton owned half the patent.2⁶ Cole called it the 'Tesselated Pastime', and he wrote a booklet to go with it, in which a juvenile Felix Summerly instructs two little girls in the principles', as he significantly calls them, of flat pattern making. The toy came out in time for Christmas 1843. Lord Langdale told Cole that his daughter, who was about six years old, 'was delighted' with the toy and the booklet, which he had to read 6 times a day'.27 This pleasingly responsive girl was later to marry the Count Teleki, and to be a benefactress of the South Kensington Museum. The guidebook to Hampton Court appeared in a second and expanded edition in 1843. Here Cole upgraded the historical information, making the publication more scholarly. It received a somewhat critical review in the Athenaeum, which deprecated the increased amount of 'antiquarian information', preferring the earlier, chattier tone. Popular Guide Books should be brief. We do not object to Felix Summerly's gossiping and suggestive style; indeed we think it a great merit; it helps people to a pleasant idea, and gives its humanizing moral to inanimate things.'28For once in his career as an art publicist, Cole was criticised for being academic rather than populist. In a publicity flier for a later handbook, he claimed that what he wanted to provide for his readers was the guidance of a good natured and intelligent friend'. The friend would be willing to match his reader's pace, loitering or hurrying as occasion demanded, but he would also be authoritative, supplying judicious guidance'that would help the reader to acorrect' view.29 Cole's guides were sufficiently successful to bring him trouble with imitators. Over Hampton Court he had to take out an injunction to prevent Edward Jesse, deputy head of Woods and Forests, from selling his version of the guide. Over the National Gallery he clashed with no less a person than the famous radical Member of Parliament, Joseph Hume. When a piracy of Cole's book appeared under Hume's name, selling for one penny, Cole was encouraged to get an injunction by Edward Protheroe and Mulready. But after calling on Hume and finding him as garrulous about himself as ever',30 Cole gave up and the injunction was not issued. Cole not only wrote the texts of his books and contributed a few illustrations but also, demonstrating a growing interest in the aesthetics of book production, designed decorative covers and title pages.31 The books led him to acquire some practical experience of tourism administration. For instance, selling the guides at the sites required organisation and tactful negotiation.32 Ensuring that pictures were numbered to correspond with information in the guides was a task he had to do himself both at Hampton Court, and at Cobham Hall.33 After seeing two thousand people turned away from Hampton Court on a Friday, its closed day, Cole compiled a Summerly guide with accurate opening times for all London attractions, the Handbook for holidays spent in and near London (1842). Hampton Court remained a matter of concern. In December 1859 Cole published a pamphlet advocating that the palace's interiors should be ‘restored as much as possible to their original state of decoration', and that its works of art should be conserved.34 On the face of it, a pioneering conservationist tract, this had a hidden agenda, underlying its proposal that works of fine art at Hampton Court should be brought to the Metropolis'. By 1859 Cole had already carried off many objects from the palace for his new museum at South Kensington, and in due course he secured the Raphael cartoons.35 Having achieved some success with the guidebooks, and perhaps having formed a taste for small-format publications, Cole now turned to children's books. By 1843 his two little girls were aged five and three, and the first boy, Henry Hardy, was born in March of that year. Cole was looking for books for the children, and he found that those available fell far short of the standard that he desired. The texts were either too didactic or too yulgar, and for the illustrations any daubs were thought good enough. Nursery rhymes and fairy tales were almost forgotten. 'Little Red Riding Hood, and other fairy tales hallowed to children's use, are now turned into ribaldry as satires for men; as for the creation of a new fairy tale or touching ballad, such a thing is unheard of.'36 ![](images/5c2c675cd76676262281b771b24915029773b26ef98bcc43a639272ecc876bc0.jpg) Figure 5 Pages, printed in red and black, from A glance at the Temple Church, 1844. Most children's books of the time, claimed Elizabeth Rigby - a periodical essayist of some weight, who was to marry Sir Charles Eastlake - contained ‘an insipid tale [thatl goes feebly wriggling through an unmerciful load of moral, religious, and scientific preaching'. Such books, she said regretfully, 'have been the most abundant and characteristic product of the present day, not intended, we are assured, for school use, but, we speak equally from experience and observation, with no charm for any other'.37 Cole's friend Thackeray was equally severe on the books that he had had as a child: Abominable attempts were made in those days to make useful books for children, and cram science down their throats as calomel used to be administered under the pretence of a spoonful of currant-jelly. Such picture-books as we had were illustrated with the most shameful, hideous, old wood-cuts which had lasted through a century ... Some painted pictures there were in our time likewise, but almost all of the very worst kind; the hideous distortions of Rowlandson, who peopled the picture-books with bloated parsons in periwigs, tipsy aldermen and leering salacious nymphs, horrid to look at.38 Cole set out to produce books that, in both text and illustrations, would appeal to the imagination of children. He wanted both to amuse his own children and to reform children's literature. His publishers, up to 1843, had been Charles Knight, George Bell and Wood, Hugh Cunningham and, for pamphlets, Henry Hooper. Of these, Bell and Wood, who were publishing the guidebooks, seemed the most likely to be interested in a series of children's books. But when he approached George Bell he found no support, because Bell's partner, Wood, had absconded, leaving him short of working capital. Bell did, however, continue to publish the guidebooks. In March 1843, Cole had the good fortune to meet a new publisher: the 25-year-old, red-haired Joseph Cundall,39 who had just taken over a shop in New Bond Street, known as The Juvenile Library. He had already written some children's books and published others. With him Cole embarked on a long series of children's books that he called The Home Treasury'.40 Cole paid great attention to the appearance of his books, both outside and inside. They were small for small hands to handle. The bindings were decorative in themselves, with designs related to the contents of the book. He took the trouble to go to the British Museum to look at covers and bindings that he could use as models. Though he wrote some of the stories himself, he roped in others, and Marian, Charles Cole and Harriett Mill all wrote for him. Marian, in the thin disguise of 'Mrs Felix Summerly', was acknowledged as the author of The Mother's Primer (plate 17). Charles Whittingham the Younger of Chiswick, one of the best printers in the country and a friend of John Thompson, printed the books. John Mill encouraged Cole by saying that 'Felix Summerly would be as notorious as the Brothers Grimm'.41 The old tales retold in ‘The Home Treasury' included Little Red Riding Hood, Beauty and the Beast, Jack and the Bean Stalk, Cinderella, Jack the Giant Killer, Sleeping Beauty and Dick Whittington, plus the less well known Sir Hornbook, Reynard the Fox, and Chevy Chase. There was also a volume of nursery rhymes. The longest text was Cole's version of Berthold Niebuhr's Heroic Tales of Ancient Greece. Cole announced that all The Home Treasury' books 'will be illustrated, but not after the usual fashion of children's books, in which it seems to be assumed that the lowest kind of art is good enough to give first impressions to a child. In the present series, though the statement may perhaps excite a smile, the illustrations will be selected from the works of Raffaelle, Titian, Hans Holbein, and other old masters. Some of the best modern artists have kindly promised their aid in creating a taste for beauty in little children'.42 Cole's triumph was to persuade the fashionable painters who were now his friends to do the illustrations. So Mulready, Redgrave, Horsley, Webster, Townsend, Linnell and his sons, and Cope all worked for him, together with Frederick Tayler (18o2-89), who specialised in hunting scenes, and Henry Corbould (1787-1844). The pictures were lithographed and hand-coloured. Cole himself coloured the illustrations in the set of these books that he sent to the Queen for her children, not trusting anyone else. The series also included several books whose illustrations were drawn from the work of the Old Masters. Holbein's Bible Events, Raffaelle's Bible Events and Albert Durer's Bible Events had illustrations based on work by these three artists. An Alphabet of Quadrupeds included illustrations after Paul Potter, Teniers and Rembrandt, among others. 'Pictures by the Old Masters' illustrated A Century of Fables. In The Little Painter's Portfolio were to be found illustrations after Giotto, S. Del Piombo’ and Holbein.43 Cole's series of books was an immediate success. Already in November 1843 a reviewer in the Athenaeum could greet the latest volumes with enthusiasm: Another bouquet of flowers, immortals' as the florists would call them for they are not subject to change, and will be fresh and fragrant at Christmas, or on New Year's Day, or at Easter, or at any other and all other holiday seasons – for which our young friends are indebted to Felix Summerly. We earnestly hope that these little volumes will be welcomed in every family: the paths of learning may thus be made paths of pleasure, and children's libraries become lustrous with beauty, pleasurable as well as profitable. The illustrations are greatly superior to what is usually met with, even in the best works of the class.44 It would be interesting to know who wrote this cordial review. Since Cole himself was writing regularly for the journal at the time, it is quite possible that he was the reviewer of his own work. However, there were other reviews, no less ardent. Thornton Leigh Hunt, for example, hailed the books as reaching a new level of good taste. This is a novelty in infant literature; a novelty as important as it is daring. It is due to Felix Summerly, a name synonymous with taste. All the 'common sense' of the kingdom will be ready to declare that art is thrown away on children ‘who cannot understand it'. Does ‘common sense'itself understand it? No. Why not? Because it has not taste. Because in infancy its taste was never cultivated; because children's books were filled with glaring daubs of red and green, which were considered ‘good enough'... In bestowing our hearty approbation .. . let us not forget to thank the eminent artists for their good nature in thus lending their great talents to a task hitherto performed by the humblest draughtsman. Think of Mulready, Cope, Horsley, F. Tayler, Webster, Redgrave, and the rest illustrating children's books! In a country like England where 'appearances' are so highly prized, we can understand the sneers of ambitious mediocrity when hearing of such 'degradation'. But the above artists are superior to any fantastic pride. They thought - and justly - that their talents were not ignobly employed, in endeavouring to introduce a new element in early education.45 In 'The Home Treasury' we see Cole at his most engaging, directing his overflowing vitality into a project that was both creatively satisfying to himself and sympathetic to the developing pleasures of his children. Having embarked on publishing reproductions of classic art in his children's books, Cole was not slow to seize an opportunity to do it for adults also. On 3 November 1843, through the good offices of John Linnell, he met Henry Josi, keeper of prints and drawings at the British Museum, to look at the original woodblocks of Dürer's Small Passion. Josi had bought 35 (out of 37) of these for the museum in 1839. Josi ‘said he thought I might have casts if I chose to apply'. Stereotypes were indeed made for Cole, from which he printed an edition of Dürer's designs. For The Passion of Our Lord Jesus Christ, pourtrayed by Albert Durer Cole wrote a 16-page preface, a serious excursion into art history, with a characteristic aside on the virtues of the (relatively) new technology of stereotyping.46 He published the book not under the name ‘Felix Summerly' but under his own name - ‘Henry Cole, An Assistant Keeper of the Public Records' - because, having laboured long over it, he regarded it as a work of scholarship. The book had an impressive imprint, being issued in 1 844 by a consortium of superior publishers: Cundall, Pickering and Bell in London, with Parker of Oxford and Deighton of Cambridge. Another publication for adults that Cole arranged was So much of the Diary of Lady Willoughby as relates to her Domestic History, and to the Eventful Period of the Reign of Charles the First. This, while purporting to be a seventeenth-century diary, was in fact a new work by Hannah Rathbone, a member of the well-known Liverpool Quaker family. Richard Rathbone, her husband, asked Cole to arrange its publication, anonymously, by Longmans. Whittingham designed a special pseudoseventeenth-century typeface for it, and the book appeared in an archaic-style binding in the autumn of 1844, though it could also be bought bound more cheaply in half leather or paper. It caused a great stir and several authors, including John Murray, were thought to be the inventors of it. It ran into several editions, and 'Some Further Portions' of the 'Diary' were published in 1848, when it was admitted that a 'lady' had written it. In various ways, Cole moved towards critical writing on art. His first venture was an attempt to explain some basic principles to children. In 1838, his utilitarian friend Horace Grant had written a manual, Drawing for Young Children, with which Cole had helped before it was sent off to Charles Knight for publication. It claimed to furnish . . . the parent with a pleasant means of turning to account the wanton scrawls made by an infant on slate or letter cover'.47 Cole followed it up with First Exercises in Light, Shade and Colour, with numerous illustrations, being a supplement to Drawing for Young Children'. The text of the book took the form of questions addressed to the young reader. This was a variant on a common device in educational children's books, described by Elizabeth Rigby: 'dialogues, where a profound mother and a docile child play alternately into each other's hands, and where a question is set up, like a ninepin, only to be knocked down by the next answer'.48 Cole was not quite so prescriptive, for he provided questions but not answers, which were left to the child to supply from its own experience. The book also contained diagrams, some black and white and some hand-coloured, to demonstrate the relationship between different colours and shades. Cole's well-meaning intention was to encourage children to be observant and to enjoy their surroundings. In his preface he set out his aims. The Art of seeing opens a long and never-tiring voyage of discovery where freshness and innocent excitement are never exhausted. Everyone may possess himself of a picture gallery of his own, as far superior in beauty to all Louvres as Nature is to Art, – where nothing is paid for entrance or maintenance - where all is gain and nothing loss. The desire to aid increasing numbers to enrich themselves with the powers of seeing and appreciating the beauties nature displays to the eye, rather than of teaching the rules of practice of art, is the object of this little work.49 The book is a creditable attempt to get into the mind of a child, and it is interesting that Cole says he attempted it because other such books have always seemed to the writer to take for granted the possession of a certain amount of observation and experience in those to whom they were addressed'. Cole might have been speaking for himself. He started with little knowledge of art, and one way to teach himself was to teach his children. Let it not be thought, however, that Cole only engaged with art history and criticism at a childish level. As he prepared his handbooks he caught up with the latest literature, such as Anna Jameson's Handbook to the public galleries of art in and near London (1842), Franz Kugler's Hand-book to the History of painting (of which Part I, The Italian schools, appeared in 1842, edited by C. L. Eastlake), J. L. Petit's Remarks on church architecture (1841) -'very interesting book'- and Joseph Gwilt's Encyclopaedia of architecture (1842).50 In his characteristically efficient way, Cole knew how to look things up, and was a member of the new London Library⁵1 and a reader at the British Museum Library.52 As a critic, even in these early days, he seldom failed in confidence, as may be seen in three papers on house decoration which he wrote for the Athenaeum in 1843. Was he again drawing on personal experience, this time the experience of setting up his home with Marian in Notting Hill Square? At any rate, an interest in colour and the theory of colour was very much in the air. David Hay of Edinburgh, the principal interior decorator of the day, had written the standard work in the late 1 8zos. Titled The Laws of Harmonious Colouring adapted to House Painting, it developed a system by which colours were analogous with harmony in music. The book ran into many editions and was still selling well in the 184os. Cole read it before he reviewed Charles Eastlake's translation of Goethe's Theory of Colours for the London and Westminster Review in 184o. It was still in his mind when he expounded his own theories in the Athenaeum articles. He pointed out that fashions in decoration change every seven years or so. ‘Sometimes the rage is for warm shades of colouring, at others for cold.' But it might be possible to find styles of decoration that would remain valid for ever. It appears to us, that certain principles of decoration may be laid down, which, if recognized and applied, would make our dwellings much more cheerful and comfortable .. . not only without any additional cost, but would make the keep of them more economical, by rendering them, to a great degree, independent of the caprices of fashion. It is the absence of correct principles which causes decoration and furniture to be out of fashion - tiresome – palling to the eye, and subject to constant change, - whereas, what is really beautiful, being based on everlasting principles, is subject to no change.53 When Cole here again advocates principles’of design, we begin to recognise his emergence as a design reformer. As we have seen from his holiday journals, he had all the bumptiousness of youth in telling Marian what was in bad taste. Now, in the Athenaeum articles, he seemed ready to tell the world. His diary for 1 1 January 1842 notes that he read Pugin's True principles of pointed or Christian architecture, published the previous year. He makes no comment on it, but in view of his ready deployment of the concept of 'true principles' in later debates about taste, it is tempting to believe that he was fired by the urgent, incisive prescriptions and interdicts of Pugin. Anyone in the London art world in the 184os must have known about the project to decorate the new Houses of Parliament with frescoes.54 Cole moved in a circle of artists who were deeply involved in this project, so he naturally paid it some attention. Prompted by the first report of the Fine Arts Commission (which, under Prince Albert's chairmanship, was in charge of commissioning the frescoes), he persuaded William Hickson on II May 1842 to accept an article in the Westminster Review. This reveals him in the role of politician rather than art critic. After an initial review of the state of the arts in England, he took issue with the often expressed view that public money spent on artistic works would be wasted. This gave him the opportunity, since he was writing in the Westminster, to make a statement, from a utilitarian standpoint, in support of state subsidy of the arts. It is the fashion to ascribe motives of economy, in respect of the arts, to the Radical party especially. We do not think that the Radical party are more enlightened on the subject than other political parties, but it is only justice to them to say that they have generally regarded a grant for the encouragement of the fine arts as an outlay to promote public education, and have been more liberally disposed than the Whigs and not less so than the Tories, who assent to such grants for the good of the arts themselves, rather than for any moral effect they may have . . ..55 After this, Cole went on to air another opinion derived from his utilitarian background. Although his article has been adduced as an expression of faith in artists as against amateurs,6 its chief point was repudiation of administration by committee. The commission, Cole thought, was far too big. 'It is impossible to accomplish any thing great by a numerous agency. Who would think of fighting a battle with a Council of War, consisting of twenty-three commanding officers, and what single instance can be shown where a fine work of art has been produced by a committee or corporation?’ He thought the fresco project more likely to succeed if it were left as the individual responsibility of Prince Albert, or Sir Robert Peel, who was one of the commissioners, or Charles Eastlake, who was secretary to the commission. An exhibition of designs, or cartoons', for frescoes was opened in Westminster Hall in summer 1843. Cope, Horsley and Townsend were among the prizewinners. Even before the private view on 1 July, Cole had been setting up, on 26, 29 and 30 June, a 'speculation' with Longmans, to publish reproductions of the winning designs, to be made as lithographs by John Linnell and his sons. A portfolio of 1 I illustrations for £5 was advertised in summer $\cdot 8 4 3 , ^ { 5 7 }$ and these lithographs eventually (‘a longer delay has taken place in .. . publication . . . than was anticipated')58 appeared. The preparatory process of commissioning designs for the frescoes was long drawn out (with three further competitions in 1844, 1845 and $\mathbf { I } 8 4 7 )$ , and when work started on the site (Dyce led off with his The Baptism of King Ethelbert in 1845) progress became even slower. Cole seems to have lost interest, for he liked quick results. On the other hand, he had a habit of retrieving good ideas that had not been achieved. The Westminster fresco project may have stayed in his memory as an inspiration for the future, and in that connection, it is worth pondering T. S. R. Boase's remark that ‘the real successor to Westminster in scale and variety was the decoration of the South Kensington Museum'.59 The interior fittings and finishes of the new Palace of Westminster were eventually left to Barry and Pugin, but in the early stages the commissioners took an interest in these too. Craftsmen were invited to submit specimens of woodcarving, metalwork, stained glass and floor coverings,60 which were exhibited in spring 1844 in the former St James's Bazaar, King Street, St James's. Cole visited this exhibition on 22 April, and that same evening started work on an article about it. It is not known where (or if) his article appeared, but there was a detailed, two-part review of the exhibition in the Athenaeum on 27 April (pp.387-8) and 4 May (pp.409-10). If Cole did not write this, he must have read it. He would have approved of the way it advocated sound 'principles', such as that 'the first object of decoration is its perfect and subordinate suitableness to its specific purpose'. The article remarks, prophetically: 'We cordially welcome this exhibition ... as a beginning, by forcibly directing public attention to the subject: it is a thing, once begun, we shall hope to see followed up year by year, and year after year, we doubt not, to be able to record a progress.' This is almost a prescription for Cole's future life. The two men must have felt some inclination to work in harness together, for on 5 December $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 5$ Russell tried to persuade Cole to join him in the railway department of the Daily News. Cole, however, was more interested in another of Russell's activities. After drinking tea on 5 December, they passed the evening preparing subjects for Prizes for the Soc of Arts'. Russell, appointed secretary to the Society of Arts in autumn 1 845, found himself responsible for the continuation of a project begun by his predecessor, Francis Whishaw: a Grand Annual Exhibition of Manufactures. The first such exhibition, in $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 8 4 5$ , had not been successful. Now it fell to Russell to take on the project and do it better. Obviously he drew Cole into it, seeking his advice on how to run the next annual exhibition.64 Cole decided to enter the competition himself. Russell put up some of his own money as a prize fund, and he and Cole decided to make it a rule that the pieces put in for the competition should have the names of the makers on them. At that time, retailers hardly bothered to draw attention to the makers of the things they sold. The manufacturers Wedgwood and Spode had begun to stamp their wares with their names, but Herbert Minton was certain that he would be ruined if he dared to put his name, as opposed to a coded mark, on his china. Cole's friendship with Minton had ripened. He stayed with Minton at his house at Hartshill near Stoke-on-Trent, on 10-11 September 1844. Minton showed him over his enormous china works and he experimented with throwing a teacup. Two years later, in April 1846, Cole again went to stay with Minton, and, while there, turned a tea set – with the help of an appropriately named Mr Turner6s – for submission in the Society of Arts competition. It was made in a red unglazed clay, and was intended to be cheap to produce. To equip himself to design the tea set, Cole, who had absolutely no experience in manufacturing, studied Etruscan pots at the British Museum. He accompanied his entry with a note in which reverence for art history combines with practical sense in problem solving. The aim in these models has been to obtain as much beauty and ornament as is commensurate with cheapness. A higher standard in the ornamental parts would have led to much greater cost. The forms in principle are new combinations of those of the best Etruscan Pottery, with ornaments at the handles, &c., superadded and designed so as not to interfere with the simplicity of the outlines. The Cup being deep rather than wide, offers least scope for the radiation of heat and will keep the tea warm. The Milk Pot has three lips like some articles of Etruscan Pottery, enabling the liquid to be poured at both angles, right and left, which requires only a motion of the wrist, whilst the usual method needs the lifting of the arm. The plate is smaller than usual in the rim, because much size in that part is needless. The red clay being unglazed is calculated to exhibit the forms of the Tea Pot, Sugar Basin, and Milk Pot to most advantage . . ..66 Cole persuaded Minton to enter two jugs for the competition. When entries were judged in April 1846, prizes were awarded to Minton for his jugs and to Cole for his tea set (plate 19). Prince Albert admired the tea set, though he must have been shown a grander version than the one in the exhibition, because he said that he thought the gilding was not as good as that done by the French. He ordered one to use as a breakfast set at Balmoral. Some 4o years later Cole could write that the cups were still being used at the Society of Arts and that hundreds of thousands had been exported all over the world. 'I presented a set to the South Kensington Museum, which I hope may be kept and always exhibited there, as a link in the circumstances leading to that great Exhibition, which sowed the seed for the beginnings of the South Kensington Museum itself.'67 Cole viewed this second Society of Arts exhibition (which was more successful than the first) as a turning point. The acclaim bestowed on his tea set (and Minton's jugs) ‘strengthened my conviction that an alliance between fine art and manufactures would promote public taste, and conduce to the interest of all concerned in the production of art manufactures.' Consequently he set upan organization of artists, manufacturers, and designers', and, resurrecting Felix Summerly, called it 'Summerly's Art-Manufactures'.68 This new project, as often with Cole's ventures, was not an entirely unprecedented idea. Indeed, in the little manifesto (fig.6) with which he announced his scheme, and which was reprinted in subsequent catalogues, he made a point of emphasising that his purpose was to revive the good old practice of connecting the best art with familiar obiects in daily use'. In recent times, the ‘good old practice' had fallen into disuse and it was very rare for fine artists to design anything to be manufactured, though the sculptor, John Flaxman, had designed for Wedgwood, and for Rundell, Bridge and Rundell, the royal silversmiths.69 None of Flaxman's designs, however, could be said to be for daily use, and Cole wanted to bring art down to earth again.
