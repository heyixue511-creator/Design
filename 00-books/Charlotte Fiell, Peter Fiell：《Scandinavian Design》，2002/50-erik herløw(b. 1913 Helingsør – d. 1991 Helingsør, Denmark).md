Between 1952and 1954 Herløw was director of design instruction at the Guldmedehojskolen (College for Gold- and Silversmiths), Copenhagen, and from 1955 to 1979 he served as artistic adviser to the Royal Copenhagen porcelain factory. Herløw was a major force in the evolution of Danish industrial design, who believed that the role of the designer included working closely with production specialists so as to have an influence on all aspects of the design process, from initial feasibility studies to the final manufactured product. In this way, products could be assessed and closely evaluated during their development so as to ensure that the best possible solution was achieved. ![](images/8d1fe24fd18492c82c9347ebaa16b22a576334256cc95a5b0056487c0768cf24.jpg) At the beginning of the 19th century, the progressive thinker Count Christian Danneskiold-Samsøe decided to use the peat that occurred in abundance on his large estate as fuel for a new glassworks. Having died before he could see this project through to fruition, his widow, the Countess Henriette Danneskiold-Samsøe, finally managed to establish the glassworks at Holmegaard Marsh in 1825. The first furnace for the factory was built by a Norwegianglassmaker, Christian Wendt, who had previously worked for the Hadeland glassworks. Initially, the factory manufactured cheap dark green bottles, known as bouteilles, which were used for beer or schnapps. After several years, however, the Countess bravely decided to up-grade and broaden the company's product line. To this end, she hired glassmakers from Bohemia and Southern Germany, who in 1835 began producing European-style traditional domestic stemware and “art” glass. In response to increasing competition from Swedish glass manufacturers, in the early 20th century Holmegaard recruited its first well-established designer as a permanent member of staff. Orla Juul Nielsen was employed by the company as part of a co-operative enterprise with Royal Copenhagen to produce glassware for its porcelain dinner services. It was noted in a company letter at the time that, “the factory has found an artist who shares our view of remaining down to earth, primarily in the shape of the drinking glass and the glass bowl, which attempt to come as close to the essential form as the nature of the material will allow, avoiding everything with a hint of over-refinement or artistic lightness, but in its pure simplicity striving for the grand effect." Certainly, the idea that beauty could be achieved through simplicity was not only at the cutting-edge of design thinking at the time, but when put into practice by Holmegaard it led to an important new identity for Danish glassware. In 1924, the architect Jacob Bang joined the design office at Holmegaard and four years later he was appointed the firm's artistic director. Bang subsequently designed a variety of glassware within a modern idiom. His well-known Primula and Viola ranges were widely acclaimed and revealed his talent for producing designs that were simple and practical yet pleasurable to the eye and hand. While Bang's work was highly rational, being intended for the most part for industrial ![](images/291eff8c9733d895a75ec13ddd7678794f2c0f532790d7ed59c9a35476d260b1.jpg) manufacture, it was not devoid of the human touch. Unlike the machine aesthetic that was being promoted at the Bauhaus, Bang's product designs projected a softer and more emotionally seductive aesthetic, which predicted the Scandinavian approach to Modernism that flourished from the 1930s onwards. Bang's work for Holmegaard was also a demonstration of his belief in democratic design – he preferred “not expensive drinking goblets for the Rockefellers, but beer glasses for Mr. Hansen of Denmark”. Nevertheless, Bang diddesign on occasion exclusive“art” glass pieces, including crystal glasses, bowls and cocktail shakers that were delicately engraved and cut with Art-Deco-style motifs. In 1942 he was succeeded by Per Lütken, who remained Holmegaard's artistic director until his death in 1998. During his 56 year tenure, Lütken not only introduced the latest developments in “art” glass, but also attempted to “renew the classical wine glass" by continuing to adopt the traditional cold cracked-off way of producing such products. Believing that design should “delight our common daily lives”, Lütken perpetuated the Holmegaard ethos of simplicity, classical proportion, and high-quality craftsmanship through his work. In 1968 Jacob Bang's son, Michael, joined the glassworks and subsequently designed a number of successful glassware ranges, such as the elegant Fontaine wine glasses (1987) with bubble encased stems.
