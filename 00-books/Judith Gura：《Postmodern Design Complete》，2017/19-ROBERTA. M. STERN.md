# ROBERT A. M. STERN b.1939 NEW YORK ARCHITECT WHOSE ELEGANTLY REFINED DESIGNS EVOKE THE CLASSICAL REVIVAL. RIGHT Rendering for a doorway in the Strada Novissima exhibition, Venice Biennale, 1980. In Robert Stern's (also see under “Living with Postmodern Design") interpretations, Postmodernism is urbanized and sophisticated, bearing the classical hallmarks of the style but eschewing the extravagances that made it either irresistible or anathema to other architects. Save for the occasional diversion, his restrained approach makes postmodern works by this architect more closely allied to classical revival, prefiguring his recent New York apartment buildings, which meld past and present with suave translations evoking the grandeur of prewar Park Avenue homes. Though defnitely a postmodernist, and respectful of the style, Stern viewed it as a logical continuation of Modernism rather than a complete break from it, recognizing that admiring historic styles need not call for slavishı copying or precise revivals, but judicious adaptation of appropriate elements. In his view, Postmodernism “looks back to history to see how things were done and to remind itself that many good ways of doing things which were cast aside for ideological reasons can be usefully rediscovered."35 Stern was born in New York, attending Columbia University as a fine arts major, and received his architecture degree from Yale University in 1965. He worked briefly in the office of Richard Meier\* and in city planning before opening an architectural office with a partner in ı969, and his own practice in ı977. His firm was prominent enough by i98o to be invited to contribute to the Strada Novissima project organized by PAOLO PORTOGHESI for the Venice Biennale, for which he designed a striking doorway that recalled the majesty of a grand classical temple. Stern's residential work often combines classical elements with suggestions of traditional shingle-style Victorian homes. In a New Jersey residence from i982, a spectacular indoor swimming pool area is glamorized with Deco-Grecian stairs, columns evoking the palm trees in the Brighton Pavilion, and a caryatid-decorated freplace. In other projects, he combined elements drawn from Stanford White and Josef Hoffmann with a postmodern vocabulary. In a showroom for Shaw-Walker (1982), he tamped down Postmodernism's flamboyance for the traditional office furniture producer, creating a dignified but stylish environment with a temple-like entrance, marble floors, and copper-colored torchères. His firm, known as RAMSA, now employs several hundred people and works on projects all over the world. Stern has combined architecture with teaching, heading the Historic Preservation program at the Columbia Graduate School of Architecture, Planning and Preservation (1984–88), and from 1998 to 2016 was dean of Yale School of Architecture.
