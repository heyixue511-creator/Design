Morris announced to Blunt on 23 February 1891 that his type had now arrived and he could begin printing Blunt's poems. A few days later (28 February) Jane offered this report to her lover: ‘The printing [of The Story of the Glittering Plain] is now fairly started, though the work goes very slowly with our two men, soon we shall take two more and get on much faster, your book I believe will be the third produced by the “Kelmscott Press".’ Blunt's book — the partial manuscript of which he delivered to Morris in January — was to consist mainly of poems previously published in Sonnets and Songs by Proteus (1875) and The Love Sonnets of Proteus (188o), with [as the Kelmscott title-page phrased it] many sonnets omitted from the earlier editions'. The expressive pseudonym ‘Proteus' had also been employed by Blunt in an exchange of letters about religious belief published in 1878, and in the preface to the first edition of The Love Sonnets he remarked, ‘The author of these sonnets, styling himself Proteus, acknowledges thereby a natural mood of change. However, in February 1891 Blunt completed an additional sequence of thirty-one sonnets entitled Natalia's Resurrection' which he submitted to Morris for inclusion in the Kelmscott volume. It is a peculiar group of poems, filled to overflowing with surrealistic allegory, exotic imagery, and supernatural events. Young Adrian falls in love with a Roman matron (the setting is ambiguous but probably medieval), who rejects him because she feels an obligation to bear a child for her decidedly unpleasant husband. The baby is stillborn, Natalia dies in childbirth, and Adrian falls into deep mourning. In a dream Natalia comes to him declaring her love, for she says (and here the logic of the sequence becomes rather tenuous) that she is now free of the legal restraints of marriage; strangely, even her husband, who is also present in the dream, agrees. As Natalia and Adrian are about to marry, he awakens to discover that her funeral procession is at that moment passing by on the road. That evening Adrian goes to Natalia's grave and opens it, whereupon Natalia rises up, very much alive, and they dash off to get married. Natalia's Resurrection' is not an impressive poem (though on other occasions both Yeats and Pound expressed admiration for Blunt's verse), but it requires no great psychological insight to understand why it appealed so strongly to Jane Morris. Writing to Blunt on 13 April 1891, she commented, ‘Yesterday I finished reading the 2nd part of your M.S. poems. I like the "Natalia's Resurrection" so very much. I should like to see that placed first in the book. What do you say?'A comment on the sonnets by Blunt's wife Anne in her diary offers a different perspective: ‘Poets' theories of "love" ignore honourable feelings.'4 Her husband, following Jane's advice, agreed to put Natalia's Resurrection' at the beginning of the book, and on 21 April, from a ship bound to Naples, he posted the completed manuscript to Morris. Printing of the Love-Lyrics went much more slowly than Morris had anticipated: he was still naïvely optimistic about the amount of work his few printers could do on a hand-press, the rubrication required double presswork, and The Golden Legend was in production at the same time. Typesetting did not begin until the summer of 1891, and proofs of Blunt's volume reached him in the country by early September, but his pleasure in seeing his words set in the beauty of the Golden type was considerably diminished by an unexpected letter from one of his former lovers. Nearly all of Blunt's love poetry reflects his relationships with particular women – Esther, for example, offers a fictionalized account of his prolonged, unhappy passion for Skittles - and the evidence in Lady Longford's biography suggests that Blunt's ladies were usually flattered to find their amours celebrated in graceful verse, with of course no real names or incriminating circunstances mentioned. During 189o, however, Blunt had carried on an affair with Margaret Talbot, the wife of the Military Secretary at the British Embassy in Paris, that ended abruptly when Mrs Talbot confessed everything to her husband; uncharacteristically, there were bitter recriminations on both sides. During the summer of 1891 Mrs Talbot learned about Natalia's Resurrection' and concluded, no doubt rightly, that it contained indirect references to her. Like Natalia, she was married to an older man of considerable social standing but was childless; she too had turned away a lover because of loyalty to her husband. Whatever message she now sent to Blunt was sufficiently peremptory to force him into action at once. On 28 September, Blunt, his wife, and daughter spent the day in London: Blunt went to his club while the two women shopped. That evening, when Blunt did not return home, Anne surmised correctly that he had decamped to Kelmscott Manor, where he had an urgent conference with Morris. Blunt made the following entry in his diary on 3o September (though part of the explanation he offers here appears to be a later interpolation): I have been away for two nights to Kelmscott to see Morris about the printing and to wish Mrs. Morris good bye for the winter. It was very perfect weather, and we did our gudgeon fishing and walks as usual, and I made a little love to Mrs. Morris, poor woman, for the quite last tine. . . . We have arranged that Natalia is to be suppressed and instead ofit that the old Proteus Sonnets shall be reprinted as the second part of the Kelmscott volume with cęrtain new sonnets and some of those omitted from the earlier edition. The suppression of Natalia was decided on in consequence of a wild letter from Margaret who imagined that the poem described the relations between herself and her husband and me. And, although the poem had really nothing to do with her, she was so urgent that I could not refuse. It put Morris to great inconvenience, for Natalia was already in print, and I was obliged to explain the circumstances to him, when he at once and most good naturedly consented to the change. In a volume of proofs of the Love-Lyrics Cockerell added one of his characteristic notes indicating that it contained a portion of the cancelled Natalia's Resurrection'— with which', Cockerell wrote, the K.P. volumes was to have opened — but a lady, so WSB told me, pretended that the poem was about herself, and, to please her, he removed it'.³ Though Cockerell is usually a reliable witness, we must recall that he always displayed a fierce loyalty to his friends and turned a blind eye to their faults: asked once whether he was aware of Blunt's infidelities, Cockerell replied, I realized that there was usually a lady in the offing. But Blunt always behaved like a gentleman: he never told a dirty story.'6 The proofs preserved by Cockerell show that eighteen pages of type for the Love-Lyrics had been set in vain, and, despite his complacent note, we can assume that Morris and Blunt did not make this decision lightly or merely on the basis of an unfounded accusation. How much did Jane Morris know about the ‘Natalia's Resurrection' episode? Since she proof-read Blunt's book (something that she did for no other Kelmscott Press volume), she must have learned what had happened. Yet she never mentions it in her letters; what does emerge in her correspondence with Blunt during 1891 is her great admiration for a group of twelve poems entitled ‘A Woman's Sonnets' in the Love-Lyrics: the “Woman's Sonnets"I liked immensely', she wrote to Blunt on 11 December, and I think them some of the best, though I don't agree with all the sentiments therein contained.'The sequence is obviously different from others in the volume, for it describes the breakup of a love-affair from a woman's point of view. What Blunt apparently never told Jane — or the readers of the Kelmscott Press edition – was that the sonnets were in fact written by Lady Gregory, another of Blunt's former mistresses, about the change in their relationship. Lady Gregory also read the proofs. The Love-Lyrics & Songs of Proteus — a 264-page small quarto — was finally issued on 27 February 1892 in an edition of 300 paper copies (two guineas), and it sold out very quickly. On 26 April, having received an inscribed copy from Blunt, Jane sent him her final evaluation of it: I think it is beautiful in all ways. I spent much time alone with it yesterday, and read again and again with the greatest pleasure my favourite poems. I admire more than ever the “Women's Sonnets" series, they flow so sweetly and smoothly, no hesitation anywhere. . . . I had no copy of your book. We bought two and gave one away, then we sent for another, but all had been sold within the fortnight of publication. Jane's husband, meanwhile, was supplying financial reports to Blunt from time to time. Blunt was in effect his own publisher, paying Morris for the printing and providing a fee of ten shillings to Reeves and Turner for each copy sold. On 12 March 1892 Morris was able to offer him good news: ‘The 22o copies offered for sale have all been sold, and Reeves has handed me £2oo on account for you[.] . . . I am charging you £1 5o for the printing which was a good deal more than I thought it would cost: but the book finally was much longer than I had estimated and the red printing, there being a great deal of it nearly doubled the cost of the press-work. . .. I am glad that you will make something out of the book; for when Reeves pays up the whole there will be a good balance in your favour, without reckoning the copies you keep. So I think we may congratulate ourselves on a success.’ A more detailed account, sent by Morris to Blunt on 3o April, indicates that the total cost of production was £195 and that Blunt's net profit was £142. In the second letter Morris rejoiced that the book had been well received by reviewers: ‘The only discordant note on the subject was by [Joseph] Pennell of the Star. However as he is a cur by profession, one should not be annoyed at his making his livelihood by the only art he understands.' But there were also mutterings of discontent fron Ellis, who took a dim view of Blunt's poetic pretensions: ‘The fact is Morris is so good natured that he does not like to say “no" but is printing this book surely against the grain.'37 The great unanswered, perhaps unanswerable, question (which lies beyond the scope of this history of the Kelmscott Press but is irresistibly fascinating nevertheless) is whether ‘good natured'Morris was aware, as he designed and printed The Love-Lyrics & Songs of Proteus, that he was making a book for his wife's lover. ## What general conclusions, if any, can we draw from the story of these three richly diverse books? The Golden Legend illustrates Morris's weakness for the large, sumptuous volume that he was not yet really capable of producing; impressive as The Golden Legend may be, it is not the book that Morris envisioned or the sort of book that he was later able to create at the Kelmscott Press. Poems by the Way, a much less grandiose undertaking, shows the slow evolution of Morris's typographical style while the Kelmscott books were still in an experimental stage. The Love-Lyrics & Songs of Proteus gives off echoes of very odd psychological resonances from the Morris marriage. If the three books have anything at all in common, it is a certain note of improvisation (sometimes urgent, sometimes amusing) in the history of their production; if we grasp this, we will perhaps avoid thetemptation of treating the Kelmscott Press, with reverential solemnity, as merely a portentous episode in nineteenth-century cultural history.
