# 4 The Fundamentals of Bauhaus Pedagogy: Premises, Parallels, Trends

The previous chapter demonstrated that the core of the program for the Bauhaus concentrated on two goals above all: aesthetic synthesis (the integration of all genres of art and branches of the crafts under the primacy of architecture) and social synthesis (the orientation of aesthetic production around the needs of a broad segment of the population and not exclusively around the demand of a tiny stratum of the socially andeconomically privileged).

The realization of both goals required modifications to education for artists and designers and thus new pedagogical conceptions. Before we go into the details, we need a brief digression to sketch the stations in the development of artists' training from the Middle Ages to the nineteenth century, in order to locate the Bauhaus and its pedagogy in the larger historical context. After that, one section will show that this "art school of modernism" should not be understood as a singular phenomenon, but rather as it is embedded within the overarching context of art school reform from 1900 to 1933. The reform of the art schools in postrevolutionary Russia, especially in the Higher Artistic-Technical Workshops (Vkhutemas) in Moscow, reveals strong parallels with the Bauhaus.

## Notes on the History of Artists' Training up to the End of the Nineteenth Century

In view of the theme of this book, it would go too far afield to trace the history of artists' training back to antiquity. In light of the deliberate allusion intended inGropius's choiceof the term "Bauhaus" and of the program of the Bauhaus we have sketched, it seems appropriate to start our brief retrospective with the masons' lodges (Bauhütten) of the Middle Ages.

The masons' lodge of the twelfth and thirteenth centuries was a cooperative organization of the artists and artisans engaged upon the building of a large church or cathedral under the artistic and administrative direction of persons appointed or approved by the body which had commissioned the building.'

The masons' lodges represented a tightly organized social system with a clearly defined hierarchy (master buildermaster of other crafts—apprentices—assistants) and a precisely delineated distribution of tasks. It was typical of the lodges that

restrictions [were] laid upon the members' rights of intellectual property in their own work, and its unconditional subordination of the individual to the artistic requirements of the common task. The aim was to achieve a frictionless division and integration of the labor available.... This aim could only be attained where a real unity of spirit possessed those taking part, for only a willing subordination of personal wishes to the will of the architect and continuous and intimate contact between the artistic director and each of his fellow workers could enable the required leveling of individual differences to be achieved without impairing the artistic quality of the particular products.²

It is self-evident that a concept of collective aesthetic production of the sort characteristic of the medieval masons' lodges—and evoked anew in the manifesto for the founding of the Bauhaus—is an anachronism in the early twentieth century. Gropius recognized this relatively early, and from 1921–22 on he advocated discarding all the romanticism of salvation inspired by the Middle Ages.

The artists or artisans active in the masons' lodges gained the necessary qualifications during an apprenticeship with a master—specifically, by practicing on the spot, without any set canon of instruction, according to the age-old principle of imitatio (demonstrate and imitate).³ Thisprinciple of training continued to be legitimate even as, with the rise of the urban middle class, which from the fourteenth century on represented, alongside the clergy and nobility, a “regular market for works of art," painters and sculptors "emancipated themselves from the lodge and went into business on their own account."4 Like other artisans, painters and carvers began to organize in guilds. The guild orders, which acted as a sensitive social regulator, determined, among other things, the length of apprenticeship, the requirements for acceptance into the guild, and the total number of workers, but they still did not have a fixed canon of instruction. The training continued to be based entirely on actual practice and focused on the particular tasks to be fulfilled. Despite the rigid statutes of the guild orders, the artistic freedom in individual medieval workshops was significantly greater than in the masons' lodges, in which individuality was entirely subordinate to the collective. The guild rules strictly limited the masters' freedom of movement, and tradition-bound thinking permitted only limited room for aesthetic innovations, as the concept of the “artist as individual personality" did not yet apply. Nevertheless, the autonomous master of the late Middle Ages, accountable only to himself, already prefigured the modern type of the independent artist.⁵

With the emancipation of the individual from the intellectual and social obligations of the Middle Ages—in Italy at least from the period around 1400, in other European countries about a century later—began a development that led to the dissolution of the medieval unity of practice and teaching in artists' training. This process, which happened gradually and required that the social corset of the guild be relaxed (even in progressive Florence the workshop apprenticeship dominated throughout the fifteenth century), took the form of a gradual separation of artists' training from the sphere of the workshop and its transfer to teaching facilities, the academies, established specifically for this purpose.

The Platonic background of the idea of the academy cannot occupy us here. It is enough to recall that the academy was originally a school for philosophers founded by Plato in 385 B.c. and closed by Justinian in A.D. 529. Its true goal was to transmit a comprehensive intellectual education to prepare the youth for service to the (ideal)

state; the later academies—apart from certain exceptions like the Platonic Academy in Florence— were considerably more limited in their goals, focusing on subsections of society (e.g., art, music, mining, or architectural academies) and abandoning the ambition for more general training and education in response to pressures from professional specialization and increasing differentiation within society.

The history of the art academies can be traced back to the famous Medici Garden in Florence at the end of the fifteenth century. This garden, under the supervision of the bronze sculptor Giovanni di Bertoldo, not only held the antiquities collection of the Medici but was also a meeting place for the intellectual and cultural elite of the city. It was also a place for artists' training, where Bertoldo gave instruction to young talent (including Michelangelo). Around the middle of the sixteenth century, Giorgio Vasari, the biographer of Renaissance artists and to some degree the father of modern art history) spoke "of this instruction as a 'Scuola' or 'Accademia.' He must therefore have had the feeling that the teaching was done differently than in the workshops associated with the guilds."6

The true history of the art academy, however, begins only in the second half of the sixteenth century, when Vasari founded the Accademia del Disegno in 1561 or 1563.7 There, "theoretical auxiliary subjects, such as geometry, perspective and anatomy were to be taught in regular academic courses" to supplement the workshop apprenticeship, which continued to exist.8 With this came a priority on reality, which the Parisian master builder Jean Mignot had called for as early as 1398 in a report for the masons' lodge for the Milan Cathedral, “Ars sine scienta nihil est"9(Art without science is nothing), and which Leonardo had formulated as follows: “Those who enjoy practice without science are like sailors who travel without rudder or compass; they are never certain where the journey is headed. Practice must always be based on good theory."10

In the age of mannerism, the art academies were already “organized from top to bottom"11 with systematic teaching plans. This was especially true of the Accademia di San Luca, founded by Federigo Zuccari in Rome in 1593, whose teaching program, in contrast to the Accademia del Disegno, was supplemented by life drawing and courses in art theory. Both Vasari's and Zuccari's academies were, however, only short-term programs. The focus of artists' training was still the apprenticeship to a master, and artists continued to belong to a guild.

This changed only with the founding of the Parisian Académie de peintre et de sculpture in 1648.12 This academy, originally a free association of members of equal rights, began to be transformed in 1664, under the supervision of Colbert, the French minister of finance and trade, and Le Brun, the “premier peintre du roi," into a statecontrolled,official teachinginstrument that servedabsolutist politics as an “instrument of stategovernment with the special function of raising theprestige of the monarchs."13 That meant that the Académie predominately producedart that enhanced the monarch's status or otherwise conformed to the intentions of the court. At the same time, the Académie—which was, as it were, a state monopoly in the area of artists' training—became the normative authority in all aesthetic questions and thus an important social regulator, the seat of centralized control over an artist's future artistic or social success or failure, in that the Académie alone decided on admissions to competitions, the awarding of prizes, and so on. The teaching program of the Paris Académie was precisely specified. The student sketched first from drawings, then from plaster casts, and finally from a live model. Antiquity, Raphael, and Poussin were considered the models and requisites in all aesthetic matters. (We can mention only in passing that these norms were relaxed during the later controversy between the respective advocates of Poussin and Rubens.) The themes that could be addressed had a clear hierarchy: at the lowest level were still lifes and landscapes, followed by depictions of animals and people, and at the top the depiction ofhistorical, mythological and allegorical themes.14

In addition to its task of aiding the apotheosis of the ruler through art, the academies, both in France and in other absolutist states, were also subject to the economic (i.e., mercantilistic) goals of absolutism. Thus the Academy in Vienna was reorganized in 1725 “in recognition of art and to support trade," and in Dresden in 1763 it was said, "It does honor to the country to produce excellent artists, but it is no less useful to increase the demand that the overseas trade has for products." In Copenhagen, "around the same time, the all-powerful minister Struensee believed that those 'qui n’arrivent point à l’excellence' could be directed into the craft industry. Here, for the first time, we encounter the distinction between high and low art."15 This distinction, despite all of the reform efforts of the Bauhaus and more recent efforts to break down hierarchies, has survived into the present.16

In the wake of the French Revolution, the Académie in Paris was closed, initially, but it soon survived as the defender of a conservative and classicistic conception of art, a rigid institute in support of traditions and an enemy of progress against which modernizers like Eugène Delacroix, Gustave Courbet, the impressionists, and others would struggle for decades. In Germany, under the influence of romanticism, there were strong antiacademic currents, especially amongthe Nazarenes. The artists branded the academy as an "infirmary" or a "chicken coop." Johann Friedrich Overbeck, one of the outstanding Nazarenes, said that at the academies "every genuine feeling, every worthwhile thought... was suppressed."17 Despite the reform efforts in the early decades of the nineteenth century—in Düsseldorf Peter von Cornelius and especially Wilhelm von Schadow led the way—the academies remained, in general, organizations characterized by a remoteness from life and a hostility toward progress; they found themselves increasingly in the firing lines of criticism. The German impressionist Max Liebermann, for example, found that “In and of itself, 'academic' is not an insult. . . . But gradually . . . it has come to be the case that no self-respecting artist wants to be called an academic. .. . Now academic has come to mean 'antiquated.'"18

In the second half of the nineteenth century, decisive impetus for renewal came from the arts and crafts schools. They had originated from a recognition of the need to "improve the taste of artisans and thus bridge the gap between the trades and art [that had resulted from mechanization—R.W.]."19 Thus, the hoped-for aesthetic education for producers²º was based on a mercantilistic motive: namely, to elevate the crafts, hard hit economically by competition with machine production, to the level of arts and crafts, in order to regain a portion of the market with tasteful, high-quality work. Arnold Hauser has shown that this conception of the "ennobling of the craft to art" was born of a typically romantic way of thinking; and the political economist and sociologist Werner Sombart had, in Hauser's view, rightly raised,the objection to this "idealism" that the "mass of the artisans never attained a respectable level of artistic quality" and that craft and artisanship had always been distinct.²1

Worried by the rapid decline in the quality of artisanship and by the rise in badly designed, machine-produced mass articles, the German architect Gottfried Semper (1803–1879), in his essay "Wissenschaft, Industrie und Kunst" (Science, industry, and art) on the 1851 World's Fair in London, called expressly for a "general development of the people's taste."22 He proposed the establishment of collections (what would later become arts and crafts museums), whose exemplary exhibits promised, in his view, to make a decisive contribution toward educating the tastes of both producers and consumers. In keeping with Semper's conception of education, the rooms of these collections would have supplementary educational offerings on “art and industry" that would cover the areas of ceramics, textiles, wood, and stone as well as comparative architectural theory. In light of the program at the Bauhaus, it is instructive to note that Semper—like Gropius later— sought a "working together" of the first four of these educational topics "under the hegemony of architecture, "23 and that, in addition, he advocated “the promotion of workshop instruction [as] the only instruction beneficial to our future artistic conditions," while harshly criticizing traditional drawing schools. Only in this way would it be possible "to rejoin what a false theory earlier separated."24 According to Semper, the "separation of ideal and applied art"—as manifested in the institutional juxtaposition, or more precisely, superposition, of art academies and industrial schools—was "altogether inappropriate." Practical education in a workshop offered, through the "fraternal relationship of the master to his fellows and apprentices," an opportunity to "bring about the disappearance of the academies and industrial schools, at least in their present form."25 This anticipated the idea of the integrated art school that Gropius sought to realize through the Bauhaus.

Semper's recommendations led—following the establishment of the South Kensington Museum (today's Victoria & Albert Museum) in London—to the founding of a number of museums and schools for the arts and crafts on the continent during the 1860s and '70s. One pacesetter in this development was the Österreichische Museum für Kunst und Industrie (Austrian museum for art and industry), founded in Vienna in 1864, to which an arts and crafts school was added three years later. Both held—in some respects as an ensemble—an "authoritative position"26 throughout Europe for many years to come. The 1868 statutes for the founding of the Kunstgewerbeschule (Arts and crafts school) divided the faculty into three specialized schools for architecture, sculpture, and painting and drawing and a socalled preparatory school of two to three years. The latter was intended to provide qualifications for “more mundane forces" and thus was clearly quite different from,the preliminary instruction that Itten would introduce at the Bauhaus some fifty years later.27 Despite Ferstel's new building, it was not possible for the Vienna Arts and Crafts School “to run the teaching in the manner of a workshop [and] model the school according to the practical aspect," that is, "to develop further the aspects of the training that applied to art in general to a more specific sort that addresses the art industries."28 Later, this would begin to take place around the turn of the century, when the Secessionists Josef Hoffmann and Kolo Moser began to take over the Arts and Crafts School. In their double function as teachers at the school and artistic directors for the Wiener Werkstätte (Viennese workshops) founded in 1903, Hoffmann and Moser gave priority in their teachings to the principle of design work oriented around actual practice "rather than copying inherited customs,"29 That the Vienna Arts and Crafts School (like other reform schools of the period) was in many respects a “Bauhaus before the Bauhaus"30 can now be taken for granted. However, it is not just parallels in its program and conception that speak for such a claim. Gropius was very well informed about the situation in Vienna owing to first wife, Alma Mahler; and, of course, Johannes Itten should be mentioned as another informant, as he lived in the Austrian capital from 1916 to 1919, where he too had contact to the circle aroundAlma Mahler, before being called to the Bauhaus.

During the secondhalf of the nineteenth century in Germany, southern Germany initially led the way in promoting education in the arts and crafts. Here we will mention only the founding of the Kunst- und Industrieschule (School of art and industry) in Offenbach in 1868, because it reveals especially saliently the changes in objectives with respect to traditional academies. In a speech at the opening, the architect was given priority as a teacher over the painter and sculptor, because the architect has "above all the right to work to elevate and support the arts and crafts," since “in superior periods of art architecture has always been the teacher of the other fine arts and of the crafts" (a view that is taken for granted in design education in Italy to this day). The instruction at this school should achieve “an interpenetration of the fine arts and applied utility in producing an object that satisfies a particular need," that is to say, a "fusion of the beautiful with the necessary."31 This view, which clearly has its intellectual origins in Semper, was formative throughout the history of the Bauhaus as well— despite otherwise different positions among the Bauhaus members.

## Bauhaus Pedagogy in the Context of Art School Reform

Descriptions of the Bauhaus as the "art school of modernism" that give the impression it broke radically with tradition and in some sense began anew at square one overlook the overall, largescale historical context within which this school was just one point of crystallization, though admittedly an exceptional one. In our necessarily fragmentary and deliberately selective excursus on the history of artists' training, we can point to several lines of tradition that found their vanishing point in the founding of the Weimar Bauhaus after World War I. The ideas of the medieval masons' lodges (which had, of course, already been picked up by the Nazarenes and the circle around Morris), Gottfried Semper's ideas for reform, and the arts and crafts school movement of the nineteenth century must all be borne in mind.

Gropius himself never denied the historical prerequisites of Bauhaus pedagogy. On the contrary, in a speech he gave during the budget debate in the state parliament in Thuringia on 9 July 1920, well over a year after the institute had been founded, he expressly emphasized-no doubt in part out of tactical considerations, in order to persuade the members of parliament, who were for the most part conservative andinclined against the Bauhaus—that the Bauhaus was not an "experiment," not an "original idea" that had arisen from a single head, but rather the logical realization of "reform ideas typical of our age":

On the basis of incontrovertible facts, I will demonstrate clearly that something has taken place at the Bauhaus that must take place throughout the country, in a seamless and logical development, and is already happening; and I will also demonstrate that the Bauhaus signifies a continuation, not a destruction, of tradition.32

In his argument, Gropius called on the "views of men competent in the field," including Otto Bartning, Rudolf Bosselt, Wilhelm von Debschitz, Bruno Paul, Richard Riemerschmid, and Fritz Schumacher, who had all supported a reform of artists' training and played a role in its practical realization.

In Pressestimmen für das Staatliche Bauhaus Weimar (Voices in the press for the State Bauhaus in Weimar), published in 1924 in order to ward off attacks on the Bauhaus from reactionary circles, there is a list of “literature on modern arts education that calls for the kind of reuniting of artistic and technical work that the Bauhaus has carried out in practice."33 Christian Schädlich, one of the promoters of the scholarly reception of the Bauhaus in the former GDR,34 has pointed out that this list, the original of which is in the Thüringisches Hauptstaatsarchiv in Weimar, was compiled by Gropius in the summer of 1920. He did so not only for the above-mentioned budget debate in parliament but also to put material in the hands of the Bauhaus student representative, Werner Chomton, that would make it easier for him to influence the delegates at the founding meeting of the Reichsbund deutscher Kunsthochschüler (National association of German art school students) to support art school reform and the Bauhaus.35

Here the term “art school reform" is meant to include all of the antiacademic movements of the first third of the twentieth century that sought a renewal of artists' training. In these art schools of a new type, whether they merely remained ideas or were actually realized, the production of fine art of the old art academies and the applied design projects of the more recent arts and crafts schools should not only exist side by side with equal rights but should, as much as possible, achieve a synthesis. There is no question that the Bauhaus came especially close to this controversial goal. In addition to the Bauhaus, however, several other schools should be mentioned that—sometimes earlier, sometimes simultaneously—became supporters of art school reform with varying degrees of prominence, such as the Obrist-Debschitz-Schule in Munich, the Kunstschule in Frankfurt, the Kunstgewerbeschule in Düsseldorf, the Akademie für Kunst und Kunstgewerbe in Breslau (Wroclaw), the Reimann-Schule in Berlin, the Vereinigte Staatschulen für Kunst und Kunstgewerbe in Berlin, the Folkwang-Schule in Essen, the Itten-Schule in Berlin, and the Weg-Schule in Dresden. To discuss the teaching programs, organizational structures, personnel, and achievements of all of them would exceed the scope of this book. In this context, we can simply allude to the exhibition Kunstschulreform, 1900–1933,36presented by the Bauhaus-Archiv in Berlin, and the accompanying volume of primary materials, as well as to several of the notes in the section “The Weimar Republic" in chapter 12.

The goal of the following collection of quotations from primary sources on art school reform is to show that the basic pedagogical concept of the Bauhaus did not simply fall from the sky but was based on the discussions of reform during the final years of the war and in the early postwar period. Regardless of certain differences of opinion that existed in the discussion of art school reform at the time (e.g., with respect to the question of the significance of studying nature), there seems to have been agreement on a number of points:

(1) The academy was outmoded; it was "finished as a vocational school";37 that is, it was no longer in a position to fulfill its duty to society, to educate the future generation of artists in a manner keeping with the times (i.e., true to life). “To get rid of or reform the art academies must therefore be one of the first requirements of the reform program. No one will dare claim that the art academies have proven themselves over the past fifty years."38 Several of the reasons that the academies failed were suggested in the previous chapter and thus need not be repeated here.

(2) Insights gained from the failure of the old academies led the apologists of art school reform to aspire to found artists' training in the crafts.

"The reform must be based on the handicrafts. . . ."39 (Bode).

—"Every great artistic achievement develops from the basis of a craft skill."40 (Debschitz)

—"[Education] should be focused on an approach that is based on the solid ground of the handicrafts."41 (Fischer)

—"Back to handicrafts!"42 (Waetzoldt)

—"[A] thorough training in the crafts . . . is required of all students as the indispensable basis for all artistic production"43 (Gropius)

The postulate "Back to handicrafts!" whose social and intellectual history has already been sketched elsewhere, was justified with motives not only from pedagogy but also social policy and political economy. The pedagogical motive sprang from the recognition that art cannot be taught; only techniques from the crafts could be taught and learned:

"[A]rt [is] not teachable and one [cannot] educate artists. What can one teach, then? All of the technical activities that must be practiced to produce an artwork, that is, handicrafts in the narrow sense.. . ."44(Bosselt)

—"All that one artist can teach another is the technical method; everyone must strive alone for the intellectual result."45 (Hoeber)

—"Certainly in any art one cannot teach or learn the truly 'artistic' aspect. .. . But can the technical aspect of art be taught? Of course."46 (Waetzoldt)

—“Art rises above all methods; in itself it cannot be taught, but the crafts certainly can be."47 (Gropius)

In addition to the motive, common since the eighteenth or nineteenth century, derived from mercantilistic or economic goals—namely, making local products competitive in foreign markets through quality artisanship—the discussion on the reform of art schools almost always had another motive for grounding art in handicrafts, this time based more on social policy and the economic effects of education—namely, to integrate the so-called proletariat of artists produced by the academies into the trades:

—"[I]n the future. . . we must have a unified art school, ... where those with special talent obtain training for the high arts, but who can then, thanks to groundwork in handicrafts, can turn at any time to arts and crafts—to put it another way, to manual labor!"48(Bode)

—"[T]oday the reformation of the usual form of arts instruction seems to have become a necessity of political economy, in order to integrate the excess of talented people into our present, difficult economic life" and to lead students "to intellectually and economically secure places in the life of our nation."49 (Hoeber)

"The disparity between the number of young men leaving the academy as so-called fine artists and the number of those who are in a positian to establish an economically secure existence-that is to say, the creation of the 'proletariat of artists'—would lead one to ask whether the fine artists who fail might not still have a way to survive by retreating into the applied arts, if they were to have a serious training in the crafts behind them."50(Waetzoldt)

(3) Closely related to the call for basing art in the crafts was the principle of workshop training that the arts and crafts schools hadalready sought, and sometimes even achieved:

—"To start there [with handicrafts—R.W.], means to turn back, to turn home from academic abstraction to active, true work, from the school into the workshop."51 (Fischer)

—"[T]he learning process must, as much as possible, correspond to a true apprenticeship like that of a workshop."52 (Paul)

"The old schools of art... must be merged once more with the workshop. ... The school is the servant of the workshop, and will one day be absorbed in it."53 (Gropius)

The discussion on art school reform remained undecided on the question of whether these workshops should serve exclusively for apprenticeship (and possibly also for design experimentation or research on artistic and technical fundamentals) or should have the goal of real production for a specific employer or the anonymous market—that is, whether they should be exclusively teaching workshops (perhaps also with elements of a laboratory) or productive workshops.54 The ambivalence that is found in the literature turns out itself to be a feature of the historical development of the Bauhaus. In the founding years, the principle of the teaching workshops dominated (even if they were sometimes involved in addressing real projects); in Dessau, under Gropius and even more so under Hannes Meyer, productive work gained in importance; and for Mies van der Rohe, finally, there was a decisive return to the status of the teaching workshop.

(4) The thoughts of the art school reformers culminated in the idea of the unified art school. Thus, they were in no way interested in eliminating the possibility of being trained as a fine artist (though certáinly interested in breaking the monopoly on that training held by the art academies), nor did they wish to make the vocational education of applieddesigners as practicedby the arts and crafts schools absolute; rather, they wished to combine both fields together with the faculty of architecture of the technical universities or academies of architecture:

—“The first priority is to consolidate all branches of artistic activity that belong to the visual arts into a single pedagogical entity and to keep the various fields within an internal coherence from the shared starting level onward. . . . In this way, the future advanced school of art would strive for an intimate fusion and intellectual interpenetration of the three main areas: architecture, the fine arts, and the building technology of the engineers. But there is yet another area to be counted among the visual arts, the so-called arts and crafts."55 (Behrens)

—“Just as we now have a unified primary school, so in the future we must also have a unifiedart school in which the studentsare trained both for handicrafts and for fine arts."56 (Bode)

—“[T]he fulfillment of the followingdemand is indisputable: . .. Consolidation of the whole range of artists' training, both the 'fine' and the 'applied' arts in a unified art school for architecture, sculpture, and painting."57 (Paul)

—"Transform the academies into master studios—combine these master studios with what is now separated out as the 'arts and crafts school' and take the architecture departments out of the technical universities in order to attach them to a newly created body of a 'school for artistic design'!" Then “for the future one group will be desired that will combine all areas of design now defined by the terms 'civil engineer,''architect,' 'artisan,' and 'fine artist,' into a new, comprehensive term."58 (Schumacher)

Fritz Schumacher seems to have been the first to coin the term of the new type of unified art school, which was then still just an idea, when he spoke of “universities of design" (Hochschulen des Gesta/tens).59 The term still retains its validity even today; it was taken over, in the slightly modified form "Hochschule der Gestaltung," as the subtitle for the Bauhaus, when it was recognized as a university in 1926; and after World War Il it was used by Max Bill for the university founded in Ulm in 1953 (see “After 1945: The Federal Republic of Germany" in chapter 12).

The inclusion of architecture in the new unified art school, in the new “university of design," was a central concern of almost all the art school reformers, and it is explained only partially by the fact that most protagonists of the reform movement were architects (e.g., Bartning, Behrens, Fischer, Gropius, Schumacher, Riemerschmid) and thus were speculating on the triumph of the particular interests of their profession. The determining factor, however, was the general view that the separation ofarchitecture from painting, sculpture, and arts and crafts was not only a disadvantage for architecture itself but also for the other artistic disciplines:60

It has become clear that almost all artisanal activity only finds its true existence in the context or against the background of architectonic intentions. .. . This led to the architect's being given an important role in the trainingprogram. . . . The idea is in the air that every problem presented to the decorative sculptor, the decorative painter, the carpenter, the fitter, and so on, should be developed in context with a specified space or other architectural form. .. .61

This concept was self-evident in Jugendstil around the turn of the century, and around twenty years later, the first signs of its realization by the Bauhaus can be seen, for example, in the Sommerfeld House in Berlin (1920-21), in Gropius's administrative office (1923), or in Muche's model house (the "Haus am Horn," 1923). An idea very close to Gropius's perspectives is found in Waetzoldt's conception that

architecture deserves the leading role around which its sister arts will gather in the new type of school. .. . The assignment of a building commission—say, the reconstruction of a theater— would bring together .. . a series of a university's master studios into a 'masons' lodge.62

(5) From the literature on art school reform that is, as Gropius put it, "related to the ideas of the Bauhaus,"63 one can distill yet another shared idea, that of a preliminary stage or preliminary

school that would provide the beginning students with self-experience and self-knowledge, allow them to explore their creative abilities, and help them decide on a specialty or study major. At the Bauhaus, this was institutionalized as the preliminary course:

"We must begin with a class that includes everything, in which everything is done, in which everyone can pursue his inclinations, observe everything, examine all possibilities, explore and express himself."64 (Bosselt)

—"The possibility that an . . . artistically talented child always first tries something out itself and then keeps the best for itself founders on the . . . rift within our institutions that teach the techniques of art today. This evil could be remedied by a preliminary school that retains an overview of the artistic paths of artistic development but also places the 'principle of design' in the forefront of the instruction. It must be made clear from the start to every disciple of art that the essential part cannot be the mechanical sketching of arbitrary events in nature. . . ."65 (Hoeber)

—“This would commend the idea of allowing leniency to win out in doubtful cases when considering admissions, especially since the first semester of a unified school should be a trial period. . . . A loafing or trial semester, in which one can look around, try things out, observe oneself . . . , would allow the beginner to find his own way into the clear, saving him many detours and disappointments. The outlook of the teacher toward these students as they grope along should, as much as possible, not be one of correction andregulation but rather of advice, guidance, and showing the way into the essence of artistic means."66 (Waetzoldt)

This compilation of passages on the central theses of art school reform may well have revealedparallels to the Bauhaus, convergences with it, and the historical standpoint of this then still new "art school of modernism." We should not overlook, however, that, after all the rhetorical pathos that speaks from the reform proposals we have cited, it is ultimately deeds that count. The Bauhaus was "the art school that took up the reform ideas most thoroughly and led to a practical solution."67More than that, it marked the art school reform movement with its own stamp, which can be seen in the appropriation of its principles, methods, and structures by other art schools of the twenties and thirties (see "The Weimar Republic" in chapter 12). Only in postrevolutionary Russia was this form of artists' training put into practice with the same rigor as at the

Bauhaus. The main proponents of this development were the Higher Artistic-Technical Workshops in Moscow known as Vkhutemas (later renamed Vkhutein), which played the most prominent role among the many reformed art schools of the young Soviet Republic that grew out of the ruins of czarist Russia.

## Bauhaus and Vkhutemas/Vkhutein

While the Bauhaus was constantly fighting political reactionaries from its founding until it was forced by the National Socialists to close, the reform of art schools in Russia after the October Revolution had full state support. "Experimentation wanted" seems to have been the motto of the policies for official art and education until Stalin took power, which resulted in a large number of competing and to some extent incompatible reform programs and experimental models.

One of the early stages in this development was the “art program"68 that Anatoly Lunacharsky, the intellectually gifted leader of the People's Commissariat for the Enlightenment of the People, developed from an artists' college. The program amounted to an effort to redefine the principles for art's function within a socialist society and to reorganize fundamentally the arts institutions. This program called for, among other things: “The elimination of the classifying distinctions between sculptor and plasterer, painter and sign painter. The elevation of crafts to an art. . . . Fertilization of the crafts by the artist-innovator."69 One is struck by the affinity—even in the language—to Gropius's goal to counter the "class-separating distinctions" that had raised an "arrogant barrier between craftsman and artist."70 It is equally striking that Gropius's policy in making appointments corresponded very closely to the concept of a"fertilization of the crafts by the artist-innovator" in that, alongside the masters of the crafts, he also appointed such artists as masters of form at the Bauhaus. From their creativity and aesthetic competence he hoped for innovations as well, above all in the domain of the trades or industry.

Another important point of the“art program" was the call to establish "free state art workshops" in place of the existing art academies "miserably" vegetating in "routine classicism." The goal of these art workshops—which were founded in 1918 as successors to the two czarist artistic training institutions in Moscow, the Stroganov Arts and Crafts School, and the School for Painting, Sculpture, and Architecture/—initially remained rather ill defined: "the dissemination of artistic education, the awakening of aesthetic instincts, and the development of artistic vision."72 Konstantin Umanskij reportedin his 1920 book Neue Kunst in Rußland(New art in Russia) that in the Free State Art Workshops, alongside the presence of “all modern art movements," the "interest in artistic crafts" was especially strong:

Even many youngartists are convinced that painting and its culture are dying out, that a new, monumental—but not specifically decorative—art would develop on the ground of the crafts. . . . Fine artists should . . . play a role in the fundamental formal restructure of those factories that in earlier times were so symptomatic in their neglect of the artistic in favor of petit bourgeois taste. .. . Thus . .. the attempt has been made to prepare the application of new forms of art to daily life and even to view them as the most important factor in the work of every “productive state workshop." . . . The state wants to restructure the “free workshops" into active factors within industrial life and create a solid economic basis for life in the workshops by increasing their productivity.73

Setting aside the young socialist state's concern for welfare at these free workshops (an official interest that was almost entirely lacking at the Bauhaus), the parallels between the two countries are evident: the foundation of art in crafts, bringing the artist into design projects, and the establishment of productive workshops. These are parallels that raise the question whether this was the result of temporary analogies in social and political development (the October Revolution in Russia in 1917 and the November Revolution in Germany in 1918) or a direct transfer of ideas in one or the other direction.

The possibility of an exchange of ideas is supported not only by the formulations in the 1919 founding manifesto of the Bauhaus that closely resemble statements in the 1918 Moscow "Art Program,"but also the fact that in December 1918 (that is, shortly after the November Revolution in Germany and four months before the founding of the Bauhaus) the People's Commissariat for the Education of the People had sent the painter Ludwig Bähr to Berlin in order to inform the artists active in the capital of the Reich who were progressive in both aesthetic and political views about the situation of art and the art schools in the new Russia and to bring them programs of Soviet artists. (At the time, in December 1918—as already noted in “From Henry van de Velde's Arts and Crafts School to the Founding of the Bauhaus in 1919" in chapter 3—the Arbeitsrat für Kunst74 [Work council for the arts], in which Gropius played a prominent role as chairman for a time, was being founded in Berlin on the model of workers', soldiers', and farmers' councils.) It seems probable that the "Art Program" of the Commissariat for the Education of the People, which was quoted above and was published by PaulWestheim in Kunstblatt in 1919, was among the programs that Bähr distributed, and thus the possibility that it influenced Gropius's Bauhaus program seems quite likely. This view is also supported by the fact that Gropius replied to the programs that Bähr distributed in a letter in which he notes with satisfaction that the ideas of the Russian artists agree with his own in principle; he qualified this only by calling attention to the lack of an idea he considered central, the unification of all genres of art and aspects of the crafts under the primacy of architecture,75 which may, in turn, have influenced the decision in 1920 to combine the two Moscow Free State Workshops into a single educational institute: the Vkhutemas.

The Vkhutemas, which in view of the parallels in their conception can rightly be called the "Russian Bauhaus," was established under a decree signed by Lenin and had the goal of training highly qualified artist-practitioners, of providing support for educating architects in the arts, and of encouraging art, crafts, and product design to the benefit of the national economy.76 Systemat-

In 1928 the Vkhutemas changed its name to Higher Artistic-Technical Institute (Vkhutein), an expression of the school's increased emphasis on technology and science and its increased focus on architecture and product design (industrial design). In 1930, by order of the government, the Vkhutein was closed and divided into specialized universities where theartistic element ceded more andmore to technical utility—a development that the institute's last rector, P.Novitskii, harshly criticized as a “perversion of higher education in the arts" and a “break with the perspectives and plans of the cultural revolution."80

So much for this brief overview of the goals, organizationalstructure, and historical development ofthe“MoscowBauhaus" Vkhutemas/ Vkhutein, which despite its brevity may still have shed some light on features it shared with the German Bauhaus.81 In puttinginto practice its goalofintegratingart, crafts, and industrial design andarchitecture, the Vkhutemas hadincomparably many more difficulties to overcome than the Bauhaus did. This was the result not only of the ideological and artistic differences within the teaching faculty that were alluded to above— these existedat the Bauhaus as well—but above all with the size of the institute and the resulting organizational problems. In the beginning, more than2,000 students studied at the Vkhutemas, and later about 1,500. “With 150 students at the Bauhaus the unification of the arts on the basis of practical, interdisciplinary cooperativework could be realizedmore easily than at the Vkhutemas, which was ten times that size. Only the basic division had an integrating function."82

It remains only to mention several direct connections between the Bauhaus and the Vkhutemas/Vkhutein in the 1920s: El Lissitzky, a teacher at the Moscow institute from 1925 to 1930, visited the Bauhaus several times; in 1927–28 there were student exchanges; and Hinnerk Scheper, director of the workshop for mural painting at the Dessau Bauhaus and advisor for polychrome architecture in Moscow from 1929 to 1931, wrote an "Open Letter to the Students of the Vkhutein" in 1930, in which he took a critical position vis-à-vis the return to objective painting—i.e., socialist realism—that Stalin had decreed: "You cannot simultaneously paint academic nudes and construct a chair for a house, for the masses. ... You are learning academic art painting, but academic art painting is dead—throw its abandoned corpse out of the house!"83

## An Overview of the Goals and Organizational Structure of the Teachings at the Bauhaus

Before the pedagogy of the Bauhaus is discussed in individual presentations of the leadingartists teaching there, we should first sketch itsfundamental goals and its organizational structure. Our goal cannot be to cement a static image, as always happens in the literature when the famous 1923 circular diaqram of the teachings of the Bauhaus is used, but rather to outline the sometimes subtle, sometimes massive changes in the curriculum that transformed the “art school of modernism" into a modern architectural academy.

As was shown in the chapter on the history of the Bauhaus (chapter 3), while the Bauhaus program of 1919 contained an extensive catalog of subject areas, it did not have a true teaching plan; this fact should be our starting point. The sketch of a utopian society presented in the founding manifesto overshadows at first the question of how teaching at the Bauhaus was organized in detail, how the subject areas related to one another, and how much time each would take. This circumstance is little altered by pointing out that it was planned to "divide the teachings" into three programs—one for apprentices (Lehrlinge), one for journeymen (Gesellen), and one for junior masters (Jungmeister)—because the founding document does not yet reveal what these programs should look like in practice. It merely states that teaching in crafts is an integral part of the pedagogy of the Bauhaus. According to the program, such teaching forms "the basis of all teaching at the Bauhaus. Every student must learn a craft."84 The following are the occupations that Gropius saw as relevant in view of his idea of the Gesamtkunstwerk (total work of art) under the primacy of architecture:

(a) sculptors, stonemasons, stucco workers, woodcarvers, ceramic workers, plaster casters, (b) blacksmiths, locksmiths, founders, metal turners,

(c)cabinetmakers,

(d) painter-and-decorators, glass painters, mosaic workers, enamelers,

(e) etchers, wood engravers, lithographers, art printers, enchasers,

(f)weavers.85

What is new, what goes beyond the approach used in reforming the arts and crafts schools, is Gropius's concept of a formalized double qualification in the arts and the handicrafts in the sense that the Bauhaus always had regular teachings in the handicrafts in parallel with, or integrated into, the artistic teachings that were obligatory for all Bauhaus students according to the statutes of 1921 and that concluded with a journeyman's exam (administered by the handicrafts department and the council of masters). This joining of the crafts portion of training at the Bauhaus with the handicrafts department as the external authority for control and certification was above all a tactical decision: in this way Gropius hoped to get the local, conservative handicrafts in Weimar to commit to the goals of the Bauhaus or at least allow it to be tolerated in silence.

In this context, we need to clear up a widespread misunderstanding found in the history of the reception of the Bauhaus. Contrary to common interpretations, which are certainly encouraged by the recourse to the Middle Ages in the founding manifesto of the Bauhaus, Gropius's concept of handicrafts never attempted to actually reconstruct an old professional category or a historically dated form of production—something Morris clearly did still want, as his utopian novel News from Nowhere demonstrates. Rather, as he emphasized repeatedly, handicrafts were for Gropius not an "eccentricity" but "an indispensable means to an end."86 This relationship of means to ends was at its heart socially and pedagogically motivated. Behind it stood the vision that the collective of those working on the Gesamtkunstwerk would achieve the form of social organization that the sociologist Ferdinand Tönnies characterized with the term "community" (Gemeinschaft), which he sharply distinguished from "society"(Gesel/schaft). With the overstatement that comes with an ideal type, Tönnies confronted the living, "natural" life form, the "organism" grounded in neighborhood, friendship, and solidarity with the "mechanical aggregate and artifact" of society. "Community," according to Tönnies, is based on selfless, mutual aid of its members, whereas "society" is characterized by increasing profits, the use of money, and egoism and must necessarily lead to states of tension.87 These sorts of ideas, which were common in intellectual circles at the time, may have inspired Gropius in 1919 “to gather together from the exploded isolation of the individual a small community here [at the Bauhaus—R.W.]"88 that would be the seed for a new, more humane, more just arrangement of a society based on ideas of harmony.

In addition to this implication of social reform inherent in the idea of handicrafts, we should emphasize the eminent pedagogical value of manual labor in Gropius's thinking. Manual labor was for Gropius a fundamental pedagogical category, signifying the basic form of practical work and

vocational learning, which, though it arose in preindustrial arts and crafts, is completely independent of any organizational form of crafts economy. For that reason, no external change in the form of the work world can invalidate it. To the extent that professional work requires the use of the hands, demands practical ability, consists of technical use of objects, it can only be learned in through manual activity. However highly industrialized the situation may be, "manual labor" as the fundamental form of working and learning is indispensable.89

The following brief, necessarily extremely incomplete excursus should cast a spotlight on the fact that there is a tradition of manual labor as a pedagogical motif in the history of education even completely independent of artists' training.90

John Locke (1632–1704) is one of the first in the history of pedagogy to have recognized the importance of manual activity for education. His ideal of education is the English gentleman who-—in order to be useful for a practical life— not only must master skills like dancing, fencing, and riding but should learn a trade that is useful for life and contributes to good health (gardening, farming, wood- or metalworking).

The pedagogy of Pietism, whose main proponent was August Hermann Francke (1663- 1727), also emphasizes the practical aspect of education for life:

Francke includes in the teaching plan for higher education . . . practical fields as well, such as geography, history, arithmetic, geometry, natural history, home economics. . . , as well as crafts activities, such as lathe work, glass polishing, engraving, gardening. . .. The humanities and natural sciences andthe craft activities are intended for “recreation" time, for relaxation, for free time.91

Jean-JacquesRousseau (1717–78)has the protagonist of his novel Emile (1762)—who is meant to grow up naturally, that is, as free as possible of cultural obligations—learn a trade, not in order topractice it as a profession but to contribute to a well-rounded development of his humanity and personality. Rousseau writes:

Now, of all the occupations which can provide subsistence to man, that which brings him closest to the state of nature is manual labor. . . . The goal is less to learn a trade in order to know a trade than to conquer the prejudices that despise a trade. . . . I absolutely want Emile to learn a trade. . . . All things well considered, the trade I would most like to be to my pupil's taste is the carpenter's. . . . It keeps the body sufficiently in shape; it requires skill and industry from the worker; and while the form of the work is determined by utility, elegance and taste are not excluded.92

In philanthropism, which tries to utilize the ideas of rationalism and naturalism in the field of education and training, the acquisition of realistic knowledge and craft skills is again seen under the sign of utilitarian goals. Thus Johann Bernhard Basedow (1724–90), the main proponent of philanthropism and founder of the Philanthropinum in Dessau, placed great value on manual labor within the framework of an educational program directed to practical life, placing it alongside subjects like history, training in the student's native language, education to citizenship, the study of nature, physics, and physical education. The goals of the philanthropist Christian Gotthilf Salzmann (1744–1811) shared such tendencies, encouraging the students to crafts activities (producing objects from paper and thread, making toys, working with baskets, cardboard, paint, carpentry, and lathes).

Efforts of this kind, as well as the introduction of so-called industrial training by Ferdinand Kindermann (1740–1801), can be subsumed under the general designation of eighteenth-century “industrial pedagogy."93

Feeding on mercantilistic trade policies as well as enlightenment and Pietist thought, the industrial schools of the eighteenth century aimed less at realizing a specific idea of education than "supporting individual trades or introducing them into the population."94 In the linguistic conventions of the day, industry meant "gainful employment" (Lessing), "the application of a skill" (Pestalozzi), "dexterity," and above all "industriousness" (Stapfer), and so the program of the industrial school can be understood as, among other things, an attempt to instill industriousness in the poor in place of a deeply rooted aversion to work (it has been estimated that beggars comprised 25 percent of the overall population in the ecclesiastical territories of the eighteenth century).

We should also mention here Johann Heinrich Pestalozzi (1746-1827) and Friedrich Fröbel (1782-1852). Going beyond the utilitarian goals that dominate in industrial school pedagogy, these two educators viewed manual labor as the means to a well-rounded, comprehensive education. With Pestalozzi begins "the epoch of the pedagogical appreciation of manual labor,"95 which would find its way into diverse ramifications of the work school movement of the early twentieth century, of whose main proponents we mention only Georg Kerschensteiner (1854- 1932). For Kerschensteiner handicrafts are the "foundation of all true art,"96 a belief that, as we have seen, corresponds with Gropius's ideas and those of other art school reformers.

With that, we return to the Bauhaus. It is striking that in the area of training in art—or as it was worded there, "drawing and painting"—the program of 1919 still leaned heavily on the traditional catalog of courses from the arts and crafts schools, which can be seen most clearly from the item "design of ornaments." The drawing and designing of ornaments occupied a significant place at the arts and crafts schools; and ornamental exercises with the basic forms of circle, square, and triangle played a certain role even in Johannes Itten's preliminary course, though not with the goal of training future pattern designers. To the extent that Gropius's view of modern architecture and design moved in the direction of unadorned functionalism, the design of ornaments quickly lost its raison d'être and was soon eliminated from the canon of instruction.

Alongside training in crafts and drawing and painting (i.e., art), the third pillar of Bauhaus teaching, according to the program of 1919, was in science and theory, which included basic subjects in the natural sciences and technology (science of materials, physical and chemical theory of color, rational painting methods), art history (not in the sense of the history of styles but as a history of techniques), anatomy (not as part of the academic discourse but from a living model), and basic information for running a business (accounting, contracts, etc.). The purpose of teaching such business skills was to counteract the growing notion among artists of their supposed freedom from economic considerations and to make them able to act as economic subjects, as partners in negotiations and contracts (this idea is typical of the Werkbund).

The guidelines of the 1919 program are essentially repeated in the statutes of the Staatliches Bauhaus in Weimar from January 1921, especially the idea that "thorough training of all students in the crafts provides the unifying foundation."97 In many respects, though, the statutes supply additional precision or modifications: in place of "training in drawing and painting," the statutes speak of "form instruction," which serves as the artistic complement of “instruction in the crafts." Compared to the program of 1919, the teachings at the Bauhaus seem clearly strictly disciplined, so that the "bold, though perhaps utopian, blueprint for society" now takes on the character of a "realistic plan for school reform."98

The instruction in form now included just four (as opposed to ten) areas of teaching, namely:

(a) study of elementary materials;

(b) nature study;

(c) instruction in design (drawing, painting, modeling, building), study of elementary forms, design of plane, body, and space, instruction in composition;

(d) technical drawing (instruction in projection and construction drawing) and building of models of all three-dimensional structures (objects of daily use, furniture, rooms, buildings).99

The “design of ornaments" had already disappeared. Another change with respect to the 1919 program is that the high expectations for “training in science and theory" have been scaled back in the meanwhile, so that now the reference is merely to “supplementary subjects of instruction."

The definite progress over the 1919 program does not, however, lie in the changes in terminology and content just discussed but in the establishment of the preliminary course (Vorkus), which was then still called preliminary instruction (Vorunterricht) and was later renamed basic instruction (Grundlehre). As mentioned before, this was the work of Johannes Itten. The statutes of 1921 read:

Every applicant will at first be admitted for a trial period of six months. . . . During this period the preliminary course is obligatory. This course consists of elementary instruction in form, in conjunction with studies of materials. . .. Final admission is dependent on the applicant's completion of the above classes and on the quality of his independent work finished during this sixmonth trial period. Only after being finally approved by the Council of Masters may the newly accepted student join the workshop of his choice and freely select his artistic master from among the membership of the Council of Masters.100

The preliminary course thus became the pedagogical basis of the Bauhaus:

The object of this course was “the knowledge and proper evaluation of the individual means of expression." The point was above all“to liberate the creative forces in the student" while “avoiding any binding attachment to any style movement." For that reason, the course was limited to "observation and depiction" with the intent to recognize “the identity of form and content." The young artist should strive for "liberation from dead conventions" and become aware of "the limits nature sets on his creative power." No formal categories exist; rhythm will become the “primal means of expression" for one student; for another, chiaroscuro; for a third, color, and so on.101

Although in this overview the architectural instruction is the heart of teaching at the Bauhaus—very much in keeping with the goals of the founding manifesto: "The ultimate goalof all artistic activity is architecture"—there was no formal architectural department until 1927, as we have seen. This means that the Bauhaus remained a torso—judgedon its own terms—for many years, only to turn the primacy of architecture into dogma during the era of Hannes Meyer and Mies van der Rohe.

Let us sum up at this point. At the end of the Weimar period, the judgment of the work at the Bauhaus and especially the pedagogical efforts of the Bauhaus is full of contradictions. For example, shortly before the school was moved to Dessau, as part of a series of articles "For and Against the Bauhaus," the Anhalter Anzeiger for 18 March 1925 had two quite different appraisals. The chief master of the Dessau carpenter's guild sharply criticized the Bauhaus's orientation toward industry and concluded that the crafts "have no interest in the Bauhaus in its present form, since it does not benefit the crafts at all."103 On the other hand, a representative of the school board, Commissioner Blum, came to a extraordinarily positive assessment of the institute, especially with respect to its pedagogical efforts. Under the title "The Bauhaus: An Educational Achievement," he wrote:

Concerning the matter itself, we must recognize that in this case we are dealing here with new ideas which must of course struggle against all the difficulties that are encountered when one introduces something new. . . . In any case, it seems to me that the essence of the work of the Bauhaus is the pedagogical interpretation of new ethical problems and educational achievement.

Also, the logical founding and pervasion of the new ideas in the handicrafts and in architecture and their educational interpretation are certainly original. . .. Gropius pursues his basic idea with the utmost resolution and almost ascetic rigor. But in this fact lies, in my estimation, the educational significance of his endeavors.104

Following the move of the Bauhaus to Dessau, the addition of several junior masters to the teaching staff, and in the wake of the Bauhaus's change in orientation away from crafts and toward industry, we can detect some striking transformations or shifts in emphasis with respect to pedagogical goals, the canon of instruction offered, and the organization of the teaching. In the formulation of the goal, there are differences that are more than just linguistic nuances:

In contrast to the pathos of the founding program, the later statements of purpose are soberer and focused on the present, as technology and industry began around 1925 to be explicitly included among the goals of the Bauhaus. At the same time, the idea of synthesis in construction was picked up again on the teaching plan of 1925—after having been dropped in 1921, probably under Itten's influence—but now was freed of the romanticizing undertones of the founding program.

The basic structure of the teachings at the Bauhaus was retained in its entirety in Dessau, but it took on a trend toward practical school. This can be seen, for example, in that the preliminary course, which had already been extended from a semester to a year during the winter semester 1924-25 and was now called "basic instruction," was completely structured in detail, a move that also justifies, from the pedagogical standpoint, the name "phase of consolidation" for the years 1923 to 1928. In a plan for 1925–26, the basic instruction was subdivided, by analogy to so-called main instruction, into a basic practical instruction and a basic formal instruction and the subject matter was precisely specified:

## I. Basic practical instruction:

Becoming acquainted with various types of materials and tools; devising and building of useful objects in the specialworkshop for the basic course.

Working out original designs and justifying them with respect to the choice of material, economy, and technique.

Independent execution of designs.

Mutual criticism of the finished product with respect to its function and expressiveness and the possibilities for improvements, with respect to form (scale, material, and color), material (quality, value), economy (expenditure, return) and techniques (construction, production).

Collecting and systematically tabulating samples of materials.

Guided tours through workshops and plants.

Projection anddraughtsmanshipconstitute an introduction to the specialized graphic art of the general course.

## II. Basic form instruction:

Theory and practical exercises:

Analysis of the elements of form (orientation, designation, and terminology).

Organic and functional relationships (principles, construction, structure).

Introduction to the principles of abstraction (appearance, nature, scheme).

Primary and secondary, elementary and mixed application of the means of design.

Design exercises: Drawing, painting, building.105

It is obvious that this plan for the basic instruction significantly limits, in comparison to the preliminary course under Itten, the students' room to find themselves creatively, to develop their design ego. It is equally obvious that this plan very precisely represents the increasingly instrumentalist and utilitarian orientation of the Bauhaus in its postexpressionist, constructivist, and technological phase. The three years of main instruction as part of the practical instruction that follow the basic instruction are reduced to five of the seven workshops: wood, metal, color, weaving, book and art printing; the workshops for ceramics, stone, and glass—i.e., those in which purely craft production methods dominate, and the principle of mechanical reproduction is only somewhat feasible—had been dropped. The practical instruction was supplemented by the instruction in form, but in the early years in Dessau the canon of instruction remained essentially unchanged. The architectural instruction that followed the main instruction was retained, and it now lasted for three semesters.

The 1926 brochure Bauhaus Dessau: Statutes —Teaching Rules essentially follows, in its goals and seguence of instruction (basic instruction, main instruction, architectural instruction), the teaching plan of 1925. A semester plan printed in a prospectus from that period,106 however, reveals anincreasing tendency toward practical schooling. Under Hannes Meyer this tendency reached its apogee with the establishment of obligatory coursework of precisely specified length for the first four semesters.107

These required courses were then supplemented, as electives, by the so-called free painting classes of Kandinsky and Klee. As we have emphasized elsewhere, this was a logical consequence of the view that art and technology cannot be fused to the unity that Gropius had seen as the ideal. The tight interaction of artistic instruction (instruction in form) and practical training in crafts began palpably to recede. Art and handicrafts (or industry) were clearly growing apart. Despite the practical experimental work that was sought and to some degree achieved, the training of the architects was strictly regulated, so that the instruction could hardly be distinguished from that of the technical universities;108 and psychology and sociology were given great weight as auxiliary or reference sciences. Hannes Meyer remarked of his own term as director that it was characterized by

an emphasis on the social mission of the Bauhaus, by the increase of exact sciences on the teaching plan, by limiting the influence of the artists, by cooperative work in the workshop units, by building up the work pedagogy by means of real commissions, by developing the norm and standard of the people's needs, by the proletarization of the student body, and by closer cooperation with the workers' movement and with the unions.109

In the territorial waters of Marxist pedagogy, Meyer wrote in a 1927 letter to Gropius of the "functional, collective, constructive"110 basic tendency of his future teaching plan for the Bauhaus, and looking back more than a decade later, he used the even more unequivocal term "polytechnic pedagogy."

Under Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, the Bauhaus idea underwent a final reinterpretation. This can be seen in the statutes of 1930 and even more clearly in the study and teaching plan for 1932. Both in terms of goals and the organization of Bauhaus teachings, the clear profile that had existed under Gropius began to lose its contours. The original concept of the unified art school seems tohave been diluted to the point of being unrecognizable; the rest is noncommittal: "The goal of the Bauhaus is to educate the students thoroughly in crafts, technology and art."111

The tripartite study program was retained, but the terms "basic instruction—main instruction—architectural instruction" were replaced by the neutral terms "first stage—second stage— third stage." In the first stage, which corresponed to the preliminary course, "students who differ with respect to previous education and talent are brought to a common standard."112 Where Itten sought to free the students to activate their own creative possibilities and contribute to their individual development, the first stage ten years later pursues an education of conformity to the dominant conception of design at the Bauhaus, resulting in a new form of academicism.

Acceptance into the “second level," formerly the "main instruction," entitled the student to decide on a course of study in one of the following departments:

1. architecture and interior design

2. commercial art

3. photography

4. weaving

5. fine art.

In this stage students are given a practical, technical, and scientific background for their work in the various special fields. Theoretical instruction is supplemented by practical experimental work in order to develop a sense for design and quality in manual and technical work.

Based on technical knowledge and professionalcapabilities, in the third stage begins independent design work.113

Two things are worth noting here: first, the abandonment of the decidedly crafts-based character of the main instruction and the degree qua journeyman's certificate; second, the abandonment of the architectural instruction in the comprehensive and integral sense that Gropius originally intended. In this 1932 study and teaching plan, hardly a trace remains of the idea of a synthesis of all artistic genres and areas of the crafts in architecture. Contrary to all the ideals of the art school reform movement, the fine arts lived an isolated, independent existence in the socalledfree painting classes, peripheral to the school's productive cooperative. They had regained their autonomy but at the same time had sunk to being an appendix to a school for architecture and design, in which the goal of qualifying students to be competent, specialized professionals clearly outranked that of educating them to be creative generalists.

## The Bauhaus Directors as Educators

## Walter Gropius

To conclude this chapter and serve as a transition to the individual depictions in the chapters that follow, we should sum up by shedding some light on the ideas on education and pedagogical conceptions and practices of the three directors of the Bauhaus.

Chapters 3 and 4 have surely made it clear that Gropius had a more sharply distinguished pedagogical profile than either Hannes Meyer or Ludwig Mies van der Rohe. Not only was he was founder, long-term director, and “intellectual leader” of the school but also he clearly reflected much more on questions of aesthetic education than either of his successors did.

Not without reason, the sketches that follow are derived entirely from sources contemporaneous with the Bauhaus period (for Gropius in the United States, see chapter 13). This means, for example, doing without Gropius's own later reflections from the forties, fifties, andsixties, which show that his intentions with respect to questions of education changed significantly in certain aspects.114 This somewhat restrictive approach seems especially warranted because the founder of the Bauhaus liked to remark in retrospect that "he never had to change his fundamental views, with the exception of small details."115 Though such a claim might be understandable from a psychological point of view as a way of achieving congruence of self-perception andestablishing identity, it is in fact unmistakably clear that Gropius modified his views in questions of education in response to changing times and calculations about opportunities, and that his pedagogy was by no means as monolithic as it has often been presented in retrospect—not without help from Gropius himself in later years.116

One determining factor in his interest in questions of education may have been for Gropius a very personal experience: namely, his own study of architecture in Munich, Berlin, and again in Munich from 1903 to 1907, which he broke off without taking final exams. In these student years—interrupted by military service as a one-year volunteer (Einjährig-Freiwilliger)—he became aware that architectural training of the sort found at the technical universities and architectural academies of the time was inadequate preparation for the solution of the architectural tasks of the future; that there was a deep chasm between the encyclopedic information being taught and later practical demands; that, despite changes in society and innovations in building technologies (iron structures, skeleton construction), they were still teaching how "to apply historical styles to new buildings,"117 an exercise, by the way, that became popular again in the architectural training of young architects in the 1980s under the sign of so-called postmodernism.

Gropius's first conception of a school with clear antiacademic tendencies dates from 1916, namely, the “Recommendations for the Founding of an Educational Institute as an Artistic Counseling Service for Industry, the Trades, and the Crafts," already discussed above, which was sent to the State Ministry in Weimar as part of the process of deciding on a successor to Henry van de Velde (see “From Henry van de Velde's Arts and Crafts School to the Founding of the Bauhaus in 1919" in chapter 3). It is a document in which Gropius lays out his ideas for a design education of the future, which had fed on contemporaneous debates about art school reform and were deeply inspired by the Werkbund. The fact that he was in a position to make these sorts of proposals while writing from the front in the middle of the war shows that he had long been considering questions of aesthetic education and that he had closely followed the prewar discussions about reforms in education for designers.

The focus of the “Recommendations" is a clear affirmation of technology and the machine and—along with the cooperation among the businessman, the technician, and the artist that economic opportunity demanded—the “continual perfecting" in the process of “ennobling" craft or industrial production as the "basic idea of education":

The teaching of organic design takes the place of the old, discredited method, which was to stick unrelatedfrills on the existing forms of craft and industrial products. The arbitrary stylistic form of the rococo or the Renaissance no longer suits the streamlined precision of modern technological and economic life or the proper exploitation of material, finances, labor, and time. A form that is in itself noble becomes a sentimental phrase if used senselessly. The new age demands its own sense. Accurately cast form, bare of ornamentation, having clear contrasts and consistency of form and color become commensurate with the energy and economy of modern life—-the aesthetic equipment of the modern applied artist. It has to be the unceasing aspiration of the leading teacher, from the start, to implant a clear artistic credo into the students as the most important endowment.118

Only in this way would it be possible to give a uniform "expressive image" to the “modern manifestation of life," and Gropius concludes with the hope that these efforts would one day "merge into a 'new style.'"119 The search for the “new style" as a manifestation of the modern industrial society was, of course, in no way original or new but quite typical for the latter half of the nineteenth and the early twentieth centuries. The unity of this new style was for Gropius, following on Nietzsche,120 the expression of true culture; and thus it was a given that the creative artist, and especially the architect, had to take part in the creation of this new, unified style. This could be achieved not only through the artist's own exemplary activity as a designer, to which Gropius attributed—very much in keeping with the ideology of the Werkbund, which he had joined in 1912—not only an inherent contribution toward educating the public and advancing culture but also a social responsibility to participate in the aesthetic education of a new generation of designers. The sense of mission manifested here, as in many other statements by Gropius, had historical roots that can easily be traced back to the early nineteenth century. We need mention only the French philosopher Claude-Henri de Saint-Simon (1760–1825) and his social theory (Saint-Simonism), which did not simply devalue artists as the court jesters of bourgeois society but gave them, as a result of their creative potential, a leading role in society, along with scientists, engineers, and entrepreneurs.121

Already in these“Recommendations" of 1916, Gropius associates this vision of a new kind of learning institute focused on the economic needs of actual practice with the model of the medieval masons' lodge and thus postulates a quite specific concept of aesthetic education based on the principle of "social learning" (as it is called today). Social learning refers to a mode of vocationally oriented appropriation of reality, acquisition of knowledge, and attainment of practicalabilities and skills—without an explicit teaching program—that consists in the participation of everyone, teachers as well as students, in collective work motivated by a shared need. While still a student, Gropius read a study of medieval masons' lodges that clearly had a lasting influence.122 Now, in the middle of the war, he became convinced that a renewal of the spirit and its form of social organization was called for, in order to move toward the desired new style and thus the goal of a new (German) culture. We should say in passing that Gropius—coming out of the nineteenth century—owed much to a romantically transfigured and exaggerated ideal image of the Middle Ages that had little to do with the social realities of that period.

In any case, it was this conception of the medieval masons' lodge,123 revived to a new life, that then formed the intellectual heart of the Bauhaus founding manifesto. After the catastrophe of the world war, Germany's defeat, the collapse of the old social order, and the effects of economic depression (it is no coincidence that Oswald Spengler's Decline of the West appeared at the time), the Gothic was now evoked—not just by Gropius but by a large number of progressive artists and intellectuals——as the integral, ideal age of German culture and intellect, and its cathedrals were venerated as the purest symbolic expression of the Gothic. And so, just as the medieval masons' lodges had raised those cathedrals, the Bauhaus was conceived by Gropius as the starting point and center for a new culture and a better, ideal (socialist) society. That is to say that this backward-looking utopia in the service of social and cultural renewal provided great impetus for the future. This utopia was eminently pedagogical in the sense that Gropius held unwaveringly to the possibility of realizing it by means of an aesthetic education based in the crafts. In a speech at the first exhibition of student works from the Bauhaus, in June 1919, he laid out his hopes and expectations in a fervent, pathosladen language typical of the time:

The German predicament will make us more introspective and profound. Although the material possibilities are diminishing, the intellectual ones have already begun to increase enormously. . . . Conspiracies will form which will want to watch over and artistically shape a secret, anucleus of belief, until from the individual groups a universally great, enduring, spiritual-religious idea will rise again, which finally must find its crystalline expression in a great total work of art. And this great total work of art, this cathedral of the future, will then shine with its abundance of light into the smallest objects of everyday life. .. . We will not live to see the day, but we are, and this I firmly believe, the precursors andfirst instruments of such anew, universal idea. ... We artists therefore need the community of spirit of the entire people as much as we need bread. If the signs are correct, then the first indications of a new unity which will follow the chaos are already to be seen. I am now dreaming of the attempt to gather a small community from the scattered isolation of the individual; if this succeeds, we will have achieved a great deal. ..

In closing I will turn to . . . the crafts. The coming years will show that for us artists the crafts will be our salvation. We will no longer stand by the side of the crafts but be a part of them, since we do have to earn money. For great art, this is a historically inevitable process, a necessity. All great works of art in past ages sprang from absolute mastery of the crafts.124

This effusively written speech—in which the founder of the Bauhaus styles himself not only as the "leader and savior"125 in a time of trouble but as the tool of the world spirit, as the messenger and executor of the new—contains no more concrete hints of Gropius's concept of education than the Bauhaus founding manifesto did, which was also rather vague in this respect (see "An Overview of the Goals and Organizational Structure of the Teachings at the Bauhaus" in this chapter).

Only the daily realities of the school, with all its contradictions and conflicts, and also the ubiquitous turn away from the spirit of utopia and toward addressing practical, everyday tasks—already evident in the early twenties—led Gropius to a gradual fermentation process in which his pedagogical thinking became more precise. This precipitated in the essay "Idee und Aufbau des Staatlichen Bauhauses" (The idea and structure of the Staatliches Bauhaus) of 1923.

Without having to go into the various statutes of the Bauhaus and the changing organizational structure of the teaching program, one detail is especially useful for an understanding of Gropius's pedagogical ideas. The statutes of 1922 state: "Throughout the entire training a practical theory of harmonization is taught on the unified basis of tone, color, and form, with the goal of bringing the physical and psychological characteristics of the individual into equilibrium."126 This "practical theory of harmonization" was being taught in the early twenties by Gertrud Grunow (1870–1944), who had come to the Bauhaus at Itten's suggestion, and her thinking clear-Iy accorded with Itten's as well as Gropius's—despite the many other differences of opinion between these two, which sharply increased after 1922 (on the Gropius-Itten conflict, see "The Founding Phase" in chapter 3 and“Between Art and Design" in chapter 5). Lothar Schreyer called Gertrud Grunow's "theory of harmonization" the "ever-flowing source of all the work at the Bauhaus."127 A look at the 1923 diagram of the Bauhaus teaching plan reveals the comprehensive nature of this theory (fig. 39). Because of its seemingly metaphysical character, which strikes us as rather hermetic today, Grunow's theory is one pedagogical component of the Weimar Bauhaus that has yet to be adequately assessed. We cannot here examine Cornelius Steckner's views, which point to a relation between the tone theory in psychology and Grunow's theory of harmonization, so we will cite just a single passage from Gertrud Grunow's essay "The Creation of Living Form through Color, Form, and Sound," which can be taken as central to her theory: "The supreme law, according to which every order is structured, is called equilibrium."128

Nerdinger has shown the extent to which Gropius's pedagogical thinking was influenced by Grunow's theory of harmonization. Whereas the Bauhaus manifesto of 1919 has a social utopian perspective of considerable range (since ultimately it was no longer concerned simply with the unity of a new style but with the establishment of a new social architecture on a socialist basis), from 1922 to 1923 Gropius concentrated his “theory of unity in a kind of anthropological turn toward the individual in his relation to the world,"129 which amounted to a palpable withdrawal from politics (although, both before and after this turn, the Bauhaus offered the conservatives sufficient reasons for sharp attacks). This tendency to neutralize politics is expressed quite clearly in the following sentences taken from the programmatic essay “Idee und Aufbau des Staatlichen Bauhauses" of 1923.Gropius wrote: “The dominant idea of the Bauhaus is thus the idea of the new unity, the collection of many 'arts,''directions,' and 'phenomena' to an indivisible whole, that is anchored in the human being itself and that only gains meaningandsense through the living life"130 [my emphasis—R.W.]. And he continues: “Human performance depends on the proper equilibrium of the work of all creative organs. It is not enough to train one thing or the other but everythingneeds a through education at the same time. This is the reason behind the nature and range of teaching at the Bauhaus. It embraces both the crafts and scientific areas of artistic creation."131

It is not difficult to see that this idea of an organic equilibrium, which Gropius also allows to enter into the definition of the "new building spirit" ("rhythmic balance," “overcoming of inertia," "balance of opposites"),132 is inspired by Gertrud Grunow. It is also easy to see that Gropius was clearly using Grunow's theory of harmony to address the topic of the "whole human being" that would play a central role in his pedagogy at the Bauhaus and later. This idea of the "homo totus" is directed against the exclusive domination of instrumental reason, and against the advance of a one-sided educational materialism in the service of functional training, against troubling over abilities of sensory perception. Consequently, Gropius calls for the harmonious development of all human abilities—the reconciliation of reason and the senses; the equilibrium of crafts and "head work";133 the unity of mens, anima, and sensus134and privileges on principle the development of a "personal culture of life"135 over early specialization, so that specialization starts as late as possible.136

These sorts of pedagogical maxims—which would have been shared, in essence by other Bauhaus masters as well, such as Itten, Moholy-Nagy, and Schlemmer, and thus can be counted among the basic principles of Bauhaus pedagogy—were, of course, not solely a consequence of Gertrud Grunow's influenceat the Weimar Bauhaus.

Rather, they can be seen within a complex context in the history of education that includes names like Herder, Humboldt, Goethe, and Schiller and would not have been entirely foreign to someone like Gropius, who was educatedin the neohumanist tradition. In his study"Schiller, Hegel, Marx and the Esthetic Ideal of Ancient Greece," Philip J. Kain has shown that this educational ideal of the whole or complete human being had its origin in ancient Greece:

What characterized this idealperiod, it was thought, was wholeness of personality, spontaneity ofculture, andunity with external objectsandinstitutions. The individualGreek possesseda wholeness in the sense ofan extraordinarily complete development ofhis powers and capacities.137

Characterized in this ideal form, it is clear that the idea of the whole or complete human being is more than the result of mere self-cultivation; rather, it requires mediation between individual needs and interests and the needs and interests of society. Conseguently, it really is no contradiction when Gropius evokes in 1923 personal experience and "subjective observation," the "personal idea of form," and the "originative creative power of the individual,"138 on the one hand, and in the same essay (and elsewhere) calls for the students to be committed to the "objective bedrock foundations of creativity"139 and to acquire universal principles of design, on the other. Only the presence of both components, the subjective and the objective, is the prerequisite for wholeness in Gropius's view (and Itten's as well; see chapter 5). This does not by any means define what this dialectical relationship would be concretely. If the subjective aspects clearly dominated in the early, expressionistic phase of the Bauhaus, in 1925 Gropius considered an "emphasis on the individual at all costs"140at least when solving design problems and tackling building projects—no longer tenable. Although he says that “every living form is always the expression of an inner result," he nevertheless insists on "objectivity, i.e., the overcoming of the ego, ... so that the product acquires more than personal validity"141 [my emphasis—R.W.]. It can be demonstrated without difficulty that from the midtwenties on at the Bauhaus—i.e., in parallel with the move to Dessau, and under the dictates of increasing functionalism—the education of the whole human being fell increasingly out of equilibrium. Subjective aspects declined in significance in the teachings, and the ability to solve objective design problems—which, according to Gropius, should range "from the simplest household appliances to the finished dwelling" and be based on "research into the nature of objects"142 became the focus.

## Hannes Meyer

This development, which had already begun under Gropius, was cortinued, with more radical ideology, when Hannes Meyer took over the directorship of the Bauhaus in 1928. One external sign of this process of dissociation, having to do with the organization of the school, was the establishment, discussed above, of so-called free painting classes—-separated from the actually coursework and on the basis of the voluntary participation of interested students——in which it was still possible to cultivate individuality as a designer. Everything else, by contrast, in the form instruction and especially in the areas of architecture and design, was dominated by teaching"objective" solutions to form. Thus the dream of the "homo totus" was all but over, less than a decade after the founding of the Bauhaus. Only Oskar Schlemmer made another attempt, in his ambitious, wide-ranging class “The Human Being" (see “The Human Being as an Object of Study" in chapter 10) in 1928–29, to work toward the totality that had been evoked often enough in Bauhaus programs. But it was in vain, for functionalist thinking and instrumentalist goals increasingly gained the upper hand under Hannes Meyer.

In comparison with Walter Gropius's more complex pedagogical thinking, which built on the maxims of the Werkbund, categories from utopian socialism, ideas from the debate over art school reform, Grunow's theory of harmonization, andneohumanist ideals of education, Hannes Meyer's ideas on questions of education were rather modest. In any case, they were not permeated with reflections on pedagogy in general as Gropius's were; even the educational euphoria borne by the spirit of utopia represented by Gropius during the founding phase of the Bauhaus is not found in Meyer. Admittedly, the historical situation of 1928 provided no opportunity for this. Meyer's pedagogical thoughts and deeds were, however, imbedded in an all-embracing, socially motivated general concept that sought to improve the living conditions of the wider public, especially the working classes.

Hannes Meyer is a tragic figure to the extent that bourgeois critics of the Bauhaus have long tended either to neglect him or make him the victim of a very one-sided history. Walter Gropius himself laid the cornerstone for this sort of treatment with the New York exhibition Bauhaus, 1919-1928 that he initiated after emigrating to the United States in 1938, by choosing the ending date of 1928, the year he left the Bauhaus, which gave the impression, deliberately or not, that Gropius and the Bauhaus were synonymous and there was never a Bauhaus after him. And in his groundbreakingcollection of source material, Hans Maria Wingler never included anything that could have corrected the clichés about Meyer that were current at the time—that is to say, he never tried to refine the common image of Hannes Meyer as a supposedly aggressive, dogmatic person, an “irrational rationalist," nor did he try to correct to view that Meyer's Marxist convictions and his commitment to society and politics made him the "gravedigger" of the Bauhaus.

To counteract such negative judgments, it seems appropriate to show that Hannes Meyers in no way severed the continuity of development at the Bauhaus, as is often claimed, but rather he continued the line that Gropius himself had begun since the midtwenties: a gradual increase in emphasis on the sciences in the design process together with a focus on social needs in the work done at the Bauhaus. Admittedly, he did not do so without different emphases on a number of points related to education at the Bauhaus that in some sense can be seen as a problematic reduction of the original Bauhaus idea or as a betrayal of Gropius's original idea of unity. However, the discussion above should have made clear that, as circumstances changed with time, Gropius himself would have found it difficult to uphold this conception of unity.

An outline, however brief, of several stations from Meyer's life143 may help provide a background against which his pedagogical convictions can be better evaluated. After completing his education as an architect in Basel between 1905 and 1909, Hannes Meyer worked from 1909 to 1912 in two architectural offices in Berlin and was an auditor at the School of Arts and Crafts and at the Landwirtschaftliche Hochschule (School of agriculture) in Berlin. At the time, he was already an advocate of the land reform and free land movement led by Damasch, among whose goals were a more socially just distribution of land and property and the reduction of land speculation. One of the pillars of his later architectural and pedagogical practices is thus already visible here, namely, his engagement on behalf of the socially disadvantaged.

This was followed in 1912-13 with trips through England, where Meyer studied problems of English urban development and especially the garden cities that were then still new. In his 1898 book Tomorrow Ebenezer Howard had advocated the garden city, surrounded by a wide greenbelt to be used for agriculture, as a reform model to counteract rural exodus and urban overpopulation. Like many of his contemporaries, Meyer saw this as a decisive means for overcoming the existential problems of the lower classes that had been provoked by industrialization. From 1916 to

1918 Meyer was active as a department head in the building offices of Krupp's in Essen, where he took part in the planning of the Margarethenhöhe housing colony.

From 1919 on he was involved in the design and realization of the first large-scale building project of his own: the Freidorf housing colony near Basel, a model housing colony for a workers' cooperative, which can be considered one expression of the architect's social engagement. Two reform concepts combined in this project: on the one hand, the idea of the garden city and, on the other, the idea of a cooperative, egalitarian social order. The formal design corresponded to the egalitarian lifestyle of the inhabitants: "The unified form of the apartment buildings, the similarity of form and color of the housing blocks and the harmony of the building elements.... Also an attempt at symbolism? More than that: a struggle for the truth."144 Though other aspects of these buildings were rather traditional, after establishing contacts with Le Corbusier and the Dutch de Stijl group in the midtwenties, Meyer joined the so-called Neues Bauen movement. His 1926 essay "Die neue Welt" (The new world) is a “hymn of praise to modernism"145 and reveals him to be an uncompromising functionalist, for whom architecture is not primarily an aesthetic process but rather a technical one, as the following brief citation shows: "Architecture as a continuation of tradition and as a search for effects has ceased to exist. The individual form and the building body, the color of the material and the structure of the surfaces arise automatically, and this functional view of building . .. leads to pure construction. Pure construction is the characteristic of the new world of forms."146 This somewhat mistaken and misleading view—that form results as a logical consequence of the function to be fulfilled and that technical construction is ultimately the formal equivalent of these functions—was one that Meyer repeated forcefully, although a glance at his competition entries for the Peters School in Basel (1926) and the Palace of Nations in Geneva (1927) as well as the Federal Union School in Bernau near Berlin (1928-30) and the summer homes in Dessau-Törten (1928–30)147 shows that these plans and realized buildings can in no way be reduced to functionally determined forms of construction but reveal a specific "will to design" (to paraphrase Riegl's term "will to art"). Nevertheless, Meyer clung fast to his favorite notion of constructive form, as can be seen from his commentary on his design for the Palace of Nations building: "This building is not beautiful and not ugly. It wants to be evaluated as a constructive invention"148 [emphasis in original]. And even before Meyer was appointed to the Bauhaus, he let Gropius know that the basic direction he would see for instruction in the department of architecture would be “absolutely a functional, collective, constructive" one149 (see “An Overview of the Goals and Organizational Structure of the Teachings at the Bauhaus" in the present chapter).

Gropius had taken the occasion of the opening of the new Bauhaus building in Dessau in December 1926 to ask Hannes Meyer whether he would be interested in taking over the direction of the department of architecture that was going to be established, which, according to the programs of the Bauhaus from the beginning, would be the goal and focus of this new school for design (see “The Phase of Consolidation" in chapter 3). After considerable hesitation, Meyer accepted the position in the spring of 1927, not least because, by his own admission, as a decided “collectivist" he was attracted not only by the prospect of “dealing with the vocational education of students of architecture" but also by "collaboration within a working collective"150—the latter was, one could say, a common idea at the Bauhaus.

Hannes Meyer agreed with Gropius that a “proper education in architectural design [could] only be done in direct association with the practice of architecture itself." Otherwise, as Meyer put it, “nothingwould distinguish this new department of architecture from that of some technical university—i.e., it would be superfluous."151 As has been shown in the previous chapter and the present one, the clear orientation toward practice in the architecture program and—after Meyer took over the directorship in 1928—teaching at the Bauhaus in general in no way represented the rejection of theory; on the contrary, there was a definite increase of theory in the Bauhaus curriculum. For Meyer, it was neither aesthetic considerations nor Gropius's somewhat ominous-sounding “research into essence" that would form the basis for the process of planning and designing, but rather analyses that were as scientifically precise as possible. Consequently, disciplines like psychology, sociology, engineering, and political economy were given greater space on the teaching plan. We should recall the lectures of the Leipzig psychologist Count Karlfried von Dürckheim and the Marxist sociologist Hermann Duncker, as well as the classes by the civil engineer A/car Rude/t, which were intended to contribute to the scientific basis of education at the Bauhaus and in the architecture department in particular.

In all this, Meyer's overarching cultural and pedagogical perspective was “the needs of the people not the needs of luxury." In his view, then, the main task of the Bauhaus was not the reform of bourgeois design culture—which was, in the end, true of Gropius, who was still operating in the Werkbund tradition—but the service of the people, whose mass needs would become “the starting point andgoalof every design activity,"152 though by “the people" Meyer meant the large class of workers andlow-level employees who were dependent on wages. In the workers' movement in particular, in light of increasing class conflicts, Meyer saw a political and cultural alternative to the petit bourgeoisie and its focus on rights; the working class was, in his view——following the model of socialist Russia—the bearer of hope for a new, broad-based democratic culture of the future.

Initially, however, the goal was to improve the actual living circumstances of these people, to which endMeyer established a program at the Bauhaus that aimed to develop low-cost and functional "people's apartments" (Volkswohnungen). With this, the Bauhaus director and his institute were following a trend of the time, as parallel activities in other places make clear. It was certainly no coincidence that, in light of the approaching world economic crisis, the problem of the general housing shortage became the subject of the Second International Congress for Modern Architecture (Congrès international d'architecture moderne, CIAM II), held in Frankfurt am Main in 1929 under the motto "Die Wohnung für das Existenzminimum" (The dwelling for the subsistence minimum). Nor is it a coincidence that new large housing colonies were being built from the midtwenties on—for example, by Bruno Taut in Berlin and under the direction of Ernst May in Frankfurt (“The New Frankfurt")—to address mass poverty. Against this background, Hannes Meyer's concerns for instruction at the Bauhaus were no longer aesthetic experiment but the fulfillment of a social mission:

[not]individuallyproducedfurniture for some snob with “modern" tastes. Rather, standardized furniture for the use of the people, the product of modern mass production, the product of a study of the people's habits, social standardization,physiologicalandpsychological functions, the result of norms in the production process and the most careful economic calculation. .. . Whereas the metal workshop was previously occupied with silver jewelry and formalist lamps, now it iscontractedto sendevery month, or even every week, one coworker to Körting& Mathiesen in Leipzig, Germany's largest manufacturer of lamps, where he inspects the entire production of this manufacturer for export and brings new models of lamps from the workshop to be mass-produced. Inven\~ tive students have caused the static forms of the metal chair to fold, turn, and fan out, in order to achieve greater biological utility. As a result, we designedwork stools for the kitchen, work chairs for laborers, folding chairs for public halls—all in forms suited to mass production.

In the cabinetmaking workshop it was easier to realize this new approach, because that workshop hadalwaysrespectedstandards and norms. There, the challenge was to move from the standard of the bourgeoisie to that of the masses. “Plug furniture" wasdeveloped, in which the individual elements could be easily plugged together. The life of the masses had become more "mobile," the struggle for existence greater, so that it was necessary to move more often. Consequently, weconstructed“lightweight furniture" of the thinnest types of wood, so that it did not pay to transport them, and they could be abandoned as worthless. Relatively large deliveries of furniture, for entire apartment units or even buildings, were produced according to the idea of being able to combine all the individual pieces of furniture by using standard dimensions. This gives the user the ability to arrange (alter) the entire ensemble of furniture to suit any purpose.

Finally, in order to provide for mass production of the models designed in the cabinetmaking workshop, we contracted to cooperate with thecabinetmakers' union in Bremen, and its products have gone on the market as “Bauhaus Furniture."153

Both in the department of architecture and in the closely related workshop for interior furnishings (the result of the combination in 1929 of the metal workshop, the cabinetmaking workshop, and the workshop for mural painting; direction: AlfredArndt), the pedagogical maxim under Hannes Meyer was "Study through practical work."154 This meant that ideally it should not be a question of solving invented design tasks but rather of collaborating in real projects, not about dry exercises in the pedagogical laboratory but about serious examples. ## Ludwig Mies van der Rohe

Meyer's successor, Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, had already been recommended by Gropius in 1928 to be the director of the Bauhaus but at that time he was rejected. Although Mies had been a member of the Novembergruppe (November group)—an organization founded in 1918 that was inclined to social revolution and called for a radical, new cultural beginning—and although the monument for Karl Liebknecht and Rosa Luxemburg he designed was not dedicated until 1926, he was no longer considered a leftist. This was because he was known as a designer of elegant steel tube furniture as well as of villas and country homes for the bourgeoisie (Wolf House, Guben, 1925- 27; Lange House and Esters House, Krefeld, 1927–30; Tugendhat Villa, Brno, 1929–30), the elegant Pavilion of the German Reich at the World's Fair in Barcelona (1929), and a block of apartment houses designed for bourgeois living at the Weißenhofsiedlung in Stuttgart (1927). In appointing Mies to the Bauhaus, "Hesse's strategy was apparent. Mies was a man neither of the right nor the left, nor even of the center; he was an artist. Thus he could be expcted to keep the political dogs at bay without compromising the progressive standards of the school."160

Whereas Hannes Meyer had placed great value on promoting democracy at the Bauhaus, Mies, as an “elitist jail warden,"161 made every effort to seize control of the school in authoritarian fashion. One of his first acts in office as newly appointed director was compulsory removal of students from the enrollment lists in order to readmit them to studies if they passed a test of attitudes in the form of “in-depth personal interviews"162 in which they were prepared to give up all political (meaning leftist) activities—all in the name of keeping the Bauhaus out of the crossfire of the political parties.

In a 1931 brochure—the "brochure with capital letters" (so called because, after years of writing without capital letters on principle, they reappeared here for the first time)—the explicitly apolitical character of the Bauhaus is emphasized: "The administration and teaching staff direct the institute in keeping with the statutes in full awareness of their pedagogical and cultural responsibility. They declare explicitly that the Bauhaus is run completely apolitically and purely from an objective point of view."163 The statutes of 1930 referred to here, and even more so the "addendum to the statutes"·that “contains the precise ’provisions for the students,'"164 represent an enormous “concentration of the director's powers of decision"165 with a corresponding decrease in the participation of the students and a rigorous tightening of disciplinary rules. The Bauhaus curriculum was also tightened up, both in terms of content (see “An Overview of the Goals and Organizational Structure of the Teachings at the Bauhaus" in the present chapter) and in terms of length of study. For example, the maximum length of study of nine semesters (in the architectural department) was reduced to six semesters. This action cannot be explained entirely with reference to the economic difficulties of the time but implies a pedagogical decision, namely, against the "generalized study"166 in Gropius's sense and in favor of specialized study. The preliminary course was no longer a required course. Given adequate prior training, it was possible to be accepted directly into a higher year.167 Last but not least, Meyer's productive workshops were transformed back into teaching workshops,

Unlike Gropius and Hannes Meyer, Mies did not formulate a pedagogical program, much less something like a philosophy of education. He was dismissive of the socially based functionalism of someone like Hannes Meyer and the educational maxims that derived from it, and he even made nobonesabout his“negative judgment of Gropius as an architect."168Mies can, without qualification, be classifiedas a classicist within the modern movement (his borrowings from Schinkel are evident), and for him reflections on questions of aesthetic education in the sense of a unified education were at least marginal if not totally irrelevant. In his view, the educational task of the Bauhaus consisted entirely in solid vocational training of future architects and designers of high quality. That meant that the Bauhaus under his direction became “something approaching a school of architecture,"169 which was expressed in, among other things, the increasing influence of the architect and city planner Ludwig Hilberseimer170 and the interior designer, exhibition designer, and new director of the textile department, Lilly Reich.171 Questions of form and their most aesthetically satisfying solution now stood at the center of teaching at the Bauhaus, as can be seen, for example, in the famous "courtyard house project" (design for a singlefamily dwelling with a walled interior courtyard) that Mies had his students work on as an assignment in extenso again and again. By unanimous agreement of contemporary witnesses, Mies, who was certainly impressive as a personality and enjoyed great respect as an architect, was anything but a good educator. Though the American Bauhaus student Howard Dearstyne remarked in 1931 that one could“learn a great deal” from him ("If he doesn't make good architects of us, he'll at least teach us to judge what good architecture is"),172 in his teaching methods he was "more inclined to advise a student to try again, rather than to help him with detailed critique or encouragement."173 Looking back, the Bauhaus member Hubert Hoffmann came to the following critical assessment:

Mies van der Rohe was the worst educator one could imagine, because—unlike, say, Gropius— he was not interested in what possibilities existed or could be awakened in the student but only whether and to what extent the student was in a position to think "Miesish" and to design within the limits of such a narrow framework! Mies could not express (in words) what he wanted. He would sketch on a sheet, shove it at the student, and say, "Try doing it like this." Mies's method was an extreme case of "academy." An academy built around a single master— that is, precisely the thing the Bauhaus was originally founded to overcome.174

It is only possible to form an image of the Bauhaus with a sufficiently differentiated methodological and didactic perspective by abandoning the level of the global analysis of overarching circumstances, parallel tendencies, and lines of development internal to the institute in order to focus on the level of detaiíed analysis— that is, by providing·depictions of the pedagogicalpractices of individual teachers at the Bauhaus. That will show, more clearly than has been possible so far, that Bauhaus pedagogy was anything but a massive, monolithic structure, a sel.fcontained and coherent system; it took as many forms as there were teachers at the Bauhaus. This in no way refers merely to nuances in the choice of methods and decisions about content but also to differences on points of principle such as conformity to the fundamental goals of the Bauhaus (from the synthesis of aesthetics and society to the education of the new human being for a new society) and the ability and the readiness to apply these things pedagogically.

The goal of the following seven chapters is not to provide an encyclopedic list of the pedagogical activities of all of the teachers at the Bauhaus—the primary masters of form and craft and the secondary teaching staff and guest lecturers—but rather to examine the course offerings that were central to the institute's strategy and that were fundamental for the way the members of the Bauhaus understood themselves and made themselves understood. This refers to the courses that were required of all students, either because they were prerequisites to special training in a workshop (the preliminary course) or because they were offered in parallel to the practical instruction as an instruction in form intended to provide greater depth.
