Designers will often specify surfaces in order to create a particular identity for an interior space. An ‘off the peg’ approach to this process ensures that each design project can be considered through tried and tested procedures. Some of these procedures include ‘sampling’ and also prototyping. Sampling is where fragments of the materials to be used are obtained and experimented with, arranged and presented to a client via a board placed together to give a sense of the projected identity of the new space. In some instances samples will be more rigorously tested for performance in particular conditions, for instance a fl ooring material will need to be tough, and provide resistance to heavy footfall, and so on. In

some instances mock-ups or full-scale prototyping will take place to ensure a full testing of surface materials. Whatever methods the testing of materials takes the form of, the recognition and then the sampling of materials, their exhibition and availability to potential designers, is a very important part of the design process.

How do designers get information about new materials that they can add to these processes? Fairs and exhibitions on construction materials are one method of getting information about new products, their capabilities and also their qualities, as well as sometimes seeing the actual materials deployed in a life-like situation. The BAU exhibition in Munich is one of the largest trade fairs in Europe concentrating exclusively on materials. Armstrong DLW is a producer of linoleum and vinyl fl ooring. In order to make an impact with a series of new products, and in order to ensure that more experimental designers and architects may consider their materials as a new and exciting product, they commissioned Ippolito Fleitz Group – Identity Architects, to make an exhibition that would stand out in the large hall of the trade fair.

The stand not only needed to communicate the company’s new materials, it was also required to encapsulate and express the expertise and experience of the business. The stand was also required to facilitate discussion, both formal and informal, seated and standing, and allow prospective customers a variety of experiences ranging from picking up information to being able to sit and discuss the products in much more depth. It also

needed some storage. Whether a short or long stay on the stand, all of these functions needed to be condensed into a spatial installation that impacted signifi cantly upon the visitors’ design sensibilities.

Using two particular surfaces, a woodgrain effect surface and a range of coloured vinyl, the designers constructed an expressionist, geometrically complex form of a stand that was wrapped in a bold and vibrant complex pattern of surfaces. A reception desk and three long tables were placed upon a fl oor that folded up to become a large wall that ran the length of the platform. Both were fi nished in a colourful geometrically distorted blur of asymmetrical dynamic shapes and colours.

## Mercato

Location Shanghai, China

Remodelled 2012

Designer Neri & Hu Design and Research offi ce

![](images/752c4b9bb8f5fd281b0321960c7d69369293a0a7bd14e68d5021907c8933b1d5.jpg)

The design is really a play on materials. It’s not so much a stylistic thing. – Neri, Architectural Record, September 2013. <sup>35</sup>

Figure 3.66 The ‘originator’ status of the steel-frame host building has infl uenced the development of the design language, a mixture of both new and old, salvaged and reused materials.

As well as importing new and carefully chosen materials into an existing site, designers remodelling existing buildings will also need to respond to the surfaces of what is already there. Sometimes there is no relationship between the existing and the new materials, but in other circumstances the old can have a direct infl uence on the new surfaces chosen. If a building that is to be reused has a particular history, or past, then the extant surfaces may become an expression of the narratives of these times. In other

approaches to reuse, the surfaces of an existing building may be treated in order to act as scenography or a backdrop for the new, as well as illustrating the activities that are to be undertaken within the space. Whichever methods or processes the designers choose, the response to what is already extant is of paramount importance.

Situated on the sixth fl oor of the seven storey 1916 Union Assurance Building, on the Bund in central Shanghai, the Italian restaurant Mercato serves high-end rustic Italian food to a discerning clientele. For one of the fi rst steel frame buildings in Shanghai, the designers were able to open up the 1,000 square metre interior space to house the reception, cocktail bar, pizza

bar, lounge and main dining area by demolishing a series of non-structural internal partition walls. Without enclosing this new large open space, they carefully organised a series of distinct dining experiences. At the centre of the space they designed a central ‘market’ consisting of a vibrant cocktail and pizza bar, both surrounded by a number of stools, inviting guests to sit and enjoy an informal snack or dinner. Considered as the dynamic heart of the interior, and in order to represent the street market aesthetic of the space, both islands were enclosed by a steel frame, with meshed glass and recycled wood canopies. Around the bars, a network of reclaimed steel was intertwined with the services and ductwork of the interior and provided a system for hanging shelving and lighting, completing the ‘rustic’ and informal ‘street market’ aesthetic appearance of the interior space.

Situated outside of these two ‘market’ spaces were the more formal and sedate lounge, dining area and the private dining rooms. The surfaces of the interior space have been exposed in order to contrast with other restaurants in Shanghai, where luxurious and extravagant materials are often considered to be very important and demonstrate the prosperity of the host. In this project the affl uence of the restaurant is considered to be partly found in the treasures of the existing building.

Figure 3.67 The central ‘market’ space with a bar clad in recycled timber and a meshglass and steel shelving and lighting canopy.

Stripping back the strata of fi nishes that had built up after years of renovations, the design of Mercato celebrated the raw beauty of the bare structural elements of the building. This included the decision to commemorate the originator status of the steel frame by revealing the original beams and columns. Against the textured backdrop of the existing brickwork, concrete, plaster and mouldings, new insertions were clearly demarcated and

then placed. In order to extract the maximum impact between the new and the old elements, second-hand and recycled materials, such as replacing the former spaces terrazzo fl ooring with reclaimed wood from the nearby Anhui province, were prized and maintained. Other salvaged materials were chosen for elements such as the banquettes along the edge of the dining area. The private dining rooms were infi lled with panels of varying materials: reclaimed wood, natural steel, antique mirror, metal mesh and chalk board. All were used in order to engender a relaxed and eclectic aesthetic. A band of textured glass along the top edge of each personal dining room afforded some transparency, whilst sliding doors between each room provided maximum fl exibility. This language continued into the corridor between the kitchen and dining area, where a backlit wall of textured glass panels – inspired by old warehouse windows – encouraged interaction between the chef and their patrons.

In contrast to those seated at the counters, diners seated along the edges of the room experienced a very different sort of ambience. Clad in white travertine, the walls act as a temporary departure from the other rich textures and palettes of the space. The focus was simple, while the breathtaking views of the Bund beyond the building were utilised to draw the far reaches of the city, into the dining space itself.

Sushi-teria

Location New York, USA

Remodelled 2012

Designer Form-ula

![](images/e88264434ae1f4c3173f3a3adf8c71f09045af0930863a23f8325d7673acfbf2.jpg)

There are a thousand different possibilities in one material alone. – Peter Zumthor, Atmospheres.<sup>36</sup>

Figure 3.68 The pickled, painted pine wall was fashioned to be redolent of the scales of a fi sh.

Unusual applied textured surfaces can be deployed in order to give identity to a particular place and location. Normally, through touch, it is a surface of an element that receives the most human contact. But image is also a signifi cant aspect to be considered when a distinctive material is to be used in the design of an interior space. The same material can be used in myriad ways, yet depending on how it is used, fi nished, and then deployed, it can send very particularised messages about an interior space.

Sushi-teria is a 93 square metre sushi restaurant located in the atrium space of the Citigroup Center in mid-town Manhattan, New York. In order to process the large amount of hungry customers it serves daily in this busy commercial district of the city, the takeaway restaurant utilises an innovative new ordering technology that combines tablet and smart phone apps with bespoke made-to-order sushi. Diners can order either remotely or in-store and then pick up the food, ensuring that they don’t miss a minute of their hectic workday schedules.

The innovative ordering technology ensures that the interior can accommodate a large group of staff, able to deal with the busy times of the day such as the lunchtime rush, when customers require a particularly quick service. This has impacted signifi cantly upon the design of the interior. Over half of the allocated space for the project was designed to accommodate a 14-person workforce in the state-of-the-art kitchens. With the mechanics of the food preparations, and the staffi ng operation solved, the designers could focus on a surface-led strategy with which to make the interior space

memorable for the small amount of time customers may be in it. The designers derived a spatial strategy and organisation that maximised the remaining space for ordering terminals, allowing customers to make their choices of food in-store, but also, and possibly more importantly, communicated the food, and the brand via a set of surface textures. It was considered important that the space was fi nished and detailed in a way that was seen to function as a cultural signifi er or as a trigger for understanding the freshness of the products and the places they came from.

The designers did this in a number of ways. They created a simple and neutral surface for the fl oor of the restaurant by wrapping the ground level in a hard wearing, resistant, hand tooled black Kadappa stone. They contrasted the subdued and muted elegance of this surface by making the surrounding walls from mirrored polished stainless steel. An illuminated shadow gap, between wall and soffi t, reinforced the change in surface from highly refl ective mirrored wall to a pre-painted aluminium-backed suspended ceiling.

These steel and aluminium panels were perforated with a custom pattern that wrapped both wall and ceiling. The pattern was both an abstract rendition of an atmospheric fog, in its expanse of diminishing refl ection of mirror, as well as being reminiscent of a fi sherman’s woven net, hanging from above the customers’ heads. Sitting atop the charcoal grey fl oor, and framed by the refl ective shimmering walls and ceiling, was the serving counter. Clad in reclaimed poplar timber planks, it was designed to be reminiscent of a small boat, moored amongst the iridescent symbolic steel seas of the interior space.

The nautical sea-faring theme was reemphasised by the wall behind the

counter, a screen that framed the view towards the sushi chef area. Set within white subway tiles, 800 individually cut pieces of clear pine were whitewashed, pickled, and then fi nished with a gloss white and pink paint.

The blocks of timber were cut diagonally and then fi xed to the wall to produce a rippling effect, an impression that, at a glance, could be considered to be analogous to the play of light on a morning sea. As customers waited for their takeaway, a longer study of the surface could reveal that it subtly communicated the sensation of looking at the side of a colourful fi sh, as its scales ripple in the water. The subtlety of this symbolic imagery prompts ironic readings from the customers in front of it, as behind this surface the chefs are busy neatly dicing and slicing and preparing fi sh, making sushi for the hungry workers collecting their takeaway lunch.
