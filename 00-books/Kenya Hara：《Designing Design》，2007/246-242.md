The simple, red circle is an empty vessel. Being empty, it can accommodate the impression of every individual. This is the essence of a symbol. The symbol's function is as large as the meaning that can be attributed to it. At the Olympic Games and other such occasions, the slowly raised flag flies peacefully above the crowd, but produces tremendously powerful centripetal force, for it is nearly overflowing with the multitude of meanings, both positive and negative, attributed to it by the masses of spectators. Another good example is the offering box at Shinto shrines in Japan, into which visitors cast coinsto the local deity in the year's first worship. This ceremony represents an exchange between a deity and a worshipper. One way to stimulate this divine interchange would be for the shrine to give the worshipper a tangible gift, like an amulet or rice cake. But the shrine does no such thing, only quietly places the offering box in front of the main hall. Into the box the worshipper puts his money and his thoughts for the deity, and, believing in divine favor, sets off again. These are Japanese-oriented examples, but upon close inspection, we see that superb brand advertisements reveal effectiveness based on similar principles. The ad's nucleus is the centripetal force that allows for diverse interpretations. People who like it pile into it their varied expectations and wishes. The MUJI advertising concept is the recognition of this fact, developed into a methodology. That is, communication becomes effective only when an advertisement is offered as an empty vessel and viewers freely deposit into it their ideasand wishes.
