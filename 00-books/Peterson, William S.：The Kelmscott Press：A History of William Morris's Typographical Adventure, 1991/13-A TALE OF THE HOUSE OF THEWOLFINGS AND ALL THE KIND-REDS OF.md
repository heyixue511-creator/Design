# A TALE OF THE HOUSE OF THE WOLFINGS AND ALL THE KIND-REDS OF THE MARK WRITTEN IN PROSE AND IN VERSE BY WILLIAM MORRIS. WHILES IN THE EARLY WINTER EVE WE PASS AMID THE GATHERING NIGHT SOME HOMESTEAD THAT WE HAD TO LEAVE YEARS PAST; AND SEE ITS CANDLES BRIGHT SHINE IN THE ROOM BESIDE THE DOOR WHERE WE WERE MERRY YEARS AGONE BUT NOW MUST NEVER ENTER MORE, AS STILL THE DARK ROAD DRIVES US ON. E'EN SO THE WORLD OF MEN MAY TURN AT EVEN OF SOME HURRIED DAY AND SEE THE ANCIENT GLIMMER BURN ACROSS THE WASTE THAT HATH NO WAY ; THEN WITH THAT FAINT LIGHT IN ITS EYES A WHILE I BID IT LINGER NEAR AND NURSE IN WAVERING MEMORIES THE BITTER-SWEET OF DAYS THAT WERE. Figure 22 Morris called The House of the Wolfings, which he carefully designed, ‘a pretty piece of typography for modern times', and wrote the short pocin especially for its visual effect on the title-page. It is a revealing anecdote, for, even at a time when the Kelmscott Press had not yet taken shape, Morris was already displaying his horror of white space and his determination to fill the printed page, if not with ornaments, then at least with text. What is noteworthy about The House of the Wolfings is that its design is less self-consciously archaic than one might expect. The long ‘s' of Basle Roman was not used, and an ‘e' with a conspicuous oblique cross-bar was abandoned by Morris (no doubt reluctantly) in The Roots of the Mountains. The binding, paper (machine-made laid), and treatment of the prelims are entirely conventional. There was also a large-paper edition, which Morris immediately regretted because it played havoc with the proportions of the margins and set the text adrift in a vast sea of white paper. Morris's apprenticeship was rapidly drawing to a close. The moment The House of the Wolfings was off the press, he saw clearly that his attempt to darken the tone of the page had been subverted by the loose word-spacing which characterized nearly all Victorian typography. I am very glad that you like the new book,' he wrote to F. S. Ellis. I quite agree with you about the type; they have managed to knock the guts out of it somehow. Also I am beginning to learn something about the art of type-setting; and I now see what a lot of difference there is between the work of the conceited numskulls of to-day and that of the 15th and 16th century printers merely in the arrangement of the words, I mean the spacing out: it makes all the difference in the beauty of a page of print. If ever I print another book I shall enter into the conflict on this side also.'6o The design of The Roots of the Mountains, though broadly similar to that ofits predecessor, shows one or two more daring touches. (Morris, alas, won only a partial victory in his battle with the Chiswick Press compositors for tighter word-spacing.) The most significant change is that the proportions of the margins are closer to what Morris regarded as desirable; this was achieved in part by discarding headlines in favour ofside-notes. Emery Walker, who was beginning to influence Morris's typographical views, remarked in a lecture delivered in 189o that many of[the] early books had no headlines. The natural way of setting type was in rectangles, and he thought it looked best when composed in that way. A headline often destroyed the effect of unity in the page, and sometimes it was quite unnecessary from the reader's point of view, therefore, in such cases, it might be desirable to leave it out.’61 It was a lesson that Morris learned well: he never used a headline in a book again. <table><tr><td>The Storp of Gunnlaug the Wormstongue and Rafen the SRald. Instead of a large-paper edition, Morris arranged for 25o copies of The Roots of the Mountains to be printed on Whatman paper, which he described as the only good paper at present'.62 (Some of the remaining stock was to be used by Morris in the earliest advertising circulars of the Kelmscott Press.) Such copies were bound in a specially calendared chintz manufactured by Morris and Company (It looks so nice and such fun,’he said63) - an experiment that must have ultimately disappointed Morris, since he did not repeat it. Nevertheless, The Roots of the Mountains represented a measurable improvement upon its predecessor, and Morris was overjoyed: I am so pleased with my book — typography, binding, and must I say it, literary matter — that I am any day to be seen huggling it up, and am become a spectacle to Gods and men because of it.’4 Morris's last endeavour in bookmaking before the Kelmscott Press, The Story of Gunnlaug the Worm-Tongue (dated 1891 in its colophon but apparently completed in November 189o), was more radical in nature; significantly, it was never offered for sale, and most of the sheets remained unbound until after Morris's death.6 It is a deliberate attempt by Morris to approximate the appearance of an incunable, for there are catch-words but no page numbers, the spaces for initials are left blank, and, most extraordinary of all, the book is set in a precise replica of Caxton's fourth type which had been cast by William Howard for the Chiswick Press in about 185o. Ironically, the type was created in order to replace missing pages in Caxton books - the sort ofrestoration' which aroused Morris's fury when it was inflicted upon medieval churches.6 Though intriguing, Gunnlaug was a typographical cul-de-sac, too frankly derivative and antiquarian in style. Morris, when he was true to his own theories of art and craftsmanship, recognized that we must pay homage to the great achievements of the past not by imitating them but by closely studying their essential qualities so that we ourselves can fashion beautiful objects. It is this principle that was to make the Kelmscott Press something more than a quaint anachronism.
