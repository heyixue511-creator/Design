OVERLEAF Charles Jencks, Evolutionary Tree of Post-Modernisms, 2011. ## Life Through Rebirth Has i98os Post-Modernism died, or has Postmodernism, as it is streamlined today, been born again? Both arguments are made, but whatever one feels about PM, the life and death of social constructs is a necessary fiction. We cannot think without them. The fortunes of civilization are contemplated using Edward Gibbon's famous model, The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire (even though it survived its fall). And the crises of modern cities are seen through the lens of Jane Jacobs's The Death and Life of Great American Cities (even though New York has revived its economy twice since she wrote her prophetic words in 1961). In the i92os art world so many movements came and went that the maxim became: "all the isms are wasms," while—in the i98os—so many wasms revived so quickly that I inverted the proposition with equal validity (and dubiety). The temptation of critics, and producers such as Henry Ford, is to consign unlikeable trends to the junk-heap of history. Such death sentences follow on from the historian's metaphor of the birth, maturation, and decline of cultures. Having had a good run of ten years, Post-Modernism itself was declared moribund by critics by 1985. But the organic metaphor has a much longer and funnier history than the spasms of “isms" and "wasms," one that goes back further even than Giorgio Vasari's defining usage in Lives of the Artists (1550). And, following Renaissance architects such as Filarete, Vasari also used the phrase “modern" in a polemically vitalistic way. Vasari's notion was that the "good Modernism of the Classical Revival" ("buona maniera moderna") had thankfully rendered obsolete the bad Gothic manner of the Germans that was contemporary. His Italian readers cheered. But they did have trouble remembering that buona moderna meant ancient, and every so often Vasari had to remind them of the counterintuitive truth, that old dead means new life. Strange. One clear explanation for the life and death oscillation, or its necessary fiction, is revealed by the fashion industry, with its iron proposition: nothing is so old as last year's trend. Thus the global economy thrives on putting the recently fashionable on sale, in order to usher in the next incipient "dead stock." Turnover demands obsolescence. Furthermore, aesthetic fatigue, as observed by nineteenth-century theorists of art, also demands refreshment. The Renaissance was based on this psychological insight-spiritual reinvigoration—recounted in the Gospel of John: "unless one is born again he cannot see the kingdom of God." And as historians have recounted, there were many renascences since AD 8oo, using the same vitalistic metaphor. But how accurate is the art cycle of birth, growth, maturation decay; or of Early, High, Late, Decadent? Skepticism arrived hard in the twentieth century when historians began emphasizing the survivals of dead Gothic architecture. What if every movement, even those defined negatively—Gothic, Mannerism, Baroque, Rococo, Impressionism, Fauvism, and Post-Modernism—carried on? Chaos might rule, and PhDs on Modernism cease. Indeed confusion would exist, as it did for Vasari. ![](images/c91721bc4272289ad66bb6f45280435ec004cba785dfc02abebb8db43a24a0dd.jpg) To put some order into this conceptual tangle, the art and architectural historian Anthony Blunt wrote an analytical piece with an eighteenth-century title, Some Uscs and Misuses of the Terms Baroque and Rococo as Applied to Architecture (i973). He showed that the terms correctly rely on a common overlap of some ten definers. To put it bluntly, any stylistic or cultural category is only statistically cogent, only more or less right-with the implication that no architect is completely Baroque or Rococo. Such a fate for Poštmodernism, of waxing and waning under many aliases, of dying and being reborn, is a statistical affair. It is also the reason I have continued to map these fortunes in several evolutionary trees, showing that architects and designers jump from one school to another, "more or less” pulled by the strange attractors that make up its coherence. ## Complexity l: 1970s and 1980s As Judith Gura rightly points out in her "Introduction," Postmodernism of all persuasions is fundamentally concerned with “inclusiveness," is tied to pluralism as a philosophy, and is “not complete." It is open-ended as a historical movement, just as Modernism, its parent, was termed “an unfinished project" by Jürgen Habermas in 198o (reacting to Post-Modernism). As Gura shows regarding the first period of Post-Modernism, roughly 197o to i985, there are many differences under the capacious umbrella: late Modernists, radical designers, and certain mavericks who do not fit under any parasol. And still there is an identifiable cluster of architects who adopt the hybrid style of double coding, the characteristic mode of pluralism. Rightly, to my mind, she emphasizes the Italian design and architectural culture of the i96os-198os as seminal, and is one of the first writers to bring out its importance. What also emerges from her study is the pluralism of the individuals, something I have always stressed in my evolutionary diagrams. As the philosopher Isaiah Berlin might have said, with his emphasis on incommensurability: all Post-Modernists are pluralists, but not all pluralists are postmodern. A lot of traditionalists also support the institutions of society and the tolerance that guarantee variety. And to make sense of this global bouillabaisse, one has to observe and respect such differences: to distinguo, as the Gothic philosophers demanded. ![](images/792896af37df8e5db32ba87fce64b626547dc4b81b86d39047fe0d54df93c5cd.jpg) ![](images/99b6bcbf3914fce8faa4d0d230af59b0856919418822efa429bbf036309ae3cc.jpg) ![](images/dd6ed59ecf03fce87aa35e5ec006a3bb7cea5608ddcdfa7b0d9132605530d028.jpg) If there is a limit to Gura's view, it is the reduction to architecture and design, understandable with a book on these subjects. But it should be said that Post-Modernism, like Modernism, was unlike all the other short-lived ism-wasms. It sparked mini-movements in all the arts, in all practices and fields, including politics and philosophy. There is postmodern dance and postmodern cheese and a postmodern carnivalesque. Umberto Eco, who has written most eloquently on many aspects of the condition, considers it a transcultural phenomenon. And so it is, like Mannerism or minimalism. ROBERT VENTURI, who called himself a Mannerist-Modernist while decrying the PM label, was one of the many writers in the i96os who defined an early version of complexity theory. His “gentle manifesto," Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture (i966), was the first and most cogent polemic on the subject, although it was published five years after Jane Jacobs used complexity theory in her polemic about the city: and Herbert Simon had already applied the same ideas to other fields. This i96os complexity paradigm pushed systems design, among other rubrics. Complexity thinking became in the i97os the assumption for Post-Modern architecture and its emphases on communicating with a multicultural society by adopting multiple-coding and a host of other varying strategies. Hence the designers Gura treats here worked with a philosophy of difference to support the pluralism. ## Complexity Il and the Rebirth of Postmodernism In the i98os, as the computer emerged to simulate growing and changing complexity, the Santa Fe Institute was set up by three Nobel Prize-winning scientists. Its defining goal was to cast light on the new nonlinear sciences of complexity, which they predicted would be “the approach of the twenty-first century." Chaos theory, which went viral in ı987, and countless fields investigating self-organizing systems, from biology to galactic growth to the swarming of birds—all could be simulated by this new instrument. The computer did for Post-Modernism what the telescope and microscope did for Modernism: illuminate a whole new worldview. In brief, it shifted focus from the simple linear equations of Newton, Smith, Darwin, Marx, and Freud to the nonlinear feedback loops of self-organizing systems. In the i99os, nonlinear, emergent architecture grew in the brains of these machines. FRANK GEHRY's Guggenheim Museum Bilbao was the exemplar of this new paradigm, ushering in Complexity II, the second stage of Postmodernism and its amazing rebirth. Greg Lynn became its creative theorist, Peter Eisenman\* shifted to fractal architecture (among many approaches), and Rem Koolhaas\* and Herzog & de Meuron designed digitized PM ornament and built fragments of the Time City. Schools of architecture churned out thousands of Zahas, the Bilbao Effect turbocharged late capitalism with the iconic building, and TERRY FARRELL, somewhat exaggerating the situation, proclaimed, "today everybody is a Postmodernist." ## Canonic Buildings of Complexity Il By the mid-i99os, Post-Modernism roared back in every way but in name, no doubt aided in its rebirth by the conspiracy of silence. A partial list of recent canonic buildings makes the point. Herzog & de Meuron are at the top, since their work had been so minimalist before that their shift was most striking. Moreover, in 1995 they were at the forefront of the new ornament, having built a few libraries. This shift was followed soon by some contextual counterpoint, and then by big icons designed around multiple metaphors. Their CaixaForum in Madrid (2oo3-8) was the first supreme example of this cryptic Postmodernism, exhibiting all three qualities: contextualism, computer ornament, and the iconic Time City. Its dramatic contrasts of past and present were carried through with consummate double coding. It was as if Venturi were whispering in their ear. They designed further PM gems, which were also icons meant to communicate with both a mass and elite public, taking the linguistic metaphors of the PM movement to a new height. Their Beijing National Stadium (2oo8), aka "the Bird's Nest,' designed with the artist Ai Weiwei, is clearly the iconic building of the decade, and consciously designed as such around the metaphors of a bowl (signifying a communal gathering), the local Chinese cracked-ice patterns (of the structure and ornament), and the eponymous dish of bird's nest soup (the most expensive in the land). What might constitute a shortlist of other Postmodern returns to its earlier self, the period of the 197os? High on the list would be the complex ornament, which could be designed and built relatively cheaply, now by the computer. Whereas Modern production depended on the repetition of identical units, it could be varied endlessly, as were the curves of Gehry's museum at Bilbao. Such digitized ornament reached a convincing constructional complexity in two works by Foreign Office Architects: their John Lewis department store (2o02-4) in Leicester, England, and Ravensbourne College, London (2005-1o). A structural version of such ornament was built by Toyo Ito and Cecil Balmond, the Serpentine Pavilion in London (2oo2). Here the Modernist white square was rotated by an algorithm to produce a delightful complexity of form; one would hardly guess it had so simple an origin. Norman Foster's “Gherkin," aka 3o St. Mary Axe and Swiss Re Tower (2ooo-04), was the supreme iconic skyscraper in the City of London. Other Modernists, leaving the high church of abstraction, also produced stimulating metaphors nearby: the “Cheesegrater" (Richard Rogers) and “The Shard" (Renzo Piano, and so-named by him). Finally, a goal of early Post-Modernism had become mainstream and conventional: communication with a large and varied public. No surprise that the most successful icons were coded with multiple meanings that referred to the context of the building, or the associative metaphors related to its function. Gehry's Disney Hall, Los Angeles (ı989- 2oo4), was derived from acoustic forms and his Fondation Louis Vuitton, Paris (2oio-14), also from a collision of sail-forms; clashing curves in musical antiphony and a Cubist sailboat in the city where Cubism flourished one hundred years earlier. The suitability of such metaphors to the building task might be questioned at first, but for cultural centers that have taken on the role of cathedrals today, appreciated at second glance. Inevitably there was a complex reaction within this new tradition. Such were Peter Eisenman's anti-iconic icons, like those of Peter Zumthor. They ask us to be skeptical about the dominant genre. A grounded form of critical grammar is Eisenman's spartan, and powerful, Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe, Berlin (i998–2005). The kind of multiple meaning I find most convincing is that related to the city context, its past and its transformation—the Time City. This Postmodern genre reached a high stylistic point with David Chipperfield's Neues Museum in Berlin (1994–2oo9), a delicate collage of fragments from the past. EMBT, the Barcelona team now led by Benedetta Tagliabue, has overseen the Santa Caterina Market, Barcelona (1997–2005), a sensuous reconstruction of an old marketplace that pulls together into a multivalent whole the street life and context, the fruit and vegetables. Here the Time City is explicitly enjoyed, ornamented, and symbolized. No shortlist of recent PM buildings is complete without some of provocative creations of Rem Koolhas, which reinvent moves in architectural organization. The Seattle Central Library, the Dutch Embassy in Berlin, and the Casa da Música in Porto all innovate with new versions of the route-building, a transformation of the traditional street. But I find his versions of the Time City more sątisfyingly complex, particularly the Fondazione Prada in Milan (2oo8-15). My personal list of canonic PM, post-2ooo, ends back with Herzog & de Meuron, whose masterwork opened in 2oı7, the Elbphilharmonie in Hamburg. Another piggyback building, like their Madrid Forum, it celebrates the Time City with fragments and transformations of the past, now viewable from an elevated city square i2ıft (37m) above ground. The old urban morphology of brick warehouses is celebrated, as is the Elbe river, the shipping industry, and many other local landmarks. Clearly this is a contextual icon that both transforms and repeats the signs and realities of Hamburg, none more so than the vital place of music in the city's life. Both inside and out it is a masterpiece of musical design, with an iconology that reflects the plural reality of this independent metropolis. Although it arrived on the scene many years after the Post-Modern movement started, it demonstrates the continued relevance of pluralism as the most important, and challenging, art of our time. Such Postmodern returns, carried out by architects of different persuasions, show that the i97os movement survives, and is reborn anew. In that sense, Post-Modernism is what it has always been: the loyal opposition to Modernism, its wayward child and sometime critic. Postmodernists are sometimes Modernists on a holiday, or those rediscovering the pleasures of expression or of relating to the past. The culture of architecture is so mixed that it recalls the duality existing within physics today; as scientists say, on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday we use the classical theories of Newton and company; while on Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday it is weird quantum physics. The fact these two theories are incommensurable and not yet resolvable into a single theory is our truth and condition. ## INTRODUCTION Postmodernism is the most provocative, most controversial, most varied and least understood term in the panoply of twentieth-century design. From its pre-emptively self-designated name (most movements are named only after they have ended, and by historians rather than the practitioners) to its in-your-face designs, it invites confusion. Even in its heyday, a reporter referred to Postmodernism as a term that “everyone seems to use although no one knows exactly what it means."'In part, the confusion lies in the use of the lower-case 'postmodern," describing a period of time that can incorporate the present, and “Postmodern," designating a particular movement or style. Postmodernism lies precipitously balanced between what has been called late Modernism, and Radical Design. Adding to the confusion, many practitioners moved easily from one to the other. Every style has a theory, but Postmodernism is tied to a philosophy as well: a notion of pluralism rooted in the nineteenth century and subsequently applied to areas of culture, including poetry and literature. That history makes its translation into tangible objects both intellectually challenging and somewhat unclear. Postmodernism actually constituted both a movement and a style. As a movement, it rebelled against the sterility of rational Modernism as expressed in the International Style; as a style, it took issue with formalism and functionalism, championing the use of quotidian materials, historical references, and exuberant color and pattern. Furthermore, there were several strains of it, making definition still more elusive. Modernist critics disparaged this mixture as a “fruit salad" of design, while its creators saw it as an inventive amalgam of genres. Postmodernism was defined by attitude as much as aesthetic, as its arresting but deliberately nonconforming objects challenged accepted standards of appropriateness and good design. The use of the term in architecture was initiated by the architect and theorist CHARLES JENCKS, who became its chief apologist and spokesman through many books on the subject, beginning in 1977 with The Language of Post-Modern Architecture. Jencks defined its principles and interpreted its meaning not only for the architecture and design community but for the general population as well, viewing it as a positive force that faced the problems Modernism had failed to solve. Jencks identifies two strains of Postmodernism: adhocism, which focuses on improvisation (and justifies the inclusion of FRANK GEHRY and other maverick designers as postmodernists), and a focus on historicist references and ornament. From its earliest incarnations, postmodern design was a subject of contentious debate. Everyone had an opinion about it; it was simultaneously applauded and derided, embraced and rejected.² In combining sophisticated concepts with pop culture references, it was the first recent style to be both elitist and egalitarian, mixing high and low in the characteristic duality that its progenitors called “double coding," a term suggesting that there is more than one way to look at a building or an object. In allowing unfettered free expression, however, Postmodernism encouraged self-indulgent designs that sometimes descended into revivalism and kitsch; as one publication noted, “Bad taste and uselessness hav[e] the advantage of being within everybody's reach."3 The proliferation and overexposure of such objects—Postmodernism's lesser examples—bear primary responsibility for the style's early decline in the late i98os. ![](images/bf4bdfa742c5f9919bb5704742b0dbf932cfc2c0755d2a46b78cc219f6994da0.jpg) ABOVE Stanley Tigerman, The 'Titanic', 1978, photocollage depicting the demise of Modernism: Mies van der Rohe's Crown Hall (Chicago, 1956) sinking into Lake Michigan. Although the strands of postmodern design coa lesced in the mid-ı97os, its seeds were planted much earlier, and its fruits were harvested beyond its decline. But, given its equivocal nature, defining its precise start and finish dates is like trying to hit the proverbial moving target. Its nineteenth-century origins notwithstand ing, Postmodernism's movement into architecture and design came amid the cataclysmic social and cultural changes of the decades after the Second World War. Most narratives depict the architectural aspect of Postmodernism as primarily a response to Modernism.4 But its intellectual foundations were more complex than mere rebellion against the International Style. Postmodernists believed that architecture must be multivalent, communicating with its users on many levels, and embraced not only historicism and ornament but color and humor too. They sought escape from the limitations placed on architecture by its focus on functionalism, believing Modernism's rational approach was an obstacle to meaningful design.5 The style was set in motion by ROBERT VENTURI, with the house he designed for his mother in 1964, and the 1966 publication of Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture. Favoring forms of decoration that had been sidelined by Modernism, he proclaimed that “less is a bore," distressing committed Miesians and disrupting the modernist canon. Ornament was no longer a crime; hybridity was preferable to homogeneity. Intimations of postmodern style could be seen even earlier—particularly in Italy, where Postmodernism's classicist leanings were as much a return to the country's ancient Roman roots as a rejection of midcentury modernist ideals.
