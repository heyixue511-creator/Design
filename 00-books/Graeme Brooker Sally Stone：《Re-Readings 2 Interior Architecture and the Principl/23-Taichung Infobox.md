# Taichung Infobox

Location Taichung, Taiwan

Remodelled: 2011

Designer Stan Allen Architects

Planes such as walls, fl oors and soffi ts can be seen to link and unite disparate spaces. Through their construction and detailing they can communicate important aspects of a project. Qualities such as permanence or temporality can contribute to their character, and in turn they will convey distinctiveness. Unusual materials may initiate complex and unusual readings of their identities. Even the timescale that it takes to construct them, and the period of time they are expected to remain on site, can provide the impetus to convey important messages regarding how they have come to symbolise the meaning of a particular project and the site in which it is located.

The decommissioned 240-hectare city centre site of Taichung airport is being regenerated in order to become the new Gateway Park City. Stan Allen designed the masterplan, a mixed-use development,

arranged into four distinct neighbourhoods distributed around a long, runway-like, thin park. In order for the general public and other interested parties to feel a part of the development and also be able to monitor the progress on the site, the designers proposed the construction of a pavilion from which to oversee the work, a space that would also contain an exhibition on the site’s history and future use.

One of the key aspirations for the new city plan was the ecological footprint of the project and the long-term sustainability of the place. In order to exemplify these ambitions the designer decided to ensure that the new pavilion summarised and communicated an environmentally friendly message. The 1160 square metre pavilion was sited inside one of the now defunct large aircraft hangars on the edge of the airport. This location provided a huge open space, one that prompted the designers to utilise the full height of the building, creating a freestanding two storey element within the grand hall. The rest of the hangar around the Infobox was designated as temporary exhibition space, allowing visitors to circulate generously around the large open shed space before entering the Infobox.

Bamboo is in abundance in the Far East, and is an economically expedient and practical building material. It is often sustainably grown, and managed and harvested appropriately. Bamboo is stronger per centimetre than steel, and because of this it is a material often found on local building sites where it is used for scaffold. The Taichung Infobox was constructed using a steel frame structure that was then clad in bamboo. Bamboo was chosen not just for its sustainability but also for its strength, where it was utilised in order to bind the steel frame together. As well as its structural capabilities it was also used in order to exemplify identity and

make historical and responsive connections to the visitors to the site through reminding them that this development will not be alien to the city, but was conceived as an extension of the fabric of Taichung. The temporal nature of scaffolding also provided a connection in that it was envisaged that the pavilion will stand for two years before it is then demounted and recycled, with both the steel and the bamboo being reused elsewhere, echoing the changing nature of the cityscape around the visitors to the site.

The walls of the Infobox were made utilising a 75cm-deep three-dimensional grid made from 40,000 pieces of bamboo. The plants were cut and boiled and then dried for one month, giving them durability as well as a tea-coloured appearance. Bamboo construction is highly regulated in the Far East and it must only be worked upon by suitably qualifi ed installers.
