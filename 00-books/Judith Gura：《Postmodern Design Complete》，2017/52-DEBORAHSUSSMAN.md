# DEBORAH SUSSMAN 1931-2014 BROOKLYN-BORN. EAMES-TRAINED DESIGNER OF GRAPHICS WITH LIVELY IMAGES AND CALIFORNIA COLORS. Deborah Sussman enjoyed a more diverse and more extensive education than most of her contemporaries: she took classes at the Art Students League of New York and Black Mountain College, then studied acting and painting at Bard College, before going to the Institute of Design in Chicago for a degree in graphic design;²5 she also has a Doctorate in Humane Letters from Bard. She later interrupted her working life to study for a year at the Hochschule für Gestaltung Ulm, the prestigious art and design school in Germany. Sussman's professional career began as art director in the California offices of Charles and Ray Eames. "Of all designers in the world, I was with the best,"26 she said, and she worked with them for more than a decade, designing exhibitions, films, and furniture showrooms and absorbing Charles's enthusiasm and willingness to break new ground, and Ray's love of ornament and color. She opened her own office in i968, and four years later married Paul Prejza, an architect and urban planner with whom she opened Sussman/ Prejza in i98o. In i984 Sussman used cołor and temporary materials to create brilliant graphics for the Summer Olympics in Los Angeles, transforming the various venues around the city into telegenic attractions—this was the first Olympics to receive live television coverage. Instead of the usual red, white, and blue color scheme, she chose a vibrant palette of hues inspired by the diversity of ethnic influences in Los Angeles, splashing colors like magenta, vermilion, turquoise, hot pink, and chrome yellow on cardboard arches, fabric banners and balloons, and using shipping-tube columns and scaffolding in lieu of permanent structures. As early as ı97o, Sussman designed a shoe-store interior in colors that prefgured the luminous Olympics scheme. She referred to her approach as "supergraphics," saying that it was "not just big, but bigger than the architecture."27 The idea, now widely used in retail, entertainment, and even corporate projects, was radical at the time. Sussman was the first woman to exhibit in the School of Visual Arts' Masters Series awards show, and she received several major awards in graphics arts, in i988 becoming an honorary member of the American Institute of Architects. She founded the Los Angeles chapter of the American Institute of Graphic Arts, and was a board member of the organization. Through a career that included many types of design, for many types of clients, Sussman never lost the characteristics that pervaded every one of her projects: an irrepressible love of color and an abiding sense of fun.
