# KATHERINE MCCOY b.1945 AMERICAN DESIGNER WHO HELPED REINVENT GRAPHIC DESIGN EDUCATION IN A TIME OF SOCIOCULTURAL CHANGE. Katherine McCoy has made her mark on the graphic design profession as much by contributing to advancing education in the field as by creating her own envelope-pushing designs. Born in Decatur, Illinois, McCoy first discovered industrial design during a visit to the Museum of Modern Art in New York with her family. Deciding to pursue that profession, she studied at Michigan State University, graduating in i967 and moving to Chicago to take a job at Unimark, a leading design firm whose partners included committed modernist Massimo Vignelli. There, she was exposed to the strict aesthetic of Swiss typographic design, the prevailing style of the time and a parallel to architecture's International Style. After Unimark, she worked on corporate identity at Chrysler Corporation, then with the Boston firm Omnigraphics, and finally Designers & Partners, a Detroit advertising studio, alongside a variety of other graphic arts practitioners who were seeking to broaden the potential for innovative design in the field. She then, along with her ![](images/b1351e6303e6610808f567d76e27fd927d426becccee630a94af4eb812797486.jpg) husband Michael, also an industrial designer, established her own practice, McCoy & McCoy. In 97ı the McCoys were invited to co-chair the design program at Cranbrook Academy in Bloomfield Hills. Michigan, the school that had become celebrated under the leadership of Finnish architect Eliel Saarinen and had produced graduates including Charles and Ray Eames, Eero Saarinen, Florence Knoll\*, and many others. Katherine was responsible for the graphic design program, and Michael took over industrial design. Upending conventional procedures, they reinvented the curriculum, eliminating exams and encouraging students to work independently and to experiment. There were no classes, grades, or requirements other than a final thesis exhibition. This entirely new, and ultimately very influential, approach to design education was very much in line with the social and cultural climate that was beginning to transform attitudes toward design in every area. Influenced by ROBERT VENTURI and DENISE SCOTT BROWN (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") and Steven lzenour's Learning from Las Vegas and the avant-garde graphics of Dan Friedman\*, ED FELLA, and WOLFGANG WEINGART, McCoy also introduced a new approach to typography, one that included the study of semiotics.²3 She encouraged the use of historical and vernacular type forms that had been rejected by modernists. Her objective was to educate students to make work that was not only theoretical but marketable, encouraging them to break new ground in the profession. The graphics that McCoy designed for Cranbrook used collage and variations in type drawn from avant-garde precedents instead of doctrinaire Modernism; she also produced work for other clients, including a poster for Colorado Native American Heritage (i978) and a Fluxus book (i978). She became active in trade groups, was the first woman to head the Industrial Designers Society of America (1983-85), and was on the advisory board of the American Institute of Graphic Arts (AIGA).
