Happy Family TV Episode Comics, 1969 The introduction of television to Hong Kong caused many manhua fans to lose interest in comics. However, it also created a new genre of comics in redrawn versions of TV shows.

## 143.

Happy Fruit, 1970 Editor: Ng Sheng-gwan

Happy Fruit published short stories and six-panel strips devoted to entertainment and humor. The main attraction was Mr. Careless, a character the editor and major contributor, Ng Sheng-gwan, had created previously. In addition to Ng's work, Lee Wai-chun's 13-Døt appeared in every issue; Miss thirteen Dot is a close friend of Mr. Careless.

## 144-145.

Sweet and Gentle, 1970 Art/Script: Tse Ling-ling

This title was similar to Lee Wai-chun's 13-Dot Cartoon in its emphasis on the main character's appearance. Also like Lee’s work, Sweet and Gentle focused on fashion and featured beautiful clothes. Although the main character is Chinese, she has blond hair.

146–147.

Grandpa and Granddaughter, 1971 Art/Script: Hui Guan-man

The character Grandpa Chow was very similar to Old Master Q, right down to his traditional Chinese clothing and his sense of humor. The stories revolve around his cute granddaughter and were considered appropriate for all ages.

## 148.

Miss Silly, 1971 Art/Script: Hui Guan-man

After 13-Dot proved such a success, Hui tried to capture a share of the female market. 'The overall style of Miss Silly was more influenced by American comics, but the humor was not as funny, nor was the fashion as glamorous.

## 149.

Young Couple, 1971 Art/Script: Hui Guan-man

The story follows Handsome Dak, his wife, and their new baby. It was aimed at the large audience of baby boomers, who were beginning to have children of their own.

## 152.

Father and Ms., 1968

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

This cartoon was first published in the newspaper Ming Pao as a four-panel strip. It originally focused on Father and one of his colleagues, Ms.; Father's adorable son, Ngau-chai, also appeared from time to time. In response to its readers’ preferences, the comic's focus was shifted toward warmhearted stories of father and son.

## 153.

Father and Son, 1971

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

This four-panel strip had no text, just action sequences. Beginning with this series, Wong's comics focused on the father-son relationship and included stories that revealed lessons about life. The covers also emphasized the caring relationship between father and son.

154–156.

Father, Ngau-chai, and Ms., 1972

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

The story of this four-panel strip takes place in the office and at home. When Ngau-chai became more popular, Ms. was eventually phased out.

157–158.

My Boy, 1980

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

My Boy was a collection of approximately one hundred strips that represented Wong Sze-ma's best work from the 1970s. Themes included love and the close relationship between a father and son.

159–160.

Ngau-chai, 1982

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

Ngau-chai was a collection of the comics Wong Szema drew at the peak of his productivity and popularity. The four-panel strips mostly followed the relationship between a father and his adorable, understanding, and energetic son. The series was published until 1983, when Wong Sze-ma died of cancer.

161–167.

Ngau-chai's Collection, 1983

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

After Wong Sze-ma's death, one of Hong Kong's biggest publishers put out this forty-one-volume, 132- strip collection of his work. Each issue was numbered and had a subtitle; issue two, for example, was called “I Want a Star,” issue four was “Father Can Do Anything,” and the final issue was “Good-bye Ngau-chai." The series published most of Wong's Ngau-chai comics, but it did not publish them in chronological order, nor did it indicate when the strips were originally created.

168–171.

Colored Ngau-chai Selections, 1987

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

This series of Ngau-chai reprints was a tribute to Wong Sze-ma by several famous writers, including Yik Shu, A-nung, Lee Ying-hou, and Choi Lan. Each author gave a description of a strip, since the originals were free of any text. The comics themselves were colored for the first time. Although the explanation of each individual strip may have been helpful, the descriptions were later criticized for restricting the reader's imagination and damaging the artist's original meaning. Only five issues were published.

172–174.

Colored Ngau-chai Collection, 1989

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

This series reprinted the Colored Ngau-chai Selections without the descriptive text. The added color, however, was not always well executed or planned. The publisher also marketed a wide variety of Ngau-chai products, including stickers, coloring books, calendars, and pencil sharpeners, and redesigned the format to include quizzes, greeting cards, and other features. These additions were quite popular with its young audience.

175–177.

Gorgeous Susan, 1983

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

Gorgeous Susan was another major work by Wong Szema; this collection was published after his death. The four-panel strip follows the life of Susan, an attractive secretary, through the trials of office politics and her relationships with male colleagues.

178–180.

Debussy, 1983

Art/Script: Wong Sze-ma

These comics were drawn by Wong Sze-ma under the pseudonym Debussy for the newspaper Ming Pao. They had a critical and satirical approach to politics and social situations, and an emphasis on contemporary life in Hong Kong. Wong illustrated the city's crowded living conditions, the incessant construction and noise, and the effects of current events, such as the 1983 depreciation of the Hong Kong dollar.
