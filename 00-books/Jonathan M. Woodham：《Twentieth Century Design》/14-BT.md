Following detailed analysis of the position, Eliot Noyes was brought in to Westinghouse in 196o in order to improve standards. In turn he brought in Paul Rand, who produced a striking logotype, based on electric circuitry and the letter 'W'. Noyes's other consultancies have included Mobil (1964) and Pan-Am (1969-72), whilst Rand has also produced logos for ABC Television and United Parcels Service. The interiors of many large corporations were also endowed with an internationalizing modernist aesthetic, sustained by the furniture of companies such as Herman Miller and Knoll. Although Herman Miller had begun to explore a modernist outlook in the 193os through the enlightened appointmentof Gilbert Rohde as the company's designer, it was not until the appointment of George Nelson as director of design in 1944 that the company began to orient itself towards the architectural, and thus largely corporate, market. Nelson also brought in Charles Eames as a consultant to the company, reinforcing the modernist ethos in furniture production. There was considerable activity in the field of office furniture, most notably the Action Office, which was developed from the late 195os by Nelson and Robert Propst, and marketed in its first version from 1964. Knoll Associates, founded by the German-born Hans Knoll and his wife Florence in New York in 1946, also did much to support the modernist corporate environment through its furniture and furnishings, which could be found in many of the impressive foyers of large corporations [106]. Florence had studied at the Cranbrook Academy of Art and at the Illinois Institute of Technology, both institutions with strong European affiliations. She also knew Mies van der Rohe and worked withGropius and Breuer. Building on such links, the Knolls marketed designs by leading Europeans, many of them being seen as classics' of modern design, especially those by Breuer, Saarinen, and Mies, who granted the company the production rights of his designs of the 192os. Other companies gained considerable recognition for their identity in the same period. In Germany, for example, the Braun company set high standards of a coherent rational design aesthetic with the introduction of Otl Aicher and Hans Gugelot from the pioneering Hochschule für Gestaltung at Ulm alongside the industrial designer, Dieter Rams, in the mid-195os. In 1956 the company's design department was established, coming under Rams's direction in 196o. During his lengthy association with the company a clear identity emerged, characterized by the white, sculptural form of the KM₂ Kitchen Machine designed by G.A. Müller in 1957 [107] or the HLD4 Hairdryer designed by Rams in 197o. Furthermore, those charged with selection policies and the acquisition of material were generally drawn from a narrow social and cultural spectrum and, when they were bold enough to step outside their own national conventions of artistic excellence, oftenfavoured objects which conformed to the modernist canon. The meanings of such clean, abstract forms, stripped of superfluous ornamentation and symbolically compatible with the modern machine-age production technology, have changedalongside the reshapingof culturaland political perspectives both before and after the Second World War, as has been discussed in Chapter 2 (and will be examined further in Chapter 8). As has been argued, modernist products became increasingly associated with notions ofGood Design', opposed both to retrospective indulgence in historicizing ornament in domestic products on the one hand and to the science-fiction-inspired futuristic chrome fantasies and tail-fins of automobiles on the other. Modernist products were also seen as oppositional to notions of built-in obsolescence and ephemerality and thus, particularly when placed in a spotlit, pedestalized museum—rather than a social—context, assumed something of the status ofdesign classic'. ## Modernism and MOMA, New York MOMA, founded in New York in 1929 to encourage and develop the study of the modern arts and the application of such arts to manufacture and public life', did much to establish such an outlook.
