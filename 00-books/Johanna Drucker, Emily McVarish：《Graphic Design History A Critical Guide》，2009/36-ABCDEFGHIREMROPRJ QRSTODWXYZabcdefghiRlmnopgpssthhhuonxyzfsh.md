PETER BEHRENS NEUBABELSBERG Hamburg Amerika Linie 8.24a Peter Behrens, Behrens-Schrift, 1902, and 8.24b Block letter for AEG, 1916. Behrens was interested in reviving a quill-based Gothic script. He wanted to design a typeface whose proportions and stroke weight would express a distinctly German character. While his original calligraphy is more open and delicate than the typeface produced from it by the Klingspor foundry, both reveal a combined influence of roman and Gothic letters, and both are highly legible. The design of the 1916 block letter that became the house typeface for AEG bears a strong resemblance to Edward Johnston and Eric Gill's typefaces from around the same time. Simplified forms, free of reference but still modulated by the human hand, Behrens's block letters mark a distance from earlier Arts and Crafts and Art Nouveau styles and point ahead to modernist ideals of universalism. 8.25 Richard Harlfinger, 40th Secession exhibition poster, 1912. This design's stark pattern subsumes the lines of lettering as solid blocks within its rhythm. Although the letters are hand drawn, the design's iconic strength overwhelms any individual, expressive quality. The composition's solidity and graphic force derive from an elaboration of the letter S, which produces an overall labyrinth pattern. The weight of negative space is expertly calibrated to strike a compact and pulsing balance, but a sense of constriction still dominates the poster. Abstraction and geometry are paramount here. Organic motifs have been relegated to the past. The poster's formal language is removed from referents or imagery, expressing the functional operations of a machine abstracted from the context of human labor. From formal analysis to historical reference, from an embrace of application to defiant rejections of utility, from a celebration of tradition and the work of skilled hands to a nationally competitive industrial orientation, art's dialogue with production took many forms. Each of these stances added to the modern definition of design disciplines and to the profile of graphic design among them. In the following decades of the twentieth century, artistic experimentation and theorization would continue to form and fuel the visual thinking, professional identity, and social practice of graphic design (Fig. 8.25).
