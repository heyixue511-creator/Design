Pietro Krohn, Heron service for Bing & Grøndahl, 1886-1887 ![](images/0687465b4dcbddbf6de97ba90dfebeb0756f1cf08c5cc74edb2d7eeb85aca12a.jpg) Effie Hegermann-Lindencrone, pierced porcelain bowl for Bing & Grøndahl, 1917 # bing & grøndahl (founded 1853 Copenhagen, Denmark) In 1833, Frederik Vilhelm Grøndahl began an as apprentice at the Royal Copenhagen factory and six years later was promoted to the position of designer/sculptor. Eventually he was approached in 1852 by two brothers, Jacob Herman Bing and Meyer Herman Bing. The Bings were established art and paper dealers in Copenhagen and wanted to set up the first privately owned porcelain factory in Denmark. The following year the business was established in Copenhagen under the name Bing & Grøndahl Porcelænsfabrik. Initially, the factory mainly produced large biscuit Neo-Classical porcelain figures and reliefs based on sculptures by the Danish artist Bertel Thorvaldsen (1770-1844), which were retailed through M.H. Bing's various galleries. Around 1880, J.H. Bing's sons Ludvig and Harald joined the factory as business and production managers respectively. Later in 1885, Harald Bing appointed the painter and illustrator Pietro Krohn(1840-1905)as the company's artistic director, which heralded a new progressive direction for Bing & Grøndahl. Three years later, Krohn designed the Heron service, which was remarkable for its austere style and asymmetrical patterning. The design of this service not only reflected the growing interest in Japanese subjects and Orientalism in general, it also predicted the Art Nouveau style. The majority of the products emanating from Bing & Grøndahl, such as those designed by F. A. Hallin, were either in the Beaux Arts style or were strongly influenced by the work of the Royal Copenhagen factory – indeed several artists were employed by both factories. In 1895 Jens F. Willumsen(1863–1958), who like his predecessor oversaw the production of “art” figures as well as more functional wares, succeeded Krohn as artistic director. During the 1920s and 1930s Bing & Grøndahl continued to produce art wares that included stoneware figures by Mogens Bøggild, animal statues by Knud Kyhn(1880-1969), refined classical tableware by, among others, Kay Bojesen, Ebbe Sadolin (b. 1900) and Hans Tegner, and other decorative pieces by Axel Salto, Kai Nielsen and Jean René Gauguin (1881–1961), son of the post-impressionist painter Paul Gauguin. Briefly between 1937 and 1938 Gunnar Nylund was artistic director of the company before returning to Rörstrand. In 1947, Finn Juhl received the Eckersberg Medal for his interior design of the Bing & Grøndahl showroom in Copenhagen, which highlighted the company's commitment to design excellence. During the post-war years, Bing & Grøndahl continued producing its earlier designs (including its well-known blue and white porcelain figures) alongside more contemporary wares such as Gertrud Vasegaard's oriental-style stoneware pieces and her Chinese inspired porcelain dinner and tea service. In 1961, Henning Koppel began designing for the Bing & Grøndahl factory. According to him, the “perfect, pure material of this factory invited attempts to go beyond the limits”, with some of his designs being “based entirely on the characteristic of the porcelain”. Other designs by Koppel, such as his Form 24 service, were translations of his work in silver for Georg Jensen. During this period, the company continued to maintain its reputation for welldesigned tableware as well as figurative sculptures, and kept its long-established studio, that not only employed artists on a full-time basis, but also played host to visiting artists, such as Carl-HarryStålhane. In the late 1960s, Erik Magnussen also designed several important pieces for the factory, including a stoneware teapot(1963), the Form 25 Termo service(1965), and a porcelain teapot (1969) which had an extremely innovative double-walled form that was incised to provide an integrated handle. In contrast to Magnussen's restrained modern style, the company also produced humorous sculptures by Sten Lykke Madsen made of stoneware. Eventually the factory merged with Royal Copenhagen in 1987 and the original Vestergade plant was closed, but the company's prestigious studio was retained. In Denmark, Bing & Grøndahl was historically second only in importance to Royal Copenhagen, and as such it played a major role in establishing the Danish reputation for progressive yet high quality design in porcelain and stoneware. ![](images/a3bac5419acb6592ab51a6b0b391a98aed9d862e6b1d08b13e2fe9dbd3757355.jpg) ![](images/d70d2bfd922f77220c8ff39e173fccd6e57328f4e4168d9fdad8a5df5f6e63da.jpg)
