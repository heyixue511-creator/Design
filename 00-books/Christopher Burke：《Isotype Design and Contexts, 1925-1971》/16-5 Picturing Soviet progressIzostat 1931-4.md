# 5 Picturing Soviet progress Izostat 1931-4

Emma Minns

The time spent in the ussR by Otto Neurath and his colleagues from the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum between 1931 and 1934 is an intriguing episode in the history of the Vienna Method and Isotype. It has attracted considerable interest but has not previously been examined in detail.¹ Indeed some mystery still surrounds the institution that Neurath helped to establish, the All-Union Institute of Pictorial Statistics of Soviet Construction and Economy – Bcecoroçíûié xo3ařctBa; Vsesoiuznyi institut izobrazitel'noi statistiki sovetskogo stroitel'stva i khoziaistva – commonly abbreviated as Izostat (İçoctat).²

Links between Austria and the ussR had been developed since 1925 by an Austrian society for the promotion of intellectual and economic relations with the ussR'.³ Among the proponents of this society were Moritz Schlick, of the Vienna Circle, and Hans Kampffmeyer, Neurath's colleague in the settlers' movement. Neurath became active in the society along with others from the Vienna Circle. The society's point of contact in the ussR was.the All-Union Society for Cultural Relations th Frign ntris ( 3arpaíöeé; Vsesoiuznoe obshchestvo kul'turnoi sviazi s zagranitsei – voks), from which the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum had sometimes obtained research material. Neurath offered the Volkshalle at Vienna's Neues Rathaus to voks for an exhibition planned to mark the tenth anniversary of the October Revolution, but the Vienna city council denied permission for this on political grounds. The Museum did host a smaller exhibition in early 1931 of Soviet children's books.ª

In autumn of 1929, and again in early 193o, Neurath proposed to voks that an exhibition could be made by the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum for travelling in the UssR.⁵ Through such contacts the Soviet embassy in Vienna was made aware of the Museum's work. Marie Neurath remembered visits by Soviet embassy staff, who were interested in the representation of statistics, as everybody is who is proud of progress'. But the Soviet authorities did not commission the Vienna Museum to make pictorial statistics for use in the UssR; instead they decided to invite the Viennese team to help establish a Soviet institute. In preparation for the work of Izostat, the Council of People's Commissars passed a decree on 12 September 1931 stating that all public and cooperative organizations, unions and schools are obliged to apply pictorial statistics according to the method of Dr Neurath'.' In November 1931 the Izostat Institute opened with Neurath as its director, and it was contractually agreed that at least five members of the Vienna Museum staff would always be present in Moscow, principally to instruct the Russians in the application of the Vienna Method.8 Neurath himself agreed to spend sixty days per year in Moscow.º This arrangement continued until the end of 1934, at which point the Izostat Institute was reorganized; it continued to operate without Viennese involvement until 1940.¹º

Five-Year Plans Joseph Stalin attempted to transform the Soviet Union through massive economic and industrial growth. Financial investment was targeted at heavy industry, but the most infamous outcome of this economic strategy was the forced collectivization of agriculture, a process that brought suffering to millions.1² During the time that Vienna Museum staff worked at Izostat, the Institute was primarily engaged in producing charts that depicted the success of the First Five-Year Plan (1928-32) and others that predicted the fulfilment of the Second Five-Year Plan (1933-7). In both of these cases, a notable feature of Izostat charts is the frequent inclusion of estimated data for future years. In Vienna, as a rule, charts were produced only according to empirical data;¹³ in the ussR, visualization of projected targets was an important part of Izostat work. It must be acknowledged that much of the statistical data provided to Izostat staff for transformation into charts was undoubtedly exaggerated, distorted, or falsified.¹⁴ This is a troubling and contentious issue: how, for example, could Neurath and his team actively participate in visualizing statements about the availability of consumer goods that were simply not true?1⁵ (See example overleaf.)

Izostat offered a state-sponsored opportunity to expand the Vienna Method in terms of staff, resources and distribution. At one stage the Izostat staff numbered more than 7o, many more than the staff of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in Vienna. Neurath's hopes for the development of pictorial statistics in the ussR are clearly expressed in an article he wrote for the German-language Soviet newspaper Moskauer Rundschau in June 1932:

The Institute of Pictorial Statistics, newly founded at the end of 1931, which is to make the whole of the Soviet Union aware of the new method from its base in Moscow, is training a team of Soviet specialists, who are to devise pictorial statistics for newspapers, schools, business operations and many other purposes. Special games, teaching aids and other tools of enlightenment will be developed. Exemplary museums and touring exhibitions are planned according to the Vienna Method, and the construction of a large museum in Moscow is already being considered, along with the establishment of an Institute building with all the necessary test facilities. 20

Marie Neurath recalled that plans for the museum went as far as to commission a design from Josef Frank, and that a central Moscow location was earmarked, opposite the Bolshoi Theatre on what is now Teatral’naia Square.²¹

Another factor that may have contributed to Neurath's decision to work in Moscow was the friendship Neurath and Marie Reidemeister had developed with the Russian artist Lazar (El) Lissitzky and his wife, art historian Sophie Lissitzky-Küppers. The two couples first met in spring 1928 at the International Press Exhibition Pressa' in Cologne. (Lissitzky had overseen the design of the Soviet Pavilion there and the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum contributed to the Austrian section; see p. 94 above.) They all met again in Lissitzky's 'dacha' half an hour by train from Moscow; 'I spent any free day there, when I was in Moscow without Otto', recalled Marie Neurath.2²² Yet this was more than a personal relationship; Sophie Lissitzky-Küppers later wrote that her husband was fascinated by the Vienna Method: For Lissitzky this was a discovery which seemed to him to have importance for Moscow. Dr Neurath was very interested in the Soviet Union and there arose a relationship based on mutual understanding, which later led to the arrival in Moscow of the Neurath group.'²³ This 'mutual understanding was perhaps not the primary reason for the establishment of Izostat, but such enthusiasm for the Vienna Method from a respected figure in the world of Russian art and design (although not always officially in favour) must have encouraged Neurath and his team.

The series demonstrates that Ivanitskii and his fellow artists were engaging with the work of the Vienna Museum sometime before Neurath arrived in Moscow, even if they did not adhere strictly to Vienna Method principles at that time. Indeed, the postcards employ a variety of graphic techniques to present data, some more successfully than others. For example, Ivanitskii created the graphic device of charts constructed by the means of an illustrated strip (like a filmstrip)’. This, he argued, combined the Vienna Method with the more familiar means of data presentation – comparison by length 'like with ordinary bar charts.'³7 According to Ivanitskii, the principal benefit of the filmstrip method was that each frame of film could be divided into ten parts (marked by sprocket holes on the edge) and therefore could present data with greater accuracy than Vienna Method pictograms.

Modifications to the Vienna Method, in particular the addition of backgrounds and illustrations, were seen by Ivanitskii and the Soviet authorities as crucially important for emphasizing the success of socialism and comparing it favourably to pre-revolutionary Russia and conditions in the capitalist West: we tried by the means of pictorial backgrounds to trigger certain associations, to interest the viewer, to acquaint them with the subject without prejudice to the actual chart.' The examples reproduced here show that the background illustrations did impinge on the presentation of statistical data in some cases, and, in one notable passage of Ivanitskii's introductory text for the postcards, he acknowledged such faults. He stressed their experimental nature, arguing that both the Vienna Method and the filmstrip method were new to the ussR, and therefore a number of errors and defects in the series is quite natural'. The poor quality of printing was partly blamed: 'machinery was not up to scratch and therefore the background of the cards sometimes conflicts with the charts.' Ivanitskii and Neurath clearly shared an enthusiasm for disseminating the Vienna Method of Pictorial Statistics throughout the Soviet Union. Ivanitskii concluded his book by outlining a plan for the cheap production of pictorial statistics by non-specialists:

It is essential to simplify and standardize the manufacture of charts, so that they will take a minimal amount of time, so that they can be made not only by an artist, but by every worker, and so that the cost of a chart will not be ten roubles, but a few dozen kopecks. ... Anyone, who needs to construct a chart, will be able to stick ready-made figures, like postage stamps, onto a ready-made standard grid and will get a nice, cheap, attractive chart. In the case where there are no ready-made figures available, it will be possible to order a rubber stamp of one figure and print this as many times as required.⁴º

It seems that such a mail-order pictogram scheme was never realized during the time Neurath and his team were in Moscow. Neurath was not in favour of fully devolving the design of charts to the public; instead he wanted the process to be controlled by centralized offices, like Izostat.

Between 1932 and 1934 Izostat produced numerous books and albums published in Russian, English and other European languages, as well as contributing charts to publications for which it received no credit or acknowledgement.4¹ Apart from Neurath's own references to the activities of Izostat, the work of the Institute appears to have attracted little contemporary comment. In May 1934 the Soviet magazine Prozhektor (Spotlight) published an article that provided a brief outline of Izostat work.“² In addition to charts in publications, the article highlighted its more ephemeral outputs:

Recently the Institute has initiated an extensive programme of exhibiting its work. Among the most important displays made was the exhibition for the Seventeenth Party Congress, the street decorations for May Day, an exhibition in the Central Park of Culture and Recreation to commemorate the eleventh anniversary of the ussR constitution, project layout charts for the construction of the Moscow metro, a series of charts displaying the saving of the crew of the Cheliuskin,4³ flights into the stratosphere, the aeroplane Maksim Gor'kii', 'Bobriki', 'Dneproges' and others. In the exhibition of its work the Institute has penetrated many centres of the Union, including Alma-Ata and Ashkhabad.

The article was accompanied by images of Izostat charts in public spaces: one photograph showed a chart as part of a shop window display; another chart, showing the transformation of the Soviet economic base from agricultural to industrial, was apparently painted on the side of a Moscow apartment building (see overleaf).

The Russian version consists of three rows of naturalistic peasant dwellings placed on a background of small fields; the rows are dated 1928, 1931, and 1932. The process of collectivization over these years is shown through a number of visual devices: in 1931 the fields lose their piecemeal appearance, a grain silo is under construction, tractors appear on the horizon, and the wooden huts are transformed into standardized buildings bathed in a red that also colours the earth. In 1932 the emphasis on modernization is even more pronounced: the silo is now complete, and a train carries the processed crop away; more tractors are on the horizon, and the traditional peasant dwellings have almost all been replaced by new buildings. This chart is in sharp contrast to its equivalent in the English edition, The struggle for five years in four. Here the peasant huts are presented as pictograms, rather than realistic drawings, on a buff background with white lines to indicate individual fields. The spread of collectivization is once again shown through the replacement of black with red, but there are no longer any additional pictures. In both charts one hut represents five per cent of the total number of peasant farms.
