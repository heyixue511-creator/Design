DESIGN AND NATIONAL IDENTITY The use of design to express a country's identity is part of a movement now known as Romantic Nationalism (or the National Romantic movement). At the turn of the last century, many politicians, designers, craftspeople, and manufacturers sought to establish or reinforce their country's identity by idealizing its past. Romantic Nationalism was most prevalent in countries that were trying to free themselves from foreign domination. Norway was seeking independence from Sweden; Finland from Russia; Ireland from Great Britain; and Hungary from the Austro-Hungarian Empire. The material culture of Romantic Nationalism drew from the British Arts and Crafts, especially the movement's advocacy of indigenous design, traditional ways of making objects, and the use of local materials. Peasant crafts and regional buildings were considered essential to each country's heritage: their preservation and renewal supported efforts to foster and legitimize a strong national identity. This idealization of the preindustrial past did not originate with the Arts and Crafts movement. It is prevalent in the Enlightenment of the eighteenth century, with its worship of life in a state of nature. and the Romanticism of the same period, with its search for "authentic"experience.' The ideology of Romantic Nationalism, however, as it applies to design at the turn of the century, was largely inspired by the British Arts and Crafts movement, which imbued vernacular production with a moral imperative. The notion that native crafts wore the halo of time-honored practice was not necessarily backed by reality. Historian Eric Hobsbawm coined the phrase "the invention of tradition"to describe how, at the end of the nineteenth century and into the twentieth. the past had been manipulated, updated, selected, or otherwise reconfigured in order to serve the purpose of national identity.8 National characteristics were often encoded into design to create a shared heritage of positive attributes. For example, after southern Ireland became an independent country in 1922, metalsmith Mia Cranwill was commissioned to make a casket containing a manuscript with the names of all members of the first senate (1.9). The casket had to embody Irishness: as historian Nicola Gordon Bowe points out, it was "in the form of an ancient cumdac, a reliquary or bell case presented by pious early Irish princes to holy men." In the same work, however, other Celtic imagery was freely adapted to represent the hopes of the Irish Free State as well as its anxieties. Elisabet Stavenow-Hidemark's essay discusses how Scandinavian countries, especially Norway, revived Viking myths and symbols in architecture, silver, furniture, and book design to achieve similar aims. ART AND LIFE The concept of completely integrating art and life was fundamental to Arts and Crafts beliefs. This ideal was constantly echoed in the movement's organizations and magazines, from the English Art Workers' Guild's motto, "Art is Unity," to American Will Price's subtitle for his journal The Artsman, "The Art that is Life." Life itself was to be a Gesamtkunstwerk—a total work of art. In the context of architecture and the decorative arts, the building, its furnishings, and its setting were intended to form an environmental whole (1.10). To truly fulfill this goal, art must be widely available, not only for the elite. The American essay in this volume especially focuses on "democratic design." The dream of unity was most fully—albeit briefly—realized in the establishment of utopian art colonies. Four are studied in this book and exhibition, each providing a paradigm of Arts and Crafts ideals. As in other arenas, Britain, especially C. R. Ashbee's Guild of Handicraft, provided the model for craft communities. Established in 1888 in the slums of London's East End, the guild abandoned urban life in 1902 for the bucolic peace of the Cotswolds in order to further its goal of creating an alternative to factory production and integrating craft production with daily life. In both locations, Ashbee designed luxury goods (1.11) as well as those for a broader market. The artists' colony at Darmstadt represents aristocratic patronage and an ambitious commercial agenda combined with the German concept of Lebensreform (life reform). Darmstadt artists worked in all media, creating a Gesamtkunstwerk well documented in product design and the buildings of the community that still remain. People absorb these voices and then alter them to address their own deepest fears and desires. Some looked to the Arts and Crafts movement to provide a small, still center of handmade work; some for a way to revolutionize the production and consumption of manufactured goods; and others to give material form to national aspirations. The legacy of the Arts and Crafts movement can never be definitive, since wherever it developed, people invented the movement that they needed. The varied ways William Morris has been perceived exemplifies this phenomenon. As Amy Ogata points out in her essay, for the Belgian designer Henry Van de Velde—champion of individuality in design—Morris represented opposition to the machine.'° For Chicago socialist Oscar Lovell Triggs, he was the hero of the "New Industrialism."Henry Watson Kent, a founder of the American Wing at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, drew from his "long-time beau ideal, William Morris" to support the conviction that museums must function as practical places for the improvement of design." Morris's beliefs continually evolved throughout his life, so it is understandable that his acolytes interpreted him selectively. The Arts and Crafts movement could claim the adherence of progressives and conservatives, proponents of the handmade and of industrial production, as well as those who believed that "the service of modern art" must include the revival of traditional crafts.'²The movement had to be both modern and antimodern, since as Marshall Berman observed, "It has been impossible to grasp and embrace the modern world's potentialities without loathing and fighting against some of its most palpable realities."³These seeming contradictions were united by the Arts and Crafts movement's quest for meaning in a time of radical change, by its need to retain a sense of the individual in an increasingly mass society, and by its attempt to make mechanization and urbanization serve people rather than enslave them.
