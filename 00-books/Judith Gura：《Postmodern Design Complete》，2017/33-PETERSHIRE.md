# PETER SHIRE b.1947 CALIFORNIAN CERAMICIST WHO BECAME THE ONLY NON-ARCHITECT CONTRIBUTOR TO MEMPHIS. It is a big leap from teapots to discos, but Peter Shire (also see under “Living with Postmodern Design") has made more than one unlikely crossover move in a career that has included jewelry design, furniture, exhibitions, and fine art—even though his primary medium has been ceramics. The designer's laid-back, California approach is reflected in his use of lively color and irregular, playful forms. The only non-architect invited to design for MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design"), and the only one with a background in crafts, Shire was postmodern before the term was applied to design. Shire was born in Los Angeles and trained at that city's Chouinard Art Institute. He began to work in ceramics, inspired by the examples of forerunners like Peter Voulkos and Ken Price, who helped to erase the barriers between craft and art. But instead of shaping amorphous forms, Shire chose a pedestrian object, the teapot, as a vehicle for his creative expression. Forming his pots by hand with the slab method, rather than working on a wheel, he reinvented the idea of what a teapot should be. Shire became known for idiosyncratic pieces whose clashing colors and angular shapes turned utilitarian objects into collectible art. He continued to produce a variety of teapots in an aesthetic that, despite making occasional references to classical styles, is impossible to label and very much his own. Invited to design jewelry for ACME (see under “Edited Design"), Shire jumped at the opportunity to diversify, and created a group of enameled pins and earrings to form the firm's debut collection. The colorful, collage-like pieces were like nothing else on the market, and their success led to other designs for the company. When ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") saw Shire's work and identified in it a shared sensibility, Shire became the first non-member invited to contribute to the Memphis collection. His first piece was the Brazil table (i98t), a composition of triangles half-cylinders, and circles. It was joined by Bel Air (1982), a high-impact colored, asymmetrical armchair that starred in a number of magazine features about postmodern design. The Big Sur sofa (i986) was another success. As Shire expanded his range to design glassware, his witty approach transitioned into rounded forms, but with the same combinations of intense. sometimes clashing colors. The angular silhouettes of intersecting planes and swaths of saturated color put Shire's furniture designs into the same classification of object-cum-artwork. Although he credits the influence of such predecessors as Bauhaus, Futurism, Art Nouveau, and Art Deco, Shire's tables, seating pieces, lamps, and cabinets ![](images/a1fab58e036a62ee77d16ef87655dc6bb4c0f145010c49eefe6421eccd6ccf0c.jpg) ![](images/8f87cbcddb477e7617fa9f17a4ca2a2776949cf13a3dff235a4096a9e6ac0259.jpg) reflect no apparent style so much as a refusal to commit to any single aesthetic—barring that of playful, free expression. Nevertheless, it is almost impossible not to recognize one of his designs. Expressing himself in other directions, Shire has designed store interiors and worked on the aforementioned disco, as well as displays for the 1984 Olympic Games in Los Angeles. His artwork includes works on paper and ceramic-glazed paintings on slabs of clay that either become individual pieces or are assembled into murals; the latter have been commissioned for private and public locations in and around Los Angeles. Teapots, however, have remained in Shire's design vocabulary, as documented in the book Tempest in α Teapot (i991). His work has been featured in many exhibitions internationally, as well as in catalogs and books, and as curators have taken notice, his designs have been collected by major museums. Neo-Baroque is a term that has been applied to designs that recall the most ornate and pretentious periods of the past, not always in a flattering context. But Bořek Šípek's (also see under “Living with Postmodern Design") work adapts that style's usual ornateness with a fine sense of proportion and a refreshing note of whimsy. His subtle wit, seen in the titles of his objects as well as their appearance, recalls that of the leading postmodernists, though the sumptuous aesthetic is very much his own. Šípek was born in Prague, Czechoslovakia, and his training helps to explain the diversity of interests shown in his work. He studied furniture design at the Prague School for the Applied Arts, graduating in i968 and moving to Germany to study architecture in Hamburg at the Hochschule für bildende Künste. Balancing practical skills with more esoteric interests, he took up the study of philosophy and aesthetics at Stuttgart University. After working for two years as a scientific assistant at the University of Hannover's Institute of Industrial Design, he then finished his architectural schooling in i979 at the Technische Universiteit Delft, completing what may be the broadest educational background of any designer of his generation. Nevertheless, Šípek considered himself specifically an architect rather than a designer, explaining that “Design consists of packing things into form," which he does not do.27 Continuing his peripatetic movements, Šípek moved to Amsterdam in i973, where he lived for many years, establishing an architecture and design practice and later forming Alterego in partnership with industrial designer David Palterer. Over the next decade, as design became a prestigious and high-profile profession, Šípek became a celebrity in his adopted country, appearing in the media so often that a local publication headlined an article, "Enough Borek Šípek: Why Him?"28 His popularity was unsurprising: his designs were sumptuous, curvilinear forms, often in surprising mixes of materials and spiced with dashes of traditional artisanry. Bambi (i983), a slender chair of tubular steel, was adorned with brass mounts and a silk back, and the attention-grabbing Prosim Sni chaise longue (i987) paired the classic form with an abstract-shaped back that seemed almost an afterthought. Šípek's glassware, which he considered a diversion from his other design pursuits, mixed frivolous and functional in silhouettes that were often punctuated with blobs or projections of contrasting color. Enormously popular, it has been credited with helping to revive a moribund Czech glass industry. Šípek's prolific output also included furniture for Driade\*, accessories for CLETO MUNARI (see under “Edited Design") and ALESSI (see under “Edited Design"), and designs for Sawaya & Moroni\*. ![](images/e906b2e18034bc63f964ebc0f059b57714c01ab9de84aef23e7a949ccb01f20d.jpg) ![](images/e79d204de082123e6ecb666469cc49b3f908f7fbfc36f4d10a0e7e6f90d8e0f8.jpg) Šípek taught design theory at the University of Essen and architecture at the Prague Academy of Applied Arts, and in ı993 began to accept commissions for architectural projects in the Netherlands and the Czech Republic that included private residences, public spaces, and retail facilities. His buildings, like the award-winning Glass House in Hamburg (i983) and his own residence, are as appealingly whimsical as the objects he designed. His interiors projects included the redesign of state rooms in Prague Castle for then Czech president Václav Havel. Summing up almost four decades of work, Šípek's book Life in Motion (2oo8) chronicles his work and his inspirations, and includes fanciful illustrations that reveal his considerable drafting skills. One of the founding members of MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design"), George Sowden was not yet thirty years old when he joined forces with a group of young Italians, one Japanese, and two Frenchwomen (one of whom he married) to invigorate the design world with a new vocabulary of convention-flouting color and form. Of the group, Sowden was perhaps the most conservative, as his pre- and post-Memphis work suggests, but his designs for the collaborative's collections reveal a youthful exuberance and subtle sense of humor that inform even his most practical work. Over several decades, he has practiced in two parallel but altogether different areas, designing both electronics and art objects with equal skill and success. Sowden was born in Leeds and studied architecture at Gloucestershire College of Art, graduating in i968. In ı97ı he was hired by Olivetti and moved to Milan, and has not left Italy. His firm, Sowden Design, was founded in ı98ı, and in his preferred field of industrial design he has designed hundreds of objects, from ![](images/bafc130a29dc5880a075b85c816e16f28cec3f5acd39ce33d5698d81abb78e05.jpg) computers and electronics to houseware, appliances, cookware, lighting, glassware, and even telephones for a variety of prominent firms. Having worked with Sottsass Associati on Olivetti projects, Sowden was recruited to be part of Memphis. For him, Postmodernism was an invigorating change of pace from more practical pursuits, and the objects he devised for Memphis are almost polar opposites of his industrial design work. His contributions include the leggy D'Antibes cabinet (i98ι) and the perky Saragoza chair (ı984), both of which are sleek forms with historical overtones, ![](images/4a13eab748b1ebe46795c1dd5ac0cbb59ab8b5e94f3c9bf33c39835f087a7581.jpg) ![](images/db40d73fcd466f1ce339f1b98db52dae5a7b5722abb1f75d5987286b114cc8fa.jpg) ![](images/8bb454c57764ae7451480485b9fb703fdeefeb75cd51180478f402e3887693b9.jpg) sparked with lively color. His engineering skills are evident in the imaginative clocks of the Objects for the Electronic Age collection that he and his wife and frequent collaborator NATHALIE DU PASQUIER exhibited in i983. Sowden also designed textiles for Memphis whose simple, geometric forms contrast with the exuberant patterns of Du Pasquier, and he later played with pattern in wallpapers for Rasch. Like many of his Italian colleagues, Sowden contributed several pieces to ALESSI's (see under “Edited Design") collections. In his post-Memphis career, Sowden's work has become more focused on industrial design, with work for firms such as Bodum and Swatch. These have primarily been practical household goods that utilize his engineering skills—objects with refined silhouettes and only occasional touches of color. With the revival of interest in Postmodernism, however, Sowden was persuaded to reprise his earlier frivolities in a collection of Swiss-made home textiles, designed with Du Pasquier in 2015.
