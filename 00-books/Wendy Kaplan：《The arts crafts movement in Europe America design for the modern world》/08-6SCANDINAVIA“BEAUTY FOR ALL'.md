# 6 SCANDINAVIA: “BEAUTY FOR ALL' ![](images/11d2acf782f96d7957030e12be4c77eae1c812da2c170b1b5e0381daad0e97ba.jpg) he Scandinavian countries of Sweden, Norway, Denmark, and Finland form not only a geographic entity but also a cultural one. This cultural connection was as true during the period when the influence of the British Arts and Crafts movement was greatest—the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries—as it is today. Swedish, Norwegian, and Danish, though different, are mutually understandable, and at this time educated Finns spoke and wrote Swedish despite nationalist efforts to promote Finnish, which belongs to an unrelated linguistic group. Consequently, these four Nordic countries also shared language as a common denominator. Yet, separate nationalist movements existed throughout Scandinavia at the turn of the twentieth century. Norway was striving for political independence; for centuries it had been in the shadow of Denmark. As a result of the Napoleonic Wars, the country became the subordinate partner in a union with Sweden in 1814. Only in 1905, after decades of strenuous effort, did it succeed in achieving self-rule. Finland had also been part of Sweden, but after Sweden's disastrous war with Russia (1808-9), Finland was annexed by its larger neighbor. Russia initially allowed Finland wideranging autonomy and its own constitution. The Pan-Slavic movement of the 189Os, however, motivated Czar Nicholas Il to limit the Finns'freedoms. This, in turn, spurred the drive for Finnish independence, finally declared following the Bolshevik success in the revolution of 1917. The search for national identity that characterized all four countries was greatest and most self-conscious in Norway and Finland, since each was engaged in a struggle to free itself from the political control of another. While the ceding of Finland, and Norway's increasing restiveness, had also triggered a period of self-examination in Sweden, the country had always remained independent. Denmark, too, had suffered losses—first its dominance over Norway, and then its rule over two provinces after bloody wars with Germany in 1848 and 1864. As with Sweden, the need to establish an independent cultural identity was not quite as compelling, but it was still a critically important impetus for design reform. The new nationalism was manifested in each country by a growing interest in its past. This was first seen in literature in the early decades of the nineteenth century: ancient legends and sagas were rediscovered, and, particularly in Finland, the native language was studied. Later in the century, this passion for time-honored traditions became evident in material culture— through the revival of folk crafts and vernacular architecture. The transformation of folk arts and crafts and other indigenous practices to express a modern national identity—now known as Romantic Nationalism or the National Romantic movement—was found in all four countries. The Scandinavians' deep awareness of and penchant for the British Arts and Crafts movement went hand in hand with this approach to design reform, and each of the four countries adopted the Arts and Crafts movement's moral aesthetics, adherence to regionalism, and glorification of the handmade. However, other profound influences came first. By the 1870s, following the ideas of German architect and theorist Gottfried Semper, many manufacturers and designers in Sweden, Norway, and Finland were eager to improve the applied arts. They produced ornamentation rife with national symbolism (and marketed as such) to embellish industrial design. Yet, the followers of Semper were mainly promoters of the Neo-Renaissance. It was their approach—garnering inspiration from their own artistic past and emphasizing the value of the applied arts—that was progressive, rather than the styles that resulted from it. Exposure to Japanese art was the most significant factor in the rejection of historicism. Japanese applied and graphic art had already generated great interest at the International Exhibition in London (1862), but Scandinavian artists had their most significant encounters with Japan during their stays in Paris in the 1880s. The Swedish painter Carl Larsson exclaimed in a letter, "My home country as an artist is Japan." Siegfried Bing's shop, L'Art Nouveau, was known and admired by Scandinavians; also, Le Japon Artistique, a periodical Bing published, reached Norway and Sweden. In 1885 the Danish art historian Karl Madsen published the first book on Japanese art in a Scandinavian language. Contact with Japanese art encouraged direct inspiration from nature and bold asymmetrical compositions. Knowledge of William Morris and the Arts and Crafts movement did not come to Scandinavia until the late 1880s and did not really make a significant impact until the 1890s. Industrialism had developed late and traditional farming society still continued, making a return to natural materials and craftsmanship less pressing as a statement against mechanization and urbanization. The movement reached Scandinavia mainly via Germany, since contacts with that country were much closer than those with England. As German was the best-known foreign language, one can assume that the new attitude toward the fine and applied arts was disseminated through German periodicals. ![](images/dc728ab934edd66092fac6158a8e17404e5fcc9b15288e2773d8c23bcc4e4e36.jpg) However, direct contacts were also important, and the teaching methods used in British schools of design and applied arts were well known. In 1886 the Norwegian architect, H. M. Schirmer, who lectured at the Statens Håndverks- og Kunstindustriskole (National School for Handicrafts) in Christiania (now Oslo), made a long study tour of Britain and reported on the state of art teaching there. Through this and other accounts, Norway became familiar with the modern English attitude toward the applied arts by the beginning of the 1890s.2 In Sweden the journalist Gustaf Steffen wrote not only about John Ruskin and William Morris and the latter's workshops at Merton Abbey, but also about the Aesthetic movement and Liberty & Co.3 Since 1887 he had been the special correspondent of the Göteborgs Handels- och Sjöfartstidning (Göteborg Newspaper for Trade and Shipping), and he was interested in social as well as aesthetic questions. He had met Morris at his home, although the article describing this visit was not published until after the designer's death. A photograph taken about 1895 of Steffen and his wife on a Sunday outing with Morris's daughter May and her husband shows the connection to have been personal as well as professional." An even more important link with England was forged through Erik Gustaf Folcker, an assistant at the Nationalmuseum in Stockholm and later the secretary of the Svenska Slöjdföreningen (Swedish Society of Industrial Design). A substantial inheritance enabled him to embark on the great adventure of his life, a series of travels to England, where he visited the foremost advocates of the Arts and Crafts movement. On his first journey, in 1891, he called on William Morris and Walter Crane at their homes. In Leek he met the textile printer Thomas Wardle. He also became acquainted with William De Morgan and the director of the wallpaper firm Jeffrey & Co. In 1892 he opened a small shop in Stockholm, Sub Rosa, and carried the very best English wallpapers, fabrics, ceramics, and artistic photographs (with the exception of products from Morris & Co., since Folcker did not obtain the right to sell these). Folcker was not a businessman and the shop closed three years later. Its large stock of fabric samples and wallpapers was taken to Folcker's home, and it remained there until 1985, when Folcker's son donated the material to the Nordiska Museet in Stockholm.s In the 1890s some Scandinavian designers and architects received government subsidies to travel to England. They visited art schools and embroidery centers, and they studied with admiration the collections of the South Kensington Museum (now the Victoria and Albert). Their travel accounts describe their enthusiasm for Morris & Co. and the work being done at Merton Abbey, The Studio was universally read in artistic circles from its first issue in 1893. The magazine, in turn, covered Scandinavia to a certain extent, including an article on Finnish art that illustrated work by the artist Count Louis Sparre. Sparre and his wife visited The Studio's editor, Charles Holmes, at his home outside London, Red House, which had been built originally for William Morris. According to Sparre's wife, the visit to England inspired the establishment of the Iris Works, which produced exclusive furniture and applied arts in Porvoo, Finland (6.3), and which will be discussed in more detail later. Since the process of industrialization had only begun in Scandinavia around the midnineteenth century, traditions of craftsmanship really had not been disrupted. Furniture factories were now mechanized but most of the work was still done by hand. The output of the large porcelain factories was largely machine produced, with products made in molds and then decorated by transfer printing. Individual studios, however, were maintained for making artistic wares, especially to prepare the tour de force objects intended for big international exhibitions, where receiving coveted medals and diplomas boosted company sales and national pride. International expositions—such as those at Paris (1889 and 1900) and Turin (1902), and the large Nordic exhibitions such as Copenhagen in 1888, Stockholm in 1897 and 1909, and Christiania in 1914—were important stimuli for exclusive production in all media, and many of the objects illustrated in this essay were purchased from such exhibitions for museum collections. Jenő Radisics, director of the Museum of Decorative Arts in Budapest, for example, purchased the tapestry Pentecostal Choir by the Norwegian textile artist Frida Hansen from the Stockholm exhibition of 1897 (6.4). Industrially manufactured goods were imported (in large part from Germany) to the Scandinavian countries. These goods represented the cheapest alternative for the new working class, former country dwellers who now crowded into the cities. Even the old peasant culture began to be replaced in these new circumstances—store-bought goods were regarded as superior ![](images/ee4bd4dae8257432e6fb2f1f9a2f6567cf0d37cc9f527155885c4f5bac81fe98.jpg) to those made at home. The bourgeoisie also purchased factory goods, albeit more expensive ones. They particularly embraced the German Neo-Renaissance, at least for dining rooms. By the end of the century, many museums had been created to save national cultural heritage for posterity. Here Sweden took the lead. In Stockholm Artur Hazelius founded the Nordiska Museet in 1873 and the world's first open-air museum, Skansen, in 1891. Skansen simulated peasant life by housing its collections of artifacts in vernacular buildings, a strategy that was imitated throughout Europe and America. The Folkmuseet in Oslo opened in ![](images/ec55316404b1291236a8f0e713aadbcf70a8552e6c9e595dc43087965a7738a2.jpg) 1894; the Dansk Folkemuseum outside Copenhagen in 1897; and the open-air museum Seurasaari in Helsinki in 1909. Preservationists turned especially to the textile arts as a source of renewal. A wealth of folk weavings and embroidery still remained, and continued to be made, but the application of complicated techniques was on the decline, and chemically dyed yarns were coming into use. The preservationists researched and collected traditional techniques, then attempted to record what was locally distinctive. They also sought out elderly women weavers who could pass on their skills to others new to the craft. Associations were set up by upper-class Scandinavian women (as was also the case throughout Europe and the United States) to safeguard and continue this heritage. The weavings and embroidery produced were actually intended for a well-heeled bourgeoisie. This, too, was the pattern found in all other European countries and America—the market for these goods was not the peasants themselves, but a wealthy, urban clientele committed to both promoting crafts as an expression of national identity and providing the rural poor with an alternative to factory work in the cities. The Föreningen Handarbetets Vänner (Friends of Textile Art Association), established in Sweden in 1874 with both artistic and patriotic aims, developed from about 1895 onward in a modern spirit by leading artists. In 1879 the Suomen Käsityön Ystävät (Friends of Finnish Handicraft) followed the model of the Swedish association; it too produced outstanding modern work based on old techniques. For example, the ryijy rug, traditionally used as a blanket, was transformed into modern applied art, as will be discussed in more detail later. The Den Norske Husflidsforening (Norwegian Association of Home Crafts) was established in 1891 by the amalgamation of a number of older associations, and it advanced above all wood carving and tapestry weaving rooted in old prototypes. And in 1899 the Föreningen för Svensk Hemslöjd (Swedish Handicraft Association) was founded in Stockholm by Lilli Zickerman, a woman of unusual organizational as well as artistic talent. The Dansk Husflidsselskab (Danish Association of Home Crafts) was founded in 1873, and in the following decades it promoted tapestry weaving, the revival of native lace, and embroidery with motifs considered native to Denmark. Like their British counterparts, all these organizations sought not only to preserve and copy the old but also to create incentive for the new. From associations such as these, Nordic textiles began to flourish around the turn of the century. The successful transmission of Arts and Crafts goals meant higher status for the applied arts. Painters and architects started to design furniture, ceramics, and textiles in a way that had not been done earlier. Carl Larsson threw down the gauntlet by writing an enthusiastic letter after visiting the Exposition Universelle in Paris in 1889. He exhorted artists to "go out and preach to all the joyous and beauteous message of art: whittle stoups and stave vessels, carve doors and cupboards, storm the porcelain factories and drive away the Germans with their dreary Lutheran art!" The artists themselves, however, seldom executed their own products—they designed objects that were mostly intended to be made in tandem with ceramic workshops, textile studios, silversmiths, and cabinetmakers. The ideals of breaking down the distinctions between fine and applied arts and creating an environment where everyday objects would be infused with beauty were universally held, but the course of development veered off in a different direction in each of the four Nordic countries. The rest of this essay is devoted to illuminating these distinct paths of development by taking a brief look at each country's leading designers and workshops. DENMARK In Denmark, Skønvirke was a concept introduced in 1907 to describe the renewal of activity in the decorative arts. The name of both an association and a periodical, Skønvirke was also used during the period to refer to all design, except architecture. Scholars have long debated whether it was the influence of the British Arts and Crafts movement that made the decorative arts in Denmark so rich and exciting, or if it was Japonism, combined with intensive contacts with modern French art, that provided the greatest influence. Paul Gauguin, whose wife, Mette, was Danish, showed paintings and ceramics in Copenhagen, and Siegfried Bing brought his collection of Japanese decorative art to the Great Nordic Exhibition in Copenhagen in 1888. German art periodicals were widely read, and many other international contacts played a role as well. Danish porcelain, especially innovations in painting under the glaze was the medium most admired internationally. These wares, made by the Royal Copenhagen porcelain factory under the direction of Arnold Krog (6.5) and by the porcelain factory Bing & Grøndahl under the leadership of Pietro Krohn, were a great success at world's fairs. Museum directors such as Justus Brinckmann bought many examples from these expositions for the Museum of Art and Industry in Hamburg. (The floor vase on page 186 was purchased at the Exposition Universelle in Paris in 1900 [6.6]). Brinckmann acknowledged the companies' most obvious stylistic debt: "their painters have learned a lot from the Japanese,"but he also observed that "their nature has remained entirely Danish."8 Not only were Danish porcelains acquired by museums and collectors throughout Europe and the United States, many ceramic firms also copied Danish designs. The Rookwood Pottery's Iris line, for example, is very similar in both palette and imagery to Royal Copenhagen. Silver was the other material that Danish applied arts became renowned for at the turn of the last century. No Nordic silversmith is better known internationally than Georg Jensen, who started his own shop in 1904.ª His early work, in particular, demonstrates the kind of self-conscious handwork (for example, the retention of hammer marks) and, especially in his jewelry, use of local materials (often native Danish stones) that is typical of the Arts and Crafts movement. This work is vastly different from the refined, expensive pieces made at that time in Paris by craftsmen such as René Lalique. ![](images/2753e82dbca94b845e1c911d36e1b765a57efee04a1b15e4c45c29604e26b12b.jpg) ![](images/2e23ff13c437aadc5e560a4a7e5a33b19316b6ab62d69f4dd4b471444a0fead2.jpg) ![](images/417b5b86c9deaa16e933d79cd2b25af1ee0cb5d57cefabfbcad661b9079e3455.jpg) In 1905 Jensen designed his first piece of hollowware, a small teapot with a flowershaped finial on the lid and an ivory handle (6.7). His later work is often much more elaborately decorated, but the silver always has the special texture produced by small, dense hammer blows. Jensen achieved such success that the workshop soon had to be expanded, and in 1909 he opened his first shop abroad, in Berlin. The scale of production became so large that Jensen needed to hire many talented craftsmen, the most notable being Johan Rohde, who joined the firm in 1906. Rohde originally trained to be a painter, but the experience of designing objects for his own home convinced him to switch to the decorative arts and to follow Arts and Crafts principles. Although the large porcelain factories and Jensen's silver company had the most international audience, the story of the Arts and Crafts movement in Denmark is best told through an examination of smaller-scale production. Trained as an architect, Thorvald Bindesbøll provides the ultimate Scandinavian example of an artist who became a master designer in all media.1º His ceramics, embroidery designs, bookbindings, and silverwork, like his carving and furniture, all speak the same powerful, sensual language. Decoration is abstract and two-dimensional, and motifs often consist of rhythmic cloud formations or remarkable plant-life forms. Although his father was a Neoclassical architect, Bindesbøll was most influenced by Japanese and Chinese ornamentation, which he transformed and made almost primal. There is no harking back to stylistic history here, no sense of national identity—everything is new and vigorous. ![](images/1bfdf89cefbdad2956313656f5656eb796e9e609c48788b5376b2cc758c8d987.jpg) ![](images/7abc5502421822693f426c4f665ee44b346ffa7beeeee543832e86424f0c7ae6.jpg) Bindesboll was one of the many artists at the turn of the century who chose to make his ceramics of earthenware, which was simpler to fabricate than stoneware or porcelain (6.8). A coarser material, earthenware seemed more expressive to artists trying to work closer to nature and with less-manipulated materials. Bindesboll sketched the shape and abstract decoration of his vessels and participated in their production by incising the decorative elements in the wet clay before painting and glazing. His ceramics, made between 1883 and 1908, consist mostly of big swelling vases and large plates. Production was executed in different workshops; the most important ones being Wallmann in Utterslev, Herman A. Kähler in Næstved, G. Eifrig in Valby, and P. Ipsens Enke in Copenhagen. Because his style for the different workshops remained consistent, it is often difficult to identify precisely where a particular piece was made. Between 1873 and 1903 Bindesbøll's sister, Johanne, ran an embroidery shop with another craftswoman, Kristiane Constantin Hansen. Bindesbøll designed a number of half cross-stitch patterns for the shop. His motifs resemble clouds and fungi, and they are rendered in subdued colors against a dark background; the occasional color produces a strong, luminous contrast. They are true originals, far from the motifs typically embroidered in cross-stitch from German patterns at this time (6.9). Bindesbøll also designed silver—first in collaboration with the well-known Danish silversmith A. Michelsen in Copenhagen, and from 1904 onward with Holger Kyster in Kolding. The cloud formations recur here, but now they are hammered in relief with a soft, rounded surface. Bindesboll's silverwork and ceramics, with their innovative ornamentation, created a sensation at the Exposition Universelle (Paris, 1900) His book designs and other graphic works have equally powerful, clear forms; his label for the well-known Danish lager Carlsberg Pilsner is still being used today, one hundred years after it was introduced. Like Rohde, many other Scandinavian and European designers during this period were first trained as painters, but they quickly embraced the applied arts as well. Lorenz Frolich received a classicist training from the Royal Danish Academy, but as early as 1854 he was designing furniture for his own home inspired by medieval examples with painted decoration." The dining room chairs made in 1895 as a wedding gift for his daughter Edma (6.10) seem closely related to the Gothic Revival furniture of the British architect A. W. N. Pugin. Since Frølich lived in London from 1870 to 1871, it is possible that he saw some of the furniture made by William Morris's company: this may have strengthened his resolve to continue along the same path. ![](images/c8ca054371976c1ecdde31d5154656ad8104e9bed1c1c787a9cc66c43e4f644c.jpg) About 1890 the painter Harald Slott-Moller designed silverware inspired by Japanese art, and around the turn of the century, he worked as a designer at the Aluminia earthenware factory, where he produced both form and decoration. A cradle that he and his wife, Agnes, made for their daughter in 1893 is late medieval in style, with elaborate carving, painting, and gilding full of symbolism (6.12). On one side, outlined against a starry sky, a Doric column appears with the words "A thing of beauty is a joy forever," a quote from the Romantic poet John Keats. This ![](images/d074e2f688e2de31eb034aef22319b714aa655196922ce00eb569ee364fb3fa2.jpg) is one of the few Danish examples of an unequivocal relationship with the British Arts and Crafts movement. Slott-Møller called the cradle 'painted wooden sculpture" when it was exhibited at the Copenhagen exhibition in 1894 and at the Exposition Universelle in Paris in 1900.'² While Denmark is the Scandinavian country with the fewest documented direct contacts with the British Arts and Crafts movement, it shared with all other European countries the British inclination to associate the preservation of traditional ways of making with national identity. And like the other Scandinavian countries (as well as those in the rest of Europe), this attempt to revitalize and update traditional designs began earlier in the nineteenth century, independent of the British movement. The Arts and Crafts movement at the end of the century, however, provided an ideological language to relate crafts—textiles, in particular—with national identity. In the farmland west of Copenhagen a special type of whitework embroidery, known as Hedebo needlework, was made during the first half of the nineteenth century.'3 These embroideries were studied intensely and were later animated with nationalist content; a society for their study and revival was established, chaired by the architect of Copenhagen's new city hall, Martin Nyrop. Inspired by earlier peasant embroidery, a number of artists created modern whitework embroidery patterns on linen tabby (6.11). Agnes and Harald Slott-Møller NORWAY Archaeological excavations of Viking ships in Norway in 1867 and 1880 helped stimulate the Viking Revival, also called "dragon style." While the Danes and the Swedes had their own versions of this style, the Norwegians were particularly drawn to Viking legends, since they represented a time when Norway was the conqueror, not just the secondary partner in a forced union between countries. Lars Kinsarvik's chair is an example of the Viking Revival style (6.14). Carved dragon heads top the back stiles, and an intricate polychromed interlace pattern covers the entire surface except the seat. The cabinetmaker Johan Borgersen was also known for furniture in this style, and he received praise for it at international expositions, such as the Exposition Universelle (Paris, 1900) (6.13). Norway made its most distinctive contribution to Scandinavian Arts and Crafts through silverwork, particularly through a technique, pioneered by the firm Jacob Tostrup, that combined filigree and plique-à-jour enamel. The technique was greatly refined by Torolf Prytz, who became head of the company in 1882. Prytz had closely examined what was called "Hungarian wire enamel," as well as French and Russian experiments with this difficult process, and he developed a variant that became a hallmark of Norwegian work.'ª Many other silversmiths mastered this technique, and some, such as Marius Hammer, made a specialty of its application to vessels in the shape of Viking ships, with dragon heads for handles and exuberant interlace decoration (6.17). While these motifs were the most common, silversmiths applied the technique to objects with a less nationalistic agenda, such as a cup by Gustav Gaudernack, the leading designer for the firm David Andersen, the Tostrup company's biggest competitor (6.18). Munthe was commissioned to furnish a dining room at Hollmenkollen Tourist Hotel, just outside Christiania. The so-called Fairy Tale Room was probably completed in 1896 (6.19). Contemporary descriptions are effusive; no surface was left undecorated. Large carved reliefs—with motifs from folk tales, songs, and legends, framed by abstract patterns in high relief or stenciling—covered the walls. This room was a truly unique creation, inspired not only by Nordic design, but even more by Aesthetic movement interiors, such as those by architect Edward Godwin and the painter James McNeill Whistler. SWEDEN An essential quality of the Arts and Crafts movement is that it produced domestic art, intimately tied to home life. From a good home come good citizens who build a good society. This idea was central to Ellen Key, a Swedish author influenced by Ruskin and Morris. Her books were read throughout Scandinavia and reprinted many times in Germany. Some of her writings have also been translated into English by the American "Mamah" Borthwick Cheney, who left her husband in 1909 to live with Frank Lloyd Wright, and who learned Swedish expressly in order to translate Key.20 Key was involved in social and political causes from early in her career, and her interests also embraced art and morality. In her small volume Skönhet för alla (Beauty for All), published by the radical student association Verdandi in 1899, she develops her thoughts on the home. Key's main thesis is inspired by the philosophy of the Arts and Crafts movement: beauty must be an integral part of everyday life. Beginning in childhood, one should grow accustomed to beauty so that it becomes essential. Women should not waste their time sewing meaningless embroideries, but they should create an aesthetically pleasing home (they should also read good books and study art). With lectures, essays, and exhibitions, Key and a number of young architects tried to improve the aesthetics, morals, and hygiene of the home. A home with a light, cheerful atmosphere, simple furnishings, flowers in the windows, good reproduction art, good books, and well-scrubbed inhabitants would bring about a renaissance. No longer would people need to escape the harshness of daily life via alcohol or other sordid means; they would instead become aware of the beauty in art and nature. And it was not only the workers who were to reform their homes. Key also makes an assault on the "Germanness" of bourgeois furnishings, with their heavy plush curtains and stands holding dust-gathering ornaments and arrangements of dried flowers and feathers. ![](images/27b0dd687cac589ec85f7b02eb72c524a887fba780d20cefbd20e5e35b8f8fb9.jpg) Key used the home of artists Carl and Karin Larsson in the province of Dalarna as a model: the Larssons had created beauty in their domestic environment, and without great expense.2' In 1888 the couple were given a simple red-painted peasant cottage that had belonged to members of Karin's family. Lilla (little) Hyttnäs in the village of Sundborn became the family's summer home, and after 1901 their permanent abode. Despite being enlarged to accommodate the growing family, the home retained its original concept. The dining room, with its red-painted furniture and green paneling capped by painted mottoes over the doors, is fairly dark. The drawing room, on the other hand, has a large window facing south and is very light. This room is considered the essence of Swedishness, with its tiled stove, scrubbed-white wooden floor, and early-nineteenth-century white-painted furniture. The Larssons used the furniture they had inherited and decorated the room in the beloved Gustavian style, the Swedish version of Louis Quatorze. In his bedroom, Larsson made a bold, individualistic statement: he placed the bed he designed at the very center of the floor (it is almost the only piece of furniture in the room); its posts extend right up to the ceiling (6.22). The studio has an English-style lancet window decorated with medieval motifs and a plain brick fireplace (6.23). Karin's portrait adorns the top of a sliding door. Old and new were blended: the Larssons repainted the seventeenth- and eighteenth-century vernacular forms so that, like the other rooms in the house, the studio glowed with color. Larsson designed the settee, crowning it with a whimsical self-portrait. Karin played a central role in the entire décor; for example, she designed, wove, and embroidered textiles for the house. Her work was innovative and self-confident, even if her husband took credit for it. Their home was a total work of art, a Gesamtkunstwerk. ![](images/273fb75878b989920c9be27db6b7733edbe5625052d2a00a6653ceb5170e9293.jpg) 6.24 Ellen Key's Green Room exhibition, with furniture designed by Carl Westman, Workers' Institute, Stockholm, 1899 Carl Larsson published a series of watercolors depicting Lilla Hyttnäs in Ett hem (A Home, 1899), which featured the illustrations in four-color printing. Here he writes that his home might serve as a guide for others. (And it did. Inspired by the Larssons, many people at the turn of the century painted their furniture white, red, or green.) The book was an immediate success and was followed by four others with very similar formats. Pictures of Lilla Hyttnäs were most widely disseminated via an inexpensive German edition, Das Haus in der Sonne (The House in the Sun), a compendium of texts and images from Larsson's previous books published in a large print run of forty thousand copies by Karl Langewische in 1909.² The book had an enormous distribution, and now, with the forty-second edition, almost five hundred thousand copies have been produced. The German version was edited without consulting Larsson: the interior decorating ideas were played down, while family happiness, a child-friendly environment, and the simple country life, all seen from a patriarchal perspective, were emphasized. Color reproductions of the illustrations were included, both in Sweden and in Germany, as art supplements in magazines, and the images could be ordered for homes and schools through bookstores all over Sweden. Nothing, except perhaps our present-day IKEA, has had a greater impact on Swedish interior design than these pictures. There has been much speculation about where Carl Larsson and his wife acquired the ideas for creating their home. Both trained at the Konstakademien (Royal Academy of Fine Arts) in Stockholm, were widely traveled, and subscribed to German, French, and English art journals. Predictably, The Studio is found in their library from its very first issue. The basis of their design, however, was already established in 1893, before the influence of The Studio. It is an extremely personal blend of Arts and Crafts movement ideas on the home adapted to a Swedish environment; it draws on the country's rural past as well as the individual creativity of the two artists. Key organized two small exhibitions at the Arbetareinstitutet (Workers' Institute) in Stockholm in collaboration with architects and artists. For the Gröna rummet (Green Room) display of 1899, the architect Carl Westman designed a suite of "workers' furniture" in green-glazed pine (6.24 and 6.25). The furniture appears to have sold fairly well—though ironically, to radical intellectuals. Carl Westman Westman was one of the best Swedish furniture designers of his day. For several years during the 1890s, he worked in architects' offices in the United States. A study tour of England, where he encountered the Arts and Crafts movement and the new domestic architecture, had a decisive influence on the private houses he built and the furniture he designed. He used Swedish woods—either stained or painted—for his distinctively constructed furniture, which was sometimes adorned with ornament that had been sensitively executed by his wife, Elin Westman. Many of his pieces were made by Bröderna Erikssons (Eriksson Brothers), the wellknown workshop in the province of Värmland. Westman designed a private house with all its interior furnishings as the main prize for a large lottery sponsored by the Publicistföreningen (Press Association). The Pressens Villa (Villa of the Press) was completed in 1902 (6.26).23 The house was of red-painted wood with white details, and thus conformed to Swedish tradition, but it also had an American-type open plan. On the upper story were the bedrooms and nursery. The nursery chair backs and furniture panels were openwork, with a playful decoration of trees, cats, and roosters. Westman also used this same chair for the nursery in his home (6.27). The inspiration for this particular decorative scheme clearly derives from English architect M. H. Baillie Scott; the general idea of providing artistically designed surroundings for children also comes from England. The person in Sweden who took up Morris's message in words and deeds most palpably, although considerably later, was the cabinetmaker, designer, and educator Professor Carl Malmsten.²4His work is characterized by the emphasis on the structure of the wood, clear joints, good craftsmanship, and simple design. He often incorporated motifs from Swedish vernacular furniture along with a pared-down classicism. His breakthrough as a furniture designer came in 1915, when a chair he designed won the competition for the furnishing of Stockholm's city hall (6.28), which was being built at the time. The masterpiece of architect Ragnar Östberg, the building was inaugurated in 1923. With its intense nationalism, contributions from the greatest Swedish craftsmen, and overarching design unity, Stockholm's city hall can be considered one of the last great statements of the Arts and Crafts movement. After the completion of this commission, Malmsten established schools of ceramics, textiles, and furniture making, which have contributed significantly to the survival of craftsmanship in Sweden. ![](images/109b68f318efaf3f3d412a16c4f2d1896c5b66e1eac0bfa0632ec8be35015530.jpg) As previously discussed, Scandinavian ceramics was the medium most recognized and disseminated internationally: the two leading Swedish firms exemplify this. In 1895 the Rörstrand factory in Stockholm employed painter Alf Wallander as artistic director, with the specific assignment of creating a modern design statement for the great Stockholm exhibition of 1897. One of the pieces he made for the exhibition is quintessentially representative of his style: a boldly modeled urn, with starfish swimming and crawling as in Japanese drawings (6.30). Rörstrand hired a number of other skillful modelers, who soon followed this example with similar work and established the firm's reputation as a leader in porcelain production. These designs are more clearly associated with the international Art Nouveau movement, however, than with the Arts and Crafts movement.25 Gustavsberg, the other great porcelain factory in Sweden, employed painter Gunnar Wennerberg during the same period. Wennerberg's artistic temperament was less dramatic ![](images/b2462256c502f2ff906738c28dfb9af3f39435fef00c6d6e1c82a1b4aa62692e.jpg) than Wallander's, and he used his knowledge of botany to create designs incorporating the native Swedish flora with a previously unknown freshness (6.31). Lily of the valley, cowslip, and lime leaves are characteristically nationalistic plant motifs used on services exhibited in Stockholm in 1897, and these were later reproduced in quantity. Wallander and Wennerberg were directly inspired by Siegfried Bing's periodical Le Japon Artistique. Contemporary developments in France sparked the renewal of the Swedish glass industry. For the Exposition Universelle (Paris, 1900), Wennerberg designed a series of overlay-glass vessels, executed by the Kosta Glassworks, that were very much in keeping with the style of Emile Gallé and the Daum brothers (6.32). His sensitive way of rendering flowers was particularly suited to overlay glass, which was usually made in two layers around a core—a lower layer cut at different levels for the base, and an upper layer for the flowers. The skillful glass cutter A. E. Boman rose to the challenge of executing the color shades indicated by Wennerberg in his drawings.2⁶ Wallander and Wennerberg were extremely versatile artists. Wallander produced tapestries and furniture of his design through his own firm. Wennerberg made the prototypes for embroidery patterns and tapestries for the Friends of Textile Art Association. Both belonged to the same artistic circle as Prince Eugen, the youngest son of Sweden's King Oscar Il and an important artist. Gunnar Wennerberg, Kosta Glassworks While Prince Eugen was a gifted painter, he also played a central role as a promoter of progressive design, including texti!es. As in Norway, modern textile art in Sweden developed from hemslöjd (handicrafts). A telling example is that of Märta Måås-Fjetterström, who, when dismissed as manager of the handicraft organization in Malmö (in southern Sweden) for being too modern, was hired by Lilli Zickerman and the Swedish Handicraft Association, which had a more up-to-date orientation. (Prince Eugen served as chairman of the association.) Måås-Fjetterström designed rugs for the association that united the compositional principles of oriental rugs with southern Swedish ornamentation: they are architectonically developed with such motifs as stylized birds and stars in bold but harmonious colors (6.29). She also worked in the same free manner on tapestries and hangings. In 1919 she was able, with financial help, to open a weaving workshop of her own in Båstad, in the province of Skåne, where she now produced her work signed MMF.2 She collaborated, too, with Carl Malmsten, whose furniture was often exhibited with her rugs. The weaving workshop still remains, and even with new artists in charge, Måås-Fjetterström's rugs continue to be produced. ![](images/348bc24c859a6f4366ab750292e2309c572f15968bc9d41bffa94a72d1d4b27b.jpg) ![](images/ff377b7c6ac7dc2141fcd498049cd349f8d350d6b84c068c7241851bd04cb194.jpg) FINLAND Seldom have the arts of painting, music, literature, and architecture interacted as intimately as they did in Finland during the 1890s. And since painters and architects also devoted themselves to furniture, textiles, and other interior furnishings, there were links between all aspects of artistic life. A collective opposition to Russian domination was the unifying factor. People sought out the primordial Finland not only in the spheres of ethnography, architecture, and folk music, but also in untouched nature. Finnish nationalism was intimately connected to the eastern province of Karelia, a remote wilderness, cherished as the heart of Finnishness. Oral traditions among the Karelians were the basis of the Finnish national epic, The Kalevala: The Land of Heroes, which was first published in 1835. Karelia and the Kalevala inspired a generation of Finnish artists, including the poet Eino Leino, the composer Jean Sibelius, and the painter Akseli Gallen-Kallela. Axel Gallén had changed his Swedish-sounding name to a Finnish one—Akseli Gallen-Kallela. He traveled on foot through Karelia with his Swedish colleague, Louis Sparre, and took inspiration and motifs for his pictures from the Kalevala and from Kanteletar, a collection of folk poems. One early example, Velisurmaaja (The Fratricide), underscores the power of these drawings, which are stylized and full of symbolism (6.35). Gallen-Kallela decorated the vaulting for the Finnish Pavilion at the Exposition Universelle (Paris, 1900) with Kalevala motifs; he later returned to the same theme for the vaulting of the Suomen Kansallismuseo (National Museum) in Helsinki. Both Gallen-Kallela and the architect Lars Sonck built their own homes in a timber technique inspired by old buildings in Karelia. Sonck's greatest masterpiece in stone is the Tampere Cathedral, built between 1902 and 1907; another is the Helsinki Telephone Company Building, which features a façade of coarse-hewn granite in the romantic medieval style (6.34). A tower with narrow window openings strengthens the impression of a fortress or other defensive building. The windows appear as if they might look into a dungeon or even a cave: a remarkable manifestation, indeed, for a building with a modern purpose. Influenced by the Arts and Crafts movement, Sparre started Finland's first applied arts firm, the Iris Works, in 1897 in Porvoo, near Helsinki. His objective was "the development of Finnish taste to a higher and more artistic level."8 The furniture made there was mostly executed from his designs: it was characteristically simple, more or less undecorated, and imbued with a clear statement of structure (6.36). The firm's shop in Helsinki also sold fabrics from Liberty & Co. ![](images/2848d37081f9c12b450b85006ee3babfa2ae6a9df0c622927ae81cc010a9260c.jpg) ![](images/300cdd3de1b94d6e12e724ce5941e8a3c4dab12f8ffd85dbd106cd3b0ed098bd.jpg) ![](images/9eab76bc2f162467354a559c3f6d422d9fb6f7bc2c0669731fa353f3ceeee51c.jpg) Sparre's wife, Eva Mannerheim-Sparre, also an artist, helped with the company and made elegant bookbindings as well. During a journey to Belgium, Sparre met the ceramicist Alfred William Finch (see 7.20), who became associated with the Iris Works in the autumn of 1897. Finch's earthenwares are simple vessels, glazed in powerful colors with austere sgraffito decoration (6.37). Neither Sparre nor Finch borrowed shapes much from the Finnish vernacular: they were modern and international, and the hallmarks of their work were good materials and craftsmanship. The Iris Works was short-lived, however—it closed in 1902. Like those in other countries, Finland's commercial potteries that added an "art line" to their existing production were more financially successful. Arabia, the porcelain works, attempted renewal by using old peasant patterns in industrial production. The Fennia line of earthenware included bowls, vases, coffee services, jugs, and basins, and featured geometric decoration in clear colors; it formed an extremely popular expression of nationalism around the turn of the century (6.38). Karelianism was a living concept for the three successful young architects Herman Gesellius, Armas Lindgren, and Eliel Saarinen at the Exposition Universelle (Paris, 1900), when ![](images/fceabafa90579a12be7f1c9f2b577592d94539401dd2aa483ef7c9b66abd6646.jpg) their firm won the architectural competition for its Finnish Pavilion (6.39). This was the first international exhibition where Finnish art was displayed in its own pavilion, and the building became a central statement about the country's national identity. One room was furnished by the Iris Works and the Friends of Finnish Handicraft, for whom Gallen-Kallela designed chairs of birch upholstered with a woolen fabric embroidered with spruce twig motifs (6.40), as well as curtains of the same pattern. He also designed a ryijy rug, which was worked in a centuries-old technique, but whose display was a complete innovation in function: originally used as a warm cover on beds and in sleighs and boats, the rug was recast here as a wall-seat-floor covering. After the success of the Finnish Pavilion at the Exposition Universelle, the architectural office of Gesellius, Lindgren, and Saarinen received so many substantial commissions that in 1901 they were able to buy a large piece of land in the forest west of Helsinki. There they created Hvitträsk, a house and studio. or more accurately, a castle, of timber with granite foundations (6.41).29 Hvitträsk was a large complex of structures—one freestanding, the others connected with skylighted studios—built during the architects' most successful period of collaboration, between 1901 and 1903. (By 1905 Lindgren had already returned to Helsinki for his own practice; Gesellius left the partnership in 1907, although he continued to live at Hvitträsk until his death in 1916.) Just as the Finnish Pavilion became a symbol of national identity, so, too, did Hvitträsk. Following the Arts and Crafts principle of harmony with nature, the elevation follows the contour of the landscape. Stone walls; a series of outdoor terraces, pavilions, and porches; and local building materials—granite, plaster, and logs, with pantiles and shingles for roofing—all serve to connect Hvitträsk to its site. Saarinen played the leading role in the interior. The living room—filled with handmade furnishings of his design, and a ceiling and walls of round-log construction—is a model of the vernacular ideal (6.43). The ceiling lighting takes the form of a medieval church candleholder, comprising a large ring suspended from five cords. On the ground floor, there are handmade ![](images/b498b9dea6d1a5750ad3efc7ead9229839286950010a78a1879e50c739c39a58.jpg) furnishings (6.42), hand-woven textiles of unusual beauty, and huge fireplaces throughout—everything combining expressly to protect against the winter. The rooms in the upper-story living quarters, on the other hand, are light with white-painted furniture, similar to the architects' work that the journal Moderne Bauformen had published during that period.30 In the Exposition Universelle's Finnish Pavilion, Gallen-Kallela had modernized the traditional ryijy rug through the use of strong abstract, geometric patterns; he also gave the rug a new purpose. The living room rug at Hvitträsk—a variant using different colors from the ryijy at the Finnish Pavilion—is also draped across wall, seat, and floor. (Ryijys designed by other artists became something of a specialty for the Friends of Finnish Handicraft [6.44]). Saarinen did watercolors of the interior furnishings for many of the houses the partners designed. In these, one finds rich color, painted-glass windows, depth, different floor levels, and ![](images/675a04add382a98cc49413fbbe4efd7079627322891561e118e1821ecac162e1.jpg) vaulted rooms; there were intimate, light places for sitting near leaded-glass windows, and dark corners with great fireplaces and benches (6.46 and 6.47). No such watercolors depicting Hvitträsk, however, are to be found; probably because these kinds of drawings were intended only for client presentations. The relationship between Finland and the Arts and Crafts movement becomes particularly clear in the case of architect-designed furniture. The wood is untreated, the structure emphasized, and the archaic ornamentation in relief carvings and wrought-iron mountings is meant as an echo of the Karelian forests. One example of this relationship is the dining room buffet designed by Valter Jung (6.45). By 1904, however, the Arts and Crafts had begun to seem outmoded. The interest in England had shifted to Germany and Austria, and peasant characteristics and great iron mountings were no longer considered progressive design. The arts in Scandinavia reached remarkable heights at the turn of the century. Painting, architecture, the decorative arts, literature, and music flourished and were received with enthusiasm on the continent and, to a certain degree, in Britain as well. It was a time when the different branches of art nourished each other and friendships between artists were established across borders. In this era of openness, new artistic trends—including Romantic Nationalism and the Arts and Crafts movement—became important as each of the Nordic countries searched for ways to assert its unique cultural essence. ![](images/ac01049dfb17bbba6644c1c904a8bf84542495e12219e8b9ef253a65a2f1d4bf.jpg) ![](images/e1a97699796963c6b48974201f57b549891eae18c0fef64875f0180d9a5f05fe.jpg)
