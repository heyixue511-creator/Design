# Cultural differences-the way we think and act

## How I came to think about this

When I began teaching in China in about 1985, it was an adventure for me. I was unprepared, loaded with western ideas, filled with curiosity and admiration for an oldculture that has survived whilst other classical cultures like the Greek or Roman have vanished. Iwas filled with many prejudices too, but had an open mind. In the course of my first teachings I found that Chinese students reacted differently to what I had experienced compared with students in western societies. That puzzled me. I began to think and found that it had to do with history, culture, social context, perception, and language. Now I know that the fundamental way of processing information is not universal but depends on culture, and that the need for research into cultural acting and behaviour becomes obvious.(Ulrich Kühnen)

Naturally| was eager to make myself understood, first by discussing the matter with my Chinese friends and colleagues, but that was helpful only in a minor sense. I found I had to go back to the formative factors of eastern civilisation and eastern thinking. So it turned out to become a cross-cultural study that has occupied my thoughts since then.

I now know that I am only dabbling in this matter, realizing that the more I know, the more I don't know. And each timeI return from China and my efforts at cross-cultural understanding, I am filled with new questions. There are lines of thinking in China,I had to discover, like“Moulüe”(HarrovonSenger), a far-reaching level of planning and horizon of thinking almost unknown in western societies. These go back to the old master Sun, whose ideas go far beyond our western thinkingof tactics and strategy. Western efforts to understand the cultural background of a vital partner in commercíal cooperations have provoked serious research in order to find out how Chinese people think and act. I had to learn that the critical dialogue, a basic element in Western thinking, is not considered a virtue in China, but a serious danger to social harmony and cultural reception. It is not only a matter of content, but a matter of procedure. It is aboutwhat other people expect from youand how to approach the partner you want tocommunicate with. It has explained to me the hesitation of Chinese students entering a critical discussion, their lack of a challengingattitude toward authority(Jin Li) emphasized by the Chinese tradition of respect for the teacher, whom you are never expected to criticise.

My findings arevery cryptic and personal but they have led me to change my teaching approach. But I want to describe them in the hope that theyfoster mutual understanding in the process of educational exchange. Ialsowant to encourage students who have studied both in China and Western countries to be aware of their chance and challenge; being familiar with two cultures, they are of great value in a global society. They are the transmitters of cultural exchange and learning and therefore of inestimable importance to us all.

我现在了解到，我本人只是略微涉猎到这个方面：懂得越多，越发现自己不知道的东西也变得多了起来。每次从中国回来，我都对跨文化方面有了新的理解与认识，进而脑海中就又产生了新的问题。我发现中国有很多自己的思维套路，就像“谋略”一样(胜雅律)，它是指一种更为深入的计划，以及西方社会所未知的思维视野。关于这点，谋略大师孙子的观点，与西方关于策略的思维方式和想法截然不同。商业组织中的西方人了解重要谈判对手的文化背景时，都要做深入而细致的研究和调查，弄清楚中国人思考和行动的方式。我必须承认批判性的对话在西方思维方式中是一项基本要素，在中国却不被认为是一种美德，而是一种威胁社会和谐和文化接受性的严重风险。这不仅仅是内容的问题，而且是程序的问题—关于其他人如何看待你以及如何同你希望建立交流关系的人进行接触。这就解释了为什么中国学生在参加批判性讨论之前，会犹豫不决。由于受到中国传统中“尊重师长”观念的影响，他们缺乏挑战权威的态度(李瑾)，师长是不应该被批判的。

这些发现可能有些主观臆断，但是已经让我改变了教学方法。然而我仍然要把它们描述出来，希望学生们能够在课堂学习交流的过程中建立相互理解的关系。同时我也想鼓励在中国和西方国家都学习过的学生们了解自己的机会和面临的挑战，熟悉这两种世界上颇有价值的文化。它们是文化交流和学习的承载者，因此有着不可估量的重大意义。
