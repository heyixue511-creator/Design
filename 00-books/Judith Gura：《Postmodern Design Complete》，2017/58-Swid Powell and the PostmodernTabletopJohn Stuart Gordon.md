# Swid Powell and the Postmodern Tabletop John Stuart Gordon “The age of architect-designed objects is upon us," declared the Chicago Tribune in i985, and few companies better embodied this trend in houseware than the American firm Swid Powell. Its products transformed the kitchen counter and the dining table into miniaturized cityscapes populated by coffee pots, bowls, and other items that emulated the look and exemplified the theories of postmodern architecture. Its highbrow yet accessible houseware encapsulated the era's desire for eye-catching prestige objects, its infatuation with architects, and its recalibration of what constituted good taste. Swid Powell was established in New York in i982 by Nan Swid and Addie Powell. They met while working at Knoll International\*, where they saw first-hand how interested architects were in designing domestic objects, such as ROBERT VENTURI and DENISE SCOTT BROWN's (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") line of chairs. Realizing there might be a market for corresponding houseware, they presented their concept to a close-knit group of architects over lunch at the Four Seasons restaurant, with its famed modernist interior designed by Philip Johnson\*. Swid Powell then expanded its list of potential architect-designers, asking them to create any type of houseware they wanted and inviting them to assess the submissions as a group. “We were afraid they would be very clinical, harsh on one another," Swid recalled, "But exactly the opposite happened."3 Their shared enthusiasm for the project created camaraderie rather than competition. The first Swid Powell collection of porcelain, glassware, and silver plate debuted in June 1984 with designs by Charles Gwathmey and Robert Siegel, ARATA ISOZAKI (see under “Architecture"), Richard Meier\*, Laurinda Spear, ROBERT A. M. STERN (see under "Architecture"), STANLEY TIGERMAN (see under “Architecture"), and Robert Venturi. The roster eventually expanded to include FRANK GEHRY (see under "Architecture"), MICHAEL GRAVES (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), Zaha Hadid, and many others. The company's promotional materials included photographs and biographies of the architect-designers and each object was marked with their signature or monogram. Swid Powell presented its houseware as an opportunity for design aficionados of average means to “experience the aesthetic" of famous architects, but it was simultaneously a way for the architects to broaden their commercial reach.4 Swid Powell's timing was propitious. Branding products with the names of freelance designers to attract sales was a long-established practice, but in ı98ı the New York Times observed that "designer names are coming to dinner," noting a resurgence in houseware styled by fashion houses and artists. Swid Powell and the Italian manufacturer ALESSI garnered particular editorial and commercial attention by virtue of their collaborations with the generation of younger contemporary architects. Swid Powell's goal was to use manufacturing to make progressive design accessible to the public. "I don't think you can say these are collectors' items," Powell advised in i984, “We want people to eat off these dishes, use these things for entertaining."7 Ultimately, however, Swid Powell's and Alessi's products were priced just beyond the reach of the average consumer and coded with visual references geared toward the intellectual elite. They embodied the era's penchant for conspicuous consumption, which some critics felt was as much a by-product of political conservatism as of financial prosperity.8 Notably, when Spy magazine parodied this materialism, it used architect-designed houseware to make its point: Now imagine a teapot. It's about being able to pay a lot of money for a teapot. It's a teapot that, once in your possession, reflects your obvious good taste.9 ![](images/047d0dc7be7c795aa63ecf829fd519fec33038ef8007cf9d8ed9d722419805c3.jpg) ![](images/48c359980ac9a7955165533fc417c59a8fe0d7d0dbae67856ba2f6bc23344bae.jpg) Swid Powell's symbolic, aspirational objects made the company an immediate commercial success. Given this demand, it faced pressure to produce new, marketable lines each season, much like a fashion company, and the architects were eager to contribute, as many found designing houseware unexpectedly satisfying. Swid Powell's most prolific collaborator was Richard Meier. As with the articulation of his buildings, Meier's silverand dishware relied upon repeated and intersecting geometric shapes evocative of German and Austrian Modernism. A band of pierced squares encircled the King Richard silver plate bowl, simultaneously evoking rows of windows and the square perforations of Josef Hoffmann's metalwork for the Wiener Werkstätte. Plates formed the core of Swid Powell's line. To facilitate production and regulate cost, they used standard-sized porcelain blanks, which also made them tabulae rasae upon which the architects could project their distinct ideas. Robert Venturi, who saw his work for Swid Powell as the logical extension of his furni ture designs for Knoll, covered one set of dishes in a black-and-white pattern derived from the cover of a quotidian composition book,'º undermining the type of highbrow motif traditionally used on fine porce lain. For Stanley Tigerman, “a plate is not a building and therefore can exhibit a good deal of whimsy and humor."" This allowed the cartoon angles and clouds that existed on the periphery of Tigerman's architectural drawings to become the subject matter of his Heaven and Sunshine patterns. Steven Holl used theory as subject, with imagery that occasionally broke free of the rim and extended into the plate's center, raising questions about the relationship between structure and decoration. In these, as with most of Swid Powell's plates, the figure-ground relationship was perpetually in flux, rendering them simultaneously utilitarian and conceptual. Among Swid Powell's most experimental and influential designs were porcelain tea- and coffee pots. Graves's The Big Dripper reduced a drip coffee maker to a series of crisp geometric forms accented with symbolic decoration: waves of blue on the filter and pot signaling areas in contact with liquid, and dark red at the base evoking a heat source. Tigerman's Teaside service recreated one of the firm's building projects in miniature while the name punned on Teesside, the region in the North of England, and Seaside\*, the postmodern planned community in Florida. Venturi's Village tea and coffee set used a different historical building type for each vessel, creating an accumulation of styles that evoked the heterogeneity of many town centers. The economic recession in the late i98os and the growing number of AIDS-related deaths eroded Swid Powell's consumer and retail base.'² Competition from pirated designs sold at lower prices also affected them.'3 To generate publicity, in i99o the company embarked on an ambitious project titled The Architects' Collection, consisting of limited-edition objects made from precious máterials including sterling silver, lapis lazuli, marble, and enamel. Twelve prominent architects designed sumptuous houseware that ranged from glass goblets to towering candelabra.'4 Stern recreated the Choragic Monument of Lysicrates as a silver ice bucket, and a modular centerpiece by ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") quoted his work with MEMPHIS. Robert and Trix Haussmann\* revisited an earlier design for a trompe l’oeil-decorated bureau for a cloisonné enameled jewelry chest. The collection, with prices ranging from \$5,ooo to \$30,00o, was unveiled in a gallery-like setting; each piece stood on its own pedestal, suggesting that these housewares were in fact art objects. The Architects' Collection echoed the influential Tea & Coffee Piazzas that Alessi commissioned in i98o, which were also costly limited editions designed by a group of international architects. These were atypical products both for Swid Powell and Alessi, but as opulent yet rigorously theoretical statements they proved to be important promotional tools. Together, they bookend the decade when architect-designed housewares captivated the marketplace. Swid Powell continued to expand its product line by developing houseware for companies like DISNEY and Calvin Klein and partnering with the established silver manufacturer Reed & Barton on flatware and serving pieces. But by the mid-ı99os Postmodernism and the celebrity architect had lost their novelty amid a new set of cultural references. Over the course of the i98os Swid Powell was instrumental in creating objects that reflected an economically prosperous, aspirational society and that expanded the discourse surrounding contemporary architecture. Through its extensive roster of architect-designers, its heterogeneous aesthetic, and its promotion of attainable luxury Swid Powell domesticated Postmodernism and brought the skyline to the tabletop. ## ABET LAMINATI MILANESE FIRM WHOSE WORK WITH MEMPHIS MADE PLASTIC LAMINATE INTEGRAL TO POSTMODERN DESIGN. There are three major ways in which manufacturers can ally themselves with new design and designers. They can commission the designers to make new products. They can use the products in their advertising or displays. Or they can sponsor exhibitions of the designer's work. Abet Laminati took a fortuitous fourth option, in a unique collaboration that made the company and its products integral to the development of postmodern furnishings. When Postmodernism was in its infancy, Abet Laminati was an established producer of plastic laminates, founded in Milan in the late ι95os as Italian modern design was making its first inroads into the international marketplace. Looking for innovative ideas and embracing new technology, many designers turned to plastics, erasing the stigma associated with cheap, man-made materials. Abet laminates provided the “neutral surface" in the black-and-white grid pattern of the Ollo collection by ALESSANDRO MENDINI (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") for Alchimia\*. Alerted to the possibilities for more varied applications of its products, Abet worked to develop new textures, colors, and patterns to show the virtually limitless potential of its material as a new design medium, and even a participant in design. Silk-screened rather than digitally printed, the laminates could achieve a greater intensity of color and clarity of pattern than would otherwise be possible. When the fledgling MEMPHIS collaborative was formed, Abet became a key supporter, helping to underwrite the venture and supplying material for its products. Using an inexpensive material to make luxury products was part of the Memphis rebellion against consumer culture, and its introductory exhibition in i98ı was as much a showcase for Abet as for the collective. Barbara Radice, in her book about Memphis, called the materials “privileged vehicles of sensory communication...that cohabit the design without mixing,"'5 and the fluorescent-colored, squigglypatterned laminates drew as much notice as the skewed geometry of the furniture forms. Memphis and Abet was an ideal marriage of product and purpose: the riotously patterned laminates conveyed the ornament-loving approach of the postmodernists, and the products served as unpaid advertising vehicles for Abet, propelling its name to international recognition. In the first few years of the i98os, Postmodernism was becoming the defining style of the decade in architecture and furniture, but it would soon move into categories that had not been anticipated by either historians or the progenitors of the style. That move would be made by forward-thinking merchandisers who saw the potential for translating the new aesthetic into other mediums. One of the early, and smallest, firms to carry postmodern design beyond its first manifestations, Acme marketed affordably priced jewelry and accessories with lively colors and provocative patterns, enlisting the miost prominent names in the field to shrink their creative visions to a scale of inches. Acme Studios was started in i985 by Lesley Bailey and Adrian Olabuenaga, husband-and-wife jewelry entrepreneurs. New Zealand-born Bailey had a jewelry business in Sydney, Australia, and Buenos Aires native Olabuenaga had one in Los Angeles when they met, married, and opened a gallery there, selling Olabuenaga's furniture, jewelry, and colorful radio designs. The decision to form Acme Studios came about almost by chance. As the founder explains "My father was an architect, so I grew up around design. We saw that all the pieces were there... we just put them together."'6 Olabuenaga had long admired the ceramic teapots of PETER SHIRE (see under “Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), and when he learned that Shire. conveniently based in Los Angeles, was also designing for MEMPHIS, it seemed an opportunity too good to miss. Since Shire had already shown an interest in working in other materials, Olabuenaga proposed the idea of a jewelry collection, and the resulting twelve enameled metal brooches, each named for a different Los Angeles street, became the first products made by Acme Studios. Shire introduced the Acmé principals to ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") when they flew to New Orleans to hear him lecture. After admiring the new collection, Sottsass sketched out an idea for jewelry, which would develop into his first designs for an American firm. Sottsass then suggested that he invite his Memphis colleagues to also design some jewelry. The response was enthusiastic on both sides, and the result was the collection Memphis Designers for Acme, which debuted at the Cooper-Hewitt, National Design Museum in 1984. It included more than one hundred pieces by ANDREA BRANZI, ALDO CIBIC, MICHELE DE LUCCHI, NATHALIE DU PASQUIER. MARCO ZANINI (see all under "Design"), Ettore Sottsass and others, as well as—at Sottsass's suggestion—several by Olabuenaga himself. The collection became part of a Memphis exhibition that traveled around the US and helped spread the postmodernist message to an increasingly responsive American market. Curators, too, took note, and many of the Memphis Designers for Acme collection were acquired for several museums' permanent collections. The business started small, operating out of one bedroom of a rented house, with a closet serving as warehouse. In the years when architects were becoming superstars with international reputations, nothing helped that reputation spread beyond the design community as much as their ventures into the previously uncharted territory of product design. The pioneer in taking them in that direction was Alberto Alessi, who in i97o took over the company his grandfather had founded in ı92ı to produce nickel, chrome, and silver-plated brass household objects. He had just graduated from law school. "Being the first of my generation in the family," he says, “I knew from the beginning my destiny was to take care of the company."17 At the time, Alessi was a successful firm with a fine reputation, though not a particularly glamorous one. Alberto sought to change that, believing that it was just as important to appeal to consumers' aesthetic senses as to meet their practical needs—that even ordinary products could be beautiful. In a 1983 project for which he enlisted ALESSANDRO MENDINI (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") as “choreographer," twelve celebrated architects were commissioned to participate in what was called the Tea & Coffee Piazza, designing services that would be produced in sterling silver. The group included American postmodernists MICHAEL GRAVES (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design” and "Living with Postmodern Design"), ROBERT VENTURI (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), STANLEY TIGERMAN (see under “Architecture"), and CHARLES JENCKS (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), Italians Mendini, ALDO ROSSI (see under “Architecture"), and PAOLO PORTOGHESI (see under “Architecture") and to round out the internationál roster, Richard Meier\*, OSCAR TUSQUETS BLANCA (see under "Architecture"), Kazumasa Yamashita, HANS HOLLEIN (see under “Architecture")—and ARATA ISOZAKI (see under "Architecture"), who failed to complete his design on time. Mendini called the eleven results “micro-architecture" or "Domestic Landscapes," recalling the Museum of Modern Art exhibition of i972. Some were indeed landscapes, of geometric forms on land-like trays that created an architectural ensemble. Some were clearly postmodern, with overtly classical elements. Others were riffs on different styles, or basic architectural shapes, reflecting the eclecticism of an era when Postmodernism coexisted with late Modernism The invited designers were generally venturing into unfamiliar territory, and not all the results yielded coffee pots that poured well or were comfortable to hold. but all were highly original, and several carried messages with symbolic meaning. Robert Venturi followed his “more is more" approach by designing each object in a different historical style. Cleto Munari is the archetype of the design editor-entrepreneur. His commissioning and marketing of elite products played a key role in the dissemination and promotion of the postmodern style. Left to themselves, the architects whose decorative objects and jewelry he produced would never have ventured into areas so far removed from their customary practice. The Walt Disney Corporation would not generally be considered a promoter of any designs other than its own, but it was a surprisingly successful participant in the spread of postmodern architecture. In a happy coincidence for both sides. Postmodernism was the style of the moment in the years when Disney was expanding, and planning major building projects. Michael Eisner, a longtime architecture aficionado who became Disney's CEO ín i984, decided that famous architects could help market its brand identity and pursued the idea of developing "entertainment architecture," first for hotels, then corporate buildings, and finally an entire planned community. The roster of prominent names commissioned for Disney projects included postmodernists MICHAEL GRAVES (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design"), ARATA ISOZAKI (see under “Architecture"), ROBERT A. M. STERN (see under "Architecture"), FRANK GEHRY (see under “Architecture"), and ROBERT VENTURI and DENISE SCOTT BROWN (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design”). Since Postmodernism's double coding and its use of color and ornament were perfect companions to Disney's people-pleasing identity, the postmodernists designed buildings that were ideal projections of the Disney message, generating consumer approval and considerable publicity. There were specific guidelines: architects could recreate historic buildings (as had been done within the Disney theme parks), they could adapt storybook images, or they could devise images of their own that would capture the imagination or engage the emotions. All of this was very much in line with the ideas expressed in the Venturis' Learning from Las Vegas. The most crowd-pleasing result of the collaborations was Graves's Swan and Dolphin hotels in Orlando, completed in i99o. In an exuberant departure from staid luxury hotels, they eschewed gilt and mirrors in a fantasy environment that was warm, welcoming, and relatable to families with children. The Swan's attractions—a pair of 28-ton (25-tonne) turquoise rooftop swans, seashell fountains, pilasters in the form of potted palms, and parrot light fixtures—created irresistible inducements to guests to come in and have fun. Across a lake, the Dolphin had a similar blend of pastel color and whimsical decor. When Postmodernism was making news in the media, there were many advantages to appropriating the style for marketing activities. Formica was one of the earliest and most creative in making the most of the controversial—and attention-getting—new aesthetic. At the time, the company was a wellestablished and successful producer of popular-priced plastic laminates, for unglamorous applications like kitchens and coffee-shop countertops. It had developed a new product, ColorCore, a solid surfacing material with homogeneous color, allowing the material to be shaped without exposing the base material and eliminating the black edge that was a drawback of laminates. Seeking to establish Formica as a source for the design industry, the company's new marketing director Susan Grant Lewin decided to launch the product to a prestige market with a i973 competition called Surface and Ornament. The competition was announced with great fanfare, including a call-for-entries poster designed by former Museum of Modern Art curator of architecture and design Emilio Ambasz soliciting designers to submit objects using ColorCore. To ensure media-worthy results, high-profile participants FRANK GEHRY (see under “Architecture"), CHARLES MOORE (see under "Architecture"), Helmut Jahn\*, STANLEY TIGERMAN (see under “Architecture"), and JAMES WINES (see under “Architecture") were invited to submit designs as well. The submissions, as diverse as they were imaginative, showed ColorCore's ability to be routed, beveled, layered, sandblasted, or broken, and included an Art Deco-inspired elevator cab from Jahn and a zoomorphic fish lamp from Gehry, assembled from broken shards of the material. Tigerman's two-tone tête-à-tête lounge was probably the most published work, and Gehry's fish lamp the most enduring, though the competition was won by Jim Lewis and Clark Ellefson of Lewis+Clark\*. The Formica project elicited more than seven hundred entries, and the debut exhibition of the winning designs, at the Art Institute of Chicago in June i983, was covered by the New York Times and dozens of other publications. The winners were also showcased at Milan's Salone del Mobile, and the following year, Surface and Ornament II followed up on its success, awarding prizes to a kitchen design, a patterned table, a series of signs, and a bead necklace. Pursuing the postmodern aesthetic, Formica commissioned THOMAS BEEBY (see under “Architecture") to design its Merchandise Mart trade showroom in Chicago (i984). His sophisticated interpretation of postmodern classical elements was a high-fashion environment in bright pastel colors that underscored the company's move out of the kitchen and into in the designer market. Making the most of every opportunity, Lewin documented the imagebuilding activities in a i99ı book, Formica and Design: From the Counter Top to High Art. In his 1996 showroom for Formica, Tigerman used an intersecting "Grid Axonometric" of ColorCore to create the illusion of depth in a modest space. Formica toured the winning pieces in a five-year traveling exhibition called “Surface and Ornament" to cities in the United States and abroad—everywhere that the company's products were distributed—adding projects by local designers in each city and garnering considerable media coverage. Pieces from the exhibitions were thereafter donated to several major museums. Another i984 exhibition, at the Victoria and Albert Museum in London, called "Post Modern," featured one-off products by prominent-name designers, reasserting ColorCore's self-positioning as a material for architects and designers. The same year, a furniture exhibition opened at the Workbench Gallery in New York in which nineteen artisans who customarily worked in wood, including Garry Knox Bennett\*, Mitch Ryerson\*, and Wendell Castle\*, showed designs in ColorCore. When the exhibition closed, the Smithsonian's Renwick Gallery in Washington, DC, organized another touring exhibition. Unlike the other producers profiled in this book, the program for Formica used architects and designers to create products never intended for actual use, but only to enhance the company's image. In so doing, it contributed to the rise of the architect-as-brand-name, and gained attention for postmodern design in many countries where it had not yet been seen.
