Despite their professed admiration for pseudo-antique impryntynge', the Falkners owned the largest steam-presses in England. Not all old-style printing was done on such a low level of sophistication, but even very respectable firms had only a muddled notion of what was ‘old-style’ or‘antique’ or even (in some cases) ‘medieval’. The Gresham Steam Press, operated by the Unwin brothers, were among the first printers to follow the Chiswick Press's example in adopting Caslon type, yet in an 1862 type-specimen book they supplied this extraordinary note: ‘Type of the old style of face is now frequently used, more especially for the first class of books and ornamental works. The series in use at the Gresham Steam Press are from the original matrices which were cut at the beginning of the last century, and thus possess all the peculiarities of the mediaeval letter." It is easy to mock late-Victorian printers self-deluded by ‘old-style' pretensions, with their verbose colophons in imitation Middle English, their confused appeals to antiquity, and their sham paper and leather (stained, of course). Nor is it difficult to point out the inconsistency of their alleged commitment to the ideal of handicraft: the Falkners might display a hand-press in their device, but they were also notorious for owning the largest steam-driven presses in England. Nevertheless, old-style printers — and the publishers who patronized them — occasionally produced books which, while not possessing the massive authority of the Kelmscott volumes, still have considerable charm. The publisher Joseph Cundall, who was virtually forgotten until recently, issued a series of children's books, mainly in Caslon and often beautifully illustrated by Birket Foster and others, which are extremely handsome by any standard.²² Did Morris ever see them? We do not know, because he is silent about the Victorian books he considered worthy of admiration, but certainly Cundall, like Pickering, belongs to a very small group of nineteenth-century publisher-designers whose work may have influenced Morris. Likewise it would be fascinating to discover whether Morris was aware of the books published and printed by Andrew Tuer, whose Leadenhall Press (‘Ye Leadenhalle Presse' was the quaint version) in London issued many lively, amusing exercises in pastiche. The founder of the Paper and Printing Trades Journal as well as a wholesale stationer, Tuer avoided merely imitative period typography by a playful approach to book-design that at times reminds one of the zestful spirit of Francis Meynell in our own century.
