![](images/1d378d7d36b7a28efc0cf44bde37fc8e30243f0fe6c128f4b765928872dfe678.jpg) > Gertrud Vasegaard, glazed porcelain and bamboo lea service for Bing & Grøndahl, 1955-1957 Gertrud Vasegaard, stoneware vase, 1937 # gertrd agaardh101 10 Dm) ![](images/4122585abf879fee1b21f3bd570018a28aab42276972e60353ca4a044f807cf6.jpg) The Danish ceramicist Gertrud Vasegaard came from a family of potters who lived on the island of Bornholm. She studied at the Kunsthåndværkerskolen (School of Arts & Crafts) in Copenhagen from 1930 to 1932, and then trained in the ceramics studio of Axel Salto and Bode Willumsen (1895–1987) in Saxbo from 1932 to 1933. Following this she set up a studio with her sister, Lisbeth Munch-Petersen (1909–1997), in Gudhjem, Bornholm. She later established her own independent workshop/studio in 1936 in Holkadalen, Bornholm, where she produced high-guality handmade stoneware ceramics inspired by Oriental antecedents. The influence of Oriental art on Danish designers such as Vasegaard not only reflected Denmark's centuries-old sea-trading links with the Far East, but also a general respect for economical forms and high levels of craftsmanship. Between 1949 and 1959 Vasegaard worked as a designer (on both a freelance and full-time basis) for the Bing & Grøndahl ceramics factory in Copenhagen, where she executed a number of designs suitable for industrial mass production, including decorated bowis and vases. She also designed a porcelain dinner and tea service (1955–1957) for Bing & Grøndahl, which with its hexagonal teapot complete with a wicker handle and its curious flask-like tea-box was directly inspired by traditional Chinese forms. Awarded a gold medal at the 1957 (XI) Milan Triennale exhibition, this design predicted the mainstream taste in the 1970s for all things Oriental. Between 1959 and 1975, Vasegaard also designed a number of ceramics on a freelance basis for the Royal Copenhagen factory, including her stoneware Gemina tableware range decorated with an abstract Oriental-style pattern in Chinese blue. Like many other Scandinavian ceramicists working in industry, Vasegaard used traditional craft methods – hand throwing on a potter's wheel – to produce the prototypes for her mass-produced designs. Her choice of stoneware for the Gemina range ensured a slightly uneven texture that gave the service a hand-made effect. In 1959 she established a studio with her daughter, Myre Vasegaard (b. 1936), in Frederiksberg, and also worked with Aksel Rode (1905– 1989) until 1969. ![](images/6cdd01bed95913343230f3b6f599c16a15d293fa6436abcc51365121e1f78664.jpg)
