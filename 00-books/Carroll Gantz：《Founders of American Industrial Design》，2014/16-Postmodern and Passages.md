# Postmodern and Passages Finally, after 30 years, designers were tiring of the black boxes and white cubes of Bauhausstyle modern design. It was Italians who broke the ice in 1981, when an avant-garde Milanese group founded an organization called Memphis. It created the design media event of the year with garish furniture designs with bold colors and laminates that emphasized surface decoration more than function. Presented to the beat of rock music in the manner of a fashion show, the designs were deliberately designed to be antifunctional, preposterous, and faddish. They were colorful, cheerful, and playful, the direct opposite of Bauhaus traditions. One of the designs, the Casablanca sideboard, would become the icon of Memphis. By 1984, some U.S. educators were seeking new theories to free design from the formal dictates of so-called modern design. They came up with likening design to language, which they called “product semantics," defined as, “The study of the symbolic qualities of man-made forms in the context of their use and the application of this knowledge to industrial design. It takes into account not only the physical and physiological functions, but the psychological, social, and cultural context, which we call the symbolic environment." Got that? Although this was a highly academic and theoretical theory, many designers began to abandon the formal “good design” of the 1950s. Many new designs were being described as “postmodern," which meant that they had departed from the formal, Bauhaus-style, simple, geometric style to more informal, expressive, humanistic, and playful styles. These included cameras that suggested humanoid facial features, modern furniture that imitated 18th-century styles such as Sheraton or Chippendale, teakettles with a little bird whistle on the spout, a bathroom shower squeegee with S-shaped forms suggesting waves, and travel irons that looked like miniature steamships. In 1990, Alessi would introduce a lemon juicer called the Juicy Salif that looked like an insect and would become a cult object. In effect, design now offered an infinite variety of styles that could just as easily be based on traditional historical forms as those of future science fiction. The “ideal” style was that which best fit the specific individual product's function and usage and its primary audience, the consumer. Ironically, these stylistic modifications were little different conceptually from the highly criticized (by elite modernists of the 1950s) streamlining or skyscraper styles of the 1930s, which were simply 50 years ahead of their time! One can only wonder what the remaining pioneer machine designers of the 1930s thought of this deja-vu development. Unfortunately, many of them were disappearing. R. Buckminster Fuller, inventor of the geodesic dome, passed away at age 88 in 1983. Gordon W. Florian, who had started with General Electric in 1934, passed away in 1984 at age 75. In 1991, Business Week magazine sponsored the annual IDSA Industrial Design Excellence Awards (IDEA), then celebrating ten years of operation, and featured the awards in a major, illustrated article in the magazine. This annual recognition of industrial design in a major business magazine benefited both the manufacturers, the 800 to 1,000 design offices in the country, and the individual designers. The automobile industry rebounded in 1994 to capture 75 percent of the U.S. market share, and for the first time in 14 years led the world in car production. But many other corporations were in serious trouble. One hundred fifty-one Fortune 500 companies, or 30 percent, lost money in 1993. The previous decade had been a litany of mergers and acquisitions, as many companies sought to convert failing markets into cash. By this time, computer aided design (CAD) was driving the manufacturing, engineering, and industrial design of products. Installation of CAD technology was revolutionizing inhouse and consulting design firms across the industry. The historical restrictions of machine tools that controlled design were now replaced by computer-aided tooling that enabled designers to specify complex shapes without regard to those earlier physical restrictions. CAD proficiency was a prerequisite job requirement for all industrial designers and for anyone in the product development field. The days of hand-drawn renderings of product concepts were over, replaced by realistic computer-generated illustrations. In the March 20, 2000, issue of Time magazine, an article, "The Rebirth of Design," subtitled, “The Redesigning of America," claimed that form was the dominant characteristic of many product designs. Forms were soft, voluptuous, highly ergonomic and even exotic. The "black boxes" of the 1970s were definitely out. Bauhaus geometric shapes were out. The color and form of functional details such as keys, levers, and switches had become the new decorative ornamentation of the era. The turn of the century was a big year for retrospectives. By 2009, there were about 50,000 industrial designers, of which about 3,500 were members of IDSA. Median annual salaries were around \$55,000. This was at the depth of the severe financial recession, when unemployment reached 10 percent and Chrysler and General Motors received bailout funds from the government. But industrial design is traditionally a recessionproof profession, and in 2009 there were over 20 position advertisements on the IDSA website, and thousands more posted on the Internet. IDSA had a staff of 12, an annual budget of about \$5 million, and 27 chapters in five national districts. By 2009, in the annual International Design Excellence Awards, there would be 1,631 entries, and 66 of 150 awards would go to 15 countries other than the United States. In 2011, the U.S. Postal Service issued stamps honoring 12 of the nation's most important and influential industrial designers with an image of a design created by each designer. They were Frederick Hurten Rhead (1936 Fiesta dinnerware), Walter Dorwin Teague (1934 Baby Brownie camera); Norman Bel Geddes (1940 Patriot radio), Raymond Loewy (1934 pencil sharpener), Donald Deskey (1927 table lamp), Gilbert Rohde (1933 clock), Greta Von Nessen (1951 Anywhere lamp), Russel Wright (1950 Highlight/Pinchware tableware), Henry Dreyfuss (1937 Model 302 AT&T telephone), Peter Müller-Munk (1935 Normandie pitcher), Dave Chapman (1947 streamlined sewing machines), and Elliot Noyes (1961 Selectric typewriter). They are all gone now, those extraordinary designers of the Machine Age, and many are forgotten. Their proud legacy is today's prospering profession of industrial design, which they invented in the 1930s, practically out of thin air, by transforming our everyday environment of 19th-century, unwieldy, unattractive, mechanical devices, from automobiles to toasters, office equipment, and kitchens, into sleek and attractive works of art that enhance our daily experiences emotionally and visually, as well as functionally. Today, we regard our man-made physical environment of mechanical, electronic, and digital devices as an unremarkable and a natural result of modern technology, as if they magically appeared out of nowhere. But in 1930, neither technology nor engineering knew how to shape the products it produced by the millions into forms that were designed to address human convenience, utilization, or emotional needs that made them attractive and desirable to consumers. Ordinary artists, the Machine Age designers of the 1930s, bravely ventured from their comfortable lives into a new world of complex, dirty factories, business offices, and brutal sales competition in order to transform everyday products into modern design, which encouraged people in the depths of the Depression to visualize a better future and lifted a failing manufacturing industry into profitability. A recovered economy led to the highest standard of living in the world after World War II, and the machine age designers established an educational system and business models for the new profession that became integrated into the modern world of manufacturing, production, and sales. Today's industrial designers perform the same services for manufacturers and consumers that the machine designers of the 1930s did, only better and faster. The process remains the same, except that instead of hand-drawn illustrations of proposed designs and manually prepared mechanical drawings, computers now perform these tasks with the instructions of designers. Prototype models are often not built by hand but generated into three dimensions by computers. Contrary to many pessimists in the 1960s, who concluded that all consumer products had already been “designed" to perfection, technology continues to generate new products that need to be shaped and modified for human use and understanding, and this is likely to continue indefinitely. Many of the organizations and designers mentioned in this book ceased operations over the years due to death or business closure, but, surprisingly, quite a few of the design organizations of the founders survive to this day, a tribute to the tenacity of their founders and a source of pride to their heirs. They continue to design the world about us. In 2006, Walter Dorwin Teague Associates, founded in 1926, now called Teague, in Seattle, Washington, celebrated its 80th anniversary as a leading force in industrial design and its 60-year working relationship with Boeing, with a Frank Gehry-designed Experience Music Project held in downtown Seattle, and an elegant publication of Teague history and designs, Design This Day, the same title as his 1940 book. Teague staff in 2012 totaled 250 designers and support staff. This is a pretty good survival record of design firms founded by designers of the Machine Age. We also have their classic, iconic designs, their books, and perhaps most of all, their legacy of today's creative, innovative, and successful industrial design profession, which continues to shape our physical environment of tools, machines, furnishings and equipment in a manner appropriate to our use, needs, and desires. We will continue to be inspired by the courage of the first machine designers to envision the modern world when there was none, and to implement it into reality despite the Great Depression of the 1930s. It was an extraordinary accomplishment that is unlikely to ever be repeated. The guidebook of the 1939 World's Fair probably said it better than anyone could: The true poets of the twentieth century are the designers, architects, and the engineers who glimpse some inner vision, create some beautiful figment of the imagination, and then translate it into valid reality for the world to enjoy.⁴
