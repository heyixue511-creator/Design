# FRANK GEHRY b.1929 CALIFORNIA ICONOCLAST WHOSE WORK MOVED FROM VERNACULAR TO DECONSTRUCTIVIST. AND BEYOND. Though he would not now be thought of as a postmodernist, Frank Gehry's approach to architecture, particularly his early work, justifies his inçlusion in a compendium of that era. Gehry was also critical of the sameness of International Style Modernism, and his early work suggested vernacular influences similar to those seen in the buildings by ROBERT VENTURI (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design”).'9 His designs have always challenged convention, particularly through the use of quotidian materials corrugated alumnum and chain-link fencing for his Santa Monica home (i978), ordinary cardboard for his Easy Edges furniture, or ColorCore lamnate for his award-winning fish sculptures and lamps. Even his later architecture, with undulating computer generated forms like those of the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao (ı997) and the D)ISNEY (see under "Edited Design") Concert Hall (2oo3), is sometimes designated postmodern, despite the absence of visible historicist influences. Ephraim Owen Goldberg was born in Toronto, Canada; the family changed their surname when they emigrated to Los Angeles in i947. When he became interested in architecture, Frank (the name he adopted in his school years) drove a truck to pay for art classes, and attended the University of Southern California on a scholarship, receiving his degree in 1954. He worked for the architectural firm Victor Gruen Associates before spending a year in the US Army Special Services, making furniture for soldiers. Enrolling at Harvard Graduate School of Design in 1956, Gehry studied city planning for a year, but left to return to California. In 1962 he opened Frank Gehry & Associates (now Gehry Partners) in Los Angeles. Though his early work was influenced by International-Style precepts, Gehry shared the rebelliousness of the active Los Angeles arts community and was one of the new wave of California architects and designers who challenged establishment ideas in their inventive use of materials and unconventional forms. His first success was in 1971, not in architecture but with Easy Edges, a collection of furniture assembled from glued layers of corrugated paper. It upended notions of the materials from which furniture could be made, and was successful enough to merit a reprise collection, Experimental Edges, which debuted in ı979. Gehry's use of ordinary packing material accorded with CHARLES JENCKS's (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") designation of the ad-hoc strain of Postmodernism. Gehry's first prominent architectural project was his own home in Santa Monica, a 192os bungalow that he remodeled in i978 (and expanded in i99ı) by encasing it in an exploded structure of rough plywood and corrugated metal, exorcizing what the architect described as the "ghosts of cubism" with a convention-defying design that placed him in the front ranks of the newly designated Deconstructivist movement. Deconstructivism was considered by many to be an offshoot of Postmodernism's espousal of quotidian materials and rule-breaking forms. Though eschewing the postmodern use of color and historicist motifs, the house was a clear break with the form-follows-function tenets of orthodox Modernism. It was followed by other radical projects, including the Winton Guest House (i987) in Owatonna, Minnesota, an assemblage of geometric forms in varied materials that cleariy suggests a postmodern sensibility. The originality of his work brought Gehry international renown and, in i989, the coveted Pritzker Prize. His product designs have drawn equal attention, most notably the quirky fish lamps of Formica ColorCore, originally conceived as part of a commission for which FORMICA (see under "Edited Design") invited designers to use the new material. In the often-reprinted story, Gehry was frustrated in his efforts to come up with something and threw a large piece of ColorCore on the floor, where it shattered into fragments of translucent color, inspiring him to use it as layers of fish scales.²0 Moving from drafting-table to computergenerated imagery, Gehry's work changed completely with the organic forms of the Weisman Art Museum (i99o) in Minneapolis, Minnesota, prefiguring his iconic Guggenheim Museum Bilbao (1997). The witty Chiat/Day building (1991) in Venice, California, with its famous binocularsculpture entrance by Claes Oldenburg and Coosje van Bruggen, and the so-called Dancing House in Prague (i996) are postmodern in their doublecoded visual messages, but it is the undulating forms and polished titanium panels of his later work that have plačed him at the forefront of the "starchitect” generation.
