# MASSIMO IOSA GHINI b.1959 BOLOGNA-BORN ARCHITECT AND DESIGNER KNOWN FOR HIS DYNAMIC, STREAMLINED FORMS. Postmodernism is not all about jagged-edged design and disconcertingly clashing colors. It also includes designs that are unquestionably avantgarde but sinuously curvilinear and subtly hued: witness the work of Massimo Iosa Ghini, whose contributions to the postmodern vocabulary are very different from those of his contemporaries, and impossible not to recognize. His distinctive aesthetic of sweeping, asymmetrical curves and dynamic furniture forms evoke both Futurism and Streamline style Bologna, known as the source of some ot Italy's finest cuisine, seerns an appropriate birthplace for an original like losa Ghini. His education followed fairly conventional lines; he studied architecture in Florence, graduated from the Politecnico di Milano in i98t, and opened his own studio that year. Departing from the orthodox career track, however, he began working as an artist and illustrator for several periodicals, and his first recognition was as a comic book illustrator By 1985, Iosa Ghini had become interested in the Italian avant-garde, founding the Bolidismo group with Pierangelo Caramia\*, Stefano Giovannoni, and other Florentine designers. Bolidismo focused on speed as a dominant element óf design and believed that through an emphasis on sensory perceptions, objects could have an emotional relation to their users. Some of the most expressive—and most refined— objects of the postmodern era are the work of this celebrated Japanese designer, who combined craft and technology in furniture that won accolades from museum curators and connoisseurs for its fusion of Japanese minimalism with Western ornamentation. The intriguing names he gave them enhance the visual appeal of his designs, which include two chairs that are among the most admired objects of twentieth-century furniture Kuramata studied woodcraft and design at Tokyo Polytechnic, graduating in ı953, and worked for furniture producer Teikokukizai before returning to school to study interior design at Kuwasawa Design School, where he became familiar with Western aesthetics. After completing his studies he worked for the department stores San-Ai and Matsuya before opening his own practice in 1965 He was part of the new generation of architects and designers who brought international recognition to Japanese design in the decades after the Second World War, not only helping Japan move out from under the shadow of their defeat but establishing a tradition-bound country as a fertile source of modern design. During his prolific career, Kuramata designed the interiors of more than three hundred bars and restaurants, but became far more celebrated for his innovative furniture designs, which show a particular interest in transparency. He used simple materials like wire mesh, aluminum, acrylic, and glass, working them in unexpected ways into forms that transcended function. The leaning towards minimalism reflects his Japanese heritage, but his fondness for ornament was drawn from Western precedents. Kuramata's earliest and simplest exploration of transparency, the Glass Chair (i976), was an armchair of solid glass slabs secured with a glue that made it almost invisible. More experimental, and instantly iconic, were How High the Moon (i986), which rendered a classic club chair in steel mesh to gave the massive form a look of weightlessness, and the ethereal Miss Blanche (i988), an entirely original piece with paper roses floating in an armchair of thick Lucite. The latter is perhaps his most famous design, and has set sales records at auction. Kuramata contributed to MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") collections, but his designs are notably more restrained than those of his colleagues; the Kyoto and Nara tables embed bits of broken colored glass in concrete for a terrazzo effect, and the Sally table wraps broken glass in a metal frame. During the Memphis years, Kuramata was also designing boutiques for celebrated Japanese avant-garde fashion designer Issey Miyake, who shared his experimental sensibility.
