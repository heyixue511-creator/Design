Andrea Branzi does not consider himself a postmodernist, although for more than two decades he participated in every important Italian avant-garde group, and so helped to chart the course of progressive Modernism in twentiethcentury design. Among the most versatile of his contemporaries, he believes he is more researcher than designer, a student of technology and particularly of nature, the latter of which has informed his work over a career lasting almost six decades. “Elements from nature are always unique," he says, "Nature doesn't repeat itself; industrial materials will never achieve the same thing."9 Branzi was trained as an architect, but has focused his attention on design experiments and objects rather than buildings. He was born in Florence and graduated from the faculty of architecture at the University of Florence in i965. The following year he joined with Gilberto Corretti, Paolo Deganello\*, and Massimo Morozzi to found Archizoom Associati\*, organizing the conventionflouting “Superarchitettura" exhibition and developing Pop art designs including the seating units called Superonda (iı967) and Safari (i968). Branzi opened his design studio in Milan in ı973 and, while pursuing his own projects, was also associated with Global Tools\* and Alchimia\*, two other radical groups. For Alchimia he contributed to the Bau.Haus I and Il collections of experimental furniture (1979–8o) and the following year designed experimental pieces for Alchimia as he becarne a participant in the newly formed MEMPHIS (see under "Edited Design") group. He also designed decorative objects for CLETO MUNARI (see under “Edited Design") and jewelry for ACME (see under “Edited Design"), as well as furniture for several other producers. He is a three-time winner of the coveted Compasso d'Oro, Italy's most prestigious design award. Aside from his design work, Branzi has been an influential force through other activities: he was a co-organizer of “Il design italiano degli anni '50," an important 1977 exhibition in Milan that showcased midcentury Italian design, chronicling its movement away from conventional Modernism to avant-garde design. He became involved with the most influential design publications, writing for Casabella from 1972 to 1976 and directing Modo from 1982 to 1984, and has been a contributor to Domus since 198o. He coordinated the International Design section of the i5th Milan Triennial, and participated with personal shows in the following two triennials as well as in several Venice Biennales. Branzi's role as an educator includes years of teaching at the Politecnico di Milano, where he headed the School of Interior Design, and ![](images/10b662f2a77af8c931ce7af99bf5b97e69423b9741e09ea0a1fda92508d4cfb1.jpg) he was co-founder in i983 (with ALESSANDRO MENDINI; see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") of the postgraduate design school Domus Academy. His book The Hot House (i984) recounts the history of progressive design movements in Italy, primarily in the decades following the Second World War, highlighting the dramatic changes that were radically reshaping the domestic landscape. He noted the need to “[put] together a different domestic culture...recovering a system of ties and functions that cannot be explained in purely ergonomic or functional terms."o As his work indicates, Branzi seeks a close connection between man and his environment. Though Branzi has reprised his use of raw tree trunks in recent designs, his design vocabulary has consistently evolved, as he continues to explore new themes and introduce more emotional elements: "You can't spend your life doing the same kind of project: design should not solve problems, but should open new ones." Alhough best known for his connection to MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") and ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design"), Aldo Cibic differed from his colleagues in that iconoclastic venture. His designs are more subdued, as though reluctant to demand attention, and when he left the collaborative he moved in an altogether different direction. A native of Vicenza, Cibic moved to Milan in i979 and began working with Sottsass, who became his mentor in the study of design. The only Memphis collaborator without an academic background, Cibic was in effect an apprentice who trained on the job to become a designer. Like the others who converged in the nucleus of the postmodern movement, he felt constrained by the rigidity of functionalist design, responding with enthusiasm to the siren song of the avantgarde. Cibic worked with MATTEO THUN and MARCO ZANINI when all three were employed by Sottsass Associati and with them became a founding member of Memphis in 198o. He designed pieces like the Belvedere console (ı982) and the Cabbage Pepper Radish ceramic tea service (ı985), both using black-and-white graphics rather than the livelier colors most associated with Memphis designs. Despite his contributions to the Memphis collections, Cibic functioned primarily as an organizer and administrator rather than a designer. After Memphis disbanded, he established Cibic & Partners in 1989 and implemented his own ideas with a new furniture collection and an entirely different approach. He felt that Memphis had become an “indigestion of forms."1² Instead of individual objects with distinct appearances, Cibic developed what he called "families" of basic everyday pieces, which were produced and marketed under the trade name "Standard." The furniture was clean-lined and well-proportioned, with a look that was contemporary but of no specific identifiable style. The logo for the venture was a jovial JAVIER MARISCAL-designed figure, suggesting that the pieces, though simple, had personality and flair. The Cibic firm expanded its scope, assembling a team of architects, interior designers, graphic designers, industrial designers, and a network of collaborating consultants, and has since designed furniture and accessories for a variety of companies as well as handling architecture and interior design projects. Cibic's professional interests also include teaching: ![](images/f891946e0667ce0928ffd9b0d05d77c2a3721f976785597efa181dbc79c73ceb.jpg) at Domus Academy, and in the industrial design programs at the Politecnico di Milano and at the Università luav di Venezia. In contrast to the activities of the partnership, another group, the Cibic Workshop, in Vicenza, was established primarily to focus on research into the relationship between design and society. Concerning his work ethic, Cibic has said, "I'm always looking for less professional plans and more poetic ones."3 Spoken like a true visionary. OPPOSITE Caffè Bongo interior. ![](images/e1f5acc21d8b2f26bdb9ff27fb3a0e31878aa400c94351cf8b11a6d9b4e17b27.jpg) Nigel Coates is an architect, theorist, and educator whose idiosyncratic style was enthusiastically received by lapanese clients before it was appreciated in his native England. His storytelling approach, more closely akin to that of fashion and theatrical designers than of his architecture colleagues, has produced extravagant and immersive spaces that inject historic replication into fantasy environments. The almost indescribable variety of his designs places him somewhere between Postmodernism and its offshoot, Deconstructivism. Coates originally planned to study engineering, but in 1972, after graduating from the University of Nottingham, he entered London's Architectural Association, studying under Bernard Tschumi\*, and later won a scholarship to the University of Rome. Inspired by the work of the Italian Radicals, he sought to move beyond architecture to design buildings and interiors that were closer to experiences than objects. He was intrigued by London nightclubs, which, like avant-garde theater, erased the separation between audience and performing space. His controversial approach, developed with several of his students at the Architectural Association, where Coates had been teaching since 1977, led to the formation of the NATO group (Narrative Architecture Today) in i983. Brash and ambitious, the group saw itself at the frontiers of architecture, staging exhibitions and publishing an eponymous magazine that showcased narrativedriven designs inspired by art, fashion, and the aforementioned nightclubs. NATO believed that buildings should relate to their users, viewing architecture as a language drawn from everyday experience. In 1984 a Chinese developer saw Coates's Kensington flat, a whimsical space that evoked a Venetian palazzo (and was at the time his only built project) in a magazine and invited him to Tokvo to design. At the time, Cóates recalls, "London was, for them [the [apanese] the most fashionable place on the planet,"'4 and a British architect was a desirable import. Moreover, they were as fascinated by history as they were by avant-garde ideas—making them ideal clients for an unconventional designer. Coates partnered with Doug Branson as Branson Coates Architecture to produce the Metropole restaurant (i986), an elegantly theatrical environment embellished with archaeological references. Caffè Bongo, built the same year, was a sculpted space evoking both ancient Rome and current fashion in a commentary on the passage of time, reportedly inspired by Federico Fellini's flm La dolce vita. Its undulating walls enclosed diversc and unrelated elements like classical columns, a jet engine, and historical figurines. Both projects, which would have been far too extreme for London, were enthusiastically received in Japan. Coates's frst total building project, L'Arca di Noè (ı988), was a Sapporo restaurant and bar suggesting a landlocked Noah's Ark. Another celebrated project, The Wall, was a multi-use structure suggesting a remnant of ancient Rome that had been excavated in modern Tokyo. Coates's provocative, even bizarre, designs were widely published, and made his reputation back home. In i986 the Victoria and Albert Museum dubbed Coates's style "New Baroque," grouping him with André Dubreuil, Tom Dixon, and Ron Arad in an exhibition featuring the London avant-garde. Though happy to be considered nonconformist, Coates did not necessarily think of himself as postmodern—even commenting, "postmodernists were the enemies"'5—until he was designated as such by the V&A. Like others so labeled, Coates uses classical language in rebellious ways, but departs from them in drawing from other areas of culture and society.
