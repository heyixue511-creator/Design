Johann Wolff, who had previously worked in the Copenhagen potteries, initially managed the works, which during his two-year appointment began manufacturing cobalt blue and white faïence. During the early 18th century, Rörstrand became especially noted for its bianco sopra bianco (white on white) painting. Later in 1790 the factory began producing an English-style cream-glazed earthenware known as Flint-porslin. In 1782 the factory acquired its main competitor, Mariebergs Porslinfabrik, and in 1873 established the Arabia factory in Finland in order to take advantageof the burgeoning Russian market. Throughout most of the 19th century, Rörstrand manufactured ceramic designs that were based on successful models developed by other factories, such as its well-known Blå Blom service(1870), which emulated an earlier English design. It was not until 1895, when Alf Wallander (1862-1914)was appointed its art director, that the factory began producing truly original and innovative ceramics of its own. Primarily responsible for the introduction of the Art Nouveau style into the factory's œuvre, Wallander designed numerous vases decorated with highly stylized motifs of flowers and leaves or abstracted female figures painted in subtle dusky under-glazed shades of pink, blue and brown. These Art Nouveau designs were distinguished by the use of soft modelling and melting organic forms. Wallander also designed a number of sculptural high-relief vases that were stylistically inspired by Japanese ceramics, such as his Magpie in a Tree vase of 1897(now in the National Museum, Stockholm). His designs for Rörstrand were awarded a Grand Prix at the 1900 Paris “Exposition Universelle et Internationale”, and were also retailed by Siegfried Bing(1838–1905)in his influential Paris store, L'Art Nouveau. Other designers, such as Anna Katarina Boberg (1864-1935), Karl Lindström (1865–1936)andNils Erik Lundström(1865– 1960) also modelled Art Nouveau style vases for the company. During this period, Rörstrand diversified its output by manufacturing various art ceramics, including figurines of animals and birds. In 1926 Rörstrand moved from Stockholm to Gothenburg, and four years later exhibited at the landmark 1930 Stockholm Exposition. The factory's most notable exhibit was a domestic tableware range designed by Louise Adelborg(1885-1971)decorated with a simple repeating pressed pattern of ears of wheat. In 1931, GunnarNylund(1904-1989)was appointed Rörstrand's artistic director, and the following year the factory's operations were expanded to include a new site in Lidköping. Five years later the whole factory was relocated there. During the post-war period, Rörstrand produced simple stoneware designs by, among others, Gunnar Nylund and Hertha Bengtson (1921–1995), which reflected the contemporary taste for abstract organic forms. During the 1950s the factory also produced a number of informal tableware ranges designed by Marianne Westman (b. 1925), includingMon Amie (1951), Pomona (1956), and Picknick (1956), which incorporated colourful printed patterns of highly-stylized flowers, vegetables and fish. The Danish architect and designer Anton Rosen wasthe principal architect for the Landsudstillingen (Danish National Exhibition) held in Århus in 1909, and was also responsible for the design of the Palace Hotel in Copenhagen (1907–1910), which was intended to provide luxurious accommodation for officials visiting the nearby Rådhuset (Town Hall). For this latter project, he designed site-specific furniture, including a mahoganyarmchair (now in the collection of the Danske Kunstindustrimuseum in Copenhagen), which incorporated decorative metal fittings that shared a similar aesthetic to Johan Rohde's stylized floral designs for Georg Jensen. Rosen would certainly have been aware of the metalwork being produced by Georg Jensen, as later in 1917 hedesigned a ceremonial silver cup for the Butchers' Guild that the silversmithy executed for him. This unique design was richly encrusted with hand-sculpted figurative elements and incorporated decorative seals suspended from stylized rams heads and an Arts & Crafts style band of galleons with billowing sails. Around this time, Rosen also designed various other silver objects for Georg Jensen that were notable for their use of coral, malachite, amber and other semi-precious stones. During the 1920s, however, he adopted a less ornate style, as demonstrated by his celadon green glazed tobacco jar designed in 1925. Like other Danish designers at the time, Rosen was influenced on the one hand by the emergence of Modernism in Western Europe, and on the other by the art of Japan. With his well-craftedand thoughtfully detailed work, which formed the transition between the New Art of the early 20th century and the Modern Movement, Rosen sought to enhance the beauty of everyday living while reviving a national Danish identity in the handcrafts and design. ![](images/c9ef103f95e5aea0db095c740641be57475e9372fd5219b519ce67d2120f9907.jpg)
