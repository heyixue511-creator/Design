Its chief spokesman was the designer-writer Alessandro Mendini, and its main protagonists included EttoreSottsass Jr., Paola Navone, Andrea Branzi, and Michele de Lucchi. The practice of Alchemy' implied a pursuit of the quest to turn base substances into gold, a philosophy which evoked considerable controversy and critical attention in much of the group's work which centred on the idea of 'banality': ordinary, everyday consumer objects were transformed, or re-designed, into objects of aesthetic contemplation through the application of colour, pattern, and additional elements. Through this process they harnessed the potential of what Sottsass described asnon-cultural imagery', that is, imagery without conventional aesthetic status. Their output ranged from individual items of furniture through to total environments or performances and was often highly decorative, drawing upon references from Braun to Bauhaus and from kitsch to Kandinsky in the exploration of the communicative boundaries of design. However, as the 197os drew to a close there were increasing tensions between Mendini and Sottsass. Sottsass wished to explore the potential impact of a richer design syntax than Mendini's concerns with‘redesign' appeared to allow, as they were confined to what might be seen as the laboratory-like isolation of the experimental studio and confines of the exhibition gallery. For Sottsass the use of imagination and a rich visual language in design was both a means of enhancing the consumer experience and of persuading manufacturers to re-examine their understanding of the aesthetic possibilities inherent in everyday products. Notions of homogenization, standardization, and the production of universal models for global markets had been very much bound up in the notion of Fordism. In the Post-Fordist era, designers and manufacturers could turn their attention to the opportunities afforded by batch production, flexible manufacturing systems (FMS), or computeraided manufacture (CAM). This allowed for a greater diversity of product types and enabled producers to satisfy more easily the varied cultural inclinations and semantic values of different consumer groups. The Memphis design group was founded by Sottsass [128] and Michele De Lucchi in Milan in 1981, with the help of a Milanese cabinet-maker, Renzo Brugola, and Mario and Brunella Godani, who had a showroom. Financial backing was provided by the lighting designer and president of Artemide, Ernesto Gismondi, together with the industrialist, Fausto Celati. The group's activities and exhibitions provided a vital fulcrum for experimental designdebate for two or three years, stimulating the design world in a positive fashion. Apart from Sottsass himself none of the founder members was over 3o years of age. Like a number of other Italian twentieth-century avant-garde design groups, Memphis was originally Milan-based, but it went on to include members from other countries including Japan, the United States, Austria, Britain, and France thus becoming a standard-bearer of the New International Style', as was discussed in relation to the globalization of culture in Chapter 6. The title Memphis' has many perhaps apocryphal stories attached to it. For instance, it has been claimed by Barbara Radice, a founder member of the group, that it stemmed from the repeated playing on Sottsass's record player of a Bob Dylan song, Stick Inside of Mobile with the Memphis Blues Again. She maintained that the formative members of the group present felt that the name Memphis reflected the rich creative possibilities afforded by the ability to draw on cultural experiences of all kinds, whether the American blues, Tennessee, and rock' n' roll, or ancient Egypt and the capital of the Pharaohs. Whether or not Radice's account is entirely accurate such diverse cultural provenances contribute to the vitality of form, surface, and vocabulary associated with the group. Memphis patterns derived from the mundane printed plastic laminated surfaces of counters in suburban coffee bars, from American consumer culture, Art Deco, Hollywood or ancient civilizations; materials could range from the cheap and ordinary to the expensive; colour was another vital ingredient and, like the surface decoration deployed, was a linguistic and expressive means of indicating references to cultural experience of all kinds. The significance of Memphis lay in the ways in which many different elements came together in fresh and surprising ways, reminiscent in many ways of Isadore Ducasse/Comte de Lautréamont's Surrealist adage,the chance encounter of a sewing machine and an umbrella on a dissection table': Memphis embraced the meeting of cheap and expensive materials, of mass- and high-cultural references, of invigorating colour combinations and new forms. Furthermore, as Radice wrote in 1984: Memphis, like fashion, works on the fabric of contemporaneity, and contemporaneity means computers, electronics, videogames, science-fiction comics, Blade Runner, Space Shuttle, biogenetics, laser bombs, a new awareness of the body, exotic diets and banquets, mass exercise and tourism. Mobility is perhaps the most macroscopic novelty of this culture. Not only physical mobility but also and above all mobility of hierarchies and values; and mobility of interpretations which has liquefied the contours and solidity of things, shrinking the long, lazy waves of our 'Weltanschauung' to increasingly high frequencies until they burst and evaporate in a dust of hypotheses, in a world of events. What matters to us is not their substance but their appearance, their virtual image.29 The group worked across a range of media and products: clocks, textiles, furniture, glass, ceramics, flooring, and interiors all attracted their attention. Its members also believed that design was part of the fashion process. However, its very success ensured that many of its ideas were taken up by countless imitators, thus engendering the very commercial conformity from which its participants had sought to escape. Indeed, as one trenchant critic wrote in the late 198os: The visual promise of simplicity—though the ideological and technical bases ofMemphis designs are far from simple—has since the early eighties spawned a multitude of imitators who consider themselves to be designers'. Ever since the English poet William Blake attacked Sir Joshua Reynolds's ‘world of reason' in the late eighteenth century a succession of articulate writershave attacked the spiritual andcreative consequences of the division of labour. William Morris's misgivings about many aspects of the industrialization process in the mid-nineteenth century helped sustain the enduring belief held by many that the hand-made article is superior to the machine-made, both in terms of aesthetics and function. The crafts provided an experimental arena in which new possibilities could be explored, avenues of investigation which weregenerally closed down by the structure and outlook of manufacturing industry. All Memphis pieces were, with the exception of blown glass, intended as prototypes for industrial production, and the group always allied itself to the potentiality of industrial production to produce goods on a scale capable of satisfying the respective desires and imaginations of a variety of consumers. But craft skills were seen as an important vehicle for experimentation and exploration of materials, surfaces, and forms. To this end Sottsass had brought into the Memphis circle his Milanese cabinet-making friend, Renzo Brugola, who was encouraged to help lend material substance to the new ideas of Sottsass and his colleagues. Similar tendencies can be discerned in the graphic work of Javier Mariscal inSpain, although his work also extended into other design media such astextiles, lighting, interiors, and furniture, in connection with which Ettore Sottsass invited him to participate in the Memphis exhibition of 1981. A radical spirit could also be discerned in British street-style magazines such as i-D, published, edited, and art-directed by Terry Jones from 198o, and The Face, art-directed by Neville Brody between 1981 and 1986. Both designers were highly influential in their abandonment of conventional grid-base layouts, combining all kinds of surface marks with varieties of type and letter-forms and exploring the expressive possibilities of different textures and decorative motifs. Initially motivated by the vitality and crudeness of Punk, Neville Brody, in the eclectic manner of post-modern design, also drew upon the collagiste, photomontage, and typographic experimentation of Dada, Russian Constructivism, the Bauhaus, De Stijl, and other avant-garde trends of interwar modernism as a means of revitalizing the page.
