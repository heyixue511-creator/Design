She took little interest in the activities of the Press and had almost nothing to do with its daily operation. However, in the case of Wilfrid Scawen Blunt's The Love-Lyrics & Songs of Proteus she read and criticized both manuscript and proofs, conveyed messages between her husband and the author, and in general carried the responsibilities that were later to be borne by Cockerell in overseeing the work through the Press. The explanation for this departure from her usual behaviour is that she had been, for nearly a decade, Blunt's mistress — a fact that lends peculiar colour to the story of how the Proteus volume came to be printed at the Kelmscott Press. The relationship between Blunt and Morris's wife was first revealed in a biography of Blunt by Lady Longford published in 1979 and was described more fully a year later by Peter Faulkner in a lecture to the William Morris Society.28 Both of these accounts are based on Blunt's voluminous diaries in the Fitzwilliam Museum, to which scholars had earlier been denied access. Despite the massive quantity of words recorded in the diaries, Blunt strikes one as an enigmatic figure whose motives and inner life defy analysis. He was in many respects an old-fashioned Tory squire, a Member of Parliament (briefly), a traveller, a writer of some reputation, and a fearless opponent ofimperialism at the zenith of the British Empire. And he was devastatingly handsome and charming. Initiated as a young man into the mysteries of sex by Catherine Walters (Skittles’), the most celebrated Victorian courtesan, Blunt thereafter romped amorously through life, leaving behind him a trail of seduced women, outraged husbands threatening divorce suits, and numerous illegitimate offspring. These adulterous affairs, even when they produced some of his most serious and intense love poetry, often had comic overtones: when Blunt was visiting Kelmscott Manor, a jealous Morris would from time to time burst into the room where Blunt and Jane were sitting, and midnight assignations at Kelmscott were made more hazardous than usual by creaking floors. Blunt's bizarre career as an amorist inevitably calls to mind the adventures of Byron's Don Juan, and it seems wonderfully appropriate that in 1869 he married Byron's granddaughter, who mysteriously tolerated her husband's erotic adventures until they at last separated in 1906. ![](images/2427a0d380f184e7bfaba6f1239018536e8d18173d4c58ec46dc7f0ce1975d3c.jpg) Figure 68 Wilfrid Scawen Blunt as a parliamentary candidate for Camberwell in 1885. Politician, cxplorer, poet, and womanizer, Blunt persuaded Morris to publish his poems as the third book printed by the Kelmscott Press. It must be emphasized that Blunt was not a cold-hearted villain who had stepped out of a Victorian melodrama. He was unusually kind to women and wrote high-minded poems about his affairs, which in time were generally transformed into solid friendships — as was to be true of his relationship with Jane Morris. Throughout his life Blunt oscillated dizzily between manic hedonism and devout Roman Catholicism. After his worst debauches he often went on retreats or tried to confess his sins to any eminent Catholic churchman he encountered: Manning, Newman, on one memorable occasion even Pope Leo XIII himself. Despite frequent introspection, especially during his more religicus phases, Blunt was never able to justify or explain to himself the sexual obsessions that dominated his life. The nearest he came to doing so in his diaries is this revealing passage, in which, significantly, he falls back on a gambling metaphor: Friendship plain and simple with a woman is to me an impossibility. It is not that in theory I should not delight in such a friend. I have often wished for one, but in practice and in spite of all possible good will I find it freezes me. If I do not make love, I don't know what to do or say and she is bored and I am bored and insensibly the friendship and all else withers away. I lose my interest in a game where there is nothing staked at all to win or lose. It is not that I want much but there must be just something."9 Blunt met Jane Morris in 1883 and her husband a year later. Exactly when she joined Blunt's stable of mistresses is not clear, nor do we know precisely when their sexual relationship subsided into a generous, mutually satisfying friendship. Blunt preserved the 145 letters Jane wrote to hiın between 1884 and 1913, but for the most part they are oddly formal (the two lovers never address each other by first name) and tell us only what is self-evident: that she felt great affection for Blunt and was flattered by his attentions.3° Blunt's diaries - our other chief source of information - are slightly more helpful and contain accounts of his love-making' (a phrase that he uses in both the Victorian and modern senses) in the Morris household. It is obvious that Blunt was attracted to Jane partly because she was one of the legendary beauties of the nineteenth century, partly because she had earlier been the mistress of his hero Rossetti, and partly because she was the wife of a man he admired very deeply. Seducing the wife of Morris may seem a peculiar way to honour him, but Blunt's mind always moved in a mysterious way. Faulkner has suggested that the romantic entanglement reached its peak in 1889; certainly Blunt's references to Jane in his diaries during the 1 89os are elegaic in tone, speaking of both her beauty and their love in the past tense. When Morris died in 1896, Blunt called at Hanmersmith to pay his respects (5 October). The coffin lay in Morris's bedroom on the ground floor; upstairs he found Jane reclining on the sofa as usual, not — he emphasized - wearing black, and he kissed her which I had not done for long and comforted her as well as I could'. Jane's words, as recorded by Blunt, were chilling: I am not unhappy . . . though it is a terrible thing, for I have been with him since I first knew everything. I was 18 when I married — but I never loved him.' Blunt, always resourceful, seized the occasion to persuade Jane to join him in Egypt that winter — something he had been urging her to do for years, since he found the Levant a highly satisfactory backdrop for amorous encounters. After the drama of that scene at Kelmscott House, the expedition to Egypt, was an anticlimax, for Jane brought along May, whom Blunt found awkward and intimidating. On 29 November Blunt made this unhappy, self-mocking entry in his diary: ‘Mrs. Morris's visit has been rather a disappointment - her daughter May is an obstinately silent woman³ and Judith [Blunt's daughter] is bored by her and I fear they are likely to be a little bored by us. Neither of them can ride, even donkeys, though Mrs. Morris has made an attempt and life without riding here is impossible. One cannot walk on account of the heavy sand, so they sit indoors all day. I go to see them twice a day but with May it is impossible to keep up any conversation and I am now at my wit's end how to amuse them for I cannot make love to either of them and what else is there to be done.' Jane and May stayed on until early April. When we turn to the production history of The Love-Lyrics & Songs of Proteus, we are constantly aware of this strange triangular relationship in the background. In November 189o, before the Kelmscott Press was officially in operation, Blunt convinced Morris to print a volume of his poems, and Jane's comment on the decision (in a letter to Blunt dated 4 December 189o) captures perfectly Morris's mood of euphoria in the early days of the Press: My husband wrote to you consenting to print the poems with his new type. he does not begin till after Xmas — when he will first print his "News from Nowhere". if it succeeds I believe he contemplates bringing forth quartos and folios in large numbers.' Another letter (29 December) also reflects Morris's naïvety about the costs of the kind of printing he proposed to do at the Kelmscott Press: About the book, my husband says he thinks the cost of printing will be very little above the ordinary cost. he has not started yet, but will soon. he has taken a little place where he can set up the press.' Morris's letter of agreement to Blunt (19 November 189o) was brisk and businesslike: I have put off writing to have more news first of my type-founding adventure. I think now that I shall be ready to set to work in about 3 months perhaps earlier. I shall of course be very pleased to print your book, if I may have my own way about the get up — which, I take it is what you want.' There are in fact only two known instances in which Morris succumbed to outside pressure in designing the Kelmscott books: Charles Fairfax Murray claimed that he was responsible for the format of the Epistola de contemptu mundi,³² and Blunt, who had found the rubrics in Poems by the Way extremely attractive, persuaded Morris, against his better judgement, to print the initials of the Love-Lyrics in red.
