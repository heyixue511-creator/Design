# CHARLES MOORE 1925-1993 AMERICAN ARCHITECT AND ACADEMIC WHOSE PERSONALITY WAS REFLECTED IN HIS EXTROVERTED DESIGNS. It is not often that architects are remembered as much for their winning personalities as for their artistic achievements, but Charles Moore (also see under "Living with Postmodern Design") was an exception, lauded as frequently for his good-natured conviviality as for his design skills, which were considerable. He was both prolific and peripatetic, relocating around the country and establishing new, collaborative practices as he went along. He left a legacy of successor firms as well as hundreds of buildings, ten books, many awards, and a generation of architects whose careers he nurtured Moore's buildings and the several residences he designed for himself are examples of a stellar creative.talent and a penchant for unpredictability, mingling innovative ideas with history and the vernacular. He never lost sight of the importance of humor, or as he put it, "the sense of the superficial."26 He concerned himself with the essential questions of architecture: privacy and public space, building with respect for the environment, and the significance of both past and present. Moore's academic credentials were impeccable. A Michigan native and preternaturally bright, he entered the University of Michigan at the age of sixteen. receiving his BArch there in 1947. Moving to San Francisco, he worked in several architectural offices and became a registered architect by the time he was twentyone. A fellowship from Cranbrook Academy in Michigan gave him the opportunity to travel through Europe to see, sketch, and film historic architecture before taking his first teaching job at the University of Utah. After serving in Korea in the US Army Corps of Engineers, he earned an MFA and PhD in architecture from Princeton University (1957). He moved to California to teach at the University of California at Berkeley, then back east to become dean of Yale School of Architecture (where he hired ROBERT VENTURI and DENISE SCOTT BROWN—see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design"—to teach), to California again to teach at UCLA, and finally to Texas to become a professor at the University of Texas at Austin. In each location, he formed new partnerships and continued to practice architecture as a principal of each firm.27 Moore's architecture, even at its most understated, always had an edge; he took familiar elements and put them together in unfamiliar ways, spiced with bold color, lively graphics, ![](images/314cf6b1ed8673ea1c32973c3f6b36471a0e8512b42a9b2344b2705be7b0a423.jpg) and the fillip of materials like aluminum film and neon. He was also seriously committed to a sense of place. His design for the Sea Ranch community (i963) in Sonoma County blénded spare geometric forms seamlessly into an ocean-view landscape and became a paradigm for contextual design in resort communities. His idiosyncratic—and somewhat disorienting— New Haven residence (i969) was published in Playboy magazine, but the Piazza d'Italia (ı978) in New Orleans, Louisiana, is his best-known work, and his favorite. A fantasy recreation of a Roman ruin, with neon-trimmed arches, parodied classical columns, comic-book colors, and a tiled map of Italy underfoot, it treads lightly on the border between populist and kitschy. It was intended to be the centerpiece of a downtown urban redevelopment project; the development never happened, but the Piazza became a muchpublished postmodern icon. Of his many writings, the most influential was “You Have to Pay for the Public Life" (i965), a piece for the Yale journal Perspecta (then edited by ROBERT A. M. STERN (also see under “Living with Postmodern Design"), which anticipated the theories of Postmodernism in celebrating the vernacular and proposed Disneyland as an admirable example of monumental architecture and public space. In thirty-five years of working, teaching, traveling, and collecting admirers (he was often described as the “pied piper of postmodernism"²8), Moore accumulated a personal library of thousands of architectural monographs and exhibition catalogs, travel slides and i6mm films, which are administered by the University of Texas at Austin along with his architectural archive. The Charles W,. Moore Foundation maintains his Texas home and promotes his teachings about the importance of environment, landscape, and a sense of place.
