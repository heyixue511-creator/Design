# CASA MAUI MAUI, HAWAI, UNITED STATES, 1997 ETTORE SOTTSASS BOLD GEOMETRIC FORMS SHAPE A HILLTOP RESIDENCE SUFFUSED WITH COLOR. INSIDE AND OUT. RIGHT Color-block forms cluster on a plinth-like base. OPPOSITE Approaching the house, the assertive forms and colors demand notice. Lesley Bailey and Adrian Olabuenaga, partners in business and life, had successfully collaborated with ETTORE SOTTSASS (also see under “Masters of Postmodern Design") on a collection of jewelry by MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") designers for their firm ACME (see under “Edited Design") when they asked the architect if he would design a home for them on a hilltop site in Maui, where they had recently relocated from Los Angeles. It didn't concern them that Sottsass had never designed a house, or that he lived in Milan and would not be able to personally supervise every aspect of construction. They wanted his extroverted style and brilliant use of color. And they were willing— anxious, in fact—to be thoroughly involved in the process as design collaborators and facilitators. Communications were via fax and snail mail, which in part explains why the project took six years. But the results were everything they had hoped for, and more: a remarkable two-story structure of vari-shaped block forms in saturated colors that resembles nothing on the island, or almost anywhere else. The interior deploys color with equal abandon, but primarily in softer hues that provide a background for color-block furnishings —many by Sottsass, others by Olabuenaga—that reflect the owners' postmodernist leanings but manage to mix congenially with classic Hawaiian pieces. The result: a livable residence for Bailey and Olabuenaga, despite the attention it continues to draw from passers-by and architecture fans; a design magazine called it “the most Memphis house imaginable." BELOW Throughout the apartment, embellished doorways serve as wall decor. Comfortably ensconced on the parlor floor of an old Venetian palazzo, this apartment is a part-time home for the producer and design editor CLETO MUNARI (see under “Edited Design") and his wife Valentina, who had long sought a pied-à-terre in that romantic city as an accessible and appealing location for forays from their home (and work) base in Vicenza. When they found it, they turned to ETTORE SOTTSASS (also see under "Masters of Postmodern Design") to outfit the interiors, although it had been some time since he had dealt with an entire living space, let alone such a traditional environment. But the Munaris are not traditionalists, and Sottsass took on the challenge. He and his associate ALDO CIBIC (see under “Design") came up with an ingenious solution: they built an entirely new structure within the existing one, creating a fluid, contemporary space without destroying the classical surround. In the high-ceilinged main room (the portego), which flows from the front to the back of the palazzo, a tall, totem-like storage unit stands squarely in the center of the space, modulating the too-grand scale of the room and incorporating not only bookshelves and a desk, but hi-fi equipment, a bar, coat rack, and umbrella stands, as well as halogen lights. Doorways throughout the apartment are imaginatively framed, each differently, in bright or patterned laminate; the tall white walls are otherwise bare. The furnishings are a discreet but discernably modern blend of old and new, with antique carpets underscoring a combination of MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") furniture and less assertive, functional pieces. The result is a comfortable, modern residence wrapped in history.
