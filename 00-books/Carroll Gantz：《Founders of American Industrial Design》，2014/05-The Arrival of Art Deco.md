# The Arrival of Art Deco France was the main attraction in the art world of the 1920s and naturally wanted to promote and reinforce its prewar reputation and culture of fashion, style, decorative arts and ine arts. By the early 1920s, many aspiring young American artists were being influenced by the dramatic impact of French modern art and considered a visit to, or study in, Paris as an essential part of their artistic education in modern art and culture, as well as a carefree, youthful experience in the good life of Europe. So from April to October of 1925, an Exposition International des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes (International Exposition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts) was held in Paris, the first "World's Fair” since the one in San Francisco ten years before. The lengthy French title would much later be shortened to Art Deco to describe the many European varieties of new designs and their subsequent impact around the world, but at the time, the designs were generally described as “modernistic." The exposition was unique in one key respect: former similar expositions traditionally celebrated past national accomplishments. In this one the intention of the organizers was to establish new standards. Exhibitors were required to display only "those exhibits that were not dependent of the art of the past."¹ Invitations went out to all countries except Germany, which was still regarded as the villain of the Great War. Twenty-six countries accepted, except for China and the United States. China did not accept because of disruptions in political matters, and the United States ostensibly because the State Department did not recommend appropriations to do so, due to lack of support from commercial interests. As Herbert Hoover, then secretary of commerce and in charge of the Coolidge administration's industrial policy, explained to the Fourth Annual Exposition of Women's Arts and Industries: The advice I received from manufacturers was that while we produced a vast volume of goods of much artistic value, they did not consider that we could contribute sufficiently varied design of unique character or of special expression in American artistry to warrant such a participation.² Obviously, U.S. manufacturers saw no significant consumer market potential in Europe, but more to the point, they had nothing to display that met the exposition's intent of new standards or its specific criteria of products that were “not dependent of the art of the past." Most U.S. products of the time were, in fact, copies of European designs. Hoover's decision did not set well with the cultural establishment, where many were already quite aware of how advanced European design and fashion had become. When Charles Richards, the same art educator who conducted the 1922 disappointing study of industrial arts education, asked Hoover to delegate a commission of 100 representatives óf trade associations, along with some artists and designers, to visit and report on the exposition, Hoover gracefully complied. The six-month exposition opened on April 28 with 4,000 guests at the inauguration, and thousands of visitors arrived on following days. It was located between the esplanade of Les Invalides and the entrances of the Grand Palais and Petite Palais, a 57-acre site, two-thirds of which contained French displays. Exhibits included work by famous glass and jewelry designer René Lalique (1860–1945), who also designed the exposition's crystal tower fountain. There were displays by a number of Parisian department stores such as Bon Marché, Printemps, and Magazins du Louvre, which created entire room “ensembles” in modernist styles. The work of French furniture and interior designer Émile-Jacques Ruhlmann (1879–1933) was displayed in a special, classical “Pavilion of the Collector” designed by French architect Pierre Patout (1879–1965). In a far corner of the exposition, generally ignored by the press, was Le Corbusier's Pavilion de L'Esprit Nouveau, a spare modern home interior and furnishings along the lines of his Maison Citrohan concept, that attracted both admiration and criticism for its lack of decoration. Russia had a pavilion displaying the work of Alexandr Rodchenko, Varvara Stepanova, and students of the art and design school, Vkhutemas, which they had established. The students won several prizes. The popular Swedish pavilion was simple in form and devoid of decoration, featuring modern crafts in typical Scandinavian styles inspired by Gregor Paulsson's (1889– 1977) 1919 book, More Beautiful Things for Everyday Life, which challenged the past and encouraged Arts and Crafts workers to use new forms of the modern age. Danish architect and designer Arne Jacobsen (1902–1971), still a student, won a silver medal for his chair design. Austria contributed Frederick Kiesler's (1890–1965) City in Space exhibit to house displays of the work of Josef Hoffman (1870–1956), architect, furniture designer, and one of the founders of the Vienna Secession. With 16 million people visiting the exposition, it was destined to have a lasting impact on applied arts around the world. Certainly Le Corbusier influenced architecture in what would become known as International Style in 1932. The decorative arts were influenced by the unusual new styles that were generally called modernistic but which included an entire range of imagery and motifs that have been variously described as streamlined, geometric, symmetric, or as a Machine Age look. Some imagery was clearly derived from French Cubism, German Bauhaus, Italian Futurism and Russian Constructivism, all described earlier. American critic Helen Appleton Read (1897–1974) reported on several important principles of modern design she observed at the exposition: It is characteristic of the new décor that the nature of the material is at all times respected and is allowed to dictate its treatment. Wood is wood, iron is iron; the bad taste that invariably ensues when the attempt is made to give the quality of one material to another, to make wood look [like] iron or marble like lace, is avoided. This universal tendency toward simplicity of outline should be of the greatest interest to the American designer if he will recognize that this is the fundamental note. All design in this country is governed by the factor of whether or not it can be reproduced in mass production. It costs no more to get out a good design than it does a bad one, and the fact that the best designs of the new décor are the simplest to the point of being geometric makes them so much the more easy to put on the market.³ Read's assumption that simpler forms were easier to produce may seem logical, but in fact most manufacturing processes could produce complex forms as easily as simple ones. Few design forms are as complex as the Model T or a 1922 kitchen stove, both popular designs of the time. Many manufacturers would argue that copying an existing design was much easier than investing in a new design, which required extensive design work as well expensive new tooling. They would argue that the cheapest of all was to keep producing the old design as long as possible. Read went on to explain her theory of the reasoning behind the new modern forms: The whole trend of modern design toward the geometric and the unadorned had its inception through the omnipresence of the machine. Because we had absorbed the shapes of the machine into our consciousness, we ended up by creating designs which reflected it, and which in turn are the most suitable for machine made processes.4 Read may have had a point here. Designers over a generation may have been so preoccupied with the impact of machines and mass production on society that they unconsciously felt compelled to invent new forms that suggested machines. The simplest explanation, however, may have been that as artists and designers often do, they simply made things different than the past to demonstrate progress in the creative arts, or to be more inventive than those who went before. It is always speculative to precisely pinpoint the intent of creative people. They just love to create new things! The American Commission submitted a report to the Department of Commerce, warning that the modern movement would inevitably reach the United States, and that Americans needed to initiate a parallel effort of our own upon lines calculated to appeal to the American consumer.. As a nation we now live artistically on warmed-over dishes. In a number of lines of manufacture we are little more than producing antiquarians. We copy, modify, and adapt the older styles with few suggestions of a new idea. It is true that this practice of reproducing older forms has been an invaluable education to our people. It is also true that the adaptation of old motives when performed with intelligence and skill continues and probably will continue to give us a large proportion of the decorative manifestations acceptable to American taste. It would seem equally true, on the other hand, that the richness and complexity of American life call for excursions into new fields that may yield not only innovations but examples well suited to the living conditions of our times.⁵ The ambiguity of this report clearly reflected conflicting opinions among commission members as to what to do about the obvious differences between the new European design direction and the traditional practices of design in the United States. Copying of older styles was justified as educational and acceptable, but innovation may yet be of some value. Some members doubted that the new modern styles would appeal to Americans. The report gave the Department of Commerce no clear recommendations for action, and even if it had, it is doubtful that Commerce could have done anything to change the current traditions and practices of manufacturers. Any shift in direction would have to come from the manufacturers themselves based on consumer demand, and at this moment in history, the general public was blissfully unaware of what was happening in Paris. This would soon change. Two and a half million Americans now had radios and were becoming more aware of world events. In England, John Logie Baird was even demonstrating a mechanical television system. Reports from other Americans who attended the Paris exposition quickly circulated within the art and design communities and the press. The Art-in-Trades Club issued a manifesto challenging American artists to compete in the design of modern furnishings. In January 1926, an editorial about the Paris exposition in the New York Times urged industry to change because the public would soon catch up with Europe in recognizing the competitive value of new designs: American captains of industry must soon change their traditional characters. True, the public may still cling to its conception of them as iron-jawed, level headed giants of efficiency .. but if the present international competition keeps up, we may find them, like the Germans, openly cooperating with museums and art schools; or, like the French, proclaiming themselves merchants of beauty.6 One of the attendees at the Paris Exposition had been Kem Weber, mentioned earlier, who had been working at the Barker Brothers furniture store in Los Angeles and represented their interest abroad. He had become an American citizen in 1924 and had Americanized his German name to only its initials, K.E.M. In 1926, he had become art director for Barker Brothers. Up to that time, Barker had exclusively featured period revival furniture in its five stores. Inspired by what he had seen in Paris, Weber in the fall of 1926 opened a new store-within-astore, christened“Modes and Manners," on the fourth floor of Barker's eleven-story building. Weber designed the wall treatments, display cases and furniture in a space with jagged shapes, bright colors, and wild patterns. Modern furniture by Weber's Austrian friend in New York, Paul Frankl, was also displayed. It was not only the first outlet for modern furniture and accessories in Southern California, but also one of the first in the country. Over the next decade, Weber would work to promote modernism with the furniture he designed there in his store studio, the Silver Tree. The store had its own production facilities to manufacture the furniture and soon opened a new special Hollywood branch, which Weber also designed.7 Museums were anxious to promote the new designs, and in February the American Association of Museums imported a collection of 400 French and Swedish objects from Paris, called Selected Collection of Objects from the International Exposition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Art, which was exhibited at the Metropolitan Museum of Art and subsequently sent to tour other American member museums. The exhibit explained that it was not to stimulate demand for European products, nor to encourage copying of European creations, but to bring about an understanding of this modern movement in design in the hope that a parallel movement may be initiated in our country ... to bring us to new forms appropriate to the living conditions of the twentieth century.8 In Vogue magazine, patterns of textiles and paper goods were illustrated and described as "modern designs” or of “modernistic influence." Advertisements in national magazines began to adapt Art Deco design formats and motifs to give their ads and products a sense of modernity. Even ads for the most modest, utilitarian objects could be glorified when presented in a modern artistic format. Ready-to-wear fashions quickly adopted Art Deco elements such as straight-line forms, squared necklines, triangular and diagonal accents, and geometric patterns. Such styles soon appeared in every category of fashion accessories, particularly jewelry, compacts, handbags, hats and scarves. Younger generations today probably wonder why there was such a fuss about these European designs, when fashionably designed products are available today from all over the world. It is a bit hard to relate, I know. But depending on your age, you only need to remember what it was like in the 1950s, when home television sets became available, or in the 1980s, when personal computers were introduced, or in the 2000s when iPhones hit the market. The public is always excited when a new technology, previously available but only affordable by large institutions, finally becomes inexpensive enough and available to ordinary people, or when a fashion trend, such as short skirts in the 1960s, casual wear in the 1980s, or sport shoes in the 1990s, suddenly erupts everywhere in a short period of time. In the 1920s, the latest technology was, in fact, mechanical devices, such as new electric appliances or any new household decorative accessories, furniture, or bathroom and kitchen equipment. People simply wanted to buy the most modern-looking things, and at that time, “modern” was dramatically different from the past. Or as flappers would have said in the 1920s, “Modern design is swell! The bee's knees! The cat's meow!" (Translation: Cool! Awesome! Mind-blowing!) They were no different from young consumers today. While the new modern design styles were being promoted aggressively everywhere, there were additional incentives encouraging consumers to buy. Increasing efficiencies in manufacturing over the last ten years had enabled a reduction in production and labor costs, thus offering lower consumer prices. The vacuum cleaner that cost \$52 in 1918, in 1926 was priced at \$29.95, 49 percent lower (in 2013 dollars, that's a reduction from \$804 to \$394). So the new mass-produced products were much more affordable, for many more people. In addition, more jobs were available at higher salaries. The economy was better than it had ever been since the war. People were primed to buy, and they wanted the latest styles. Now it was up to the manufacturers, many of whom had neither the inclination nor the specialized skills to provide such new styles. The first of the so-called artless industries of mass production in the late 1920s to recognize the public desire for new design styles was in fact the automobile industry, in which 43 firms produced four million new cars each year. Travel by car was increasing rapidly with the opening of the Lincoln Highway, the first coast-to-coast roadway, connecting San Francisco and New York (now Route 40). In 1926, the Model T, which in 1923 had commanded 52 percent of the U.S. market but was virtually unchanged since its 1913 introduction, was now challenged by Chevrolet's new 1926 model with bold new styling and colors. General Motors (GM) head Alfred P. Sloan had initiated annual styling changes because he was convinced that new styling was the key to success. Chevy sales doubled, and Ford dropped by 25 percent. Within a year, Chevrolet would win 45 percent of the market, and by 1927 Ford's share would drop to 15 percent. Ford was forced to drop the Model T and produce its redesigned Model A in 1928, designed by Edsel Ford, Henry's son. Some manufacturers figured that if Henry Ford himself, the king of Fordism, needed styling, they had better follow suit. Now, in early 1927, about when Charles Lindbergh flew solo across the Atlantic, GM's Sloan charged Harley J. Earl (1893– 1969) with designing the 1927 LaSalle, a cheaper, productionmodel version of GM's luxury Cadillac. It was to be the first massproduced car to have its appearance completely styled from bumper to bumper by a single individual, and the first to use chrome plating on the trim. Earl used full-scale clay models to preplan every detail and blend all components into an integrated form. Earl had been born in Hollywood, California, had attended the University of Southern California in 1912 and studied engineering at Stanford University from 1914 to 1917. Earl, ca. 1930s (General Motors). Carriage Works, and after 1908 called the Earl Automobile Works, which built custom car bodies for film stars starting in 1911. Clients included Roscoe “Fatty" Arbuckle, Tom Mix, Mary Pickford, Cecille B. DeMille, and Al Jolson, among others. Earl designed the first complete car produced by the firm in 1918. That year, Don Lee, a West Coast Cadillac distributor, head of Don Lee Coach and Body Works, bought the Earl Company and kept Harley on as designer and general manager of its custom body shop in 1919. In 1921 Earl patented a design that suggested the wraparound windshield he later would pioneer. In 1926, the Fisher Brothers of General Motors, impressed with Earl's technique of using modeling clay to develop the forms of his designs, hired him as a consultant to Cadillac, and he relocated to Detroit. His first assignment was to design the LaSalle, a low-cost Cadillac. The 1927 LaSalle would become an instant success and would convince Sloan to establish the first internal corporate design department at GM, called the Art and Colour Section, and to name Earl as its first director. Within a year Earl would hire a staff of 50 automotive designers. In 1927, promotion of modern design to the public continued on many fronts. The Metropolitan Museum of Art held an exhibit of Swedish industrial arts, introducing Scandinavian design to the U.S. public for the first time. In May, Jane Heap (1887–1964), editor of a literary journal called The Little Review, organized a Machine Age Exposition in Steinway Hall in Midtown Manhattan. Inspired by Le Corbusier's Pavilion de L'Esprit Nouveau, and by Soviet Constructivism, Heap displayed actual machines: a Studebaker crankshaft, a Curtis airplane engine, and a Hyde Windlass propeller, along with photographs of skyscrapers, the Bauhaus in Dessau, Soviet industrial buildings, and architecture by Walter Gropius. E. B. White (1899– 1985) wrote in the New Yorker that the exposition demonstrated “that the machine is the tutelary symbol of the universal dynamism."9 Department stores, such as Macy's and Franklin Simon of New York, Wanamaker's in Philadelphia, and Marshall Field in Chicago, inspired by the success of Paris department store ensembles during the 1925 exposition, staged exhibitions in May 1927 featuring the new Art Deco designs. Typical was Macy's Art and Trade exhibition, which was attended by 40,000 people and was hyped aggressively by radio announcements and talks by notable participants. Consumer goods manufacturers were being advised to incorporate modern design into their products by advertising agencies. In the August 1927 issue of Atlantic Monthly, Ernest Elmo Calkins (1868–1964), head of the Calkins & Holden advertising agency, who was called by many the “Dean of Advertising Men," wrote an influential article, “Beauty, the New Business Tool," in which he stated the case for more attractive products: We passed from the hand to the machine, we enjoyed our era of triumph of the machine, we acquired wealth, and with wealth, education, travel, sophistication, a sense of beauty; and then we began to miss something in our cheap but ugly products. Efficiency was not enough. The machine did not satisfy the soul. Man could not live by bread alone. And thus it came about that beauty, or what one conceived as beauty, became a factor in the production and marketing of goods.10 ## Calkins continued to define beauty: The only art that can survive and grow is art that is related to our life and our needs, and that has a sound economic foundation. It is to be hoped that manufacturers in the search for design to beautify their products will start with a clear conception of what beauty is, especially beauty in an article of use ... the surest guide in divining new beauty in machine-made things is to grasp and interpret the beauty they naturally and intrinsically possess." In other words, beauty in useful products is not extraneous artistic decoration or embellishment, but the honest and simple visual expression of its natural function through form and character. Whether Calkins knew of the Bauhaus or not, he was expressing the exact same philosophy of design, but in business terms. Calkins asked his advertising colleagues, "How could ads work, if the styling of products did not express the qualities claimed for it?"12 Many manufacturers by this time were paying attention to their advertising agencies, which were already incorporating the modern look into their ads. In many cases, such manufacturers asked their agencies for help in designing products, requests that were honored by nameless artists on the agency's staff and given no credit. Other manufacturers sought such design assistance by advertising for employees or consultants. One can imagine what such a help wanted ad for an industrial designer might have looked like in 1927: Wanted: Artistically trained industrial artist to create new modern designs for products made by company X. Applicants need to be familiar with manufacturing processes such as casting, stamping, grinding, milling, welding, fastening, and assembling, and willing to work collaboratively under chief engineers and efficiency experts in actual factories. Designs must be able to be reproduced easily and inexpensively by machines, and must be attractive to consumers in the modern style. Applicants must provide examples of mass-produced products they have designed recently. Compensation for such work is to be negotiated. An advertisement like that would have attracted few applicants, because at that time, practically no one existed with such extensive qualifications that required both art and engineering training, and who would be willing to work in mass-production facilities. As we have seen, few art students would even consider such a position, even if they had been trained in engineering or manufacturing matters, and of course none were. As noted earlier, most graduates of art schools wanted nothing to do with industry. Engineers who were trained in manufacturing processes, and may have designed massproduced products, had absolutely no art education, nor any inclination to acquire it. The industrial art position described in the ad would have been, as some would say, “neither fish nor fowl," that is, neither true artist or true engineer. Traditional separation of art and industry had, at least in America, left industry without an essential, competitive, business tool. Jane Heap, who had organized the Machine Age Exposition in New York, wrote that an effective artist “must affiliate with the creative artist in other arts and with the constructive men of his epoch: engineers, scientists, etc."13 In 1927 there were a few professionals who recognized the increasing demand for industrial design, were trained in art and design, and responded enthusiastically to the call by manufacturers, knowing full well that this would require their willing collaboration with industry, engineers, and scientists. They were the farseeing individuals who almost single-handedly became pioneers in what was to become a brand new profession. They were interesting and unusual men and women. They were the machine designers. Here's where things began to get interesting. As the demand for modern design increased both by manufacturers and by the public, professional people in a number of art-related, advertising, or commercial fields responded with interest, or were sought out and targeted by industry. Some, of course, were already in the art industries, including furniture and lighting products. One of these was Donald Deskey (1894–1989), then 33, who had just opened an office, Deskey-Vollmer, in New York in 1927, with his partner, Phillip Vollmer, specializing in the design of furniture and lighting. Deskey was born in Blue Earth, Minnesota, and had studied architecture at the University of California, Berkeley, and painting at the California School of Design, the School of the Art Institute of Chicago, and the Art Students League of New York. After military service in World War I, he started working with advertising agencies in Chicago and New York, but soon left to study painting in Paris from 1920 to 1922, where he also married. He returned to the United States to begin a career in painting; to teach art at Juniata College in Huntington, Pennsylvania; and to exhibit his work in Pittsburgh. In 1925 he returned to France to attend the Paris exposition and visited the Bauhaus in Dessau, both of which greatly influenced his approach to design, and upon his return in 1926, he settled in New York to work for furniture designer Paul Frankl, mentioned earlier. He designed furniture and lamps for Frankl, but also designed an apartment interior for Adam Gimbel, the founder of Gimbel's department store, who had bought out rival Saks & Co. and opened an uptown branch called Saks Fifth Avenue in 1923. ![](images/18d30d7f42cc6b4a1fbe650500524e2baac22f2469e50482f7cc18bd2995d6b9.jpg) Meanwhile, in California, Kem Weber left the Barker Brothers store in 1927 to open an independent design studio in Los Angeles, but continued to work as a consultant for Barker, shaping its products well into the 1930s. His design of an armchair for the John Bissinger residence in San Francisco in 1928 was in the sleek and sophisticated modernism that would come to defne the Hollywood of the 1930s. In his new office, he sought out collaboration with manufacturers and explored new materials. Deskey; Kem Weber; set designer Lee Simonson (1888–1967); set designer, architect, and artist Frederick Kiesler (1890–1965); Paul Frankl; and a few other designers were justifiably concerned with the dishonest but common practice of manufacturers copying designs, a practice that museums historically encouraged by displaying traditional European designs as superior models of design quality. At the time, there was no reason to believe that the new innovative modern designs in America would not be subjected to the same practice. Now, as early as 1927, the new modern designers realized that, for the first time, American designs could be more innovative than European ones and felt that such borrowing, as Frankl put it, “degrades the producers and corrupts the taste of the public."14 They formed an organization called the American Union of Decorative Artists and Craftsmen (AUDAC), which idealistically aimed to “cooperate with manufacturers and the public in the placing of American arts and crafts on a basis of honesty, dignity, and merit." Another lighting designer was Walter von Nessen (1889–1943), then 38, who opened Nessen Studios in New York's Murray Hill section in 1927 with his wife, Greta von Nessen (c. 1900–1975), then 27, to design and fabricate architectural lighting. His designs, such as his innovative swing-arm lamps for table, floor, and wall applications, were simple, polished metal, modern, and highly functional in allowing the lamp to be easily pushed into a range of horizontal positions without moving the floor or desk lamp base. The Nessens also designed furniture and accessories for the Chase Brass and Copper Company, including a tubular steel chair. Von Nessen was born and trained in Germany, and his wife studied at the School for Industrial Arts in Stockholm, Sweden. They both emigrated to the United States in 1925. There was also Gilbert Rohde (1894–1944), then 33, who in 1927 traveled to France and Germany and was inspired by modernism there to design and produce custom furniture in a modern style. That same year, he opened his own office in New York, designing furniture and showrooms and consulting with manufacturers such as General Electric and the Hudson Motor Car Company. Rohde was born in New York and became familiar with cabinetmaking in his German father's shop. He studied at the Art Students League and the Grand Central School of Art, both in New York. His early work was as a drama and music critic, cartoonist, reporter, and furniture illustrator. In an environment where women led the fashion industry, it seemed appropriate that some of these women would be attracted to the design of modern products. One of these was Helen Dryden (1887–1981) who, inspired by the flurry of modern design activity after the Paris Art Deco Exposition of 1925, turned her career in costume design to industrial design in 1927 at about age 40. She designed tableware and lamps for the Revere Corporation and as art director with the Dura Company in Ohio, where she designed chrome decorative objects, textiles, and glassware and was soon acclaimed as “the highest-paid woman artist." ![](images/d19d6c5c4e5fce969d1868577b54e89555a7c23e80ffdbaeba23c29e993e3232.jpg) Gilbert Rohde, ca. 1940 (courtesy Herman Miller, Inc.). Dryden was born in Baltimore with unusual artistic ability, designing and selling clothes for paper dolls that appeared in newspaper fashion sections. She trained in landscape painting under Hugh Breckenridge and studied briefly at the Philadelphia Academy of Fine Arts before moving to New York to begin her career in fashion design illustration at Vogue magazine in 1909. From 1913 to 1922, she designed dramatic and outstanding Vogue covers, as well as covers for Charm, Harper's Bazaar, House & Garden, Vanity Fair, and Harper's Weekly. From 1914 well into the 1920s, she designed costumes for Broadway shows and fabrics for Stehli Silks and Aberfoyle Fabrics. Photograph by Lazernick). Other entrants to the new profession were in the advertising business. In 1927, Walter Dorwin Teague (1883–1960), then 44, was awarded a contract with Eastman Kodak to design cameras, starting on January 1, 1928. Teague immediately added "Industrial Design” to his firm's letterhead, since he was already a highly successful advertising artist, who had opened his own typographic studio in 1912 and by 1920 had been involved in commercial packaging. Born in Decatur, Indiana, as one of six siblings, Teague's grandfather had moved from North Carolina to Pendleton, Indiana, where one of America's largest Quaker communities existed. Teague's father became a circuit-riding Methodist minister and later a tailor in Pendleton. At age 16, while still in school, Teague worked as a handyman at the local newspaper, and later as a reporter. He was inspired to become an artist by books on architecture in his high school library. At age 19, he left Indiana for New York City, where he studied painting from 1903 to 1907 at the Art Students League of New York, an art school founded in 1875 for both amateurs and professionals that offered reasonably priced classes. Students could study part or full time, but there were no degree programs or grades awarded. The school is still operating today in the same manner. Teague earned money by checking hats at the YMCA in downtown Manhattan, and also by painting signs. His skill in lettering soon evolved into drawing illustrations for mail-order catalogs, such as for neckties and shoes. This led to more elaborate advertising illustrations, which caught the attention of Walter Whitehead, an advertising executive at the Ben Hampton Advertising Agency he had met at the YMCA, and who promptly hired him. When Whitehead left the Ben Hampton Agency in 1908 and joined the more prestigious New York agency of Calkins & Holden, Teague went with him. In his four years there, Teague became known for his distinctive frames, or borders, for advertising art, which blended Baroque and Renaissance influence with a unique simplicity. After a year of freelancing, Teague left Calkins & Holden in 1912 to work from his own typographic studio, creating graphic designs for magazine ads. In 1920, when he designed frames for the famous Arrow Collar ads, he was already recognized widely for what were known as “Teague borders. By the mid–1920s, the demand for border designs weakened, and Teague became involved with commercial packaging. He was intrigued by the Paris exposition of 1925, and in June 1926 he traveled to Europe to view Bauhaus work in Italy and was inspired by the work and writings ofLe Corbusier. Upon his return, he reestablished his office devoted to package design. In 1927, Richard F. Bach (1887–1968), curator of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, recommended Teague to Adolph Stuber, a top manager of the Rochester, New York-based Eastman Kodak Company, which was considering engaging an artist to design cameras. Teague took Stuber on a tour of the modern art shops in New York, including the contemporary shop of Rena Rosenthal, in order to show him the clean lines of home furnishings that were coming into style. Teague had no knowledge of cameras or how they were made, but he proposed working on-site with Kodak's engineers, claiming that "designing according to engineering necessities ultimately leads to greater beauty and heavier sales."16 Stuber apparently agreed. Teague's relationship with Eastman would result in dozens of camera designs over many years, and he would continue to consult with Eastman for the remainder of his life. Teague's first designs for Kodak would include its 1930 Art Deco gift camera and packaging, and its Beau Brownie, both of which featured a colorful geometric pattern of rectangles and squares reminiscent of Dutch artist Piet Mondrian (1872–1944) and De Stijl art. By February 1928, Teague was already promoting the new profession of industrial design by writing an article, "Modern Design Needs Modern Merchandising," in Forbes, a business magazine. He advised, “The designer who gets results for the manufacturer plans with all departments of a business before he ever lays à pencil to drawing board."17 That same year, 1928, manufacturers were trying to make design changes in their products. But rather than changing the form of their old products, which would require expensive tooling costs, it was much easier for them to change the color of their product. In 1930, Fortune magazine recalled that in the early 1920s, “kitchen and bathroom were a porcelain and surgical white. Furniture was in natural wood tints, surfaced with a colorless finish. The stove was black. Faucets were metallic." Now, in a typical home in 1928, in the cellar stood a Redflash Boiler, more crimson than the flames of the adjacent furnace. On the door of the orange refrigerator was perched a green parrot. Not only the dishes, but the pots and pans were decked out in bright gay tints and tones. A green-tiled bathroom was set off with green tub, green towels, and, monstrously enough, green toilet paper. For afternoon tea there were blue glasses and a yellow cloth. A yellow alarm clock—a cherry garbage can, a scarlet typewriter—surfaces had turned suddenly into palettes. Fortune concluded, "The acceptance of color was undoubtedly significant from the standpoint of old traditions being abandoned and old suppressions being released."18 The word was getting around the advertising world that designing products for industry was inancially lucrative. An early example of this was the ticket scale designed for International in 1929 by Joseph “Jo" Claude Sinel (1889–1975), a 40- year-old artist working for the Lennon Mitchell advertising agency in New York. Sinel in a later interview recalled, ![](images/139ea3d8bb5f4a0ef3dc4bf4607f751847a4ee1db93886500a4757befb496cfd.jpg) Joseph “Jo" Sinel, 1928 (courtesy California College of the Arts Libraries). One of the first designs I made was the International Ticket Scale, and I made a model from a Del Monte (wooden) fruit case.... I took the model in to show the executives of the company and they paid me a fee of \$10,000 [\$136,000 in 2013 dollars] and royalties for 25 years.19 This design is well documented and is in a style that many would call “skyscraper style," with vertical and graduated geometrical elements, typical of the period. Sinel led a most unconventional life for his era. In later times, he might have been called a wandering “hippie," but his earnings in commercial art demonstrated outstanding skill and diligence in his career. He was born in Auckland, New Zealand, where his father, Thomas, ran a stevedoring operation and sired ten children. Jo's primary school's headmaster recognized his talent for drawing and urged him to attend the Elam School of Art. From 1904 to 1909, he served an apprenticeship as a lithographic artist for the New Zealand Herald newspaper. He did freelance work for printing firms in Wellington and Christchurch, then gained valuable commercial art studio experience in Australia as well as working as a harvest hand and sheep shearer. Impulsively, he left for England and arrived in Liverpool in the middle of World War I, but he had no trouble landing a job at Hudson-Scotts, England's largest lithographer, in Carlisle. ![](images/2d45e101a7e9740358929c4b2bb7fb6cc753527ebe0555f3baa940287ac84754.jpg) 1929 International Ticket Scale by Sinel (Designscape magazine, February 1977). Next was Carlton Studios, England's most prominent art studio, and after that, the C. F. Hyme advertising agency in London. The war became increasingly dangerous to Londoners due to Zeppelin air raids, so Jo shipped out as a merchant seaman during wartime to avoid military conscription. After unloading guns off a British ship in Newport News, Virginia, he landed once again in Australia where he easily obtained a high-salary art job. When Australia passed a statute saying that men of military age could not leave the country after a certain date, Sinel decided it was time to leave. In 1918 he left for the United States and landed in San Francisco at the time of the influenza epidemic, when people on the street were wearing surgical masks. Again, he landed an art position at Foster & Kleiser advertising agency but soon quit his job and set out for the high Sierras to build a cabin with an artist friend on the shores of Lake Susie. When snow arrived, he headed back to San Francisco to find several job offers waiting. The one he chose was in New York with First National Pictures, a film company in which Charlie Chaplin and Douglas Fairbanks were associated. For First National, he designed promotional graphics for film distribution, but soon, driven by wanderlust and fascinated with automobiles, bought one in 1920 and drove across America, which was virtually unpaved at the time, to see its natural wonders and cities. He had advertisements for First National Pictures painted on the side of his car that attracted photographers at many cities, fuelingJo's natural flair for publicity. Returning to San Francisco, he worked simultaneously for Foster & Kleiser and the western branch of the McCann-Erikson advertising firm. When Jo refused to do a certain job at Foster & Kleiser, they fired him, but he and three others, including Harold von Schmidt, set up an independent business to design posters for various San Francisco advertising agencies, including Foster & Kleiser. In 1921 and 1922, Sinel taught design and lettering at the California School of Arts and Crafts (CCAC, but now, California College of Arts, CCA) under its founder, Frederick Meyer. Jo would continue his association with CCAC, off and on, until his death. But in 1922 he took a position as art director for C. C. Ronalds, who was starting a new advertising agency in Montreal, Canada. After five weeks there, Sinel went to New York to do freelance work with von Schmidt in a studio in Greenwich Village, where they were members of the Society of Illustrators and the Artists' Guild. Through these contacts, Sinel landed a job with the Lennon Mitchell advertising agency in 1923. In 1924, he published a book, A Book of American Trademarks and Devices. During this period, Sinel claimed, so he probably did, work on a number of product designs, as was not uncommon for advertising agencies of those days, but documented examples have not yet been found.
