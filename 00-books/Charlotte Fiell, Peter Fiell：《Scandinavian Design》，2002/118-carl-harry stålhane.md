His studies also took him to Spain, Greece, Turkey, Egypt, Mexico and the UnitedStates. In 1939, he began working at the Rörstrand ceramics factory, initially assisting the painter Isaac Grünewald(1889-1946)in the production of colourful tin-glazed earthenware objects decorated with Fauvesque motifs. By 1948 he was designing his own ceramics, which were inspired by Oriental art and which explored, like the work of other contemporary ceramicists at the time such asBerndt Friberg, the ultimate in “classical” pottery forms. His soft-edged vessels with mottled matt glazes marked an important stylistic departure for Rörstrand and typified the new understated style of ceramics that began to emerge in Scandinavia during the immediate post-war years. In 1951 he was awarded a gold medal at the (IX)Milan Triennale for his highly influential work. By the early 1950s, Stålhane was also designing ceramics with geometric motifs, such as his Abstrakt series, which reflected the influence of contemporary French and Italian abstract art as well as Japanese raku pottery and calligraphy. Apart from his studio pieces, Stålhane designed functional domestic tableware for mass production, such as his SB, Blanca, and Vieta dinner services. During the 1960s, his work became increasingly sculptural and larger in scale, often functioning more as ceramic sculpture than as domestic pottery. Between 1963 and 1971 he taught in the ceramics department at the Konstindustriskolan (School of Arts & Crafts) in Gothenburg and in 1973, having designed ceramics for Rörstrand for forty years, established his own studio, Designhuset, in Lidköping, where he was assisted by Kent Ericsson. Beyond his work for Rörstrand, Stålhane also designed a simple hand-painted decorative motif for the Delfi tableware range manufactured by Bing & Grøndahl, the shapes of which were designed by Martin Hunt (b. 1942). Like so many other Scandinavian designers, Stålhane skilfully straddled the worlds of both craft manufacture and industrial mass production in pursuit of “ideal” forms. ![](images/d61623e13a11a5b04a47efadeb1d865555fd2845ecef95684d563d0eb8c6b656.jpg) ![](images/bc225e7400627562af0c335e9650c18daf90e689f40bd63894942bfadcf513d4.jpg) Arne Jacobsen, teapot from the Cylinda Line stainless steel hollowware range for Stelton, 1967 ## stelton fo rp, Denark ![](images/94c160f65796f68652fb18623b04ba6b3a4e4e2213c22915321885b8be733782.jpg) Renowned for its elegant metal and plastic products, the domestic ware manufacturer Stelton was founded in 1960 to sell the stainless steel hollowware – sauceboats, platters, salad bowls etc. – manufactured by the Dansk Rustfrit company based in Fårevejle, a little town in Northwest Zealand. The products, which were of mediocre design, were intended by Stelton to form the basis of a range of contemporary housewares aimed primarily at the Danish market. Salesman Peter Holmblad(b. 1934)was hired to market the line and in 1964, with a view to raising the company's profile, he persuaded the well-known architect and designer Arne Jacobsen to design a range of hollowware for the firm. Jacobsen subsequently produced some sketches of a few cylindrical forms that were “terse, logical and functional”, but the technology needed to translate his ideas into threedimensional products did not yet exist: stainless steel is an extremely demanding material to work, as its surface can become easily distorted if it is not handled in exactly the right way. Consequently Stelton first had to develop new machines and welding techniques, before it could produce Jacobsen's Cylinda Line, which was eventually introduced in 1967. In recognition of the superior quality of its design, the Cylinda Line was awarded an ID Prize by the Danish Society of Industrial Design in 1967, and the International Design Award by the American Institute of Interior Designers in 1968. The Cylinda Line initially comprised around 18 pieces, but Jacobsen continued to add new designs up to 1971, while others were introduced posthumously from drawings. Today, the range comprises 34 pieces in total. Described as “products which live up to the demand for a beautiful, functional, and practical hollowware range in contemporary design”, the success of the Cylinda Line can be largely attributed to Jacobsen's ability to express the inherent qualities of stainless steel through highly rational yet aesthetically pleasing, timeless forms. From 1976, Erik Magnussen also worked for Stelton and designed a number of well-known table pieces in both stainless steel and plastic, including his classic threepiece vacuumjug set of 1977. The understated elegance and high object integrity of Magnussen's products, has like Jacobsen's, enabled them to remain utterly impervious to the whims of fashion. The company's range today also includes designs by Peter Holmblad (the managing director and owner of Stelton since the late 1970s), such as his Model no. 100–15 house plant watering can, which has a similar rational aesthetic to the Cylinda Line. Stelton's high-quality products exemplify Danish design and are represented in the permanent collections of museums around the world, including the Museum of Modern Art and the Cooper-Hewitt Museum, New York, the Victoria & Albert Museum, London, the Neue Sammlung, Munich and the Kunstindustrimuseum, Copenhagen. ![](images/a8f9a217f085a957c4a4e431a1e03b08a91cbec4d635d7e812c9abf6b718573e.jpg) ![](images/c263f9de59cc542c16e63685fbb369172ee4dd3335c945b892329f69c10e18f2.jpg) △ Erik Magnussen, Model no. 460 cake knife, Model no. 462 pizza servers and Model no. 463 fish server for Stelton, 1980, 1990 & 1990 Arne Jacobsen, ice bucket and ashtray from the Cylinda Line stainless steel hollowware range for Stelton, 1967 ![](images/53cb63a38a9f4fbe7ac06df96a7959698c1a684fb994af1bc10ea24e80091969.jpg) Erik Magnussen, Model no.2000 vacuum jug, Model no. 2001 creamer, Model no. 2002 sugar bowl and Model no. 2005 serving tray for Stelton, 1999 ∇ Erik Magnussen, Mills spice jars and Model no. 580 salt & pepper grinders for Stelton, 1999 ![](images/0c704f8a1801cae1dc1963436dc5faaf95d8ddca03e73818e9de43561490dbc3.jpg)
