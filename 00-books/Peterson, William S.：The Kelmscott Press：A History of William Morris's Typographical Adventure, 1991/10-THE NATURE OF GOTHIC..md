At Exeter College, Oxford, in the 185os Morris, Burne-Jones, and several like-minded friends discovered Ruskin, Rossetti, and the Pre-Raphaelites, toyed with the notion of founding an Anglo-Catholic monastic community of craftsmen, visited country churches and rubbed brasses, and read Tennyson, Chaucer, Keats, Carlyle, and Charlotte Mary Yonge's The Heir of Redclyffe. After taking his degree Morris worked for a short time for G. E. Street, the architect, but within a few years he was married to Jane Burden and well launched on his own distinctive career as a renewer of traditional crafts. Architecture was always for Morris the supreme art, and throughout his life he never wavered in his conviction that all other arts were subservient to it. Yet Morris came to realize that minor crafts such as weaving, wall-decorating, and printing, because they were less ambitious and involved simpler technologies, could be more easily restored to their former glories than architecture itself could. The book in particular, whether written or printed, appealed to him because it was a small, self-contained object; Morris doubted whether it were possible to have buildings generally good in the present day', but he noted that there are not the same difficulties with a book as with many other articles of commerce'.9 In the last year of his life Morris told Wilfrid Scawen Blunt, I remember as a boy going into Canterbury Cathedral and thinking that the gates of heaven had been opened to me. Also when I first saw an illuminated manuscript, these first pleasures which I discovered for myself were stronger than anything else in life.'° (This close identification of the Gothic cathedral and the Gothic book in Morris's mind is significant: in the 189os he was to speak of the arrangement of the Kelmscott Press Chaucer as purely architectural.) Morris's passion for the decorated book never diminished: May Morris remarked that her father and Burne-Jones were for many years such frequent visitors to the manuscript room of the British Museum that ‘no young man in London, outside the staff of the department, was so familiar with its treasures as my father was'.² Indeed, Morris was prepared to argue that ‘if all the other art of the early Middle Ages had disappeared, they might still claim to be considered a great period of art on the strength of their ornamented books'.'3 In 1894 Morris contributed an article entitled Some Notes on the Illuminated Books of the Middle Ages' to the Magazine of Art, and at about the same time he began an essay, never completed, ‘Some Thoughts on the Ornamented Manuscripts of the Middle Ages'. In both essays Morris displayed an impressive technical knowledge of medieval manuscripts; H. M. Hyndman claimed that Morris possessed such remarkable expertise that, during a visit to the Bodleian in about 1882, he was able to identify a whole group of manuscripts in rapid succession for the benefit of one of the librarians.'4 What impresses the reader of Morris's essays today, however, is not his scholarly grasp of a specialized subject (though that is considerable) but his fervently held conviction that such manuscripts represent the craftsmanship of the Middle Ages at its very highest level. There is no evidence that Morris began to collect manuscripts seriously after he left Oxford, though in October 1858 he wrote from France that he was buying old manuscripts and armour and ironwork and enamel'.' The earliest extant catalogue of his personal library, dating from about 1876, lists only six manuscripts, most of which he later sold.' Nor did Morris systematically acquire early printed books in his youth, yet there are ample indications that he was drawn to them just as inevitably as he was to illuminated manuscripts. As a boy, for example, he found in his parents' library a copy of Gerarde's Herbal (1 597), which was to influence the floral patterns of his wallpapers and tapestries and which he was to consult again in 1875 when he studied the recipes of vegetable dyes. Greater pleasures lay ahead: during their undergraduate years Morris and Burne-Jones were introduced to the delights of fifteenth- and sixteenth-century wood-engravings. Two luminaries burst upon us at that time,'Burne-Jones remarked. One was Chaucer and the other was Dürer."7 Morris later felt vaguely embarrassed about his admiration for Dürer, who worked a century too late to be regarded as a medieval artist, but his ingenious solution to this problem was to assert that ‘though his [Dürer's] method was infected by the Renaissançe, his matchless imagination and intellect made him thoroughly Gothic in spirit'.18 At about the time that Morris was founding the Kelmscott Press, he was able to put together within a few years an outstanding collection of incunabula, with particular emphasis on French and German illustrated books. He lectured and wrote enthusiastically on the subject in the 1 89os, and it is characteristic of how Morris's mind worked that his lecture on ‘The Woodcuts of Gothic Books', delivered to the Society of Arts in January 1892,9 concluded with a discussion of the prospects for improving the modern illustrated book; for Morris never approached the history of printing or book-illustration from a merely antiquarian point of view. How, he wanted to know, could we learn from the printers and illustrators of previous centuries, and how could we match their achievements in our own day? As early as 1855 we hear of Morris trying his hand at drawing on wood and cutting the designs himself', inspired by ‘The Maids of Elfen-Mere', an engraving designed by Rossetti which appeared in William Allingham's Day and Night Songs.2° It is clear what Morris and Burne-Jones found offensive in routine wood-engravings of the nineteenth century. Burne-Jones summed up their indictment of contemporary work in a letter written to his sister-in-law in 1862: As to scribbly work, it enrages one beyond endurance. Nearly all book and periodical illustration is full of it — drawings, you know the kind, that have wild work in all the corners, stupid, senseless rot that takes an artist half a minute to sketch and an engraver half a week to engrave, for scribble is fearful labour to render. My dear, look at most things in “Once a Week" — the wasted time of poor engravers in rendering all that scrawl, if rightly used, might fill England with beautiful work." The solution, Morris and his friend came to believe, lay in stern simplicity: austere outlines in bold, vigorous strokes enhanced by only a minimum of shading, which was never to be rendered with cross-hatching. Such a formula, as Morris recognized, pointed back directly to the fifteenth century, and it is noteworthy that all the wood-engravings produced by Morris or done under his direction — from the first tentative experinents of 1855 to the great Chaucer illustrations of 1896 — are, in their primitive strength, more closely related to the medieval woodcut than to the post-Bewick engraving. Morris and Burne-Jones were occasionally capable of responding sympathetically to modern wood-engravings. In fact, his interest in the book as a physical object flickered on and off for several decades. Morris seems to have been consulted about such matters from time to time by Rossetti, who drew a highly successful title-page for his sister's Goblin-Market (1862) and designed attractive bindings for his own and others' books. Burne-Jones continued to contribute occasional illustrations to books and periodicals. One day in the early 187os Morris is reported to have picked up a volume printed by Aldus Manutius in about 149o and to have remarked as he turned over the pages, Ah! I wish I could get my books printed like that!' Surrounded by friends with a professional interest in book-production, Morris, as both a writer and an artist, felt much drawn to printing. On the whole, though, he resisted the temptation: one senses that, when the Kelmscott Press was established in 1891, Morris was able to throw himself into it with such extraordinary pent-up energy because it was something he had longed to do for decades. ## In the meantime there were several forays into the world of bookdesign which deserve to be described in niore detail, since in them we catch glimpses of Morris working his way, tentatively and circuitously, towards the goal, as yet unseen, of the Kelmscott Press. These episodes include the unsuccessful attempt to produce an illustrated edition of The Earthly Paradise in the 186os; the abandoned ornaments and illustrations for Love is Enough in 1871; Morris's calligraphic experiments, mostly dating fron the 187os; Morris's editorship of the Commonweal in the 188os; and the last three books designed by Morris – The House of the Wolfings (1889), The Roots of the Mountains (1890), and The Story of Gunnlaug the Wormn-Tongue (1891) — before the opening of the Kelmscott Press. Morris's first volume of verse, The Defence of Guenevere, was published by Bell and Daldy in 1858 and printed at the Chiswick Press. The typography was neat and inoffensive — precisely that sort of bland mid-nineteenth-century printed page that was later to make Morris apoplectic - ornamented only by the usual Chiswick Press woodengraved headpieces and decorated initials cut by Mary Byfield. When in the following decade Morris met F. S. Ellis, the antiquarian bookseller and publisher who was to play such a key role in the Kelmscott Press, he decided to allow Ellis to publish his books; and thus Morris's early connection with the Chiswick Press was broken, not to be renewed until the 188os. Morris's great literary undertaking of the 186os was a massive collection of poetic tales grouped together under the title The Earthly Paradise. (The Life and Death of Jason, published in 1867, was originally conceived as a part of it.) Since the first edition (an unillustrated octavo) of The Earthly Paradise contains 1,22o pages of text, this projected illustrated version would no doubt have been a stupendously large book. It seems fair to assume that Morris abandoned the book primarily because its production (especially the engraving of the blocks) would have proved interminable. Furthermore, the trial pages reveal another serious problem, the incompatibility between the typefaces and the illustrations (not to mention Miss Byfield's initials). The heavy, bold wood-engravings overpower the text, even when it is set in the slightly darker Basle Roman. What this unsuccessful experinent must have demonstrated to Morris was that the revival of fine printing could not be brought about by the improvement of illustrations alone. At the conclusion of a lecture delivered in 1892, Morris summarized the lesson that he himself had learned, at least partially, in the 186os: An illustrated book, where the illustrations are more than mere illustrations of the printed text, should be a harmonious work of art. The type, the spacing of the type, the position of the pages of print on the paper, should be considered from the artistic point of view. The illustrations should not have a mere accidental connection with the other ornaments and the type, but an essential and artistic connection. . . . This is the only possible way in which you can get beautiful books.' Yet the great illustrated edition of The Earthly Paradise, though it never materialized in exactly the form envisaged by Morris, was to have a curious afterhistory. With the help of Walter Crane, Burne-Jones, who often borrowed from his own earlier work, painted in 1872, a ‘Cupid and Psyche' frieze, based on some of the Earthly Paradise designs, at No. I Palace Green, London, the Earl of Carlisle's town house.33 In 1897 there were plans afoot to publish theCupid and Psyche' section of the poem, set in Troy type, with Burne-Jones's illustrations; a contract was drawn up with Longmans, Green, and seven trial pages were printed, but the undertaking was abandoned with Burne-Jones's death the following year.
