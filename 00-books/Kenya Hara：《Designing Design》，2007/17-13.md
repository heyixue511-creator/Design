My personal favorites for this book are chapters 4 and 7. I've always wondered about the topic of “white" but never thought to write about it at length. Given Hara's incredible history of design work that exhibits a rare purity in form and meaning, I’m not surprised that Hara can carry on a perfectly rational conversation about something so basic as the color “white"as if it were as important as a national crisis. The word “exformation"is new to me. Wikipedia tells me that this word was coined by the Danish writer Tor Nørretranders and can be defined thus, “Exformation is everything we do not actually say but have in our heads, when or before we say anything at all." I think the word describes a kind of unwritten spirituality with respect to information that implicity underlies the animist culture ofJapan. For a long while, I've given out Hara's books to friends as gifts but they've always lamented that by being written in Japanese, the volumes are just great books “to look at." Now with this new book published by Lars Müller, Hara can not only get people to see how he thinks, but to actually read how he thinks. And you will find in this book that his thoughts are incredibly complex and deeply rooted in concepts that span all cultures andcivilizations with nothinglost in translation to themysterious world of Japan. The irony of Hara's work is that in a culture of perceived "Japanese simplicity,"he expertly reveals that what lies at the heart of a Zen-like nothing is an incredibly deep, complex, and meaningful something.
