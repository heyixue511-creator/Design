# PHILIPPE STARCK b.1949 FRENCHMAN WHOSE PROVOCATIVE DESIGNS HAVE BROUGHT HIM INTERNATIONAL CELEBRITY. Joie de vivre is the perfect phrase to describe the aura surrounding the designs of Paris-born Philippe Starck. His products are ubiquitous, transcending categories to include everything from furniture to lighting, tableware, and all manner of household accessories, bringing celebrity status to something as quotidian as a lemon juicer. Starck's designs are imbued with personality, humor, and a bit of swagger, as is his public persona; in 2o04, an awards jury designated him the "most unusual, quirkiest, and exciting designer of the past twenty years.' He is certainly one of the best-known, even to a non-design-savvy public. Starck was trained in design at the prestigious École Nissim de Camondo in Paris, graduating in ı968, and in 1969 was named art director at Pierre Cardin, where he designed furniture under the Cardin name. He formed his namesake firm in i98o and began designing chairs that gained notice for both their offbeat design and provocative names, like Miss Dorn (1982), Dr. Sonderbar (1983), Von Vogelsang (i984), and Dr Glob (i988). He designed several nightclubs, but it was his work for the Café Costes (i984) in Paris, and the much-copied three-legged Costes chair, that made him internationally famous. Starck went on to design two New York boutique hotels: the Royalton (i988) and Paramount (i99o), both of which, widely published, served to secure his reputation as designer of the moment and trend-setter for the young and hip. These interiors were not so much postmodern as eclectic mixtures of untraditional, freewheeling forms and mismatched furniture. He reprised his success with the Delano Hotel (i995) in Miami Beach, where glossy white walls and floors set off an assortment of furniture in retro-modern styles. Since then, several other hotels have enjoyed the benefits of the striking interiors and copious publicity gained from the designer's deft touch and marketable name. Starck soon became the public face of avant-garde design in France, a country more often known for its ties to traditionalism, and he was awarded commissions for high-profile projects including, in i984, redecorating the interiors of the Élysée Palace quarters of then president François Mitterand, and in ı988 designing a major exhibition showcasing French design at the Centre Georges Pompidou. ## ![](images/c732a8b91aaf96a9b5af0e45ef31b1b938b809e15ff2fac9f2de0497e971bf9f.jpg) He has also designed objects for ALESSI (see under "Edited Design"), the most notable being the Juicy Salif (i99o), a skinny, three-legged lemon juicer that is more a piece of sculpture than a functional kitchen utensil. Starck has also applied his design skills to glassware, lighting, cutlery, and numerous other objects, all of which have benefited from bearing the designer's name. Following MICHAEL GRAVES's (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") successful example, Starck brought his distinctive style to the mass market in 2oo2 with acollection of colorful and curvy houseware for Target that included what might be the ultimate designer object: a seductively curved toothbrush (i992). He has designed for Driade\* and for his firm XO, which he established with Gérard Mialet in 1985. For Kartell\*, his designs include the bestselling Louis Ghost chair, a traditional form rendered in transparent polycarbonate. The book Philippe Starck (2oo4), chronicling his career and his designs, sports a fittingly tongue-in-cheek cover depicting the artist, barechested, with his head on backwards.
