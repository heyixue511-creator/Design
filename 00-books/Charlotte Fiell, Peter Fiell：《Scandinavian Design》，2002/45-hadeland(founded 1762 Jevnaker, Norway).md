![](images/b313539c9747eebe818f6f2ff7efc9a348976c64bfbb1d3b418da851930de5a6.jpg) Gro Bergslien, apple shaped glass paperweight in opaque glass for Hadeland, 1976 # hadeland (founded 1762 Jevnaker, Norway) ![](images/b8a7d6c53fefd8cc3bba70343daba8b73e97c7bfff0168108e2373194c6f2241.jpg) In 1762, the Danish-Norwegian state under the rule of Christian IV began building a glassworks on a site it owned in Jevnaker. Three years later the Hadeland Glassworks fired up its ovens for the first time and started producing colourful cobalt blue bottles, pharmaceutical glassware, and floats for fishing nets. Hadeland was an enlightened employer who granted its workforce various social rights and advantages, including the provision ofschooling for the workers' children. The glassworks was sold in 1824 and in 1842 Hadeland was amalgamated with two other glassworks to form a privately owned company. After the introduction of transparent colourless glass in 1852, Hadeland began producing tableware products, including drinking glasses, bowls, dishes and vases – both plain and undecorated. In 1898, the glassworks became part of the Christiania Glasmagasin company, which had (and stil has) a large shop on the main square in Oslo (or as it was known then, Christiania). In response to government pressure on factories to increase the design quality of their products, in 1929 Hadeland appointed Sverre Petterson (1884-1958) as its first artistic director. Initially, Petterson's designs were light and graceful, but by the mid-1930s they became heavier and more architectural, thus reflecting the influence of the Art Deco style. Under his guidance, the glassworks hired thetalented designer Willy Johansson (a third-generation glass blower) in 1936. At Hadeland, Johansson learnt sandblasting techniques from an in-house apprenticeship with the sculptor Ståle Kyllingstad, as well as engraving methods at the Christiania Glasmagasin. From 1947, Johansson headed the Hadeland design team and in 1954 he received a Diplôme d'Honneur at the X Milan Triennale for his glassware, which was distinguished by simple elemental forms. During the 1950s, Hadeland also produced glassware designed by Herman Bongard (b. 1921)and Arne Jon Jutrem (b. 1929), which also possessed an unadorned solidity that emphasized the intrinsic qualities of the glass. Since the 1950s, Hadeland has produced both pressed and blownglassware, ranging from theutilitarian to the artistic (such as Gro Bergslien's apple forms of 1976). In 1988 Arne Jon Jutrem became the artistic director of Hadeland's design department and during his tenure the glassworks has produced distinctive sculptural glassware designed by, among others, Maud Bugge and Lena Hansson. Although perhaps lacking the sheer brio of Finnish glassware, Hadeland's output has nonetheless been central to Norwegian design.
