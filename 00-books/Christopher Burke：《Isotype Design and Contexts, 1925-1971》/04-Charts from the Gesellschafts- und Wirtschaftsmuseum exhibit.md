## Design at the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum

In 1927 Otto Neurath wrote:

Social museums have as their object the person as a social being; here is the place where the methodology of visually representing social connections is cultivated.

The Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum has begun the construction of such a methodology.7³

From around May 1927 both the permanent and travelling exhibitions of the Museum were served by its offices and workshops above the central municipal savings bank in Vienna's fourteenth district There it had about a dozen large rooms at its disposal, where all aspects of its production process could be accommodated. The number of people employed ranged between 24 and 5o."ª

The Museum's first office had been in a prefabricated building of the övsk, where initially only Neurath and an administrator (Josef Jodlbauer) worked.7⁵ A key member of the team began work on 1 March 1925: Marie Reidemeister (later Marie Neurath). As already noted, her expression of willingness to collaborate with the Museum was an important factor in spurring Neurath to move ahead with his plans. Her initial terms of employment stated that she would work for a third of the normal working week (14 hours) on administrative duties, statistical tasks and various preparatory work for the hygiene exhibition.7 Her small salary was supplemented by being paid (from publisher's advances) as Neurath's secretary for writing his book Lebensgestaltung und Klassenkampf (1928).

She remembered that sketches for pictorial charts were made initially by Neurath, and then by them both together: 'what he needed was educational talent (with a visual slant)' – a description that fitted Marie Reidemeister perfectly." Otto Neurath's own graphic skills were sometimes crude, but effective, whereas Reidemeister's were more refined and controlled.78 In these early days she once designed and executed a whole chart herself by hand, which would not have been possible a few years later as the process became more systematized and mechanized. She and Neurath formed a close personal and professional relationship from the beginning: they often met before work, at the Café Dobner near Vienna's Sezession building, and she almost always accompanied him to the frequent lectures he gave in the evenings."º

The Museum's offices soon moved to the administrative building of Vienna's third district, where there was more space for the artists and architects employed on a temporary basis to graphically execute the charts (among them was Grete Schütte-Lihotzky; see the detailed list in appendix c below). With work on the two large exhibitions of 1926, the team began to expand, notably by the appointment of a full-time artist, Erwin Bernath, in autumn 1926. Bernath was a Swiss who had studied at the school of art in Vienna, and he remained with the team for ten years. At the same time Reidemeister's position became clearer:

My role in the production process was now clearly established, even though the name given to it – transformation – was not yet in use. I put each sketch to Otto; he usually had a further idea, which I then pursued, until we were both satisfied. So developed, step by step, our method, to which Otto one day gave the name 'Vienna Method'.8º

At the beginning of 1927 Reidemeister's working hours at the Museum were doubled and her salary was increased to match that of its best paid draftsperson [Zeichner] in respect of the fact that she now carried out 'predominantly scientific work'.81 Soon afterwards, around the time that the Museum's offices and studios moved to spacious accommodation in the building of Vienna's central municipal savings bank, Friedrich Bauermeister was employed by the Museum as a second transformer. He was a German who had come to Vienna to participate in the settlers' movement. He had worked with Neurath in the secretariat of the övsK, and when that work ended, Neurath suggested that he come to work at the Museum.

The role of the transformer (Transformator in German) was considered by Neurath as the linchpin of the Vienna Method and Isotype. The first use of the term ‘transformation' in print was in 1932, where Neurath explained it as the metamorphosis [Umwandlung] of statistical material into pictorial sketches'.8² A phrase that later became attached to the transformer – 'trustee of the public' – appeared in print from the end of 1929, as Treuhander der Konsumenten in German.8³ This concept became influential later, in the development of information design as a discipline from the 1970s.

In the first lengthy essay by Neurath about the Vienna Method published in English, 'Museums of the future' (1933), he summarized the purpose of transformation:

Perhaps the most original section of the Vienna central institution is the department of transformation. On it the educational purpose of the whole enterprise is concentrated. While in other museums it is customary to have each department administered by a specialist who in turn must direct as best he can the painters, modellers and other assistants

Here Neurath emphasized the virtues of consistency and standardization that emerge from the controlling role of the transformer, in accordance with his original concept of the Museum as 'a schoolbook on a grand scale':

we must conceive each individual chart as part of a colossal system, a new Orbis pictus; so, we should not only consider how to harmonize the new chart with previous charts but, further, how it will fit with charts that will only be made later on.

Although he sometimes stressed the potential for international standardization in this respect, he did not advocate simple uniformity:

Each chart may be distinct from all the others – each has its own life – yet all together they form a whole, towards which the representatives of the Vienna Method are working.

From this it follows that the transformation' of scientific material is such a responsible and essential task. The scientists bring data and collected material. They wish to define the area of the problem, but the statistical material thus gathered is not yet ready to be drawn graphically. First the visual conventions must be applied. The reduction of figures is necessary, rounding off, the choice of form and colour. Only when all that is complete does the difficult work of the graphic artist begin. 86

In this sense the transformer was the guardian of the educational principal of Isotype, that he who knows best what to omit is the best teacher'.87 Furthermore, in creatively exploring graphic possibilities through analysis of source material, the transformer fulfilled a role that would now be labelled 'designer'. The modern concept of a designer was not yet established in the 192os, and this is one of the several ways in which the Vienna Method team were pioneers.88 Its leading members were certainly aware of contemporary developments in design (due to connections with the Bauhaus, for example) but the processes of graphic design and museum design at the Gesellschaftsund Wirtschaftsmuseum were subservient to its overriding priority of education. Neurath made this clear from the beginning, when he wrote in 1925: 'Educational considerations are decisive for what is included in a pictorial representation, and what is not.'89 By 1931 he could describe the work in the 'technical department' of the Museum as: 'A typical collective work of modern graphic design [Gebrauchsgraphik], which is being brought to ever greater perfection; a close fusion of scientific research work, systematic pedagogy and artistic design [Gestaltung].'

This description fits best the Museum's activity at its high point around 1929, when the monumental publication of 1oo charts, Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft, was in preparation. For this, many new consultants, artists and technicians were employed. At around this time Marie Neurath proposed new rules for transformation, which Neurath accepted. She recalled: 'This was an interesting time; our “academy" came to meet us every week: a historian, a statistician, a cartographer, an art historian, a historian of technology, and us two.'1 She described her role in an interview late in her life:

It was my task to convert the knowledge that they brought us into pictorial form; in other words, I sketched the charts, then we had our graphic artists [entwerfenden Grafiker], who were handed my sketches in order to design the final image. But I was the connecting link between these scientists and the artists.9²

According to Marie Neurath, early experiments in producing charts by cutting shapes from coloured paper were unsatisfactory: 'the charts did not have yet the typographic quality which Neurath wanted'.9s 'We were still groping for a technique and a style. It was still noticeable to us which artist had made which chart, in shapes as well as in choice of colour.'9 In fact, an attempt was made at an early stage to give the charts a ‘typographic' character by having some pictograms cast as metal type, which could then be composed in the traditional way for letterpress printing. Neurath explained later that this was quickly abandoned ‘because one can hardly get a sufficient amount of differentiation in the appearance of charts and the whole becomes monotonous'.97

The move towards a consistent and apparently impersonal style was due principally to the third key member of the Vienna Museum team – Gerd Arntz, a German artist specializing in woodcut prints. Some of Arntz's work was exhibited in Düsseldorf in 1926 and Neurath saw it there when attending the ‘Gesolei' exhibition (on his first trip back to Germany since being expelled in 192o; see p. 21 above). Neurath immediately perceived a schematic treatment of figurative subjects in Arntz's prints that would suit his intentions for the Vienna Method. Neurath's friend, the art historian Franz Roh, set up a meeting with Arntz (then living in Düsseldorf) and Neurath invited him to work for the Museum.98 Arntz demurred initially but began to contribute work by post. Neurath acquired some of his prints for the Museum at this time and these also contributed to a gradual infiltration of Arntz's style into its work.

In 1928 Arntz went for a few months' trial work at the Museum, and at the beginning of 1929 he moved to Vienna with his family to work almost full-time (he stipulated that one day a week be reserved for his own ‘free' work, but he was not able to really do this until 193o, after the very busy period preparing Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft).” He was impressed by the socialist vigour' of Vienna, which he considered to be stronger there than in Germany, where he had moved in communist circles.1º0 He lived with his family in an apartment in one of the new municipal apartment buildings, Rabenhof, in Vienna's third district.

Attempts at systematic design extended beyond the graphic work of the Museum to its exhibition spaces. Neurath commissioned the architect Josef Frank to design exhibition furniture. Frank, who had designed some of Vienna's housing settlements for the övsK, was considered by Neurath as the most versatile and adaptable socialist architect then at work.¹º Frank had previously designed an acclaimed interior for a museum of East Asian art in Cologne, which had featured customized cabinetry.1º⁸ For the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum he designed a flexible system of wood framing for the charts, based on a standardization of chart sizes. This system stemmed from Neurath's conviction that the Museum's exhibitions should be easily modifiable and transportable, given that they did not deal with rare or unique treasures' but rather with reproducible items. He considered the use of standardized modules as particularly suitable for travelling exhibitions, the construction of which the Museum always keeps in mind'.109

The framing structures were already in use at the Museum's first premises on the Ringstrasse. Through his close friendship with Frank, it appears that Neurath was involved in this reunification of the Austrian Werkbund, which occurred around the same time that Neurath became active in another Viennese forum – the Verein Ernst Mach (Ernst Mach society), where the ideas of the Vienna Circle were first made public. ## Statistics in pictures

As an empiricist, Otto Neurath considered statistical data to be a precondition for sociology and modern planning: 'All discussions of social and economic questions link to statistical data. Planned control of healthcare, transport, production and consumption today needs extensive statistics.'¹³7 He perceived this need to have arisen at the beginning of the modern era: centralizing capital needed statistics but so too did critics of capitalist economy, such as Marx and Engels. Neurath was quite aware of the controlling purposes for which statistics had been employed, not least by official bodies of the Austro-Hungarian empire, yet he felt that this did not render them unusable:

Statistics have the reputation of being boring, incomprehensible and forged. Statistics are really often wrong; but not because this way of examining things has a falsifying' effect, rather instead because it is applied in an unskilled and unclear way, or because it consciously employs falsehood. This happens frequently, but statistics should not be marked with the responsibility for this. Statistics really are often boring and incomprehensible'; but this is principally due to their not being purposefully represented, and to, among other things, the fact that statistical writers mostly use too many numbers and rows of numbers, and in general include too many details and thereby confuse the reader. To represent purposefully' means this: using the results of this extremely useful research method, which provides the decisive relationships of earthly and social things in countless areas by numbers and proportions, in educating people only when they are simplified enough for looking through; when one has extracted from the immeasurable stock of figures, of which most are only the concern of specialists, the really important and in broad terms instructive material, and synthesized it into clear and meaningful part-representations of our worldscape.138

It was a central task of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in Wien to represent such data in attractive and memorable form so that it could be understood by all kinds of people. In the years around 193o, Neurath wrote about the possibilities offered by pictorial statistics in children's education, but also, with an augmented rhetoric, about the role that they could play in the class struggle. In an essay titled 'Statistics and proletariat' he wrote: 'Statistics are a tool of the proletarian struggle, statistics are an essential part of socialist order, statistics are joy for the international proletariat struggling hard with the ruling classes.'139 Neurath went so far as to propose pictorial statistics as a Marxist tool, in an article about their applicability to the workers' movement:

But such quantity-pictures [Mengenbilder] are not only easy to grasp visually and thereby well suited to the worker. They give from the outset a fundamentally Marxist philosophy, because they reproduce all that can be objectively represented in terms of quantities. The quantity pictures force a certain completeness of view, they immediately excite comparison. They give a feeling of security, as one has no fear of the little figures as one does of curves and numbers.¹4º

He also pre-empted criticism for what today would be called dumbing down':

The over-educated and the over-refined will find something to carp about in all this, and will think that the rounding off in the figures is too coarse. The Museum and its collaborators counter with the position: to remember simplified pictures of quantities is better than to forget accurate figures! Or more precisely: than not having looked at accurate figures in any way correctly! Just at this moment, when understanding of the whole world should be opened up to Central-European workers, it is important to have such a means of modern enlightenment at our disposal.141

Instead of condescending to less-educated adults, Neurath wanted to empower them with far-reaching information and show them how they fitted into the world's complex of interconnections. In his essay Colonialpolitical enlightenment through picture statistics' he suggested:

The worker has an interest in knowing how war and peace can depend on the oil conflict between Standard Oil and the Shell group, and wants to understand how it happens that the Soviet Union makes its oil available to the usA when it stands against the capitalist world in other ways. ...

It is not sufficient to learn about the interdependencies, one must know how to assess their relative implications, the extent of the individual measures and movements! 142

This last sentence makes explicit that the Vienna Method could never be simply neutral: the adaptation of raw data into what Neurath called Merkbilder (memorable pictures) required opinions and decisions on the part of the producers about what elements were most important. Pictorial education with the Vienna Method and Isotype moulded information into ‘visual arguments' (as Neurath later came to call them) for educational and sometimes political purposes. Perhaps this partly explains the mysterious remark made by Neurath in 1929 that, not only was the Vienna Method less accessible to bourgeois intellectuals due to its optical content', but that 'for certain reasons the spread of this [Vienna] Method goes against bourgeois ideology.' 143

## The Vienna Method, anti-metaphysics, and socialist education

From around 1928, there was a confluence of three streams in Neurath's work: the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum & the Vienna Method education for socialism, and his contribution to the logical empiricism of the Vienna Circle.148 Although the beginnings of the Vienna Method date from around 1923/4, it acquired a maturity with the collaboration of Gerd Arntz from 1928. This was also the year in which Neurath's book Lebensgestaltung und Klassenkampf was published,149 and in which he became active in the Verein Ernst Mach. Neurath led the participation of Vienna Circle members as officers and lecturers at the Verein Ernst Mach, initiating the public phase of logical empiricism. In a printed announcement of the society, 'Otto Neurath, museum director' was listed as its secretary and he was scheduled to give a lecture on 'Antimetaphysics and social science'. Friedrich Stadler suggests that Neurath intended to ally the educational work of the Museum with adult education of a 'popular-theoretical' kind through the Verein Ernst Mach. Neurath indicated these intertwined objectives when summarizing the trajectory of the Vienna Method of Pictorial Statistics up to 1933:

Lectures and instruction continue to take place about this new method and its application to general adult education, and in particular workers' education, which served as the original starting point for these endeavours.1⁵1

Neurath's ‘anti-metaphysical' approach was certainly a common feature of all his work at this time. In terms of workers' education, he expressed it as follows:

In this, enlightenment about human beings and material things, about connections of all sorts, plays a decisive role. Excitation of enthusiasm and the portrayal of glorious goals do not come to the fore, rather much more the purely factual description of that which is. 152

These words are taken from an article in the socialist newspaper Die Arbeiterzeitung about the 'scientific world-conception' ('Wissenschaftliche Weltauffassung'). This was also the title of the Vienna Circle manifesto published in the same year and co-written by Neurath.¹⁵³ Here it was explained that‘The scientific world-conception rejects metaphysical philosophy', reflecting Neurath's strong antipathy to the German philosophical tradition. In this sense, the Vienna Method could be seen as a manifestation of 'physicalism', which was the term Neurath used to describe his version of logical empiricism. As the word implies, physicalism restricted discourse to descriptions of the physical world; it only recognized 'statements about spatio-temporal things'.167 The Vienna Method was perfectly suited to this, using pictograms and maps to show quantities (changing over time) and spatial distribution. Neurath's colleague in the Vienna Circle, the physicist Philipp Frank, recalled later Neurath's intentions for a physicalist language:

He suggested that sense data should be dropped as elementary concepts of the logical structure of the world and replaced by physical things. Instead of building up the system of human knowledge upon concepts like 'red spot' or 'feeling of warmth', one should use elementary symbols expressing concepts like 'rock' or ‘table', and define 'redness', or 'warmth' as derived concepts. ... Neurath worked out a system based on physical things as elementary concepts and called by him physicalism'.1⁶8

With its use of pictograms to iconically represent physical objects, the Vienna Method corresponds directly to this strategy. As Neurath explained:

## Diversification

Although keeping archives is a conventional task of museums, it may not seem obvious that the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum, with its orientation to the present and future, should keep a historical archive: yet, alongside the photographic records it kept of its own work, an Archiv für bildhafte Pädagogik (Archive of pictorial education) was assembled at the Museum by collecting 'descriptive pictures [Sachbilder] from all times and peoples'.17 This reflected Neurath's interest in the history of visual education and his willingness to learn from past attempts.¹" Also within this archive was a collection of what Neurath called 'sociological graphics', consisting of prints by artists associated with the Museum but not made specifically for the Museum's purposes (see chapter 4, pp. 234–5 below).

Additionally, the Museum built up a collection of original photographs taken by its staff photographer, Walter Pfitzner. These included a record of the new housing constructed by the Vienna municipality, but also series of photographs documenting work procedures of different professions for career advice purposes (see pp. 249–56 below).

A new departure from 1931, which Neurath probably included under the fourth category above, was the publication of the Museum's own journal, Fernunterricht (Distance learning/teaching). This featured pictorial charts (some recycled from other sources) and statistical tables with short texts, including some written (anonymously) by Neurath.183 He outlined his intentions for the periodical to Else Herz (wife of Carl mayor of Berlin-Kreuzberg):

It should be a beautiful thing. We have high hopes for it. I want to add a children's supplement to it later so that we create a new type of enlightening literature! I believe that you and your husband will see immediately that, in these dreadful times of crisis, when the organizing of exhibitions meets with difficulties, such a publication can be used to achieve real success.184

Indeed the world economic crisis was a recurrent theme in the journal, as well as providing the likely explanation for its increasingly irregular appearance until its eleventh and last issue in 1933. (For its last two issues the publication was renamed Bildstatistik [Pictorial statistics].)

The introduction to the first issue appealed to readers for suggestions on content; in the sixth issue this was reiterated: 'Not we, the editors, but the readers should direct Fernunterricht and determine or influence its content.' In this spirit, a page of each issue in its first year contained answers to readers' questions. With both its title and editorial policy, Fernunterricht seems to have been intended to create the kind of reading community that is now so easily achieved with the interactivity of the internet.

## Dissolution

After Austria's brief civil war in February 1934, and the subsequent seizure of power by Austrian fascists under Engelbert Dollfuss, police came to search Neurath's office at the Museum. Luckily he was in Moscow at that time, and from there he travelled via Czechoslovakia to The Hague, never to return to Vienna. Marie Neurath later recalled the prescience of her husband's preparations:

The plans that Otto had thought out proved themselves well: to rent a nearby room in Vienna, at once, to equip it with the name of our ‘Stichting' (in Dutch), and to take there everything that was essential for the continuation of our work in Holland. This had in fact been accomplished by the time that our office in Ullmannstrasse was officially sealed, with a small piece of paper.199

Some vital reference material was saved in this way – including the files of pictograms, charts and photographs produced by the Museum. (By swift action of friends in the Hague, these then survived the Nazi occupation of the Netherlands and were returned to the Isotype Institute after the Second World War.) The rest of the Museum's equipment and material in Vienna was seized and valued at 15o,ooo Austrian Schillings. The Volkshalle was cleared of exhibition material, which was subsequently destroyed in a warehouse fire.2ºº The organizing body of the Museum was reconstituted in 1936 as the Österreichisches Institut für Bildstatistik (Austrian institute for pictorial statistics), and then, after the Nazi take-over of 1938, as the Institut für Ausstellungstechnik und Bildstatistik (Institute for exhibition technique and pictorial statistics). ## Die bunte Welt (The vivid world) 1929

Die bunte Welt was the first substantial book conceived, written and designed at the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum. It was also the first publication of charts made with the Vienna Method of Pictorial Statistics to feature colour extensively. The book reflects the dual nature of the Vienna Method in producing material that could be understood simultaneously by schoolchildren and by adults with little formal education. Although it is subtitled 'quantitypictures for youth' (Mengenbilder für die Jugend), the graphic qualities and complexity of its charts are equal to those made largely for adult audiences. The Museum stated: 'Its design makes it suitable principally for older children, in order to broaden their horizons and to awaken in them interest for important questions of life.'¹

Not long after the book was published, Marie Reidemeister discussed Die bunte Welt exclusively in terms of workers' education. She proposed it as a body of factual information presented in a unified way, instead of piecemeal through various lectures and courses: 'The great advantage of these pictures ... is that they are more rapidly grasped than words, that they cannot be so easily misunderstood, that they stick better in the memory, and that they awake and leave a clear impression.'²

Despite some surprising simplifications, the book's concise text deals with political and economic issues in a way that is still interesting today, even for adults. Its deceptively simple summary of world cultures moves subtly away from a Europe-centred point of view, and is driven by an inherently critical stance on Western capitalism. (cB)
