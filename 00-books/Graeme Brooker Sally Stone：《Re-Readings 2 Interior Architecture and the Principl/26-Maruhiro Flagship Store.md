The fl oor has first of all a pragmatic meaning, much more than the walls and the especially the ceiling. One must be able to move around on it and place objects on it.

– Pierre Von Meiss, Elements of Architecture. <sup>23</sup>

As well as being pragmatic, and satisfying particular requirements such as the placement of objects, for example furniture, the thoughtful design of a fl oor plane can produce an unusual and inspiring element of the interior; one that can be startlingly provocative, even though simply executed and elegantly thought through.

Maruhiro, based in the area around Nagasaki, is the leading producer of Hasami Porcelain in Japan. It has a history, dating back to the 17th century, of producing tableware and making and distributing ceramics across the country. In order to make Maruhiro’s new fl agship store memorable, Seki designed a new fl oor inside the simple one storey building constructed from 25,000 pieces of rejected pottery from around the region. Each piece was carefully stacked and then moulded together with poured concrete to make a series of ‘bricks’ from the pottery. Seki did this in order to form an elevated plinth upon which products could be displayed and

customers could interact with them, never losing the connection between the fi nished object and the processes that it took to get them there. The 25,000 pieces of pottery were sourced from the Hasami region. Each of these pieces was known as Shinikiji in Japanese. These are objects that were found to be fl awed after the initial bisque fi ring by their respective local production facilities. ## Sake no hana

Location London, UK

Remodelled 2008

Designer Kengo Kuma & Associates / Denton Corker Marshall

![](images/566e2a9dfe962cf5d70818d29ddfedebf4aaa9d90df2d8ce2a8a0f76584b2dff.jpg)

Figure 3.31 The Japanese Cypress timber soffi t and walls of the upper level restaurant signify the difference in quality of food and also the time expected in which to consume it.

As well as enclosing services and ordering internal spaces, a soffi t can be used to exemplify the narrative of both its context and the function of an interior space. Sake No Hana, an exclusive Japanese restaurant owned by Alan Yau, the originator of Wagamama and Hakasan, occupies the ground and fi rst fl oor of the Grade II listed Economist building, in Mayfair, central London. Designed in 1964 by Alison and Peter Smithson, the building was highly regarded for a number of innovative features, including its carefully thought out approach to not just the buildings themselves but the open public spaces in-between. The structural simplicity of the building, along with its

‘Roach’ cladding, an expressive fossil-rich Portland stone, created a landmark building, described by Reyner Banham, and the Smithsons, as an example of ‘New Brutalism’.

The restaurant was designed to occupy two fl oors of the smallest of the three towers, marking the corner of Ryder and St James Street. The ground fl oor was designed to accommodate a sushi bar, while the upper level would contain a more formal Japanese restaurant. The corner entrance to the restaurant encouraged the diners to choose the more informal sushi bar experience, arranged around a counter-kitchen and facing St James St, or to ascend the escalator to the upper-level restaurant.

Both the informal sushi bar and the upper-level main dining space were enclosed by an atmospheric tree-like framework of bamboo, on the ground level, and Japanese Cypress on the upper fl oor. On the ground fl oor the bamboo columns were 100mm in diameter, and formed into a series of columns that supported a soffi t consisting of a series of 60mm bamboo beams bolted together to form an overhead lattice. The frame of beams undulated through the interior and across the ceiling. Like an inverted ziggurat, its edges were just one beam thick as they reached the edges of the space, and the external windows, but becoming much deeper as it signalled something important, such as the entrance, where it acted as a signalling device to passers-by on the busy street. Lighting, in the form of Japanese paper lantern pendants, were suspended over the sushi counter. The interior walls were

lined with bamboo while the exterior walls were constructed from cypress veneer.

Upstairs the soffi t was constructed from a framework of Japanese Cypress. The 100 by 100mm columns supported a lattice of 100 by 30mm beams. These were fastened with wedges, as opposed to bolts, aligning the craftsmanship of this level’s interior shell with the slower pace of the consumption of the food in this part of the restaurant and hinting at the quality and time expended on not only the food’s intake but also its preparation. The interior wall was constructed from simple cedar louvres.

The main dining space was placed on a raised platform, and organised around the human scale of tatami mats and low tables. Guests are invited to sit on cushions on the mats, cross-legged in a traditional Japanese dining style. ## Clyde Frazier’s Wine and Dine

![](images/e5487c74d2e5f2cefb7adb4b9d2434b22f6050c1867132908a0d8d76a3d13abe.jpg)

The calculations of George Bailey on the economics of the slab skyscraper, bring together three environmental aids – acoustic tiling, air-conditioning, and fluorescent lighting, that were to add up to one of the key inventions of recent architecture: the suspended ceiling. – Reyner Banham, The Architecture of the Well Tempered Environment. <sup>24</sup>

Figure 3.34 Careful analysis of Walt Frazier’s wardrobe resulted in the development of the conceptual thinking and ultimately the deployment of the expressive fl oating soffi t.

Often considered as a workmanlike component of any interior, an expressive soffi t design can instead become the most startling and original element of an interior space. In many projects the important aspect or main role of a ceiling plane is to disguise the servicing and acoustic dimensions of a space. Beyond that the designer is free to develop the element in whichever way they see fi t. In some extreme instances, the soffi t can be used as metaphor representing the client, a

depiction of the identity of the space and a symbol of the person who has generated the impetus of the project.

With its frontage adjacent to 10th Avenue and occupying a whole block between 37th and 38th Streets in New York, Clyde Frazier’s Wine and Dine is not a sports bar in the traditional sense. Initiated by its owner, a former professional basketball player and now announcer for the New York Knicks, the restaurant houses a dining space, lounge, bar and kitchen as well as a 3 by 6 metre basketball court, situated between the bar and lounge.

Like many traditional soffi t designs, this is an acoustic ceiling, and the electrics, lighting and mechanical ducts of the services for the space are all contained within it. But outside the duty-bound performing role of the ceiling plane, its fl amboyant exterior represents a dynamic and colourful identity, much like the character after whom the place has been named.

## Object

## Introduction

The design and positioning of particular objects and elements that inhabit the existing building is a process of exploring and taking advantage of the opportunities and constraints inherent within the original structure.

– Graeme Brooker and Sally Stone, Elements and Objects. <sup>25</sup>

Objects – whether an element of furniture, a pair of shoes in a shop, or an exhibit in a museum – are focal points in an interior environment. The positioning of these signifi cant entities is an act that can exemplify the essential principles of any interior. In other words, the careful selection and thoughtful situating of the object, will exemplify a number of different ideas and narratives about a space, and indicate that this is how the space or room will be used. For instance, the placement of a piece of furniture, such as a chair, will delineate how the designer anticipates how a space will be utilised through the particular placing of its occupants. The orientation of the chair, its relationship to others, and if it is a low or high chair, formal, or informal, at a table, or low and reclining, particularises how the space will be used and in turn how it will be viewed when the user is seated. Fixed furniture elements like banquettes and tables ensure that the occupant is invited to use and to view space from one angle or place,

In other words, the way in which any objects are located and exhibited, whether it is a person seated in a space such as a bar or restaurant, an object in a museum, or products in a shop, such as clothing, will exemplify distinct narratives about a space.

The story of the interior can be told through the medium of objects: elements that enclose and thus disclose all of the narratives of the domain of inside space. Objects are an interior entity that can be realised at a variety of scales: from the size of the city, to the dimensions of a human body. They are the defi nitive interior elements, things that can represent all inside environments through their physical formation. Utilising intellectual and psychological dimensions: all components that are material constructs, elements designed in order to portray particular spatial identities and atmospheres. This narrative also includes objects that are ‘found’, elements that are selected and used in a particular way in order so that their existing properties can ‘resonate’ with the new uses that they are subsequently prescribed. Found objects can be selected precisely because they come already imbued with meaning and loaded with resonance.

For instance, Maidan, a project by Bureau A, took a number of 100 by 60cm aluminium shields used by Ukrainian soldiers and security forces during the months of resistance in Kiev in 2014, and reappropriated them, in order to turn them into an expandable shelter, capable of protecting small or larger groups of resistance workers. The resonance of the found object lay in its appropriation from an object of attack and shielding its user from missiles, to a more passive roof, covering its occupants and keeping them from harm.

Bureau A have described it as a metaphor for architecture:

Give them a shield, a shelter or a barricade and they will create a small project out of it, the manifesto for how architecture will be in years to come. <sup>26</sup>

Objects are by no means static. Their movability will lend them a liquid dimension and one that will allow them to affect differing environments through their infi ltration of particular zones. Bao House by Dot Architects consists of an unusual cube mounted on the back of a bicycle. The cube is formed from polyurethane foam inserted into a mould. Bao means ‘bulge’ in Chinese, hence the name of the house. The house was designed as a moveable shelter that was human powered. One side of the cube slides open and there is enough space for three people to sleep in the foam-insulated water-resistant space.

The object as a receptacle of stories and an element in which each interior can be analysed using its own theoretical principles, or terms of reference, will include an examination of spatial organisation, atmospheric conditions, and will often demonstrate pioneering technological innovations. In short, each object positioned in a space, whether it be a pair of jeans or an impressionist painting, will often convey a clear vision of the spirit of the age, a paradigm of the zeitgeist in the matter of the interior.
