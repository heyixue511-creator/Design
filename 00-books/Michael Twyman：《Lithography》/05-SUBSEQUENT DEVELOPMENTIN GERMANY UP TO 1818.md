# SUBSEQUENT DEVELOPMENT IN GERMANY UP TO 1818

ENEFELDER'S PRIVILEGE OF I799, WHICH GAVE HIM, AND LATER André, the exclusive right to use his process in Bavaria, proved to be virtually ineffectual. It is true that he managed to stop his brothers when they first tried to set up a press in Munich, and that later he prevented Strohofer from doing the same thing, but these appear to have been two of the rare occasions on which Senefelder was able to exercise his rights. And when Aretin complained to the proper authorities in Munich about the other lithographic presses that had been established there he was virtually told that since the art was no longer a secret they could expect nothing else, as though, adds Senefelder, keeping it a secret had been the condition of the privilege'.'

Presses infringing Senefelder's privilege were established in a number of Bavarian towns and many more were set up in Prussia, Austria, and the other German states. By 182o lithography had been introduced at one time or another into Augsburg, Berlin, Breslau, Durlach, Karlsruhe, Dresden, Hamburg, Heidelburg, Kassel, Leipzig, Mannheim, Offenbach, Prague, Regensburg, Straubing, and Stuttgart. But Munich still remained the centre for lithography at the beginning of the nineteenth century,² and Senefelder himself reveals that even as early as 18o9 there were six other presses working there, apart from a number established by amateurs for their own private use.i Naturally enough most of the earliest printers stemmed directly or indirectly from Senefelder's own press. In the first instance he trained his own brothers and they provided one of the principal means of disseminating the art in Germany. As early as 1799 Theobald and Georg Senefelder broke away and tried to found a press with the publisher Gombart in Augsburg, and Franz Anton Niedermayr learned the secret from the brothers and set up a press at Straubing in 18oo which he transferred to Regensburg in 1802. Grünewald, a former apprentice of Alois Senefelder, left to set up a press in Leipzig some time after 18o4 and one of Karl Senefelder's workmen, Strohofer, bought the secret from his master in 18o6 and began printing in Stuttgart in the spring of 1807.

The original lithographic press which Senefelder had established in Munich for his own use was taken over by his two brothers, Theobald and Georg. They continued to print there until 18o4 when the press passed to the ‘Feiertagsschule für Künstler und Techniker’ under the direction of Professor Hermann Joseph Mitterer.² A lithographic press had already been set up there by Niedermayr some time before this, and Mitterer had even made two drawings on stone as early as 18o1, but Theobald and Georg were called in to put lithography on a sounder basis and to instruct Mitterer in the technical side of the process for an annual sum of 7oo florins. Under Mitterer's guidance lithography made great progress, and he played a leading role in Munich as a popularizer of the art and as a sponsor of artists.³ Mitterer used the process mainly as an aid to teaching and found that it was ideally suited to the production of cheap versions of his own and other artists’ drawings which could be used as models for his students to copy. Two oblong quarto works of this kind, a set of sixty-six crayon drawings of the human figure showing its construction on a basis of simple fornms and in relation to classical sculpture,4 and another set of plates of arabesques, geometrical shapes, decorative foliage and fowers,5 were published as early as 18o4. Though these works carry on from the tradition of engraved drawing-books, lithography was much quicker than either etching or engraving on copper, and it had a particular advantage in that pencil and chalk drawings could be imitated by using the crayon on stone. This was a branch of lithography that Mitterer did much to perfect; early historians erroneously credited him with being the inventor of the chalk manner, while even Senefelder himself admitted that it was a branch of lithography in which Mitterer achieved a degree of perfectionthat may properly be termed the ne plus ultra of the art'.² Mitterer's other main contribution to lithography was that in 18o5 he invented the cylinder or star-wheel press, which Senefelder considered to have been the only improvement to his invention which had not stemmed directly or indirectly from him.³

Some of the artists who contributed to Mitterer's educational works also produced prints for their own sake, and in 1805 Mitterer began publishing a collection of prints by Hauber, Klotz, Mayrhofer, Seidel, Wagenbauer, and Warnberger with the title Lithographische Kunstprodukte.4 This was a landmark in German lithography, for not only was the new name for the process used for the first time there on its wrappers, but it was the first important collection of artists' lithographs to be published in Germany. A set of small and rather immature landscape lithographs by E. J. Aurnhammer had been published as early as 1802,5 but no lithographic publication of the scale and quality of the Lithographische Kunstprodukte had yet appeared in Germany. Publication began in October 18o5 and continued in twenty-six monthly parts until December 1807, the entire work consisting of 156 lithographs. Each part contained six plates, page size approximately 18%× 12½ in., and consisted of two flower drawings, two landscapes, and two figure subjects or portraits. The six artists who contributed were some of the most prolific of the early Bavarian lithographers and the publication contains some of the most competent lithographs of the period. J. N. Mayrhofer, who specialized in flowers, and the landscape artist Max Wagenbauer were the most regular contributors, and between them account for over 10o of the plates. Mayrhofer's flowers were drawn in the idiom of eighteenthcentury flower plates, and his careful and unobtrusive chalk-work provided an ideal basis for hand-colouring.' Wagenbauer's picturesque landscapes were more fully drawn in chalk and do not rely on hand-colouring in the same way; their foregrounds in particular were handled with a consistently laid, but rather coarse, system of hatching and need no additional treatment. Similar techniques of hatching were employed by other artists in the figure and portrait plates of the collection, and those of Joseph Hauber are drawn with a particularly open and rigid system of lines which derives from the traditional syntax of the copper engraver. Though few of the plates in the Lithographische Kunstprodukte are of any real artistic significance, they are of a technical standard not achieved until about 1818 in France and 182o in England.

The other important lithographic establishment in Munich was the one Senefelder himself managed, trading under the style ‘A. Senefelder, Fr. Gleissner & Comp.’. This was founded in 18o6 and undertook a great deal of jobbing work besides the two important publications mentioned in the previous chapter: Albrecht Dürers christlich-mythologische Handzeichnungen (1808)² and the Musterbuch über alle lithographischen Kunstmanieren (1808).³ The first of these contained lithographed facsimiles by J. N. Strixner of a Dürer self-portrait and of forty-three of the marginal drawings he made for the Prayer Book of the Emperor Maximilian, and proved the value of the process for reproducing works of art; the second, which included brief descriptions of twenty-four different ways of working on stone, together with specimens of some of them, showed something of its versatility both for making drawings and for commercial purposes.

Soon after Senefelder began working for the Steuer-Kataster-Kommission, however, he was forced to sell his own lithographic business, and in 181o it passed to J. G. Zeller and J. C. von Mannlich. Zeller, a printseller and stationer, does not appear to have taken a very active part in the business and sold his interests in it to J. Selb five years later, but Johann Christian von Mannlich, Court Painter, Director of the Royal Museums and Galleries, and an important figure in Munich, did much to encourage the growth of lithography there by virtue of his position alone.⁴ As a gallery director Mannlich was a great educator with a real mission to promote an understanding and appreciation of the German schools of painting, and it was obviously with this in mind that he directed the publication of two sets of lithographed reproductions. The first of these, Les Oeuvres lithographiques. Contenant un choix de dessins d’ après les grands maîtres de toutes les écoles, tiré des Musées de sa Majesté le Roi de Bavière (Munich, 1810–16), was published by Strixner, Piloty et Compagnie in seventytwo monthly parts each containing six lithographs.² Mannlich supervised the publication, chose the works to be reproduced, and even drew sixteen of them on the stone himself, but nearly all the remainder were lithographed by J. N. Strixner and F. Piloty. The plates vary considerably in both size and style and an attempt was made to capture the spirit of the originals by using either crayon or pen and ink, or occasionally both combined. Most of the plates are printed with an additional tint stone, and some with two, and the success of this publication set the style for tinted lithography all over Europe. The second publication, Königlich Bayerischer Gemälde-Saal zu München und Schleissheim, began to appear in 1817 as soon as the other work was completed. Two hundred paintings were reproduced, mainly by tinted lithography, but the plates were on the whole larger and more ambitious than those for the Oeuvres lithographiques. Piloty and Strixner again lithographed the pictures, but this time with the help of Jacob von Dorner, Dominicus and Lorenz Quaglio, and many others. These two publications were probably the most important lithographed works produced in Germany during the first part of the nineteenth century. As examples of tinted lithography they were far superior to anything that had been produced before, and they had a wide influence by virtue of their subject matter. In this respect as well they are important since they represent the first successful application of lithography to the reproduction of a collection of works of art.³

The other early presses in Munich mentioned by Senefelder⁴ all seem to have been mainly commercial undertakings. Mettenleiter set up a press at the Königliche unmittelbare Steuer-Kataster-Kommission', which Senefelder himself took over in 18o9. This institution was principally concerned with printing maps and plans, and was the most important lithographic printing works in Munich; about thirty engravers were employed there and maps were printed in editions of 15,000–20,000 for taxation purposes. Theobald Senefelder was appointed to direct the lithographic press established at the Royal Foundations Office, where he was allowed to take on work for other government offices and even private clients; Winter set up a press for the use of the Róyal Privy Council; and Sidler, who began by printing for the Central State Pay Office, later turned to printing music and other commercial work on his own account. The other presses Senefelder mentions as having been working in Munich in this period were the lithographic office established at the Royal ‘Armen-Anstalt auf dem Anger', and the presses of Dietrich and Helmle and Roth.

While Munich was certainly the main centre for lithography in Germany, the mass of ordinary jobbing printing done there appears to have hindered its use by artists for making original drawings on stone. In this respect Offenbach and Berlin deserve special mention. At Offenbach, Johann André told his cousin, François Johannot, about the process and the latter set up a press which was used at first largely for the printing of music and various kinds of jobbing work.¹ By 18o4, however, Johannot had begun to print artists' lithographs, and amongst those who produced drawings at his press were Matthias Koch, Nicolaus Vogt, Prince Karl von Isenburg, Susemihl the younger, and Wilhelm Reuter. Reuter also worked in Berlin, and he is perhaps the most important early German lithographer, for besides producing some very fine lithographs himself he attracted many other artists to the process.² Reuter (1768-1834) was a miniature and portrait painter by profession, and was one of the first German artists to draw on stone. According to his own testimony he began making experiments for himself in Berlin in 1798, soon after hearing about Senefelder's invention. His earliest lithographs were produced in 18o1, but his real enthusiasm for the process dates from 18o3 when he undertook a trip to Paris which enabled him to find out more about lithography in other parts of Europe. Apart from seeing what was being done in Paris itself, Reuter saw some prints from the English Specimens of polyautography while he was there, and on his outward journey he stopped at Darmstadt and met the lithographic printer François Johannot. This last event is of particular interest, for Reuter seems to have been one of Johannot's first recruits in his attempt to encourage artists to make drawings on stone. When Johannot returned to Offenbach he sent Reuter two lithographic stones already prepared for drawing, one grained and the other polished, and in a letter dated 17 July 1803 gave him simple practical instructions in the art of drawing on stone.' These contacts with other lithographers must have kindled Reuter's enthusiasm for the new process, and in the following year he invited a number of artists to contribute to the Polyautographische Zeichnungen vorzüglicher Berliner Künstler. Besides Reuter himself, many of the leading artists of Berlin made lithographs for the publication, including the neo-classical sculptor Johann Gottfried Schadow, until it finally lapsed in 18o8. Years later Reuter planned a similar but more ambitious undertaking, Polyautographische Zeichnungen der vorzüglichsten europäischen Künstler unseres Zeitalters, to demonstrate the various applications of the process. A start was made with a tinted lithograph by Wolf in 1818, but the series was never completed. Reuter himself continued to make lithographs until 1824, though scarcely improving on the strength and freedom of his early work.

By 182o lithography was being widely used in Germany for a variety of jobbing work such as circular letters, diagrams, and statistical tables, and for map and music printing; it was used occasionally too for items such as visiting cards and book covers and for producing facsimiles of both text and illustrations from old books. Lithography had also become firmly established as a suitable medium for the draughtsman—for original drawing and for making illustrations for books, topographical and portrait prints, and copies of paintings and drawings of the old masters. By the time Senefelder's treatise was published in 1818 the techniques of pen and chalk drawing and engraving on stone had all been brought to a competent level. In particular, the two techniques which were later to become the stock-in-trade of professional lithographers in England and France were being successfully practised. The second was the technique of using one or more tint stones, often with the lights removed from them, which was employed to such good effect in the Oeuvres lithographiques and the Bayerischer Gemälde-Saal. In both these techniques Germany certainly provided the model for those early English and French lithographers who began to study the new process seriously.

For the most part, lithography in Germany followed the path mapped out for it by its inventor. The emphasis from the beginning was on the commercial and industrial ápplications of the process, and these continued to be the dominant influences on the development of lithography there throughout the nineteenth century. The practice of making cheap imitations of intaglio printing by etching or engraving on stone, which continued in Germany long after it had been abandoned in England and France, and the superiority of German craftsmen in chromolithography later on in the century are symptomatic of this attitude. The lithographic products for which Germany became noted were in the very same fields that had attracted the attention of the first lithographers—music printing and the reproduction of works of art; but as far as the development of the original lithographic print was concerned her contribution virtually ended with the rise of French lithography in the 182os.
