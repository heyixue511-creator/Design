# SELBY AVENUE HOUSE LOS ANGELES, CALIFORNIA, UNITED STATES, 1977 CHARLES MOORE UNCONVENTIONAL TOWNHOUSE ENHANCED WITH QUIRKY SPACES AND VISUAL PUNS. One of a cluster of three townhouses designed for himself and two fellow professors while he was teaching at UCLA, this highly original residence by CHARLES MOORE (also see under "Architecture") was among his several efforts to explore different architectural aesthetics while expressing his irrepressible fondness for unexpected juxtapositions and visual puns. The three townhouse exteriors are companionably aligned, in convenient walking distance from the college campus. Bridging a large, partly covered courtyard shared with its neighbors, the house fits five levels into a four-story volume, with angled stairs, lofts, and overlooks creating an intriguing spatial puzzle. A series of ingeniously located seating areas provide perches from which to view the interior from different angles, each revealing another aspect of the unconventional space. Informally arranged furnishings of no single style and an accumulation of collected objects reflect the architect's eclectic taste, standing in whimsical contrast to the severe architectural background and crisp white walls. Light streams into the interior from three sides, and is shaped into patterns by the multifaceted roof.
