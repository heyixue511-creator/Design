# WOVEN CARGOES # Indian Textiles in the East John Guy The dazzling cloths presented in this book are the visual record of one of the great untold stories of Asian design history: the trade in Indian textiles to Southeast and East Asia. Outstanding among them are the patterned cottons – the famous chintzes - and the tie-dyed silk patola, reserved for rulers and the nobility. The chintzes made for export to Europe in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries are now well known; but for over a thousand years Indian cloths were traded for the spices and forest and mineral wealth of the East by Asian, Arab and European merchants. Universally in demand, the textiles were designed to suit specific markets; among their functions, they served as attire for royalty and as diplomatic gifts, as displays on festive occasions, and as clothing for the populace at rites of passage and other ceremonies. John Guy examines the history of the cloth-forspices trade, describes the techniques of textile production, and then looks in detail at the place of imported cloths in the Malay world, Indonesia, Thailand and Japan. The historical focus is on the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, when the trade was at its peak. In his narrative, the author draws on vivid firsthand descriptions by travelers and merchants, on historic images of people and places, on other arts that provide a design context for the cloths, on ethnographic studies, and on his own research in the field. He is especially well placed to tell the story, being a curator at the Victoria and Albert Museum, London, whose holdings of Indian trade textiles have few rivals.
