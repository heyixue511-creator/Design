Several other companies went into production with designs of their own around the same time. In selling vacuum cleaners, manufacturers were quick to emphasise their hygienic properties. The arguments for vacuum cleaners as labour-saving appliances were not particularly convincing: Christine Frederick, an American household efficiency expert, had shown in 1920 that vacuum cleaners saved little time, though they did the job more effectively.\* Consequently, it was as instruments of hygiene that vacuum cleaners were generally advertised. The advice that Christine Frederick (who had become a marketing consultant to domestic appliance companies) gave to the British electrical goods industry in 1927 on how to sell vacuum cleaners was to stress their hygienic qualities and to use the familiar anxiety-inducing arguments to drive the message home. Having explained the faults of old-fashioned cleaning methods, she continued: "The principle behind all new cleaning methods is not scattering but absorption of dust. This new principle is exemplified in the many dustless and specially treated dustcloths and mops, but reaches the height of perfection in the electric suction cleaner. This electrical appliance, whatever the specific model, extracts dust, holds it, absorbs it, actually removes it.
