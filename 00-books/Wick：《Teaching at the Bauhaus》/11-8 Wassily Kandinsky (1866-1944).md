# 8 Wassily Kandinsky (1866–1944)

## Between Theory and Practice

It is not easy to treat Kandinsky's contribution to pedagogy at the Bauhaus with the brevity required here. The artist was active as a teacher at the Bauhaus for eleven years, andthe variety of material that he taught during that time makes a comprehensivepresentation considerably difficult. (It is possible to get some notion of this diversity from a manuscript published in French in 1978, Cours du Bauhaus, for example.)

Overlooking these difficulties, however, it is at least possible to establish in principle that Kandinsky's teachings at the Bauhaus are more interesting in terms of method, as was the case for Itten, Albers, and to some extent Moholy-Nagy, than in termsof content. Kandinsky wrote his book Point and Line to Plane both as a balance sheet of his work at the Moscow Institute for Artistic Culture (Inkhuk) and his teaching activity at the Free State Art Workshops and Vkhutemas (see“Bauhaus andVkhutemas/ Vkhutein" in chapter 4) and as the summa of his first years as a teacher at the Bauhaus. As a theory of design, Point and Line to Plane is based on his theoretical reflections from the period around 1910 and builds on them in a systematic and didactic form. Although it is subject to criticism for its theoretical premises, its often seemingly subjective assertions, and its apodictic formulations, it belongs among the pioneering achievements in the field of rational exploration of the foundations of design. Evidence of the fascination that was evoked by Kandinsky's theory, its internal unity and coherence, can be seen in the numerous subsequent theories of design that were implicitly or explicitly built on Kandinsky's work and sometimes even took over specific formulations (e.g., Boris Kleint's Bildlehre [Pictorial theory]).

Only Paul Klee contributed to the elementary language of the pictorial with similar impact.

Kandinsky came to the Bauhaus in mid-1922, that is, more than a year after Klee. Nevertheless, we are treating him ahead of Klee, despite the chronology at the Bauhaus, because as an artist and theoretician he clearly has priority in the process of "questioning the means." His first theoretical book, On the Spiritual in Art, had already appeared in 1911 (though dated 1912), in which he took up the call,

which even predated Goethe, for the formulation of "hardand fast rules, principles resembling those of the figured bass in music."1 It should be noted here that Kandinsky had completed the manuscript the previous year, that is, 1910. This means that the writing of this theoretical work coincided with the incubation period of abstract or absolute painting (i.e., independent of the figure): Kandinsky createdhisfirst nonobjective painting in 1910. This context is by no means arbitrary, for it casts a spotlight on the dovetailing of theory and practice that is typical of Kandinsky. Art and science seek an inner harmony in the interplay of "hypothetical postulate and practical experiment,"² as it were.

This special talent for theoretical reflection on his artistic practice and practical application of theoretical reflection is related, no doubt, to the specific circumstances of socialization having to do with the period preceding Kandinsky's career as an artist. Born in Moscow in 1866, he began to study law and political economy at the university in Moscow in 1886. In 1893 he passed his state exams in law, and in 1896 was offered a professorship on the law faculty of the University of Dorpat (Estonia), which he declined. The thirty-year-old went instead to Munich in order to study painting, first at the private art school of Anton Azbé and then at the academy with Franz von Stuck. A decade of intense study of the questions of law had trained his abstract thinking so that in the period that followed he approached problems in the fine arts with a distinctly analytic spirit, probing them rationally and giving them a new direction through thought--and of course artistic action. Eberhard Roters has remarked on this: "He possessed the ability to express his ideas and reflections in logically founded hypotheses and to formulate them clearly and coherently. .. . This ability was of great advantage to him in his teaching, particularly as he had to find entirely new explanations for creative experiments for which no words had previously existed."3

## The Concept of Synthesis

In addition to his ability to think analytically, Kandinsky was also eidetic and capable of synesthetic experiences. The latter in particular has a decidedly synthetic character and as such was significant for Kandinsky's artistic practice and theory of art in equal measure. As an eidetic Kandinsky was, especially as a youth, able to call up vividly events or images he had once seen, sometimes long before. This is an ability that is often found among primitive peoples but is very rare in advanced industrial societies. In his autobiographic "Reminiscences" (1913) Kandinsky spoke in this context of his "visual memory," and he reported that as a boy he was able to paint “from memory" at home the paintings that had particularly enthralled him at exhibitions and to "recite by heart the names of all the shops in a long street, without mistakes," because he saw them before his eyes.4 Later, Kandinsky apparently lost this ability but gained a corresponding increase in his “powers of concentration," which allowed him to penetrate the “inner life of art."5

Kandinsky's synesthesia was still more important for his artistic activity than his eidetic ability. Synesthesia, an intermodal quality, is generally defined as “responding to a stimulus from one sense modality with sensations which belong to another sense modality."6 That is, it refers to the phenomenon whereby automatic experiences appear in conjunction with particular sense impressions from other realms of the senses. Experimental psychology has determined that the percentage of synesthesics is significantly higher among people active in the arts than in the general population.7 Thus it would be superfluous to allude to Kandinsky's synesthetic abilities if they did not play such an important role in his artistic oeuvre and his theory of art. Psychology is aware of a number of synesthetic phenomena. A discussion of dual experiences of associated forms of sensory manifestations must distinguish between associations of perception and perception, of perception and idea, of idea and perception, and of idea and idea. In light of this complexity of the forms of manifestations that can be associated with one another, it is necessary to distinguish between "simple synesthesias" (usually sound-color sensations) and "complex synesthesias" in which “phenomena of varying complexity (e.g., letters, numbers, ... people, landscapes, etc.) are combined."8 Kandinsky seems to have had a form of simple synesthesia, namely, the ability to have a double artisticmusical experience. In this context, Kandinsky remarked, in the “Reminiscences" mentioned above, on a key experience in which visual perception combined with acoustic imagination: Rembrandt "moved [him] deeply" and the "great divisions of light and dark" appeared to him as “a mighty chord" and "reminded [him] immediately of Wagner's trumpets."9

This correspondence between music and visual art is also demonstrated, for example, by a series of woodcuts by Kandinsky, published by Piper in 1913 as K/änge (Sounds), and by the stage composition Der ge/be Klang (The yellow sound), published in 1912 in the almanac Der Blaue Reiter (whose very title suggests an artistic transformation of synesthetic experiences). Der gelbe Klang is also an interesting early document of Kandinsky's ideas on a synthesis of the arts aimed at the integration of various media: theater, music, painting, and dance (something of a common theme of twentieth-century art, extending to the happenings of the sixties).1º Even Kandinsky's later conception of synthesis remained, on principle, within the realm of aesthetics, distinguishing it fundamentally from Gropius's vision. This was true even if it appears that Kandinsky at least temporarily felt an obligation to the idea of an aesthetic and social synthesis under the primacy of architecture while directing the workshop for wall painting.

It wouldbe misleading to attributeKandinsky's concept of synthesis entirely to the author's ability to experience sensory perceptions in two or more ways at the same time; other factors must be considered, In this context, a biographical detail seems especially worthy of note: as a student on an ethnographic study trip in a remote Russian province, Kandinsky encountered houses whose interiors were completely painted with colorful ornaments and decorated with pictures, so that he was surrounded on all sides by painting and had the feeling he had entered into a painting, was moving around and living in it. This experience of a total pictorial space clearly influenced Kandinsky heavily and is presumably not without significance for the development of his concept of synthesis.

It was only a critical diagnosis of his time, however, that led Kandinsky to recognize the "inner necessity" of the aesthetic synthesis he sought. His theoretical writings are filled with an early and continuous critique of the intellectual legacy of the nineteenth century: a critique of the predominance of materialist and positivist thinking, a critique of the splintering of related things, and a critique of economically motivated specialization andof the increasing isolation in all spheres of life.11 In his opinion, the latter was especially evident in education, where colleges produce "highly trained specialists and completely uneducated people"12 and feature "endless varieties of 'specialist training,' which have nothing whatever to do either with education in general, or with each other."13 He felt that traces of a former unity had been retained in the arts only in the church, the theater, and in building but that in the architecture of the nineteenth century the connection between the arts was purely superficial and dispensed with any "internal necessity." For the theater, Kandinsky diagnosed, "in addition to external 'effects,"" a quest to "affect people inwardly, something made possible by Wagner's efforts in the realm of opera."14 For the church—for which all artistic genres served a single internal goal with equal prominence and to the same extent: prayer—Kandinsky noted a merely superficial retention of its old unity with no internal content.

Kandinsky saw tendencies of a new“synthetic art," in which the walls between the genres into which the arts had been divided appeared to be collapsing, in the interaction of music and painting (he mentioned Scriabin's Prometheus) and in dance, at least where it was not a goal in itself but rather an integral element of a work combining music, visual art, and dance. This synthetic art, in an overarching sense, was thus seen in the new unity of art, technology, and science at the Higher Artistic-Technical Workshops (Vkhutemas) in Moscow and the Bauhaus (see "The Bauhaus and Vkhutemas/Vkhutein" in chapter 4). At the Bauhaus, in Kandinsky's words, "the student should, apart from his specialist studies, receive as broad a synthetic education as possible." Ideally, they should be equipped not only as new specialists, but also as new people, for there are "no 'specialized' questions, to be identified or solved in isolation, since in the end everything is interconnected, interdependent."15

These brief notes on Kandinsky's ideas of synthesis seem necessary as background for an adequate assessment of Kandinsky's teaching at the Bauhaus, which at first glance seems extremely analytical and elemental but which ultimately aimed to establish a synthetic art. Therefore, in Point and Line to Plane Kandinsky entertained the expectation that through a "microscopic"16 analysis of form it must be possible to create a "dictionary of elements" which would lead to a "grammar" and finally to a "theory of composition" which "will overstep the boundaries of the individual arts and refer to 'Art' in general."17

## The Influence of Theosophy

In order to be more precise, it is useful to interject some remarks on Kandinsky's intellectual orientation and artistic development between 1908 and 1914. In his groundbreaking study The Sounding Cosmos18 Sixten Ringbom has demonstrated the extent to which—alongside romanticism—theosophical ideas (Madame Blavatsky, Annie Besant,

Leadbeater, Rudolf Steiner) and other occult and esoteric theories contributed to the development of Kandinsky's ideas of synthesis and acted as a catalyst in the creation of nonobjective "painting as pure art" (to borrow the title of a 1918 essay by Kandinsky). Ringbom makes it clear that the elimination of the objective in Kandinsky's Improvisations andCompositions between 1910 and 1914 should not be understood as the result of a process of abstraction motivated by form but rather as the artist's search for an adequate form of expression for the “epoch of the Great Spiritual" that he evoked.19Objective art was, in Kandinsky's view, a correlate of the materialistic worldview of the nineteenth century. It exhausted itself in depicting external reality and was incapable of internal vision. Only an abstract design language freed of the objective and embracing all genres of art was suited to reviving the spiritual unity—of which an internal orientation formed an essential part—that had been destroyed by the materialist and positivist thinking of the nineteenth century. This internal orientation, which Kandinsky, following the theosophists, saw as the "true essence of man,"20 revealed itself to those sensitive enough to perceive it as an “inner sound."21 This concept, central to Kandinsky's theory of art, goes back directly to Rudolf Steiner, who commented on ur-images in his Theosophie: "As soon as the 'clairvoyant' ascends from the country of the soul into the country of the spirit, the archetypes will also be perceived as sound. . . . Ore only has to imagine that everything that is described as 'image,' as 'luminosity,' is at the same time something musical. Every color, every perception of light corresponds to a spiritual sound, and every interaction of color corresponds to a harmony, a melody, etc."22

It is evident that Kandinsky was especially receptive to such occult-sounding ideas because of his own synesthetic experiences. The subject of his painting between 1910 and 1914 was the exploration of the inner world of the spiritual, whose sounds manifested themselves to him in "pure" forms and colors—that is, those freed from the obligation of being defined as objects. In this context, it seems appropriate to mention what Steiner called“colors floating in space,"23 which appear in Kandinsky's painting of that periodas a “running-over of color beyond the outlines"24 and which were clearly inspired in their form by several abstract representations that Besant and Leadbeater had illustrated in their theosophical book Thought-Forms (1905; German edition, Gedankenformen, 1908).25(Other possible influences that may have ledKandinsky to nonobjective art include the abstract forms of Jugendstil ornament and Islamic art, which the artist encountered on a trip to Tunisia in 1904–5).

Although we cannot elaborate here on the speculative theories of "thought-forms" and “soul vibrations" developed by Besant and Leadbeater, or on their significance for Kandinsky,²⁶ it should be said that, according to Kandinsky, colors and forms are the external manifestationof "inner content," and that harmonies of color ànd form must be based on "the principle of the purposeful touching of the human soul" (the "soul vibrations”), a principle he also called the principle of internal necessity.²7 Kandinsky did not precisely define this term, which he probably borrowed from Konrad Fiedler's philosophy of art. In light of the genesis of his theory of abstract form elements, however, the following paraphrase seems especially illuminating:

Internal necessity arises from three mystical sources. It is composed of three mystical necessities:

1. Every artist, as creator, must express what is peculiar to himself (element of personality).

2. Every artist, as child of his time, must express what is peculiar to his own time (element of style. . ).

3. Every artist, as servant of art, must express what is peculiar to art in general (element of the pure and eternally artistic . . .).28

In Kandinsky's view, only this element of the "pure and eternally artistic" transcends the individual and is invariable in space and time. The predominance of this third element in a work of art is "the sign of its greatness and of the greatness of the artist," so that the process of the development of art consists "to a certain extent in the ability of .the pure and eternally artistic to free itself from the elements of personality and temporal style."29 Or to put it another way: "this ineluctable will for expression of the objective is the force described here as internal necessity."30

Thus it is the artist's task to realize a maximum of the “pure and eternally artistic," of the "eternal-objective," in the "temporal-subjective,"31 that is, in an individual style that is codetermined by the epoch (the parallels to Blavatsky's phrase “eternal truth"32 are self-evident). The implication of this postulate of objectivity is that the artist must strive for artistic forms that are as free as possible of any subjective elements. It is thus logical that in his book On the Spiritual in Art (first published in 1911 but dated 1912) Kandinsky discussed the possibility of a "figured bass of painting" or a "grammar of painting"³3 on which he would elaborate in his magnum opus of the Bauhaus period, Point and Line to Plane, published in 1926. This is why Kandinsky saw the latter book as an "organic continuation"34 of the work from 1911, even though the ideas taken from the storehouse of theosophy retreated during the Bauhaus period to be replaced by an analytical approach: the goals remained the same.

This is not the place to explore the reasons that led to the decline in theosophy's influence on Kandinsky's thought. Kandinsky's conformity with the goals of theosophy seems in any case to have been limited to a compatibility with his own ideas of a new spiritual unity in the "epoch of the Great Spiritual."35 Even so, both during the period from 1914 to 1921 spent in Russia as a result of the outbreak of the war and in the Bauhaus period from 1922 to 1933, the artist occasionally referred to theosophy and especially to Besant and Leadbeater's Thought-Forms.36 (This book and Leadbeater's Man Visible and Invisible also brought Itten within theosophy's circle of influence in 1918; see "The Background of Itten's Pedagogy" in chapter 5).

## Kandinsky's Inkhuk Program

Although he accepted numerous official posts, Kandinsky can hardly have had a decisive influence on artistic developments in the new Russia after the October Revolution. He was, among other things, a member of the People's Commissariat for the Education of the People, which was ledbyAnatoly Lunacharsky; professor of the Vkhutemas; vice president of the Academy of Studies in the Arts; and commissar of the Russian museums. In 1920 he outlined a program for the Institute of Artistic Culture (Inkhuk) with the goal of combiningMalevitch's suprematism, Tatlin's concept of material culture, and his own theories into a pedagogical program that was as coherent as possible. Although this program was rejected by the majority of Inkhuk members, it is usefui to cite it at length because it already contained elementsofKandinsky's later teaching at the Bauhaus. Theprogram consistedof two parts, a“[t]heory of separate branches of art" and a “[c]ombination of separate arts to create a monumental art."37

The first part consists of an analysis of the specific qualities of each artistic medium. The point of departure is to be the psychological reaction of the artist to the quality. . . . On the results of such analyses an Art Dictionary of conscious line and forms is to be made covering every medium. . . .

Colour is to be examined in (a) its absolute, and (b) its relative values.

a. The colours are to be studied first individually and then in combinations. These studies are to beco-ordinatedwithmedical,physiological, chemical andoccu/t [my emphasis—R.W.] knowledge and experience of the subject, e.g. sensoryassociationandcolour;colour and sound, etc.

b. Colour in combination with drawn forms. This is to be approached by:

1. Combination of primary colours with simple geometric forms.

2. Combination of complementary colours in similar forms.

3. Crossed combinations of primary and complementary colours in simple geometric forms.

4. Primary colours combined with free, exceptional forms.

5. Crossing combinations of colour and forms.38

In the second section of the program Kandinsky called for the creation of a synthetic monumental art that could be most easily realized in the theater because he believed that the various genres of art would there achieve an optimal unity (this harmonizes with Oskar Schlemmer's concept for the Bauhaus stage, see “The Teacher" in chapter 10).

It is not surprising that Kandinsky didnot find acceptance from most of the other artists of the Inkhuk for this program, as it remained focused on the inherent aspects of the artwork and clearly insisted on a psychological, subjectivist position without taking into account the social developments of the early years following the October Revolution. The ideas founded on the artist's theosophicaltendencieswouldhave been subjected to especially sharp criticism: in studying color he recommended considering insights from the world of magic in addition to knowledge from medicine, physiology, andchemistry. Clearly in response to such criticism, in his essay“Color Course and Seminar," which appeared in the 1923 book Staatliches Bauhaus Weimar, 1919–1923³9 and whose content is closely related to the ideas of the Inkhuk program, Kandinsky deliberately avoided mentioning occult disciplines in his discussion of color research, although he did allude— if only in a footnote—to the significance of sociological (!) contexts: “Color, like all other phenomena, must be examined from different viewpoints, in different ways, and by appropriate methods. From a purely scientific point of view, these ways may be dividedinto three areas: that ofphysics and chemistry, that of physiology, and that of psychology."40

As early as On the Spiritual in Art there were frequent references to a figured bass of painting, to a grammar of painting—an idea that appears in the Inkhuk program in the form of a call for an "Art Dictionary," a glossary of artistic means and the possibilities for employing them, which Kandinsky later implemented in his book Point and Line to Plane. Even the Inkhuk program is permeatedwith the dualism, characteristic of Kandinsky's theory, between an analytical and a synthetic principle (individual explorations of elementary pictorial means and the formulation of an abstract language of signs unify the various genres of art, and follow the principle of “internal necessity"); this same dualism will constantly recur in the following treatment of Kandinsky's contribution to pedagogy at the Bauhaus

## Teacher at the Bauhaus

Kandinsky, who made no secret of his antimaterialist and anticommunist views even in socialist Russia, was invited to the Bauhaus in 1921. He arrived in Germany at the end of the year and, following an interim stop in Berlin, took up his activities in Weimar in mid-1922. His appointment by Gropius came at a time during which the Bauhaus—not least under the influence of Theo van Doesburg—was clearly beginning to free itself of its earlier expressionistic tendencies ard working to base design more thoroughly in a system of basic elements. After Gropius's double-edged experiences with Johannes Itten, the expectation at the Bauhaus now was that Kandinsky would provide new stimuli with his development of a pictorial figured bass (his book On the Spiritual in Art was already known) and with his active support for a synthesis of all genres of art. Ré Soupault, a student at the Bauhaus between 1921 and 1925, later recalled: "Kandinsky was known to the students. Several of us had read his books, which were much discussed at the Bauhaus. And we knew Der Blaue Reiter. But above all the students were interested in the synthesis of the arts that Kandinsky was striving for. . . ."41

Gropius assigned Kandinsky as a master of form in the workshop for wall painting, which until 1922 had been led by Itten and Schlemmer in alternation. Kandinsky woulddirect it from 1922 until the Bauhaus moved to Dessau in 1925 (the directorship was then taken over by Hinnerk Scheper and later by Alfred Arndt in the interim between 1929 and1931). On taking over the workshop for wall painting, Kandinsky seems to have embarked on realizing his concept of a synthesis, which accorded well with Gropius's idea to unify all artistic genres under the primacy of architecture. Still, even though Kandinsky himself had been engaged as a painter several times for monumental paintings (the entry hall for the

Juryfreie in Berlin, 1922; the music room of the German architectural exhibition, Berlin, 1931), he clearly saw his ideal synthesis differently. Thus the works created in the workshop under his direction—-sketches and finished wall paintings—are few in number. It seems telling, moreover, that, although Kandinsky contributed an essay, "Abstract Synthesis on the Stage, "42 (a sequel to the Inkhuk program and the article “On Stage Composition"), to the great Bauhaus publication of 1923, he included not a single line on problems of wall painting. Only the following year, 1924, did he propose to the council of masters a "systematic program" for the workshop for wall painting:

The workshop for wall-painting differs from all the other workshops of the Bauhaus in that one cannot produce any articles with color alone.

Of the various forces that are inherent in color, the Bauhaus is primarily concerned with that force by which color is able to change a given form so that a new one evolves from the given form. In principle there are two possibilities for this:

1. The concurrence of color with the given form, whereby the effects of the form are increased and a new form is created.

2. The divergence of color, whereby the given form is transformed.43

For Kandinsky, dealing with the related problems results in

[t]wo separate investigations . . . which embrace the nature of color in the sense which is most essential for the workshop:

1. The chemical-physical characteristics of color—its material substance.

2. The psychological characteristics of color—its creative forces.

Associated with these two problems there are two methods of working:

1. Technicalwork—the utilization of various characteristics, various pigments and bindingagents, application of the color.

2. Speculative experiments—analytical and compositional experiments—designs and decoration of surfaces and the treatment of space. . ..
