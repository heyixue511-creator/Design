# New German Design: Brief History, Big Impact Hans Maier-Aichen German design has long been linked with a highly functional aesthetic and a focus on workmanship. The phrase "Made in Germany" brings to mind concepts such as robust technology, high-quality engineering. ingenuity, and reliability. Institutions such as the Deutscher Werkbund, the Bauhaus, and, for a brief period, the Hochschule für Gestaltung Ulm came to symbolize functionality, ergonomics, and usability, as well as a socially and economically informed perspective, and had a lasting impact on German design. For decades, German industrial design was thought to possess the recipe for surefire market success. ![](images/66257ba36ede2540d313f12580d7a93713d2c36ab106399aaff245fd297ed0a1.jpg) Nevertheless, in the early i98os the influence of eclectic postmodern and neomodern movements, such as those that gave rise to the MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") group in Italy, was increasingly being felt in Germany as well. By 1983, design theorists Rolf-Peter Baacke, Uta Brandes, and Michael Erlhoff were announcing its arrival, seen in a veritable boom in design as well as an overthrowing of the doctrine of functionalism. For a brief period, "New German Design' attracted attention on the international stage. A generation of young designers—some of whom were still students—began to rebel against the prevailing ideology of academic design and in its place posited a supposedly new kind of anti-functionalism. This uprising against the functionalist ideology of German industry and conventional product design was heavily influenced by leftist ideas. In this regard we should not forget the effects of the widespread student unrest across Europe in i968, which also exerted a political influence on the younger generation of designers. These new designers believed that function should no longer be the sole driving force of furniture design: it should also perplex or provoke feelings in the user, or convey a message. As well as individual designers, several design groups emerged in this period, as did design galleries and artists' cooperatives that organized the manufacture of their own products, exhibited them in their own spaces, and sold them directly to the public. These designers were using communal action as a strategy, and creativity and experimentation, with all their potential risks and shortcomings, played a key role. An important element of this was what is known as “naive testing," according to which play, chance, and tinkering were accepted methods. In response to the success of the design establishment and its mass production mentality, these groups developed a range of formal, sensory, Brutalist, and socially and politically motivated alternatives. While some of the New German Designers succumbed to the fascination of Italy's Memphis group, or were at least strongly influenced by its bold patterns and brightly colored surfaces, a larger proportion of these avant-garde designers, or "author-designers," took a much stricter approach, in the form of a radical detachment from all forms of classicism and an irreverent attitude toward established ideologies. OPPOSITE Heinz Landes, Solid chair, steel wire and wood, 1986. The Bellefast group aspired to occupy an autonomous position within New German Design. In this self-proclaimed "workshop for experimen tal design," the designers worked in tandem with craftspeople to define and control the entire development process of an object. Bellefast created one-offs and limited editions that, though based on relatively simple themes, were elevated through the use of experimental design methodologies. The overriding goal during this period was to seek out the freedom to explore new ideas, unencumbered by those that already existed. In autumn i98o, Gerhard Fischer and Harald Hullmann, former members of the Dreistädter group, formed the Düsseldorf-based Kunstflug along with industrial designers Heiko Bartels and Charly Hüskes. A ı983 exhibition at the Treppengalerie in Krefeld entitled “Kunstflug— Neues Deutsches Design" gave the New German Design movement its name. Most Kunstflug designs were intended to represent a complete departure from conventional industrial design. The group developed a distinctive ecologically oriented aesthetic, which contrasted with the work of Bellefast and its use of exposed Brutalist materials. Another innovation of Kunstflug was to challenge the sociocultural patterns of behavior relating to the object; their Kaffeebaum (Coffee Tree), for example, is best suited to informal meetings over coffee in a standing position, and so became a potential site for alternative forms of interaction and communication. In 1985 the Frankfurt-based designers Uwe Fischer and Klaus-Achim Heine founded Ginbande. The result was a new kind of furniture that was both simple and technically challenging; though both designers declared their commitment to a form of classical Modernism, critics viewed their multifunctional works as belonging within the context of New German Design. Indeed, their Tabula Rasa pull-out table and benches, which despite its technical complexity remains a simple and discreet piece of furniture, anticipated the New Simplicity movement that followed. ![](images/4f52bcf53403e8b0d5da3f1abfd7fa39028a85c49e797babed0ba99006f3fd43.jpg) ABOVE Volker Albus, Wiener Blut chandelier, sheet steel, 1987. At around the same time, a group of architects came together to work as designers, first under the name LUX Neonlicht and later as Möbel Perdu. They provoked the public with bizarre furniture designs that not only resisted the ideas of good form and taste but also contrasted strongly with the smooth aesthetics of the Italian avant-garde. Their objects were based around a kind of aesthetics of ugliness and adulteration through which they hoped to overcome the taboos of classical design and break free of its traditions, blurring the borders between architecture, art, and design. Some individuals are notable for the fact that they did not join these ideological group projects, or quickly left them to pursue their own interests: Volker Albus\*, a designer and author of some of the most important texts on the New German Design movement; Herbert Jakob Weinand\*, a curator and gallery owner; the architect Heinz Landes, with his cantilevered Solid chair; and Wolfgang Laubersheimer\*, whose braced steel shelves are one of the few designs to still survive from this shortlived wave of protest. Weinand, with a gallery in Berlin, primarily served as an organizational and inspirational force, raising the profile of solo shows and serving as an intermediary between designers and clients who were interested in acquiring avant-garde furniture. In this respect, the concept of the gallery clearly stood out as a place that permitted the emergence of gut response and marginal action. Weinand even lined the facade of his gallery with reinforced glass peppered with holes, as though from a bank after a robbery, like a testimony to the violence within. In i984 the English designer Jasper Morrison\* came to Berlin on a DAAD scholarship. His pieces, such as Flower-Pot Table (i984) or Directional Light Source with Small Table (i984), count among the most striking examples in the field. With Andreas Brandolini and Joachim Stanitzek he became a member of Bellefast and co-designed the student project “Kaufhaus des Ostens" (Department Store of the East), which went on to become one of the most important and influential exhibitions of New German Design. The project's premise—to work without a brief, using readymade materials-aimed to document the mood of rebellion among the students and staff at the Berliner Hochschule der Künste against Bauhaus-inspired good taste, form, and function. By ı984, however, criticism was already coming from within the movement's own camp. Jörg Ratzlaff produced a piece called Auf den Hund gekommen (Gone to the Dogs), an industrial lamp mounted on a wood-veneered plywood board on wheels. Ratzlaff said of the piece: “What was once undoibtedly a fresh approach, namely 'New German Design,' has now become jaded. Today art objects are shunted from exhibition to exhibition, without having any real influence on industrial production."4 Other critics too suggested that the honeymoon was over; Jochen Gros commented, "A true stylistic alternative cannot be based solely on the principle that everything is permitted, except good form."5 He accused the designers of navel-gazing, of being devoted purely to self-promotion and ignoring the social responsibilities of design. By the end of the i98os, both the press and the public had lost interest in the movement. The ret rospective exhibition “Gefühlscollagen—Wohnen von Sinnen" (Feelings Collages—Living through the Senses) in Düsseldorf in i986 ended up being both the apex and swansong of New German Design. Even Kunstflug, which had several works on show, called the exhibition “the highpoint and endpoint of this orgy of insolence and free-for-all groinkicking." criticizing the anti-functionalist rebellion for having “taken place predominantly on a formal level."6 It was not by chance that the abrupt end of the movement coincided with the reunification of Germany in November 1989. New German Design, with its brash, strident attitude, burned out, and the New Simplicity movement prevailed. Using one-off pieces and limited editions to fight against a powerful industry with clearly defined market sectors and an audience receptive to conservative design was always going to be a risky undertaking. Every revolution has its time. Although this anti-establishment uprising lasted barely a decade, its efforts have continued to pay off. The spirit of this raw and challenging movement is now experiencing a long-awaited revival, and may yet inspire a new generation of designers to counteract the tired and routine offerings of the marketplace and instead concentrate on producing “less, but better." ![](images/d2c97a29c4591da4075c732014be9ae6921a9a61a820d356e840907f480be2a0.jpg)
