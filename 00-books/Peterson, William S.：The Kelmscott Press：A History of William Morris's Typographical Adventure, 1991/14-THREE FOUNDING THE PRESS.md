Ruskin exclaimed in a letter to Morris in 1878.' His proposal (or perhaps it was only a passing whim?) is rooted in the assumption that all the arts - even the socalled minor or applied arts - have a common basis in the human creative faculty, and that the excessive honour paid to painting in the nineteenth century was unwholesome. In 1884 the Art-Workers' Guild was formed in London in order to allow the practitioners of various arts to come together for discussion: the Masters of the Guild wore a badge bearing the mottoArt is Unity'. Morris joined the Guild in 1888 and was the Master in 1892. When the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society was created a few years later, in 1887, it drew both its goals and membership largely from the Art-Workers' Guild. But, whereas the Guild operated quietly behind closed doors, the Society mounted a series of widely publicized exhibitions and energetically promoted the arts-and-crafts ideal. In the preface to the catalogue of the first exhibition (which was held at the New Gallery, Regent Street, in October 1888), Walter Crane, the president, declared: ‘The true root and basis of all Art lies in the handicrafts. If there is no room or chance of recognition for really artistic power and feeling in design and craftsmanship — if Art is not recognized in the humblest object and material, and felt to be as valuable in its own way as the more highly rewarded pictorial skill — the arts cannot be in sound condition. . . ." The Arts and Crafts Society was founded on ideals that had their origin in Morris's exemplary career (and, at a second remove, Ruskin's writings), yet Morris was less enthusiastic about the Society than one might suppose. By this time in his life he had come to the unhappy conclusion that individual craftsmen - or even groups of craftsmen — were powerless to change the fundamental structure of a corrupt capitalist society; only political action, culminating in revolution, could bring about the root-and-branch transformation that was required.3 Despite these misgivings, he was active in the Arts and Crafts Society. At the first exhibition he lectured, while seated at a loon, on ‘Tapestry and Carpet Weaving', and the products of Morris and Company were amply represented in the display cases. The Century Guild Hobby Horse and many books, including sixteen produced by the Chiswick Press, were shown at the exhibition; but significantly, not one of Morris's books could be seen at the New Gallery. This was only a temporary absence, of course. The House of the Wolfngs made an appearance in the Chiswick Press case at the 1889 exhibition, and in 189o six sheets of The Roots of the Mountains, illuminated by E. G. Reuter, and the Whatman-paper edition of the book itself were exhibited. Nevertheless, that first exhibition of the Arts and Crafts Society must have served as yet another uncomfortable reminder to Morris that hisown books were still inadequately printed. Morris's lecture on weaving at the 1888 Arts and Crafts Exhibition was one of a series of six talks sponsored by the Society. Of these, two dealt with the book arts: on 15 November Emery Walker spoke on 'Letterpress Printing and Illustration', and on 22 November T.J. Cobden-Sanderson lectured on ‘Bookbinding'. It was this public exposition of the principles of good typography by Walker which, without warning, suddenly aroused Morris's dormant enthusiasm for printing and set off a chain of events that led directly to the founding of the Kelmscott Press. The evening of 15 November was, therefore, an unexpectedly momentous occasion: many historians have argued that the modern revival of fine printing can be dated precisely from Walker's lecture. When Sydney Cockerell, who was hardly likely to underestimate Morris's contribution, wrote an obituary of Walker for The Times (24 July 1933), he summed up the argument for Walker's importance in this bold generalization: It is scarcely too much to say that his influence, direct or indirect, can be discerned in nearly every well-designed page of type that now appears, and to him more than to any other man this century's great improvement in ordinary book production has been due.'⁴ Walker, the son ofa coach-builder, early displayed bookish interests and, while still an adolescent, stumbled upon a bibliographical treatise which introduced him to the history of printing; from booksellers' penny and twopenny boxes he was soon collecting examples of the work of the most eminent printers, past and present. When his father became blind in 1864, however, Walker was forced to leave St Mark's School, Chelsea, and to find employment. In 1873 he joined the firm of Alfred Dawson, the inventor of a process-engraving technique, and there Walker laid the foundation of his extraordinary knowledge of printing and methods of graphic reproduction. Walker and Walter Boutall left Dawson in 1886 and set up their own engraving business, which was to perform so many indispensable tasks for the Kelmscott Press. ![](images/3af0a4fa7942f849289466f573f79f185986f3785133e50d86efca058403d820.jpg) Figure 25 Emery Walker with his friend and fellow-socialist, Bernard Shaw. Walker was Morris's unofficial partner at the Kelmscott Press and to a large degree responsible for the Press's creation. By a happy coincidence, Walker was Morris's neighbour (the latter's daughters, seeing Walker frequently hurrying past Kelmscott House, nicknamed him the brown velveteen artist' because of hris coat) and found himself drawn into the activities of the Socialist Democratic Federation. By 1883 or 1884 Walker and Morris were close confederates in the socialist cause. The two men quickly discovered the bibliophilic interests they had in common, and the relationship blossomed. Walker's description of the friendship, though far too self-effacing, is worth quoting: I have lived since I was a child in Hammersmith, and since 1878 a few minutes' walk from Mr. Morris's house. Mr. Morris possessed in his fine library a considerable collection of early printed books. . . . Mr Morris finding I had some knowledge of the practical side of printing and was thus able to explain certain details of the methods of the early printers, a subject which was beginning to occupy his mind, he invited me to visit him, which, as we had other interests in common, I was glad to do. From this time until his death in 1896 I was a frequent visitor and learned much more in bulk from him of the artistic side of printing than I could teach him of the simple elements." Such a modest estimate of Walker's contribution, however, does not tell the whole story. As Sydney Cockerell testified, the Kelmscott Press simply could not have existed without the technical expertise of Emery Walker. It was he who was able to tell Morris where to find good paper and ink, skilled printers, presses — anything, in fact, that Morris needed: the universal Samaritan, Walker, comes, from his nature, to be laid-on, like the water, only we don't pay the rates for him,' Philip Webb observed whimsically in a letter to May Morris. And it was not only Morris who was served in this fashion by Walker; until his death in 1933 he was connected with many of the most influential experiments in fine printing in England and Germany, acting usually in his characteristic role of the quiet, unassuming yet infinitely knowledgeable adviser. It is surprisingly difficult to assess how many of the typographical views associated with Morris and the Kelmscott Press came originally from Walker. Both argued for the necessity of close word-spacing and little or no leading; both had emphatic convictions about margins; both believéd in the superiority of old-face types. What seems likely is that, during those long talks in Morris's study, they worked their way together towards a radical — and at the same time conservative — understanding of typography based on the practices of the earliest printers. Each had his distinctive gifts to offer to the partnership: Morris possessed a strong, intuitive grasp of the principles of design, and Walker had a craftsman's first-hand knowledge of how books were actually printed. Yet it must not be forgotten that there were differences between them as well. Walker collected primarily sixteenth-century books, and the volumes he had a hand in designing at the Doves Press show a decidedly Renaissance influence. Morris, of course, looked back always to the'manuscripts and printed books of the Middle Ages. Their attitudes towards technology also diverged. Walker, as a professional process-engraver, was familiar with, and was willing to use, the latest methods ofreproduction (though, like Morris, he favoured wood-engravings for illustrations in books printed by letterpress), whereas Morris distrusted nearly all forms of Victorian technology, and it was only with difficulty that Walker was able to persuade Morris that the ornaments of the Kelmscott Press should be electrotyped. Despite their occasional disagreements, Morris knew that he had found in Walker the gifted artisan who could help him print books at last as he had wanted to print them since the Earthly Paradise scheme of the 186os. One is still left wondering, though, why Walker's Arts and Crafts Society lecture in November 1888 had such a profound effect upon Morris, for they had, after all, long been discussing together its key propositions. Unfortunately the lecture itself was never published, but it was reported in the press, and Walker's notes have survived (see Appendix B below); what these sources make clear is that the most striking element of the lecture was a series of approximately two dozen lantern-slides, several of which were of books and manuscripts that Morris himself owned. In a letter to his daughter Jenny, written two days later, Morris offered this account of the lecture: ‘Thursday I was at the Arts & Crafts at Walker's Lecture on printing; he was very nervous and ought to have written down his words; but of course he knew his subject thoroughly well: There were magic-lantern slides of pages of books, and some telling contrasts between the good and the bad." (Walker was in fact extremely shy and had agreed to give the lecture only at the urging of Cobden-Sanderson.) Walker's own recollections of the occasion also laid emphasis on the slides: ‘To demonstrate my point, which was that the main factor in a well printed book was the type, I selected many examples of earlier and later printing and had lantern slides made from them. This method of bringing home a point to the audience was then comparatively novel and the lecture, in spite of my bad delivery, was generally considered a success."8 Walker began by demonstrating the close connection between types and handwriting, comparing several early printed books and manuscripts of the same period. When he showed a page of one of the Renaissance writing manuals owned by Morris, Arrighi's Il modo de temperare le penne (1523), his listeners paid tribute to its beauty by bursting into applause, and Walker put beside it a passage about handwriting from Ruskin's Fors Clavigera (1875) set in three Victorian typefaces. Walker's method throughout the lecture was the comparative technique that had been perfected by Pugin, Carlyle, Ruskin, and Morris: he attempted to open the audience's eyes to the horrors of Victorian typography by juxtaposing it against the most beautiful and dignified books of the past. The lecture ended anticlimactically with slides of Bodoni's type, Miller and Richard's Old Style, and a page of the Century Guild Hobby Horse, which Walker used to demonstrate the sin of excessive leading. A final page from the Hypnerotomachia emphasized again the poignant contrast between fifteenth-century and nineteenth-century typography. (When Walker delivered essentially the same lecture again on later occasions, he was able to conclude on a happier note with a slide of The House of the Wolfings.)º The publisher Charles Kegan Paul had publicly suggested in the Fortnightly Review (April 1883) that ‘there could scarcely be a better work for the artistic future of books than that which might be done by some master of decorative art, like Mr. William Morris, and some great firm of type-founders in conjunction, would they design some new types for our choicer printed books'. Morris had more than once remarked casually to Walker that he would like to ‘have a shot' at type-designing, but that evening of 15 November 1888, as Morris made his way back to Hammersmith with those huge pictures of early types still vivid in his memory, he turned to Walker and said, ‘Let's make a new fount of type.'º For a person of Morris's obsessive energy, such a proposal led at once to its corollary — the founding of his own press — and within a few weeks he asked Walker to join him in the venture. The exact form of Walker's polite demurraǐ is not known, since there are several recorded versions of it: Cockerell noted in his diary (after a conversation with Walker) on 1o December that ‘his engagements compel him to decline the tempting offer'; Cockerell later asserted that it was because Walker had no capital to offer; Walker himself said that he refused because he had ‘some sense of proportion'." According to May Morris's amusing version of the conversation, her father said, ‘Will you go into partnership with me? . . . but mind I shall want to do everything my own way."² Walker's refusal was a purely technical one, for, as Cockerell said, he was all the while a virtual partner, and no important step was taken without his advice and approval'.3 Morris had long owned a small but attractive collection of incunabula (part of which he sold in 188o), but now, in search of models, he began to buy books from all the principal presses in Germany, Italy, France, and the Low Countries during the fifteenth century. Within a few years he put together an extraordinarily fine library: Paul Needham has declared that, if we exclude men such as William Beckford and Robert Curzon, who are known primarily as collectors rather than authors, it may be claimed that William Morris possessed a library of higher quality than any other major English literary figure'.¹4 An inventory made by F. S. Ellis and his son a week after Morris's death appraised his manuscripts at about £,12,o0o and the printed books (excluding modern works) at about £,7,3oo." Ironically, Morris never thought ofhimself as a collector. These books and manuscripts were merely his working library, and in an ideal world, Morris said, they would be made available to everyone — with ‘a public library at each street corner'. 'I should not then have to buy all these old books, but they would be common property,' he told a journalist in 1 891, and I could go and look at them whenever I wanted them, as would everybody else. Now I have to go to the British Museum, which is an excellent institution, but it is not enough. I want these books close at hand, and frequently, and therefore I must buy them.' Morris's protests are not persuasive: despite the evident guilt about his acquisitive instincts, there can be no doubt that he found deep, almost sensual pleasure in the ownership of beautiful books. Before the Kelmscott Press was actually in operation, he conducted his final experiments with commercial methods of production in The House of the Wolfings, The Roots of the Mountains, and The Story of Gunnlaug the Worm-Tongue at the Chiswick Press. In the autumn of 1889, after a conference sponsored by the National Association for the Advancement of Art and Its Application to Industry (at which Morris spoke on dyeing and Walker on printing), a small socialist group visited Glasgow in order to deliver some further lectures, and at Glasgow's Station Hotel, according to Walter Crane, Morris talked to his friends with warm enthusiasm about his plans for the Press.9 By this time Morris had begun designing the first of his three typefaces, which was to be known as the Golden type, and early in 189o Edward Prince started cutting the punches. The technique by which Morris created his types is wort examining in some detail, if only because it illustrates his considerable dependence upon both Walker's expertise and the Victorian technology which Morris professed to despise. This is how Walker remembered the aftermath of his lecture in 1888: On the way home after the lecture Morris renewed the subject we had often talked about before, of starting a small printing office from which he might issue limited editions, or as many as he thought he could sell, of his own works, and a day or two afterwards he decided upon making the venture. The first business was to get a type cut to his own design. He supplemented his collection of Early Printed books by buying examples of every fine type - gothic or roman — obtainable. These we, i.e., my firm, photographed upon a large scale to enable him better to distinguish the actual shape of the letters apart from the excess ofink by which in early printed books the shape is often obscured. . . . After a prolonged study of these types lasting throughout the year 1889 Mr. Morris began to design the models for his own letters."º Given Morris's well-known disdain for mechanical devices, why was he willing to make such heavy use of Walker and Boutall's camera in designing types? The simplest answer, no doubt, is that all of us are guilty of deep inconsistencies in our behaviour and thinking. Had the question been put to Morris, however, he probably would have been able to offer a plausible-sounding explanation. He was not (he might have said) opposed to machinery per se: he rejected it only when it threatened to enslave human beings rather than lift their burdens. The camera was merely a tool — quiet, unobtrusive (not, after all, operated by steam power), unthreatening - that simplified the task of the designer. It may also be significant that the Pre-Raphaelite painters, with whom Morris was closely associated, were fascinated by photography and were among the first artists to employ it for making studies: some of Rossetti's prints have survived, including several striking photographs ofJane Morris. For more than a decade Morris had used the camera to enlarge the small sketches that Burne-Jones produced for stained-glass windows to be manufactured by Morris and Company. And of course Morris was undoubtedly much influenced by Walker, who placed the full resources of his processengraving firm at his friend's disposal. Still, it is important to recognize that the Kelmscott Press, the quintessential example of an arts-andcrafts longing for the pre-industrial age, was paradoxically built upon a foundation of photography, one of the most sophisticated forms of technology in late-Victorian England. The photographs made for Morris by Walker's firm are now in the St Bride Printing Library, London, and, as Walker indicated, they include pages of a wide range of fifteenth-century books, each enlarged to a scale of five times the size of the original. ‘By instinct rather than by conscious thinking it over,' Morris wrote, 'I began by getting myself a fount of Roman type. And here what I wanted was letter pure in form; severe, without needless excrescences; solid, without the thickening and thinning of the line, which is the essential fault of the ordinary modern type, and which makes it difficult to read; and not compressed laterally, as all later type has grown to be owing to commercial exigencies."'
