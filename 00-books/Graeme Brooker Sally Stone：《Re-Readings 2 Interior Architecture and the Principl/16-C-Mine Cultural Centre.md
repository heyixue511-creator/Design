It is significant that some of the most popular tourist destinations are the reworked sites of industrial production. Our post-industrial society has a certain nostalgia for the drama of these places of production, not just as sites of scientifi c interest, but also, in this digital age, to gaze in wonder at the analogue complexity of the physical and mechanical technology.

Figure 2.10 The pit head lift tower now houses a viewing platform that provides a fantastic panorama across the surrounding fl atlands.

The C-Mine Cultural Centre is one such destination. It superimposes the light industry of heritage tourism over the obsolete industry of heavy production coal mining. This project, which was completed in 2010, reworks the substantial and demanding structures into a cultural complex containing: two differently scaled multipurpose auditoria, meeting rooms and spaces for fl exible cultural programming, and accommodation for technical support and administration. To facilitate this, the architects, 51N4E, have created a collection of quite simple but not subtle interventions which echo the language of the original complex of industrial buildings without compromising the needs of the new users.

The original buildings were fairly typical of this type of industrial structures; vast brick clad steel-framed buildings and an open steel tower set in an immense and fl at landscape of forests and slagheaps. The new additions appear to complete the buildings. Rather than retain the T-shape of the original, the architects have created extensions on either side of the leg of the T, thus creating an outwardly complete rectangular (in plan) building. This bold move which seemingly contradicts the nature of the mine buildings, is very much a contemporary approach to the remodelling of the existing building. This method does not contrast the new elements with the original structure, but attempts to complete the building, to create a new entirety, totality or whole.

This approach is also a development away from the more traditional methods of exhibiting heritage: that of placing the new adjacent to the old, and making each distinct and obvious. The new would usually contain the cultural; the old, the heritage. This contemporary approach establishes a compressed building with a substantially more spatially dynamic exchange between the original industrial buildings and the new elements of culture.

An enormous new, sturdy black steel box signals the relocated entrance, an element which seems to draw directly from the uncompromisingly sculptural qualities of the original water tanks that are still suspended on the roof. This leads to the foyer, which is situated within the former machine hall on the ground fl oor, and it is from here that the whole complex is accessed. This quite dark and gloomy lower level, which is littered with the detritus of the previous occupation, contains all the necessary equipment for the production of culture. The architects have reinforced the clutter with simple robust interventions that contain the offi ces, café, galleries, staircases and access to the new auditorium.

The fi rst fl oor, in contrast with the busyness of the ground fl oor, has little in the way of programme to create a barrier between the visitor and the building. The vast fl oor of red and white tiles has been extended to create an enormous horizontal plane that unites the old with the new within the extension of the rectangular plan. It appears to be of such capacity that the huge machinery of industrial production is somewhat stranded in this vast top-lit space. The nostalgia connected to the abandoned technology is tangible, but the sensitivity of the adaptation celebrates the beauty of the machinery, rather than revelling in its loss. Outside, the pit-head winding gear has been retained as a sculptural reminder of the previous occupation and a plate-steel maze has been installed immediately adjacent to it. These objects appear to contain the same shared robust language of the pit itself.

The cultural centre epitomises the condition that most western post-industrial societies fi nd themselves: the problem of what to do with the vast number of obsolete sites of industrial production. This occasion proved to be a very successful conversion, but it does lead to the question as to whether these sites should just be removed and forgotten about, allowed to fall into ruin – or perhaps it is important that they are always celebrated?
