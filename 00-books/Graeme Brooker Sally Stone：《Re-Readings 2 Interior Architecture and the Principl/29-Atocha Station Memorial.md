Location Madrid, Spain

Remodelled 2007

BUJ+COLÓN Arquitectos with: Esaú Acosta, Mauro Gil-Fournier and Miguel Jaenicke

The deployment of light and shade in any interior project will bring focus to a space as well as the ability to enhance its dynamic tendencies. The manipulation of natural light can be undertaken in order to impress very particular feelings upon the occupant of a space. Its poetic and emotion-altering qualities can be used to articulate both light and shadow, and thus create affecting responses to both interior and exterior spaces. Sometimes the handling of light in a very particular manner can be a device used to reflect upon tragic circumstances and direct what can be diffi cult emotions. Light can be the raw material that gives both comfort affects poignant responses.

On the morning of 11th March 2004, ten terrorist bombs were detonated on four commuter trains between Madrid’s Alcalá de Henares and Atocha stations: 191 people were killed, and 1,800 injured, in what was one of the worst terrorist attacks in Spanish history. Built to commemorate the bombing, the Atocha Memorial is located at the front of the main station, in a peaceful side room, removed from the hustle and bustle of the comings and goings of the trains and commuters of the busy central station. The monument consists of two connected environments: an empty underground chamber and an oval shaped glass tower. The former is quiet and

Figure 3.53 The 11 metre high tower is an illuminated beacon in the city and a constant reminder of the legacy of terrorism.

introspective, a space used to fi lter out the busy station and gather thoughts. The latter is externally orientated, and rises up into the outside environment to connect to the city: incorporating the noise of the urban surroundings back into the interior space.

The empty underground chamber affords the visitor some respite from the noise of the busy station and was devised to fi lter the transition from the outside to the seclusion of the monument. At its entrance the names of the victims of the atrocity have been etched onto the glass doors that separate the chamber from the station. Its dark blue walls were designed to be calming and subdued, in sharp contrast to the harsh and vibrant lighting of the station in which it was embedded.

The second part of the memorial consisted of an 11 metre high glass cylinder. The oval tower was placed on top of the second chamber. Its verticality provided a stark contrast, as well as a partial relief, to the

sombre, low-ceilinged, relentlessly deep blue despair of the entrance chamber. The tower was composed of 15,000 curved glass blocks, each one glued together with liquid-acrylic and hardened by ultraviolet lamps. During the day the tower is illuminated by sunlight, ensuring that natural light pours deep into the space. This shaft of illumination draws the visitor through the fi rst chamber to its centre and offers some respite from the darkness of the room. The interior of the tower was lined with an Ethylene Tetrafl uoroethylene (ETFE) plastic membrane etched with thousands of messages, left at the station by the mourners of the attack.

Throughout the day the change in light conditions as the sun moves around the tower animates the turret of words. The combination of the tower and the words brings to life the powerful expressions of dismay, anguish, shock and anger, as well as illuminating the defi ance of people responding to this senseless act.

![](images/5df93097523620d12d47acc7ab01950b20ebc437f348d75172fe093ace4d6f8b.jpg)

From the otherwise dark chamber, people can look up into the light, and see words of hope and remembrance.

At night the mood of the chamber is reversed, as artifi cial lighting at the base of the tower projects the words outwards and back to the city. In this project the range of the properties of light, such as direct, indirect, bright, soft, subtle, shadow etc, are used to elicit powerful emotions. In this project, natural light is intrinsically connected with the memories and responses of a particular place and its unfortunate role in a moment of history.

Natural light can be considered as a site-specifi c raw material when considering the design of interior spaces. This encourages its consideration as a principle spatial element, and one that will always be regarded as a fundamental component of the design of any interior space. The understanding of the range and movement of natural light, throughout the day and the seasons of the year, will provide the potential for a designer to organise and

![](images/1340a285dffc61bbc75e819c61481d3ffe7964e264c6e3d89dc435c9f00ae9e4.jpg)

arrange a variety of spaces and objects that will offer the articulation of a range of particular atmospheres. In some designs, lighting can provide the key material, a substance that, whilst immaterial, can shape and articulate all manner of spatial ideas, forms and identities.

Natural light is the fundamental material in the design of the lobby of the Hempel Hotel, an exclusive fi ve star residence that was carved out of a row of fi ve Georgian townhouses in West London. Anouska Hempel designed the hotel in the mid-1990s. It was created as a space that would transform the experience of the weary traveller as they arrived from the street and stepped into the hotel. Reviews of the space were written in response to the transcendent quality to the interior:

Crossing the main entrance is almost a mystical experience. Beyond it, the visitor fi nds a wide empty space, in which the only outstanding element is the monolithic block of the reception desk, which recalls the calm and contemplative atmosphere of a temple. – Arian Mostedi. Design Hotels and Architectural Design. <sup>31</sup>

The exclusivity of the hotel, and the sequential narrative of the journey from outside to inside, was exemplifi ed by the fi rst entrance space: a small white room for the concierge to relieve you of your luggage. A sliding glass screen, playfully fashioned to look like a Georgian six-panel door, was triggered to open by a sensor revealing the main lobby of the hotel. The lobby was arranged across the entire width of the ground fl oor of three of the fi ve townhouses. The space was bookended by a bar and restaurant in the fi rst and fi fth townhouse. Both were accessed behind monolithic screens placed at either ends of the lobby. The reduced, ordered, minimalist, pale, white aesthetic of the interior offered a striking antidote to the noisy street outside.

The visitor is greeted by a reception that has been articulated as a long solid block of stone, perched above an unyielding stand. The screens separating the hall from the bar and restaurant, at either end of the lobby, were expressed as solid walls, containing deep recesses for fi replaces. In front of both of these walls were seats. These were recessed into the ground, with elegant woven bamboo and wicker low tables offering the only colourful alternative to the unrelenting pale stone interior.

The lobby was punctuated by a shaft of natural light, a sharp-edged beam of illumination that pours in from the roof, six storeys up, through a precise aperture carved into the soffi t of the space. The light has been used to defi ne and embrace the monastic qualities of the room.

The meticulous and crafted elegance of the space was personifi ed in the careful detailing of all of the junctions and materials. Shadow gaps were employed where the wall meets the soffi t, to express the clarity of the junction and articulate their particular and different qualities. The soft edge of the grid of stone tiles was used to articulate the proportions and organisation of the desk, and the recessed seats and fi replace walls. Ultimately the grid had been used to order the whole room.

In the lobby of the hotel, the disjunction between the existing building and the new interior was presented as an initially startling and yet ultimately soothing quality to the interior space. Natural and artifi cial light was used to create a distinct ambience and atmosphere. Light becomes a solid material, and it is used to express junctions and volumes of space. Elegant proportions and robust construction ensured that the light animated the space.

It is these light and material conditions, their composition and their proportions, that infl uenced the form, organisation and the aesthetic of the new hotel lobby.

Traditionally spaces such as public bathrooms require a very particular approach to their illumination. Subtlety and discretion, as well as a functional approach to clinical and hygienic sources of light, are usually the driving factors behind how these public yet also intensely private spaces are lit.

In a recent project in Japan by Klein Dytham, an approach to how bathrooms can become less discrete and more fun places to visit, has used light and fi lm in order to make visiting the toilet an enthralling experience.

In a partnership with TOTO, Japan’s leading toilet manufacturer, Klein Dytham designed a working showroom, displaying their latest products, in the Sky Lounge, in Terminal 2 of Tokyo’s Narita Airport. The TOTO gallery acted as a showroom as well as a fully functioning public toilet. The project was a collaboration between the Narita Airport Authority, which was upgrading the airport’s facilities in preparation for the 2020 Olympic Games, and TOTO, which celebrated its 100th anniversary in 2017.

The Gallery TOTO is located at one end of the Sky Lounge. It is alongside cafés, relaxation areas and children’s play spaces. With over 14,000 travellers passing through the lounge, and on to Terminal 2 each day, it was important that the gallery was eye-catching.

Instead of fading discretely into the background, like most bathrooms do, the designers wanted the toilets to stand out and spark the imagination of the passer-by with something memorable and funny. They wanted to create an unexpected experience, one that they hoped viewers might share on social media and spread the word of the TOTO showroom. The gallery was arranged as a 20 metre wide clear glass box, 7.5 metres deep, containing ten individual elements. Each element was a bathroom, four each for male and female, one accessible universal toilet and a nursing room. Each space was designed to provide an open and bright unique bathroom experience.

Along with long-time KDa collaborators black\*bath creative agency, and the Japanese contemporary dance company Strange Kinoko, the designers choreographed and fi lmed a 20-minute performance of silhouetted happenings. These included and ranged from dance sequences to cleaning scenes, all at a life-sized scale. They also fi lmed water droplets and coloured waves, to add to the water-based sensual experiences. They applied fabric walls, each one containing low-resolution LED screens, to the external walls of the fi ve bathrooms, facing out into the lounge. Suggesting that the passers-by could see activities being undertaken within the toilets, the fi ve walls relayed a series of moving silhouettes to the lounge. Akin to

the traditional semi-transparent Shoji screens of a Japanese house, the panels suggested a semi-transparent view into the interior of each bathroom. In reality the user had complete privacy.

Each toilet space was fi tted with individual digital indicators, changing colour like a thermometer, and showing time elapsed in each space. So those waiting could see at a glance which toilet was likely to be free fi rst.

Inside the bathroom extra-large-sized panels of Hydrocera ceramic sheets were printed with large-scale landscape images of iconic Japanese features such as Mount Fuji. Ironically, in the often-described ‘littlest room’, the occupant was shown inspirational images of great landscapes as well as inspirational images of other journeys and travel. Of course at the same time, each toilet showcased the very latest in TOTO toilet technology.

Using innovative artifi cial lighting technologies, and with a keen eye for making the most unassuming spaces become magical, the gallery provided an innovative and entertaining backdrop to the large waiting passenger area, directly in front of the gallery, and in front of the planes as they drew up to the nearby gates.

## Surface

## Introduction

Materials are selected and deployed in the design of an interior in order to create specific spatial identities. Surfaces are critical tactics for the designer to consider, as they are the cover or overlay of the space with which the inhabitant or occupant of the environment will interact. The ‘palette’ or ‘sample’ is the language used to describe the assemblage of materials that designers and architects will collect in order to portray the proposed new identity of a space.

The importance of selection can also be extended to the ‘found’ or existing surfaces of the building that is to be reused. These may embody useful narratives from the history of the building. These stories may be symbolic, as they may be materials and surfaces that illustrate the previous life and uses of a space, or they will physically embody its history through the scratches and indentations of their previous lives. However found surfaces are considered, if they are to be reused and retained, their incorporation, along with new applied surfaces, can create a rich weave of a spatial identity that is a complex representation of the past, present and future of the interior space.

A diffi culty facing the architect is how to negotiate a past that concerns the detective and the historian and a future that is of interest to a client and the occupants.

– Irénée Scalbert. <sup>33</sup>

In the design of the Raven Row gallery, in Spitalfields, London, a detailed examination of the history of the building enabled 6a architects to understand how two 18th century silk mercers’ houses, and a 1970s concrete-frame block, could be meaningfully combined to make a space for art. The history of the buildings included the devastation of a fire in 1972. Some of the ornate details and mouldings of the houses survived, and inspired by the surface qualities of the conflagration, the architects reused the fashioned and charred timbers as moulds for the design

New parts had been shaped after old parts, for instance, the charred timber panels and the cast iron handles and railings. All of a sudden old parts seemed to depend on new parts...Construction became overwhelmed with fi nishes. <sup>34</sup>

Some found surfaces are so unique, and their endurance is so vital, that they must be conserved and retained as found. This approach to existing surfaces can be exposed when the designer of a new space least expects it, and the stripping back of a space that is to be prepared for new use, reveals some long-hidden important elements. If retained, as well as being incorporated into any new design, these surfaces will also need to be cleaned and maintained. Westminster Hall is the oldest building on the Parliamentary Estate in the centre of London. A chamber has been on the site, in some form or another, for over 900 years and the Hall has been the venue for many of the important events in British history ranging from the trial of Guy

Fawkes and Charles I, and the lying in state of such figures as Winston Churchill. Jorge Otero-Pailos is the Director of Preservation at Columbia University and over a period of five years designed a project for the hall entitled The Ethics of Dust: a work that cleaned the 919-year-old east wall of the hall. The title of the work was taken from John Ruskin’s book about cleaning, and housekeeping. Otero-Pailos covered the entire wall with liquid latex, reinforced it with fabric, and then peeled it all off, extracting not just the dirt, grime and markings from the wall, but in essence 900 years of history of the city not only through its use as the centre of British political drama and life but also just as a building in a city that had endured a tumultuous history of change and endurance.

The resultant latex ‘skin’ was then hung a suitable distance away from the original wall from which it was extracted, so that viewers could walk through history, observing the new clean wall, and its resultant exposed markings, and its suspended doppelgänger: replete with the detritus of years of dust, breath, smog, fire, human hair and skin, accumulated throughout the ages. Both applied and found surfaces can be loaded with all kinds of meaning. It is up to the designer of interior space how they utilise these codes and symbols.
