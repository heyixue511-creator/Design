Since that date these types . . . have gradually been adopted largely for the printing of the better class of bookwork, especially that of a more antiquarian nature. . . ."3 Caslon type does not look particularly remarkable to us today, because its frequent use in the early twentieth century has made it seem ordinary and very English'. (In time Caslon became so closely identified with Englishness that it was adopted by at least some printers for patriotic motives.) Bruce Rogers, the distinguished American bookdesigner, complained in 1917 that Caslon was, if anything, excessively familiar — ‘a much overrated type', he said; its best quality was ‘a certain amount of irregularity, restful to the eye after the smoothness and monotony of our more modern letters'.'⁴ England seemed to him at the time a Caslon-ridden country'. It was precisely the occasional clumsiness and crudeness of Caslon, so offensive to Rogers, which recommended it to Morris, and in the last decade of his life he adopted it enthusiastically for his own commercially printed books. Morris in turn influenced Bernard Shaw to use it in his books from the late 189os onwards; this had the effect of bringing Caslon into even more general circulation. Because Caslon seemed appropriate only for ‘period' typography to Victorian readers, the Edinburgh typefoundry of Messrs Miller and Richard offered their customers in 186o a design by A. C. Phemister called at first ‘Modernized Old Style' (later simply ‘Old Style'), which attempted to capture the flavour of Caslon without sacrificing many of the characteristics ofmodern-face type. It also had the merit of being designed to work well with modern inks and papers. Other typefounders soon sold their own versions. Old Style was a deliberate compromise, and a good deal of scorn was poured on it through the years. One of the earliest to register a protest was Emery Walker, who, though he admitted that it was more suitable for smooth-surfaced papers than Caslon, described it as ‘far inferior in beauty to types of the early printers'; similarly, Morris remarked that the design of the letters of this modern “old style" leaves a good deal to be desired, and the whole effect is a little too grey, owing to the thinness of the letters'.' Bruce Rogers found it at best inoffensive, at worst monotonous. Whatever its limitations, Old Style proved to be an immensely popular typeface — by some estimates, indeed, the most widely used book face in England during the final decades of the century. (Modern face, however, continued to be the preferred type in periodicals, school-books, and scientific works.) A writer in the Printers'Register (6 November 1884) offered the opinion that probably no variety of type at present in use has so firm a hold upon the cultivated taste of the printer or of the public'. It was eventually supplanted in the second decade of the twentieth century by the revivals of historic types that began to emanate from the Monotype Corporation. The resurrection of Caslon by the Chiswick Press in mid-century, followed by the introduction of Old Style by Miller and Richard, brought about gradual but important changes in the appearance of English books. We must remind ourselves that the rejection of modern-face types had begun long before 1891 and that the Kelmscott Press was, in a sense, merely the most visible manifestation of the antiquarian winds which had been stirring the bibliographical waters in England for a number of decades. The two figures who - apart, of course, from Morris — played the most decisive roles in sponsoring the revival of older types and ornaments were the publisher William Pickering and Charles Whittingham the Younger, the owner of the Chiswick Press. Stanley Morison, in fact, has suggested that Morris's celebrated achievements as a printer were firmly rooted in the less spectacular work ofPickering, Pugin, and certain publishers associated with the Oxford Movement. In a letter to Geoffrey Keynes, written in 1924, he remarked that ‘Pickering was the first to design books in an eclectico-antiquarian spirit: I mean that he was the first to go back to XVIth century Venice & Lyons for inspiration. IfI am right Pickering is the tribal chief of the Morris-Ricketts-Rogers school."7 It is an astute observation: period typography - that is to say, typography which deliberately imitates the style of older books — has become so commonplace in the twentieth century that we assume it has always been with us, but the evidence suggests very strongly that it was invented by Pickering and several contemporaries of Catholic or Anglo-Catholic sympathies. As early as 183o Pickering acquired a set of wood-engraved initials designed by Thomas Stothard, but it was a decade later when the ornamentation of the Chiswick Press-Pickering books began in earnest. Inspired perhaps by the publication of Henry Shaw's Alphabets, Numerals and Devices of the Middle Ages (1845),18 Whittingham commissioned more than a dozen sets of initials from Mary Byfield and launched a series of sumptuous reprints — including several versions of the English Prayer Book — adorned with blackletter titles and attractive borders, initials, and tailpieces. (Gladstone testified in 189o that ‘the pages of their Prayer Book are a perfect picture, reminding one of those early printed books which are still marvellous examples of printing'.9) Though Whittingham turned occasionally to other printers, his finest books were produced by the Chiswick Press, which had the deserved reputation of being the most distinguished old-style' printer in England during the latter half of the nineteenth century. Even Morris had to serve what was, in effect, an apprenticeship at the Chiswick Press before he could set up a press of his own. But there were other old-style printers, less frequently remembered today perhaps than the Chiswick Press but famous in their own time. The firm of George Falkner and Sons in Manchester, for example, were celebrated for thelarge collection of woodcuts, decorated initials, and head- and tailpieces with which they ornamented pseudo-antique books. Falkner productions were invariably described by reviewers or the firm itself as ‘quaint' — i.e. self-consciously antiquarian in flavour. (It is significant that Morris never uses the wordquaint' when writing about his own variety of old-style printing.) The Falkners had set up a wooden hand-press in ‘the quaint Caxtonian Printing Offices in Old Manchester', and their printer's mark, designed by Walter Crane, showed a pressman at work on a hand-press. Printers like Falkner and Sons issued a good deal of ephemera as well as books: their Old Style Calendar for 1882', for instance, contained ‘many delightful and entertaining particulars, very commodious indeed and profytable, worth the labour of your reading. (A reprint from many early Kalendars.)'Their book titles included The Shakespeare Show Book and The Old English Fayre Volume; many of the books were stained both internally and externally to suggest great age.2° (This sort of fakery was, needless to say, the typographical equivalent of the fraudulent restoration of ancient buildings which Morris condemned near the end of his life.)
