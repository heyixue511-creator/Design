17–19.

## Renjian Pictorial, 1920 Art: Zheng Nu-quan

Renjian Pictorial is a collection of about 190 works by Zheng Nu-quan, one of the first-generation mainland-Chinese cartoonists. The artist was critical of traditional Chinese values and customs, and used cartoons to discuss topics such as “The Blemish of Early Marriages,” “The Fault of Inadequate Family Education,” “The Vice of Opium Smoking," and “The Failing of Prostitution." The easily recognizable brush-and-ink style of Zheng's drawings is typical of Chinese cartoons of the time. Renjian Pictorial was published posthumously by the Leung Kwok-ying Drugstore in Hong Kong and given away free to customers. A block of text advertising various products appears at the top of each page.

## 20–26. Shanghai Sketch, 1928 Art: Various artists

Shanghai Sketch was one of the fist successful manhua magazines, selling about 3,000 copies a week. Each issue of the eight-page, tabloid-size newspaper included four pages of manhua, printed in two-color lithography. Published in Shanghai and edited by the famous manhua artists Ye Qian-yu and Zhang Guangyu, Shanghai Sketch included work by artists such as Huang Wen-nong and Zhang Zhen-yu. Some of its content was political. Power in Hand, by Huang Wennong, for example, shows Chinese president Jiang Jieshi with a fist out of proportion with his head and body, reflecting his control of the Nationalist Party. Shanghai Sketch also carried the first recurring fictional manhua character in China, Mr. Wang (Wang Xiansheng), created by Ye Qian-yu. Mr. Wang's focus on everyday life influenced the development of other manhua characters and story lines, but the plot of Mr. Wang also often reflected the political situation in China as well as the tension caused by an impending invasion by Japan.

32-37.

Modern Sketch, 1934

Art: Various artists

This Shanghai-produced magazine was one of the most important manhua publications of the 1930s. Distributed throughout China, the magazine became a magnet for the best manhua artists, including Lu Shao-fei, Zhang Guang-yu, Huang Miao-zi, and Huang Wen-nong, as well as editor Lu Shao-fei. The cover of the first issue, drawn by Zhang Guang-yu, featured an ink-bottle knight riding a horse made of paper and pencils, implying that the magazine itself was courageous in challenging the evils of society. During its three years of publication, Modern Sketch’s artists shifted toward serious manhua about the impending Japanese invasion. Thirty-nine issues were published before Japan attacked China in July 1937.

33-40.

Shanghai Manhua, 1936 Art: Various artists

Although short-lived, Shanghai Manhua was one of the most influential cartoon magazines published in Shanghai. Its thirteen issues——the first published just a few months before the Sino-Japanese War broke out—included work by Zhang Ding, Liao Bing-xiong, and Huan Jun-wu, and introduced Sam-mo, by Zhang Le-ping, and Mr. Chan's Journey to Beijing, by Ye Quian-yu. As befitting the tenor of the times, much of the content was politically oriented, and addressed the inequalities emerging in Shanghai, China's first modern city.

41-46.

The Modern Chinese Cartoonist Exhibition catalog, 1939

Art: Various artists

Before Hong Kong was occupied by Japan and the Pacific region was thrown into the Second World War, some of the Shanghai and Guangzhou artists who had fled to Hong Kong made the territory a center for anti-Japanese propaganda. Under the leadership of Ye Quian-yu, a branch of the National Cartoon Association was established in Hong Kong. In 1939, the group organized an exhibition of antiwar cartoons and comics, The Modern Chinese Cartoonist Exhibition. Participating artists included Zhang Zhen-yu, Huang Miao-zi, Cheng Ka-chun, Zhang Guang-yu, Lee Fanfu, Ting Chung, and Ye Quian-yu.

47-51.

This Is a Cartoon Era, 1948

Art: Various artists

During the civil war in mainland China, several Shanghai and Guangzhou cartoonists moved to Hong Kong. Once there, they made the territory a new center of manhua, organizing a cartoonists' club called Renjian Huahui and a cartoon studies department, and putting out This Is a Cartoon Era. Although they published just one issue, it revealed a moment of transition in the relationship between China and the rest of the world. For the cover, Zhang Guang-yu borrowed the cover images of Modern Sketch's first issue: an ink-bottle knight holding a pen fights the devil. Inside, Zhang and other artists, including Liao Bingxiong and Cheng Ka-chun, expressed their concerns about the relationship between China and the U.S., about the Nationalist Party, and about the unfairness implicit in capitalist society, among other topics.

## 52–61.

## New China Little Picture Books, 1950s–1960s Art: Various artists

After the Communists took power in 1949, the traditional mythological and costume genres of lianhuantu were no longer allowed on the mainland. However, the format itself was not banned, and several high-quality lianhuantu lauding Communist themes appeared. These manhua mainly expressed Communist views of power, the struggles of the poor under capitalism, and working-class heroism. Even the life story of Lenin was made into a lianhuantu.

## 62-64.

## Cry for the Death of a Crazy Man, 1955 Art/Script: Lui Yu-tin

Anti-communism was among the many topics Lui Yutin covered during the 1950s in his famous series Wulung Wong (King of Blunders). The example shown is not from the Wu-lung Wong series, but it does carry an anti-Communist message. The story depicts the power struggles in the Communist party, wherein people could accuse each other without reason. The story ends by saying all people involved in the Communist Party will go to hell after they die.

## 65-66.

## Three Men Going Home, 1955 Art/Script: Lui Yu-tin

Most of the anti-Communism comics series drawn by Lui Yu-tin in the 1950s were published by Asia Press Ltd., an American-funded publishing house. The story follows three American soldiers who make the mistake of moving to a Communist country because they admire its ideology. After arriving there, they discover the ugliness of the Communist system and with some effort manage to escape.

## 67-69.

The World of E-king Yen, 1968

Art/Script: Yen E-king

Yen E-King was a regular editorial cartoonist for the Dai Chung Daily and Express Daily, which usually dealt with international affairs. His work was well received by the public, and eventually collected into book form. The World of E-king Yen’s thirty-four pages covered such topics and events as the Cultural Revolution, the Vietnam War, the depreciation of the British pound, the Civil Rights movement in the U.S., and conflicts in the Middle East.

## 70–71.

Four People Thematic Caricatures, 1970

Art/Script: Lam Jong-git, Nam Ying, Heung-shan Awong, Chau Hang-jok

This book collected work originally published in the Sing Tao Night Daily by editorial cartoonists Lam Jong-git, Nam Ying, Heung-shan A-wong, and Chau Hang-jok. Their themes were usually realistic portrayals of Hong Kong life, such as these restaurant scenes.

## 72–74.

The Hundred Manhua Poems, 1983

Art/Script: Nam Hui-man

Nam Hui-man based these drawings on his studies in mainland China. Each manhua and simple poem is intended to illustrate a problem of Chinese Communist ideology, often in a sarcastic or critical tone. Nam's book includes strong critiques of the destruction of the Chinese economy and the suppression of free speech and thinking.

## 75–78.

Theater of 1997,1985

Art/Script: Zunzi, Ma Long, Local Boy

The main contributors to Theater of 1997 included top newspaper editorial cartoonists such as Zunzi, Ma Long, and Local Boy. Their book addressed such topics as political asylum and the Chinese-British negotiations over the 1997 handover of Hong Kong, and included satirical cartoons about Communist politics.

## 79-80.

Rather Die Than Change, 1990

Art/Script: Yat Mok

This trendy, liberal-minded strip, which first appeared in a daily Hong Kong newspaper in 1988, explored ideas about the political relationship between Hong Kong and China. The strip ceased after the June 4 crackdown in 1989. When the series was collected in a book the next year, the artist titled it Rather Die Than Change in tribute to those who died in Tiananmen Square.
