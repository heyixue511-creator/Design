# 2 The graphic formation of Isotype 1925-40

Robin Kinross

This discussion attempts to show the development of the graphic approach to the Vienna Method and Isotype.¹ The examples considered have been drawn only from the years between 1925 and 1940 and, principally, from the first five of these years. It is in these first years that the most intense development took place. By 193o one may consider the work as having attained maturity. And certainly when, around 1935, the name Isotype was devised, it could refer to a coherent approach to ordering material in graphic form. The work continued to develop after 1935, of course. The discussion of configuration here shows that the work of the later 193os was marked by notable extensions in this aspect of its approach. An examination of the work done in England after 1945 would show further extensions (see chapters 10 and 11). But these are extensions of, rather than departures from, a way of working that had been established. And it is the formation of this typical approach to ordering material that is examined in this chapter.

After a discussion of issues that refer generally to this body of work, particular examples are discussed. In the cases of the charts on births and deaths and the charts on unemployment, every known example (from the years 1925 to 194o) of a chart on the subject is reproduced and discussed. In the cases of the other examples discussed, a selection has been made; examples that seem to show best the formation of the graphic approach have been chosen.

## Considerations in dating charts

## The T files

Illustrations in this section have been drawn principally from the set of files compiled in Vienna and in The Hague between 1929 and 1940, and known as the ‘T files' (1C 5/13-17). These contain bromide prints of photographs of charts (for the most part exhibition charts rather than charts designed for publication); each print is numbered and given the prefix ‘T', standing for ‘Tafel' (chart); the numbers of these prints have been retained here as a means of referring to the charts.²

Up to about T16o, charts are grouped by subject, and from this point the order is approximately that in which the charts were completed. The T files can thus be of help in dating charts, from the point at which the order of compilation matches the date at which the chart was completed and photographed. After 1929 it became a matter of routine to photograph charts as they were completed. The T files are thus the best index to the work discussed here, in the sense that they offer a very full picture of its development, and in that they provide a reference number for each chart that they reproduce.

## Criteria of dating

The position of a chart in the T files is a means of dating charts made after 1929. Other more general criteria of dating are:

– the evidence of the dates in the material of chronological charts; this may provide a date after which the chart was made;

– the date of publication of those charts reproduced in contemporary publications; this provides a date before which a chart was made; the chronology of graphic conventions that may be constructed from those charts that can be quite precisely dated; this chronology may then be applied to those charts whose date is otherwise unclear. There are however dangers in an unconsidered application of these criteria; the chief of these dangers will now be discussed.

A more general lesson that may be drawn from these examples is that one cannot assume the process of making charts to be simple – they should not be taken ‘at face value' – and any discussion that touches on the date or process of manufacture of charts will need to be interpretative and conditional.

## Unevenness of development

Besides the physical alteration and revision of charts – which was perhaps not a usual procedure – there are other difficulties in dating charts, and particularly in the attempt to date on the evidence of style or of graphic convention. Retrospective commentary on the work will tend to make an assumption of even progression – that once a particular feature has appeared, it will appear in all possible subsequent charts - and the claim of the work to the status of 'method' or 'system' encourages this assumption. But, though there are certainly clear progressions and developments, these are not absolute: conventions may not be used where one would have expected them to be used; there are occasional inconsistencies within a single piece of work; and one may detect experiments - pictograms or configurations that appear once and which were then replaced by other versions, perhaps new or perhaps previously tried. These things will disturb only a very abstract view of the work. For each chart had its particular circumstances, being the outcome of particular sets of people confronting particular and various materials. During the years in Vienna, the composition of the working team was never stable; even in the years of comparative maturity between 193o and 1934, the composition of the team would have changed frequently, largely due to the commitment to keep five people in the ussR as supervisors to Izostat (see chapter 5). There were always part-time and occasional members in the team. In the light of this frequent variation in the composition of the team, and in the light of the external difficulties of life in Vienna at that time, the consistency that was achieved was remarkable. After the upheaval of 1934 and the move to The Hague, the team became smaller and more stable and, in this respect, conditions for ‘even development' were more favourable than in the Vienna years.

## Reuse of charts

Another kind of evidence that may be adduced for dating is the ascription of charts to exhibitions whose date is known. For example, the large exhibitions to which the Museum contributed and which were marked by special issues of the Österreichische Gemeinde-Zeitung: the 'Gesolei' exhibition (1926) and 'Wien und die Wiener' (1927). Charts may be taken to have been exhibited at these exhibitions on the evidence of their reproduction in the accompanying publication, or of some verbal description there, or for reasons of visual similarity with charts that can be ascribed to the exhibition with certainty, or for reasons of thematic consistency with the subject of the exhibition. But even if such evidence suggests that a chart was shown at a known occasion, it may yet have been made some significant time before that occasion. Charts were specially commissioned and made for exhibitions, but charts were also shown that had been made for some previous occasion. This was certainly a practice of the formative years; the photographs of the Linz exhibition of 1929, for example, show charts that are by no means a stylistically united set. Certainly too, the permanent exhibition areas in Vienna were largely supplied with charts that had been made for other, specific occasions; this was the basis on which the Museum had been founded (see appendix A). And one may notice that Neurath's articles were often illustrated with charts that had been made some time before the date of publication; occasionally the lapse of time is a matter of years – a long time in a period of rapid development.

As well as the assumption of an even development of graphic conventions and of style, retrospective examination may tend to assume in the minds of the producers an attitude that continually discards work that has been stylistically superseded. This was clearly not the attitude of the makers of this work, and it would not have been consistent with Neurath's characteristic approach of using the materials that were to hand (rather than waiting for perfect circumstances to arrive). Charts continued in use – in exhibitions, or reproduced in printed form, or as slides – for some time after the point at which they had been stylistically superseded. A commentary on the graphic development of this work may need to remind itself that a chart can be stylistically surpassed and yet still educationally useful.

## The evidence of contemporary comment

The general commentary that follows draws principally on two kinds of evidence: the work itself and Neurath's written comments on the work. It is necessary to consider the discussion of graphic method and the descriptions of charts in Neurath's writings because this can provide evidence that cannot be conveyed by the reproductions through which we largely know this work. It is only from such descriptions that we know anything of the colour or size of the early exhibition charts. Neurath's comments also illuminate those aspects of the work that do survive in reproduction; and, besides this, his discussion of graphic method allows us to see what was significant in the work in the mind of its chief architect.

## The guideline notes

Neurath's published comments on graphic method are, however, not extensive. As will be explained, it did not seem necessary to him to discuss publicly the details of the processes of designing and constructing charts. The most detailed discussion of this dimension of the work is a set of notes, each on a particular aspect of graphic method and each typed on a separate sheet of paper; they are called here the 'guideline notes'.³

It seems likely that these notes arose from discussions of the core members of the Museum's team (Neurath, Reidemeister, Arntz) and that they were a record of current practice.4 Though it is tempting to see these notes as something more than this – as guidelines for all those working in the Museum – one must say that they do not have the formality of expression or physical form that would have accompanied a set of notes with that purpose. There is no evidence (of a material kind, or in the recollections of those who worked in the team) to suggest that instructions were ever communicated through a set of guidelines.

The guideline notes, then, make explicit some of the conventions of graphic method in the period of first maturity, and are thus an important source of supplementary evidence for any commentary on the development of the work.

## The size of charts

The dimensions of the Museum's exhibition charts seem to have been standardized from the start; Neurath refers to their 'unified dimensions' in his first article on the work of the Museum, in 1925. The maximum vertical dimension of charts was at first fixed at 128 cm. In a discussion of the charts shown at the Gesolei' exhibition of 1926, Neurath refers to this as the dimension of the square charts.7 One may assume from later discussion of chart size by Neurath that the dimension of 128 cm was chosen primarily in order to obtain a size that was convenient for the viewer – to allow a perception of the chart untroubled by the necessity for movement of the head or other parts of the body. The unit of 128 cm was later changed to 126 cm, to obtain a dimension that was easily divisible by three as well as by two; and, as it happened, this had a further advantage of approximating to 4 ft. All references to the measurements of the Museum's charts, subsequent to the reference of 1926 referred to, imply a basic unit of 126 cm.

The principles of the sizing of the Museum's charts were explained by Neurath most fully in 1933:

Every effort has been made to choose the dimensions of the standard chart so that, when the eye of the viewer is level with the centre point, the chart will still allow room for a table or glass case to complement it. Thus the bottom edge of the chart should generally be placed about 9o cm above the floor level. This produces, as the basic measure for the charts, a 126 cm square. The square is visually especially suitable, and is used, where possible, in the exhibitions of the Museum and the Mundaneum. If smaller charts are needed, they are derived from these squares. The derivation is a visual one. This would not be the case if the basic unit was a square metre.

## The graphic and typographic elements of charts

## Colour and form

The original charts of the Museum that survive date from around 1933, and they show a standard range of colours (see pp. 178–84 below). But in the earliest years colours were not strictly standardized, nor were the limits placed on the application of colours as clear as they were to become. This is known from the personal testimony of Marie Neurath and from certain remarks in Otto Neurath's writings. For example, commenting on charts made for the Gesolei' exhibition of 1926, Neurath referred to these colours: red (used for men), a lighter red (for women), blue, orange, green, grey, violet, brown.1º There is no attempt yet to specify a limited number of colours. Limitation to, perhaps, eight standard colours is evident in Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft (193o) – though not in the cartographic charts, which show a more lavish use of colour. In 1936, however, Neurath wrote: ‘There are only seven colours for use in Isotype pictures: white, blue, green, yellow, red, brown, black; and only some of them may have a further division (as into: light blue, dark blue, light green, dark green, light red, dark red, light brown, dark brown), or be mixed with others, as white and black to make grey, yellow and red to make orange etc.'11 These seven, with the addition of a pure grey (not a toned black) were the colours that were used in Modern man in the making (1939).

A feature of the early charts, and something that was discussed by Neurath in his writings of the first years, was the use of background colour to carry meaning. This device was employed extensively on the ‘Gesolei' exhibition charts.² In an article published shortly after that exhibition, Neurath remarked that: 'the membership of the different groups of the insured, as well as the fact of the uninsured people, is expressed merely through background colour' (see images overleaf). And he goes on to refer to the disadvantages of this device:

In future the Museum will try to make this kind of thing more directly understandable, through alteration of the human figures or through appropriate symbols. It is not sufficient to denote land workers insured against illness through a green background, when perhaps the sickle could be introduced as a symbol for agriculture. It is certainly very hard to design the characterizing marks to be as schematic and as simple as possible. To start with, we must he satisfied with striving for simpler forms.'

From this point (autumn 1926) therefore it was a matter of principle that charts should not rely on colour to convey meaning. Colours were

## Male and female

The differentiation of male and female through the use of darker and lighter coloured pictograms is one of the principles explained in the early chart on method (p.146 below). And, in support of the convention, in an article of 1927, Neurath refers to this differentiation as a convention of Egyptian wall paintings.¹⁵ The practice seems, however, to have been dropped from the Museum's charts soon after this time: Marie Neurath remembers Otto Neurath's reservations about showing women as ‘weaker' than men – this offended his feminist convictions.

There is evidence of one early (1926) chart showing women as red and men as blue – following the popular convention of 'blue for boys, pink for girls' – though this was not a settled convention: it does not appear in later charts (for example, those reproduced in colour). And in the first (1925) charts on clothes makers, women had apparently been shown as blue and men as red.

From about 1928, male and female were always signified through differences in the form of the pictograms, whether or not any extra differentiation of colour was used. The move from symbolizing through colour to symbolizing through form was, it becomes clear, a general tendency in the work of the first years. The differentiation of gender through form is the subject of one of the guideline notes:

## Masculine-feminine:

the visible, outer differentiation of male and female, which occurs in all manifestations of nature, must also be maintained in the pictograms. In black and white, the rectangular forms of the male and the rounded forms of the female will be stressed. Through this, the imagination of the viewer receives considerable support – though this remains an unconscious process.

The development of the ways in which the gender of pictograms was shown may be seen in the charts on clothes makers and in those on the membership of free trade unions. (See pp. 169–7o below.)

## Qualifying symbols

The need to group pictograms within a chart and to show subdivisions within groups of pictograms led to the use of secondary symbols qualifying the principal pictograms. As has been discussed in the section on 'Colour and form', the function of qualifying pictograms had at first been performed by colour – in the pictogram itself or as background. The charts reproduced in this section show the development of qualifying devices. Two kinds of qualifying symbol have been distinguished, and the development of each is shown through two sets of comparable charts.

The charts T64f, T175b, and T247i show the development of the means used to group related pictograms in a chart. T64f uses, as well as verbal headings, backgrounds composed of symbols signifying agriculture', 'industry', 'trade and commerce', to distinguish these three groups of the principal pictograms. Incorporation became the conventional way of distinguishing subcategories within a group of people, and it features in the exposition of method in International picture language (1936, p.52).

## Guide-pictures [Führungsbilder]

The image standing outside, usually above or to the left of, the main body of the chart, was termed the Führungsbild' (translated as 'guidepicture' in International picture language). The guide-picture was seen by Neurath as having two functions: to give some indication of the subject matter of the chart, and to make apparent any differences or divisions in the content.18 In this second respect, the guide-picture performs much the same function as what has been identified in the preceding section as the first kind of qualifying symbol – those secondary symbols that serve to group or to associate pictograms. And when, in the mature work, these qualifying symbols are moved out of the area of the pictograms – as in T247i – they might be regarded as guide-pictures. But they remain individual symbols, rather than pictures' or collections of symbols, and so are not considered here to be guide-pictures proper.

## Fractions

## Juxtaposition of pictograms (direction of reading)

The two principles that came to be adopted in deciding whether pictograms should be placed on a vertical or horizontal axis were: that the conventional pattern of reading, from top left to bottom right, should be followed; that pictograms should be disposed in the way that the objects depicted usually dispose themselves in reality.²’ This meant that most simple charts concerned to compare quantities would dispose their pictograms in rows, rather than columns. There are, however, certain charts that contradict these principles – but very few, and almost all of them made in the first year of work. T3e is an example from 1925, where the coffins are unnaturally disposed; later versions change the axis to horizontal and show circular coins. The births and deaths charts provide a series of charts with unnaturally disposed objects and these, together with T41, reproduced here, are almost the only examples of this that were made after 1926.

Vertical stacking of pictograms was occasionally employed in charts that show the height' of money values: T398d is concerned with pension sums, and T525b with interest on capital outlay.

## Titles and other non-pictorial elements

The principal feature of the development of the typographic elements of the charts is the decisive change in treatment and method of production that occurred towards the end of 1928. Before this point, lettering was produced by hand (usually with a 'Redis' pen), titles and any text-matter were placed centrally and, in the earlier charts especially, there was a tendency to make 'block layouts' (to extend or compress lines so as to produce a rectangular shape); some charts of 1928 employ calligraphic lettering. From late in 1928, lettering was printed from metal type and placed so as to range from the left.

The point at which the conventions for treating typographic elements changed is also exactly the point at which metal type (Futura) was introduced.2ª But these two things seem not to have been directly connected: Gerd Arntz was responsible for determining the new typographic conventions of the charts, but not for introducing the use of lettering printed from metal type, or Futura in particular; this had been decided before he joined the team.³º

## Data and keys to charts

Other non-pictorial components of the charts show certain developments. In general, the earliest charts carry verbal and numerical matter that was later dispensed with; the treatment of numerical data exempli fies this. In the charts of 1925, exact numerical information is included in the body of the chart. And in his first article on graphic method, discussing the lack of precision that pictorial methods necessarily entail, Neurath suggests that 'pictorial inexactitude may be easily allowed for, indeed the exact figures can be placed nearby'.³³ The charts shown at the ‘Gesolei' exhibition of the next year (1926) show data placed in the margin of the chart; by 1928, this information was normally omitted from charts altogether.

33. 'Darstellungsmethoden des Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseums' (1925), p. 19.

A similar development may be noticed in the way in which pictograms were identified. In early charts, pictograms were reproduced in the margin of the chart where the quantity represented by each was given. The explanatory pictogram appears usually in the same size as the pictograms in the body of the chart, though in some early charts the pictogram in the key is a reduced version (thus T13c). This latter practice could be the cause of confusion, and in contradiction to the principle that forbids the magnification of pictograms. It was soon dropped and, from about 1927, the function of pictograms was, except in unusual circumstances, explained in words. One such exception is T1823; this is essentially a 'cartogram', employing many symbols, the meanings of which are most easily explained by showing each symbol in a key (in the size in which they appear in the body of the chart).

## Configuration: principles of ordering material

The body of work under discussion may be classified according to the kind of material shown and according to the ways in which the material is shown – the conventions of configuration or disposition that are employed. It is difficult to separate these two aspects (content and form) but the list that follows may serve as a working scheme of configurations. Even in the earliest work the full range of these configurations may be discerned (see examples in chapter 1).

Charts showing quantified material:

1. rows or columns

2. division of a whole (usually a checker-board’)

3. geographically ordered (cartograms and more diagrammatic charts)

4. quantities related to area (usually showing densities)

5. flows.

Charts showing non-quantified material.

The lack of any subdivision within the category of charts showing non-quantified material reflects the fact that the great bulk of this work was based on numerical information, and that any other kind of chart must seem exceptional. An inspection of the whole body of work suggests, however, that after about 1936 a significantly greater proportion of charts fall into the 'non-quantified' category. This tendency becomes very clear in the work done in England after 1945.

In one of his published discussions of configuration Neurath confined himself to the first category, of rows or columns, within the larger group of charts showing quantified material.³4 He broke down this category as follows, indicating the purpose for which the particular configuration might be used; each of these subcategories is illustrated here by a chart dating from around the time of Neurath's discussion of the issue (1928).

The categories suggested here (including those put forward by Neurath himself) are no more than an initial, rough scheme. That they may be applied most easily to the earlier work – that of, say, the first five years – is significant. One may offer the following interpretation: the work of the first year shows a perhaps surprising variety of configuration of quantified material; in the next few years, the work is concentrated largely in just one of these categories of configuration – rows or columns; these were the years in which basic elements of the Vienna Method were being discovered and, though the concentration on rows and columns was largely a consequence of the tasks asked of the Museum at that time, this provided a necessary delimitation of the field within which basic elements such as the colour and form of pictograms, interrelation of pictograms, and typography, could be explored; after these basic elements had reached a stage of first maturity (around 1930), the other and perhaps most basic element of configuration could be taken up again as something to be explored; the work of the 193os – and particularly after 1936 – provides a large number of charts in the categories outside that of ‘quantified: rows or columns’.

The discussions of charts that follow are intended to show the graphic formation of the work. For the reasons just advanced, this means that the examples come primarily from the category ofquantified: rows or columns'. To correct any impression that this category of chart might be typical of the work as a whole, this discussion of categories of configuration will conclude by reproducing and commenting on charts that show both the crudeness of the categories and something of the scope and variety of the work in its maturity.
