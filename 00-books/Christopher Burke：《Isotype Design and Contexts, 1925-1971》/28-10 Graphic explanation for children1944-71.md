# 10 Graphic explanation for children 1944-71

Sue Walker

The children's books produced by the Isotype Institute provide an excellent insight into Marie Neurath's work as a transformer and show that she had a particular skill in making charts and illustrations that were accessible to children of all ages. She did this through careful and consistent use of pictograms, drawings, colour, and page layout, combined with text that was relevant, accurate, and written with the needs of younger readers in mind. She was gifted in providing clear explanations. A ten-year-old child wrote to her and asked 'Where did you get your information on “Rockets and jets"? Did you draw the pictures or did someone else? Is it fun to write books?';1 in reply Marie Neurath offered a child-friendly explanation of how the book was made:

We got the information from many books and periodicals, one of our institute went to the library and read the latest material. Then we talked, and she explained everything to me, and I sat down and made new sketches, and talked them over with other people, and showed them to a man who knows everything about the subject, and then the final drawings were made by the designers in our institute. You see, this is like a little factory making picture books, we make one after the other, it is great fun.²

This brief description encapsulates the process that underpinned an extremely prolific period in the work of the Isotype Institute. It began in the 194os, soon after Otto and Marie Neurath settled in England. After Otto Neurath's death in 1945, Marie Neurath continued to work on children's books as author and transformer until the early 197os.

Accompanying this letter was a detailed typewritten rationale, 'Isotype books for children', that summarized Neurath's thinking about how books should be designed for children. Many of these ideas were drawn from his childhood experiences and memories as well as his views on visual education. He suggested that children's learning benefited from pictures designed to encourage children to look, compare and reach conclusions for themselves. He wanted to find ways to encourage children to engage with the content being presented through familiar objects and situations, and characters and conventions that they could get to know and relate to:

There are many fine books, giving explanations to children and adults, but usually they are learned'. These IsOTYPE BOOKs try to avoid that in any case, therefore just the creating of an environment for a child is the point. It is not so much the question, how to transfer in the most direct and simple way some knowledge, but how to satisfy the possible questions of a child, his love of action, his identifying himself with a person on the page etc.

The Neuraths' commitment to what they described as the active attitude' underpinned the view that children should be encouraged to engage with the charts and pictures in order to find out information for themselves. Young readers were thus advised in many of the introductory texts to the children's books that the more you look, the more you will see':

There are no special rules about the way you should read' these charts, but you will find it useful to make sure first that you understand what each symbol stands for. Then look at the whole chart and try to see how the different parts fit together. ... You will be able to answer most of the questions by looking carefully at the pictures ..

This approach to designing pervaded all the children's books produced by the Isotype Institute. Accuracy, clarity, and attention to detail were important not only in the design of the final chart or illustration, but in the way that Marie Neurath and her team approached the work. Much time was spent gathering materials from libraries and museums, and asking experts in particular fields whether the sketches and drawings they produced made sense.

## Production challenges in the early days

The first project that appealed to people at Adprint, and which brought together Otto Neurath's ideas about the kind of content that would benefit children's learning and Marie Neurath's approach to visual organization, was the 'Visual history of mankind'. This was an ambitious, collaborative project involving the Neuraths, the educationist Joseph Lauwerys, and others.¹º As had been the case with Isotype work in Vienna, it approached the teaching of history through consideration of issues that affected society as a whole, rather than individual people. It looked at the past through the lens of everyday activities covering topics such as ‘Making light' and 'Travelling on land', rather than kings, queens, and battles.

Marie Neurath worked very closely with her husband on the first two of the three 'Visual history' books, as she noted later:

I always showed my sketches to Neurath, right to the last, before I gave them for production. If he proposed another approach, it now often happened that I had already tried and rejected it. But if we found that his suggestion was an improvement, he was pleased, he said, that something could still occur to him too. It was, however different with the work on the visual history of mankind: with his extensive historical knowledge and vivid imagination, Neurath came very much to the fore, and I was just his helping hand. He contributed to the graphic details, for example in the plan and elevation of the medieval town. 11

After Otto Neurath's death, Marie Neurath continued to work on the 'Visual history' and some of his other ideas, too. She wrote:

Of the many continuing jobs, the completion of the visual history was the hardest. ... Joseph Lauwerys was always available for advice over the choice of subjects for the charts to be done in the second and third books, and we managed it in the end.1²

It was also a challenging time for Marie Neurath in other ways, and it is evident from the correspondence that Lauwerys helped her in connection with her work.1³ The Isotype Institute's relationship to Adprint between 1946 and 1948 was uneasy.¹4 She recalled:

... difficulties arose from dealing with a new man for the book work: Foges enlarged his firm and created his own publishing department with Max Parrish in charge. Unlike Foges, who had known about us already in Vienna, Parrish had never met Neurath, and our whole approach was foreign to him. I gave him International picture language to read, but that did not help; he said only that it was utopian. 15

Despite this, Foges invited Marie Neurath and her team to set up office at Adprint's premises in Rathbone Place, London, and the ‘Visual history' was completed there.

The interest and support of Foges and others at Adprint was timely. Adprint's approach to publishing through 'book packaging' meant that the books, printed in many colours, could be produced in times of austerity. However, Max Parrish was particularly concerned to ensure that the books were financially viable. In 1946, for example, he summarized his concerns about production costs (as a result of the many colour printings required by the Neuraths for the charts in the ‘Visual history') in a paper ‘Visual schoolbooks' for colleagues at Adprint. Parrish assumed that there would need to be 'school book' and 'bookshop' editions of the series to cover costs, with print runs of 2o,ooo in each edition. The plan was to print 40,ooo using the same colour plates, but different black printings for the text (the idea seemed to have been that the bookshop edition contained the answers to the questions that complemented the charts; see p. 402 below). Parrish wrote of the issues that concerned him over the schoolbook edition:

I am still of the opinion that if the books are to have a real chance to get into the schools in quantity they must be priced at not more than 2/6d. This means cutting the format to the bone, but in fact the new design gives a not unpleasant book, which we might expect to seem value at half a crown. We cannot, however, give it anything but a stiffish paper cover, printed in two colours. If we publish, and if we take our normal profit, the amounts left for Isotype (.5o [pence] per copy) and plant are negligible. Nor is there any margin for additional profit for another publisher. Of course, we should continue our research to find whether the schools can be expected to pay more than half a crown [2 shillings 6 pence]; but I hardly think we can get much further than this without a book in proof. 1⁶

Later in the paper he noted that he could not see how to reduce the manufacturing costs further, but suggested that For the geographies, where drawing has not started, we might try to redesign; we should certainly cut the number of colours to six'. Nevertheless, and despite such issues, in 1948 the Isotype-Adprint relationship was affirmed through an exhibition at Adprint House promoting ‘the work of the Isotype Institute for industry, commerce, public information and education' (as the invitation put it).

The Isotype Institute, under the direction of Marie Neurath, went on to produce many series of children's books. Many were worked on concurrently and also alongside other Isotype projects, such as the work in West Africa (see chapter 11). It is difficult to establish just how many people worked for the Institute at any one time; many worked on a part-time basis and did so from their homes, and some took on a variety of roles. In 1974 Marie Neurath recalled the various stages involved in the making of the charts:

It is the responsibility of the transformer' to understand the data, to get all necessary information from the expert, to decide what it is worth transmitting to the public, how to make it understandable, how to link it with general knowledge or with information already given in other charts. In this sense the transformer is the trustee of the public. He has to remember the rules and keep to them, adding new variations where advisable, at the same time avoiding unnecessary deviations which would only confuse. He has to produce a rough of the chart in which many details would have been decided: title, arrangement, type, number and colour of symbols, caption etc. It is a blueprint from which the artist works.18

She used the term ‘designing' to refer to her role in this process in relation to the planning of the ‘Visual history of mankind':

My personal contribution will be the designing of every chart and the supervision of the research and drawing work connected with it, within our institute. 19

In practice, and perhaps indicative of the collaborative ethos that both Otto and Marie Neurath considered important, there was considerable overlapping and sharing of roles: the designated 'designers', for example, undertook research and commented on the wording of the text. Marie Neurath was actively involved in the supervision of various stages of the work from writing briefs for those involved in the research, to annotating final drawings with comments about accuracy and detail.

Thus, making the charts and illustrations for the books involved research and gathering of background information; transformation, including drafting the text in many cases; drawing the components of the illustration and making artwork for printing, which involved pasting down elements of the illustration, applying mechanical tints and producing overlays for colour printing. It is evident from notes and correspondence that each of these stages was undertaken by different people. ## Developing ideas about children's books

Work that began in the mid-194os resulted in If you could see inside (1948), I'll show you how it happens (1948), Railways under London (1948), and in the two series ‘Visual history of mankind' (1948) and ‘Visual science' (1950-2).

Otto Neurath's idea in Just boxes' had been that approaching things from an unexpected angle could interest young readers: 'modern implements present themselves usually in boxes, therefore one has to look into the boxes, to know how they work.' His sketches showed his initial thoughts, and his proposal to Adprint in the form of a handmade mock-up comprised double-page spreads showing the inner workings of particular kinds of box', such as a greenhouse and a diving bell.

Marie Neurath was quick to realize that the boxes' idea was too constraining, and moved instead to the more general notion of 'what's inside' that resulted in If you could see inside. She recalled:

... it played with the idea that boxes which look similar from the outside are inside quite different; jack-in-a-boxes, cameras, radios, etc. But one then got on to things that were too difficult, and so it was not yet satisfactory. I avoided this by giving up the similarity of outer form: so I could take a wasp's nest and a lighthouse, volcano and cave with stalactites.²²

If you could see inside (1948), the first of the 'Wonders of the modern world' series, was a 32-page book that comprised a series of explanations in words and pictures of something likely to interest curious young minds: 'The wonderful thing that happens inside an egg'; 'The underground home of the mole'; as well as enigmatic titles such as 'If they could see inside!', which showed how tunnels could be dug for escape from a castle under attack. The seemingly miscellaneous choice of topics was connected through the use of cross sections, thus following through on Otto Neurath's original idea. Marie Neurath ensured that other graphic attributes reflected Isotype principles: omission of unnecessary detail, simplicity of shapes, and use of Isotype pictograms as appropriate. She wrote the text that accompanied the illustrations so that it was relevant to the needs of younger readers (see p. 424 below). I'll show you how it happens was produced at the same time as If you could see inside and followed the same format. A somewhat miscellaneous selection of topics, it contained visual explanations ranging from the birth of a dragonfly to how fire can lift a balloon.

![](images/fad5e5c6ae711703c2cdd8ba7d4be282ebb69488aec7cb2385cfa92ecc007c40.jpg)

In both books, rules for graphic presentation were not yet fully formed and the illustrations lacked the consistency evident in later publications. In If you could see inside, for example, the sun was yellow on one occasion, and red on another. The layout of text and pictures varied considerably throughout the books. Typographic inconsistency was particularly noticeable: type-size, line-spacing, justification, and style of headings varied from page to page. In one case lines of text extended across the entire width of the double-page spread (Tower Bridge makes way for a tall ship'), on another the text was arranged in small clumps around the illustrations (‘The wonderful; birth of the dragon fly'; see p. 425 below).

The 'Visual history of mankind' series, comprising Living in early times, Living in villages and towns and Living in the world, was published by Adprint under the Max Parrish imprint. Intended for classroom use, each of the books comprised a sequence of 2o double-page spreads, each of which contained an Isotype chart and a list of questions, the answers to which could be worked out by examining the chart (see pp. 428-3o below). The charts made for ‘Visual history' provide excellent examples of transformation and different approaches to the design of narrative structures, the depiction of space, and the representation of distance, quantity, and time. Many of the charts contained a vast amount of information and affirmed the Isotype principle that readers should learn something from a first look and then glean more information from looking more closely. In this series colour was used carefully and consistently and in many charts the questions helped readers work out what the colours meant. The 'Visual history' was described by the Times Educational Supplement (11 December 1948):

The first of a series designed to show how our ancestors began to evolve methods of living which culminated in modern civilization. Thus such stories as that of the ship, the wheel, the machine are depicted, but in the new visual method known as Isotype. This may be described as a

picture language, but it is by no means simply an easy way of escaping the more rigid discipline of the printed text-book: it requires an equal amount of thought and effort on behalf of the pupil to be fully effective. The special value of this method lies in its providing the teacher with an inherently more appealing background of interest on which to base lessons.

Like the 'Visual history' series, ‘Visual science' (1950-2) was intended primarily for use in schools. It aimed to present the instruction of science in a way that was creative and stimulating for pupils and teacher alike. The six books each combined Isotype pictures with short pieces of text and sets of questions; readers could only answer these by making a careful study of the accompanying visual material. Each book also contained a bibliography of other relevant works. Look and Listen (December 1953) praised this innovative approach and suggested that the series ‘should both revolutionise science teaching and do much to help alleviate the acute shortage of science teachers and equipment'.

The teacher's notes that prefaced each of the books in the series explained the Isotype approach, the reasoning behind the books and how the Isotype charts were intended to be used. The notes in Second book, for example, began with a very clear description:

As you turn the pages of this book, you will notice that it uses more pictures and fewer words than most schoolbooks you have seen. These pictures, called Isotype charts, are not meant to show you exactly how things look but to give you information about them, like a map or an engineer's blue-print. Because they have an unusual job to do they are made in an unusual way. Everything which would not help you understand the meaning, or which would confuse you, is left out. Colours are used only to help make the meaning clearer, never simply as decoration. This means that every line and every colour in these pictures has something to tell you.

The notes explained some of the graphic conventions: green dots were used for air throughout the book; scales and eyes were left out in the depiction of a fish because they would not add to the meaning of a chart about a fish's weight. The notes included a selection of the pictograms used in the book with verbal definitions. Science is not simply a subject' which you learn in the classroom, in the laboratory or by reading books. It is a way of observing, or understanding, of doing things. The Parrish Junior colour books' (1961–3) were described by the publisher as a response to parents' and teachers' demands for a series for younger children (aged 5-8) to complement the 'Wonder world of nature' and ‘Wonders of the modern world'. In the Junior colour books' the child and his or her experience was the starting point. The books were organized in short sections between four and ten pages long. The explanations relied on established Isotype techniques such as cross-section and minimal details, though many of the illustrations had a representational, pictorial quality. The Times Literary Supplement (9 July 1964) found the books to have softened over the years: the illustrations have a more human quality, are less coldly diagrammatic'.

## New approaches: integrating contemporary culture in They lived like this'

The series ‘They lived like this', published between 1964 and 1971, was developed from the Institute's work for the filmstrip publisher Common Ground.2⁵ It was a departure in style from earlier series, as Marie Neurath explained:

Drawings and photographs lend themselves equally well to being used on film and we were able to mix chronological tables, diagrams and maps with photographs of landscapes, people and works of art. The illustrations in They lived like this' involved research in the British Museum, the Victoria & Albert Museum, and the London Library. The drawings in the books – of contemporary artefacts, buildings, friezes and writing systems – were often based on tracings or copies taken from photographs and drawings in academic texts. Each of the books was co-written by one of three artists: Evelyn Worboys, John Ellis, and Muriel Turner, and it is clear from the preparatory sketches for books that the pictures and text were considered alongside one another (see p. 437 below).

In 1974, Marie Neurath noted that she was occasionally asked what 'They lived like this' had to do with Isotype; she responded thus:

Certainly, there are only a few Isotype diagrams in these books. What I have done here is assemble pictures made by certain peoples in the past which can be read in a similar way to Isotype charts, and which thereby give some information about the people who made them; at the same time we could give an impression of the style in which these people worked, without even mentioning anything which reminded of a history of art. We gave a visual report about the way of life of the peoples, about the magic they applied, beliefs they had, customs, working methods, houses and temples. We arranged the books in a picture/text style, making the people's own pictures part of the story. In a way, we had learned our Isotype language from the ancient Egyptians; to make a book about Egypt with Egyptian pictures was natural and delightful. I could not fail to notice that once our method had been called 'modern hieroglyphics'.²7

Here she reminded her readers that Isotype was more than a stylistic attribution, and that she was assembling pictures that could be 'read' in a similar way to Isotype charts. The working method for these books - in particular the transformation of complicated material into childfriendly text and pictures - was no different to the procedures followed for the earlier books.

The journal Books and Bookmen (December 1968), commenting on They lived like this in ancient Rome, remarked that the work 'demonstrates clearly that it is possible to write a serious history book for young readers which is both informative and absorbing – and above all does not talk down to them'.

## Using colour to add to meaning

A distinctive feature of Isotype children's books was the use of colour, something mentioned by the publishers in their marketing and commented on favourably in reviews. The Isotype approach was to encourage children to think about how the colours were used as they read the charts. In the introduction to Living in the world, part of the Visual history' series, the young readers were told:

We hope that you will like the bright colours we use, just as we like them, but their main purpose is to help make the meaning of our charts clearer and not just to make them more attractive.

The first editions of books in the series ‘Visual history of mankind' featured a lavish use of colour, which the Neuraths considered an important visual resource, despite its financial implications. Eight solid colours (i.e. separate printings) were used: green, black, orange, brown, yellow, blue, red, and grey. The use of tints (dots of varying size, straight and wavy lines) added depth to this colour range, and helped to create a very colourful impression, particularly on spreads where all eight colours were used. In the first editions of the ‘Visual history', colour usage followed many of the principles that were set out in International picture language.28 Thus, green was associated with wood, grass, field, young, early; red with industry, machine, metal, warm, present, and so on. Printing in eight colours, however, conflicted with the publisher's aim to price the books so that schools could afford them, and fewer colours were used in later editions. ## Publishing overseas and editions in other languages

The Neuraths were committed to ensuring that their work had international reach and took pains to ensure that books were distributed and promoted internationally. The 'Visual history' series provides a good example of this and there is evidence to suggest that it was wellreceived in Europe and further afield in the uSA and India.² The series was shown in 195o in a German exhibition: 'Deutsche und englische Schülbucher der letzten 3o Jahre' where it elicited comments such as:

Concisely formulated questions, asking for an answer. Exceptionally good visual material: simple, symbolised, impressive, reduced to essentials, well arranged and therefore clear. Technical processes are better explained by visual material than by a lot of text.³º

We like the clear and impressive symbolism very much indeed and it seems most useful for teaching.³1

Many of the children's books were co-published through the us division of Adprint, Chanticleer Press. ## Feedback and review

Getting feedback as the books were being made, by asking experts in particular fields to comment on working drawings, was a key part in the design process for the children's books. Marie Neurath also requested feedback and comment on the books after they had been published and seems to have sent out many copies for review. Isotype Institute correspondence contains comments from teachers, librarians, and publishers.

There was much positive feedback, but what stands out is the care and attention that her correspondents took in providing this, and particularly noteworthy is that many of the comments took account of the views of children. In one letter, for example, a mother reported her eight-year old son's opinion of the first two 'Visual science' books: ‘They are wizard books – I can read them by myself – I don't need help from anyone – and the little pictures and drawings are easy to follow. His mother went on: He spends a lot of time with my own set of Children's Encyclopaediae – but so often the diagrams are rather complicated and he has to appeal to the grown-ups, who naturally can't always stop what they are doing to explain the difficulties of diagrams etc.' 38

By the early 195os Marie Neurath and her colleagues were receiving feedback as a result of the books being used in classrooms. A letter from a school inspector, Hedwig Brookes, began by identifying 'two dangers in books of this kind' (referring to the 'Visual history of mankind').³ One was that while the wording of the questions could be simple throughout, children of average and below average ability may find it difficult to work out the answers from the charts; ⁴º and the second was that some of the charts were too complicated.⁴¹ She continued, however, that these were minor points of criticism and went on to list seven points in their favour, the most important in her view was 'inculcating a sense of proportion about time'. She concluded: 'Above all, the terrible bookish approach to history is jettisoned'.

## Marie Neurath's legacy

The working material for Isotype children's books, together with the published editions, demonstrate Marie Neurath's ingenuity and skill as a transformer. As part of her process of designing she incorporated feedback from experts in particular subjects so that she could be sure

that her explanations were scientifically and factually accurate, and from the users of the materials she designed – the teachers and children who read the books. Such attention to detail, when combined with Isotype principles of clarity and consistency, resulted in some remarkable Isotype charts, such as those in the 'Visual history' and 'Visual science' series. More generally, though the books themselves now appear somewhat dated, they highlight attributes that are surely still relevant to designing effectively for children: consistency in the use of pictograms and other graphic images, and in the use of colour to enhance the meaning of an explanation; the use of child-friendly language and encouragement of children to engage with the illustrations; and the balance of just enough detail in the pictures to meet with a child's expectations. Moreover, the high quality of the books is evident from the standard of drawing and the attention to detail in their production. Inevitably there was compromise to be reached in terms of production standards: a commercially acceptable product was not compatible with some of the ambitious ideas that the Neuraths had, and this had some consequences on the effectiveness of some charts. But despite this, even today, the books display a fresh, vibrant quality.

The children's books demonstrate the value of engaging different kinds of people with different ideas and skills in the process of designing. This approach was ahead of its time, and is in many ways a more important legacy than the books themselves.

## Working method

The first stage in the design of all the children's books was the gathering together of background material. Many of the concepts in the books were technically and scientifically complicated, and understanding and accuracy were prereguisites for both the verbal and the visual explanations. Marie Neurath worked closely with experts in whatever area the topic fell. Such background information provided context for the transformers and designers, as well as technical detail. Marie Neurath and her team worked through ideas in the form of notes and sketches. Many of these were made in duplicate books that were landscape in format and approximated the actual size of the double-page spread of the finished book. The use of such duplicate books provided a monochrome record of the 'top copy', which was produced with coloured pencils and was used by the graphic artist to make the artwork for printing. Getting the words right was also an important part of the transformer's role, and Isotype working material shows evidence of careful drafting and iteration. Once the text was finalized it was sent for typesetting, based on a specification arising from the sketched pages, and returned as galley proofs for use in the final page artwork.

In her article, 'Report on the last years of Isotype work' (Synthese, vol.8, nos.1/2, 1950–1, pp. 22–7), Marie Neurath explained the rationale behind some of the charts in the 'Visual science' books. These explanations show the thinking behind the visual solutions, and how the likely understanding of children was taken into account. The captions below and on p. 432 are extracts from the article.

![](images/f15d64965117a66039bdca645beb6267e04e44436d4e5c2d57793904720beb8b.jpg)

'The science charts also start from the familiar. The law of gravity is only a mystery on a theoretical level. The children have become used to things falling, and we can use this experience to make some general statements about falling things, and thus induce them to make more detailed observations without mentioning the word gravity. They know

see-saws well, and if we show boys on see-saws in various arrangements it puts their former and perhaps future experience into order. And if we show certain kinds of tools at the side of it, something which is similar in them, and some rules about levers may become obvious without being fully explained in the orthodox way.' (pp. 24-5)

![](images/1ebd90de1d0ec18d85a91c3162539344af1002318932f53ee4024d683222640d.jpg)  
Marie Neurath and J.A. Lauwerys, First book (1950).

'Floating in water, or feeling oneself lighter in water, are part of children's experience. We can go on to ask: which things sink, which are suspended, which float on top? And we can go on to make a visual statement which makes use of a simplified balance to tell "lighter" and "heavier than water" and "of the same weight”, at the side of a picture which shows

"heavier” at the bottom of a vessel filled with water (which, in Isotype, is shown as a section of a vessel with a wavy line texture inside), and the "lighter" sticking out of the surface of the water; the one of "equal weight" being somewhere in the middle. Such statements take less space visually than verbally, and certainly require less effort to be understood.' (p. 5)

![](images/cd8b489a6226913761b5d25221098b7e1f1258fe65ee6856fa67795713b49de4.jpg)

'We have made a chart: "air is needed for burning", "less air slows burning down", "more air makes burning stronger and quicker". We have now made another chart which modifies this, and states, that it is not true for all sorts of air, and that there are different sorts. We had to introduce a symbolism which distinguishes between the different materials, for example we represent oxygen by a red dot texture, carbon by black shapes, carbon dioxide by red and black dots; and enlargement of the texture can tell how the arrangement of atoms is changed in the chemical process of burning.' (p. 27)

![](images/a4e4de8138091a5106857e95b065be45e996c0f3accb71684f89d1154cfcbc3e.jpg)

'We have to show anything about which we want to make visual statements. There is for instance the story of water: ice – water – steam. We have designed symbols for these. We can even make the statement: steam is invisible. We made two pictures side by side, one symbolic, the other naturalistic: – we show in our symbolism stipples for steam, droplets for condensing part – which in fact looks like this: – a kettle with a cloud some distance from the spout.' (p. 25)

## Future Books & Future magazine 1946–52

The four 'Future Books' and five subsequent volumes (29 numbers) of Future magazine addressed many facets of post-war economic and social development principally in Britain but also elsewhere, especially in Britain's overseas territories. The books were published in 1946; numbers of the magazine were published between 1947 and early 1952. They were produced by Adprint, under the direction of Wolfgang Foges and his production manager Walter Neurath (subsequently founder of Thames & Hudson). The integration of text, graphic, and photographic content in both the books and the magazine, much of it in colour and including many Isotype contributions, was sophisticated and engaging.

The Isotype Institute became involved in Adprint's 'periodical project' (as Otto Neurath called it) in early 1944. From the start, Fortune magazine, the lavish, large-format business monthly published in the usA, was taken as a point of comparison and a model. Neurath envisioned the proposed periodical as a 'symposium', to which Isotype would make a substantial contribution through charts and other graphics that were partly self-standing, offering a counterpoint to the articles they accompanied rather than simply illustrating them. But Neurath also understood that the new periodical should not contain too much Isotype work in general, or pictorial statistics in particular. Whatever was included should be 'informative, educational and explanatory' and visually impressive.

Although Otto Neurath directed work on lsotype contributions to Future Books, his death in December 1945 meant he did not see them reach publication. But these, and subsequent contributions to Future magazine made under the direction of Marie Neurath, would by the end of the magazine's run amount to perhaps the most varied body

of Isotype work within a single project. Graphically, it encompassed different kinds of maps (thematic, relief, cross-sectional), illustrated diagrams and views, organizational charts, and pictorial statistics. In places, principally in the magazine, the work employed features untypical of isotype, suggesting a loosening (or compromising) of its rules in search of novelty and impact. The work was also technically varied in response to the several methods of print production employed for both Future Books and Future magazine, which alternated between (and usually combined) photogravure, lithography, and letterpress, and monochrome, spot colours, and 4-colour process, all a consequence of the complex printing and logistical arrangements adopted by Adprint.

In total, the Isotype Institute contributed to each of the four Future Books, and to roughly two-thirds of the magazine numbers. Under the art editorship of Bauhaus-trained George Adams, which continued through the first half of the magazine's run, Isotype charts featured consistently and often prominently. But they increasingly shared space with what might be called 'information illustrations', many designed by Abram Games in a style similar to those commissioned by Fortune from Ladislav Sutnar, Herbert Bayer, and other designers in the usA. The use of such illustrations in Future was probably due in part to the encouragement of F. H. K. Henrion, who served as an editorial consultant under Adams, before replacing him as art editor. During Henrion's tenure, Isotype contributions decline in number and then largely disappear.

The Future Books and Future magazine were identical in format, with an average page size of 295 × 215 mm. The books were bound with a flexible hard cover; the magazine was soft cover. (εκ)

## British Council Study Boxes 1950–1

British Council Study Boxes presented information on a range of subjects relating to governance, health, education, and social and economic life and welfare in Britain. According to a British Council prospectus (1951–2), the boxes were produced for students and professional workers overseas who wished to 'acquaint themselves with British practice in their specialist fields'. They were classified as primarily for 'more advanced' or 'less well-developed' countries, depending on the subject addressed.

The subject of each Study Box was surveyed through a text written by an expert, complemented by books and pamphlets, specimen documents and official reports, wall charts, filmstrips and photographs, and a gramophone recording of a discussion/interview. The contents were assembled in a 'brown fiberite container, carried by a webbing strap, of a size that will allow it to be held comfortably under the arm'. The boxes were designed for group study, which was to take place during five or six meetings convened by an 'inexpert leader'. The leader was supported by a guidebook containing 'notes on discussion group method, group organisation, the use of teaching aids, a suggested syllabus, and a list of topics for discussion'.1

Two of the Study Boxes intended for advanced countries dealt with Britain's new National Health Service (in operation from July 1948), and industrial relations. The British Council commissioned the Isotype Institute to design a series of charts for both. The four charts for the box, ‘The National Health Service' (1g5o), employed a combination of diagrams and pictorial statistics to present the organization of the NHs, its estimated cost and personnel for 1949–5o, how a family accessed treatment, and how it was paid for. The five charts for the box, 'Industrial relations' (1951), employed similar means to show the function of the Ministry of Labour and National Service, trade union structure and growth in Britain, and the nature and resolution of industrial disputes.

In 1953–4, the British Council reported that 57 copies of 'The National Health Service' Study Box had been sent to 34 countries, while 74 copies of 'Industrial relations' had been sent to 37 countries.² It also noted that the NHs box was being recalled for renewal and revision'. The NHs charts were printed lithographically on card at crown size (15 x 2o in.); because it is unlikely that they were folded, this size may have established the size of the box overall. The charts on industrial relations were quad crown (3o x 4o in.); unusually for Isotype work, they were screen printed on newsprint, though the latter allowed them to be conveniently folded twice (French fold) to crown. 11 Isotype in Africa Gold Coast, Sierra Leone, and the Western Region of Nigeria 1952-8

Eric Kindel

Early in the development of the Vienna Method, its international aspects became plainly evident, at first in its content, and then in its spread to parts of Europe beyond Austria, to the Soviet Union, and to North America. In time, this internationalism was embedded in the Vienna Method's new designation, 'Isotype', invented just prior to the publication of Otto Neurath's International picture language. But if, in this book, which offers the fullest explanation of Isotype, Neurath argued that it was comprehensible internationally, the claim necessarily lacks force since in practice Isotype had not yet been used beyond the modernized West. Indeed, looking back over more than four decades of Isotype work, the question of how well it functioned in the developing, non-Western world is not easy to answer since few opportunities arose to deploy it there. But in the 195os, one opportunity did arise in British colonial West Africa where, under the direction of Marie Neurath, the Isotype Institute completed projects in the Gold Coast (Ghana), Sierra Leone, and the Western Region of Nigeria. Work in these territories, as each advanced towards independence, provides valuable evidence of Isotype in action at the farther reaches of the international sphere it was intended to serve.

If Otto Neurath's conception of Africa took on general and summary forms in the context of Vienna Method work, there is evidence that later his thinking about Isotype's relationship to less developed parts of the world became more specific, at least operationally. In his text, 'Visual education', written between 1943 and 1945, Neurath considered how visual aids could assist those he identified as semi-literates, of whom there might be a large proportion in colonial territories. ## First contact

As these events show, Otto Neurath clearly wished to engage with Africa and Africans to discover what insights such encounters might bring to Isotype. If, for Neurath, this wish went unrealized, some years later an opportunity was presented to Marie Neurath and the Isotype Institute to do work in Africa. The work would be associated with Britain's three West African colonies, the Gold Coast, Sierra Leone, and Nigeria, where in the immediate post-war period a process of increased self-determination and de facto decolonization was gathering pace. The first project taken on by the partnership was a prospective colour magazine entitled Forward.¹9 According to Foges, the Colonial Office 'wanted parts of West Africa, which were soon to become independent, to be linked by magazines and books'; Forward appears to be a contribution towards this goal. Work on the magazine began in 1952, apparently in association with the Colonial Office and the governments of the Gold Coast and (later) the Western Region of Nigeria.2º A trial issue, priced 9 pence, was circulated later that year under the editorship of Frame Smith, to gauge demand; it incorporated illustrated articles and comic strips dealing with culture, adventure, sport, and practical topics such as bicycle repair, first aid, and personal health. Isotype contributions were limited to illustrated diagrams on power and heat, and on digestion. An issue 'number 1', priced 1 shilling, was published a year or so later, in late 1953 or early 1954, for circulation in the Gold Coast; it was produced in English and three vernacular languages.²¹ Articles and Isotype diagrams from the trial issue were re-used and several new articles and a comic strip were introduced. Contributors were predominantly from the Gold Coast and Nigeria, while the cover featured the Gold Coast missionary and educationalist James Aggrey. Inside, a message from the editor (Frame Smith) set out aims and policy: Forward would be educational, informative, and entertaining, non-political and free of commercial influence, and as far as possible the product of Africans. Printing, however, would be done in the Uk to take advantage of high-quality colour work not then available in West Africa.
