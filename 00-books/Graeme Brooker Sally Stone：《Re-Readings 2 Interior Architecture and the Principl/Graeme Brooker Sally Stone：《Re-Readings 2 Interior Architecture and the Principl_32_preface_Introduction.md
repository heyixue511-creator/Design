## Introduction

Throughout the 20th century, the stair as an element of architecture has maintained the importance which has been attributed to it since the Renaissance. Such a status still derives from a combination of utility, or functional considerations, with an inherent aesthetic potential in terms of both the articulation of the structural form, and its further enhancement. – Michael Spens, Staircases. <sup>44</sup>

Stairs, corridors, bridges, lifts, escalators, walkways, ramps; there are numerous ways in which movement will take place in the built environment and in the interior. Whether public or private space, all of these elements facilitating movement, circulation, route and journey, will have been carefully considered and designed to facilitate their particular function in an effi cient, safe and economical manner.

Like all of the six tactics explained in this chapter, movement will often be considered in conjunction with other tactical ideas. Objects placed in specifi c spaces will be organised in relation to how people may circulate around them. Planes such as fl oors, walls and ceilings will be designed to accommodate fl ow, as will surfaces, openings and the articulation of light. Therefore it is important to note that movement and the elements that facilitate it are one part of the tactical jigsaw that the designer of inside space is required to assemble.

The design of a bespoke stair is one of the most invigorating challenges for a designer of the interior. Whilst appearing simplistic, its key elements, combined with the relevant code and regulations prescribing the correct measurements for tread, riser and balustrade, provide a multitude of ways of realisation of what can be a mundane journey between fl oors or an epic fl ight of fancy between spaces. Whichever way stairs are utilised, their design and their placement in the space will provide the designer with not just the movement they require but also an opportunity to articulate space through the rhythmic journey that an ascent or decent of a staircase can provide. This can be seen in both the BUDA art Centre by 51N4E and in the Caixa Forum in Madrid by Herzog & de Meuron, where the quite differently constructed stairs were each utilised in order not only to celebrate the spaces they were in but to offer very particular and distinct views through the interior environments in which they were moving.

The design of lifts and escalators are the overlooked elements of interior movement. Often, off-the-peg or readymade solutions are the workhorses of mostly large public interiors such as airports, shopping centres and train stations. These are environments where large numbers of people need to be processed effi ciently and safely. Occasionally they will be deployed with some thought in their design and positioning.

The unforgettable escalators of the Lloyds building in London, by the Richard Rogers Partnership, provided a metaphorical focus for the atrium of the banking building. The exposed cogs and wheels of the workings and mechanics of the robotic stairs signalled to the traders using them how the fi nancial markets they were manipulating constantly fl uctuated like the stairs moving up and down between fl oors.

As soon as [architecture] is built it becomes the scene and sometimes the scenario of comings and goings, of gestures even, even of a succession of sensations.

– Pierre Von Meiss. Elements of Architecture. <sup>45</sup>

Walkways, bridges, ramps and corridors facilitate circulation and movement between spaces in a way to articulate sequence and in some cases heighten sensation. In the Kunsthal in Rotterdam by Rem Koolhaas, the floor of the side gallery was articulated in a steel mesh, creating a journey across the space that was not for the faint-hearted (or for the wearer of a kilt or a skirt). The large open squares of the mesh dematerialised the floor, collapsing the distinctions between solidity and floating, as viewers in the gallery below would have a view up through the extremely tall space of the quadruple height gallery.

Whichever movement tactic is used, it does not need to be banal or mundane. Movement through a space, when articulated with thought and creativity, can make space exhilarating and dramatic.
