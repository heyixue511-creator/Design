The Kelmscott Press books, intended to symbolize a protest against the ethos of Victorian industrial capitalism, became themselves, in all their opulent splendour, an example of conspicuous consumption. The irony of this improbable transmutation was not lost on Morris. If Morris found it difficult to evaluate the success of his Press, it is not surprising that others, from 1891 onwards, have offered remarkably contradictory appraisals of the Kelmscott books. Probably the majority of bibliophiles have always been willing to accept the proposition trenchantly expressed by Emery Walker in 193o, that Morris showed how a printed book might be on its own plane a work of art'; but the Daily Chronicle was undoubtedly also speaking for a vocal minority when it declared in 1896, ‘Truly it is awful to contemplate the terrible effect that the Morris doctrine of decorative illustration has had upon the world." What is puzzling is that evidence to support either of these views lies abundantly at hand: the immediate consequence of the Kelmscott Press was to produce a horde of imitators who lacked Morris's skill and sensitivity as a book-designer, yet the ultimate impact of the Press has been so powerful and pervasive that it is not easy even now, a century later, to assess. The issue is further clouded by Morris's insistence that his typographical adventure was linked with a root-and-branch rejection of modern life. Ashbee, who had established the Guild of Handicraft in London's East End in 1888.4 Ashbee saw himself as merely putting Morris's principles into practice, though with greater emphasis on the workshop rather than on the individual craftsman, but he received little encouragement from Morris, who suspected rightly that Ashbee wished to reform, not overthrow, the capitalist system. Laurence Hodson, a wealthy young businessman with an interest in Pre-Raphaelitism and the Arts and Crafts movement, visited the Kelmscott Press in December 1897, and early in the following year he provided funds that made it possible for Ashbee to buy the Press's equipment. Ashbee was overjoyed, since the transaction offered him a symbolic claim to discipleship that had been denied while Morris was still alive: the great event of the week had been the purchase of the Morris Press by the Guild ofHandicraft,' he wrote at the end of March 1898. ‘Great jubilation! . . . So next Monday there will be vans of presses and gages, and chairs and tables and paper and machinery — lock, stock and barrel moving East from Hammersmith. We are thinking of a musical procession. We engage Morris's printers and carry on the great tradition.' To Cockerell's annoyance, Ashbee emphasized the Kelmscott connection by adopting the same ink, paper, and vellun as Morris had used at the Kelmscott Press; illustrations were to take the form of wood-engravings, with, in some instances, illuminations supplied by Florence Kate Kingsford (who later became Mrs Cockerell). Since Morris's types did not constitute part of his purchase, Ashbee printed the earliest books of the Essex House Press in Caslon, but he was eager to follow Morris's example by creating his own typefaces. Ashbee's designs were known as the Endeavour type (named after the first book in which it appeared, An Endeavour towards the Teaching of John Ruskin and William Morris, published in 19o1) and the King's Prayer Book type, a larger, modified version of the Endeavour. In the design of these founts I sought to follow upon the lines laid down by Morris,’ Ashbee wrote. Development appeared possible in the direction of combined letters, and my bias was towards a black (in preference to a grey) page and the introduction of a rather gothic and perhaps uncial feeling into the roman character." Ashbee's types are in fact ugly and eccentric, and, if one wished to argue the case that Morris's influence on subsequent type-designs was in the main pernicious, the Endeavour and King's Prayer Book types would probably constitute Exhibit A. The Essex House Press printed eighty-three books over a period of eleven years, and often they rise above the limitations of Ashbee's types and wood-engraved initials (the latter more art nouveau than Morrisian): the illustrations, some of them by Ashbee himself, are undeniably attractive, and even at their weakest the books now have a certain period charm, not unlike that of an awkward Victorian building which has mellowed with age. In 19o2 the Essex House Press, along with the Guild's other craft workshops, moved to Chipping Campden in the Cotswolds, and there, for six years, Ashbee and his men strenuously searched for the simple life far from the Modern Babylon. They sang newly revised texts of old folk-songs (one stanza of Joan's Ale' celebrated a compositor who joins the jovial crew' and declares, The printer's worth his pay | Especially if he come Kelmscott way!’), explored the countryside together, shared their profits, and attempted to revive a somewhat sentimentalized version of the medieval guild. It was a noble experiment, but, as Morris had predicted, the guild ideal was difficult to sustain in a society dominated by a hostile economic system. The Essex House Press found itselfincreasingly in competition with machine-press books patterned after the Kelmscott Press style, and the remoteness of Chipping Campden interfered seriously with efforts to market the Press's products.
