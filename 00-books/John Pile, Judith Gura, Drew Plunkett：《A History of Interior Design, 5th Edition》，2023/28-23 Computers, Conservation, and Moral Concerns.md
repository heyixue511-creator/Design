An interior that relates to its purpose and its location, handcrafted with sustainable reed as its primary material, woven to reflect movements of the human body. At the beginning of the twentieth century, architects and designers searched for a new direction. At the end of the century there was no need to search: radical stylistic change had happened in the 1980s but technical advances in the 1990s produced changes that were initially viewed suspiciously but which changed the practice of interior design fundamentally. The computer offered designers better ways of developing and communicating ideas and, as young, computerliterate designers joined the profession it became its essential tool. As digital mobile phones became commonplace there were worries about how they would affect social interactions and concern, in particular, for the future of the retail and hospitality sectors. Anxieties about climate change challenged raw materials and working methods. The profession’s response took time to gather momentum but scientific knowledge and technical advances presented designers with new materials, new construction methods, and new aesthetic challenges and, from that, original work emerged. ## DIGITALITY In the 1990s, as computers became more sophisticated and specialist software evolved, it became clear that to persevere with drawing by hand, which had been seen to be the essence of creativity, was counterproductive. An ability to sketch had been synonymous with an ability to design but drawing by computer put the focus on creative thinking. The operating structure of design studios changed as, by the end of the decade, the first graduates who had grown up with computers joined the profession with an intuitive understanding of the technology. They designed and drew quickly and coherently on computers, and eliminated the need for the office juniors, who had made scaled versions of the roughs passed to them by senior designers. A computer with the right software draws curves as easily as straight lines and can convert two-dimensional ideas immediately into three dimensions. A CAD machine (computer-aided design) may be linked directly to CAM (computer-aided manufacture) and CNC (computer numerical control) machines that will translate drawings into three-dimensional realities. In 2011, Grupa XM3, the designers of Zmiana Tematu, a coffee shop and restaurant in Łódź, Poland, used CNC technology to shape the plywood ribs that transformed an existing foursquare room. Time spent generating digital data for variations in rib form was inconsequential when compared to the time needed to mark out and cut pieces by hand. Where ribs were too big to be cut from one sheet of plywood, CNC’s precision produced interlocking pieces that gave rigid connections—and a decorative motif (23.2). ![](images/0b841fdeca8a44c18c655964a273637d59dd8f97acbc8e8f845e54382fdeb685.jpg) 23.2 Grupa XM3, Zmiana Tematu, Łódź, Poland, 2011. XM3 transformed this space for a coffee shop and restaurant. Computer-cut plywood ribs transform a conventiona foursquare room and provide slots for shelves and light fittings. Such ribs could also provide a framework for a smooth plaster finish in other contexts. Digital cutting is ideally suited for making intricate repeat patterns in flat sheet materials. Such work can be done by hand but the process is slow and therefore expensive—each pattern must be marked on each sheet, cut out and rough edges cleaned. It might appear that a pattern of random-sized holes cut in a random pattern would be easier to achieve but even a random pattern has to be planned and marked out, and the human brain tends to slip into repetitious rhythm while the human eye tends to look for pattern. An appropriately programmed computer will simulate and achieve randomness more effectively and economically. South-African born Stanley Saitowitz (b. 1949) is design principal with Natoma Architects in San Francisco and employed CNC technology in Toast, a restaurant in San Francisco completed in 2009 that serves comfort food. Its 39-feet (12-m) high walls, ceiling, and freestanding central servery are clad in sheets of ¾ inch (19 mm) particle board perforated with what appeared to be randomly sized holes in random locations that are intended to suggest the texture of bread. The boards were finished with a clear water-based sealant and fixed to timber framing with concealed clips. The absence of a repeat pattern helped disguise the line of panel joints. In 2010 Tokyo-based Kidosaki Architects Studio designed the interior of AG Café, a coffee shop and gallery in Nagoya, Aichi, on the island of Honshu, Japan. The designers accepted that their contribution should be restrained to avoid distracting attention from the artifacts for sale but they also recognized that a modest input was necessary to give the coffee shop an identity. They constructed a new particle-board wall in front of existing structural piers to provide uninterrupted display surfaces. They then used the void created behind the new wall to conceal backlighting for a continuous and complex pattern, cut by CNC technology, into wall panels. The illuminated strip became the equivalent of a traditional cornice molding (23.3). ![](images/900e8069f779dc2c23de0626da8b80ef23e5025da95c2f85b022f42a830fe7b9.jpg) ## 23.3 Kidosaki Architects Studio, AG Café, Nagoya, Aichi, Japan, 2010. CNC technology can create delicate tracery to provide the equivalent of a traditional cornice molding. Where labor is cheap and plentiful, the precision and economy of CNC technology is less important. The Beijing branch of the Japanese Sako Architects, founded by Keiichirō Sako (b. 1970) in 2004, used hand labor to create the complex net structure intended to suggest the fluidity and movement of clothing fabric for the interior of the Romanticism 2 fashion store in Hangzhou, China. Sako talked about: “bone,” which was the reinforcing steel rod that provided an armature; “flesh,” which was insulation foam and fiberglass molded to soften the profiles of the steel struts and junctions; and “skin,” a finishing coat of a proxy resin and oil-based paint. A CNC equivalent would have been possible but would have lacked, for better or worse, the slight variations that were inevitable in the hand-making process (23.4). ![](images/30ac976900db3a2108560f41b5d49e3e3ff82293e153a2eb79dacc739a69f89d.jpg) Sako Architects, Romanticism 2 fashion store, Hangzhou, China, 2007. Plentiful and cheap labor can substitute for CNC. Here plaster was applied to a wire-mesh armature and rubbed smooth by many hands. In the 1970s, when Frank Duffy was considering the impact of the computer on office design (see here), he worried about the volume of wiring required to connect machines in and beyond offices. This changed with the release of the first versions of Wi-Fi in 1997 and 1999. A designer could now operate with a laptop and a seat in a coffee shop—it would not be efficient but it was feasible. By the end of the 1990s, the expansion of the Internet had changed how businesses connected with their customers and when the first iPhone was launched in 2007, quickly followed by rival smart phones, that interaction became more sophisticated. Apps became ubiquitous after 2010 and changed how people shopped and socialized. Initially, cell phones and social media raised fears that people would no longer gather in restaurants, bars, and coffee shops. However, by 2004 the FOMO (fear of missing out) phenomenon was identified, and people seemed keener than ever to socialize and to be seen to socialize. Interior designers created places to meet. Well-designed interiors were a means for hospitality businesses, from small coffee shops to expensive restaurants, to compete and the McDonald’s fast-food chain became stylish. The most modest business now accepts that it needs an online presence to attract new customers and remind old ones of its existence. Retail businesses expect that their first—and often their only—contact with customers will be digital. ## SUSTAINABILITY Environmental concerns that had emerged in the 1970s gathered momentum and guidelines relevant to interior design, sometimes enforced by legislation, were introduced. Embargoes were placed on tropical hardwoods, scarce raw materials and animal products—the materials that had characterized Art Deco. Changes in climate patterns were ascribed, along with other human activities, to polluting emissions from the construction industry and the heating, lighting, and maintenance of old and new buildings. Interior designers were seldom in a position to improve the performance of buildings but adapting existing buildings to new uses and eliminating the carbon emissions of the construction processes had obvious environmental benefits. In addition, the conundrums of inserting new activities into buildings intended for some other function offered designers challenges that led to more exciting solutions. Designers began to specify local materials, to reduce the carbon footprints of transportation, and to check on the origins and production methods of materials. Manufacturers began to understand the economic importance of providing satisfactory answers to such questions. Businesses have learnt the importance of developing environmentally responsible products and there are rewards for designers as new plant-based and recycled materials provide creative stimuli for those who commit to using them. Commitment to sustainability greatly influenced the way many designers began to think about how they designed. Nina Woodcroft (b. 1986) had worked for fifteen years in the mainstream interior-design industry, specializing in the creation of luxury hotels and private residences for wealthy clients, but became concerned about the impact of prevailing priorities and methods of practice on the environment. In 2014 she established Nina+Co, a London-based studio, to work for the growing number of clients interested in evolving sustainable solutions, prompted by both environmental concerns and business strategies. To achieve this she collaborates with designers and craftspeople across the spectrum of creative activity who share her aspirations and have the skills she needs to achieve her concept. In 2022 she created a store in London for Monc, an Italian retailer of eyeglasses made from bio-acetate, which is produced without fossil fuels and packaged using recycled leather. She used compostable cornstarch foam shelves and mycelium display plinths under an undulating ceiling of corrugated panels of cornstarch foam (23.5). ![](images/24d4f6b0f36e986d93b9f7975e09bd9724d49e8c408be71c168fab4d056a7619.jpg) 23.5 Nina+Co, Monc store, London, 2022. Monc sells eyeglasses produced without fossil fuels. Compostable cornstarch-foam shelves and mycelium display plinths sit beneath an undulating ceiling of corrugated panels of cornstarch foam. Long mirrors are secured by lumps of concrete salvaged from nearby roadworks. Everything is reusable or biodegradable. Exploring the potential of new environmentally friendly materials is crucial but the ingenious reuse of existing elements is equally valuable. Offhand Practice, founded in Shanghai in 2018, set out to find realistic solutions that respond to both a site and the people who use it. Their approach is exemplified by a secondhand bookstore in Hangzhou, China (2022), in which they redeployed secondhand units usually used in fruit and vegetable markets to display secondhand books. The systematic, repetitious layout is reinforced by the complementary grid of tube lights overhead (23.6). Mainstream Chinese interiors, catering for an ever-more affluent population, tend to relish complex forms and lavish materials; the stark simplicity of the bookstore project suggests an abrupt departure from that and the emergence of a more challenging approach for a more sophisticated clientele. ![](images/2ba879a37a7f1f031f21b1641150337f7d8f0301228e2e54098b8a01c5627eb9.jpg) 23.6 Offhand Practice, second-hand bookstore, Hangzhou, China, 2022. An emphasis on sustainability can encourage creativity. Less can be more: secondhand books are displayed on secondhand display stands that are usually used in fruit and vegetable markets. The repetitious units and tube lights create a spectacle that is intensified by its simplicity. ## Retail However exotic an interior may appear to be, creativity is necessarily underpinned by practicality and retail design has accumulated a body of pragmatic specialist knowledge that makes it as near as art gets to science. In 1988 when Britain’s leading retail designers, Fitch & Co, joined forces with Richardson Smith in the United States, it inherited access to the American company’s design laboratory. Consequently, the firm decided to set up a London branch with a team that included psychologists, sociologists, and linguists in order to shape strategic thinking. In 2007 Dutch design magazine Frame, commenting on the results of its Great Indoors Awards, concluded that innovation was alive and well in the commercial area. In a consumers’ world, money is the driving force and retail design continues to offer indications of how the broad practice of interior design may develop. Retail designers have led the way in responding to the importance of digital media as integral components in interiors. Digital technology has also changed clients’ priorities, but virtual shopping requires real interiors. Stores are transmuting from places for selling into showrooms—places where products seen online can be examined and where companies can promote their brand. Shopping has become tribal and the tribes are defined by brand loyalties, which are as likely to be to a supermarket chain as a small independent store. Brands and logos are not new. The Hoover Company so dominated its field that its name became a synonym for vacuum cleaners. Interior design defines and redefines the tangible expression of a brand but each iteration will be brief. In competitive worlds there is a need to change, to demonstrate that a business is progressing and, by implication, improving. Changes to interiors should attract newcomers to the tribe without alienating old members. ASA Studio Albanese, headed by Flavio Albanese (b. 1951) and based in Vicenza, Italy, has, since 2017, designed a dozen stores for Thom Browne, the New York fashion label. Each store has remained true to the basic formula, derived from midtwentieth-century office design, with fluorescent lighting tubes, slat blinds, and furniture suggestive of the period. Walls and floors draw more on entrance lobbies than work spaces. Each iteration makes small advances that remain true to the formula and the store in Costa Mesa, California (2020) is distinguished by the extraordinary grain of the gray Bardiglio marble wall panels that contrast with the Calacatta marble floor (23.7). ![](images/2d5f778de20c1387ed69d6e57909195472861a61825ad237512829daf82845f1.jpg) ASA Studio Albanese, Thom Browne fashion store, Costa Mesa, California, 2020. ASA Studio Albanese has worked on a succession of Thom Browne fashion stores, all of which make references to midtwentieth-century office interiors. The spectacular graining of the wall marble here demonstrates that the designs are more than a simple replication. Fashion retailing is particularly committed to branding. Haute couture wants flagship stores —interiors that have drama with exquisite materials and fastidious detailing that confirm quality of product, exclusivity, and success—making them shrines for acolytes and potential acolytes. Fuksas studio created Armani’s flagship store on Fifth Avenue in New York. It is enormous, spread over four floors with 30,000 square feet (2,800 square m) devoted to retail and 5,200 square feet (480 square m) to a bar and restaurant. The significant element is the stair that rises through the core of the building providing spectacle for those climbing through its writhing forms and those viewing it across sales floors. It challenges customers to buy Armani and match its magnificence (23.8). ![](images/38eabd2d4e660406086b704365c374b90cf118cc3f499e08fe4b90f47d07a3ab.jpg) In a flagship fashion store, like this in midtown Manhattan, the interior is more important than the clothes. It is a manifestation of the brand’s success—the clothes are no different from those displayed in less prestigious locations. The perception of the flagship store was subverted in 2004 when Rei Kawakubo (b. 1942), the Japanese founder of the Comme des Garçons label, opened Dover Street Market in Mayfair— London’s most exclusive fashion shopping area. If Paris was associated with haute couture, since the 1960s London had been the acknowledged center of street fashion in which trends emerged from usually young fashionistas rather than by decree from established fashion houses. The market occupied five floors of a nineteenth-century townhouse with small ad hoc constructions of found materials and bric-a-brac, on concrete floors among bare brick walls, each conceived by their designer–retailer. Kawakubo said she wanted to create a market that was a meeting place as much as a place to make purchases, “where various creators from various fields gather together and encounter each other in an ongoing atmosphere of beautiful chaos; the mixing up and coming together of different kindred souls who all shared a strong personal vision.” She implied that radical fashion was not to be found in flagship stores but in feral boutiques. Dover Street Market became a destination for fashion tourists and, inevitably, became a brand with branches in Beijing, Los Angeles, New York, Paris, Singapore, and Tokyo (23.9). More modest guerrilla and pop-up stores followed. ![](images/b21b458aec51805b2975aad7ab37f87826dd9d564f47685594a5435781da12c5.jpg) 23.9 Rei Kawakubo, Dover Street Market, London, 2007. The little structure could be assembled, probably more easily, from plywood but the imperfections of found materials and expedient construction epitomize the anarchic spirit of the store. Similar structures are now found in the outposts of the brand across the world. Store interiors target particular markets, defined by age, economic status, occupation, and leisure pursuits. One-stop, not very glamorous shopping is limited to the shopping mall and the traditional department store is being sidelined by specialty stores in which design responds to the perceived taste of the intended customer: restrained and somewhat traditional for older customers, extroverted and wantonly unfamiliar for younger ones whose digital experiences are being brought into play. Some interior designers working in the retail sector wonder if their role as leaders of the design team may be passing to digital companies but, regardless of who takes the titular lead, the quality of the physical environment will remain crucial as a place of pilgrimage for brand devotees, a background for selfies to be posted on social media, and to provide the publicity that companies want. ## Hospitality In the 1980s Philippe Starck’s interiors changed perceptions of what hotels, restaurants and coffee shops could, and should, be (see here). They became models for the impeccably detailed imitations that dominated hospitality design in the 1990s. None of them quite matched the impact, or the wit, of Starck’s work. In 2003 Dover Street Market’s rejection of flagship fashion stores was anticipated when Studioilse, a London-based practice designed an outpost of the fashionable London-based member’s club, Soho House, in the six floors of a former warehouse in New York’s Meatpacking District. Chandeliers hung incongruously among distressed walls, floors and ceilings were left as found, furniture was a seemingly random collection of pieces—it was superficially discordant but added up to a melodious whole (23.11). Each bedroom had its own eclectic collection of antique, modern, and junk. ![](images/78e5cfcbaa498d4af32da226ee1c6f0eaf4d19d1b5fc4f9626fe7bdeb21aff6e.jpg) Studioilse, Soho House, New York, 2003. An orchestrated mix of rough wooden floor and cast-iron columns, of extravagantly long tables, and an assortment of old-fashioned chairs, all under a ceiling of unpainted pressed-metal tiles, creates romantic nostalgia for members from the creative industry and beyond. Soho House was founded in 1995 as a designated meeting place for London’s creatives, who were comfortable with a shabby chic style but such was the glamor of its orchestrated seediness in New York that those from more mainstream occupations contrived ways to become members. The company, unwilling to turn away potential members, amended its criteria for membership to include those with a “creative soul” and business people and non-visual professions who can be creative in their own fields. From its humble origins Soho House, with outposts across Europe and beyond, has gone on to become a global brand. The founder of Studioilse, British designer Ilse Crawford (b. 1962), was the launch editor of the luxury modern interiors magazine Elle Decoration UK in 1989, leaving in 1998 to launch the home division of American fashion brand Donna Karan. In 2000 she established the Man and Wellbeing department at the Design Academy Eindhoven in the Netherlands, which encourages students to think of design as a means to enrich and improve the quality of life for those who will use their interiors. She is a leading advocate of sustainable design and Studioilse has created affordable, sustainable, quality furniture, lighting, and accessories in natural materials for Ikea. French designer Matali Crasset (b. 1965) studied industrial design and joined Starck’s studio in Paris 1993 to develop what she described as “everyday objects at the service of people.” In 1998 she started her own company “to protect herself from Starck”—to develop her own ideas away from his influence. She advocates pragmatic design, defining pragmatism as “a philosophy that favors experience and action, but also invention and creativity.” She has said that she prefers to reinvent function, and has spoken of her pleasure in collaboration, citing her work with Patrick Elouarghi and Philippe Chapelet, for whom she has designed a number of idiosyncratic hotels aimed at young travelers on modest budgets (23.10). ![](images/dc93ac9f45daa6fe4eb8388b7637d015d6ec2a51c33b90ad72df0b40c29bc927.jpg) Matali Crasset, Hi Matic, Paris, 2015. Crasset has completed a number of hotels for the Dar Hi group, including the lobby of this eco-lodging facility. She designs all elements, which are usually simple wooden structures painted in raw colors and configured and constructed with a fascinating simplicity that attracts young travelers drawn to the unconventional. American interior designer Kelly Wearstler (b. 1967) formed Kelly Wearstler Interior Design in 1995 to concentrate on hotel design and has since designed for residential, commercial, retail, and hospitality spaces. She has gained a reputation for witty reworkings of mid-twentiethcentury modernism, specializing in catholic mixes of materials, colors, and patterns. This eclecticism is evident in her work for the Proper Hotels group and results in interiors suggesting rooms that have evolved casually rather than as the result of a designer’s diktat (23.12). ![](images/820a7855da0554333d53aaff34e991a19389566a22052118ee70a5398fec80ce.jpg) Kelly Wearstler, San Francisco Proper, San Francisco, 2018. One of a chain of hotels that Wearstler has designed for the Proper group. She relentlessly mixes styles and, as in the mirror and paintings seen on the back wall, parodies conventional decorating ploys. The most interesting hospitality interiors are frequently those found in the shells of existing buildings in which existing decrepitude is incorporated as readymade detail and a suggestion of some sort of pedigree. Hotel Fox in Copenhagen was a modest, city-center hotel until, in 2005, public areas and bedrooms were redecorated, garishly and frivolously, by twenty-one different street artists and graphic designers from around the world (23.13). The idea of rooms in radically different styles was not uncommon in the same year, a row of seven eighteenth-century workers cottages on a bank of the Amstel River outside Amsterdam was converted into Lute Suites (23.14). Each 753-square-feet (70-sq-m) house or suite had its own aesthetic created in the wideranging imagination of Dutch interior designer Marcel Wanders (b. 1963). Each suite in the row of vacation homes is unique and none makes any attempt to respond to the brick cottages that contain them. ## Workplace Office interiors define an organization’s culture to its clients—and its employees. Bleak offices of 23.15 regimented workstations under fluorescent lighting tubes are being superseded by environments that reflect the digital workplace, and a more collaborative work style and egalitarian culture. Most offices continue to be housed in tall, modern buildings or suburban business campuses. But the creative industries sometimes prefer the more exciting challenge of converting former warehouses and factories, with windowless walls and high roofs with extensive skylights. Within these introspective worlds, a favorite solution is to construct small buildings within high and wide old building shells to house meeting rooms, and services define amorphous working areas between them. The other popular solution of a long, shared table is an impressive spatial gesture but can make for a prescriptive working environment that offers little definition of personal space. For changes of use or upgrades in existing conventional office floors, suspended paneled ceiling and compartmentalizing of workplaces are being rejected in favor of more idiosyncratic, casual interiors. The interior for the headquarters of advertising firm Headvertising, in the former Stock Exchange building in Bucharest, was designed by the Romanian Corvin Cristian who trained as an architect but become primarily involved in set design for cinema and television and claims that it was his experience in the second that underpinned his winning of the competition to design the Headvertising interior. He removed plaster to expose concrete external walls and an existing suspended ceiling to reveal a concrete structure and service pipes. He counterpointed conventional office furniture with plywood storage units on castors, to symbolize the client’s enthusiasm for change (23.15). ![](images/7e2da85ea19266b67cf2e19d66b5fd2834ba1e697193b77e8f77145c0c404c04.jpg) Corvin Cristian, Headvertising headquarters, Bucharest, Romania, 2009. A bland suspended ceiling has been removed to expose structure and services to suggest a rougher, industrial building. Plywood storage furniture, crowned with over-scaled plywood interpretations of table lamps, counterpoints utilitarian contemporary office furniture. Behavioral studies suggest that creativity thrives in relaxed atmospheres and newly built offices often attempt to recreate the informalities of the redesigned warehouse space with atriums flooded with natural light, spectacular stairs and bridges, and transparent rather than opaque walls when aural privacy is required. Informal interactions between colleagues are encouraged with places on circulation routes for extended casual encounters. Other amenities are less conventional. International business activities and flexible working hours have circumvented the nine-to-five working day and modern businesses, keen to retain productive staff, often incorporate coffee shops and recreational areas. The informality is glamorous but some occupations and some clients still prefer—and may always need—the formality and privacy of compartmentalized offices. M. The house with its own outdoor space remains the most popular model but offers little work for the interior designer as householders glean their own decorating ideas from restaurants and bars. More interesting work for designers is found in urban centers where redundant warehouses, industrial buildings, schools (see here), and churches are being converted into apartments. A language has evolved as designers interact with generous volumes and an idiosyncratic legacy of existing elements. Chicago-based Vladimir Radutny Architects demonstrated how to separate functional areas without compromising the sense of a much larger volume in a loft conversion, Michigan Loft (2018), which shows how to tune new elements to create a gentle decorative language (23.17). ![](images/f3dcaa91614c81c6bd2c00e78ef0700952742bfdd4ac45e6573e9c1c7ae6763b.jpg) ## 23.17 Vladimir Radutny Architects, Michigan Loft, Chicago, 2018. Old industrial structures allow designers to interact with generous volumes and rich existing elements. The black steel element, which divides living and sleeping areas in this apartment, contains storage and a television. The slightly raised wooden floor defines areas for different activities. Changes of material (on the stair and the column) move on from modernism’s rational uniformity. In city centers, generous floor areas are expensive, but ingenuity can create satisfying options. In 2021 the London-based studio Proctor & Shaw demonstrated how to condense practical provisions to leave space for fulfilling living (23.18) with the Shoji Apartment, a microapartment in the fashionable and expensive Belsize Park area of London. ![](images/8f78562dbec958e782e0031201d5910b8d24d956b750bed5b964cd85812f03d7.jpg) Proctor & Shaw, sleeping area, Shoji Apartment, London, 2021. Sliding translucent panels can close off the bed space or open to give views through an existing window to the street beyond. Acoustic and thermal insulation was added to the ceilings and walls, creating slots for recessed lighting and blinds. Developers of expensive apartment blocks use architects to create spectacular exteriors but increasingly call in interior designers to fine-tune internal plans and create lobbies, gyms, swimming pools, and show homes that suggest to prospective buyers how they might inhabit the empty spaces. Developers are appointing designers increasingly early in the process so that optimum interior layouts can influence exterior elevations. ## Healthcare Public-sector hospitals seldom have money to spare for interiors and do not have to attract clients, but private healthcare organizations necessarily compete for customers/patients and, while success depends inevitably on medical reputation, design has become an auxiliary way to attract them. Evidence-based design (EBD) was introduced in the United States by the Center for Health Design, which was established in 1993 by healthcare and design professionals to define and argue for good design as a means of improving patient care. In 2004 a study titled The Role of the Physical Environment in the Hospital of the 21st Century found that successful design had a direct impact on patient health and could speed recovery, while also reducing the workload of hospital staff. In 2021 a center for the treatment of multiple-sclerosis sufferers in Toronto, funded by private donors and designed by the Toronto-based architectural practice Hariri Pontarini Architects, set out to elevate the aesthetic experience of patients above that encountered in general hospitals with a sweep of stairs rising through a top-lit atrium and circular, freestanding consultation rooms (23.20). A privately funded clinic on the top two floors of a general hospital that sets out to eliminate the drab and utilitarian nature of typical hospital buildings. A stair, which is grander than might be expected in a hospital, rises through an atrium, while a wide corridor leads past consultation rooms that are partially glazed but screened from the circulation area by walnut panels. ![](images/754ad279f77e60399ea81de37dc99ecf6599d4e94d77e5004dbb5b7479a4cce6.jpg) 23.20 Hariri Pontarini Architects, Barlo MS Centre, Toronto, 2022. As people live longer and tend to remain healthy for longer, developers have begun to recognize that retirees, particularly in Western Europe and North America, are more active, physically and mentally. Those in their sixties and seventies have grown up in the prosperous post-war world and they have expectations of their interiors, both practical and aesthetic, which are different from those of the generations that went before them. Some are deserting the suburban homes where they brought up their children for inner-city apartments that offer better social interaction, easy access to amenities such as theaters and galleries, and easier maintenance. Real-estate developers have recognized this and are building retirement complexes that ease social interaction with other residents and the wider local community. The Belle Vue retirement community of sixty apartments in a prosperous area of London exemplifies the trend. The developer Morris+Company targeted seniors who are gregarious, active, and intellectually ambitious (23.19). ![](images/cabe2cbf7cb860495dcf96748e5c9e7b357afdcfb995510eb823bcf5e3911faf.jpg) 23.19 Morris+Company, communal lounge, Belle Vue, London, 2019. Sixty apartments for the over-sixties have shared areas, including a library, communal lounges, activity rooms, and a restaurant and coffee shop that are open to the wider community. A health-and-wellbeing facility includes treatment rooms, a gym, stretch studio, lap pool, sauna, and steam room. The complex recognizes that seniors are active, curious, and ambitious. ## Education EBD is also increasingly applied in educational buildings as scientific studies provide insight into how children learn. Universities, where financing depends on recruitment of students, indulge in significant building programs to both attract and house them. Libraries in particular have been transformed as the computer becomes the dominant learning-and-research tool, and the coffee shop has become their paradigm. The most radical work, however, is being done at pre-college levels where the familiar model of corridors and walled classrooms is being superseded by more informal environments, which are often organized to encourage public access and parental involvement. EBD principles have spread widely and a high school by Glasgow-based Stallan-Brand Architecture + Design, in a modestly sized town in Jedburgh in southern Scotland, completed in 2021, provides an excellent example of a response to new teaching philosophies and the integration of school and local community (23.21). 23.21 ![](images/6107742a144a20711de7b202992c71cf4fa1060b90abbda5fdc40da544c104c4.jpg) Stallan-Brand Architecture + Design, Jedburgh Grammar Campus, Scotland, 2021. It is seen as a civic building that contains a school and is open to the public after school hours. ## Furniture and Furnishings Experimentation with new materials and the technologies of rapid prototyping and digital printing has led to limited production of furniture (usually a chair) that goes far beyond the generic utilitarian object and is really intended for exhibition and an audience of collectors. The idea of the rhetorical chair was not new but the chairs of the early modernists, and of Charles and Ray Eames (see here) and Philippe Starck (see 1 and 2), had been conceived as functioning furniture. In 1994 Swedish furniture retailer Ikea asked Verner Panton (see here) to design a chair and he created Vilbert (23.28), an assembly of flat, melamine planes that has some resemblance to Gerrit Rietveld’s Zig Zag chair (see here). All Panton’s chairs might be said to belong in the category of design art but they, and Vilbert, were intended for use in homes and restaurants. ![](images/8498febcea6d8b714ac62273c86eb2b1c4102dafc14b32f19c5a88c5404d679e.jpg) Verner Panton, Vilbert, 1994. In response to an invitation from Ikea to design a chair that could be produced as a limited range, Panton exchanged his molded curved pieces of the 1960s and 1970s for an assemblage of melamine flats, appropriate to the company’s simple, cost-cutting production techniques. Examples have become collectors’ pieces. Ikea offers a gamut of practical styles from the gently retro to pieces that touch the cutting edge. It allows good designers to experiment, and develops popular taste by respecting popular taste. Some 85 per cent of its products are common to all its stores worldwide—Scandinavian Modern with its bedrock of traditional values would seem to have triumphed. Those chary of popular taste may prefer to pay a premium for products simply because they are not by Ikea. Sometimes better made, sometimes better looking, they are found in stores that are the delicatessens of furniture and fittings, offering ingredients to those wishing to cook up more esoteric interiors. ## The Future It is optimistic, probably foolish, to imagine that the future of a creative activity that is, of necessity, in a permanent state of flux can be predicted. The interconnectivity of today’s world means that the influences of science, technology, art, public taste, and even politics continually interact with individual creativity and the collective response to it. Ecologically responsible and socially responsible design has taken a firmer hold and are being embedded in design culture as practitioners begin to accept their responsibility—and ability—to make improvements. Advances in technology and communication will continue to change the way design is conceived and executed but those changes are likely to affect the manner in which projects are implemented rather than the way they look, which will be found in the imagination of designers. Since the postmodernist realignments of the 1980s there is much greater popular awareness and appreciation of interior design. The new interiors of that decade were unprecedented but changed users’ expectations, the interiors of the twenty-first century have to work harder to impress. The International Style of the mid twentieth century, epitomized by the steel and glass towers of big business, was elegant but bland and seen as a universal formula. It might be assumed that the digital technologies that now ease the mechanisms of daily life must promote broad homogeneity, but the interiors of the first decades of the twenty-first century suggest that there is now a postmodern International Style, one that does not attempt to impose a universal aesthetic but responds to its locality, in a mission to give the physical world something tangible that the virtual cannot, something more subtle than pastiche. The Vikasa Yoga Studio in Thailand by Thai design firm Enter Projects Asia (2020; 23.1) is flamboyantly responsive to local materials and craft techniques and the activity it houses. The Elgin Café in Punjab (23.31), by New Delhi-based Studio Renesa, uses the green marble of its region and various details seem to suggest an awareness of, and, perhaps ironic, nostalgia for long-established links to Europe. ![](images/2cf7117311f6b44a8b081b427d1b7c20748b61ee7ac35a8819ba9dc2f7839c1e.jpg) Studio Renesa, Elgin Café, Punjab, India, 2021. A coffee shop and restaurant that refers to its location with local green Udaipur marble for walls and tabletops and matching paintwork, as well as to India’s colonial past, with the wicker chairs and brass light switches. However, links need not be old, or reverential. London-based Brinkworth Design drew on recent phenomena to create a Los Angeles flagship store for Supreme, a skateboarding company, in the deserted shell of what had been the world’s biggest music store (23.30). Much of the building fabric was retained but skylights were added and the roof was raised to accommodate an enormous suspended skate bowl, With its exposed structural plywood ribs it was an inflated replica of the original installed in Supreme’s San Francisco store, both were designed and hand built by Steve Badgett (b. 1962) of the American artist collective Simparch. The structure acts as an amplifier and fills the store with the thunderous echoing sounds of board on bowl. The concept is from the culture of skate boarding rather than architecture or mainstream design. ![](images/3cd712598cd6cf1aad0730c319732bcbf05a4e73f7ea398e7dc2822caf53660e.jpg) Brinkworth Design, Supreme, Los Angeles, 2020. The interior of this skateboarding megastore is dominated by the plywood-ribbed structure of the hanging skate bowl. Sculptor and professional skateboarder Mark Gonzales (b. 1968) created the full-size F1 car that occupies the middle of the polished concrete floor where customers try out new boards. Other skateboarding artists provided equally esoteric wall decorations. A good interior is of its time but its time is short, determined by the social and commercial pressures of the worlds it serves, and those, driven by social media, are becoming more intense. Having broken free of the stylistic dogma that inhibited its development in the mid twentieth century, interior design has become fascinatingly amorphous and in the twenty-first century’s digital world, where ideas are shared globally without the filter of reactionary mainstream opinion, it can only continue to evolve.
