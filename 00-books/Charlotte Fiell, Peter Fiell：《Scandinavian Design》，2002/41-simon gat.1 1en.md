In 1913, Johan Ekman purchased the Orrefors glassworks and subsequently began looking for a designer to help develop the company's products along more artistic lines. Already an accomplished figurative artist, in 1916 Simon Gate became the first “artist-designer” to join the glassworks. His product designs fell into two categories – simple functional wares that employed updated traditional forms which were produced by Sandvik, Orrefors' sister company, and finely engraved pictorial pieces with Baroque inspired decoration which epitomized the “Swedish Grace” decorative style. One of Gate's most celebrated designs was the Parispokalen (Paris Cup) that was produced specifically for the Swedish Pavilion at the 1925 “Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes” in Paris, where it was awarded a Grand Prix. This large showpiece vase measuring 75 cm in height was adorned with intricate classical motifs, which required around 450 hours to engrave. On seeing this design, as well as others by Gate and Edward Hald at the exhibition, the French critic Léon Deshair declared that the two designers “pensent en verre” (thought in glass). Certainly Gate and Hald's work from this period though quite different in style from one another, marked a crucial turning point for Swedish glass and opened up new markets for Orrefors. Apart from his delicately engraved pieces, Gate also designed undecorated glassware such as the Slottglass (Castle glass) of 1923 – a green glass goblet with a fluted form that predicted the later and more sculptural Scandinavian style. From 1916 onwards, Gate collaborated with the master glassblower Knut Bergkvist and developed a new technique known as Graa/ after the Holy Grail, which involved casing clear glass blanks with thin layers of colouredglass. Patterns were then cut, etched or engraved into the objects to expose the underlying clear glass. These pieces were then reheated and coated with a layer of clear glass, and finally blown into their required form. By using this laborious and expensive method, decorative patterns could be encased to dramatic effect within glass bodies. During the 1930s, Gate's designs became increasingly simplified in both form and decoration. Often he would use broadly cut geometric motifs that gave his objects a jewel-like faceted quality. Around the same period, Gate also designed bulbous thick-walled vases which accentuated the inherent optical and reflective qualities of glass and provided a strong sense of mass. Throughout his career Gate experimented profusely with glass and developed numerous innovative techniques that were used by later designers at Orrefors. Like so many other Scandinavian designers, Gate perfected his craft by developing studio art pieces that were subsequently translated into designs suitable for industrial mass production.
