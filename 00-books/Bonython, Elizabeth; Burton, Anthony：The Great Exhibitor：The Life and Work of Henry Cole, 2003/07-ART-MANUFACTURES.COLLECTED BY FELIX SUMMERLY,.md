This institution, founded by some go-ahead men nearly 1oo years earlier, in 1754, was properly titled the Society for the Encouragement of Arts, Manufactures and Commerce. It became the Royal Society of Arts in 19o8. As its original name suggests, it had a wide interest in human ingenuity in all its forms. In its early days the society involved itseif in, among other things, agriculture, forestry and land drainage; in medicinal drugs and soap; and in the manufacturing techniques, especially in the field of textiles, of the industrial revolution in Britain. It was also interested in drawing, painting and sculpture, and in Fancy, Design and Taste'.1 Through its support for art exhibitions, it paved the way for the foundation of the Royal Academy in 1768, and, while it kept clear of art exhibitions thereafter, it continued to make awards to artists and art students. Its chief method of proceeding in other fields was by offering prizes for new inventions, and it contributed tirelessly to the gadgetry of nations. Founded in a coffee house, and meeting at first in a circulating library, in 1774 the society established its headquarters in the purpose-built premises it still occupies, in a building that formed part of the Adam brothers'redevelopment of the Adelphi area, south of the Strand (plate 28). In the early decades of the nineteenth century, after many successful years, the society was running down, and by the early 184os it was almost bankrupt. It had only 32o members² and was about to sell its investments to pay off its debts. A rescue committee under a young patent lawyer, Thomas Webster, was appointed in 1841, and was mastering the problems by 1843. The assumption of this office marked an important stage in the prince's creation of an independent sphere of influence for himself. Richard Redgrave, Matthew Digby Wyatt and Owen Jones were first presented as 'Prophets of the Modern Movement' in 1937.66 Cole and the Journal of design were brought forward to join them in 1948,67 and thereafter were regularly acclaimed, especially in the works of Nikolaus Pevsner.68 If such critics were inclined to attribute rather too distinct an outline to the doctrines of Cole and his allies, in their zeal to see them as proto-Modernists, other critics have looked more searchingly at the journal.69 One of them stresses that at first ‘much confusion reigned in the editorial office' over what might be true principles of design; and among the new manufactures illustrated were all kinds of designs, later to be the targets of scorn and anathematisation'. Through the journal can be traced the development of the ideas of Cole and his group (from naturalism to conventionalism), and by the end aclear and consistent set of principles' was formulated.70 We can assume that Cole wrote most of the unsigned articles and paragraphs in the Journal of design. Often enough, his own voice seems to come through, as when he dismisses the retrogressive party at the Society of Arts as twaddling with incipient patents, nursing jealousies as to the origination of this lever or that crank'./1 And, although he had to hand over the editorship to his brother Charles for a time when the demands of the Great Exhibition became all-consuming, we can be sure that he was deeply involved as an editor, and that the journal stands as his intellectual construct. It is here, if anywhere, that we meet Cole as a thinker, rather than an administrative force. Tantalisingly, the Journal of design contains a trace of what must have been his first encounter with Ruskin. The number for October 1 849 contains a brief review by Cole of Ruskin's Seven Lamps of Architecture - 'this thoughtful, eloquent book'. While acknowledging that its beauties will sink deep in the minds of all who feel that true art is the earnest expression of realities and human sentiments', Cole does criticise Ruskin for shunning the modern world. Instead of boldly recognising the tendencies of the age, which are inevitable, perceiving the wide distribution of certain kinds of art, and the positive good that is in this;- instead of considering the means of improving these tendencies and results, he either puts up his back against their further development, or would attempt to bring back the world of art to what its course of action was four centuries ago! - as easy a thing as to put the world itself back. Our course in this nineteenth century may be hateful if you please; denounce it, but as it is our course, wise men should recognise the fact, and try, by all the light that God gives them, to direct it rightly.72 Cole sent the review to Ruskin, with an invitation to contribute to the Journal of design. Ruskin replied politely, declining. The paths of the two men crossed from time to time in the future, and they maintained a civil acquaintance. Each, however, was aware of the other as representing a diametrically opposed tendency in art education, and indeed in philosophy of life. Robert Hewison sees them as standing for two traditions in the nineteenth century, 'the utilitarian and the romantic anti-capitalist, the rationalist and the imaginative'.73 Cole, probably, did not think about this very much. He was always up and doing. Sometimes it has been interpreted as if it were itself a sort of Hallelujah Chorus - a grand, harmonious outburst of praise, celebrating the stable values of Victorian Britain. Recent accounts, however, have stressed that the exhibition, like Victorian Britain, was ‘a site of contention, a source of conflict and debate',1 and have criticised previous accounts as having little appreciation of the complexity of the question'.² Complexity and contention would have been recognised all too easily by those who were actually involved in setting up the exhibition. Cole, for one, was not loath to explain all the difficulties he had gone through. He left two accounts. One was in his introduction to the general Catalogue of the exhibition. In supplying this official account he was instructed by Prince Albert that ‘accuracy is . . . the first essential' and that there should be total absence of anything like "puffing"'.3 So, with due sobriety, he explained the problems that had to be surmounted. First, from small beginnings in the Society of Arts, the project had to be built up into an international event, under the patronage of Prince Albert. Then the government had to be won round, money had to be raised, and support mobilised throughout the country. Third, a site had to be secured and a building erected at high speed. Finally, the exhibits had to be assembled and put on show. In the 1oo pages that Cole devoted to the exhibition in Fifty Years, his second account of it, he took pains to emphasise that at each of these important stages he played a decisive part. It would be wise to bear in mind a reviewer's judgement that Cole's account ‘is not in all respects accurate, and he, unconsciously no doubt, has somewhat overlooked the services of his coadjutors' (the review was in the Athenaeum, so was probably well-informed), but on the whole the evidence bears him out.4 This chapter does not give another conventional account of the exhibition, but a Cole's-eye-view. Lest this should give the impression that preparing for the exhibition was nothing more than one long squabble, it is necessary to remember that the enterprise was an inspiring one, especially for people of the time, who believed so strongly in the value of making and selling things, and even more especially for Cole, for whom the exhibition of art-manufactures had now become a life's mission. Cole manoeuvred tirelessly to bring the scheme to fruition, and to win for himself a dominating position in an enterprise in which it seemed that many more important people outweighed him. The Society of Arts'exhibitions of British manufactures in 1847, 1848 and 1849 seemed to Cole to have opened a prospect of something more ambitious, so he persuaded the society to send a petition to Parliament. In this he tried to show - for it was he who wrote it – the progressive success of the annual exhibitions of the Society of Arts, and that they had now become a source of income to the Society, after paying their expenses'.5 He proposed a similarly self-supporting 'National Exhibition' to the government. After flying this kite, the society's group of exhibition enthusiasts went to test the wind in Paris, where the latest of the French exhibitions of manufactures was held in the summer of 1849. Leaving for Paris on 29 May, Cole joined Matthew Digby Wyatt, from whom the society had commissioned a report. They stayed in a cheap hotel and Cole remembered that we were awakened at midnight by the stifling odour of emptying the cesspools, altogether a novel sensation, which years have not obliterated from my mind'.6 On 1 June Cole went with Herbert Minton (who was also in Paris) to see Louis Buffet, the French minister of agriculture and commerce. For long Buffet had thought that the French should hold an international exhibition, but had failed to find supporters for such a scheme. In Fifty Years, Cole quotes Buffet's proposals at length, which suggests that he regarded them at the time as a significant contribution to the climate of opinion he was trying to create. Buffet gave the British party tickets to visit the exhibition before it opened. It was held in a specially built 'Vast parallelogram’of a palace (675ft × 328ft; 2o6m × 1oom), painted outside to look like stone, while inside 'all the pilasters are papered & painted in a species of graining to imitate chestnut wood, and even the ceiling is covered with the same work. . . . The architecture of the whole is mesquin, although the gigantic scale of the building necessarily elevates the general effect into something of impressiveness'.7 In the course of the next week, the English visitors, meeting at the exhibition or dining together, discussed the possibility of a British international exhibition, and Cole asked everyone he met what they thought of the idea. He came back to London on 7 June, leaving Wyatt to complete his report. The first public disclosure of the Society of Arts's hopes for a major exhibition of British manufactures in London in 1851 came on 14 June 1949, when Prince Albert, as president, gave away the prizes at the society. With his permission, Scott Russell, as secretary, mentioned the project in his report. A few days later the prince's equerry, Col. Cole described this meeting in Fifty Years. It was obviously important to his own narrative of his life to establish that it was he who had first contrived to secure the prince's assent on the vital points that determined the organisation of the exhibition. The membership of the commission would include cabinet ministers, and people representing 'the various interests of the community'. Also, significantly, for the execution of its details, some of the parties present and who are also Members or are officers of the Society of Arts, and who have been most active in originating and preparing for the execution of this plan should be suggested as members'. He thought that f1oo,ooo might be raised. He wanted ‘to carry forward this business without delay'. On 17 October the Egyptian Hall was packed. As the prince was still avoiding the limelight, it fell to Cole to make the main speech. It was, Cole recorded, my first public speech', and he posted old Dilke at the back of the audience ready to signal to him in case he could not be heard. His speech was repeatedly interrupted with cries of 'Hear Hear!' and cheering. He began by following the prince's instructions to make it quite clear that such preparations as had already been made were not final. Then he tried to paint for his audience a picture of what the exhibition might be like. We shall have spices from the East; the hops of Kent and Sussex, the raisins of Malaga, and the olives of the Pyrenees. .. . Of Mineral productions, we shall have gold from California and from the East Indies; silver from Mexico, Russia, and Cornwall; iron ore from Wales, Wolverhampton, and Tunbridge Wells. ... Then we shall have clays from Bideford, Truro, and perhaps from Putney .... In machinery we shall have the steam-engine in all its endless applications, both to land and water purposes. We shall have marine engines in all their varieties; and we shall probably have such machines as Messrs Whitworth of Manchester have recently constructed, measuring to the fifty-thousandth part of an inch. We may have the looms of the Dacca muslin weaver, and the last new power-looms made by Messrs. Roberts....I should not wonder if Messrs. Applegarth were to exhibit a printing-machine like that now used by 'The Times' which pours forth 1o,ooo copies of that newspaper per hour. Some of his remarks revealed his concerns as a design reformer. He suggested that Britons would learn from the section on plastic arts and sculpture ‘how much the French are in advance of us in the manufacture of bronzes', though he hoped that Britain could catch up. And he trailed before his audience the prospect of seeing Indian textiles ‘which are now almost unknown to us', and which did in the event make a great impression on him. After stating that the exhibition would be held in Hyde Park, and would be funded by private subscription rather than from taxes, he reached his peroration. 'In short, London will act the part of host to all the world at an intellectual festival of peaceful industry, suggested by the Consort of our beloved Queen and seconded by yourselves - a festival, such as the world has never seen before.' The Times recorded that ‘Mr Cole resumed his seat amidst much cheering from all parts of the Hall'.36 Formal resolutions were passed in favour of the exhibition, for the setting up of a city committee, and for agreeing that the funding would be by voluntary subscriptions only. In the evening Cole made a tour of newspaper offices to make sure that he had been reported correctly, reaching home well after midnight. In the morning he received a note from Col. Phipps which read, 'Your meeting in the City appears to have been most successful.'37 Cole reprinted his speech in its entirety in Fifty Years, as well he might, since it was obviously one of his most immediately effective interventions in public life. It was clear by now that a Royal Commission would be appointed, as the prince had requested, and Cole recorded that the 'preparation of it was confided me'.38 During the next week he went twice to Windsor Castle to see the prince and discuss how the Society of Arts would work with the Royal Commission. The prince was worried that the House of Commons might yet reject the whole plan. He was in a despondent mood, partly because of the sudden death of George Anson, his private secretary. 'He was my only intimate friend. We went through everything together since I came here. He was almost like a brother to me.'39 Anson's health had been poor for several years and he suffered from bad headaches. All the letters that Cole received from the prince had been written by Col. Phipps, who was an equerry, rather than the secretary proper. After Anson's death, Phipps continued to write letters for the prince, but was promoted to be privy purse and treasurer to the prince and cofferer to the Prince of Wales. As secretary, Col. Charles Grey was appointed. He belonged to one of the great Whig families who were close friends of the Queen, and had been sent to Saxe-Coburg to fetch the prince to England for his marriage. He became for Cole a most friendly, tactful and useful ally at court, first as the prince's secretary, and later as the Queen's. He told Cole to call on him at any time. Early in November Wentworth Dilke joined the executive committee. Robert Stephenson was elected on to the council of the Society of Arts, and at Russell's suggestion also came on to the executive committee. By now, the committee was set up in an office at No. I Old Palace Yard in the Palace of Westminster, with smart headed writing paper. The government agreed to a Royal Commission, and it became urgent to decide who would be its members. On 11 December Labouchere was surprised at the delay and Lord Granville said he would write to Col. Phipps. There were difficulties in finding statesmen willing to lend their support to such a risky project. Free traders were in favour, but there was no enthusiasm from high Tories, such as the duke of Richmond. Prompted by a 'confidential letter'40 from Phipps, Cole and Fuller invited Richmond to serve, but, an arch-protectionist, he declined. There were other, more personal, reasons that held some politicians back: 'The Ministers could not of course commit themselves by publicly avowing their disapprobation of an undertaking commenced under such high auspices. It might, however, readily have been foreseen that they would be averse to such a scheme, because whilst it was sure to give them a great deal of trouble, it would afford them no compensation in the shape of patronage.'This was written by the disgruntled mathematician and inventor, Charles Babbage, who for many years had been struggling to get official help for his calculating machine. He went on: 'Those, however, who usually reflect and retail the opinions of the Government, were by no means silent; at first it was said to be Utopian, then ridiculous, then, in the slang of official life, it was “pooh-poohed",.41 While there was opposition to the exhibition, antagonism was also mounting against Cole himself. Russell told him that someone had said I was a great jobber & wondered why he had connected me with the Scheme!'. Cole was angry and noted that Russell 'rather stumbled out an explanation on perceiving my indignation'. Edwin Chadwick, who was a true friend of Cole's, called on him expressly to tell him that 2nd rate people were jealous of me, & wondered that the Prince shd set me to do the thing'. Sir Francis Palgrave called at Cole's office and was also 'very jealous'. However, Cole had his supporters. Richard Cobden told him that 'he had full confidence in any arrangements to which I was a party'. And, as Thomas Cubitt reassured him, 'The Prince was fully sensible of the share I had in the Business & appreciated it'.+2 Cole and his fellow lobbyists from the Society of Arts were worried about what positions they might ultimately hold in the organisation of the exhibition. By now they accepted that they would not be on the Royal Commission itself, but hoped that the society's executive committee might in some way be set apart from other committees serving under the commission. Russell further suggested at dinner one evening with Cole and Fuller that ‘we three shd be distinct & superior to other members of Ex: Comm'.43 They were also worried about whether they would be paid. Cole's salary at the Record Office was £45o, to which he could add something for his journalism and literary work. Among the enthusiasts for the exhibition there was an optimistic feeling that the Mundays would make huge profits, and that some of that money would find its way to them. Cole had no such hopes, and with his ever-increasing family he needed to be paid while he worked and could not afford to wait for the jackpot that might or might not be emptied into their laps at the close of the exhibition. He hoped for £1,2oo a year and told the prince so, while Fuller expected £1,o0o. Drew, Russell and Cole had dinner at Fuller's house one evening in November and Drew agreed to ask the Mundays to pay £1,2oo a year to Dilke and Cole, and £1,00o to Fuller. Russell said he would make no obstacle to this arrangement'.44 But later he changed his mind and said the public would regard £1 2oo a year as a job, that he shd be content with 7oof for his services'. Cole agreed with him, but wrote, ‘Fuller upbraided me with Upsetting the nicest pail of milk worth to me £1o,ooo at least. . . . Altogether very quarelling [sic] & personal'.45 Fuller later said that Cole had got ‘a letter from Messrs. Munday agreeing to give him £1,2oo a year for his services; and left the remainder of the Executive Committee to make such arrangements with the Contractor as they might be able. . .. I made application to Messrs. Munday for a similar letter, which they declined to give'.46 Such bickering over terms, however unbecoming, is inevitable when a large enterprise is launched. It was particularly vexatious for Cole, since his financial position was less secure than that of the others. Cole's position was further complicated when he was officially seconded from the Record Office to work full-time for the executive committee. When he called on Lord Langdale at his house in Roehampton on 28 December - the day that he officially transferred to the executive committee - Langdale 's[ai]d he grieved that I sh[oul]d leave the Record Office: thought I ought to be amply indemnified: to have fixed payment & no uncertainty: w[oul]d help me as much as he c[oul]d & w[oul]d see Trevelyan'. When Cole himself consulted Sir Charles Trevelyan at the Treasury, he thought my proposal reasonable & honourable'. Cobden and Milner Gibson both understood Cole's need to be properly paid. The prince, however, insisted that there must be perfect harmony and no jobbery', and that any remuneration should be left to the commission. Cole was in the position of poor relation, among distinguished people who could afford to work for nothing. To be paid might well mean that he lost face, Later, at the first meeting of the commission's finance committee held at the Board of Trade on 29 January, he had to stand up for himself and tell the committee 'I would not take an office or Salary which implied an inferior position to anyone else'.47 Behind this statement, and some of the disagreements just described, was not only Cole's discomfort at being a player among gentlemen, but also a rivalry that had grown up between him and John Scott Russell.48 It had already surfaced publicly. On 13 December, when Cole stayed at home 'physicking', Dilke came to tell him that there was evidently a feeling in Ex Comm: that He and I were working against Fuller & Russell'.49 Russell was a successful and still-rising marine engineer, while Cole was a minor government official, even if he was well known as a reformer and troublemaker. Russell felt that Cole was his protégé and, as Cole recorded a day or two before, 'He said that my office was essentially that of Secy, said we wd be friends on this understanding. Wanted me to agree to £8oo a year for the whole time, he having £75o for a part only'.50 The commission was eventually to give Cole £8oo a year with an extra £25o to cover the work that he had done at the early planning stage. While Cole thus struggled to protect his own position, there was little official progress on the commission. A preliminary public announcement that a Royal Commission would be appointed was put out on 2o December, but discussions as to who should be on it still dragged on. Christmas intervened. Cole had a children's party on Christmas Eve with charades and a Christmas tree. (The Christmas tree was an innovation in the British Christmas; Prince Albert introduced it from Germany, and after the royal family's tree had been featured in the Illustrated London News in 1848, the public hastened to copy it.) The guests at Cole's party included Thackeray's two little girls, the Dilkes, and Fuller's daughters. On Boxing Day Cole called on Labouchere at the Board of Trade, but there was no news about the commission. Cole spent 27 December walking with his cousin John Bond. They visited Cole's father at Kew, and then walked to Richmond and Roehampton, returning to Kensington over Hammersmith Bridge. On 28 December Cole noted in his diary, with irritation, 'Names in Commission not at all settled'. On New Year's Day Cole again saw Labouchere, who thought that the secretaries ought not to be named alongside the commissioners. Cole pleaded for Russell, who was away in Berlin, to be a secretary. However, later in the day at a meeting at Windsor, the prince 'thought that Russell's absence was significant [and] that he wd not make Commission his primary business. Proposed not to name him as Secy, but as a Commissioner'. Then they discussed whether Digby Wyatt or Samuel Redgrave (Richard's brother) might be a secretary. Cole suggested himself, saying that 'I wd see that matters went right if Secy'. To which the prince emolliently replied: 'I'm sure you would',1 but gave Cole no further encouragement to aim at this post. The Royal Commission was gazetted two days later. None of the people who had so far done the work of promoting the exhibition were on it. Prince Albert was the president, and Lord Granville the vice-president. From the political world came Lord John Russell, the prime minister, and Lord Stanley, the leader of the opposition, with MPs Robert Peel, Thomas Baring the banker, William Gladstone, Henry Labouchere, the radical Richard Cobden, Philip Pusey and William Thompson. The duke of Buccleuch, the earl of Ellesmere and the earl of Rosse represented the aristocracy. The latter was president of the Royal Society (Britain's premier scientific association) and was therefore balanced by Charles Lyell, president of the Geological Society and Charles Eastlake, president of the Royal Academy. The artistic world was also represented by Charles Barry, architect, and Sir Richard Westmacott, sculptor. Since the exhibition was to be concerned with manufactures, it was obviously necessary to include industrialists and businessmen: Thomas Bazley, chairman of the Manchester Chamber of Commerce; Archibald Galloway, chairman of the East India Company; Thomas Field Gibson, a Spitalfields silk merchant; John Gott, a Leeds wool manufacturer. Finance was represented by the MPs Baring and Thompson already mentioned, together with banker Samuel Jones Loyd. William Cubitt, builder and president of the Institute of Civil Engineers, might be presumed to have a special capacity to help with an exhibition building. This group had in common a sympathy for the liberal side of politics. Cole was disappointed in his ambition to be secretary, but at least Russell was not put over his head as a commissioner but was appointed a joint secretary. The other joint secretary was Stafford Northcote, Cole's adversary from the Schools of Design committee. An Etonian with a first from Balliol, he may well have been chosen as some kind of counterpoise to the uncouth men from the Society of Arts. To restore good relations, Cole wrote him a placating letter, which he read over to the prince beforehand. When he had his first discussion with Northcote, he found him ‘very cooperative & pleasant'.52 From then on the two men worked harmoniously together until Northcote retired six months later because of his father's illness, his place being taken by Edgar Bowring. The members of the Society of Arts's executive committee were appointed as the executive committee of the Royal Commission, with Robert Stephenson as chairman and Digby Wyatt as secretary. Cole emerged, therefore with no office. He was only one member among others of a subordinate committee. He undoubtedly felt that he had been pushed down. Later, in Fifty Years, he recalled that from this moment ‘my relations with the Prince were of necessity altogether changed'. There was now an official distance between them, whereas ‘up to that time I had had the privilege of being consulted by him on all occasions, for several months'.53 ## Now that the commission was in place, it might be expected that work on the exhibition would speed ahead. So far as Cole was concerned, however, the next few months brought only frustration and jockeying for position. The prince called the first meeting of the commission for 11 January 185o. Cole and Fuller attended the meeting and heard the decision taken to ask the Society of Arts to cancel the contract with the Mundays. It was clear that, without the £75,ooo that the Mundays were prepared to put up, the commission would have to find the money for its own building. So the task was no longer a matter of collecting assurances of goodwill towards the exhibition, but of finding substantial cash to set up the organisation and run the exhibition. The commission met again on the following Friday. Stephenson, Cole and the rest of the executive committee hung about outside the meeting and were never called in. They heard afterwards that their reports had not been adopted, even though the Prince had approved them. No instructions reached them. It was becoming apparent that the Royal Commissioners were ready to attend meetings, but averse to decisive action. They were peers, politicians and notabilities, who had been appointed to lend weight rather than dynamism to the commission. It did not help the committee that the sympathetic vice-president of the commission, Lord Granville, was away in Paris for the first three weeks of January. He later told Cole that the executive committee members were objects of suspicion' to the commissioners.54 The prince must have known that the grandees on the commission would not want to be told what to do by the middle-class members of the Society of Arts's executive committee. As a journalist pointed out with hindsight, The Roval Commission consists of noblemen and gentlemen of the nicest honour, entitled to the respect and confidence alike of the high and the humble; but it is no reproach to them to say they were entirely ignorant concerning the work they undertook .. .. This evil might have been remedied if a few practical men had been added to the Commission; but whether it was considered infra dig to mix up such men with the aristocracy, we cannot say: at all events, if we sift the Commission, we shall scarcely find one to compensate for the absence of experience, and other advantages in the Commission generally, and in their Secretaries.55 Cole and the other Society of Arts lobbyists wanted to be present at the meetings of the commission. But the prince urged them to be patient, anticipating that the commission shd find the want of us. He must beg us to give submissiveness in this point'.56 It was not in Cole's character to be submissive. He felt that he was prevented from doing the work at every turn. He could see that an organisation needed to be set up and funds collected. Government departments could give him no help. When he needed to write to towns and cities throughout the country to ask them to set up local committees he found that the Board of Trade had no list of chambers of commerce, nor had the Home Office a list of corporations. This inefficiency amused Cobden. Cole discussed his position with Mulready, who like most of his friends told him to keep calm.Advised patience & then instant action when resolved upon.' Rumours and gossip were flying around: Charles Cole told Henry on 19 January that he had heard that Wyatt had a contingent interest of f2ooo, & that the Ex:[ecutive] Com:[mittee] was at a "fix" with Comm[issio]n'. The week before, Milner Gibson had shown Cole an article in the Manchester Examiner imputing disingenuousness to me'.57 On 22 January John Scott Russell came back from Germany, where he had been building gunboats for the Prussian government, and the difficulties between the executive committee and the commission were compounded by the prickly rivalry between Cole and Russell.58 At Windsor on 28 January the Prince was quite blunt about the position of the executive committee. He would not admit it to the commission's discussions, telling Cole, Northcote and Russell that he objected to attendance of the Ex: Com: on the ground that it would make unnecessary the attendance of the Members of Commn. He thought the Ex Com might remain as it was'.59 Jones Loyd talked to Northcote about the problem of the restive executive committee. Northcote in turn saw Labouchere, Lord Granville and Gladstone. Milner Gibson talked to Cobden. The problem as it presented itself to all these eminent persons was crisply described in a letter from Stafford Northcote to Colonel Grey: It is pretty clear that the Commission is unfit for executive functions. It is clear also that the executive functions must be assigned to some one or more persons possessing the public confidence: and it is pretty evident that the present men of the Executive Committee (Stephenson being set aside) are not men of a calibre to command confidence, though they have great merits, and are perhaps indispensable to the success of the undertaking, on account of the knowledge and ability they bring to bear upon it. It would be unfair and ungentlemanly to throw them over after the share they have had in bringing the matter to its present stage, besides which I am quite convinced it would be most unwise. But they will require a strong hand over them, or they may bring His Royal Highness and the Commission into serious embarrassment. It may be unjust, but His Royal Highness and the Commission ought to be informed of the fact that there is great distrust of some of the Executive Committee; and the scheme is one which can only succeed if there be full confidence in those who are working it. Northcote thought that the executive committee must be tamed by setting over it a chairman with very great powers, that is to say, with a veto upon all acts of the Executive body, and with considerable discretion as to the mode in which he should carry out the general views of the Commission. Under such a Chairman work might easily be found for all the present members of the Executive, who might take different departments of it. . .. Their remuneration would be easily settled, their feelings not hurt, and the constitution untouched. Who should be the man?' asked Northcote, and revealed that the preferred candidate (who would replace Robert Stephenson) would be ‘an able Engineer officer, say for instance Colonel Reid, the late Governor of Barbadoes'. He thought that Reid might be appointed quite easily and quietly, and without any material disturbance of the existing constitution'.60 Sadly, Northcote was over-optimistic, and the change was not effected quietly. On 8 February, Cole and Dilke went to tell Col. Grey that they had decided to resign. Grey had a note handed to the prince during a meeting of the commission, imparting this news, and Cole and Dilke were sent for at once. They were told that Col. Reid was to be their chairman, and their link with the commission. The next day Robert Stephenson returned from a visit to Liverpool earlier than expected. Cole and Dilke told him the news and whipped him up into such a rage at the idea of being displaced that he too resigned. He told Cole that he would never consider any one his friend who did not resent the indignity put upon him'.61 So Stephenson, Dilke and Cole all signed official letters of resignation and Dilke informed the finance committee. Immediately they were called in. Labouchere and Lord Granville calmed Stephenson down; he was invited to be a commissioner – in effect, a promotion - and withdrew his resignation. Lord Granville said to Cole that, if he resigned, he shd not stand well with the public'. Jones Loyd told him that ‘my demand to be present [at meetingsl was against all precedent at the Bank: Poor Law Board: Customs: . . .' Cole thought that ‘if I were weak enough to consent [to the new arrangement] the Commissioners wd despise me. All repudiated such an idea. They wd be nearer doing so, if I declined.' He said he wd give an answer tomorrow'.62 Cole was in an awkward position, as he had positively resigned and did not know how to back down without losing face. That evening Northcote wrote a private letter to Col. Grey: 'We have been in the hottest of water since yesterday, but I think it is beginning to cool a little.' Stephenson, he reported, had been pacified. He thought that Cole would come round: I have had a great deal of conversation with him and think he is not so much annoyed at the appointment of Col Reid, as at the retention of the Executive Committee in a shape which gives him no other position than that of Mr Fuller and Mr Drew who nevertheless do not intend to, and cannot render the services that he can and will. Still I think he will not like to give up his connection with the Exhibition, and as the Finance Committee took great pains to assure him how much importance they attached to his continuing to serve, and expressed the same feeling on behalf of all the Commissioners I hope he will think better of his resolution.63 Cole still found it difficult to rescind his resignation, asking Col. Grey, pray tell me if you think I can with propriety after your letter of yesterday take another course?'64 Col. Grey tactfully suggested that refusal to serve under Col. Reid would look like jealousy, which considering his Character and position as a Public Man who has filled high situations would not be an intelligible reason to the Public'.65 As Cole recorded, John Shaw-Lefevre alsourged me not to cut off my parental connexion with “the greatest scheme in the world": that I was sure in any position to be of some use. great use, that it would be injured if I retired, that I should lose all the incidental advantages, My family would suffer. He wd not hesitate to serve under Col: Reid: &c:.'66 So Cole, after consulting Labouchere and Lord Granville, withdrew his resignation, as did Dilke. The man who was expected to save the day, Lieut.-Col. William Reid, Royal Engineers, was nearly 6o years old, a Scot and a distinguished soldier (plate 3o). He had served under Wellington, and, during the various campaigns in which he fought, he had been wounded four times and had three horses killed under him. He spent many years in the West Indies, where he became governor of the Windward Islands and where he did all he could to get some education for the black population, who were very poor since the abolition of slavery. Back in England in 1848, he was elected a vice-president of the Royal Society for the research he had done into the law of storms. He had recently taken command of the Royal Engineers at Woolwich. After his death in 1858, the Royal Society obituarist wrote of him: 'It has been said that his singular simplicity of manner and total absence of pretension caused the distinguished men, with whom he was associated . . . to wonder at first what had led to his selection for the office.'67 Over 2o years later, Cole called the appointment 'prudent and successful',68 but at the time, in the privacy of his diary, he gave vent to his frustration. His laments at enforced idleness continued. 'Nothing to do but look at proof of Col: Reids prospectus'; 'waited doing nothing till 5'; 'Nothing whatever to do'; 'Nothing to be done'; 'meeting of Commission. Nothing to do: waited till 7. heard nothing: Col Reid did not come over'.69 Col. Reid's years in the army led him to be content to wait for orders from the commission, while Cole chafed to be up and doing. Reid remarked to Cole at this time that he seemed apt with suggestions'.70 It is hard not to read a note of irony into this remark. Cole was certainly all too ready tell the colonel what needed to be done. Within a month, it began to look as if Col. Reid was not the man for the job after all. Lord Granville wrote an extremely frank letter to Col. Grey, which he asked him not to show to the prince. Sir R Peel called me out of the H. of Lords yesterday evening. He told me that he was seriously alarmed about the manner of conducting the business of the Exhibition, that the Commission was as a body unfit for work, that Colonel Reid was inefficient. ... From the enquiries we made of the Colonial Office, the Treasury, and of Officers of the Engineers, Mr Labouchere and I believed that Col Reid was a man of great energy and even of genius, whose only fault might be a liability to take offence. He turns out to be a man of very conciliatory manners, of ability, and good judgment (more I think than appears) but without any facility of writing, and without a sufficient quantity of what is called Devil. ## Granville went on to criticise Scott Russell. Mr Scott Russell is a bad secretary, his manner is disagreeable, he is stated to have been insolent in some of the Committees, and Colonel Reid seems to have reason to complain of him in several respects . . . it might be desirable, if when you are charged with instructions from the Prince, you should communicate more with Northcote and Col: Reid than with Scott Russell. Indeed I think that it would be advantageous if he were sent . . . to report on the Leipsic Exhibition, or on any other mission which would take a little time.71 Russell was preoccupied with his shipbuilding career, and was neglecting his work as a secretary to the Royal Commission. He failed to send out notices of meetings in good time, his reports were sometimes unfinished, and he tried to cover himself by evasions. He was nearly late for one meeting at Windsor Castle, and had to hire a railway engine to get him there. As we have already seen, the prince had doubts about him, and thought of making him a commissioner rather than secretary. Furthermore, the prince felt he could not rely on him for drafting, he did not write good English. Flowery style, too fulsome for him, like a Russian despatch. Manner affected'.72 Furthermore, Russell had very untidy, almost illegible, handwriting. In the spring, Northcote confided to Cole that 'some of the Comnrs had found out that Russell did not speak the truth'.73 In April, Lord Granville had the task of smoothing down Philip Pusey, to whom Russell had been so insolent at a meeting of the agricultural committee that he would not sit in the same room with him. There was friction also between Russell and Reid. They had several acrimonious discussions after Russell refused to show Reid reports discussed in committee. Cole recorded that Reid described the tension between the commission and the executive committee as: 'two different things done on two sides of the way', and that he characterised Russell as 'the wrong point of the needle'. Cole also recorded that Russell 'was the only man that had told Reid he was slow and feared responsibility'.74 Nonetheless, in spite of all his faults, Russell remained a secretary to the commission until after the exhibition. While both Reid and Russell were earning bad marks in London, the provinces were feeling neglected and resentful. Although Cole, Wyatt and Fuller had toured the country in the summer of 1849, successfully arousing support and setting up local committees, nothing had happened in the provinces for a year since then, and local papers were attacking the Society of Arts and the commission, calling the exhibition a job'. This damage had to be repaired, so in June Sir Robert Peel had Lyon Playfair appointed a special commissioner to act as a liaison officer between the commission and the local committees. Playfair was a scientist, and professor of chemistry at the London School of Mines. He had been a student under Baron von Liebig at Giessen in Germany, and on returning to London in 1844 he made friends with Peel, who presented him to Prince Albert. He advised on the dangerous state of the sewers at Buckingham Palace, and discussed with the prince his invention for ventilating a drain. His connection with the Great Exhibition began in February 185o, when he entered the discussions about the classification of exhibits,7 and brought Prince Albert, who had firm notions about the philosophical basis of classification, to agree on a system similar to that used by botanists to classify plants. Cole and Playfair had already met to discuss the subject and had got on well, Playfair being among those who dissuaded Cole from his resignation, urging that he was the real pilot of the vessel'.76 In his post as go-between, Playfair was a great success, as he had a lot of charm and an ability to get on with people. He would say encouraging things to Cole, which Cole noted in his diary along with the snipes of Col. Reid. He was ‘one of those men ... whose bright spirits create around them an atmosphere of good will and confidence which is recognised by all with whom they are destined to be brought into contact'.77 Like Cole, Playfair was a short energetic man, and like Cole he always wore spectacles. Playfair, with his fellow special commissioner Colonel J. A. Lloyd, set out to reanimate the provinces through meetings in the principal towns. A team of lecturers was sent out to whip up enthusiasm. This was to be an important occasion because the prince would at last step forward in public as patron of the exhibition. The prince held a levee in the state drawing room, and the 1 5o mayors were presented. Cole had heard that many of them had bought new clothes for the occasion. The dinner was held in the Egyptian Hall, which was decorated with trophies and with the arms of the towns, fixed to the walls and illuminated by gas. [and] contributed to the pleasure of the evening'.79 When the prince rose to speak there was enthusiastic cheering'. His speech, brief but intense, was a hymn to progress. Advances in science and industry, and improvements in communication, now made it possible, he said, for all mankind to combine together. 'Man is approaching a more complete fulfilment of that great and sacred mission which he has to perform in this world . . . to conquer nature to his use.' Cole thought the speeches capital' and the whole evening as good as it cd be'.81 Shortly afterwards, with Lord Granville's approval, Cole put out a leaflet about the exhibition to consolidate support. Although it was essentially a publicity release – he referred to it as a Popular Statement of the Nature of the Exhibition'82 it was not freely distributed but sold for ½d. each, with reductions for quantity. It was printed in very small type, such as a modern publicist would recoil from, but was well articulated with bold punchy headlines to mark each section. Its prose style was colourful, even lurid. It is expected', cried Cole confidently, that princes will be exhibitors: it is known that the most scientific agriculturists, the most skilful manufacturers, and artists of the greatest genius, will be exhibitors: at the same time every cultivator, or producer, or discoverer in the world, be he ever so humble, will have the opportunity of contributing to this exhibition if he bring forward an object of a superior character. The cottager who cultivates any seed of remarkable excellence, the cottager's son who invents any implement of increased usefulness, or, working at the loom, weaves a choice and novel specimen of skill; the cottager's daughter who works more elegant pillow-lace than common, or plaits straw in a new and more beautiful way, all may be exhibitors. The exhibition will be one enormous unrivalled storehouse for the useful results of all human industry. He wrote rhapsodically of what would be on show, including exotic products such as ‘Animal substances used for food, such as Preserved Meat, Portable Soup, Consolidated Milk, the Nepang or Sea Slug, and Nest of the Java Swallow, consumed in China'. Cole went on to describe the building that would hold these marvels, even though no design for it yet existed.Eight hundred thousand feet will be under an umbelliferous roof of glass, one more expansive than the world has ever before witnessed,' he foretold. He pointed out the advantages of using Hyde Park for the exhibition, in an attempt to quieten the mounting opposition to its use. The site, he claimed, was well placed for access by road and rail. 'Myriads of working men,' he cried, '. . . will come in “monster trains" from the manufacturing districts to witness their own noble share in this work.' At this stage, the main matter of concern in the preparations for the exhibition was the building. The commission had reason to thank Cole for writing into the contract with the Mundays the escape clause that allowed the commission, as almost its first act, to withdraw from a partnership with a commercial firm. This appeared inappropriate or even corrupt, now that the government and a Royal Commission were sponsoring the exhibition. However, Cole and his allies were expected to keep their noses out of whatever alternative arrangements the commission might choose to make. The Royal Commission appointed a building committee in February. The members were two peers, the duke of Buccleuch, who was an engineer, and Lord Ellesmere, who was well known as a patron of the arts; three architects, Charles Barry, C. R. Like the commissioners, they did not want the members of the executive committee at their meetings.92 An international competition was held, and 245 designs were sent in - a few of them, as it happened, for iron buildings. None of them was selected, and the committee prepared its own official design. This was a huge, solid brick building, with a slate roof and a striking feature to exemplify the present state of the science of construction of this country',93 namely a squat dome 2oo feet across, designed by Brunel and covered with sheet iron. When the design was published in the Builder on 22 June, there was uproar. Protests about the aesthetic inadequacy of the building brought renewed objections to the use of a site in Hyde Park. The Times of ı July suggested that the exhibition be pushed out to Battersea Park or even the Isle of Dogs. Such protests came to a head in a debate in the House of Commons, held on 4 July. Cole organised local committees to send in petitions in favour of the exhibition,94 and on the morning of the debate briefed Labouchere - for at very short notice Labouchere had to take Sir Robert Peel's place in leading the defence of both the exhibition and the site. Peel had been thrown from his horse in Constitution Hill on 29 June, and died on 2 July. A near-miraculous solution arrived from the blue, and Cole was well placed to help it along. On 7 June, a month before the Commons debate, Cole had written in his diary that 'Mr Paxton called at the offices of the executive committee] to propose his flat roof of glass'.97 Joseph Paxton was generally described as the duke of Devonshire's gardener, but he had become far more than that. It is true that he came of a gardening family and learned the trade. He was only 23 when the duke of Devonshire took him on as head gardener at Chatsworth. There, after designing the waterworks and the greenhouses, he soon became the duke's personal assistant in running the estate. He was made a director of the Midland Railway, and collaborated with Wentworth Dilke in starting The Gardener's Chronicle in 1841. It occurred to this ingenious man that it would be possible to devise for the Great Exhibition an iron and glass building, along the lines of a garden green-house. He had been talking over the delay in finding a design for the exhibition building with a Member of Parliament, who urged him to put forward his 'notion', even though the last day for entering designs had been in April. On 7 June, Paxton promised Cole and his colleagues that ‘in nine days time [I] will bring you [my] plans complete'. As he said in a lecture in the autumn, 'no doubt the executive thought [me] a conceited fellow, and that what [I} said was nearer akin to romance than to common sense.' In the midst of his other business, he reflected on his idea, and it was during a meeting in the boardroom of the Midland Railway at Derby, that his mind wandered and 'whilst the business proceeded, [I] sketched my design on a large sheet of blotting-paper'.98 This famous sketch is on long loan from the Paxton family to the Victoria and Albert Museum. Technically it was not possible for Paxton to submit a late entry to the competition for designs for the building, especially as the commission had by now called for tenders from contractors for the erection of their own design. There was a loophole, however, in the fact that they had invited tenderers to suggest ‘improvements' to their design. If, therefore, Paxton's design could be submitted as an 'improvement', on the back of a tender for the committee's design, it might scrape in. This is where Cole came in useful. He had already been sent off to Liverpool, Manchester and Birmingham to try to persuade builders to tender for the commissioners' project. At Smethwick, near Birmingham, he called on the contractors Fox Henderson & Co., construction engineers with a wide experience of building in cast iron. He found that they were ready to tender not only for the commissioners' building but also for Paxton's. Cole stayed up talking to them until half past midnight. With the help of Fox Henderson & Co., and of Peter Barlow, the mathematician and civil engineer, Paxton was able to turn his idea into a set of drawings in quick time. On his way to deliver them in London, he met Robert Stephenson at Derby Station, and in the train Stephenson ‘minutely examined the plans and became thoroughly engrossed with them, until at length he exclaimed that the design was just the thing'.99 He showed the plans to the building committee, and meanwhile Scott Russell showed them to Sir Robert Peel. Paxton told Cole his building ‘would cost £8o,ooo for the hire'.100 By this he meant that the contractor would have the building back at the end of the exhibition to dismantle and perhaps put up again elsewhere. He hoped that it would not be sold to the USA. The building committee was hesitant and deferred a decision, but by publishing an artist's impression of his project in the Illustrated London News on 6 July Paxton forced the committee's hand. On I 5 July they agreed to reject the abortion of a child of their own, and unanimously recommended [my] bantling'.101 The Royal Commission accepted their decision the next day. Once Paxton's scheme and their estimate were accepted, Fox Henderson had a frenzied rush to prepare the working drawings. A visiting engineer remembered 'that small band, stripped to their shirts on a hot night of August, planning, drawing, and calculating strength, and stress, and cost, and time'.102 This was the important contribution made by Fox Henderson. It was they, calling on their experience of building railway stations and dock buildings, who were able to turn Paxton's notion' of a huge greenhouse into one of the wonders of the nineteenth century. On 7 September, Cole and Tishy watched the arrival of the first cast-iron columns for Paxton's 'bantling'. The magical quiet of the site was now gone, and The Times reported that listened from without it seems as if some merry and incongruous village bells were in agitation for the celebration of a marriage'.114 Some 2,ooo workmen were taken on each day, and 2o horses. As the building arose, stretching from near what is now the top of Exhibition Road to a point east of the Knightsbridge Barracks, it became known as ‘Paxton's Palace' and then 'The Crystal Palace'. Cole threatened Fox, two weeks before the opening, 'that if the roof was not proved to be watertight before the end of the week: we shd publish it to be failure'.117 All turned out well. With so much wood in the building, and with such an enormous space to be filled with inflammable materials, Cole was very concerned about the danger from fire. He collaborated with Mr Braidwood, the superintendent of the London Fire Brigade, and submitted a paper that amazed the commissioners and caused them to order 9- inch fire hydrants all round the building, and to have the Sappers trained to fight a fire. As already mentioned, Col. Reid brought in the Royal Engineers, or 'Sappers', to help with the organisation. The officers worked on the details of planning and the ordinary ranks acted as porters when the exhibits began to arrive, as well as serving as firemen. In command was Capt. Henry Cunliffe Owen, who worked with Cole on the allocation of space to the exhibitors. The Royal Commission was able to hold its first meeting inside the Crystal Palace on 5 December 1850. Afterwards, the prince gave 250 gallons of beer to the men. This piece of public relations was reported and illustrated in the weekly newspapers. One reported that, as the brewer's dray arrived, 'Hurrahs were vigourously [sic] given, to the manifest pleasure of the Prince, who repeatedly acknowledged them'.118 Notwithstanding the benefits such publicity could bring, the royal commissioners were wary of the press. Lord Granville was the only one who wanted them to be allowed into the building. It was not until February 1851 that an artist from the Illustrated London News was allowed in officially. In The Times account of the building work given above, it is pointedly mentioned that the report is written from the outside of the building. When it was proposed that the press should not be admitted free, but only allowed in if they paid like everyone else, Cole ‘entreated Commissioners not to make so fatal and suicidal a rule'. He had to explain that it was in the interests of the exhibition ‘to attract the Press to come and report on everything that was going on as much as possible'. He carried his point and Dilke wonderingly commented: 'You get your way when you are in a minority of one'.119 Cole, with so many campaigns behind him, fully appreciated the usefulness of the press and the damage that it would do if thwarted. It fell to Cole to decide how much space each exhibitor should have, and where the exhibits should be placed in the building. The prince's broad classification scheme had already been considerably revised by Playfair. Now Cole's dispositions were a further departure from the prince's ideas. Lord Granville agreed to Cole's plan on 3 December, but ‘said the Prince would suspect him of conspiring against him'. The prince made a last stand for his ideas at a meeting on 16 December, but ruefully accepted Cole's plan. Half the building was reserved for Britain and its colonies, and here exhibits were grouped according to Playfair's 3o classes. The other half was allotted to foreign goods. It was impossible to intermingle all these contributions so as to observe the 3o-part classification, so each country was allowed to organise its own display. Space was allotted to countries in proportion to their trade with Britain. So France was given most space, though it was very late in applying for it. It must have been Cole's sheer joy in practical problem solving that caused him to angle for the very testing task of allocating space. Despite his inexhaustible ingenuity, it must have been frustrating sometimes. In his introduction to the official catalogue he ventured to let off a little steam about the impracticality of others, lamenting that the terms 'superficial' and 'square'feet were often misunderstood, and'the expressions "horizontal" and "vertical" space disregarded or confounded.' One exhibitor mistakenly requested '27,ooo superficial feet - to be arranged in such a way as would occupy half the length of the whole Exhibition!' However, Cole sorted out all these problems, and gracefully acknowledged that 'whilst almost every exhibitor desired some kind of special arrangement, convenient to himself, but inconvenient to everybody else, almost every one submitted to a curtailment of space, and a constraint on his wishes, with a patience that greatly lightened the labours of the Executive Committee.'120 No doubt the commissioners were glad to leave such brain-teasing problems to Cole. We can imagine him, when the exhibits began to arrive, bouncing around in his spectacles and top hat (plate 32), and literally putting the exhibitors in their places. The difficult ones he would pass on to Col. Reid, who would turn his long, earnest countenance upon them and soothe them with his slow calm. It was said of Reid: 'It was curious to see the enraged and frantic exhibitor (the foreigner particularly) swearing at the injustice and favouritism which had consigned his article to some obscure corner or some bad light . . . pass into the presence of the Chairman of the Executive Committee, and presently emerge all cheerfulness and contentment'.121 On 12 February the exhibits began to arrive. Cole insisted on only one entrance to the building by which goods could enter, which helped security and reduced the number of police needed. At first Richard Mayne, the commissioner of the Metropolitan Police, wanted to employ 7 5o constables to guard the building, but after a meeting in December he was persuaded to reduce the number to 400. The royal family visited the building frequently. On 18 February 1851, the Queen took her five children to see it, and acknowledged that it ‘really now is one of the wonders of the world . ... The sun shining in through the Transept gave a fairy-like appearance. The building is so light and graceful, in spite of its immense size. . . . We were again cheered loudly by the 2,ooo workmen, as we came away. It made me feel proud and happy.'122 Fox, however, was to complain that the royal family ‘excited the workmen and exhibitors so much that each visit cost him £2o in loss of time'.123 Owen Jones designed a colour scheme, parti-colouring', which would make clear the articulation of the building, and provide a receptive background for exhibits. He used the primary colours of blue, red, and yellow in such relative proportions as to neutralize or destroy each other. Thus no one colour will be dominant or fatigue the eye.' To avoid colours clashing, or tainting each other, he interpose[d] a line of white between them, which will soften them & give them their true value'.124 At the end of February, and with eight weeks to go to the opening and goods pouring in by the cartload, it seemed unlikely that this time-consuming scheme would be completed by the I May. Fox Henderson put in more scaffolding and extra painters, but in mid-April Cole had to threaten Fox that he would order the Sappers to take away the scaffolding. It was taken down in the nick of time. Cole and Fox were able to remain on friendly terms, despite Cole's bullying, as is witnessed by the fact that Fox took Cole in his carriage on their way to a dinner with Lord Carlisle on 12 April. As the exhibition was due to open on i May, it was high time to make arrangements for an official ceremony. On 15 April, Col. Phipps told Cole that the Queen would grace the exhibition with a state opening, but that this must take place in private. She had been attacked twice about a year before, and her fear of crowds persisted. The next morning, Cole took his introduction to the catalogue to show the prince (who thought it too long), and tried to persuade him to let the public in at the opening. The next day, he was at the building before seven, and found Richard Cobden, William Cubitt and Thomas Bazley all voicing disapproval of the proposed private ceremony. On 19 April, Easter Saturday, there was nonetheless a meeting in the building to make arrangements for a private opening. Under pressure from Cole and his allies, Lord Granville went straight from the meeting to Windsor, and with difficulty persuaded the prince to agree to a public ceremony. Granville gave the news to Cole when they met at the exhibition on Sunday. On the Monday the prince came to the building early with his programme for the opening ceremony, which he read aloud to Col. Reid and Cole. Practical arrangements fell to Cole. It was decided to admit season-ticket holders to the opening, with the result, naturally, that the sale of season-tickets soared. On 3o April, Cole arrived at the building by 6.oo a.m., and discussed details with Col. Reid and Lord Granville. The Queen, with the Prussian royal family, came for a last private visit and spoke to Cole. She wrote in her journal that 'my poor Albert is terribly fagged'. Problems and difficulties were constantly referred to him, she noticed, ‘all of which my beloved takes with the greatest quiet and good temper'.125 No doubt Cole, Reid, Dilke, Playfair and their staff were equally fagged. At two o'clock the building was cleared of workmen and exhibitors, so that the final arrangements could be made for the great occasion on the following day. The opening ceremony did not go as smoothly as Cole had planned. The police proved to have been badly instructed, and did not know the nave from the gallery. Special places had not been reserved for the commissioners. Crowds pressed towards the throne where the centre of the ceremony would be, and had to be held back by Captain Owen and his Sappers. Excepting this everything went off remarkably well,'wrote Cole, and next day he received 'universal Congratulations on the success of the opening'. He was constantly in the building, early and late, during the next few weeks, perhaps irresistibly drawn to a blaze of glory that in many ways was a personal triumph. $$ If Cole now paused to contemplate his achievement, he would have reflected that first and foremost, the Great Exhibition was a great spectacle. The building - vast but strangely insubstantial, sparkling among the greenery of Hyde Park - was a spectacle in itself. A blazing arch of lucid glass / Leaps like a fountain from the grass / to meet the sun!' versified his friend, Thackeray.126 Inside, against Owen Jones's subtly variegated colour scheme, crimson drapes provided a glowing background for unimaginably diverse exhibits. Many were household goods: in the days before department stores no-one was accustomed to see so much of so much, all together. Other exhibits were huge. Apart from the machinery section, where cogs and cranks clattered away under the impulse of steam power, the main aisles of the building were crammed with giant objects ranging from a colossal bronze lion from Bavaria, through prodigious porphyry vases, fountains, lighthouses and telescopes, to trophies of Canadian timber and American rubber, intermingled with statuary. Thackeray again, writing as a comic Irishman: 'There's statues bright / Of marble white / Of silver and of copper; / And some in zinc, / And some, I think, / That isn't over proper.'127 The bishops wanted the improper statues to be covered up, but they remained on view, unashamed.128 Visitors could see manufactured articles from advanced western countries, which, though astoundingly profuse, were fundamentally familiar; they could also, perhaps for the first time, wonder at the exotic, even bizarre, artefacts from eastern and 'primitive' cultures. The exhibition was a window on the world, as well as a temple of consumerism. The credit for designing the architectural setting of the exhibition must go to Paxton and Owen Jones, with Digby Wyatt in the background. It was Cole who was responsible for the disposition of exhibits, so he too must take some credit for the brilliance of the spectacle. The exhibition was also a community festival on a hitherto unparalleled scale. It was the stated intention of the organisers that all classes should mingle in the Crystal Palace. Although the Royal Commission's working classes committee, on which Cole served alongside Dickens, Thackeray, the bishop of Oxford and three Chartists, soon became disillusioned and dissolved itself,129 there were inducements to the working classes to attend, especially in the shape of graduated admission charges. Top people were encouraged to take season-tickets, which secured them privileged admission at all times. From the opening day for about a month the admission charges were high enough to ensure that most of the visitors were from the comfortable classes. But from 26 May onwards there was cheap entry at one shilling, on Mondays to Thursdays, and working people came in crowds. There were fears that they would misbehave, but good humour and propriety prevailed. Many common people' came on works' outings sponsored by their employers, or on specially subsidised trains, but some at their own cost and under their own propulsion. An 84-year-old woman walked all the way from Penzance. The organisers conscientiously tried to supply the services necessary for thousands of visitors. There were three restaurants (carefully collected statistics reveal that 934,69x bath-buns were consumed),130 public lavatories were provided, fire precautions were taken, lost property was carefully collected. ‘One mother did lose her child, but the first policeman she applied to set her mind at rest, assuring her that the little fellow would be certainly taken in charge by the police, and be deposited, along with the lost handkerchiefs and brooches, at the police-station opposite the south entrance.'131 Among the organisers, Cole was clearly the most eager to give all visitors a good time. Cole was delighted with the success of the public lavatories, for the use of which a charge of a penny or a halfpenny was made, with the result that they covered their costs. Why not, he thought, extend this new facility throughout London? So he took 'the W.C. question'132 to the Society of Arts, which promptly, on 14 May, formed a committee, consisting of Cole, Dilke, Lord Granville, Lord Carlisle and S. M. Peto, to establish ‘some model Water Closets on the self-supporting principle'. Next day Cole drafted a scheme, and a few public conveniences' were set up in London, only to prove acomplete failure'.133 A third important feature of the Great Exhibition was its political significance. It was the first truly international festival.All Nations' seems to have become a catchword. When two foreign visitors came to blows, urchins gathered round shouting ‘Go it, All Nations!'134 Apart from such little incidents, the nations met in amity, and world peace was fostered. The nations did not meet, however, in equality. What made a nation estimable in the exhibition's terms was its success as an advanced capitalist, industrial society. So nations such as Britain and France, with their profusion of consumer goods and industrial products, were the leaders. Nations such as Spain or Canada, which could yield up abundant raw materials but were not yet far advanced in making things out of them, cut a lesser figure, as did places like Switzerland or the Scandinavian countries, which exhibited peasant handicrafts as prominently as industrial products. Modern commentators have often felt uncomfortable over the position of indigenous populations of the southern hemisphere'.135 Their displays were at any rate prominent, since they were placed around the edge of the central transept, presumably because of their exotic appeal. But it is argued that they were presented as ripe for exploitation by greedy colonialists. This is to some extent true. The general catalogue's introductory comment on the section devoted to ‘British Possessions in Australasia', for instance, points out that 'many of the objects are of great importance to the merchant seeking a new source of known materials'. But it also calls attention to interesting collections of native produce'. The comment on British Guiana becomes quite eloquent about such indigenous objects, praising that neat but rude and simple industry which, with little or no elaboration of the raw material, produces implements and ornaments from the most convenient substances vielded by nature'. The argument over whether the Great Exhibition was an imperialist racket will continue. Cole's view on the matter was that the coverage of the colonies was ‘imperfect'; consequently, 'the idea suggested itself to me that we wd have a Colonial Exhibition in a few years. Dilke agreed . . . & Reid said "the idea was worthy of you"'.136 Cole had an opportunity to expound a political interpretation of the Great Exhibition in a lecture On the International Results of the Exhibition of 1 8 5I', given at the Society of Arts on 1 December 1852, as the last of a series on the exhibition, suggested by Prince Albert. This lecture was written at speed in the closing days of November 1852. It has been pointed out that it draws heavily on Bentham's 'Plan for a Universal and Perpetual Peace', an essay in his Principles of International Law,137 but this indebtedness does not make the lecture any the less interesting as Cole's most sustained effort at a political credo, along radical, Cobdenite lines. He urges that culture can be a more successful internationally unifying force than conventional governmental diplomacy. 'The men of Art, Science, and Commerce, have hitherto had but a very subordinate voice in the regulation of their own interests, which had been too much left to the professional superintendence of their brethren of Politics, Law, and War.'He believes that such events as the exhibition can 'keep nations from going to blows as hastily and foolishly as they have been accustomed to do'.138 And he goes on to advocate a raft of beneficent measures that might be furthered through international co-operation. Already, he points out, first steps have been taken towards standard international postage rates and patent laws, harmonisation of quarantine regulations, and subsidised cultural exchanges. The time is now ripe for harmonising international commercial law; weights, measures and coinage; customs administration and passports; and copyright law. And at the conclusion he veers off into a vision of international bibliographical control for libraries, urging the British Museum Library to lead the way in compiling a 'Universal Catalogue'to which each nation would contribute bibliographical records of its own publications, thus avoiding much duplication of work. This idea was to re-surface in his project for a universal catalogue of art books in the 186os. This lecture shows that Cole was able to see the exhibition in the widest of contexts. But his most intense reaction to it came in the narrower context of design reform. It came home to him and others that the exhibition showed that, while British goods were best so far as quality and price were concerned, they were certainly not the most beautiful in the world. His already existing conviction that British design must be improved was confirmed by the exhibition, and the repercussions of the exhibition were to shape his future life. Although, in the summer of 1851, his way ahead was not yet clear, he must have felt that he had successfully climbed the ladder of success. The strained relations between the grand commissioners and the middle-class activists were now smoothed over, and Cole had arrived as a person of repute.
