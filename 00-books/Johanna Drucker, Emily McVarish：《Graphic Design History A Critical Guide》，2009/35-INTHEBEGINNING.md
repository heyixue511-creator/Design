Considered one of the masterpieces of turn-of-the-century fine press publishing, this work exhibits the spare elegance and striking boldness for which Doves Press came to be known. The placement of the text block and use of large, well-leaded type reveal the influence of fifteenth-century Italian printing. A single calligraphic initial completes the design in a gesture that is strikingly modern, the only decoration on an otherwise stark and well-formed page. Although conservative in nature, and deeply committed to an ideal of classical proportion and formal harmony that was at odds with the more experimental thinking of some of their contemporaries, Emery Walker and Thomas Cobden-Sanderson's vision was influential. The fine press movement was one expression of the design renaissance fostered by Arts and Crafts. A constant interchange between artistic movements, private press publications, and trade book design marked the late nineteenth century. In England, Thomas Cobden-Sanderson and Emery Walker assumed a rigorous control over print and design processes at the Doves Press, using only the best materials and finest workmanship and continuing a commitment to the "book beautiful" that had inspired Morris's Kelmscott Press. Walker had helped Morris base his typeface designs on printing samples from the incunabula period. In their designs, Walker and Cobden-Sanderson were also inspired by the well-proportioned pages, exquisite printing, and elegant type designs of Venetian printers. Unlike Morris, they were not intent on a return to Medieval or pre-Renaissance styles. Instead, they distilled modern designs from examples of early printing types. At the turn of the century, the fine press movement affirmed a distinctive aesthetic that was as easily applied to trade publishing as it was beautifully realized by private press productions (Fig. 8.19). 8.20a Bruce Rogers, The Centaur, 1915. Bruce Rogers was hired by the Riverside Press, a branch of the publishing firm Houghton Mifflin, and put in charge of their deluxe publications. Working in the spirit of Kelmscott, the Doves Press, and others, Rogers produced designs for Cambridge University Press and Harvard University Press as well. Had Rogers limited himself to fine press work, his influence would have been lesser. Rogers's enormous talent for type design made him an asset to the printing industry. He worked with the Lanston Monotype foundry, among others, to design faces that adapted the principles of the great Renaissance designers to meet the demands of mass production. Rogers's influence was far greater than his fame. His book designs set industry standards but did not always bear his name. Likewise, many of his type designs were widely and almost anonymously circulated in the commercial world. Belgian-born Henry van de Velde was a multitalented designer who rose to prominence through his interior and furniture designs for the Art Nouveau gallery. Van de Velde also did graphic work. The idea of "modern" design spread through publications and exhibitions and through the private and state-sponsored workshops of German, Belgian, and Dutch designers. These workshops developed an interest in extracting fundamental modern elements from the grasp of what had become too-familiar decorative modes. Art Nouveau had been banalized by popular consumption. Functionalism was increasingly the watchword among forward-thinking practitioners. With it came a conviction that graphic design should take industrial methods into formal account (Fig. 8.21). One experiment in institutionalizing the integration of life and work, art and its applications, took place in Darmstadt, Germany, where a statesponsored artists' colony was formed. One of the first people to be invited to join the colony by the Grand Duke who founded it was architect and designer Peter Behrens. When his talents were linked to the industrial giant, AEG (Allgemeine Elektricitäts-Gesellschaft or General Electric Company) in 1907, he was poised to become a new type of designer.
