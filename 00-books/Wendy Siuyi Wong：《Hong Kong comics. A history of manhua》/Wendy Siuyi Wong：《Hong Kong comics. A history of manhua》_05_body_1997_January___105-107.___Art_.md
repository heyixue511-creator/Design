Fan Dou Collection: Record Book of Transition Period, 1996

This book is a collection of more than 100 editorial cartoons by Ma Long. ## 108–111.

## A Biography of Mr. Legislator, 1997 Art/Script: Zunzi

Mr. Legislator was another well-received cartoon character created by Zunzi and was based on the common features of a legislator in Hong Kong. This cartoon panel has been published in Ming Pao since 1993. T'he book is a collection of the works published from June 1993 to March 1997. The editorial format of the collection is presented as a daily planner including the dates when the work was published.

## 112–115.

## Special Administrative Region Manhua Magazine, 1999 Éditor: Ma Long

This manhua magazine, published quarterly, is funded by the Arts Development Council with the objective of assisting nonmainstream cartoon development in Hong Kong. Ma Long is the editor as well as the major contributing artist. He created a cartoon character, Mr. CEO Tung, for the three-panel cartoon strip. The character design is based on Mr. Tung Cheehwa, the Chief Executive of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region. Other editorial cartoons by Ma can also be found in this 160-page magazine. Other occasional contributing artists include Wang King, Tou Sam, Local Boy, Tin Pak, and Ah Ching.

![](images/3f92e47714c99a8b1acf4a7cf6112cad1a94232e598f5b5ed7dcc316ac7cd041.jpg)

the art of humor: comical manhua

For decades, humorous manhua made up one of the major genres of Hong Kong comics. Before the Second World War, most comical manhua came in newspapers, magazines, and comic books printed in Shanghai and Guangzhou, on the mainland. Since humor tends to be local, comical manhua from Guangzhou was more popular in Hong Kong, because Guangzhou is closer to Hong Kong, and the cities sharè a regional dialect. One of the most influential Guangzhou comical manhua was The Sketch (Bun-gok Manhua), which first appeared in 1929. Its style of line drawings and text balloons, with the text in a colloquial dialect used by the lower classes, was typical of the manhua produced in southern China.

This southern manhua style, as drawn by Ye Yin-quan, Lee Fan-fu, and other artists, had great influence on the comics created in Hong Kong after the war. The work of Hong Kong artist Yuan Bou-wan, including the famous characters Kiddy Cheung and Miss Lau, followed the Guangzhou style, as did other works of the era, such as Tou Jowai’s Man in Suit and Sze-to Dip’s Sporty Aunt. During the 1950s, the most common and popular venue for manhua was the daily newspaper; after enough strips had run, they would be collected and reprinted in an individual comic book.

In the mid-1950s, Guangzhou comics began to show a distinct, American influence, which soon spread to Hong Kong. One of the first local manhua magazines produced in a Western style was Manhua World, which debuted in 1956. The magazine not only provided lighthearted entertainment, but it also nurtured promising local manhua artists. Manhua World made comical manhua popular just as the population of Hong Kong boomed. The popularity of these comics spread to other genres, too, which helped boost the fortunes of titles such as Uncle Choi

In the early 1960s, humorous manhua began appearing in poorly produced, tabloid-size newspapers known as “penny papers." The first and most successful of these was named Caricature Weekly, though it proved so popular that it soon became a daily. Caricature Weekly also inspired a number of imitators, including New Comics Weekly, Entertainment News, Epoch Comic Weekly, Manhua Daily, Companion Manhua, The Cartoon and Comics Garden, and Radar Pictorial.

The best-known comical manhua character is Old Master Q, who resembles Mr. Wang, the first cartoon character in Chinese history. Old Master Q is still in print and attracts readers of al ages. Another classic comical manhua character——and one geared toward women—is Miss Thirteen Dot, who appeared in Lee Wai-chun's 13-Dot Cartoons. The female Chinese equivalent of the American Richie Rich, this millionaire's daughter used her family's money to finance a diverse array of adventures. As the 1960s wore on, a split developed in comical manhua, with traditional Mr. Wang-type characters on one side and modern characters such as Miss Thirteen Dot on the other. Works in the more modern vein included Careless Young Master, by Ng Sheng-gwan, and Handsome Dak, Miss Silly, and Young Couple by Hui Guan-man.

After Hong Kong's first television station began broadcasting in 1967, the role of comics as an entertainment form began to decline. In order to survive, many comical manhua introduced themes and stories influenced by and directly copied from television programs. The early 1970s also saw the rise of kung fu manhua, which soon dominated the market. There were some alternatives to TV-related and kung fu manhua; the most popular of these were Wong Sze-ma's comics, especially Ngau-chai.

In the 1980s, several kung fu comics artists crossed over into comical manhua. Wong Yuk-long’s Yuk-long Manhua Biweekly, for example, tried to capture a share of the funny comics market. While kung fu manhua continued to dominate the market into the 1990s, many artists did publish small editions of comical manhua. This small market was represented by Manhua Hard and Soft Angel Theater, by Ho Kin-chung, One Woman, Three Markets, by Chan Ya, and others, produced by such editorial cartoonists as Ma Long and Apink.

In the mid-1990s, a new generation of artists began creating graphic novels that were funny, although they were different from traditional comical manhua. These alternative manhua included works by Lai Tat-wing and Au-yeung Craig. Lau Lee-lee’s selfdescribed feminist manhua took the comical genre one step further, providing a humorous critique of serious issues such as gender inequality and political power struggles in Hong Kong. These innovative works paved the way for the alternative manhua artists who were active in the final years of the century.

## 1-8.

## The Sketch (Bun-gok Manhua), 1929 Editor: Ye Yin-quan

A year after China’s first cartoon magazine, Shanghai Sketch, was published, this competitor appeared in Guangzhou. Six issues of The Sketch were published each month; twelve issues made up a single volume. Although published in Guangzhou, it was widely available in Hong Kong, where it had been printed during its first six months. The main attraction of the early volume of The Sketch were the stories of Brother Ho and Mister Tai-shi, by Ye Yin-quan. Beginning with volume four, the cover was printed in full color, and the magazine took on a more cynical tone. From this point on, there were two types of content inside: First, there were realistic stories of Guangzhou life, including scenes of markets, pawnshops, funerals, and even singing on the street. Second, there was the more risqué side of The Sketch; pictures of women were used to attract readers. The Sketch was targeted at readers in southern China, which meant its style was quite different from Shanghai manhua. Its main contributors were Ye Yin-quan, Lee Fan-fu, Li Maoji, and Huang Meng-tian.

## 9-12.

## Modern Miscellany, 1930

## Art: Various artists

The bulk of this magazine was devoted to news, society and tourist photographs, and other editorial features. However, manhua were very important to its popularity. Each issue's inside back cover carried a twelve-panel comic strip, Ye Qian-yu's Mr. Wang. (Mr. Wang was the first Chinese manhua character; he originally appeared in Shanghai Sketch.) Modern Miscellany also included regular contributions by Zhang Guang-yu, Zhang Zhen-yu, Huang Wennong, and Shao Xun-mei.

## 13.

Chau Yat-ching, 1946 Art/Script: Yu Ming

Chau Yat-ching was one of the first manhua published in Hong Kong. The main character, Chau Yat-ching, came from a wealthy family, but his life consisted of a series of humorous blunders. Chau also represented the modern young man, wearing Western suits rather than traditional Chinese clothes.

## 14.

Fong Tong-geng Teases Chan Mung-gat, 1946 Art/Script: Ng Fa-pang

This early comic followed the two title characters' competitive and collaborative adventures. Their costumes and bald heads dated from the Qing dynasty.

## 15–19.

Wu-lung Wong (King of Blunders), 1946 Art/Script: Lui Yu-tin

Wu-lung Wong first appeared in 1946 in Ghuangzhou's Chum Daily newspaper, and it soon became one of the most popular serial comics in southern China. The story, about life in postwar Guangzhou, criticized the unfairness of society and the incompetence and ugliness of Nationalist Party officials. After it appeared in the newspaper, Wu-lung Wong comics were collected in book form; sometimes several issues would be released in a month. Circulation soon jumped to 50,000 to 60,000 an issue, and distribution widened to include Hong Kong, Macau, and other locations in the Guangzhou province. After the 1949 Communist takeover of mainland China, Wu Lung-wong continued to be published in the Hong Kong newspaper Chung Sing Daily. From the comic’s position on the front page of the paper, it is clear that the series was very popular. It was also later made into a film.

## 20–21.

Man in Suit, 1947

Art/Script: Tou Jo-wai

The main character of this comic always wore a Western-style suit, which symbolized the “modern man" in late-1940s Hong Kong. The comic was a sarcastic critique of the well-dressed man, who took advantage of his friends and played around.

## 22.

Sporty Aunt, 1947

Art/Script: Sze-to Dip

Created in 1938 as a newspaper strip, Sporty Aunt related the romantic affairs of the pinup girl Third Auntie, the beautiful Ah-ling, Director Sheung, and Uncle Chan. The strips were collected in this book after the war.

## 23–24.

Hot-tempered Leung, 1947 Art/Script: Yuan Bou-wan

This title was published before World War II as a newspaper column by Yan Wu-fa in Red Green Daily, then made into a movie by Yu Leung. In 1947, Yuan drew this two-volume comic book from the movie version. The story deals with the everyday lives of ordinary people; colloquial language is used throughout.

## 25–31.

Kiddy Cheung, 1947

Art/Script: Yuan Bou-wan

The character Kiddy Cheung was created by Yuan Bou-wan for a daily newspaper comic before the war and continued to be popular afterwards. Yuan used the child character to critique the adult world and reflect on the unfairness of society. In 1950, this manhua was made into a movie with Bruce Lee as Kiddy Cheung, his first film appearance. The movie was a hit and inspired more strips in a daily newspaper. These strips were reprinted in 1952 in the book Kiddy Cheung Extension

32–35.

Miss Lau, 1948

Art/Script: Yuan Bou-wan

Miss Lau was as popular as Yuan’s other comics, Kiddy Cheung and Arrogant Chiu. The story follows a pretty maid named Miss Lau and the men who pursue her. Yuan had close ties to the Hong Kong film industry, and Miss Lau became the basis of a 1949 movie that broke box office records when it premiered.

## 35.

Arrogant Chiu, 1948

Art/Script: Yuan Bou-wan

The character Sergeant Chiu first appeared in Red Green Daily, and the work was later published in booklet form. The comic book seen here, Arrogant Chiu Meets Four-eyes Choi, is full of humorous stories and lighthearted incidents.

## 36.

Wong Chai, 1949

Art/Script: Yuan Bou-wan

This Yuan Bou-wan comic follows Wong Chai, a spineless young man who cowers before his wealthy uncle. The story is funny—usually showing Wong Chai entertaining his stepmother and other female relatives——yet it also emphasized the gap between Hong Kong's upper and lower classes.

38-47.

Manhua World, 1956

Editor: Mak Ching

Manhua World, a sixteen-page monthly magazine, was typical of popular mid-1950s publications. It contained a variety of features, including short stories, songs, and photos, as well as comics drawn by Cheng Ka-chun, Lee Fan-fu, Lee Ling-hon, Chan Chi-dor, and others. In general, the manhua in this magazine were influenced by American comics, yet some artists maintained a Chinese flavor. Each issue's cover explored a different format or kind of story. In addition to publishing well-known manhua artists, the magazine contained work by rising stars such as Wong Chak, Wong Sze-ma, Wu Shu-yu, Hui Guan-man, Heung-shan A-wong, Mak Ching, and Sung Samlong, all of whom later published individual comic books. Manhua World ran for almost eight years, with about seventeen issues published annually.

## 56.

## Caricature Weekly, 1961

## Editor: Lee Ling-hon

The same artists who had made the best-selling Manhua World created this tabloid-size weekly. Known as a “penny paper” (because it cost one cent), it became one of most successful comics of its time. Its eight pages contained mainly illustrations and comics, some in single panels and some in four-panel strips, as well as some fiction and a page of works by students. Caricature Weekly proved so popular that it began to publish every day, as Caricature Daily.

## 57.

## New Comics Weekly, 1962

## Editor: Tsui Ngai

This weekly penny paper was modeled after Caricature Weekly but was drawn by different artists, including Lee Yu, Lui Yu-tin, and Sung Sam-lung. Its contents were similar—cartoons, serial comics, and entertainment news——and like its predecessor, it quickly became a daily. The most popular strips in New Comics were Little Couple, by Lee Yu, Wu-lung Wong, by Lui Yu-tin, and Uncle Fatty, by Sung Samlung.

## 58–61.

## Entertainment News, 1963

## Editor: Fung Tai-wai

This two-color tabloid-size paper sold for ten cent and essentially started “penny paper fever” in Hong Kong. Among its eight pages was a gallery of Western (mainly American) comics, which were simply reprinted with the text in Chinese, as well as local comic strips and columns. Entertainment News also encouraged readers to submit their own comics——-and paid for them—which added to the paper's popularity. A supplement to the paper, Cartoon Daily, was published in 1963; its main appeal was adventure comics. Although artists were only paid about fifty cents per comic, the paper's insatiable demand for art—enough to fill four pages every day—kept many of them in business. The publisher supplied the story, and artists only had to draw the illustrations. Although the quality of the comics was poor, the constantly changing narratives kept readers buying the paper. The comics were later reprinted in a four-by-six-inch book that sold for fifty cents.

## 62.

## Epoch Comic Weekly, 1962

## Editor: Leung Tak-wah

This penny paper followed the typical pattern: eight pages oflighthearted and humorous stories, many with uplifting themes designed to help readers temporarily escape their problems. It became a daily paper in 1965.

## 63-64.

## Manhua Daily, 1963

## Editor: Mak Ching

This eight-page penny paper included work by local artists and one page of translated Western cartoons. One of its main attractions was that the front and back pages were printed in color. Martial arts and adventure comics were inside, and single-panel cartoons filled up the remaining space.

## 65.

## Radar Pictorial, 1965

## Art: Mok Kwan-ngok and Man Yong

A popular Japanese samurai movie, Blind Knight, inspired local artists Mok Kwan-ngok and Man Yong to create this eight-page daily paper, which featured a ongoing sword fighting comic. Like the movie, it was a hit, although the demands of drawing eight pages a day meant that the quality was very crude. Still, it remained popular until the mid-1970s, making it one of the longest-running penny papers. Mok and Man started another penny paper, Ultraman Pictorial, an eight-page daily based on the Japanese manga of the same name. However, this paper was short-lived since the artist could not draw enough for two dailies at the same time.

## 66-67.

## Companion Manhua, 1963

## Editor: Unknown

This penny paper ran imitations of work by famous contemporary comic artists, including Hui Guanman, Chan Chi-dor, Mak Ching, Lee Ling-hon, Wong Chak, and Wong Sze-ma. However, it also helped create a new genre of manhua when it combined cartoon strips with adventure stories.

## 68-70.

## The Cartoon and Comics Garden, 1964

This penny paper was another eight-page tabloid with color covers. It included the work of Lee Singcheung, Ng Gei-ping, and the rising female artist Lee Wai-chun.

71-103.

Old Master Q, 1964

Art/Script: Wong Chak

Old Master Q is the most popular manhua character in Hong Kong as well as in Chinese communities all over the world. Almost four decades after the comic's debut, Old Master Q is still being published, each issue eagerly read by a worldwide audience. Old Master Q was even the subject of popular live-action and animated films in the 1960s and 1970s. The comic itself is a four-panel strip in which Old Master Q and his sidekicks (“Big Dumb” or “Big Potato” and Mr. Chun) get involved in many different kinds of adventures. Whether he's traveling through time, visiting Japan, or marrying one of many wives, Old Master Q frequently finds himself in trouble. The character of Old Master Q was greatly influenced by Ye Qian-yu’s Mr. Wang, who first appeared in 1928 in Shanghai Sketch. It was also possibly influenced by Peng Di’s Lao Fu Zi, who was popular in the 1930s and 1940s in mainland China.

## 181.

Ms. Carefree, 1974

## 182.

New Wave Manhua Magazine, 1980

Editor: Tony Chan

In response to the “new wave” of Hong Kong film, a group of young artists, including Ma Wing-shing, Chu Jo-yee, and Sham Lak-hung, published this magazine with the hopes of creating a new kind of manhua. The most popular comics of the time were violent, fighting manhua, so this magazine gave Hong Kong comics a new and fresh perspective.

## 183-185.

Siu-keung Manhua Collection, 1981

This collection, put together by Seung-gun Siu-keung and a group of young artists, includes Sau-sing Chai (Longevity Boy), by Seung-gun, and the comic-book version of Jackie Chan’s film Drunken Master, by Ma Wing-shing. It also published the work of many rising artists. Since most comic books were collections of strips that had appeared in penny papers, the cover stressed that the contents had never before been published. Covers also used imagery from popular films such as Alien to attract readers.

## 186.

Humor World, 1981

Editor: Yeung Wai-pong

The majority of this publication's contributors were newspaper or editorial cartoon artists, including Tao Sam, Yat Muk, Zunzi, Yan Ting-hau, Mok Wah, Au Ching, and Lee Tak-hong. Its content was mostly cartoon strips, almost all drawn in à single-line style.

## 187.

Manhua New Trend, 1981

Editor: Cheung Wai-kit

One of many short-lived humor magazines, this title combined four-panel strips and short stories. The cover of the first issue used caricatures of movie stars to try to appeal to new readers.

## Monthly Comic Magazine, 1990 Editor: Yuan Kin-toa

Although it claimed to be Hong Kong's first pure manhua magazine, it was actually the first serious critical magazine about manhua. It included a mix of reviews and commentary about Hong Kong manhua and reports on foreign comics. There were also features introducing new manhua artists such as Li Chitat, whose elaborately detailed drawings were praised by readers. ## 246–250.

Art/Script: Lai Tat-wing

With this work, Lai Tat-wing broke away from the manga that dominated comics in the 1990s to create something original. Even the title is fresh: the words form a sound without any real meaning. His strips left much to the reader's imagination. For example, the 160-page comic Dan-dan Ha, Go-go Nut contains no dialogue at all. Lai’s highly controlled line drawings and composition, and his originality and enthusiasm were well received by readers. Today it is considered one of the most original Hong Kong comics of the 1990s.

## 251–254.

## Three Times Seven Equals Twenty-One, 1995 Art/Script: Au-yeung Craig

This alternative comic was a mixture of illustrations and comics drawn in untraditional formats. Each page of the ten chapters contains just one frame; the frames are broken into action sequences to tell the story. The book was printed in both Chinese and English, reflecting the bilingual culture of Hong Kong. Auyeung later published more highly original work in the series My Own Private Heaven, which maintained the bilingual format while experimenting more with traditional comic forms.

## 255–256.

## Mom's Drawer Is at the Bottom, 1998 Art/Script: Lau Lee-lee

This self-described feminist manhua was the first of its kind in Hong Kong. The artist challenged social and cultural norms by engaging feminist humor, and outlining the relationships amoong gender, humor, and feminist social critique. The 228-page collection included such topics as sex and sexuality, sex roles, gender politics, life, media, home, the nation, and the earth. Lau, however, did not draw her manhua in an explicit way, nor did she directly attack patriarchal ideology.

spirits of kung fu: action manhua

To most non-Chinese speakers, kung fu is simply a martial art made popular by Bruce Lee. Yet kung fu refers not only to martial arts, or wu-shu in Chinese, but also to the common spirit shared by various styles of wu-shu. The kung fu genre of Hong Kong manhua thus includes not only stories of hand-to-hand martial arts, but also sword-fighting epics, traditional Chinese myths, gangster stories, science fiction, and tales of historic fighting in full period costume.

Until the 1950s, Hong Kong depended on Shanghai for its supply of lianhuantu, the traditional illustrated storybook. The format of lianhuantu, sequential line drawings with descriptive text at the top of the page, remained almost unchanged until the end of the Second World War. By the 1920s and 1930s, Hong Kong readers were already familiar with the martial arts lianhuantu published in Shanghai.

After the Communist revolution, when the supply of lianhuantu from Shanghai dried up, the British territory began to develop its own comics. Hui Guan-man's 1958 lianhuantu, Uncle Choi, not only broke new ground in format and style but also marked a new direction in Hong Kong comics toward a more accurate depiction of real life. Other works, such as the adventure story Boy Scout (1960), by Ng Gei-ping, followed Uncle Choi's lead.

Still, during most of the 1960s, the Hong Kong comics market was dominated by penny papers and humorous comic books. The action and adventure titles that were available were mainly copies of American superhero comics and Japanese science-fiction manga. That began to change in 1968, when the work of Wong Yuk-long appeared. Wong's Little Vagabond, published in that year, was immediately popular. Two years later, Wong established a new genre of fighting comics with Little Rascals (Siu Lau-man), a realistic story of young people living in the Hong Kong housing projects and fighting gangsters and criminals. The best-selling Little Rascals spawned many imitators; the main competition was Seung-gun Siu-bo's 1971 Lee Siu-lung (Bruce Lee's Chinese name). When the colonial government passed the Indecent Publication Law in 1975, banning explicit violence in comic books, Wong established the daily newspaper Sang Po to continue his work because only comic books, not daily papers, were covered by the law. He also changed the name of Little Rascals to Oriental Heroes (Lung Fu Mun).

By the end of the 1970s, Wong and Seung-gun were the two main artists producing fighting comics. Although several competing manhua newspapers provided venues for younger artists, theywere too inexperienced to be threats to Wong and Seung-gun. In the early 1980s, Seung-gun joined Wong's company, Jademan Comics, consolidating their positions in the marketplace. Their King Manhua Weekly, introduced in 1982, showcased Hong Kong's top manhua artists. Wong also added two martial arts titles to Jademan's catalog, Drunken Master (1982) and Buddha's Palm (1983). Included in the first issues of Drunken Master was another new series, Ma Wing-shing's The Chinese Hero (Chungwah Ying-hung). Ma's realistic style and superb drawings garnered the series immediate recognition; when it appeared separately in 1983, it marked the beginning of a new era in martial arts comics

The success of Wong's Jademan Comics helped establish Hong Kong's manhua business as a major industry. In 1986, the company was listed on the stock market, making it the first publicly traded comics firm. However, when the market crashed in October 1987, Jademan suffered greatly. In the following years, some of the company's most promising artists, including Fung Chi-ming, Lau Ding-gin, Ma Wing-shing, and Seung-gun, left to establish their own firms

In the late 1980s and early 1990s, fighting manhua became more diverse. Some of the outstanding works of the era, such as Tin Ha Pictorial, by Ma Wing-shing, and Dagger, Sword, Laugh, by Fung Chi-ming, upheld the traditions of the genre. However, several new types of fighting comics appeared, including the video game-derived Street Fighter stories, science fiction such as Li Chi-tat's Black Mask, and a gambling series called The Legend of the God of Gamblers. Wong Yuk-long also created a period-costume martial arts series, The Legend of Emperors, in 1993.

Gangster manhua remained popular, although some titles, like Teddy Bøy, introduced in 1992, came close to glorifying violence and vice. As competition between manhua publishers intensified, some works overstepped the acceptable boundaries of violent and sexual content. In response, the Hong Kong government amended the Indecent Publication Law, forcing publishers to seal explicit comics in plastic bags, attach warning notices on the covers, and limit their sale to adult readers. These regulations remain in effect.

After the Communists took control of mainland China in 1949, Hong Kong suffered a shortage of popular reading materials. The owners of the city's lianhuantu kiosks——where readers rented the illustrated books for a small fee—needed to find an alternative supply, which they did either by drawing their own or by hiring artists to draw them. As a result, many of the surviving comics from this era are missing their original covers.

Uncle Choi, 1958

Art/Script: Hui Guan-man

Uncle Choi (Choi Suk) is one of the most memorable characters of Hong Kong manhua. At first, Hui told Choi's story in a humorous manner, but the tone soon became more serious as he focused on the war with Japan and made Choi a war hero. Uncle Choi was the best-selling comic in Hong Kong for several years and continued publishing for more than a decade and a half. When it first appeared, Uncle Choi was considered a creative break from traditional lianhuantu: it used modern fashion, and its story was much closer to real life than those of classic comics. During its long run, its content was adjusted to fit readers’ evolving demands. When James Bond movies and Japanese spy manga became popular, for example, Uncle Choi too became a spy, with machine guns and lasers rather than traditional weapons. However, some of these changes were not well received by readers, and the comic began to decline. It finally ceased in the mid-1970s.

84-101.

Little Rascals, 1970

Art/Script: Wong Yuk-long

Wong Yuk-lung's most popular comic, Little Rascals, was translated literally to English from Siu Lau-man, and carried connotations of a little gangster with antisocial behavior. It was an original creation about a group of unemployed young hoodlums living in Hong Kong's public housing. The story revolves around Wong Siu-fu and his elder brother, Wong Siulung, whose one hair-covered eye brands him as “bad." He turns away from his criminal behavior, however, to help Wong Siu-fu fight evil. Little Rascals's stories emphasize brotherhood, and although the brothers are fighting, they are fighting for justice.

In the first issues, the fighting often involved chains, which led to spilled blood. Knives (and ropes, clubs, and daggers) appeared in later issues, and the enemies’ internal organs, guts, and bones were seen, often quite graphically. Little Rascals quickly acquired a large following, and although it had a positive message, its main attraction was violent action. Within a few years of its first appearance, many people in Hong Kong were criticizing the violence in Little Rascals and other fighting comics. The Indecent Publication Law of 1975 called for less-graphic violence. Depictions ofbloodshed remained common, but in response to the pressure, Wong changed the title to a more positive name, Oriental Heroes (Lung Fu Mun) and experimented with other venues.

102–103.

Sang Po, 1975

Publisher: Wong Chun-lung (Wong Yuk-long)

Sang Po was Wong's first daily publication. At first, the tabloid-size newspaper tried to imitate regular papers, covering entertainment, health, and news and featuring columns by famous writers such as James Wong, Sha Yung, Tong Mo-lee, and Fung Ka. Comics filled only three-and-a-half pages each day, with Wong's Oriental Heroes appearing in full color on the back cover. After a month, Sang Po switched to pure manhua.

## 104–119.

Oriental Heroes, 1975

Art/Script: Wong Yuk-long

When Wong gave the Little Rascals series its new title, he made a few other changes: the setting was moved to Japan; he printed a note saying that the story was fictional; and he explored ways to depict violence without explicit bloodshed. He relied on fire, water, poison, and other methods of violence. Wong's drawing technique also improved, especially in his depiction of muscles and the lines denoting action. Oriental Heroes was published every day in Sang Po and collected each week in book form.

In the mid-1980s, Wong modified his drawing style again, making it more serious and less comical in response to the success of Ma Wing-shing's realistic The Chinese Hero. After 1991, Oriental Heroes was drawn by other artists; it is still published today, with a total run of more than 1,000 issues.

## 120-134.

Lee Siu-lung (Bruce Lee), 1971

Art/Script: Seung-gun Siu-bo

This comic, which used Bruce Lee's Chinese name as its title, emphasizes the use of martial arts to fight against evil and for justice. The main character, Lee Siu-lung, was drawn with physical features similar to those of the real Bruce Lee. Other characters included Lee's follower, Sa-dam Chai, a young rebel with strong principles. Violent acts such as eye gouging were depicted graphically, although less explicitly than in Wong's Little Rascals. Still, after the 1975 restrictions were imposed, the artist was forced to publish the comic daily in the newspaper Hei Bo to maintain its audience.

Hei Bo, 1975

Publisher: Seung-gun Siu-bo

Entertainment news covered the front page of this newspaper; the remaining pages were filled with manhua. The manhua features were Lee Siu-lung, on the inside front page, and Sheng-gun Siu-wai's Brother of Dragon and Tiger, printed in full color on the back page. The newspaper lasted only two years, folding in early 1977.

The Chinese Hero (Chung-wah Ying-hung), 1983 Art/Script: Ma Wing-shing

When The Chinese Hero first appeared——as a supplement to Wong Yuk-long's Drunken Master, then as its own comic book—it made the artist, Ma Wing-shing, something of an instant legend, in part because of his rags-to-riches life story, but mainly because of the skill and style of the work. Ma's comic, which was considered a breakthrough at the time, had a realistic style, clear and detailed action scenes, engrossing plots, and skillful use of color. Ma's style was influenced by the Japanese manga Crying Killer, by the artist Ryoichi Ikegami.

The Chinese Hero tells the story of Wah Yinghung, a young man living happily with his parents and fiancée until one tragic night: a man murders his parents and tries to kill him, too. Both of his parents were experts in martial arts, and he inherited their skills. He escapes, marries his fiancée, and they have twins; eventually the story shifts its focus to Wah Yinghung and his children's fight against evil.

When The Chinese Hero appeared as a standalone comic, it was an immediate hit, selling some 45,000 copies. Over the next several years, the title's circulation averaged 60,000 and hit peaks of 200,000. Ma became the hottest manhua artist in Hong Kong, and the English version, The Blood Sword (later renamed Blood Sword Dynasty) was published and distributed in North America. In 1989, Ma left Wong's Jademan Comics; however, the company owned the title and continued to publish it under the direction of another artist, Yuan Ga-bo. This comic became a movie in 1999, called A Man Called Hero.

Sea Tiger I–III, 1994

Tang and Wan's Sea Tiger, considered one of the classic manhua of the 1990s, combines two genres: fighting and science fiction. The comic tells the story of a young man (nicknamed Sea Tiger) whose father was killed by the Dream Blue Organization. Over the course ofits three-year 144-issue run, readers followed his often violent battles with that organization, as well as romantic subplots.

![](images/0d9240418d16c5b61118e1641f916bd642115f78cc163a83cc43f2a810b17e11.jpg)

At the beginning of the twentieth century, children in Hong Kong could choose to read two kinds of things outside of school: illustrated stories that appeared in the local newspapers, either as columns or supplements, or illustrated story books imported from Shanghai. Before the Second World War, Shanghai was much more progressive than Hong Kong; in fact, it is considered the first modern city in China. Many of the cultural advances that came to China in the twentieth century appeared in Shanghai first. The first Chinese-language kids' magazine, Enlightenment Pictorial, for example, was published there in 1908. Compared to that of Shanghai, Hong Kong's media suffered from a lack of talent; the first locally produced children's magazine, New Children's Bimonthly Magazine, didn't appear until 1941. It was the most influential children's periodical in Hong Kong until the early 1950s, when the influx of refugees from mainland China increased demand for all kinds of reading materials. Unfortunately, the shortage of talent continued to affect the quality of local magazines.

Hong Kong's daily newspapers, including Wah Kiu Daily and Sing Tao Daily, began to produce original material geared toward young readers. Some small publishers also published picture books for kids, although most were reprinted prewar magazines or pirated Chinese-language versions of Western books and Disney cartoons. Palm-size picture books produced before the war—known as lianhuanhua shu or kungchai shu—were also available for rent from street kiosks. Until the 1950s, these were essentially the only sources for children's comics.

The appearance of Children's Paradise in 1953, edited by Law Guan-tsiu, changed all that and marked the beginning of the children's manhua industry in Hong Kong. The magazine's success not only inspired imitators, but it also helped nurture local manhua talent, which fueled the comics boom of the 1950s and 1960s. Television was not broadcast in Hong Kong, so reading was one of the main forms of entertainment for the city’s children. In the 1950s and 1960s, too, many public and private libraries were founded, along with public housing projects equipped with reading rooms, making children's reading materials more widely available.

After television was introduced in 1967, the production of local children's comics began to drop off. Japanese manga became more popular—a by-product in part of the Chinese-dubbed Japanese animation that appeared on TV. Pirated editions of manga soon appeared; Fujio Fujiko's Doraemon, published in Japan in 1970, was redrawn and printed without permission as Ding Dong in Children’'s Paradise in the mid-1970s. Television also inspired several non-manga comics, including Children's TV (1975), Happy Together Children’s Pictorial (1975), and Big Brother Sunny (1978).

In the 1980s and 1990s, Hong Kong's children's magazines struggled to stay afloat. The two longest-lived titles, Children's Paradise and Little Friends Pictorial, both folded in the 1990s. Several new magazines did emerge, such as Sing Tao Good Children’s Pictorial (1982), as did a short-lived children's newspaper, Children's Daily (1989), and some authorized Disney magazines. But kids’ comics have never again been as popular as they were in the 1950s and 1960s. In order to survive, publishers had to find ways of making money. Many of the original children's comics from this era, such as Little Ming Pao Weekly (1989), Whiz-Kids Express Weekly (1993), and Yellow Bus (1990), appeared as supplements to weekly publications. Of these, Yellow Bus was the most successful, although at the cost of almost constant adaptation to appeal to young readers’ shifting tastes.

Today, the market for children's comics in Hong Kong is dominated by Japanese manga; very little material is produced locally. Taking a wider view of the genre, one sees that kids’ reading materials were constantly influenced by sources outside Hong Kong: first by Shanghai manhua, then by American comics, and more recently by Japanese manga and anime. The brief era when original creations such as Children’s Paradise and Little Friends Pictorial dominated the market is now considered the golden age of Hong Kong children's manhua

## 1.

Children's Pictorials Bimonthly Magazine, 1923 This was one of the first children's magazines in China. Although it was published in Shanghai, it circulated in Hong Kong as well. Its content was mainly illustrations, but jokes, stories, activities, and games were also regular features.

## 2.

## Children's Magazine, 1932

Many early children's magazines published in Shanghai were circulated all over China in the 1930s. This type of magazine was the first example in the modern history of China of a magazine aimed at providing a well-rounded education outside the classroom.

## 3-6.

## Children's World Bimonthly, 1932

During the Japanese occupation and the Second World War, this long-running magazine moved from Shanghai to Changsha, and its content reflected wartime priorities. General information about the conflict was printed, as well as features on preserving country and family. Over a long career, Children's World Bimonthly included a more diverse array of content than previous children's magazines.

## 7-14.

## Young Children's Literature Collection, 1934

This Shanghai-based series included both text and illustrations covering health, society, and natural science, as well as arts and crafts projects and quizzes. Also featured were stories that included historical Chinese tales, Western fairy tales, and stories of everyday life. It lasted for more than 200 issues.

## 15.

## Good Children's Weekly: Children's Reading Materials, 1938

This magazine, begun at the outbreak of the Sino-Japanese War, was filled with anti-Japanese propaganda. Its purpose was to promote nationalism, and it urged children to support the war and eventually fight it, with cover stories such as “Children of China, Stand Up” and “I Want to Go Too, Papa.”

## 16-18.

## New Children's Bimonthly Magazine, 1941

This was Hong Kong's first literary variety magazine for children. Èdited by Wong Hing-wan and supported by university professors, it included work by such contemporary writers as Xu Di-shan, Fung Zikai, Yan Wen-jing, and Hu Bing-chun. After Hong Kong was occupied by Japan, the editorial staff moved to southern China, where they continued producing the magazine until 1944. The magazine returned to Hong Kong in 1946, where it published fifty more issues. In 1951, it moved back to China; it was renamed Young Pioneers in 1956.

## 19–24.

## World Famous Children's Book Series, 1951

This series of comic books borrowed heavily from Western children's stories, with titles such as Pinocchio, Dance Performance, and The Lone Ranger. In some cases the comics’ English name was used while others were translated into Chinese, as in Tsam-gao Adventures. Pinocchio's English name was removed completely; the Chinese version was titled Muk-nau Kiyu Kee (Wooden Puppet Adventures). However, the story and drawings closely resemble the original.

## 25.

## Wah Kiu Daily Supplement, 1951

After the Communists took control of mainland China, a great wave of immigrants hit Hong Kong. In response to the needs of a growing population (and it's children), some daily newspapers added columns and supplements geared toward kids. This column appeared in Wah Kiu Daily as a Sunday supplement. It featured fairy tales, stories about science, quizzes, and comic strips.

## 26.

## Children's Healthy Series Literature, 1952

In the early 1950s there were few talented local comics artists, and many Hong Kong comic books were simply direct copies of American examples. Many were reprints of the original, with the text translated into Chinese and references changed to local names and places. In other cases, American comics were redrawn. This comic book, The Christmas Gift, features a redrawn version of Felix the Cat. Neither the reprinted nor the redrawn comics give credit to the original sources.

## 27.

The Children's Educational Picture Book Collection, 1952

While many manhua publishers used American cartoons as their source, this series instead copied illustrated Western fairy tales. The collection included The Phantom of the Opera, The Snowman's Winter, Little Paradise, and Little Penguin Travels to the South. Although based on Western sources, readers were seldom informed of the original English titles; sometimes, local artists or editors received credit. These kinds of comic books were priced low enough for kids to buy them with candy money.

## 28-29.

## The Blessed Young Children Magazine, 1952

This magazine for elementary-school children was put out by a Christian educational publisher. It mainly printed Christian stories, particularly from the Bible, but also included nonreligious stories intended to promote morality. The magazine contained more text than most comic books; the pictures functioned primarily to support the story, reflecting the editorial bias toward the benefits of reading.

Children's Paradise, 1953

The first full-color magazine for children published in Hong Kong, Children’s Paradise ran for more than forty years, totaling 1,006 issues. Among the classic manhua characters created in Children's Paradise were Little Roundy, Big Ears, and Red Indian. The magazine also helped introduce manga to Hong Kong when it began running unauthorized copies of the Japanese comic Doraemon in the 1970s.

The success of Children's Paradise was largely due to the leadership of its editor, Law Guan-tsiu. He made sure that the magazine's drawings were always beautiful and appealing to children and that its stories were interesting. The content changed over the years, but the basic form and many of its features remained constant. Some of the perennial favorites were poems, quizzes, historical and mythological stories fairy tales, songs, and “living manhua." The center pages always featured colorful manhua. Many other Hong Kong children's magazines tried to copy the format and features of Children’s Paradise.

## 69–71.

Little Bobo, 1954

The target readers of Little Bobo were young children. The drawing style and cover illustrations were especially cute; the stories were wholesome and usually included a special feature, Story Time by Big Sister Wan (Wan was the editor of the comic). In 1959, Little Angeli and Little Bobo were combined into one comic, Youth Pictorial.

## 72-74.

Jungle Prince, 1954

This series was written primarily for boys and featured jungle adventure stories similar to that of Tarzan. The comic book itself used both front and back covers for comics; often, a second supplementary comic was featured on the back. These occasional titles included UU-Jet, Little Iron Man, and Rocket Man.

## 75–80.

Children's Lok-fung Bao, 1955

This magazine was read mainly by Catholic school students since it was one of the few comics approved and assigned by the schools. During the 1950s, most ofit consisted of translated American stories, although local content appeared later. It also included games, religion-oriented activities, information about topics such as botany and stamp collecting, and morality stories. In the 1960s, its main draw was the continuing story of The Life of the Members of the Lok-fung Club, as well as vivid comics drawn by Wong Chak, Cheung Hon-ming, Ng Ho-cheung, and others. Originally an eight-page, tabloid-size biweekly, it switched in the 1960s to booklet size; in the 1980s, it switched again, to a horizontal orientation, although it was still a booklet. With this last change, all of the the religious content was removed, and it became much like other children's illustrated magazines.

## Sing Tao Night Daily Pictorial Supplement, 1957

This Sunday newspaper supplement published translated American comics, work by Chan Chi-dor, Lee Ling-hon, Kwan Shan-mei, and Ding Gong, and a column, Good Children, by Wu Sin, who later headed the Sing Tao Newspaper Corporation. ## Children's Weekly, 1960

Children's Weekly was a quick best-seller, reaching a circulation of6,000 to 7,000 within a short time. ## 119–123.

Baby Comic, 1960

Baby Comic consisted of short stories and four-panel comic strips drawn by most of the manhua artists active at the time. Wong Chak's classic cartoon Master-Q (Lao Fuzi) was first published here; other stories included Caesar the Great, by Lee Sing-cheung, Tarzan, by Lee Kwok-yat, and Little Yellow Dragon, by Mak Ching.

## 124–132.

## Kid Paradise, 1961

This magazine was begun by a group of new artists, including Wong Chak, and Wu Shu-yu. Their freshness was a success, Kid Paradise was new and fashionable, and popular from the beginning. As the market's tastes changed, its style shifted from four-panel cartoon strips to longer stories. Like most manhua for children from this era, Kid Paradise’s visual style was greatly influenced by American cartoons, with round figures and smooth lines inspired by Disney.

## 133.

Children's Pictorial Story Series, 1961

Like most other children's magazines, this title published Western stories, and was usually half text and half pictures. The drawing style was heavily influenced by the West.
