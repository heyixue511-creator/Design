# THE WAREHOUSE PRINCETON, NEW JERSEY, UNITED STATES, 1984 MICHAEL GRAVES A RUNDOWN WAREHOUSE TRANSFORMED INTO A MODERN VERSION OF THE CLASSIC ITALIAN VILLA. When an architect designs for himself, it is generally less problematic than designing for a client. Certainly, nobody knows more about the specific needs and tastes of the potential occupant. But working for oneself can bring unexpected challenges. When MICHAEL GRAVES (also see under "Masters of Postmodern Design") found a dilapidated 1927 structure that had been built by Italian craftsmen working on the Princeton University campus, he was taken by its resemblance to a Tuscan barn and bought it for the bargain price of \$3o,ooo, expecting to do some fairly simple fixing up to make it livable. In the event, it took terı years and hundreds of thousands of dollars—but the result was an elegant home that combined Graves's postmodern aesthetic with the warmth of an Italian country villa. The inviting entrance courtyard was created by removing part of an exterior wall. In the rest of the two-story building, which he called The Warehouse, referring to its one-time role as a storage facility, Graves removed walls and added glass doors and steel casement windows, transforming a rabbit warren ![](images/d7a1983ae51a011e3e228f27eb27e88575c4dc039126b8c505533be6b0c42b26.jpg) ![](images/a4127bf74a917f86565aca31f90ab9d6a7e816d9edc41c09d8574962abeb6221.jpg) of small and mostly windowless rooms into airy, gracious spaces defined by barrel-vaulted ceilings and milk bottle-shaped columns. Working within the existing footprint, he created a floor plan that orients the house to receive maximum morning light. To create the effect of greater height, the ceilings were lowered around the edges, and the resulting center recesses were painted light blue. Soffits were installed around each room to house heating and cooling ducts and other mechanicals, avoiding the need to tear up the existing concrete floors. A two-story library, adjoining the living room has a greenhouse-style glass roof and floor-toceiling bookcases of PVC pipe faux-painted to imitate wood. The house is furnished with classic Biedermeier pieces, whose architectural qualities inspired Graves's own furniture designs. OPPOSITE Biedermeier furniture stars in the inviting living room.
