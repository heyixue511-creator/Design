# ED FELLA b.1938 DETROIT NATIVE WHOSE IMPROVISATIONALGRAPHICS MIX TYPE, PHOTOGRAPHY,DRAWING, AND COLLAGE. ![](images/5234ec95bb74d8b34b01fcb11d415e2c8b24518ad56a879c6ed50dae0bf7fa38.jpg) Fella spent three decades working as a commercial artist in his native Detroit before changing direction to a creative pursuit that had not existed when he entered the world of work. When he finally found his métier, he embarked on a teaching career that influenced a generation or more of graphic designers. Over almost half a century, Fella has produced a provocative body of work that helped propel the transition of graphic design from rigid Modernism to convention-flouting Postmodernism. "I'm a twentieth century postmodern designer, from the middle of the last century," he claims.2º Fella's original art training took place in high school, where he was taught the Bauhaus foundation method, an avant-garde aesthetic in an era when Beaux Arts was still dominant. Working as a commercial artist (the term graphic designer did not come into use until the i96os), he produced competent but conventional advertising and promotion pieces for a variety of clients, while experimenting on his own to develop a more personal, eclectic style that he could use to solicit freelance assignments. A voracious reader, he incorporated influences from historic documents and printing techniques into works he called "flyers." These were I1 × 17in. (28 × 43.2cm), two-sided mini-posters that folded into thirds like self-mailers. He used these as promotion pieces distributed to attendees at art events or lectures, and later to market his own work. A single flyer might incorporate photography, collage, and idiosyncratic mixtures of type fonts in an approach that took its cue from Deconstructivism but had his own quixotic tweak. Fella modified typefaces and combined elements from printed matter with drawing and hand-lettering, using photostats, photocopiers, and X-Acto knives to create effects that prefigured what would become common practice in the age of computers. Closer to art than graphic design, Fella's work is highly improvisational and visually arresting, but also more than meets the eye—hidden allusions and interjected sentence fragments mean that it requires close reading to be fully appreciated. ![](images/90eea7f694f96a8489081947a1183029b672c472c50a0518ff0b80a9fc96b114.jpg) By the late i97os, Fella's reputation was growing through exposure in design journals and print anthologies. He became part of the community of avant-garde designers who convened around Cranbrook Academy of Art in Bloomfield Hills, Michigan, visiting the school to show his work to students in KATHERINE MCCOY's graphic design classes. Finally leaving his commercial career at the age of forty-seven, he entered the school's MFA program to formalize his credentials and, he has said, to avoid empty-nest syndrome, as his children were leaving for college. After graduating in i987 Fella began teachirg at the prestigious California Institute of the Arts, and ultimately became more celebrated in his second career than he had been in his first. He continued to produce idiosyncratic posters, scrapbooks, and photography, winning recognition from colleagues, professional organizations, and major museums. He has retired, but remains Professor Emeritus at CalArts and has acquired a new following of young designers and design aficionados through his regular postings and chatty commentary on his website edfella-yestoday.com.
