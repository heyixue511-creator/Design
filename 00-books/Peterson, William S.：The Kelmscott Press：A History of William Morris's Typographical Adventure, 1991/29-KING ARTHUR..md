The Studio during the latter part of the decade sponsored periodic competitions for illustrated title-pages, and the winning pages were almost always more in the Beardsley vein than in a style of which Morris or Burne-Jones would have approved. There was another emerging typographical fashion in the 189os, most brilliantly represented in the skilfully designed books of Whistler, which emphasized asymmetric layout, minimal ornamentation, and mechanical methods of printing: the Aesthetic book' (as one scholar has called it)2° seems to owe almost nothing to Morris's example, except perhaps a shared preoccupation with minute and careful planning of the printed page. Some historians are persuaded that there would have been a great revival of printing in England at the end of the nineteenth century even without the Kelmscott Press; this overstates the case, since Morris went well beyond the achievements of artists like Beardsley and Whistler by reaffirming the primary importance of fine materials (paper, ink, bindings) and by asking searching questions about the structure of the book that never entered their minds. There is no doubt, however, that other views of bookmaking were clamouring to be heard at the time, and that in many instances people accepted only selected portions of the typographical gospel according to Morris. Bernard Shaw, for example, was strongly impressed by The House of the Wolfings and The Roots of the Mountains (Morris's two pre-Kelmscott Press experiments at the Chiswick Press), became a Caslon-addict, and for decades badgered his printers whenever they failed to produce the closely spaced, evenly textured page that Morris had advocated.²¹ Shaw never aspired to emulate the Kelmscott Press volumes, yet he had an intelligent grasp of Morris's typographical principles, which he attempted to follow while at the same time ignoring the private mannerisms of Morris's own books. Shaw's case exemplifies the difficulties in tracing the reputation and influence of the Kelmscott Press during the final decade of the century, for Morris's ideas (which seem distinctive enough in retrospect) about book-design mingled subtly and confusingly with the notions of other practitioners of bookmaking. Nor was there any unanimity of opinion about the Kelmscott Press, which evoked astonishingly varying responses from readers throughout the decade. The earliest comments on the Press that appeared in newspapers and magazines were on the whole very favourable, and even the printers' trade journals printed occasional enthusiastic notices about Morris's activities in Hammersmith. The death of Morris in 1896 and the closing of the Kelmscott Press in 1898 stimulated further public praise of his work as a printer. Herbert P. Horne offered a judicious and lengthy appraisal of Morris's achievement in the Saturday Review (24 October 1896), finding the key to his success in the ideal of good craftsmanship; the Library, in commenting on an exhibition of the Kelmscott Press books mounted at the British Museum in 1898, remarked that ‘the Kelmscott books may fitly take their place with those of Gutenberg and Schoeffer, of Zainer and Koberger, of Jenson and Wendelin of Speier, among the masterpieces of the printer's art'.22 A. W. Pollard, reviewing the same exhibition in the Guardian (8 June 1898), found encouraging evidence of the spread of Morris's ideals, for three great firms — the Chiswick Press, Messrs. Constable, and the Clarendon Press — have already begun to use richer founts of type than have been seen in English books since black-letter went out of fashion, and with admirable effect'. When Henry R. Plomer wrote A Short History of English Printing for the English Bookman's Library (19oo), the terminal date of his survey was, significantly, 1898, since he regarded the ‘brilliant but short-lived career of the Kelmscott Press' as the culminating episode in a story that had begun with Caxton in 1476. Yet this chorus of praise for the Kelmscott Press was by no means unchallenged. The most serious attack on Morris's principles of design grew out of an interview with hin entitled "Master Printer Morris": A Visit to the Kelmscott Press' which was published in the Daily Chronicle on 22 February 1893; shortly thereafter the same newspaper printed a stinging reply called ‘The Kelmscott Press: A Master Critic on a Master Printer'. The Master Critic' was apparently Morris's old adversary, Joseph Pennell, and the article was reprinted and discussed in at least two printers' journals.23 It certainly pulled no punches: the anonymous critic proclaimed that De Vinne in New York was printing finer books than Morris, assaulted the Troy and Chaucer types, and insisted that the Kelmscott volumes are filled with blemishes which would be ascribed to carelessness even in printers of lesser pretensions'. ![](images/7915447a3573149f49cb7cabd5987356523cb93846d452c9eac12c6fca92dd9e.jpg) Figure 91 By the end of the last century, Morris had become linked in the popular imagination with Gutenberg, as the façade of the Edward Everard Building in Bristol demonstrates. The Kelmscott Press books did not again come under such strong attack until Morris's death, when Pennell once more made a brutal assault in the Daily Chronicle, the British Printer revived the old charge that Morris divided the lines of verse arbitrarily in his books, and the British and Colonial Printer and Stationer declared that the Kelmscott volumes were characterized by poor inking and register.24 After the hyperbole lavished upon the Kelmscott Press in the preceding years, a reaction of this sort in 1896 was not unexpected. The Acadery T2 December 1896) remarked that ‘certain journalist writers' had used the occasion of Morris's death to air their opinions as to the superiority of the Continental and Transatlantic fashions of illustration over the style [of Morris] . . .; their opinions also as to the vast superiority of process blocks to hand engraving, machine-made papers and printing to handmade papers and hand-printing, and so on'. Other indications of the Kelmscott Press's status were equally ambiguous. The market value of the Kelmscott books – a good rough index of the esteem in which they were held by collectors — rose steadily until late 1903, when their prices inexplicably dipped. ‘The truth is, collectors have tired of [the Kelmscott volumes] and will have none of them,' W. G. Menzies wrote in the Connoisseur (May 1905). That they may regain some of their lost prestige is possible, but that they will ever reach the high values of four or five years ago is improbable.' This prediction was wide of the mark, yet the pattern of prices is suggestive indeed. Within the printing trade there were also mixed responses to the Kelmscott experiment. Trade journals that had at first welcomed Morris's attempt at private printing gradually grew more hostile as his condemnations of commercial typography became more widely known through lectures and interviews. The Caslon typefoundry was reflecting a substantial body of,opinion when it declared in 1897 that it could only condemn the fantastic types and methods adopted by Mr. Morris'.2 John Southworth, in his book Modern Printing (19oo), reluctantly admitted that the Kelmscott Press had attracted a great deal of attention, but few printers think there is much likelihood that what they regard as its peculiar characteristics will permanently influence present styles of book printing'(p. 28o). Yet printers continued to pay at least lip-service to Morris's high standards of craftsmanship even while they scorned his isolation from commercial realities. The Printing Number of The Times published on 10 September 1912, for example, though it was largely devoted to the burgeoning technology of the trade, prominently displayed a huge reproduction of a page of the Kelmscott Chaucer. An amusing example of this ambivalence can be seen on the cover of the Monotype Recorder of April 1913, which featured a facsimile of one of Morris's decorated initials; the Recorder had of course always been an enthusiastic advocate of increased mechanization, with early covers portraying a Monotype machiņe rising triumphantly into the sky above an old-fashioned compositor, often accompanied by such edifying slogans as ‘The ol order changeth, yielding place to new'. Paradoxically, at the very time when Morris's reputation as a printer had declined, particularly in the printing trade, there were signs on every side of his influence making itself felt in more oblique ways. Caslon and Old Style type continued to gain ground in book-work, though Shaw, Walker, and the Chiswick Press as well as Morris's example contributed to the growing revival of old-face types. Founts based on the Golden type still proliferated in the first decade of this century. An examination of any representative group of English books published at that time reveals a slight but perceptible darkening of the page through the use of heavier faces and less leading. Volumes in Everyman's Library series, launched by Dent in 1905, displayed a Venetian typeface and ornaments that were intended to evoke memories of the Kelmscott Press. The Oxford University Press began issuing a handsome series of English classical texts in 19o6, printed in the Fell types on pure linen rag paper; the Cambridge University Press a few years earlier acquired a new fount bearing a family resemblance to the Golden type which it used for printing limited eitions of English classics. But whereas the two university presses had special resources at their disposal, most printers before the First World War possessed unsatisfactory collections of typefaces. Harold Curwen, at the Curwen Press, was delighted when à shortage of lead for the munitions factories in 1915 enabled him to dispose profitably of vast quantities of types accumulated since the firm's founding: he cheerfully got rid of more than 2oo founts, retaining only Caslon Old Face, Monotype Old Style No. 2, and two other faces.26 Few other proprietors were willing to adopt such a drastic solution, if only because Caslon still seemed unsuitable for many varieties of commercial printing. As Morris had seen so clearly in 1888, English book-design could not be rejuvenated until the tired nineteenth-century modern-face types were supplanted by new founts modelled on classic old-face types. The earliest really successful effort to do this in England — and simultaneously to make the face generally available on the Monotype machine — came when Gerard Meynell and several associates commissioned an altered version of Caslon, named after their journal in which it was first used, the Imprint, with the first issue appearing in January 1913. It is symptomatic that the Imprint, while saturated with the ideal of technological progress in printing, is also pervaded by the presence of Morris: the first article, Art and Workmanship' by W. R. Lethaby, quotes Morris in its second paragraph; the frontispiece of the Kelmscott Press A Dreaın of John Ball is reproduced; an editorial note declares, ‘The Kelmscott Press, the Doves Press, and the Ashendene Press have done a great deal towards bringing about a Renaissance of Printing, and though the commercial printer is prone to speak slightingly of private presses, much of the improvement in his own work, especially in the design of the types he uses, is to be derived from this source.; The Imprint survived for less than a year but is a harbinger of the resurgence of fine printing, by both private and commercial presses, that was to take place between the wars, a period that deserves to be regarded as the golden age of English printing. The transformation of the mass-produced book was especially remarkable: while the private presses, nearly all exhibiting Morrisian impulses, continued to pour forth handsome volumes of every variety, the renewal of printing now frequently took the form of books produced under normal trade conditions yet displaying a refinement of typography and illustration hitherto associated only with small, experimental presses. Morris's principles, after lying almost dormant for two decades, suddenly emerged during the 192os into the different world of commercial publishing, and were applied, with modifications, to large editions of machine-printed books. What complicates this pattern of influence is that the typographical pioneers of the 192os, committed to modern printing technology, in general were reluctant to acknowledge their ties to Morris or the private-press movement; they prided themselves on being forward-thinking men of a brave new century and professed scorn for the preciousness of Kelmscott-style ventures.
