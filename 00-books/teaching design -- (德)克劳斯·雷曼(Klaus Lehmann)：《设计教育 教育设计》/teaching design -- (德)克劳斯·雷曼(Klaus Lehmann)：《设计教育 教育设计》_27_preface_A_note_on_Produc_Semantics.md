# A note on Produc Semantics

Product Semantics entered design discussions in the eighties, throwing a new light on the role of the designer and the role of the product as a means of communication. The subject caused uneasiness among members of the design community as they realizing that what was considered good and timeless design was far away from the needs of consumers, who saw more in the product than only its practical side. It was the introduction of life style into design thinking, described by the French sociologist Pierre Felix Bourdieu (1930– 2002) among others. It also stimulated a new view of consumers, who it was realized were not a homogenous community but a set of groups with different expectations that depended on their social background and education. Consumers were categorized into target groups and their lifestyle became a matter of market research and intensive design consideration.

The topic was founded in the theory of science, where semiotics, the science of signs, and semantics, dealing with signs and their meanings, became an important part of it. The theory of semiotics goes back to Charles Sanders Peirce(1839– 1914), Charles William Morrisin the US(1903–1979), and to Max Bense in Germany(1910 – 1990). Jochen Gros and Bernhard Buerdek developed it into a practical tool for designers in the eighties, calling it product language.

Product language deals with the non-verbal communication between user and product, showing how it can be used in formal design to make the product understandable to the user. Abraham Moles speaks about the readability of the world'. The basic idea behind this is the realisation that the best products are self-explanatory and do not need a manual to use them(this is the objective of the Push-Pull-Turn-Exercise).

Apart from formalfactors, shape, structure, proportion, direction, texture, or colour i. e. formal language, the use of metaphors has been introduced and widely used in what became post-modern design. Uri Friedlaender, GB, Winfried Scheuer, Germany, Reinhard Butter, USA, Michael Mc Coy, USA, Susann Vihma, Finland and others have described it and made it popular in the design profession. 该话题的基础是科学理论，其中主要研究符号及其具体含义的符号学和语义学是至关重要的部分之一。符号学理论最早由美国的查尔斯·桑德斯·皮尔士(1839—1914)、查尔斯·威廉姆·莫里斯(1903—1979)以及德国的马克斯·本译(1910—1990)提出。在20世纪80年代，约肯·格罗斯以及伯恩哈德·布尔德克把该理论发展成为可供设计师们使用的一种实用工具，被称为产品语言。

产品语言指的是用户和产品之间的、无需用言语表达的一种交流形式，通过外在的设计向用户展示使用方法，从而让产品的相关信息更容易被用户所理解与认同。亚伯拉罕·莫尔斯提出了“可读化世界”的说法，其基本要义是，人们已经意识到，最好的产品具备自我推介的功能，不需要说明书、手册之类的东西(这是“推拉转”练习的目标)。

Metaphorical design must be seen in close relation to post-modern design, that as an antimovement to functional design, called for more emotionality in the product. In the words of McCoy it is “an expansion of the possibilities for imagery in design beyond the implied limits of BauhausModernism”. Though controversial in its intention, strongly dependent on cultural understanding, and difficult to apply, I would like to describe some principles of metaphorical design:

—decodable mechanical prínciples recall practical experience and are translingual and trans-cultural.

—symbols of human or animal gestures are part of a universal body language. —familiar abstract symbols originated in a particular culture but have become universal signs through a process of globalisation and communication.

—symbols of technology, the master models of technological achievement, have become globally understood metaphors.

—emotional qualities of materials appeal to our senses of touch and feeling and give us sensual experiences.

—stylistic or historic metaphors are embedded in the culture in which they originated.

"Product semantics is not a new style. It is a serious study of the meanings that emerge in human interaction with objects.”(Klaus Krippendorf)
