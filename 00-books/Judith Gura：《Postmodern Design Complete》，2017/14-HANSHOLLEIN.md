Austrian architect Hans Hollein was not a member of any postmodernist group, but shared their interest in classicism, though his work also reflects the influence of early Austrian modernists like losef Hoffmann and the Secessionists. His designs in architecture and furniture are elegant and sophisticated, eschewing the air of casual improvisation that marks much postmodern design. In that sense, he can be considered as bridging the divide between strict Modernism and its postmodern successor. Hollein received his early training at the Akademie der bildenden Künste in his home city of Vienna, traveling to the United States to complete his studies at the Illinois Institute of Technology, where he studied with Mies van der Rohe, and the University of California at Berkeley, where he received his MArch in i96o. He made the most of his time in America, driving around the country in a second-hand car, managing to meet Frank Lloyd Wright and Richard Neutra, and visiting the seven American cities and towns named Vienna. With the same adventurous spirit, he worked in architecture practices in Australia, Sweden, South America, and Germany before returning home to open his own office in 1964. His first commission was certainly his smallest: the i2ft- (3.5m-)wide Retti candle shop, on an elegant Vienna shopping street, won him broad recognition for its sophisticated aluminumfronted design. It won a \$25,ooo award, more than the cost of the entire project. More commissions followed, and though much of Hollein's work was in Austria, the following two decades also brought commissions for public spaces in Germany and Paris, museums in Egypt and Germany, and theaters and concert facilities in Japan and the United States. Rejecting the doctrines of functionalism, Hollein was a leader in the rebellion against the existing modernist aesthetic. Taking an interdisciplinary approach to architectural criticism, they incorporated art and politics in a fashion magazine format, with bold graphics and striking images. Its most influential piece was Hollein's 1968 polemic “Everything Is Architecture," which challenged the restrictions of Modernism, arguing that all human production could be considered architecture, and suggesting the corollary that "all are architects." Though published for only six years, Bau's arresting design and provocative contents, including articles by innovative firms like Coop Himmelb(l)au\* and Haus-Rucker\*, drew attention not generally given to intellectual publications. Throughout this period, Hollein criticized functionalism in speeches, writings drawings, and projects. His idea for “Man transFORMS: Aspects of Design” was to illustrate the process of design rather than its products, in imaginative settings that encouraged viewers to make their own connections with the materials and appreciate the importance of design itself, rather than any particular style. Among the ten participating architects and designers were ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design” and “Living with Postmodern Design"), ARATA ISOZAKI, and Richard Meier\*. Hollein's Austrian Travel Bureau (1976) in Vienna is widely cited as an example of postmodern design, but its translucent glass ceiling also references Otto Wagner's ı912 Postal Savings Bank. In his contribution to the Strada Novissima exhibit at the i98o Venice Architecture Biennale, he adopted a column Adolf Loos had designed in 1922. These and other projects reflected both Hollein's interest in historicism and his allegiance to his celebrated Viennese predecessors. His product designs—consistently elegant— combined historical references with postmodern egalitarianism. Invited to design furniture for MEMPHIS's (see under “Edited Design") first collection, Hollein contributed the Schwarzenberg sideboard, a graceful piece in birds-eye maple veneer, more Biedermeier than bohemian. He also designed gold jewelry and silver tabletop accessories for CLETO MUNARI (see under 'Edited Design’), and a piano for Bösendorfer. For most of his career, Hollein combined architecture and design with teaching. He taught at Washington University in St. Louis during his time in America, and was a professor at the Kunstakademie Düsseldorf from 1967 to i976.
