# ARCHAEOLOGIES OF THE FUTURE

In the previous chapter, we discussed several ways in which design anthropologists create spaces of engagement, critique and friction between different temporal frameworks. Working with futures is generally done by working with material things and is not only something exclusive to professional design in workplaces but also happens in everyday vernacular ways of designing. In this chapter, we will examine a particular design ethnography, which focuses on material culture as a way to inform the engineering of new products and services. What the example illustrates is the ways in which designed things are not only things which cause change by design but also are understood as materialised relationships to design institutions in society, and so they manifest one’s relationship to change, history and progress. When studying material culture, interpretive archaeologists have observed that they do not simply excavate objects which past people used as evidence of events, from which modern archaeologists construct histories. Modern archaeologists work with objects which were often expressions by past people of how they saw themselves relating to history, especially monuments and funerary artefacts. In a similar way, designed things are contemporary objectifications of relationships to change and history. I call this objectification of a temporal connection the ‘design relation’.

The aspiration of design to be a way of working with time, loosely put, has close links with Rabinow’s notion of an ‘Anthropology of the Contemporary’ (Rabinow 2007; Rabinow et al 2008; Faubion 2016). This amounts to a call for radical change within the discipline of anthropology, and specifically in the ways in which an anthropologist relates temporally to her subject of study. Rabinow (Rabinow et al 2008: 59) argues that we need to develop a stance of ‘adjacency’ in social research, a stance which acknowledges how multiple ways of knowing are emergent at the same moment and necessarily accompany one another. ‘In our time, as never before’, he argues, ‘the continuity from past to present is broken’. Technology, design, and the capacity for social mobilisation mean that radical transformation is more possible than ever before. And yet present events are often considered self-explanatory and factual, and so the contemporary moment evades scrutiny, while traditional anthropology makes its evaluations from a privileged perspective, after the event. Achieving an ‘adjacent’ stance means working alongside informants on anthropological and cultural issues in studio-like environments, using collaborative ways of working and bridging the divide between theory and practice. Rabinow’s arguments have been fruitfully explored by Lury and Wakeford (2013). Their idea of ‘inventive methods’ comprises methodologies which happen, or are discovered, during fieldwork as ways of producing knowledge; they range from the ‘tape recorder’, to the ‘list’, to simply ‘pattern’. Inventive methods recognise how a relation of adjacency can emerge in situ as a material relation, a form or a thing which objectifies knowledge (Bourdieu 1992), created between the

researcher and researched.

By contrast with Rabinow, but informed by the same problems which his work explores, other anthropologists do not consider the framework of a focus on ‘the contemporary’ as necessary. Rather, they relish the emergence of shared problems and dissonances and seek to expand awareness of the problematic spaces of possibility which design responds to, beyond the contemporary:

Against design’s concern with creation, innovation and ‘future-making’ (Björgvinsson, Ehn and Hillgren 2010), anthropology systematically investigates the past in order to understand the present, including its modes of anticipating the future. It is a great challenge to extend the temporal horizon both forwards and backwards, to anchor images of the future in reliable constructions of the past, thus avoiding the risk of “defuturing” that is inherent in design (Fry 2011) and of generalizing and essentializing modern values of innovation and change (Suchman 2011).

(Gunn, Otto and Smith 2013: 4, original emphases)

The various aspirations expressed by these thinkers are ambitious. For example, to ‘anchor images of the future in reliable constructions of the past’ requires two or more convincing works of proven cultural imagination: you do not only need images of the future, you need constructions of the past, you need both of these to be methodologically reliable and you need to articulate elements based on observation with elements which may be speculative. These aspirations could be regarded as beyond the capacities of any design anthropologist working individually, but requiring collaboration. This is a project involving multiple different people whose concern with change and time differs, but who all build a sense of engagement around the material knowledge artefacts produced during design anthropology work. Design’s economy of knowledge, therefore, becomes necessarily ‘humanised’, a collective project which aims at bringing human lives together as much as it aims at any kind of coherence, and within which different elements of the knowledge have the properties of character and perspective, according to the particular collaborators.

In this chapter, I discuss empirically ‘messy’ (Dourish and Bell 2011) ethnographic examples, using material culture approaches to show how research into material things can help bring together these diverse emergent knowledge of temporality. When we interpret material things as expressions of relationships to time and change, those expressions are not always coherent, because they are experimental. Yet design anthropology research involves building a connection to society’s agents of social change and comprises an inquiry into the appropriate conceptual handles by which people gain a grasp on both change as an abstract, and their social situation with respect to structures of change in society.

By discussing these examples, I am echoing some of the themes with which I began this book. In the introduction, I suggested that design anthropology is about working within a knowledge economy of materialised concepts, as much as it is about doing forms of ethnography or studio design work. For the anthropologist, many such conceptualisations are to be found ‘out there’, by researching existing human lives and situations, rather than being invented. Second, I have suggested that design is not so much about ‘what’ you know as ‘who’ you know about. Designed things operate to evoke an imagination of other people, and they are operationalised through relationships with corporations and comparable institutions within a global industrial society. Third, I have proposed that this evocation of other people happens especially temporally, such that a lot of design is comparable to a ‘first contact’ situation. The designs which emerge within the work make proximate other people and other lifestyles which while existing in the same space are temporally different. That disturbing emergence of radically different other people and communities is at the heart of design anthropology, and it happens both in design studios and through participatory field work. Fourth, I have, therefore, expressed a project of design anthropology as developing a ‘well thought’ human social world.

## Independent living in Ireland

The Material Culture Ireland project was conducted at and by Intel Ireland in 2007–08. The remit of Intel’s Digital Health Group was to support design for independent living as people get older across the world. As the global population ages, there are new challenges which we all face in our homes if we are to be able to live independently, and not in institutions (and the ‘we’ in this instance is genuinely a global human ‘we’, not a culturally constituted community). Ageing is, therefore, subject to tremendous design attention, and there are in fact a wide range of proposals, technologies, designs and suggestions for assisting independent living. A company can draw on various resources to address independent living: new technologies being developed inside the company; existing patented technologies in the public sphere which have not necessarily found a market niche; software, data and mathematical strategies and algorithms; and design methodologies. Our team was engaged in understanding ageing from the point of view of people as they age, especially across Europe.

Designing for independent living requires anthropological input and cultural understanding, because many proposed products and services do not succeed without it. One paradox in designing for this field is that the products which actively suggest somebody needs assistance to live independently can seem actively stigmatising, and hence self-defeating. Another paradox is that what ‘independence’ may mean varies a lot and is far from clear. In many communities, independent living means being able to socialise and engage with different people more, while in other communities, living apart may be seen as an ideal. In interviews in a range of countries in Europe, in some places, especially Northern Europe, we encountered people for whom living independently meant drawing on care resources from the state or the health services, and hence being independent of family assistance, while elsewhere, especially in some parts of Southern Europe, independence meant a close association with family members.

This work, therefore, formed part of a mid- to long-term design project, which meant building connections between different professions and projects, including engineering, market research, the exploration of new product and service spaces, and the cross-cultural understanding of IT applications. Some elements of the research were very specific, for example, looking at a specific sensor or IT application which a certain engineer in our group might be working on. Other elements were very broad, and devotedly anthropological, because the research in Ireland formed part of the global ambitions of Intel. The company had developed a research infrastructure capable of genuinely cross-cultural and diverse research understandings, through parallel work by centres in the United States, Europe and India. Our topic guides and outlines had been developed to incorporate these elements and professional voices, including questions with specific applications in mind and broader questions exploring the cultural landscape. We included an extensive section about the material environments of the home, as a way to explore the ways in which our informants were materially anticipating the ageing process, not only through what they were saying but also through what they were doing. As noted in the previous discussions, by discussing material things, we would gain insights both into existing routines and established ways of doing things, as well as anticipations of futures. Using this topic guide, we developed a phased research process. We spent a day with each of a number of families recruited for the research, whom we paid for their time. Having worked through the first day’s findings with engineering and design colleagues at Intel, we later returned for another day to explore the implications and use more targeted design-inspired research tools.

My colleague Kyle Kilbourn spends time with Jack, a retired man from Cork. Among other things, Jack gives Kyle a tour of his home and gives him a sense of his daily routines. As a part of a tour of his house, he shows Kyle the bins outside, then the wall of the garage and then draws his attention to the garage roof:

JACK: I painted all that- last year. And I’ll tell you this much - see that? - ‘tis as hard to get the paint, you know. You have to take your time to it. But I did do, you know? The roof of that, it’s what they call - I’ll show it to you now. It’s a pre-stressed concrete roof. You could put a car up on that. And I’ll tell you how they do it. Now have a look at the roof. Now you see the marks there. There were boards that they put over, you know? What they do then. They get scaffolding here to hold the boards up, you know. You might have two, like, two bits of timber, holding the other timber up, you know? And when it’s all finished, what they do is knock it away, and there’s your ceiling.

KYLE: And is it pretty common to have a ceiling like this?

JACK: No. Uncommon really.

This moment where Jack shows Kyle the concrete roof in his garage is a very banal, everyday, ordinary moment, which nonetheless strikes Kyle enough to share with the other four ethnographers involved in the project, one fragment among the immense pool of ethnographic information which we collate. The roof is a detail which Jack considers worthy of interest for a design researcher from Intel, an American, and is appropriate for the research into independent living as Jack himself understands it. In that sense, the concrete roof of the garage is for some reason a part of the ‘answers’ which Jack is giving us, in response to what he thinks our question is or should be. And he is providing answers in three ways. He is first ‘temporally unpacking’ the thing. The concrete roof, presented in a short moment, is made significant through being stretched out into a narrative. The immediate narrative is a technical one, in which we are able to understand the making process and how the roof came to be there. But also, second, underlying this immediate narrative is the implication of a longer term human archaeology and personal history, by which we understand the extended personal history of Jack himself and his household. The narrative of the roof is biographical, where the lives of person and roof are in parallel. Lastly, there is a longer term historical narrative of innovation at work here. The research is conducted at a time of social, economic and technical transformation in Irish history, commonly called the ‘Celtic Tiger’, during which people who previously did not have the capacities to improve their homes and lifestyles suddenly have the resources to do so. The roof is one part of Jack’s vision for his retirement, and his establishment of a home appropriate to live in after retirement, and to do this, he is using the most up-to-date technical means.

As Jack unpacks the roof into a narrative, we begin to perceive the range of intentions and people who were involved. It has been built in a sense by Jack, but commissioned by the builders, who saw to the details. The durability of the roof is also a part of the intentions. Jack did not just throw up a wooden shed or shelter, but a significant enduring structure, to house a car which was originally bought for his late wife. The whole object forms a part of a sense of responsibility extending across time. Like so many objects in the world, even for a widower like Jack, the garage does not appear as a wholly individual object but part of his responsibilities.

Jack’s network of responsibilities is even more visible as he talks about cooking, and in doing so talks directly about what independent living means for him:

JACK: So, as I said to you now, I love cooking. I do everything for myself. There were other curtains up there last week and I took them down. I had those washed and ironed and I put them up and put the others away. Come the winter, I put the other ones up. I did the same in my own room. Most everything you know? You have to have… you can have… a hit and miss system when you are on your own. You are ultimately responsible for yourself. Not your children, yourself and I’m not a burden to any of them. They said that too me… they love coming over here, you know. And my grandchildren, they love coming over here. I get onto them now and said right I’m going to do a dinner for you. What do you want? Somebody will want Shepherd’s pie, some of them steaks, some of them lamb. I say I can’t do all of that, so I’ll have to divide you up. Come up one night, and friends come up with them, and [I] do steaks, you know? Steak, cabbage, carrots, roasted potatoes, roasted onions, the lot. I don’t use a frying pan, I use the oven. That oven is over 40 years old. And it is in immaculate condition as you can see.

KYLE: Wow, spotless.

JACK: Spotless. We got that when we were married first…and it was very expensive at the time. It was 54 pounds and that was a lot of money then. That’s good stuff. You wouldn’t get those…there was a fellow from the gas company now…he’s living down the road…tell him I had a leak there and he came over and he said Jack you’ll never buy the likes of those again. When I was a young fella, an apprentice and I use to be putting those together, that particular gas stove…said you would never ever get them again. But my wife was immaculate. It isn’t me, I clean it now like. But she always looked after it very well. But as they say, when you are on your own. You look after yourself. No one bloody else will, you know?

This quotation shows us the problematic ‘messiness’ of ethnographic data, happening in the very moment when a conversation coheres most cogently around the central design concern. Jack delivers a succinct summary of his viewpoint on independent living. Yet he moves from stoves to curtains to children to cleaning to gas in a breath. This tells us something about the nature of ‘human-centred’ness in a design project. Notably, while Jack asserts how he sees himself as responsible for himself, he simultaneously asserts a complicated wider social landscape, not living in isolation. Designing for Jack’s independent living does not only mean designing for him but also for a social situation in which his physical possessions manifest multiple relationships and responsibilities. So something such as cleaning the stove can be interpreted as a self-oriented action, but it can also be seen as a responsibility which has itself been inherited from his late wife. These social relationships are inextricably intertwined with temporal forms, because relationships are themselves temporal phenomena. The most important quality of a relationship is its capacity to endure, away from the ‘now’ moment, and into the past and the future. This is largely what Jack’s domestic possessions enable. This means that the ‘human-centred’ situation does not only mean that Jack is at the centre of our design work, as an individual, but it also means that we should see this ethnographic example through the lens of a human-infused material world.

It is clearly important that any designed product or service is capable of fitting within the environment of Jack’s home. The forms, materials and sensations of things in that home are more a meshwork of relationships than a single aesthetic or a set of useful functions, and they have many different temporal rhythms. Things which echo his late wife are alongside new things. Some things reflect Jack’s plans and intentions, others manifest other peoples’. However, there is no definite single cultural principle here which dictates how a product or service should look in Jack’s house. Instead, there are multiple sets of overlapping kinds of looks which relate to different relationships, exchanges, emotions, memories and intentions, and it is in this very diversity that the social patterning of Jack’s life exists. So any designed thing must ‘fit’, but it must fit in the way that a jigsaw puzzle piece fits into a jigsaw, where every piece is unique but forms a mutual part of the whole. In this situation, there is a need for a kind of social intelligence, not only what physical things or functional ‘things to do’ might suit, but a feeling for how they might fit into a life, so as to create a slightly different jigsaw which will still approximately fit together.

The nature of the temporality at play in this situation could be interpreted in different ways, but some fit better than others. Many considerations of how objects relate to temporality emphasise ‘experience’, that is an unfolding processual encounter with the material world and foreseeing the intersecting possibilities for the future (following Heidegger 2010 [1927]). However, Jack’s home might be more usefully understood through a paradigm of exchange than of experience. The anthropological concept of the ‘gift’ was first outlined in the 1920s (Malinowski 2014 [1922]; Mauss 2001 [1927]), and its original formulations have been critiqued and developed in many different ways, but some of the original observations of gift exchange apply in Jack’s home. At root, the idea of exchange is that it provides a means to establish social relationships by extending them in time. When one party gives something to another, this creates a situation in which there is a sense of obligation to reciprocate at some time in the future. Gift reciprocity, when it is stretched out over time, is also a way of ‘presencing’ people across space: you have my Christmas card on your shelf, and no matter how long it sits there, it will always be ‘my’ card, in your home. Gifts retain a connection to the giver, at least until reciprocated, and so gift exchange helps construct a mutual, oscillating presencing of ‘the sense of people’ in one another’s lives. While it would be inaccurate to describe all of Jack’s possessions as gifts, and they are all unique, nonetheless the principles of the gift do help us see how the temporality of the home works. The way in which the narratives of the various things in Jack’s home seem constantly imprinted with the presencing of other people, living and dead, is evidently like exchanges, of thoughts and of labour. The objects manifest multiple rhythms of considering other people, remembering them, and Jack is thinking with objects, not only about how they are for certain purposes, but about or for other people. The social rhythms produced by relationships between people cover many different kinds of intersecting periodicities, moment by moment, day by day, month by month, year by year, and over lifetimes. The rhythms frame the various social surroundings and commitments in Jack’s life, himself, family, community and long-term friendships, as well as one-off momentary encounters such as with tradespeople. Their ‘for’ness, as I discussed in Chapters 2 and 3, is one of the interesting features of designed phenomena.

What this means is that, in this particular example, designing for independent living meant actively thinking about not only how change happens, but the temporal implications of an engagement between the design lab and the home. As we have seen, designing for ageing is big business, and many engineers and designers think of it as ‘making a difference’. If a design can work in Jack’s home, it is a very solid indication that the design work is on the right track. However, Jack does not think of change in his home only in terms of a moment of intervention, a point in time with a ‘before’ and a superior ‘after’ which indicates progress. Rather, there is a constant process of unpacking and developing of plural meanings from objects. Multiple moments in time which might in a scientific sense exist at many different times, moments in

history, are in fact being co-presenced.

In a model where past and future are different, this activity is called reminiscing, or remembering. However, when people in old age are developing their material homes, and attaching significance to the memories, they are also planning for the future and their future lifestyle. As Jack talks about his gas stove, following similar principles to the reciprocity of gifts, the past is in many ways an experience which evidences the future. The meals which he has cooked, his use of this object and the events which have happened around it, are both reminiscences and simultaneously predictors. Acts of imagination, framed as design, are not necessarily abstract, nor future-oriented. As a rule, in order to work with futures, people work with pasts. This is done through the manipulation and relocation of objects which have histories, and those histories are an anchoring for unknown futures and are suggestive of futures because they give a sense of historical direction.

Thoughts about the future appear to be very like memories, such that creativity and remembering can be very similar. What acts of remembering and designing share are the capacity to instantiate things, actions and ways of being in the present from Elsewhens in time. We can of course argue that there is a substantive absolute difference between futures and pasts, between prediction and memory. We can, however, also argue that for the practical purposes of living, people act with a sense of both pasts and their futures. Social life, in which other people are sometimes present and sometimes absent, necessitates a partial sense of certainty about both futures and pasts. Social life happens with a definite knowledge of the future, which is not total but necessary, and it happens with a knowledge of the past, which is likewise not total, and is arguably a little less necessary. While one can argue that in reality there is a cause–effect relationship between what happens today and tomorrow, and that it is informed by what happened yesterday, this is not always the practical or social reality. What somebody does today may be because of knowledge of what will happen tomorrow, and often knowledge of yesterday is at least as uncertain as knowledge of tomorrow. Hence, actions and events are, in practical terms, contingent upon constructions of both pasts and futures. This is why design anthropology should explore the multiple modes of relating to temporality, such as memory, design, intention, aspiration, prosperity or hope.

## The emergence of features in ethnography

The implication of this discussion is that design anthropologists needs to consider how they can productively use messy ethnographic information about these contexts of everyday life, to help develop design’s and anthropology’s understandings of time. Most design work is predicated on particular understandings of time as a kind of consistent and overarching structure, within which design achieves point-like moments of intervention by creating new kinds of things. A lot of design anthropology instead offers us views into heterochronous worlds of multiple temporalities, within which design engagements help to cultivate appropriate change which fits. To think about this, I now discuss in a little more detail what is meant by heterochronicity, and approaches in heritage and archaeology about how we perceive and understand temporality through objects. I do this by contrasting the concepts of ‘archaeological features’ with ‘design features’. Both of these concepts involve transformations between objects and contexts similar to the contextualisation processes I discussed in Chapter 5. We can draw on these approaches to help us understand how, while design work may create new things within time, design anthropology works with temporality through conceptual re-contextualisations which may emerge within fieldwork.

To make the basic point of heterochronicity, that objects manifest different existences in time, Neil MacGregor opens his radio programme about heritage objects in the British Museum with the sound of a pulsar star heard from across space (also published in MacGregor 2012: xv). When the light and radio waves from stars reach earth, because of the immense distances involved, the star that we see or detect in radio waves can already be hundreds of years old. From a subjective point of view, some argue that we do not see the stars as they are now, that we are rather seeing back in time. The programme then moves on to examine objects from throughout human history, and the suggestion is that these things can be thought about, in a very literal sense, as seeing back in time. Although of course, one can go into the museum and see objects from many different centuries alongside one another, all existing now at the same time and in a similar space, nonetheless from a different perspective, all of these objects occupy different temporalities. Some of the objects which MacGregor discusses are things which were excavated by archaeologists, and re-emerged from the earth into human life as if they had travelled directly from the moment in time when they were buried. Other objects have had extended lives passing through many hands, being created, used, reused, adapted, re-presented and so forth. In human terms, the temporality of objects is not only about their age in years, but also the relative directness, complexity, humanity and layering of the engagement.

MacGregor’s point that different objects in a museum appear to us as if we are seeing back in time is comparable with how Gilles Deleuze writes about cinematic images:

(An image is) archaeological, stratigraphic, tectonic. Not that we are taken back to prehistory (there is an archaeology of the present), but to the deserted layers of our time which bury our own phantoms, to the lacunary layers which we juxtaposed according to various orientations and connections.

(Deleuze 1989: 234)

Among other things, Deleuze’s underlying philosophy has argued that cultural meaning is constantly emerging in the world from the experience of difference. In other words, somebody such as Jack is not to be understood as a ‘type’ of person, an Irish widower of a certain age, so as to account for what is going on based on his identity; rather, we can see an ever-emergent sense of differences, contrasts and the development of self alongside a sense of others. Now one might have thought that this approach would favour a situation in which we could appreciate people as endlessly inventing their own worlds. However, what it actually means for Deleuze is a kind of creative feel for ruination. If newness emerges as difference, the meaningfulness of design is really about how it defines itself by contrast with a world understood as already historical and intrinsically involves a sense of incipient redundancy. Although this is a very specific philosophical approach, and we do not have to feel obliged to be ‘Deleuzians’, nonetheless for many of the people we talked with on that project, and for a great deal of material culturefocussed research, this appreciation of ruination does resonate as valid. Jack expresses it quite well in the bittersweet quotations I have given.

Heterochronicity is, therefore, in many ways an alternative perspective on identity and people as subjects of design and serves to open up critical and alternative perspectives on contemporary society. Society, in general, looks to material things for a sense of stability about time, seeking out objects which bear witness to senses of time which are reliable and predictable. In order to be taken seriously, a lot of design works to distil futures into a temporal order, in the same way that a lot of heritage is organised to distil categories into structures of the past. The presumption of consistency and stability in time (Gell 1992) means that we are able to connect objects in our minds, inferring associations. A dress from the time of Jane Austen in Britain is usually seen as associated with certain other specific forms and details: architectures, forms of transport, the shapes of windows, food and certain words, for example, because they all share a ‘moment’ or ‘era’. This is one element of the exercise of design heritage in Dessau, Germany, as we saw in the last chapter: the residents of Dessau celebrate their contribution to the world through the distillation of artefacts and things conceptualised and created in the 1920s into particular heritage zones, warehouses and museums where they can be mutually associated and celebrated. Bauhaus objects often (not always) ‘belong together’, creating a modernist temporal purity.

In design anthropology, it is necessary to recognise both the distemporality (one might say temporal unexpectedness) of material things and contexts, while also recognising the general aspiration for temporal predictability among people using designed things and services, a predictability which is a necessary assumption for much of social life to happen. Doing something today is dependent both on what we know happened yesterday, and also on what we know will happen tomorrow. Hence, in many social situations it is necessary for everybody to have a common understanding of a temporal framework, and that events are happening ‘within a structure which is time. As good ethnography reveals, however, temporality is not wholly a property of context, common across things comprising that context, it is alternatively a variable property of things, as in MacGregor’s examples of different objects in the British Museum. Because material objects and materials constitute our relationships to time, for practical purposes, it is not that objects exist ‘in’ time so much as time ‘in’ things, and the work of design is partly about exacerbating that temporal heterogeneity.

In the movements between field sites and institutional sites, then, design anthropology works to loosen up the rigid temporal purity which is the legacy of modernism in our contemporary age. For the purposes of design, recognition of this variability becomes very necessary. One is engaged in designing with a judgement about the temporal touchstones of life and the changeable elements. One can design, for example, with the normative attitude that a house is long-lasting, enduring and stable, but that the interior is more changeable and temporary. Or, as often happens, one can design with the attitude that the possessions are the stable lasting element which helps make a person who they are, but that the building is transformable. Both of these stances may lead to valid designing for an informant such as Jack. In design, very often the normative temporal ordering becomes dangerously playful.

We can examine how this heterochronicity works in practice by thinking about ‘features’. In the introduction, I commented on the tendency for many designers, when asked for evidence of what design is, to point to apparently small elements of the material world. Jonathan Ive has talked about the boxes of Apple products, and that company has spent a great deal of money to protect the curvature at the corners of its iPhones. An IKEA designer pointed Garvey (2017) to the indentations on the bottom of their mugs, while Murphy (2015) discusses the texture at the edge of a pavement in Sweden. These kinds of things are often called ‘design features’, and they are in several ways transitional phenomena. Features are generally not things in themselves, but elements or styles, but nor are they environments. Their remarkability often has a limited shelf life: Dourish and Bell (2011) observe how designs as they become more successful can become unremarkable, either a normal ubiquitous part of the environment, or else unfashionable and absent. Nonetheless, features are important for people making use of design’s outputs; they help people make sense of designs. They are also important for the organisation and facilitation of design work. Designers are able to separate out what characterises a new design and makes it different, to organise their work and time, and to more effectively organise the labour of design teams, through conceptualising design features. The concept of features makes it possible to prioritise elements of a design.

I would compare the design feature to the archaeological feature. The first often reads as a future-oriented indication of human life, and the latter as past-oriented. Both kinds of ‘feature’ are important knowledge tools for their respective professions. I learned about archaeological features aged 17, on a training excavation in a field in Shropshire, which had once been one of the largest cities in Roman Britain. We excavated on our knees, all in a line, scraping off layers of earth 1 in. at a time. At each pass across the site, the director would examine the emergent earth for anything worth attention. Objects, such as pottery or a coin, were declared as ‘finds’, and generally removed and sorted. Things which were not necessarily things in themselves, but patterns, like a scatter of stones, or a new colour of mud, might sometimes be declared a ‘feature’. So as a stone emerged, for example, it could be a ‘find’, a ‘feature’, a ‘wall’, a ‘building’, ‘natural’, or none of these things. Each find or feature would be awarded a code number and a form, would be photographed or sketched, documented and would enter into the knowledge repository of ‘data’.

Archaeological features are not self-evident, and it takes experience and perception to deal with them. This is especially because digs are messy. Everything is the colour of mud. Everything is covered in mud. Perhaps it is mud. Perhaps everything has become mud. Bodies, furniture, floors and roofs have turned to mud. The question of whether something is a feature can often be rephrased on a site as ‘what sort of mud is this?’

On a particular day, it seemed a different colour of mud was emerging in my patch, a trace of yellow. By the following day, as a circular, yellow sandy patch began to be visible. It was declared to be a feature, it was recorded, and I was given responsibility for it. In subsequent days, I learned how the decisions you make about features on an excavation are actually an exercise in examining the stratigraphy of the site. The stratigraphy means the layering of different elements, trying to understand through their physical positions in space which elements came before or after others, and consequently trying to read a revealing narrative of human actions and intentionality through sequencing the many elements.

The muddy yellow circle distinguished itself from the surrounding muddy brown surface, but as with every feature its nature remained unclear. Sometimes, a circle might indicate a hole had been dug in the ground, and the yellow would then comprise the filling. However, in that part of the world, many such holes tend to be black, because they are often post-holes in which wooden poles stood, and wood or organic matter tends to decay to black earth. Alternatively, this could be a floor. This circle could be a raised bump in a new emerging surface, a floor covered over by some renovation work in the past. The circle might, therefore, grow bigger as I excavated. That possibility was evidenced when a patch of yellow seemed to emerge at the other side of the space we were excavating, against an ancient wall base. These two alternatives, hole or floor (and there were other possibilities), are stratigraphic opposites. If this feature was a hole, it would mean it was created after the surrounding surface. That would mean that the yellow part happened in historical terms after the brown part, and one would then proceed by digging out the centre of the yellow area to explore and record it first. By contrast, if the feature were a floor, it was created before the brown surface around it. If that were true, the best way to proceed would be to excavate around the feature, not in it, to remove the brown floor surface and then proceed with the new, yellow surface. In each case, by building an idea of the stratigraphy around this specific feature, we are building a sense of the entire site’s sequencing, and the sequencing of any objects we uncover within the layers. Initially, however, a feature can be in a sort of quantum state, its position in the temporal sequence uncertain. Looking at the edge of the feature, to excavate on the yellow side or on the brown side was to make an informed decision about temporal stratigraphy.

Over the days my feature, of which I was so proud, was revealed to be the grave of an infant. A very young newborn child had been buried in a hole lined with yellow builders’ sand. At a later date, something had spread a part of that body and sand across the room a few metres away, perhaps somebody digging in the area with a shovel. In Roman times, it was illegal to bury human bodies within a city, but babies were not considered to be fully human in a legal sense until they were 40 days old, and it was likely this child was younger. So in excavating this feature, an idea of a sequence of human actions was developed, and one could hypothesise about which areas were inside or outside buildings, gained an understanding of legal structures, of how memory and forgetting and modes of occupation had developed within this space over time, and indeed what it meant to be human in this site.

There is a sense of a temporal gradient, a time difference, at the physical interface of an archaeological feature with its surroundings, but the nature of the gradient is at first unclear. During the creation of data and knowledge on an excavation, features illustrate how things can become contexts, and contexts can become things. Within the interpretive frameworks of archaeological knowledge of sites, holes can become floors, stones can become buildings, insides can become outsides and vice-versa. Interpretive archaeologists have pointed out that many archaeological sites, perhaps most, contain an element of active communication about peoples’ attitudes to history. One reason why so much archaeology comprises in excavating graves and funerary monuments is that these are actively built as expressions of an attitude to history and change, expressions intended for later generations. This means that, for some archaeologists (but not all), the creation of archaeological understandings is not as simple as the experts building a new understanding of what happened but is a necessarily flawed exercise in mutual understanding of history, between archaeologist and people who lived in the past:

Social conceptions of space and time have not been and are not always the same. The manner in which people situate themselves in a particular time and a particular space has material effects on the entire gamut of social practices. Time and space, in a materialist conception, are recursively related to the social and cannot be divorced from it. They are not abstractions, dimensions, but form part and parcel of social life. The factory clock provides a medium of, for and in capitalist production. A time of memories and social tradition has a fundamentally different effect on production and social relations in pre-capitalist social forms.

(Tilley 2001: 78)

Design features are not exactly the same as archaeological ones, but nonetheless the comparison reveals some of the pertinence of design features, and how and why it is that they preoccupy so many people. Features are also conceptual. In features, it becomes possible to infer and to some extent communicate orientations to time, history, intention and change. A sense of obsoletion or ruination, far from being accidental or a natural process, can also be seen as a deliberate creation of meaning in the world, where people create some things as ‘new’, and other things as ‘ruins’. Acts of creativity, production, appropriation and making are accompanied by acts of destruction, displacement, neglect and expulsion. Material culture often expresses then a relationship to change. People do not only ‘make’ change, they also ‘conceive’ of change. Some archaeologists refer to this as ‘new materialisms’ (Witmore 2014) or ‘critical materialisms’. Two more examples from the Material Culture Ireland research can help us to examine the implications of this kind of mutual, interpretive position on design work.

## Contextual and conscious relationships to change

Two more examples of households with whom we worked on the Material Culture Ireland study illustrate how these ideas of stratigraphy and emergent features can play a part in building understandings of design work as emerging from fieldwork. They both show how a very spatialised and objectified sense of time came to predominate in this project and raise important questions about how an awareness, consciousness and explicitness of change emerge. In the first household, Mark and Marcella presented what seems to be the perfect recipe for a couple whose design can help, because they were very consciously executing a radical change in their lives and their home. In the second household, Hugh and Bernie have a more layered and gradual sense of change, which constantly records and builds a feeling of progress through their lives.

Mark and Marcella lived in an immaculately clean apartment in a relatively wealthy area of Dublin. When Mark retired from his fairly well-paid job with an airline, they decided to move entirely from the house where they had raised their children, into a penthouse apartment. In the process, their possessions were ‘pruned’. Many of the older things they had acquired over the years in the house were thrown out, and a few passed on to their children, who now had homes of their own. In moving to the apartment, most of their things were new.

I spent 2 days with Mark and Marcella in all. Much of this time was spent talking, and we also had various activities and themes to cover, especially during the second day, but I also tried to simply spend time with them in the kind of routine which they had planned for the day. During the first day, we talked a lot about the home and their history and ideas about their life. They gave me a tour of the place, and I asked about certain specific areas of material culture, using a list of about 20 kinds of object. We discussed their social network, family and connections. We specifically talked about health issues. For the sake of my engineering colleagues, I also had to record some physical and material details of the place, such as the construction materials, wiring, approximate room dimensions and so forth. The main aim of day one was to try and get a good rounded idea of Mark and Marcella’s life and ideas. Our meeting was literally a moment when they were experiencing a new kind of life. Not only did they have a new apartment, but their youngest daughter had moved out the week before. Mark was retired, the kids had all grown up, and they had a new home.

Mark and Marcella’s home was evidently partly still in process, an ongoing collaborative thinking process. What was being considered was not only how the apartment looked and seemed but also questions of taste (it was simply that in executing what they wanted, their taste and consideration was brought into play). There was also a re-conceptualisation of their lifestyle. Lifestyle here refers to an imagination of social context: on the one hand a sense of everyday routines and ways of doing things, as well as a visible aestheticisation and manifestation of these routines, and a way of living which fits within the unfolding of social history. A lot of conversation specified how social life was going to be in future: the golf, the entertaining, the church, family events, reading, cooking and so forth. The apartment was a physical vehicle for instantiating these imaginations of life as style, day by day and week by week.

Insights into how this kind of imagination of ‘lifestyle’ happens happened in the conversations about their tables. For a time, they had an apartment in Spain where they used to holiday, and that was a kind of revelation to them. By contrast with the Dublin house which they had lived in since marrying in the 1960s, with its family clutter, the Spanish apartment was relatively open and light. They would never have considered living in an apartment if it had not been for Spain, because in Ireland, houses were the norm. Since the 1990s, there have been more apartments built, but many of them are rented out rather than occupied by owners. The possibility of an apartment as a place to live is a relatively recent arrival in Ireland, and the idea of an apartment as a normal kind of dwelling for a family did struggle to become accepted. However, Mark and Marcella were pleasantly surprised by the apartment in Spain, and when they sold it, they shipped back a lot of the furniture in a van. Among these things was a large glass-topped table. Marcella would never have considered glass as a suitable material for a kitchen table, but it meant that the eating area was full of light, and it gave a sense of lightness generally. In fact, that Spanish table was very big and bulky and heavy: it proved too big for the new apartment, and while carrying it back down the stairs, they dropped it and it smashed. They bought a new, slightly smaller glass-topped table in Dublin, and they placed some of the metal chairs from Spain on the small balcony of the apartment, in the sun (and, it must be said, in the rain). Such aspirational objects created parallels between conceptions of retirement and of holidaying (Figure 8.1).

Other imaginations of a retirement lifestyle were not personal but present because the architects and designers of the block had chosen and installed them. A computerised system throughout the home controlled the sound and lights, so it was possible to play music throughout, to control all the lights from any room, or turn everything off from the bedside panel. The bathroom mirrors were heated, to not steam up when you took a shower. The professional design extended to the art, including a large wall-mounted modern sculpture near the front door chosen by the architects. While Mark and Marcella quite liked that sculpture, they did not have much to say about it.

It is not possible to repeat here all of the stories of transformation which emerged in narration of objects, styles and materials, but the resonant visions of ways of living echoed through many, underlying a shift from traditional ‘house living’ to cosmopolitan ‘apartment living’. Their previous home had been very different. From the 1960s to the 2000s, they lived in a house in Dublin. Their narratives of that time were not unhappy but nonetheless I would characterise them as ‘worthy’. They worked. They brought up the children. They looked after their own parents, now passed, and knew about the illnesses of ageing and the responsibilities of carers. They took holidays, and so the years passed. That phase of their lives, nearly 40 years, was reported as one single historical era, but life now was being lived after the dawning of a new era of retirement.

There were also conversations about objects which seemed to manifest tensions, and which might take the form of disagreements about the past, or memory. They disagree about where a religious icon came from. Mark was religious, attending mass at church almost every day, while Marcella was less of a believer, and one of them asserted that this icon was always in their previous house, while the other asserted it was bought specially for the new apartment. How a couple who evidently work very well together could remember differently was unclear, but the icon could either be an object which has roots in their life in the 1970s or could be about the renewed vision of how they plan to spend their retirement and was prominently displayed.

Other areas of tension emerged around other objects. I tried not to probe too much. As a rule, I waited, letting words emerge if somebody wishes, and if they did not, we move on. Some apparently innocuous objects lead to talking about Marcella testing or evaluating herself. She had for a while been experiencing severe fatigue, medically diagnosed as a condition. Fatigue influenced their decision to simplify life by moving home. She took various vitamins, which stood beside the cooker, but did not want to explain them all. When the phone rang, she used the speakerphone and said she would test her hearing by using the phone volume as a reference point. She played online games to test her memory. She would use the mirror beside the front door to check how she looked, not only her outfits day by day but also to examine her own face and how it was changing. Self-testing was not uncommon in this project, happening around different objects and activities in different homes. For Marcella, it was specifically informed by her experience looking after her mother, who used to live nearby and unhappily developed a form of senile dementia. Marcella used to have to examine aspects of her mother’s home for signs of her condition and that she was okay: the cooking, the cleaning, leaving pots burning on the stove, or odd disruptions of routines. ‘When they reach a certain age, they begin to hide things’, she said, meaning that her mother was worried about the reasons why things in her home were not as they should be. Suspecting burglars or that people were getting into the house when she was not there and moving things around, Marcella’s mother began to hide certain things to protect them, like soap or tea. More recently, comparable patterns had begun to emerge with Marcella’s brother. These experiences evidently informed Marcella’s own decisions in life, her sense of how she is organising her world, how she is exercising and indeed it is clear she deserved a lifestyle in which she was enjoying herself and was, at last, relaxing.

The home of another couple with whom we worked on this project illustrates a profoundly different way of working with temporality. Hugh and his wife Bernie lived in a town in County Wexford in an old house they had occupied ever since they were first married in the 1950s. This house, although rented, therefore, acted as a record of their married lives together. The home represents a ‘layered’ approach, the accretion of things, memories and connections over time. The attitudes, intentions, relationships, practices and identities of Hugh and Bernie mirror this layering in different ways. ‘I never throw things away’, Hugh commented to me (Figure 8.2).

When Hugh gave me a tour on the first day, one of the things he pointed out was a collection of paint pots at the bottom of the stairs. Hugh used to paint the house annually, in fits and starts, beginning in one room, and then moving through gradually, before beginning again. In places the continuously emerging damp can stain the walls of the old house, so the paint would cover over the bubbles. This continued for decades, with the rooms changing colour at intervals. He had a set of painting overalls, which are kept upstairs. However, the previous year he was painting up on a chair and felt dizzy and unsafe. He found that he was now unable to lift his arms up above his head, and a periodic feeling of dizziness worried him. So he stopped the painting. He was now starting to see damp in a few places and it bothered him a lot. ‘You only feel old when you can’t do something’, he said. The house was here recognised as an ongoing project, never finished, but the sense of temporal drift when he could not keep up with the changing walls constituted the feeling of ageing.

A metaphor of layering can also be applied to relationships. Hugh and Bernie’s family pictures were on display on the walls of the living room. Each of their four children, and their respective families with grandchildren, had a section of the walls to themselves. The pictures were nailed up in groups, at varying heights and in different frames. One picture was of Hugh at the local community hall, because he played the tuba in a local brass band, and in that photo you could see behind him how the photos on the wall of the band house look just the same, set out in higgledy piggledy clusters year on year. Just as a community organisation develops its sense of history over the decades through each generation adding its annual photos and images, so did Hugh and Bernie at home. As each grandchild came along, a photo was put up. There were images of two grandchildren who tragically passed away when very young, only a week old, and they too were remembered. The day before I visited had been brought to the hospital to meet their new-born latest grandchild. Naturally, talk of babies and children threaded through the day.

This layered sense of how things change related to a philosophy of ‘if it isn’t broken don’t fix it’. This is what Hugh said when we talked about the bathroom. When they got married, the house had no indoor bathroom, and they used to have baths in a big tub in the kitchen and go to the toilet out the back. At one time, however, Hugh experienced a small revelation. The house is built on a corner of two streets, so by knocking through the wall at the other side, and locating a new front door there, he realised they could convert the small front hall into a bathroom. So they did the work, changed address from one street to the other and found themselves with a long, thin bathroom with a big old front door at the end. Now this meant that the electricity meter and fuses were now located in the bathroom, against safety regulations. Until 3 years previously, it had remained that way. Periodically, the electricity man would come and read the meter and say, like Hugh, ‘If it’s not broken, don’t fix it’. Eventually, as Ireland changed and building regulations began to be carefully enforced, they had to relocate the meter.

Bernie and Hugh had no difficulty with the idea of me, as an ethnographer, participating in their ‘daily routine’. Having lived in this place for 50 years, they did have definite patterns. It was Friday, and so we ate fish. On other weekdays were specific social events: the band, GAA, church, or the womens’ association. Even though they did not manage to attend all the community events they used to, they still knew the community rhythms of when and where the redundant ones were meeting. Twice a day Hugh took a walk, and I joined him. He provided a running commentary on shops, families, hotels, fires, deaths, traditions, markets and more. The landscape of the town was peppered with disappearances and reappearances. We trekked across the open tarmac of a supermarket car park which used to be buildings, followed the dockside, and went through the old narrow streets in the centre. As we returned up the hill to the house, Hugh had to stop and catch his breath. This was, he commented, the same hill he climbed 50 years ago to get married. It is also a hill which processes down with his band sometimes. Each day, how high he reaches up the hill before resting, are measures of his body’s abilities. ‘One day at a time’, Hugh said, and in a conversation when I asked especially about how they make plans, he responded, ‘you get up in the morning, you plan the day to the best of your ability’.

These two households, therefore, illustrate two very different kinds of temporal frameworks, as manifested directly in the nature of the stratigraphy of objects and surfaces. One can infer that how designed things and services would make their way into these homes would be very different. So many of these objects are also materialisations of relationships; they are given and received, negotiated or they facilitate ways of socialising with specific people. Corresponding so closely with the rhythms of relationships, the material culture also indicates the sorts of aspiration for independent living which designing in this field is intended to support.

Because of the nature of our work, we were especially interested in the multiple instances which we found where people seemed to be experiencing their own bodily age. One common pattern, for example, was the significance of staircases in some homes. Breathlessness on the landings of two-storey homes becomes a common way to feel a moment of ageing. If one is working with sensors which undertake this kind of work, detecting changes in walking as an indication of ageing or the likelihood of falls, then this does not mean they should be on the stairs, rather it clarifies the options designers and engineers have. If such sensors are located on stairs, their work makes better sense to the people it is intended to help and becomes integrated with work already being done. If located elsewhere, for example, for technical reasons, then such sensors may still work well, but the work of making sense of them to people is greater.

Every informant who helped us had different kinds of significant things in their homes which seemed to push themselves forwards into consciousness at this time of life, and these were very often indicative of the notion of features, apparently minor emergent phenomena and patterns. What such details and objects ‘did’ was often suggestive of what design features might do, and like archaeological features they presented a strong sense of relational temporality. Hugh had his redundant paints, a remnant from a past indicating the need for a new material solution. Marcella had her specific use of the speakerphone volume, and her use of memory games which was an obvious and very direct design space. Other informants talked of plants, televisions, satellite dishes, tools or chairs which could be anticipations of aspects of life which could become predominant in the living spaces or might be redundant echoes from previous phases of life. The material culture we explored was not necessarily, in any one instance, evidence of a functioning routinised system in which each element played a supportive part. They were often rather mechanisms of the articulation of change around tension-filled experiences and relationships. That means in turn that one aspect of the work of the design anthropologist is not only to communicate a sense of other lifestyles into which design may engage but also to gain a sense of how people themselves deploy designed things as a conscious engagement with what they see as their own notion of progress and change.

## Making design tensions and ethnographic features explicit

In the Material Culture Ireland project, there were further phases of work, which I will not go into here in too much detail, as they moved into the domain of IP and concept-testing. Importantly, however, those phases were based upon and originated in the initial phase of ‘open’ ethnography which I have outlined.

One later phase of the work involved working on particular aspects of life which had emerged during the initial phase. This was not done in a systematic fashion, but according to the judgement of individual ethnographers and as an exercise in their exploration of the context. Such exercises included asking people to ‘perform’ activities, for video, and ‘what if’ scenarios. It involved selecting certain significant objects and asking people to collage elements of the home according to key themes (see Drazin and Roberts 2009). It involved putting coloured postits on objects according to what people envisaged changing in their home, or staying the same in perpetuity. In other homes, we asked informants to ‘perform’ significant tasks, in an attempt to re-imagine them. These kinds of more artistic or designerly ‘probes’ gave a more specific dive into certain kinds of theme and materiality, in more tangible ways which designers could work with.

These kinds of designerly ‘probes’ emphasised the aspects of our informants’ lives, especially their material lives, which seemed to be of interest to the anthropologist involved (while also considering both informants’ ideas, and the interests of Intel’s engineers and designers). In practice, we were identifying and exploring ethnographic ‘features’. Having photographed and recorded significant elements in the ethnography (a speakerphone function, a staircase, a tuba, a contact book, we then experimented with ways of exploring imaginations and temporalities around those features. They are seen as potentially, but not necessarily, significant ways of knowing about the temporal stratigraphy of peoples’ lifestyles. Do people regard these kinds of unfolding features as long term or short term? Are they relics or developments? Do they represent aspirations or values which can be achieved through other material means, or is the actual form and substance of this feature enduring?

Still later phases in the work involved processes of problematisation and of conceptualisation. In the first we worked to specify design spaces which could be explored, examining patterns as we perceived them of tensions and contestations. In conceptualisation meanwhile we developed a wide range of sets of ideas for possible services, products, and applications of technology. Some of these ideas involved further field engagements and explorations.

These exercises were examples of an alternation of the work of contextualisation and of conceptualisation (see Chapter 5). At one moment, a design anthropologist is working to explicate and account for individual phenomena through working to develop a sense of the surrounding frameworks and backgrounds, considering which elements are different but go together. In another moment of design anthropology, emergent designs, in the form of things which are design concepts, introduce new frameworks and lifestyles, new normalities and new contexts and assemblages. This elastic process is not a uniform one, and in the examples I have given, it can be seen that there are at least two ways it can unfold in the context of a home in Ireland. On the one hand, in homes like Hugh and Bernie’s, ongoing and incremental change is materially asserted. Diverse elements jostle for attention, each one very evidently visible as an incremental change, with a gritty relation of before-and-after consciously manifested throughout. In Mark and Marcella’s, the work of temporal unity involves a material revolution and renovation, in which the world presents itself as very coherently designed and considered. These two households both think about time as an overarching structure, although they differ in their ideas about that structure, while their material homes actually manifest a multiplicity of temporalities in their everyday experience.

Because things constantly remind people of how they relate to others, sometimes conscious relationships with specific individuals, at other times anonymous or group connections, they then possess a constant sense of reciprocity, of the contingencies of possession. This sense of social rhythm, the potential for social movement and stasis, is politically charged and full of friction. This means that in a design situation, acting to change the material world is rarely a question of simple free will. Whatever a ‘user’ or ‘designer’ figure wishes, they are operating in a socially crowded situation. What is important to realise is that it is the frictional possibilities of things, the social sensation as we work with things, which enables people to build a sense of trajectory and change. In a similar way to the movement of boats or cars being dependent upon friction, time too has its own form of social friction.

Researching material culture in this fashion is one significant way by which design anthropology can work to destabilise some of the presumptions of studio-based design about working ‘in’ time. Temporality and the qualities of time become, when we give attention to objects, unexpected. By doing so, we can then work to address some of the concerns of the theorists we have mentioned: Fry’s (1999: 2008) concern with the world becoming defutured, Rabinow’s (2007) with the black-boxed self-evidence of the contemporary moment, Suchman’s (2011) with critiquing the micropolitics of design, and Otto and Smith’s (2013) broader call to extend and work with notions of mutual pasts and futures. Change is generally to be found ‘out there’, through social research and ethnography.

## Consequences: the importance of material things for working with change

In this chapter and the previous one, I have been discussing how ideas of futures are important heuristic concepts for design anthropologists. In the previous chapter, I outlined several of the ways in which they can engineer the articulation between different temporal frameworks, largely by working across the boundaries between professional design sites and everyday ones. I suggested that futures are often not so much made or created as encountered and confronted as radical ‘others’. In this chapter, I have gone into more depth into the role of ethnographies of material culture when considering change. I have argued that material objects constitute a design relation, manifesting peoples’ own conceptualisation of their relationship to change, time and history, and that artefacts designed by, and exchanged by, powerful institutions and corporations are especially pertinent in this relation. While many ethnographic sites are understood as existing within time, and moving through it, in many instances they are experienced as heterochronous. Different objects appear to manifest different temporalities, which is one of the reasons why ethnography often appears as messy and unexpectedly varied. Working within a paradigm of design, and involving informants with the powerful institutions who undertake contemporary design, exaggerates this sense of heterochronicity. Within these messy everyday contexts where they undertake research, many design anthropologists often pay particular attention to certain elements which emerge as interesting during the work, which we can call ethnographic features. Working with such features is one way in which they explore the temporal frameworks involved in a site.

Researching potential design sites through their material culture in this way has implications for the subjects of design, for example the nature of the users, and for how we re-imagine their lives. Futuring can be understood as a space in which we design ‘for’ users, in that we research a relatively stable subject or user, and then imagine various futures ‘for’ that person. The sorts of ethnographic examples which are reported in most design ethnographic work do give an impression of stability. I have literally repeated a story about painting walls and watching them dry, the epitome of boredom. Many design ethnographies are very similar, repeating the grinding routines of life. Designing for sustainability and recycling can involve observing many people taking out their rubbish. Designing exciting new IT can involve watching people click on a screen or tapping on a photocopier panel. Anthropological work has the extraordinary talent for making the ordinary extraordinary and vice-versa. As Rabinow observes, there is a problem with the perception of contemporary life, it seems invariably self-evident, with special moments generally projected into futures and pasts. Dunne and Raby (2013) likewise point out how futures are a way of breaking out of this banality, and commenting on presents through grounded imaginations of futures.

In the ethnographic examples, I have presented, however, the contemporary subject of design research is not necessarily a banal and stable subject. The people with whom I worked were generally very conscious of a process which we call ageing, and this presents the researcher with contrasting accounts of who they are. Verbal data, based on conversations and interviews, throws up a vast amount of information which is often very certain and considered. After years of life, the knowledge, expertise and experience of many of our informants was huge. They were able to fully answer a very wide range of questions and knew their minds on a wide range of topics. At the same time, there was a deep consciousness of how they themselves might change, which emerged more through talking about things than about themselves. The ageing self can be an uneasy subject for design work, such that designing ‘for’ is particularly difficult.

By researching material culture, the design anthropologist is better able to frame people who benefit from design (so-called users) as cultural projects in themselves. One can produce a sense of contextual connection between sites of design, since objects have a tendency to cross-cut. Cultural understanding becomes an appreciation of forms of objectification, with the forms, materials and qualities of design work becoming socially pertinent. One gains a better sense of the ways in which people manifest their own relationship to changes and to progress. The political frameworks of everyday life and of institutions are thereby juxtaposed, to produce ‘critical materialisms’ capable of challenging professional design’s projects.

## Summary

This chapter has argued that designed things manifest peoples’ relationship to change, history and progress. Through material culture, people express their own relationship to change and design, rather than being simply passive in the face of infrastructures of change.

Material things are often implicitly understood as being like social vectors, meaning they have temporal qualities such as endurance and social trajectory. By implication, society and culture are not to be understood as systems which reproduce constancy, but as about managing stable ways of changing.

Design anthropology should not see design work fitting into an abstract and fixed structure of time. Rather, temporality is experienced in designed things, and generally understood as diverse and disjunctive. Designed things are temporally dissimilar and emergent, not necessarily understood as existing in the same moment.

While contemporary design appreciates temporal messiness, 20th-century modernism worked to create a more pure understanding of time where designed things coexist in a contemporary moment. Design and design anthropology are now having to deal with this modernist legacy.

Design can be understood as a critical materialism, exploring cultural and relational dissonance through material forms and substances.

Because it does not only happen in time, but actively creates dissonant temporalities, in design work, designing futures and remembering pasts are convergent and often indistinguishable activities. Designed things often appear as temporal disjunctures, but future and past orientations can appear similar.

People use material culture – objects, forms, and substances – to shape and address future possibilities. Hence, in practice, working on future is often a case of working with materialised pasts. The sense of friction between convergent temporalities is often what produces a sense of direction for future change. The implicit disjunctive temporalities of many social contexts embody and express, by contrasts of pasts and futures, what kinds of direction people are envisaging for themselves.

## EPILOGUE

Design is emergent and can be difficult to describe. It is about as easy to define design as a firework display. I favour Appadurai’s argument that it can be seen as an infrastructure which helps objects in their search for contexts, but there are many other interpretations, each with its advantages and flaws, and useful for particular purposes. Design has been described in this book as a materiality (a vernacular social paradigm for appreciating the material world), as a cynical and encompassing social ‘foam’ and as a fluid push-and-pull field comparable to waves. Design can be a finished product, the work leading to it and the intentionality behind it, and in addition, we live at a time when there are a million different experts on design, each one authoritative. If the reader has gained a broader sense of these manifold possible perspectives on design, a sense of the need to develop thinking suitable for their particular project and an awareness of the problems they will encounter, then this book has succeeded.

My account of design anthropology has been divided between the need for breadth and for depth. I have set out the wider intellectual territory with diverse examples, and also tried to build a more coherent specific argument about design anthropology and material culture. At the outset, we looked at a range of reasons for interdisciplinary engagement, reasons why design has turned more and more to social research and commentary and why anthropologists have turned to design. I suggested that we are living in a ‘third age’ of design anthropology, when the perception and tools of design have become ubiquitous and increasingly a part of everyday mass culture. Dispersed design does not only mean that the capacity to remake habitats has become widespread, but also that the work of rethinking human worlds has become normal and expected. One of design anthropology’s main concerns is dealing with this transformation, and with the cooption of design into the very fabric of the structures of global society through the aspiration for a well-thought world.

The book then examined ways of thinking about design culture, suggesting it has particular characteristics and fascinations. Because it is a cultural field which happens by definition at points of social tension, between the professional ‘push’ of designers’ concepts, and the grassroots ‘pull’ of the sites where people live who may use design, material culture is one important way of thinking about design phenomena. One way of thinking about what makes an ‘object’, is that it is basically something capable of moving between people, or ‘subjects’. In Chapter 3, the argument ran, we can think about designed things as wave-like, entities existing in social time and space, with directional qualities. By doing so, we can transcend the trap of simply thinking about design as circular subject–object processes, which would see designers and consumers as creating themselves through their favourite things. Instead, we can help to open up the appreciation of design as a global and largely corporatised knowledge economy, of manifest concepts exchanged between mutually anonymous people and groups. The perceived divide between the creative ‘us’ and the undesigning ‘them’ is how the thinking work which supports contemporary capitalism is structured, and it is a structure which can also mystify or obscure that work.

We then looked in more detail at how different design anthropologists would position themselves within that political economy, the work they do, what they produce, their orientation to knowledge and data and their social and political attitudes to the work. Chapter 4 tried to hammer home the point that contemporary thinking is work, and that it happens collectively, not only individually. The upshot of this discussion was to suggest that design anthropology produces forms of understanding which are negotiations of the problematic tensions arising in the spaces between sites of professional design and everyday lives. In that situation, the thinking outputs of the work are heuristic. They are best fit, experimental ways of thinking about specific issues.

In the second half of the book, we examined three particular areas of heuristic thinking. Again, the aim was to give a brief overview of some key approaches, their merits and drawbacks, with reference to examples, before proposing some more targeted ways of proceeding. Designers, engineers, architects, programmers and other creators look to anthropologists to give them social contexts, value propositions and a sense of appropriate change. It is possible to deliver on these demands, so long as we accept that they are collaborative interpretations grounded in observation, not facts, and that they are often critical commentaries on professional cultures.

When we examine each of these heuristic ideas in turn, we witness the fluidity of culture in design. People change when they engage with design’s proposals, projects and institutions. This book argues for a human-centredness in design work, against post-humanism. Yet the figure who is the focus of the work is not fixed. The potentially contagious contextuality of design, the associativeness by which lifestyles and frameworks for living are constantly evoked, is capable of rendering individual people as themselves reproducible contexts for living, as combinations of elements. Likewise, systemic value transformations in design work are capable of disrupting the social ecosystems which support people existing ‘as themselves’. Most significantly, when examining notions of change and futuring, futures can be understood as emergent in specific ethnographic features which design anthropologists focus on. Design research is capable of confronting people with the possibility of emergent radical otherness, envisaging and then implementing alternative ways for them to be themselves. Radical change is not therefore to be dismissed as ‘imaginaries’, it is as real as memories, and often indistinguishable from them. This is partly because when significant design emerges, it happens with the absolute conviction of its inevitable, normal existence. It just makes sense. A second reason is that design is part of a global-industrial infrastructure bigger than any individual, which has real power to implement change.

The final chapter demonstrated how these heuristic, collaborative rethinks about contexts, values and futures happen within heavily ‘humanised’ environments. Peoples’ lives are pervaded by the reminders of other people, whose presence produces spatially and temporally heterogeneous and complex environments for the design anthropologist to work in. I argued for the benefits of ethnographic research into material culture, as a way of enabling people to work appropriately with multiple temporalities through everyday objects.

Through the course of the book, I have cited a wide range of examples of different projects and outputs: designed things, sketches, problem spaces, art installations, ethnographic data and reports. The forms and aims of these various outputs are not what make them design anthropology, but their referentiality, the relation of being ‘for’ communities and people. Some of these outputs are, like traditional anthropology, modes of representing and interpreting human lives. Other artefacts are, like design, ways of envisioning and creating. In the middle are things which are capable of helping people to think cross-culturally, across many sites. Design anthropology products are more often the third kind of outputs, not necessarily the same as either design or anthropology. For example, a designer might make a ‘care app’, and a design ethnographer might suggest what sort of care would benefit a particular site or community. A design anthropologist might facilitate ways of cultural thinking about that app and its creation, which facilitate engagements across different sites by making sure it ‘makes sense’. In one part of the world, the new app might perform work for individuals responsible for care, while in another it might be better used in a family group. In each site, people expect products which in a sense have considered their situation. If you do some ethnography in one community, and build a product for that community, then you have done a piece of impactful design but it has limited application and limited consideration, and you have missed an opportunity. To engage in culturally sensitive and site-sensitive design, enduring outputs are required which can help both professional and vernacular designers think about the social issues. In making these potentially cross-culturally interpretive connections, design anthropology outputs can be both critical and productive. Most importantly, they occupy and bridge the spaces between everyday life and professional creative sites, the spaces in which the anonymity and social connection of modernity is produced.

## Why is design anthropology important?

This capacity for ‘cultural thinking’ or ‘ethnographic thinking’ through artefacts offers a middle way in design research, between arguments for the primacy of technical innovation or for the primacy of sites of living. It recognises that neither design studios nor peoples’ lifestyles are actually strong enough to endure as independent and autonomous entities for any significant period of time. Unfortunately, in the contemporary world, no social entity is so self-sufficient that it can exist independently in and of itself. Communities, persons, identities, processes, things and practices all exist as they are by the grace of their positioning in much wider globa processes, and in reference to other phenomena (Appadurai 2013). This observation also applies to the very subject of contemporary design, the person, who is in some ways a fixed and reliable point in social space, but in other terms wholly changeable and negotiable. Human-centred design evidently constructs people, not only knowledge of people but also people themselves, as it proceeds. This uncertain global-industrial reality is a part of the ambiguous tragedy of a ‘designed world’, which consequently should be a world which is well considered.

Contemporary design’s diverse institutions and processes are equally capable of cultivating or undermining humanity. These sorts of processes can happen on a grand scale (Fry 2012), and also in everyday life when people and communities (Escobar 2018; Manzini 2015) may feel diminished by designs intended to benefit them. The global macrocosms and everyday microcosms of human life are directly connected, by the capacity for mass reproduction of small innovations, mass communications, the transportation of materials and goods and the cultural work by which design is constantly evoking frameworks for living. The work of small design groups can become globally influential. Pieces of research undertaken with individuals and small groups can easily become the guiding lights of creative work: one specific user study, a ‘deep’ undestanding, can create an app which shakes the world. It would be good to ensure such work is culturally, cross-culturally and contextually well informed, in other words that we recognise we are not only building things for individuals, but also for cultural frameworks.

Through the book, we have examined some of the difficulties faced by design anthropologists. Many of them arise from the ambivalence of the pursuit of progress (Escobar 2018). A concern with the future and with change creates a sense of ownership over designs for the future, and places a sense of inequality at the heart of design research relationships (Suchman 2011). On a grand scale, human futures are being colonised and possessed, resulting in de-futuring (Fry 2012). A lot of research takes as its primary unit of analysis the individual or the self, whether or not people see themselves living as self-interested individuals, and can mitigate against the many social lenses through which people perceive themselves (Gershon 2011). Too often, humancentred work leads to designing cultural silos, sharing a sense of their own exclusivity, within which people can rationalise away others’ concerns (Sloterdijk 2011, 2016). We should be addressing the facts that a personally designed world is not a personally owned world, and that contemporary design lacks an adequate theory of liberty.

Anthropologists in general need to recognise the modern constitution of design: as broadbased, multidisciplinary, interdisciplinary and institutionalised, while engaging with humanity in general. In this book, I am arguing that there is an implicit cosmopolitanism in those moments when people engage with design and designed things. The prevalent discourses of contemporary economic and political life are neoliberal, imagining people as individuals who desire goods in a market. By contrast with neoliberalism, a world of designed things and services is an animated pluriverse, constantly presencing the intentions, anticipations and social imaginations of ‘others’, other people in other places and times.

This work of consideration of others also helps us understand some of the problematics of modern design. Making ‘people’ the focus of design provides the potential for an operation of creative fragmentation of selfhood in the modern world, by which people experience and confront new emergent versions of themselves.

## Positions on an engaged design culture

My approach to design culture is one among a diversity, and there is a longer intellectual history behind the study of material culture and the concepts of care and alterity. Three recent studies stand out. Fry’s (2008, 2010, 2012) magisterial trilogy presents a deeply philosophical argument for design as being about new ways of becoming human. Starting from a Heideggerian perspective, his thinking comprises a radical argument for how design as it has been practised, focussed on the short-term aims of design users, puts the future of our species and world at risk. A radical change in modes of being human is required. Rather than using design to address the issues of material sustainability ‘out there’, he suggests design should develop new modes of sustainment, or ways of being. His is the most up-to-date argument that design itself can be the problem (following in the tradition of Papanek 1971), and that design anthropology can also be a part of that problem. Traditional ethnographies, where the design anthropologist becomes embedded in one community, lead to successful products, but ironically do not necessarily gain a perspective on the problems of a broader global humanity and how design can cause them. Fry’s work makes this point very well, but at the same time, lacks specific guidance on how to achieve these putative new ways of being. It does not wrestle with the difficulties of ethnographic participation, of actually trying to be within other peoples’ worlds and appreciate the other ways of being which already exist.

Escobar (2018) meanwhile represents currently the most profoundly anthropological interpretation of design culture, informed by extensive long-term ethnography. Escobar is interested in the ways that design has displaced ‘development’ as a primary global paradigm. For much of the 20th century, he argues, a monolithic notion of progress through development was used to support a perceived global hierarchy. In practice, rather than pursue progress, communities experience various crises (environmental, energy, poverty, meaning) which are both local and global, some caused by the notion of progress itself. Paradigms of community action and organisation which come from outside, such as development, prove inadequate. He advances an understanding of design as a way in which local communities constantly create and recreate their worlds (again, following Papanek 1971), and he advocates processes of ‘autonomous design’ and ‘design for transitions’ (Irwin 2015). The monolithic ‘universe’ of development is replaced in this model by a ‘pluriverse’ of many paradigms. The resulting image of human existence is a social pluriverse of many worlds being designed: ‘I present ontological design as a means to think about, and contribute to, the transition from the hegemony of modernity’s one-world ontology to a pluriverse of socionatural configurations; in this context, designs for the pluriverse becomes a tool for reimagining and reconstructing local worlds (Escobar 2018: 4).

Escobar’s very valuable analysis, which I have very crudely summarised, builds a bridge from design work to anthropological work. In doing so, it emphasises notions of community and localism, and design as the work of world-making. ‘Every community practices the design of itself’ (Escobar 2018: 184). This argument is however less good at appreciating two points. It does not particularly appreciate material culture thinking, such as explaining the capacity for designed things to move across local social worlds. Second, it situates design culture in how local and regional human action opposes itself against a systemic, hegemonic and corporatist globalism, when design can also be a means by which an inevitably local existence makes meaningful and negotiated connections globally. Professional modes of design praxis are often misunderstood as processes which simply fragment localised traditions of creativity (Tunstall 2013), when we can also emphasise their capacities for global connection (Appadurai 2013), to help constitute the cultural worlds in Escobar’s pluriverse. Material objects and infrastructures are sometimes misunderstood as passive resources for culture, rather than central to it, and hence manifesting a culture which cross-cuts many sites globally.

Third, Manzini (2015) represents an influential crop of recent thinking about design as an often problematic economy of knowledge. He identifies peoples’ connection with design as closely linked to contemporary equality and inequality. As design work is increasingly democratised, and people with no design training recategorise their everyday work as designing, there is a related crisis in the authority of professional designers. A change of design practice becomes necessary, not only rethinking things but also rethinking forms of social organisation (see also Irwin 2015, DiSalvo 2012, Fuad-Luke 2009). One implication is a strong emphasis on understanding design in terms of its social structures, and the relations between particular people and communities, more than understanding design as cultural understandings. We can, in short, think about design more in terms of mutual understanding, or in terms of mutual collaboration.

These critical accounts represent just some of the recent work which has inspired this book. As can be seen, they draw on a range of intellectual traditions to wrestle with the key problems of human difference and common interest. What much of this work is missing is an appreciation of the actual environment within which design anthropology works, which cross-cuts many institutions. Private businesses are the driving engine of the field, but very large numbers of design anthropologists are also in the public sector, in charities and in academia. There is also a divide between critical commentaries and the practice of design anthropology. Many corporate anthropologists have to present the value of their work as short-term productivity, but are well aware that their actual value to the company lies in longer term critique of their value models. The converse applies in academia. An anthropological researcher, typically working on short contracts or project by project, needs to be able to translate the intellectual and critical contributions they can make into a certain amount of research time and a justified budget. Carving out that crucial extra day or week for open-ended ethnography, or that extra few days at the end to produce a wider commentary, needs a better analysis of the value of cultural thinking work, and an awareness of how it manifests itself in measurable things and artefacts. This book has tried to address these areas, particularly with its focus on design things. It has also founded its understanding of design culture within a longer term history of conceptualising care and alterity.

## Design things and anthropologies of care and alterity

If we understand design largely in terms of a material culture of concepts, then design anthropology amounts to the project of a well-thought human world, by which acts of consideration, manifested in material things, ultimately relate people to one another. This means design can be interpreted as a culture of care. Multiple definitions of care have been developed over the decades (Drazin 2011), but in recent years, it has been increasingly understood as a form of ethical reasoning, an approach which derives from feminist writing and thinking. Gilligan (2016, but originally published in 1982) put care on the ethical map in her research about how women in America talked about their work looking after homes and families. When many of them talked about their work in terms of other members of their families, for example what their children or husbands might want or need, Gilligan did not simply reject their accounts as selferasing. Rather, she pointed out that what was happening was a radically different mode of rationalising. The women she worked with valued the work of empathy, of appreciating the points of view and interests of other people, different from themselves. This was the beginning of elevating the concept of ‘care’ as an entry point into alternative modes of reasoning and of ethics (Bowden 1996, Noddings 2003). During this century, care-based forms of ethical reasoning have been advancing within the institutions of global governance (Robinson 1999). Care and caring, some argue, are not only modes of reasoning, but also can be understood as infrastructures and social networks which have developed based upon the labour and thinking work of care. Care-based ethical thinking emphasises mutual engagement and ideas of alterity, by which people attempt to engage with others very different from themselves.

Design has been described as an economy of care by Keith Murphy (2015), specifically in relation to Swedish national design traditions. When I characterise my own understanding of design as related to care, I do not mean it is the same thing. It lacks the immediate human contact of most caring work, and generally lacks the emotion (in spite of the existence of emotional design as an approach; Norman 2005). Nor do I subscribe to the idea of care as a post-human mode of being, a radically different reality (Haraway 2007, see Puig de la Bellacasa 2017). I simply mean that designed things necessitate and manifest consideration for other people, who are by definition anonymous and hence ‘other’, and that design as a field can be understood as a political economy of concepts.

That knowledge is situated means that knowing and thinking are unconceivable without the multitude of relations that make possible the worlds we think with… relations of thinking and knowing require care and affect how we care.

(Puig de La Bellacasa 2017: 69)

To put this rather simplistically, contemporary design is often misunderstood as ‘what you know’ when it is more ‘who you know about’. Beyond knowing material things, styles, forms, substances, technical functions and processes, it is about things through which we know about other people and their lifestyles. This position on design is more epistemological than ontological, about ways of knowing more than ways of being (in contrast to Fry, mentioned previously). In a broader sense, things which are understood as designed are intrinsically caring because they are perceived as things which have been intended for a certain kind of person. Designed things imply a sense of cultural appreciation, that a designer has within this thing evoked an imagination of a lifestyle, an identity, a way of existing, which may or may not be similar to the designer’s own.

Alterity, or otherness, is to a certain extent implied in the idea of care. The first to connect alterity to design was Castoriadis (1998, but first published in French in 1975). In his critique of rationalist modernism, he argued that change throughout history did not amount to progress through predictability and rational action, so much as the emergence of encounters with radically different and unexpected cultural phenomena. From these roots, the concept of alterity has two strands. One is the phenomenological tradition, within which people both imagine and experience an utterly different and disorienting kind of human existence. Leistle (2016) characterises these interlinked understandings as ‘radical’ and ‘empirical’ otherness. Designed things manifest a sense of an engagement with not only a ‘significant other’ person, but also a kind of ‘radical other’ person, the ‘new user’ who is breaking new cultural grounds and new lifestyles. The second strand in the history of alterity has been developed in debates about cosmopolitanism (Hannerz 1990; Werbner 2008), which examine the multiple conceptions of cultural pluralism and how people work with it and across it. Studies of the many different forms of cosmopolitanism place alterity at the heart of an increasingly globalising world.

Because they evoke significant but anonymous other humans, designed things can be considered to manifest an imagination of different people, who are radically different in the sense that their lifestyles are temporally different, either radically historical and existing in the past (Fabian 1983), or radically new and progressive. Alterity is then both manifested in material things (Taussig 1993), and forms a part of the many parallel forms of capitalist modernity in the world (Knauft 2002). As well as responding to already-existing problems, demands, needs or desires, design culture also produces a problematic sense of the ascription of ways of being. Designed things articulate the public imagination, and thinking, about newly desired ways of living and being, with private and personal experience. As I discussed in Chapter 5, they do this through particular processes of contextualisation and association. The resulting compounded fracture of public and private, of social identity and personal self, is identified by Friedman (2002) as the reason why alterity is not only an encounter with people ‘out there’, but also something which people feel in themselves:

From the fracture springs the well-known experience of alterity. Alterity, the founding dynamic of modernism, is an understanding of the world in which identity is reduced increasingly to social role, achieved rather than ascribed, temporary, and even alienated from the subject.

(Friedman 2002: 296)

My own experience of design work resonates with this notion of self-alienation and selfdiscovery. I am struck by the paradox of how designers are capable of using routinised and welldefined methods, step by step, to progress through sequences of wholly unexpected and radically surprising revelations (Drazin 2013). Many design processes comprise in a large number of small repetitive steps, but often one of these small steps crosses a line to evoke a new kind of existence. Cultures of design, from studio work to vernacular mass culture, are in this sense one of the most important frontiers of cultural study. Working with design is comparable to moments of ‘first contact’, something which many anthropologists will be familiar with. First contact in parts of the world such as Amazonia, Papua New Guinea, or the Andaman Islands, involves encountering communities set apart from exchanges with outsiders, often through choice. In the years immediately following the end of the Cold War in Europe, when I conducted fieldwork in Romania in 1991, it was not uncommon to meet people who said they had not talked with Western Europeans before, presenting a form of ‘first contact’ between parallel industrial societies. In design, moments of experiencing unprecedented cultural difference (first contact) happen more in time than in space. These are moments when there is a sudden dawning of awareness that the world has changed, and the people in it may now be encountered anew. As I discuss in Chapters 7 and 8, design ‘discovers’ social futures through unexpected and messy experiences which designers find difficult to rationalise, as much as it invents them.

The excitement and disorientation of design is something which anthropology needs to capture and think about. There must be thousands of design studios and environments across the world, and all of them are capable of foregrounding radical but small fragmentations of human progress and change, bringing them to our attention and consciousness (Farías and Wilkie 2015). A lot of this experience of alterity involves trying to culturally ‘make sense’, and it can accompany innovation in a material sense: technical capacities, fashion looks, styles, forms, functions and the work of making human sense of them. I recall for example in HP Labs in 2002 being introduced to a new kind of ‘fax machine’, one by which they could scan an object in the UK Lab, and produce a replica in the US Lab. This, I was told, could be a radical new way to communicate. I could send people flowers of gifts instantaneously. Many years later, this technology has become commonplace and is understood not as a communication device but as a ‘3D printer’. The time interval between my delight and surprise at this artefact, and something familiar in normal human life, was a period of some years. Likewise, I was amazed at our use of an early Microsoft touchscreen computer and wireless technologies, as we worked to try to produce a new way of learning for students. iPads broke into public consciousness some years later, a relatively long time after the first commercially produced tablet computer. As various new products and services manifest new ways of achieving social ends, there are continuous and increasing wave-like encounters with radical alterity, in the sense of evoking other people who can live very different lives and have very different ways of understanding social life, to the extent of being at first incomprehensible. These are first contact moments in a very particular sense, produced not through contact between previously unconnected people, or the global movements of people, but because culture changes, people change and cultural boundaries between lifestyles emerge into consciousness. Design research methods, infrastructures and practitioners enable such first contact moments.

Being founded in care and alterity, the understanding of design culture in this book frames it as intrinsically cosmopolitan, open and appreciative of cultural difference. In that understanding lies the binding connection between design practice and anthropological practice.

## Ways of working, ways of knowing

In any particular project, a design anthropologist can find themself doing many kinds of work: forms of market research, producing designs, developing sociocultural commentaries, usertesting products using psychological metrics, building art installations or producing publicity and marketing. You can find yourself undertaking work which is by no means either design or anthropology, such as a lab experiment or a psychological analysis. We do not wholly choose the work we do, because it is always a part of a wider social whole, involving other people and agendas. However, in this book, I am trying to advance a better sense of a core territory for design anthropology. At its heart is anthropology, more than design. This is to say that an interest in people drives the field, and the work comprises in understanding, interpreting and working with human culture.

Design anthropology does differ from traditional anthropology in notable ways. The design anthropologist should work to build value and with value models in mind; with a related engaged concern with active cultural change; and with an awareness of the ever-expanding contexts through which their work happens. These concerns give design anthropologists a heightened awareness of the implications of their research. Sharing agendas to change peoples’ lives does not mean that design anthropology is to be avoided, but does mean it must expose risks which other kinds of anthropological work may conceal. The design anthropologist is generally directly working with professions who can change peoples’ lives, rather than unintentionally through the roundabout routes of publishing, public presentation and reportage. Design anthropology concerns itself with potentialities and possibilities, more than most anthropology or most design work. The ultimate aim is the pursuit of a better thought world.

In order to advance this aspiration, as a practitioner, you need to be flexible but also be prepared to justify your particular contribution. You should know the key elements of your preferred work processes, the practices in which you are a specialist and can have confidence you can make the best contribution. You need to be prepared for ways of arguing for the value of the steps involved in your work.

There are several things which design anthropologists can generally do better.

First, trust yourself. You are the anthropological specialist, and you probably have your own way of working different from what I have set out here. Prioritise your projects: the ones which are most anthropological where your skill counts the most, the ones where you are a subsidiary element or data-provider and are just making a living and the ones you do not want to do.

Second, ethnography is important, because it is contextual. Design thinking only works in association with contextual social research. Work hard to create as much, messy, contextual information as possible. This means arguing for an ‘open’ phase of research, to learn about peoples’ lives, for however short a period, before funnelling into more specific issues.

Third, the design anthropologist works to ‘presence’ people, places and things throughout the work process. This produces a more ‘animated’ and humanised institutional space. The messy ethnographic ‘stuff’ from initial research is often also the route of the anthropologist back into conceptualisation. That is, the work does not only involve moving from observation to abstract understandings (models, values, principles, new paradigms, insights), but after that, actual material ways to re-engage using form, colour, texture, sensation and the worlds of informants’ lives.

Fourth, it is important to try to argue to maximise the space of cultural interpretation and rethinking. Ethnographic products happen early, in creating data. Design engagements happen later. The space in the middle is often forgotten, but that is the space where the tension is greatest, and where design anthropological outputs emerge. In that space, ideas for new products and services are not only propositions and ‘answers’, but also cultural questions and invitations to explore social possibilities.

Fifth, presentation is thinking work. A structured set of meetings and presentations, perhaps getting more important, is generally the motor of a project. It is also through presentations that the work of helping institutions to critique themselves happens.

Lastly, have fun. What I mean is that when a project aspire to only be ‘worthy’, it means that the aims of the work are separated from the practice. When practices and things make sense in the moment, in and of themselves, doing for the sake of doing or thinking for the sake of thinking, then that is often a clear sign you are on the right track.
