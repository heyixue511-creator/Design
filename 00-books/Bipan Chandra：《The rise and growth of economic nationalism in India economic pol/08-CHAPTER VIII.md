## Labour

We have become so accustomed to the poverty, squalor, and degradation of these unfortunate classes that we have become callous, and our rulers, as well as ourselves, have no qualms of conscience at their condition, which is truly the result of the neglect and oppression of the higher classes of the community and of the state, during centuries.

The development of modern factories, mines, transport, and plantations resulted in the emergence during the second half of the 19th century of an entirely new class in Indian society, namely the industrial working class. Up to the year 188o-81, its size was as yet insignificant. In tliat year, cotton mills, jute mills and coal mines employed 47,955, 35,235 and 11,969 operatives respectively. By 1905-o6, however, the dimensions of this new class had reached respectable proportions, at least in absolute terms. In that year, cotton mills, jute mills, and coal mines provided employment to 212,720, 144,879 and 89,995 persons, respectively. Nearly 7oo,ooo workers were employed in modern factories, worked by mechanical power, alone.i

With the advent of modern industrialism and the accompanying capitalist system came all the evils that had earlier warped the lives of generations of English workers; and the early generations of the Indian men, women and children working in modern industries were subjected to as ruthless and

revolting an exploitation as known to the modern man. The worst feature of early factory life in India was the long hours put in by the mill hands, there being no limit to the number of hours a factory could work. In the beginning, an average perennial factory worked daylight hours, that is '11½ hours a day, or 8o½ hours a week, in the cold weather, and 14 hours a day, or 98 hours a week, in the hot weather'.2 Later, from about 1887, when electric light was introduced in the factories, the daily hours of work were extended even further and ranged from 12½ to 16 in different localities; the worst sufferers in this respect were the weavers in Calcutta jute mills who worked from 15 to 16 hours.3 The physical misery of the workers can be well-imagined if it is kept -in view that in addition to working such long hours most of them had to spend two to three hours in walking to the mill and back.

There was, moreover, no regular and adequate system of rest periods to mitigate the harshness of such long hours of work. Some factory-owners did provide a rest interval, but it was wholly inadequate as it lasted only from 15 to 30 minutes; others 'made no stops but expected their hands to eat while tending their machines'.4 , Nor was such unremitting toil relieved by regular days of rest or holidays. The Bombay Factory Labour Commission of 1885 remarked that ‘on an average only 15 holidays are given throughout the year in Indian factories, while in England besides 1o holidays there are 52 Sundays and 52 half Saturdays, making a total of 88 days altogether.'s The result was the complete physical exhaustion of the workers, who sometimes fell fast asleep on the mill floor directly they had thrown the strap off their machines and before some of their fellow hands' were ‘able to get out of the mill doors'.6

What was even worse, women also worked the same long hours as men until 1891, when the maximum hours of work for them were fixed at 11. The children working in the early Indian factories were treated no better. Until 1881 they had to work as long as men, with the result that they 'not uncommonly' dropped down 'from exhaustion between the alleys and passages of the machines'.7 The Factories Act of 1881 limited the hours of work of children to 9 per day, defining a child as between 7 and 12 years of age! There were, however, 'many cases when the children worked as long hours as the men'.8 The Factories Act of 1891 further reduced the working hours of children to 7 per day and raised their minimum and maximum age to 9 and 14 years respectively. But both these provisions were often violated in practice.9

From the beginning, conditions of work in the seasonal factories and small factories, like cotton gins and presses, were much more horrifying and shocking. The Bombay Factory Labour Commission of 1885 pointed out that in the ginning and press works of Khandesh, in which the majority of workers were women and children, the usuaì working hours were from 4 or 5 A.M. to 7, 8, or 9 P.M.; 'but when working at high pressure they work sometimes day and night for eight days consecutively until the hands are tired out and lose .their health.'io It quoted long extracts from the evidence submitted to it giving a 'painful description'of the conditions prevailing in these industries and throwing light on 'the sad tale of great want on one side and cruel cupidity on the other'.ii One of the witnesses confessed that he had seen women labourers 'supplying the gins mechanically three parts asleep, and a child at the breast sucking one minute and throwing cotton into the machine the next'.12

The situation was made intolerable by the fact that the wages for such inhumanely long hours and back-breaking labour were paltry and wholly inadequate by any standards. The wages of men and women working in the cotton factories of Bombay ranged between Rs. 7 to 2o per month during most of this period.i3 In the smaller cotton gins and presses the wages ranged from 3 to 4 annas a day for nearly 18 hours of work.14 Moreover—and this is of equal importance—with the growth of industries and the rise in labour productivity, there was practically no change in real wages.15 And this at a time when industries 'were extremely prosperous, several mills having paid back the whole of their capital in four years'.16

What attention did the Indian national leadership—in general so deeply touched by the misery of the Indian peoplepay to the multi-sided evils of modern industrial capitalism and the intense economic suffering and exploitation of this newlyemerging social class? This question is of special importance as it involved two opposing interests both of which were, at least partially, Indian and therefore national. Here was an instance of poverty, in its widest sense, which to some extent was the product of the greed of a section of,the Indians themselves. Before the labour policy of the rising Indian leadership is examined at length, three important points deserve to be noted. Firstly, till the end of the 19th century, this policy did not develop independently, but emerged mostly as a reaction to the efforts of the Government of India at amelioration or regulation of the conditions of work of labour. Secondly, lack of comment concerning labour problems on the part of several sections of the Indian leadership is often as important for an analysis of their attitudes as direct and overt cominent. Lastly, there was a marked difference in the nationalist approach towards labour employed in modern factories, most of which were in part or in toto owned by Indian capitalists, and labour working in wholly foreign-owned plantations and railways.

## I. THE FACTORY ACT OF 1881 AND NATIONALIST REACTION

Surprisingly enough, the first impulse towards legislative action to mitigate the harshness of the evils of capitalist industrialism in India came from England, whose philanthropists and textile manufacturers joined hands to demand statutory protection for the health of the women and children einployed in Indian factories.17 As a result of the steady prompting, and ultimate direction, of the Secretary of State, who was in turn constantly prodded by the Members of Parliament and unofficial public bodies like the Manchester Chamber of Coinmerce, the Government of Bombay appointed on 25 March 1875 a Commission to investigate the conditions of work of the factory labourers in Bombay and suggest steps for their improve ment,18 The Commission could not arrive at any unanimous conclusions and its report was signed only by the President and one other member, Dr Blaney, who recommended the enactment of a simple law, providing for a working day of 12 hours, including one hour's rest, for adults, a weekly holiday, the prohibition of children under 8 from working in factories, and the limiting of the hours of work of children between 8 and 14 to 8 per day.19 The other 7 members of the Commission, 6 of whom happened to be, surprisingly enough, persons with financial stakes in the cotton industry, opposed any legislative interference in the working of the. factories.2o This gave the Government of Bombay a handle to decline to take any further action in the matter on the plea that a proper case for state intervention had not been made out and that in the existing circumstances such intervention was likely to do more harm than good to the growing industries of Bombay.21

The clamour in Britain for factory legislation in India was now intensified.22 Moreover, it gained new strength from the activities of some Indian philanthropists in Bombay, the most prominent among them being Sorabjee Shapoorji Bengalee. He gave a fillip to the agitation for factory legislation by drafting a bill proposing to limit the hours of work of men, women and children to 11, 1o and 9 respectively, these hours to include one hour of rest.23 The Government of India however refused him permission to move this Bill in the Bombay Legislative Council. Among other local philanthropic supporters of factory legislation was the Dnyanodaya, the missionary newspaper from Poona.24 Simultaneously, there were faint stirrings within the working class itself. For example, in December 1879, Rughaba Succaram, who was himself a worker and who, according to D. Chaman Lall, emerged as the first Indian labour leader, organised a meeting of the mill-hands and submitted a memorial to the government, signed by 578 workers and asking for a working day of 9 hours and a weekly holiday.25 Later, another memorial signed by $6 { \ ; } 4$ workers was presented by Balaji Ramchandra Facked.26

In consequence of the agitation in England and India and, at the same time, keeping in view the strong opposition put up by the capitalist interests in India, the Government of India introduced on $^ { 7 }$ November 1879 a mild measure of legislation in the Council of·the Governor-General of India. After many alterations and further dilution, the Bill was enacted on 15 April 1881 as the Indian Factories Act, 1881. The Act primarily dealt with the problem of child labour and fixed the minimum age of employment of children at 7 years; children between $\Im$ and 12 years were not allowed to work for more than 9 hours per day; they were to be given a daily rest of one hour, exclusive of the 9 working hours, and $4$ holidays in the month. The Act also provided for proper fencing of dangerous machinery and prompt reporting of accidents to the local government concerned. It was, however, made applicable only to factories utilising mechanical power, employing 1oo persons or more, and working móre than four months in a year. It also specifically excluded indigo factories and tea and coffee plantations from its operation.27 Moreover, the hours of work of men and women were left unregulated.

Thus, the Indian Factories Act of 1881 was quite elementary and, for all practical purposes, innocuous. This was admitted in so many words by the government itself when its Officiating Secretary wrote in à circular to the local governnients -thaf 'In framing it, the most careful regard has been paid to the representations of the millowners and commercial and other associations....Indeed, consistently with the objects in view, it would have been scarcely possible to frame a nore moderate measure.'28

The nationalist reaction to the early agitation for factory legislation and the eventual enactment of the Indian Factories

Act of 1881 was almost entirely confined to the Bombay Presidency-though justifiably so as the entire controversy on the subject primarily related to Bombay cotton mills—and was on the whole indifferent or hostile to the efforts being made to ameliorate the conditions of work of the factory workers. It may be noted, for example, that most of the important nationalist leaders of the Bombay Presidency, e.g., Dadabhai Naoroji M. G. Ranade, K. T. Telang, and Pherozeshah Mehta, as well as those of the other Presidencies failed to express themselves on the problems and sufferings of the new proletarian class which was then emerging. V.N. Mandlik, a leading public man of Bombay, who was a member of the Bombay Factory Commission of 1875, loyally voted with the majority against any enactment in favour of labour. The younger leaders like Tilak, Agarkar, and Chandavarkar of Bombay and S. N. Banerjea and the Ghose brothers, S. K. Ghose and Moti Lal Ghose, of Bengal were equally insensitive, and even opposed, to the cause of labour, if the editorial comments of, or the absence of such comments in, the Mahratta and the Indu Prakash of Bombay and the Bengalee and the Amrita Bazar Patrika of Bengal, of which they were the respective editors at the time, may be taken to represent their personal views.

In so far as the Indian newspapers were concerned, only a few —to be exact four—newspapers of Bombay wrote in support of the workers. Pleading for an Indian law similar to the British Factory Act of 1874, the Akhbarc Sowdagar of 24 November 1874 wrote: 'The millowners take advantage of the poverty of these people, and work them mercilessly. The work people ought to have at least one holiday in the week, and their working hours should be from 7 A.M. to 5½ P.M.'29 The Lok Mitra of 29 December 1878 criticised the millowners for being 'blinded by their own interests' and urged the government 'to make a reasonable decrease in the number of hours'.3o The Rast Goftar of 29 December 1878 and 19 January gave full backing to S.S. Bengalee's draft Bill.31 This paper in its issues dated 7 December 1879 and 7 November 188o, along with the Indian Spectator of 7 November 188o and the Akhbare Sowdagar of 22 March 1881, supported the Factory Bill moved by the government.32 However, even these few supporters, who were moved entirely by feelings of pity and humanity, tended to desert the field immediately after. The Lok Mitra became an opponent of labour legislation in 1879 itself.33 The Indian Spectator had already opposed, on 7 November 188o, 'undue interference with adult labour or the internal economy of the mills',34 even when expressing itself in favour of the official Bill; next year, it too moved into the camp of the opposition.35 Even the Akhbare Sowdagar occasionally wobbled in its support of factory legislation.36 Only the Rast Goftar remained true to the cause of labour up to the end.37 And only one newspaper of Bengal, the Som Prakash, favoured the passage of the Factories Act of 1881.38 Among public bodies, the Poona Sarvajanik Sabha was the only organisation to advocate the introduction of a weekly holiday and the limitation of hours of work of adults, though it criticised at the same time the draft Bill of 1879.39

On the other hand, an overwhelming majority of the Indian newspapers vehemently denied the need for any factory legislation and stoutly opposed and condemned any attempt to place on the statute book an ‘un-called for law' on the subject. In 1875 the Jame-Jamshed, the Bombay Samachar and the Arunodaya objected to the appointment of the Bombay Factory Commission on the ground that there was no need for it.4o When S. S. Bengalee brought forward his draft Factory Bill in 1878, the nationalist newspapers of Bombay and other provinces expressed strong disapproval of the measure.41 The Brahmo Public Opinion of Calcutta, the spokesman of the radical and reformist section of the Brahmo Samaj, also expressed on 27 February 1879 the view that protective legislation for factory workers was not at all necessary. The Amrita Bazar Patrika of 16 March 188o also in its usual way poured ridicule over Bengalee's efforts.

The introduction of the official Factory Bill in the Legislative Council in November 1879, the consequent deliberations by a Select Committee and the Council, and the eventual enactment of the Factories Act in 1881, were greeted with a roar of disapproval by the Indian Press. Such leading nationalist and prosocial reform newspapers as the Indu Prakash (22 March 188o, 21 March 1881, and 4 Aug. 1884), thie Gujarati (28 Nov. 188o, and 27 March 1881), the Indian Spectator (20 March 1881), the Native Opinion (27 March and 19 June 1881), and the Dnyan Prakash (30 June 1881) protested against the measure.42 An article in the otherwise sober Quarterly Journal of the Poona Sarvajanik Sabha, which served at this time as the tribune of the patriotic people of Bombay, declared that there was not 'the least justification for a legislative enactment to regulate factory labour'.43 The Mahratta, edited at the time by B. G. Tilak, lodged a strong protest against the Act in its issue of 13 March 1881. The two leading nationalist newspapers of

Bengal, viz., the Amrita Bazar Patrika and the Bengalee, condemned the final Act, as did the Bralımo Public Opinion.44 The Hindi Pradeep of Allahabad also expressed similar sentiments.45 And, of course, numerous other newspapers of Bombay and Bengal eagerly joined in the chorus of protest against the Bill and its final form, the Indian Factories Act of 1881.46

At times, many of these critics of factory legislation advanced interesting and novel reasons, many of which might sound ridiculous, if not brutal and sardonic, to the modern ear, for arriving at their conclusion. For instance, one proof offered by them to show the needlessness of any legislation was the complete absence of any complaint or demand for protection on the part of workers themselves, who were stated to be willing to work for long hours quite voluntarily.47 This view was put in a nutshell by the Amrita Bazar Patrika of 12 Nov. 188o: 'If great zulum (tyranny) is practised upon them (workers), why do they not seek employment elsewhere? Why are they afraid of being dismissed? It is clear, therefore, that they choose to remain employed in spite of the zulums.' Earlier, on 16 January 188o, it had given the perfectly sound, though gratuitous, advice to the philanthropists to go to the workers and persuade them to give up their work, instead of urging the government to pass laws.

Another argument advanced was that the men, women, and children working in factories did not require any outside help as, far from being oppressed, they were well-treated by their employers.48 It was asserted that in reality instead of being 'poor and wretched', they were 'comparatively rich and healthy'.49 It had also to be kept in view, it was said, that work in a factory was no strain on them 'because factory labour is one for which Indian labourers show a peculiar aptitude, being of a light and easy character, and so very popular with them'. The rulers of India were accused of always making the mistake of engrafting 'foreign and wholly unsuitable institutions on this country', thus showing ‘a singular want of insight into the practical working of the organism of native society'. If the foreign rulers had instead kept the general poverty of the country in view and tried to meet the true wants o the Indian society and the Indian workers, 'they would then have found that their crying need is more the want of food and the means of purchasing it, rather than the protection of their limbs or relief from over-work.'so In any case, pointed out the critics of labour legislation, the lot of the mill labourers was definitely better than that of the labourers in other fields of economic activity in India.51

This was particuarly true in regard to children since they were made to work for much longer hours in the fields or other places of work than in the factories.52 The Native Opinion of 29 December 1878 could even opine that ‘children working in these mills enjoy better health than those who attend school'.53 Furthermore, wrote the anonymous writer of the 'Factory Legislation in India' in the Journal of the Poona Sarvajanik Sabha of July 1881, the children's task in the factories was 'never considered as at all heavy, tedious, or tiresome by them at all'.s4 He further pointed out that the Factory Act was likely to lead to the dismissal of all children below 12 because the employers would prefer to minimise that way the annoying interference by the unwelcome inspectors.55 The large scale dismissal of children for this reason or due to compliance with the provisions of the Factory Act combined with the inevitable lowering of wages of the children, who were fortunate enough to retain their employment, would reduce the low income of the already very poor workers' families and would 'deprive those children of their bread' and make them a burden on their parents.56 But the worst consequence of the Act, according to these critics, in so far as children were concerned, would be a sharp increase in 'the number of juvenile criminal offenders', because 'the boys thus thrown out of employment will too often be forced to beg, borrow, or steal'.57

The strongest nationalist objection to factory legislation was, however, based on the belief that it would retard the growth of the rising cotton textile industry by raising its costs of production and thus reducing its competitive capacity vis-a-vis the mills of Lancashire.58 Some of the nationalists even donned the mantle of the prophets of doom. 'Thus our factories are now ruined', bewailed the Mahratta of 13 March 1881: 'the infant industry is doomed'. Some of the Indian leaders openly asserted that in view of the paramount need for industrial development there should be no hesitation in sacrificing the interests of the factory labourers. For example, the Ainrita Bazar Patrika of 2 September 1875 opined: 'A larger death rate amongst our operatives is far more preferable to the collapse of this rising industry.... We can, after the manufactures are fully established, seek to protect the operatives.'59 Furthermore, it was said that even from the point of view of the workers the legislation was prone to be harmful since it would have the effect of killing the goose that laid the golden eggs, for any check on the growth of the textile industry would in turn affect the earnings of, and the opportunities of employinent for, the workers themselves.6o

Some of tlie Indian writers also objected to the very notion of state interference ‘with the quiet working of private industrial enterprise which was fraught with danger.61 The proper course would be to let the disputes between employers and their employees be mutually settled.62 In any case, if the government was bent upon passing a law on the subject, the Indian leaders insisted that it should be made applicable throughout British India so that there was no discrimination against the Indian-owned industries of Bombay to the advantage of the English-owned industries of the other Presidencies.63 For similar reasons, objection was also taken to the exemption from the operation of the Act of the tea and coffee plantations and indigo factories, which were owned by Europeans, and the small cotton ginning and pressing factories, which promoted the export of raw cotton to England. This discrimination was all the more unpalatable as the conditions of work in the small ginning and pressing factories and on the tea and indigo estates were much more appalling than in the modern textile mills of Bombay. Consequently, it was said, it was the former enterprises that 'should be more carefully looked after by a government bent upon philanthropy'.64

At this stage the question naturally arose: Why was the Government of India so keen on putting a factory act on the statute book? And practically all the Indian writers who expressed an opinion on the subject came out with the answer that it was doing so under the 'powerful and irresistible, although unjust, pressure' of the jealous Lancashire magnates, who wanted, under the cloak of benevolence and philanthropy, to handicap their Indian competitors by making the Indian cloth dearer.65 For instance, the Indian Spectator of 2o March 1881 greeted the news of the final enactment of the Act with the sarcastic comment: 'It seems the lachrymose wails and philanthropic denunciations of a few short-sighted enthusiasts, worked up by the saints of Manchester, have been too much for a hysteric Government to resist.'66 The Brahmo Public Opinion of 24 March 1881 passed the crisp .and clear judgment: 'Manchester has won a surreptitious victory over the cotton industry of India.'

Some of the Indian commentators were quick in drawing political conclusions from the course of events leading to the enactment of the first Indian Factories Act. The vitriolic Amrita Bazar Patrika wrote in its issue of 17 March 1881: 'What is India? The cotton growing property of Manchester. What are Indian ryots? A species of animals created for the purpose of growing cotton for the use of Manchester.' The Mahratta, the spokesman of the rising generation of Poona nationalists, angrily commented on13 March 1881: 'This plainly indicates what we have all along held, viz. that India is not governed for India's sake but in the interests of England.' In fact, Indian people should know 'that we are a conquered nation and we are to be governed but as a conquered nation'.67 It urged the Indian people to 'do away with petitioning and begin to work ourselves.' It felt that one effective form this self-effort could take was boycott. It therefore appealed: 'Let us unite and resolve that no Manchester cloth will be worn....If we do this, hundred factory legislations cannot a whit affect our industries unless Government makes bold to shut them....Let Manchester Merchants have no cotton from India.'68

## II. THE FACTORIES ACT OF 1891 AND NATIONALIST REACTION

The insufficiency of the Indian Factories Act of 1881 in duly protecting mill-workers gave rise to further agitation for its amendment soon after it was passed. The philanthropists were dissatisfied with the provisions of the Act for obvious reasons; nor could the Act appease the Lancashire manufacturers who witnessed with alarm the growing textile production in India and the slow decline of English textile imports into the country.

In i882, the Government of Bombay invited Meade King, an English factory inspector, to investigate the working of the Factories Act in the Bombay region. He found mnany grave abuses prevalent in the factories and declared the existing law to be inadequate.69 He also made certain proposals with a view to improving the situation. The Governinent of Bombay appointed, on 23 May 1884, another Commnission to study the practicability of his suggestions and to consider the whole sub. ject in all its bearings'. The Commissioners, four of whom were the representatives of employers, recommended amendment of the original Act so as to provide for the limitation of the hours of work of children and women to 9½ and 11½ hours respectively, grant of 4 monthly holidays to them, and the raising of the minimum and maximum age of children to 9 and i4 years respectively.70 The Government of India, ta whom the Report of the Commission was forwarded for action, was. however, not convinced of the necessity for a general amendment of the existing Act and no further action in the matter was taken for the time being.71

But the matter did not rest here. Once again, there was brisk agitation in England for the application of the more stringent English factory legislation to India. The question was repeated. ly raised in the House of Commons and outside by the Mem. bers of Parliament. The Chambers of Commerce, who had dis. covered with dismay the fact that the abolition of cotton duties in India had failed to check the growth of Indian textile industry, made incessant representations to the Secretary of State for India; and the latter, in turn, constantly prodded and in. structed the Government of India to take satisfactory action.7

At this stage, however, a new element entered into the situation. Gradually, the workers themselves began to put forward their demands, though in an extremely moderate manner and on grounds of charity and humanity rather than of rights and justice. Moreover, these demands took the form of humble entreaties to the government to come to the aid of the workers. The moving spirit behind this agitation by, and on behalf of, the workers was N. M. Lokhanday, who had started, as early as 188o, the Anglo-Marathi weekly, the Din Bandhu, that made the cause of the mill-workers its raison d'etre.73 In November 1884, he organised two meetings of Bombay textile workers, which adopted unanimous resolutions demanding Sunday as a holiday for all mill-hands, restriction of working hours from 6.3o A.M. to sunset, provision for half an hour's rest at noon, payment of wages on the 15th of the month following that for which they had been earned, and provision of compensation for industrial accidents. These resolutions were incorporated in a memorial that was signed by 5,5oo millworkers and was forwarded in October i884 to the Bombay Factory Commission by Lokhanday, who claimed at this time to be the Chairman of the Mill-hands' Association.74 During the next seven years, Lokhanday and others made repeated attempts through meetings and memorials to persuade the government to legislate on the above demands of the workers.75 It would, however, be misleading to look upon Lokhanday's attempts as the beginnings of a vigorous labour movement in India. In reality, it was no movement at all.76 Moreover. Lokhanday was no labour organiser, but a mere philanthropic friend of labour, as the title of his newspaper literally pro claimed.77 As such, Lokhanday, instead of being branded as a revolutionary and punished by the government-as was done in the case of those who had helped and organised the indigo agitation—was appointed the local member of the Factory Labour Commission of 189o.

It may also be mentioned in this connection that whenever agitation for introducing factory legislation in India was started in England, the Indian and British manufacturers in India replied by launching a powerful counter-agitation for nonintervention by the State. The arguments advanced by them were: the Indian workers did not require any legislative protection; any new legislation was likely to injure the infant industries of the country; and, in any case, outsiders had no right to interfere in such delicate matters.78

Torn between pressures and counter-pressures, the Goverņ- ment of India finally introduced a Bill to amend the Factory Act of 1881 in the Legislative Council on 31 January 189o.79 The provisions of the amendment Bill did not however go far enough in bringing the Indian factory legislation on par with the English factory laws. This discrepancy once again displeased the pressure groups in England, who again started clamouring for more vigorous provisions.8o The decisions of the International Labour Conference, held at Berlin in March 189o, gave an added fillip to their agitation.81 Consequently, the Secretary of State insisted on a stricter measure, even though he had earlier approved the draft Bill, and suggested the appointment of another Commission.82 The Governimnent of India now appoinited another Commission in September 189o to enquire into the conditions of labour employed in factories in Bombay, Bengal, and the North-Western Provinces and Oudh.

The Commission recommended limitation of daily hours of work of women to 11, reduction in the working hours of children to 63/, provision of a weekly holiday for all operatives, including male adults, and of half an hour's rest interval between the hours of 12 A.M. and 1 P.M. for all adults. The Commissioners, excepting S. S. Bengalee, suggested that the proposed restriction on the working hours of women might be relaxed, if found necessary in their interests. This Commission too, however, refused to interfere with the hours that an adult male might be made to work.83

While the industrialists of India expressed satisfaction with the results of the Commission's deliberations and hoped that this might be the last of legislative interventions in labour affairs,84 the English agitators and the Secretary of State were still dissatisfied and pressed for further changes.85 For that matter, the recommendations of the Commission were far short of the demands of the factory workers.86

The provisions of the draft Bill were recast in the light of the Commission's recommendations and the erisuing correspondence between the Government of India and the Secretary of State, and the final Act, known as the Indian Factories (Amendment) Act, 1891, was passed on 19 March 1891. The Act applied to factories using power, employing 50 or more persons and working for 12o or more days in a year; it provided for a weekly holiday for all workers and a mid-day break of half an hour. Working hours of women were fixed at 11 per day including 1½ hours break for rest. Women were not to work during night except in factories of Bengal working on a shift basis. The minimum age for children was raised to 9 and the maximum to 14. Their hours of work were restricted to 7 in any one day. Local governments were empowered to frame rules regarding sanitary and hygienic standards.87

It is to be noted that once again the smaller and seasonal factories, in which the worst abuses prevailed, escaped the reach of legislation. Nor were the demands of Indian philanthropists and workers for a uniform 11 hour day and compensation for accidents and medical aid in case of sickness accepted.88 Moreover, the system of factory inspection continued to be defective, and consequently 'the provisions of the Factory Act were systematically evaded'.89

The provisions of the Factory Act of 1891, however, still left Lancashire capitalists out of humour. In the following years, they continued their agitation, in which they were joined by the jute manufacturers of Dundee.9o During the period following the year 1891, forces also arose in Britain in support of the stand taken by the Government of India,91 the reason perhaps being the growing preponderance in Britain of those who had made, directly or indirectly, or were planning to make, large capital investments in Indian industrial enterprises. In India, the philanthropic effort in the direction seems to have petered out during the next few years, till it was again taken up by the Times of India in September 1905.92

Once again, the nationalist reaction to the various issues relating to labour legislation was mostly pro-employer and lacking in sympathy towards labour. The need for labour legislation was generally denied and objection was taken to the efforts of the government and private individuals to bring about legislative intervention in the internal affairs of the mills.93 The general sense of this approach was well-summarised by a columnist in the Mahratta of 26 Oct. 189o as follows: 'There is considerable truth in the remark made by the Allahabad paper that the Indian operative is better off under the present circumstances than he is likely to be if he was bound up, hand and foot, like the British workman, in the meshes of over-nice and over-strict regulations.'

Some of the nationalist critics welcomed the recommendations of the Factory Commission of 189o, largely because of their 'moderation', 'fairness', and 'reasonableness', or, in other words, on the ground that the recommendations were harmless enough not to affect adversely the interests of the industry and the employers.94 For the same reasons, the draft Bill of 189o and the Act of 1891 were either not criticised at all by the nationalist newspapers, or, even if criticised, were let off very lightly.95 Moreover, most of the provisions of the Bill and the Act had already been accepted by the employers. This piece of legislation was, therefore, seen as nothing more than a rebuff to Manchester.96 Their only fear was that this temperate enactment might be used as the thin end of the wedge to impose much harsher regulations on the manufacturers.97 Moreover, this section of the nationalists was convinced that far from being miserable or suffering from any hardship, the mill worker was leading a contented and comfortable life. ## III. THE INDIAN MINES ACT, 1901

Till the beginning of the 2oth century, there was no legislation to regulate the conditions of work in the mining industry, which at this time primarily consisted of coal mining as far as the extent of employment was concerned. In 19oo-o1, coal mines produced 6,118,692 tons of coal and employed 89,248 persons.119 A special feature of coalmining in India was the extensive employment of women and children in under-ground mines.12o Moreover, the working conditions of mine workers, including coalminers, were very bad—they were indeed pitiable.iı Apart from the appalling insanitary conditions, the mining industry was especially liable to accidents due to the absence of proper precautions. Yet, if the attention of the government was not drawn earlier to the miserable conditions of labour in coal mines, it was perhaps due to the fact that the Indian coal mining industry had to face very little foreign competition and a large part of it was foreign-owned.izz There were, of course, quite a few Indian firms in the field, but their activities were generally confined to the smaller and poorer collieries.123

At last, in 1899, the Government of India introduced the Indian Mines Bill in the Legislative Council, which finally emerged as the Indian Mines Act of 19o1. The Act was a very .mild piece of legislation; it tried to regulate, though in a halfhearted fashion, the inspection of mines and the employment of women and children.i24

Even though the provisions of the Act were wholly inadequate for fulfilling the rather narrow tasks it was designed to serve, it was opposed almost unanimously by those nationalist leaders who expressed any opinion on it. Their grounds were practically the same as in the case of opposition to factory legislation. Once again, the need for any legislation, especially for a young industry like mining, was denied and the working conditions in the mines were declared to be satisfactory.125 Surprisingly even the Indian National Congress took a hand and passed a resolution in 19oo suggesting the omission of those provisions from the Mines Bill which imposed 'restrictions on the employment of labour'.126 The mover of the resolution, Bhupendra Nath Basu, admitted that it was being moved on the request. of the Mining Association of Bengal! No wonder he protested against the provision prohibiting women workers from carrying their children underground with them and declared that 14 years as the maximum age for children was on the high side since at 14 Indian women became mothers. He also opposed the appointment of inspectors on the ground that they would become'a dread to mine owners!i27

Óbjections similar to those expressed in the case of the Indian Factories Act of 1891 were raised by the nationalists in respect of restrictions on the employment of women and children underground.128 The clause in the Bill of 1899 prohibiting children between 4 and 1o years of age being taken underground was attacked on the ground that it would strike at traditional family ties.129 The hoary excuse was put forward that the workers concerned had not expressed any desire for such legislation.13o On the contrary, wrote the Hitavadi of 24 March 1899, 'he (the mine worker) does not want the uncalled for generosity of the Government. "I thank you for your charity," he exclaims, "please recall your dog"'131

Many of the nationalist spokesmen strongly criticised the. provision that only qualified persons could be appointed as managers. They pointed out quite correctly that in the absence of proper schools of mining, qualified İndian managers were not available, and that small mining companies, most of which were owned by Indians and which had to compete with larger European concerns, could possibly not afford to employ the highly-paid managers.132 They demanded in this connection the opening of mining schools in the country133 and, pending that, a temporary suspension of the provision.134

As before, the Indian leaders expressed the fear that mining legislation would be injurious to the growth of the mining industry, especially the Indian-owned part of it.135 And they freely suggested that it was designed to help the coal industry of England,136 although in this case there was not much objective basis for such an accusation.

## IV. BASIS OF NATIONALIST POLICY

We have so far examined the opposition of a major section of such Indian nationalist leaders as expressed opinion on one aspect or the other of the growing labour problems of Indian factory and mining industries to the very idea of legislative intervention in favour of the workers and also the support which a very small section extended to some of the elementary demands of the workers. It has been seen that the opposition was neither vehement nor wide-spread, except when referring to the role of Lancashire, but was rather lukewarm. Similarly, the support for workers' demands was equally half-hearted and quantitatively of little importance. Both these phenomena are to be explained by the extremely pro-employer nature of the activities of the Government of India and the innocuous character, from the point of view of the employers, of the various bills that were brought forth or enacted by the government. This inference is substantiated by the Report of the Bombay Millowners' Association for the year 1891. Referring to the Factories Act of 1891, the Report stated: 'These alterations do not go beyond the views which the members of this Association have always favoured and there has therefore been no necessity for assuming an antagonistic position with regard to the discussion on the Bill'.137 This fact of the employers' approval of most of the legislative measures being suggested or enacted was duly noted by some of the contemporary Indian newspapers.138 In fact, in view of the authoritative support lent by the employers to the findings of the Bombay Factory Commission of 1885, the recommendations of the Factory Commission of 189o, and the Factories Act of 1891, we are led to the conclusion that the nationalist opposition to them represented a typical case of the faithful being holier than the Pope.139

But much more important than the support or opposition offered by the Indian leaders to the proposed or actual labour legislation was their near-total absence of active' sympathy for the extremely oppressed class of factory and mining workers. Practically all the oracles of Indian economic thought had little or almost nothing to say on the subject. Ranade, the nyayamurti (justice-incarnate) of Indian politics and the doyen of social reformers, was completely silent on the misery of the labouring classes.14o Dadabhai Naoroji, whose heart bled at the poverty of the Indian people, made no mention of the low standard of living of the factory labourers. G. V. Joshi, whose encyclopaedic mind encompassed the whole range and every aspect of Indian economics, seemed to have been impervious to the call of labour economics. R. C. Dutt had not a word to say about factory labour in his far-ranging Economic History of India or in his numerous articles and public addresses. As editor of the Hindu till the end of the 19th century, G. Subramaniya Iyer was more or less hostile to the interests of the factory labourers. G. K. Gokhale did not'speak of their difficulties in any of his renowned public or parliamentary addresses.141 The only reference to the labour problem in the speeches of Surendranath Banerjea reveals utter lack of sympathy for the sufferings of the factory workers.142 Furthermore, the Bengalee, edited by him, was generally averse to factory legislation. The Indian National Congress did not adopt any resolution expressing concern over the lot of the factory workers, as a matter of fact the only resolution it passed relating to mining legislation was anti-labour. Most of the leading nationalist newspapers, e.g., the Amrita Bazar Patrika, the Hindu, the Mahratta, the Kesari, the Indu Prakash, the Dnyan Prakash, the Native Opinion, the Advocate, the Tribune, either remained silent or adopted an attitude of hostility towards labour. In the normal course of events such complete silence on the part of the nationalist leadership could have been perhaps overlooked. But when we keep in view that the condition of factory workers during this period was really appalling and shocking and that their miserable plight was a burning public issue at that time, especially between 1881 to 1891, this silence acquires a significant, if not ominous, meaning. Where speech is urgently called for, silence itself becomes eloquent. If nothing else, the lot of the children and women working to their bones in factories and mines should have evoked the wrath of the leading Brahmos of Bengal, the reformers of Bombay and Madras, the radicals of Poona, and the Arya Samajists of Lahore. An explanation of this indifference is perhaps to be found in a stray sentence in the writings of Justice Ranade.: Factory legislation in India, he wrote in his essay on The Re-organization of Real Credit in India, 'though reasonable in its way, affects but a drop in the ocean of Humanity in India'.143 But a much more likely and plausible explanation, we feel, lies in the Indian leaders' complete and undivided devotion to the cause of rapid industrialisation that blinded them altogether to the miseries that were being produced in its train by the capitalistic factory system of production. This does not, of course, absolve them from blame. In their anxiety to ensure rapid industrialisation of the country, which they saw as the panacea for the growing evils of the general poverty of the masses, the fast dwindling means of employment, and the growing pressure of population on land, they imagined behind every move for factory legislation either the hand of Manchester-which did indeed intrude too openly—or the threat of foreign competition, but not the objective needs and interests of the rising working class. They thus became the protagonists of the class of industrial capitalists, or, at least, played into its hands. Moreover, even the problem of industrialisation was seen by them entirely from the point of view of the employers. The only method of improving the competitive position of Indian industries vis-a-vis foreign competitors that the Indian employers could visualise, and which most of the Indian leaders advocated, was that of keeping the costs of production as low as possible by such means as lower wages, longer hours of work, etc., or, in short, by resorting to sweated labour. In fact, the Indian leaders accepted, though tacitly and indirectly, Lancashire's accusation that excessive exploitation of its labour was giving Indian industry indirect protection. It just did not occur to them that a lowering of pro. fits could also have improved the competitive position of Indian industry. Nor did they recommend the provision of incentives to the workers or adoption of certain other methods of improving industrial productivity. As a matter of fact, the rise in the productivity of Indian labour during this period was so rapid,144 and the profits of Indian mills were normally so high,i45 that the factory workers' lot could have been easily bettered without impairing to the slightest extent the competitive position of Indian industry in its contest with its English rivals. But, then, the Indian national leadership during this period considered the interests of modern industry exclusively in terms of the interests of the capitalists.

It is, of course, not suggested that the Indian leadership could, or sliould, have at that particular historical moment and under the then existing political and economic circumstances taken any steps that would have accentuated class conflict between the two new rising classes of Indian society. Certainly, for political reasons, if for nothing else, it was expedient and even essential to unite all the people for the common national struggle for political and economic emancipation. It was axiomatic at that time that this struggle was the most important task facing the people of India and that nothing should be done to divide people on other grounds. At the very second session of the Indian National Congress, Dadabhai Naoroji had laid down the dictum that the Congress 'must confine itself to questions in which the entire nation has a direct participation, and it must leave the adjustment of social reforins and other class questions to Class Congresses'.146 But differences, clashes of interests, and even conflicts could, and did, arise even within the nationalist fold. How were these to be resolved and removed? It was here that the bias of the nationalist leadership showed itself. For in the case of the conflict between the employers and their employees the national leaders did not suggest a compromise formula based on mutual concessions by both the parties to the dispute; they either remained silent, which meant supporting by acquiescence the stronger party or advocated, in the name of wider national interests, complete surrender of the interests of the factory and mining labourers. As the result of this approach the fulfilnent of national aspirations in the industrial field, viz., growth and prosperity of Indian industry, occurred in the main through the sacrifices of the labouring classes. It may also be noted in this connection that many of the Indian leaders did not even hesitate to make common cause with British capitalists and bureaucrats, who were otherwise an anathema to the nationalists, against the interests of the workers.

## V. PLANTATION LABOUR AND THE INLAND EMIGRATION ACT, 1882

The attitude of the Indian national leadership underwent a volte-face where Indian labourers employed in British-owned plantations were concerned. This complete change of front is nowhere more evident than in their attitude towards the conditions of work and of life of the thousands of workers employed in Assam tea plantations, who were commonly known as Assam Coolies. To the misery of plantation labourers, the Indian leaders responded with whole-hearted sympathy and concern. They declaimed against the heartless foreign capitalists, bewailed the fate of the defenceless and voiceless workers, and unstintingly pleaded their cause. In this case, they gave full play to their nationalism and used the miseries and humiliations suffered by the coolies to awaken the dormant feelings of collective national abasement and wounded national pride, lighting in the process the torch of nationalism in innumerable hearts. This reversal of position was quite a conscious act and was at least partially based on the fact that the employers in this case happened to be foreigners, This view was very lucidly summed up by P. Ananda Charlu, President of the Congress in 1891, in his speech on the Assam Labour and Emigration Bill in the Legislative Council in 19o1, as follows:

Such legislation may perhaps not be insisted upon when the employer and the employed are part and parcel of the same nation, and where, on that ground in particular, much less friction and much greater fraternal feeling are to be met with. When conditions for such congenial mutuality and for such hearty ‘give and take' do not exist, or rather are at a discount, there is an a' fortiori case for periodical intercession to cancel unconscious excess of unilateral proclivities.147

Another factor that influenced the judgemeut of Indian leaders was the fact that Assam tea labour was largely indentured and its conditions of work were extremely unwholesome.

Tea industry may be said to have had its real beginning in India in the year 1851; thereafter, it made rapid progress. During the period under study, most of the tea-gardens were situated in Assam. In 1903, the industry employed 479,000 permanent and 93,ooo temporary employees.148 Since Assam was sparsely populated and the tea plantations were often located on uninhabited hillsides, bulk of the sorely needed labour had to be imported from Bengal and other provinces. But to bring thousands of people every year from their far-off homes into strange lands, possessing an unhealthy climate and infected with strange fevers, required the provision of financial and other incentives, which the tea-planters of Assam were unwilling to offer. Instead, they had recourse to fraud and coercion; and they persuaded the government to aid and abet them in this unholy task by passing penal laws.149 Thus began the tale of woe and misery of the Assam tea-plantation labourers who found to their sorrow that the ‘economic development' of the country by foreign capitalists, while resulting in the creation of new opportunities of employment, threw them from the frying pan of their traditional rural poverty into the fire of misery and exploitation of indentured labour.

The recruitment of labourers for tea-gardens of Assam was carried on for years mostly by contractors under the provisions of the Transport of Native Labourers Act (No. III) of 1863 of Bengal, as amended in 1865, 1870 and 1873. Although supposedly enacted for the protection of the labourers under contract, since it provided for licensed recruitment, the Act as amended in 1865, in reality benefited only the planters. It made not only desertion but also indolence on the workers' part punishable under law; moreover, it empowered the planters. to arrest ‘absconders' within the limits of the district in which they were employed. In order to keep the labourers on the straight and narrow path, the planters also utilised an earlier Act, viz., Act No. XIII of 1859, which made a breach of contract—an euphemism for refusal to work—by a labourer, who had taken monetary advance on the promise of work, liable to criminal action and penalty, and Sections 490 and 492 of the Indian Penal Code (Act No. XLV of 186o), which made a breach of contract during the journey and at the place of work a criminal offence in case the worker was carried to the place of work at the expense of the employer.

The existing laws having proved inadequate in mecting the hunger of tea-gardens for more and more men, the Governiment of India appointed a Commission of Enquiry in 1881 to deal with the situation on an all-India basis. The Commission reported that, under the existing enactments, the planters were inadequately protected against the laziness and desertion of labourers, enforcement of contracts was difficult, and there were unnecessary restrictions upon recruitment by garden sardars.15o In accordance with these recommendations, the Inland Emigration Act of 1882 was passed, which superceded all the existing Acts, though it did not repeal Act No. XIII of 1859. The Act of 1882 gave legal recognition to recruiting agents and made the signing of a contract much easier. It fixed the monthly minimum wage for men and women respectively at Rs. 5 and 4 for the first 3 years, and Rs. 6 and 5 for the 4th and 5th years. It also provided for the arrest by the employer of a labourer under contract without any warrant, if upon desertion he was found anywhere except within 5 miles of the place where a Magistrate resided.151

The Indian national leadership, particularly that of Bengal reacted sharply to the acutely distressing plight of the Assam coolies and the attempts of the government to give legal sanc. tion and support to the system of contract labour. Already in i87o's the vernacular press of Bengal had started writing of the abuses of the system and, thus, arousing the latent nationalism of the readers with hair-raising stories of the scan· dalous treatment meted out to the labourers in Assam.152 Sometime around 188o, Ram Kumar Vidyaratna, a preacher ot the Sadharan Brahmo Samaj, wrote a book Kuli Kahini (Coolie Story), highlighting the miserable lot of the Assam coolies. The book gained immediate popularity.153

When the Inland Emigration Bill was introduced in 1881 and enacted in 1882, it was impassionately condemned by the Indian press for enslaving the coolie and leaving him entirely at the mercy of the planter.154 For instance, the Bcngalee of 17 December 1881 felt that the Bill involved 'a gross violation of the sacred right of personal liberty', and declared it to be 'in the very nature of a slave-law'. The Brahino Public Opinion of 29 December 1881 remarked that the coolies ‘are no better than chattels and the Bill treats them as such'. The Indian Spectator, from far off Bombay, censured the legislation for being 'nothing short of legalised thraldom for the unhappy Bengal cooly', and commented: Never was Labour left so entirely at the mercy of Capital.'1s5 The Indian Association, the spokesinan of the rising generation of young nationalists of Bengal, submitted a detailed memorial to the Government of India criticising certain provisions, especially Sections 17o & 172. of the Bill. The memorial urged that free rein should be given to the law of supply and demand, instead.156 Most of the critics also alleged that the Act was being passed solely under the pressure, and in the interests, of the planters.157

The working of the Act during the next few years confirmed the gravest apprehensions and the gloomiest predictions of its critics. In the following decades, the Emigration Act was constantly and vehemently assailed by the national leadership, and the pitiful condition of the tea-garden labourers of Assam was lamented all over the country. The Emigration Act came to be popularly known all over the country as the 'slave-law'.158 The people of India and, in particular, the people of Bengal, were moved to the depth of their hearts by the harrowing tales of 'enticement, elopement, torture, rape, and even murder' which were often brought to light by the Indian press.

The Indian Association, which always took a keen interest in the fate of the tea-garden labourers, once again espoused their cause when, in 1886, it deputed its Assistant Secretary, Dwarkanath Ganguli, to make on-the-spot enquiries. Ganguli narrated his experiences and the 'hideous tales' of oppression and near-slavery in the columns of the Bengalee and the Sanjivani.159 Earlier in 1886, the Association had drawn the Viceroy's attention to the condition of the Assam coolies. Now, drawing upon the experience of its Assistant Secretary and upon several judgments delivered by the courts, the Association submitted, on 5 May 1888, another detailed memorial to the Government of India.i6o The Association continued to take interest in the matter and to send memorials to the authorities in India and in England in the later years.161

In course of time, the cause of the Assam coolies was also taken up by the Indian National Congress. What is surprising is that the initial attempts at raising the question in the Congress should have proved abortive. At the Madras Congress of 1887, the delegates from Bengal tried to persuade the Congress leadership to condemn 'the legalised inhumanities of the Assam Cooly Act'; but the request was turned down on the ingenious ground that the matter, being a provincial subject, could not be discussed at the all-India Congress.16z A second attempt to raise the matter at the Allahabad session next year was again foiled.163 The popular feeling on the subject was, however, running high throughout the country and the conservative Congress leadership had to yield to it sooner or later.

Eventually, the Congress identified itself with the cause of the tea-garden labourers, when in 1896 it passed· a resolution pleading for the repeal of the Emigration Act.164 Both the mover of the resolution, Jogendra Chandra Ghosh, and the seconder, Bipin Chandra Pal, drew attention to the conditions of near-slavery prevailing in the tea-gardens.165 The burden of the resolution was reiterated at the following four Congress sessions.166

In the meantime, frustrated by their experience at the Congress session of 1887, the leaders of Bengal had organised the first Bengal Provincial Conference of 25 October 1888, primarily to express their strong views on the Assam coolie question. The Provincial Conference proved very successful and was transformed by the organisers into an annual affair. It always took keen and active interest in the problems of Assam coolies.167

Through his Economic History of India, R. C. Dutt added his powerful voice to the general condemnation of this ‘indelible stain' on the Indian tea-industry and demanded abolition of this 'system of semi-slavery'.168

However, the leading role in the agitation against the Emigration Act and the grave abuses which prevailed in the tea-gardens was assumed by the nationalist press. Showing complete sympathy for and solidarity with the coolies, the Indian newspapers protested, year in and year out, against their miserable lot, exhausting in the process their vocabulary of words expressing anger and sorrow. For instance, the nationalist newspapers throughout the country fully endorsed the memorial that the Indian Association submitted to the Government of India in 1888.169 It may be mentioned in this context that special interest in the subject was taken by the Bengalee and the Sanjivani, edited by Surendranath Banerjea and Krishan Kumar Mitra respectively.17o

The nationalist press and publicists made the systems of recruitment and transport a special target of their attack. They described in detail how, in' contravention of the law, ignorant and illiterate persons were coerced and kidnapped and how they were decoyed and duped with false hopes and fraudulent promises into contracting away their freedom, by artful and unprincipled recruiters.171 Once the coolie arrived on the tea plantation, complained the nationalists, he was abominably treated and terribly oppressed by the planters.17 He was forcibly and illegally kept on the plantation and arrested and punished under penal laws if he tried to escape.173 Dwarkauath Ganguli reported in 1886 that he had actually seen dungeons where unwilling labourers were illegally confined. He had also found that physical torture was a common practice in the tea-gardens.174 Many of the Indian publicists narrated tales of the shocking treatment meted out to the coolies.175 The nationalist newspapers and associations also publicised stories of rape of women and killing of men, and the frequent and grosş miscarriage of justice in favour of the planters whenever the coolies or their well-wishers carried the matter to courts.176

The Indian leaders further argued that the high rate of mortality in the tea-plantations was a true index of the state of affairs there.177 They also complained of the extremely low wages paid to the labourers in tea-gardens in spite of the unpleasant nature and conditions of work.178 In fact, they declared, one of the main reasons for enacting penal laws was that of keeping down wages and 'making coolies work on lower wages than they would ordinarily do, in other words, for cheating the coolie'.179 Some of the Indians pointed out that labour shortage was not a real problem in India or in Assam, for higher wages could fetch all the needed labour.18o They also vehemently refuted the theory that penal laws were needed to overcome the alleged inborn reluctance of the ignorant worker to work hard for his living even though he might be starving on his land. The truth was that the European colonisers wanted the native people to 'work as their slaves,' and 'if the latter refuse to work, they are stigmatised as lazy and ignorant of the "dignity of labour," and are forced to labour by the use of various expedients.'181

In fact, averred the Indian leaders, the entire system of coolie labour in Assam was nothing but a form of slavery, the life of the Indian coolie being no better than that of the ancient slaves or the modern Negro-slaves.182 In this connection, B. C. Pal has narrated how in the 188o's the educated Bengal read and admired Uncle Tom's Cabin and then 'readily compared the condition of tea-garden labourers in Assam to that of Negro Labour in America before the emancipation.'183 Years later, the same writer commented in his Ncw India of 26 August 19o1: 'This system of penal labour is slavery modified and made up-to-date.'

But, then, what about the benefits derived by the country as a whole in the form of development of its hidden resources and the resultant addition to national production, and by the labourers in the form of employment and wages? This aspect of the problem was also noticed by some of the Indian writers. While freely admitting these benefits, the more moderate of them opined that they ‘should not be secured by sacrificing the lives and liberties of a large section of Her Majesty's poorest, most ignorant and helpless subjects'.184 Others, i.e., the angrier ones among them, felt that economic development of this type was totally undesirable. They would rather prefer 'that the tea trade should be ruined and Assam should become the abode of wild beasts than that this fearful custom of selling coolies as slaves should be kept up.'185

## VI. THE WAY OUT

This state of affairs could not, however, be permitted to last Even otherwise, it could not have continued for long. As early as 1887, the Sanjivani had sounded the tocsin :

English rulers of India...try to put down this terrible oppression. English merchants...do" not shut your eyes to so much misery which you in your greed for gold are inflicting upon human beings. For it is impossible that much time can pass in this way'. Heaven's justice has overtaken and overwhelmed many powerful nations like you. Stop in your mad career, for you are sure to be brought to a reckoning for this. Try to remove every trace of this demoniacal custom from this country.186 (Emphasis added).

The first step urged by the Indian leaders was a proper realisation on the part of the government of the real condition of the coolies and of the oppressive effects of the working of the Workmen's Breach of Contract Act of 1859 and the Inland Emigration Act of 1882. And for this purpose, they exhorted the government to appoint an independent Commission of Enquiry.187 Secondly, they suggested that, keeping in view the labourers' ignorance and their helplessness in the face of powerful and rich planters, the government should adopt special measures to protect them from oppression.188 One such measure should be the awarding of prompt and proper punishment to those managers of tea-estates who ill-treated their labourers.189 But the most popular of the Indian demands was for the repeal of all penal laws,19o and for the introduction of free emigration, so that the tea-gardens obtained workers 'from the teeming labour markets of India under the ordinary laws of demand and supply'.191

While entreating and pressing the government to relieve the misery of Assam coolies, some of the Indian leaders were aware that the alien government was not likely to move against

European planters.192 This consciousness led the Sanjivani, the staunchest friend of the Assam coolies, to give a call for selfhelp. Appealing to the manhood of the Indians, it remarked editorially on 14 August 1886 that, if they had not lost all vital energy, 'they should kindle such a fire in the land as will burn the Coolie Act into ashes'.193 It also implored them to form an association for giving legal and other aid to the coolies, or those intending to become coolies, and for exercising general vigilance over the doings of the planters.194 One of its correspondents even proposed that the elucated Indians should stop taking tea 'for drinking Assam tea is nothing more or less than drinking the blood of the poor persecuted coolie'.19s Interestingly, though not surprisingly, no one in India at that time suggested self-help by the tea-garden labourers themselves; for example, by means of formation of trade-unions. The coolies did, of course, take recourse ,to self-help in the form of assaults and riots,196 but they were not given proper guidance by the nationalist leaders.

## VII. THE ASSAM LABOUR AND EMIGRATION ACT, 1901

The question of the tea-garden labourers once again came to the forefront of Indian politics at the beginning of the zoth century, when as a result of persistent Indian agitation and the constant efforts of Henry Cotton, the liberal and courageous Chief Commissioner of Assam, the Government of India decided to enact a new law on the subject. The Assam Labour and Emigration Bill was moved in the Legislative Council on 13 October 1899 and enacted on 8 March 19o1. While the new measure tried, though in a half-hearted manner, to improve the system of recruitmenti97— an object in which it failed on the whole, the grave abuses continuing as beforei98  one of its provisions, though of not much significance in the larger context of the purpose and effects of the entire Act, led to a great deal of controversy and became an object of nationalist agitation. The Bill, as originally introduced in 1899, provided for an increase of one rupee per month in the minimum wages of contract labourers as fixed under the Act of 1882. This provision was later amended by the Select Committee so that the monthly wages for men and women were respectively fixed at Rs. 5 and 4 in the first year, Rs. 5½ and Rs. 4½ in the second and third years, and Rs. 6 and Rs. 5 in the fourth year. Thus, the wages for the first and the fourth years remained the same as under the previous act.199 Even though the nationalists regarded the Bill as basically an instrument designed to serve the planters and perpetuate forced labour,2oo they supported the clause relating to the enhancement of wages of the coolies as 'doing a bit of tardy justice to these helpless beings', even though they considered the enhancement to be very niggardly.201

The fat was once again in the fire when the government accepted an amendment, moved on behalf of the tea-planters by J. Buckingham, suggesting postponement of the operation of the wages clause of the Act for two years.2oz In fact, Lord Curzon himself had in an earlier intervention in the debate directly invited the planters to ask for this concession.2o3 To add fuel to the fire, the Viceroy publicly repudiated and rebuked the popular Chief Commissioner of Assam,2o4 when the latter opposed the amendment on the ground that it would stultify the very inadequate concession that had just been won for the coolies.2o5 The nationalist press and public men rose as one man against this surrender by Lord Curzon before the foreign planters.2o6 Here was another reprehensible illustration, they observed, of the manner ‘in which the interests of the European capitalists are carefully safeguarded at the cost of the Indian people'.2o7 ‘The primary object of British rule in India', wrote the Bengalec of 1o March 19o1, ‘is to benefit the European capitalist and merchant, even, if necessary, at the sacrifice of justice and humanity.' While condemning the Government of India, the nationalists showered full-throated praise on Henry Cotton for his integrity and courage in championing the cause of the coolies.2o8

## VIII. THE MADRAS PLANTERS' LABOUR ACT, 1903

The wrath of the nationalists against the foreign government and their sympathy for the plantation workers were aroused again when in 19o3 the Madras Government enacted the Madras Planters' Labour Act. Based largely on the Assam Labour and Emigration Act of 19o1, the Madras Act also provided for the arrest and punishment of deserters and the levy of fines and imprisonment for absenteeism and laziness. As in the case of the Assam Act, the Indian leaders censured the Madras Act as being monstrous, blasphemous, inhuman, hateful and unjust, and criticised it for unduly restricting the liberty of the coolie and attempting to legalise slavery in the Southern plantations.2o9 They blamed the government for having become a mere tool in the hands of the planters and foreign capitalists whom it was always ready to oblige.2io

## IX. WIDER CONNECTIONS

As a by-product of their discussion of the problems of plantation labour, some of the Indian nationalist leaders did some loud thinking on some of the broader questions of politics and economics involved, and tried to establish wider connections between them and the government's attitude towards plantation labour. Firstly, the government's insistence on enacting pro-planter laws, in defiance of the opposition of the Indian National Congress, the Indian newspapers, and the Indian members of the Legislative Councils, convinced the Indian leaders that the government attached little weight to Indian public opinion.21 They also noted that the reformed Legislative Councils had been totally ineffective in this regard, and that the government had ridden rough-shod over the wishes of their Indian members.2iz This prompted G. S. Iyer to comment: 'Never was a system of legislation a more solemn farce than the Indian system is at present.'zi3 In fact, the faith of even the most moderate section of the Indian leadership in the sense of justice and progressiveness of the alien rulers was shaken. This loss of confidence is well brought out by the following comment of the Kaiser-i-Hind of 8 March 19o3 on the enactment of the Madras Planters', Labour Act :

We ask if these be the impartial legislators, who call themselves paternal but who, at the behest of a most selfish class, not to use a stronger epithet, enslave a voiceless and helpless people?... Who will deny that here is another 'solid proof of that decadence not only of British politics, but of British righteousness. Verily there are ominous signs all round of the decay of men and morals throughout the vast British Empire, which bode no good to it. Heaven defend the British nation from coming disasters.214

The Kesari of 1o March 1903 even tried to see the entire problem in the wider perspective of economic imperialism on a world-wide scale. After observing that 'In the present age of fierce competition, European nations are striving hard for material prosperity and exploiting the material and mineral resources of uncivilised countries under the pretext of carrying the blessings of civilisation there', it complained that 'wherever the Europeans go for purposes of trade or colonisation, they make the native population work as slaves', and that 'the treatment of Kaffirs in South Africa and of Indian coolies in the tea gardens of Assam are instances of such oppression'. In conclusion, it expressed its sorrow at the thought 'that one portion of mankind should roll in wealth and that the rest of their fellow-beings should be forced to toil for their sake'.215

Some of the Indian leaders were also led to view the entire question of plantation labour as a part of the larger problem of relations between the working class and the capitalist class and to identify themselves with the former. For example, G. S. lyer thought that the genesis of the policy of Government of India towards plantation labour lay in the fact that it looked upon the tea-industry as involving only the interests of the capitalists.2i6 R. C. Dutt also felt that the capitalist influence on the government was too strong for it to be able to do anything for the workers.2i7 The Sanjivani too came to the conclusion that the struggle in Assam was ultimately a conflict between labour and capital.2i8 This new understanding and its importance for the nationalist movement were most clearly articulated by Bipin Chandra Pal in a speech in 19o1, of which we are constrained to reproduce a long extract in view of its importance as well as novelty:

This question, Mr Chairman, is an old question — the world wide question of the conflict of Labour and Capital. The forces of Capital already strong in the strength of the almighty dollar and combining themselves not only to keep out the Coolies out of their due reward but also shield even those who are condemned by Law Courts and punished by

High Courts. And when that is done, should not the force of labour also combine? You represent the force of labour Prince or peasants, Mr Chairman, we all of us stand in the position of labourers in this country, and they stand, all stand, in the position of capitalists.219

This attitude was evident as clearly, though at a more mundane level, in the speeches of P. Ananda Charlu on the Assam Labour and Emigration Bill. On the one hand, he argued -that 'the claims of the mass of labourers who help no less in securing success to the enterprising, energetic and persistent capitalist' should not be ignored; on the other, he pleaded: 'Between the employer and the Labourers, the former can afford to bear the burden... while the latter can ill-afford to go without the increased wage.... To the former it may mean the reduction of some luxury, while to the latter it will be serious privation.'2zo

That the pro-labour sympathies of the Indian national leadership were aroused in the case of plantation labour primarily because of the foreign character of the capitalist enterprise involved and that they did not extend to the labourers working in the Indian-owned enterprises was dramnatically demonstrated when exactly two weeks after the passing of the Assam Labour and Emigration Act on 8 March 19o1, the Indian Mines Bill caine up for discussion and enactment in'the Legislative Council. And, as we have shown earlier, the entire Indian press and all the Indian embers of the Council opposed the very moderate provisions of the Bill designed for the protection of women and children working in the mines. This ambivalence towards labour was also clearly present in the attitude of the Indian National Congress which, on the one hand, advised the government in 19oo to omit 'the provisions of the Indian Mines Bill, so far as they impose restrictions on the employment of labour', and, on the other, passed a resolution only one year later calling for an increase in the

wages of Assam coolies.221

Another equally dramatic — or perhaps tragicomic —, though unbelievably cynical, illustration of this contradictory attitude was provided by an editorial in the Hindu of 3 March 1903. The Hindu had always been, as we have seen earlier, one of the staunchest opponents of all pro-planter legislation condemning all such laws as slave-laws. It had repeatedly voiced strong criticism of the Madras Planters' Bill. It did so in its editorial of 3 March 1903 also. But then it put forward in the same editorial the surprising demand that a similar law should be enacted for ‘all employers of labour who experience difficulty not only in getting labourers but in compelling them to perform their contract'. Such a law, it said, was especially needed in the interests of the landholders of Southern India who would also find a penal labour law 'of immense benefit'. To strengthen its argument, the Hindu appealed to nationalism : "There need be no class legislation even in regard of the Indian coolies, who if they can be made slaves to European planters, may not improperly be made slaves to Indian agricul. turists. The Indian coolies are not made to slave for one class alone.' (Emphasis added). This line of reasoning requires no comment. The Hindu concluded with a demand that brings into sharp relief, with unconscious irony, its hypocritical sanctimony on the question. 'It is as unjust to deny it (a penal law) to them (Indian landholders) as it is unrighteous to hand over the coolie to the mercy of the planter on the hill.' The Hindu could have, however, derived one consolation: it was not alone in making this ridiculous demand. Two months later a similar plea was made by the Swadesmitran, another nationalist stalwart of Tamil journalism.2zz

## X. G.I.P. RAILWAY SIGNALLERS' STRIKE

An interesting episode that illuminates the Indian national leadership's attitude towards labourers working in non-Indian enterprises occurred in 1899 on the G.I.P. Railway. In the first week of May of that year, over 8oo signallers of the railway went on strike when their demands relating, inter alia, to nonreduction of salaries, systematisation of promotions, limitation of hours of work, and provision of regular holidays were rejected by the management of the railway coinpany.223 According to press reports, the strike hit the railway rather hard; the goods traffic was generally curtailed and suspended in certain cases; the passenger traffic too suffered from heavy dislocation and frequent and long delays of the trains.2z4 This was perhaps the first organised strike by any section of the Indian working class.225 The signallers had a regular organisation that had its headquarters at Sholapur and its own hired firm of solicitors. The strike was preceded by a two year-long agitation and presentation of a charter of demands and an ultimatum.226

In a way, this organised action by railway workers, though admittedly by white-collar ones only, was, in Indian conditions of the times, a more radical step in the direction of labour welfare than any taken so far by the government through legislation. It had implications for the capitalists that went far beyond the immediate issues and parties involved.227 Therefore, keeping in mind the indifference of the nationalists towards factory labour, one would have expected in their ranks an attitude of neutrality, if not of open hostility, or, at the most, of mild approbation, towards the signallers' strike. In reality, however, all but one of the leading nationalist newspapers of Bombay supported the strike very warmly.zz8 And what is perhaps even more surprising, it struck a sympathetic chord in the columns of many other, distant nationalist newspapers like the Amrita Bazar Patrika, the Hitavadi, the Hindu, and the Hindustani. It was, of course, B. G. Tilak's two newspapers, the Mahratta and the Kesari, which made the cause of the strikers their own and for months agitated for it in their columns.

The Indian newspapers declared that the signallers were quite right in going on strike, since they had been for a long time ill-paid, hard worked and inequitably treated; and that their demands were quite legitimate, fair and reasonable, and, indeed, worthy of sympathy and support. Moreover, the signallers had been forced to resort to this course of action by the persistent refusal of the management to redress their genuine grievances.229

Some of the newspapers went beyond mere sympathy; they became ardent admirers of the strikers, and praised the firmness and unity displayed by them during the course of the strike.23o Indeed, wrote the Mahratta of 28 May 1899, the strike was of more than momentary import, for the signallers had set an example of union and self sacrifice which others would do well to emulate. Similarly, the Hindustani of 7 June

1899 exhorted the people of India to rise to the signallers' 'standard of morality' in striking together and refusing to negotiate separately with the management.231 These newspapers constantly exhorted the strikers to remain steadfast even if they lost their jobs, and denounced as traitors all those who had joined service.232

The Indian newspapers strongly condemned the outlook, behaviour and actions of the railway management towards their employees.233 The management's plea of 'discipline in a public utility' was dismissed as mere cant; and the blame.for the inconvenience caused to the public was thrown on the management.234 They urged the railway company to compromise with the strikers by removing their legitimate grievances and by agreeing, as already agreed to by the strikers, to let an arbitrator decide the dispute.235

The nationalist newspapers urged the government to intervene in the dispute and compel the management to accept the reasonable demands of the signallers.236 They pointed out that, even apart from considerations of humanity, the government was under an obligation to do so because the G.I.P. Railway being a guaranteed railway the loss suffered by it on account of the strike would have to be borne by the government, that is, ultimately by the Indian tax-payers.237 'It is obviously unrighteous', comiiented the Mahratta of 28 May 1899, 'to make the people pay the cost required to defeat and disappoint a section of their own for whom they feel profound sympathy. The nationalist newspapers also maintained that the government had a moral right, as well as a duty, to intercede in the strike since the railways were a public utility, any dislocation or mismanagement of whose services affected not only tradė and public comfort and convenience but also the very safety of the passengers.238 At the same time, they noted that the government not only refused to intervene on behalf of the strikers, or to remain neutral, but actively aided and upheld the railway management; and for this reason, they sharply .censured its conduct.239

The wide-spread public sympathy for the signallers went further than mere words. The newspapers appealed to the public and the political associations to raise funds for the strikers.24o Many people came forward on their own to help them. Public meetings were organised in different towns of Bombay Presidency, e.g. Ahmednagar, Amraoti, Dhulia, and Nagpur, to collect funds in their aid.24i In Bombay, a gathering of leading Indian citizens and merchants was held on ‘19 May at the chambers of Pherozeshah M. Mehta; it resolved to start a fund for the benefit of the strikers. A sum of Rs. 2,5oo was subscribed on the spot, while an equal amount was promised the next day.242 Money for this fund was also collected in Bengal by Surendra Nath Tagore and the Bengali monthly Bharat, edited by Miss Ghosal.243 After the failure of the strike and the dismissal of nearly 7oo signallers, a public fund on their behalf was started in Bombay of which D. E. Wacha was one of the treasurers.244 The Mahratta, in its issue dated 16 July 1899, lent its full support to the endeavour. From the far away Calcutta, the Amrita Bazar Patrika of 4 July 1899 appealed to the zamindars and mercantile firms to give employment to the dismissed men.245

The key to an understanding of this radical pro-strike attitude of the Indian leaders lies precisely in their nationalism. The signallers on strike were Indians whereas the employing railway company was owņed and administered by Englishmen — this one fact alone was sufficient to bring the dispute between the two into the main-stream of Indian nationalism and to raise it to the level of a national issue. In some cases, this particular understanding of the dispute was clearly formulated and explicitly expressed. For example, the Mahratta complained that the railway company was denying a living wage to the signallers because they were Indians. It wrote as follows on 21 May 1899 in a rather profound vein:

But perhaps the Manager felt... that a black nigger has no right to earn anything more than bare living which would be left to him for his sustenance in order that he might cheerfully bear 'the white man's burden' when the latter was carrying out the civilising maxim imposed upon him by God by a skilful exploitation of the tropics whereby he would obtain all the profits and nothing would be left to the nigger but just enough for sweating and breeding to the advantage of his master.

The Amrita Bazar Patrika also thought that the entire dispute had racial undertones. 'The Bombay Government cannot now escape from the humiliating position', it remarked editorially on 22 June 1899, 'that it helped the Railway Company and threw the signallers overboard simply because the latter were natives and the former were members of the ruling race.' The Gujarati of 14 May and the Jame-Jamshed of 16 May recounted in this connection that two years earlier when European guards of the same railway had gone on strike, both the railway company and the government had adopted a very different approach and had actually accepted their demands.246 In fact, concluded the Mahratta of 16 July 1899, the signallers had been fighting for national honour as they had been 'actuated by the motive of maintaining their own and their society's self-respect'.

The nationalist motivation of Indian leadership's attitude towards the signaller's strike is also brought out in another way. There were numerous strikes in the Bombay.textile mills during 1897 and 1898.247 None of them was taken note of or supported by the leading nationalist newspapers; and the Amrita Bazar Patrika, which had so vocally supported the signallers' strike for higher wages, did not hesitate to condemn a few months later Bombay textile mill workers' strike against reduction of their wages. In this case, it contended in its issue of 1o January 19oo that the employers could not pay higher wages and that any attempt to compel them would only mean the destruction of an Indian Industry which would mean that the strikers too would have ‘to starve in the end'.

## XI. BEGINNINGS OF NEW ATTITUDES

With the growth of the Indian working class, and the slow emergence of the conflict between labour and capital, new ideas regarding the social role of this new class and new attitudes about its rights and responsibilities began to make appearance in the ranks of Indian national leadership. It must, however, be kept in mind that these new ideas were confined to only a few of the Indian leaders and that they did not affect all of them in the same manner and to the same extent. Surprisingly enough, this new approach was enunciated for the first time at a meeting of the Bombay Millowners' Association in March 1899, when D. E. Wacha, himself a mill-agent, put forth the view that the cost of production of the textile industry could be reduced in two ways: firstly, by improving the quality of the raw cotton used, and, secondly, by 'improving the physical and sanitary condition' of the operatives. He asserted that long hours, low wages, and taxes on the food of the people had always led to the crippling of industrial development; on 'the other hand, shorter hours, higher wages, and provision of cheap food and housing facilities had inevitably resulted in energetic growth.248 Wacha repeated this advice in April 19o5 and pleaded for a working day of ten hours. Cautioning the mill-owners against industrial avarice' and the policy of killing 'the goose which lays the golden eggs', he warned that the labour question was certainly going to be 'the future vexing problem for mill-owners', and that it would be in their own interest to start solving it themselves rather than leave it to be settled through interference by the government or some other party on behalf of the workers.249

Bipin Chandra Pal was another leader who began to recognise at this time the significance of the emergence of industrial working class on the Indian scene. Unfortunately, most of his writings of the period 19o1-05 have been lost, or, at least, they were not available to me. Only a few stray copies of the weekly New India, which he started publishing in i9o1, are to be found in the public libraries of this country. A perusal of these reveals that Pal was certainly doing some unorthodox thinking on the labour problem. In an editorial in the New India of 9 September 19o1, he wrote that the condition of the two-million strong class of labourers was ‘a matter of profoundest significance to the present economic problem of the country'. It was from this class which worked under 'somewhat modern conditions' that new wants and new ideas of economic progress could percolate to the masses. Its interests were therefore of paramount importance and deserved to be promoted. While appreciating the official attempts to protect the interests of the labourers through legislation, Pal felt that because of 'the influence of the capitalists' being ‘too strong", the government had not been able to do enough. In another editorial in the New India of 14 April 19o2, he advocated the enactment of a workmen's compensation law against accidents.

It was, however, G. Subramaniya Iyer who made a systematic study of the labour question in his article, 'Our Labour Problem', in the Hindustan Review and Kayastha Samachar of August 19o1, and in his book, Some Economic Aspects of British Rule in India, published in 19o3. He was also the first national leader to try to look at the question from a consistently pro-labour angle.25o He noted that the labour problem was beginning to arise in India just as it had earlier arisen in the West. This was, he said, also evident from the frequent strikes which had occurred of late in Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras.251 In his opinion the fundamental factor in the situation was the •condition of the labouring classes and 'the poverty and misery that characterise their lot'.252 Their condition was made much worse by the fact that their ignorance and lack of education and ‘intelligence' made them 'helpless whenever their interests get into a conflict with those of their powerful and educated employers'.253 But, he predicted, this state of affairs won't last for long, for a day would come when the Indian labourers would also rise up and demand and win political rights, the right to combine, and higher wages.254

At the same time, G.S. Iyer strongly reproved the government as well as the Indian National Congress and the innumerable political associations and conferences for neglecting the welfare of modern workers.255 Upbraiding these organisations for this neglect, he observed:

We have become so accustomed to the poverty, squalor, and degradation of these unfortunate classes that we hāve become callous, and our rulers, as well as ourselves, have no qualms of conscience at their condition, which is truly the result of the neglect and oppression of the higher classes of the community and of the State, during centuries.256

In this context, he vehemently rejected the doctrine of laissez faire. In his opinion, the law of competition was not the right principle to apply to this case since 'the competition is most unequal, the labourer, though nominally free, being the slave of the imperative requirements of his daily life. Starvation, or the sale of his labour at any price, is the only alternative.'57 In fact, it was ‘the relentless law of competition, or supply and demand, by which the rich become richer and the poor poorer,’ that had reduced the Indian labourers to this pass.258 What was then the alternative to laissez faire? G.S. Iyer recognised that a possible remedy could be the ‘spontaneous development of a feeling of generosity or charity on the part of the employers towards the helpless poor'. But he was sceptical about the efficacy of this suggestion because the development of such a feeling was ‘uncertain and indefinite and its practical operation must be spasmodical and capricious'. This left only one way out. The State was 'the only agency that could and should take up the responsibility of protecting the weaker side in this unlimited and really unequal competition, erroneously called free.'259

At the same time, G.S. Iyer felt that the workers must combine and form their own organisations in order to protect their interests against the encroachments of employers.26o At present, when one worker gave up his job in protest against some action of his employer, another worker was ready to take it up at reduced wages. In the absence of combination among the workers, the latter was 'subjected to no penalty of any kind by his community'. In contrast, the workers in other countries did not tolerate the fall of wages below a certain point; they struck work instead.z61 In fact, he noted, it was only after the English workers learnt to combine, and won the legal right to combine in 1824, that they began to advance, and it was 'organised labour' that had made 'the labouring classes such a powerful factor in the Western civilisation'.26z He, therefore, urged the Indian workers to 'organise themselves into unions, and resist what they consider to be an infringement of their rights on the part of the employers.'z63 He also appealed to the public to give every help to the workers in undertaking the task of organising themselves.264

G.S. Iyer was also the first and the only nationalist economist to study the problems of agricultural labour. In his major work referred to earlier, he devoted an entire chapter to this topic. After dwelling on their utterly miserable lot, he advocated an increase in their wages and warned the government against yielding to the pressure of landowners to the detriment of the labourers.265

Another Indian writer to show a keen awareness of the labour problem was Satis Chandra Mukherjea, the editor of the Dawn, whose views on the subject are to be found in the articles ‘Aspects of Economic Life in England and India', published in the Dawn of August 1898, and 'The Indian Economic Problem', published in the Dawn of March, April, May, and June 19oo. Mukherjea had a better understanding of the functioning of the industrial system of the West than any other contemporary Indian. Moreover, he claimed to be primarily guided in his thinking by the welfare of the worker and the ryot.266 But his analysis of the labour problem gains importance for quite another reason also; it was the first articulation of the outlook of the growing class of petty-bourgeois intellectuals, professionals, clerical employees, etc., who were opposed to the rising class of capitalists but who could not, at the same time, identify themselves with the new class of proletariat. It was perhaps not fortuitous that this voice rose first of all in Bengal where educated employees and professionals abounded but native capitalists were scarce.

Mukherjea started out by accepting the premise that India's economic resources had to be developed to the full.267 But, he argued, this simple proposition would not suffice, for another pertinent query arose at this stage: who developed and gained control over the resources?268 After tracing the history of the development of British industries, he pointed out that hitherto all the advantages of industrialisation had accrued to the capitalists. All that had happened was that with the advent of modern industry

the labouring population that had so long for some (previous)

centuries experienced the evils of domination by the great land-owning class, now fell into an equally depressed condition at the heels of the capitalist employer... though they in that sense (politically) emancipated, now became more and more economically dependent upon their capitalistemployers.269

Taking his facts from the famous work of Fredrick Engels, State of Working Classes in England in 1844, Mukherjea depicted the extreme poverty and poor conditions of the British workers.27o Even modern legislation had failed, he averred, to eliminate these evils because these evils were 'more deep-seated than might ordinarily appear'.271 Consequently, if the industrial system of the West was introduced in India, it would inevitably result in the creation of 'a small highly organised minority of a capitalist class, foreign or native, the growth of whose power and influence would not mean the growth of happiness of the masses'.27z The labouring population, on the other hand, would 'be reduced into mere human machines, hewers of wood and drawers of water, lost to all sense of self-respect, helpless and liable to be domineered over by their masters.273 To escape this miserable lot, the working masses would come together and form labour organisations on a gigantic scale and assist themselves by conducting a sort of labour-warfare against organised capitalistic forces'. This would make them 'a standing menace to peace and order' and, indeed, 'a permanent social and political danger'.274

These dual consequences of capitalistic industrialisation, Mukherjea felt, would place society on the horns of a real dilemma. While organisation of labour was a danger to the stability of society, absence of such organisation would be even worse. 'For the unchecked growth of a highly organised capitalist class would, ultimately, mean only industrial serfdom.'z75 This predicament should force people to pause and analyse whether the industrial problem of India could not be solved in any other way than 'by a wholesale incorporation, the good with the bad, the bad with the good' of a whole body of ideas and institutions which were ‘admittedly tainted at the root'.276

Mukherjea's solution of the problem was a two-tiered one, moral and material. All economic life should be reorganised in such a manner that 'the competitive principle would give way to one based on a higher ethical basis'.277 The economic problem should be treated as ‘a part of the wider problem of corporate ethical life'.278 In fact, in this new order of society, material progress, though encouraged, would be assigned a subordinate position and the people would have to recognise, 'in the first place, the essentially non-material, i.e., spiritual, character of all kinds of progress'.279 At the material level, the evils of modern capitalistic industrialism would be avoided by doing away, as far as possible, with modern industrial organisation itself and by establishing in its place a system of production mainly based on industrial family organisations of workers, craftsmen, and agriculturists. These family organisations would be supplemented by large-scale capitalist enterprises like engineering projects, mines, railways, chemical and metallurgical industries, etc., which required expensive machinery and large amounts of capital and ‘whose successful prosecution would not hinder, but be the means of helping on, individual and collective culture'.28o Mukherjea claimed that this individualistic system of economic organisation would produce an independent, self-respecting class of workers whose very daily work would become a means of culture.281 Combining the moral and economic contents of his system, Mukherjea was led to recommend as early as 19oo a corporate society primarily based on smallscale individualistic organisation of industry:

My idea, then, is to help on the work of national development by putting before us the ideal of a higher culture for all workers, in all the various departments of activity, spiritual, intellectual, military, commercial and wage-earning; by giving to each class a fixed, recognised and independent place in the social organisation but all cooperating in such ordered co-ordination as to work for the advantage of whole, and as to further the spiritual evolution of each ascending grade and of the whole of Indian society.282
