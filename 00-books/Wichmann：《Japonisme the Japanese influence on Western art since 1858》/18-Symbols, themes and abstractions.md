# Symbols, themes and abstractions

The spirit world

The first generation of Impressionist painters in France had investigated natural light down to the smallest detail. Light in its evanescence, in its transparent brightness and its coloured reflections, became the principal element in a picture thanks to a swiftly developing painting technique. Light in the picture was copied', both in its evanescence and in its stationary effect; its actual physical changes were painted; its effect on other media was investigated by means of the brush. Natural light was investigated with all its effects, so that it became possible to dissolve solid objects in light. The Impressionists saw nature as through a lens; their vision was so sharply concentrated on the qualities and the time-space location of light that the real, three-dimensional, tactile form of the Realists no longer had much to say to them. The painter was reduced to a reflecting eye; but, as Renoir remarked of Cézanne, What an eye!’

By contrast, the succeeding generation of painters in France, to which the Nabis, the Prophets, belonged, no longer wanted in their painting merely to see: they wanted, as Denis put it, ‘to re-think'. Similarly, European Symbolist painting took up at this period an opposing position to that of the depiction of evanescence. It was concerned rather to depict fairy-tale regions, strange worlds, ghosts and monsters, signs and symbols (529).¹

The Japanese ghost cult, which dominated daily life, was at the centre of the popular Kabuki theatre, reached its intellectual zenith in the Nō play and became known in Europe through the woodcut, and especially through the numerous volumes of Hokusai's Manga (715). The courage to depict deformity, which in Hokusai's work can easily lead to the grotesque, shows that a religious element could become one with the bizarre, the popular. There is much here that is philosophically and existentially profound; this stimulated the Europeans, and Odilon Redon above all must have found in Hokusai's works the confirmation of his own aims (716). Certainly Goya had also provided him with numerous impressions of this kind; but he found confirmation in Hokusai's work, on which his friends, the Nabis, had modelled themselves.

In 19oz Redon painted a decorative standing screen for Princess Cystria, and in 1905 he designed the great screens Buddha and The red tree (1905/o8).³ These links with Far Eastern art facilitate one's understanding of the Symbolist and surreal elements in his graphic art. Characteristic of these sheets is their dreamlike, ghostly quality (729), already encountered in Hokusai. The much more extensive and comprehensive Hokusai contrasts with Redon and his more specialized blackened spaces', or noirs, which are in conflict with the correct rendering of the objects depicted but in harmony with the darkness of their own significance (724, 725).

For Alfred Kubin, as he himself said, the presence of the black vision'was a daily burden. Horror, too (714), remains ever present to him: 'It is, of course, an experience which I have often depicted, but it was nonetheless astonishing when many years ago my pillow fell out of my bed in the moonlight and it looked to me for an hour and a half like the naked back of a terrible giant, so three-dimensional is the manner in which the soul transforms the material."⁴

Hokusai, too, in compulsive repetition, creates huge phantoms which can be seen above all in volumes 1o, 11 and 12 of Manga (715). Kubin's pen drawing Horror (714) conveys with hypernormal clarity the impression of a monster emerging from the unfathomable ocean which forms part of the sagas of all races and periods. Man's creations totter, and every initiative is fruitless. Terrors and anxieties seem to coincide here, for both Kuniyoshi (717) and Hokusai (715) depict related themes.

As early as x883 Redon creates nocturnal settings which create an almost tactile effect of grisly presence by means of frighteningly large heads (713) which are transported into the realm of the unreal by one protruding eye. In symbolic themes and surreal contexts the eye has become the prototype of all concentrated expression. It forces its way out of the head and can make itself independent by floating in space like a strange balloon.

The isolation of a single organ was also used by Hokusai, and appears in the most gruesome and enigmatic way in his Manga. In the tenth volume of Manga, Hokusai depicts a pilgrim resting at the side of the road, behind whom a gigantic one-eyed ghost appears (715). Hokusai revels in the extreme difference in scale, and it was this very print that was influential in European Symbolism. Max Klinger depicts a nocturnal encounter with a huge ghost⁵ in his etching which almost exactly corresponds to Kubin's words although Klinger had created his print ten years before they were uttered.“

Kuniyoshi in his Sea monk (717) represents the old Japanese legend of a huge monster which brings disaster to sailors. Kubin probably knew this print.

A painting by Redon of around 19oo sums up this theme. Already in 188o he was intérested in nocturnal, dream-like scenes. Above all else the motif of a huge head appears, which in the course of the 189os becomes more strongly deformed. His painting Cyclops must, however, be the definitive interpretation of this theme in colour (716, detail). Redon chooses a motif which has a striking similarity to Hokusai's in the tenth volume of Manga (715). Inside a tall geological formation, which has blossomed into colour (the influence of Gustave Moreau is possible), lies a naked figure, and over the ridge of the mountain looks a huge macabre being with one eye. Whereas Hokusai's depiction is a realistic narrative, Redon's is enigmatic and mysterious. Redon saw the world according to his own lights. At the sight of some magnificent barbarians' from Tierra del Fuego he said: the look in their eyes is filled with meaning. They are animals in the omnipotence of their instinct ... how ugly the old bourgeois are. How beautiful are these noble children of polar life. Their nakedness rises up from the ground like an Indian flower, luxuriant, harmonious. ... One should see these noble bodies surrounded with creeper in the twilight of the primeval forest or lying full length on the golden sand of pure lonely beaches."

In these words we hear the Symbolist longing for elemental humanity. European Symbolism was opposed to the bourgeois, whom it set out to shock with horrific themes. The artistic techniques that Redon creates for himself are calculated to emphasize what is exotic in man – he begins to mirror his own dream-like fate and life in the depiction of irrational landscapes. He creates lithographs in which he develops the black-and-white technique in various colour nuances to evoke and bring closer the ‘mysterious distance' in which his subjects move. The head is projected into the foreground. Analogies with Hokusai are obvious. The large fragment of a head (7x3) and the figure demonstrate the same irreconcilable opposités that Hokusai depicts in volumes 2, 3, 4, and 10−15 of his Manga (715).

Redon tries to combine the floral element in a fantastic contrast with the isolated human head. Hokusai also, in the superabundance of his imagination, has already made the attempt to combine disparate objects together unrealistically. Redon tries to convey experience of the world of ghosts by other means; but in Hokusai's work it always remains a juxtaposition of real objects. Redon, through the pictorial means at his disposal, makes the shimmering human head look mysterious in its nocturnal surroundings. In contrast to Hokusai, these surroundings always remain undefined; they can be read' only from fragments which do no more than hint at material reality (726, 729).

The medium of charcoal gave Redon's drawing that black, homogeneous impetus which guides and determines the clammy, underwater quality of his motifs. Redon is one of the great masters of shadow.

The great head with the body of a fish (724) is from the sequence The Temptation of St Antony. Under the influence of Flaubert's work, a submarine atmosphere has been added to the horrifying object. The fish-like amphibious body represents that same elusive, slippery world that was captured by the superabundant imagination of Hokusai: men who cling to huge eels, polyps which enclasp their victims in order to drag them into the depths.

The lantern ghost' is one of the most impressive of Hokusai's depictions of ghosts (727). On the lighted lantern, which has taken on the features of O-Iwa, murdered by her husband, can be read Namu Amida butsu, the formula for calling on the Buddha Amitabha, and the name of the dead woman. The forehead of the ghost bears a Sanskrit character.

Disembodied heads, eyeballs (713, 716), winged monsters, mythological beings, faces frozen in grief, dominate the scene. In East and West alike, the meaning is ambiguous; it can only be guessed at (723). Redon, Kubin and Hokusai were all filled with a deep scepticism towards life. All three were conscious of the miseries of existence, they were all acquainted with the power of the terrible. Very often it is not the unconscious but the conscious element of a particular apparition which convinces the observer. By means of superior techniques in the use of black and white Redon sensitively developed the graphic depiction of the void.

Natural objects, in Redon's work as well as in Hokusai's, are interchangeable. One can grow from the other. The reality of eerie surroundings, of the ambiguous twilight, of fabulous monsters, cancels itself out and turns into its opposite, into a macabre abstraction. Men become animals, animals become men. The dividing line between animal and vegetable kingdoms is blurred. Nature appears denatured: Because the microscopic world from which we sprang is at once as near to and as distant from the human eye as is space with its stars, one can appear projected onto the other. The eye, the amoeba, the galaxies, are all one according to pictorial logic."⁸

To return to Hokusai, and his depictions of ghosts in ukiyo-e prints and in the Manga: ukiyo-e prints dealing with ghosts are Sarayashiki and The mosquito net (Kohada Koheiji), both from A Hundred Stories (Hyakumonogatari). It is important that, in these colour prints, Hokusai uses heavy shading in order to give the shady kingdom that gloom which Redon and Kubin elevated later on into the principal subject of their artistry.

Apart from the depictions in the ukiyo-e prints, those which made the most lasting impression in Europe were in the Manga volumes. They have, however, never yet been looked at systematically with reference to early Symbolism and Surrealism. The following remarks on this theme are intended to give a general survey of the numerous possibilities of the depiction of ghosts in the Manga volumes, without providing an iconographical interpretation. It is important that, in Manga, magicians, monsters and hermaphrodites are firmly located in daily life and grow spontaneously out of it. Nothing in the world of phenomena is excluded. Rocks and mountains, air and water, everything is filled with an abstruse mobility which can change at any moment.

In the first volume of Manga magicians and artists are contrasted and, by means of lightning changes of shape, can remove themselves from the everyday world. To the pictorial themes in this volume belong also depictions of frightful events in which, twisted in anguish, ghosts and other supernatural apparitions bend over sleeping humans.

In the second volume dragons as symbols of good fortune fill the pages. They often appear next to accurate representations of snakes, so that apparent realism can be found next to the surreal. On other prints priests invoke nature, waves and burning trees; devils sit next to hermits; fiery heads rotate in the shape of wheels.

In the third volume the world of ghosts is introduced through the fox spirit Inari or Inari Sama. Mandarins glide through the air on picture scrolls or on fiery mountains; they stride over the ocean, or float on wild ducks. Then we see a spirit who emerges from a mouth. Whole pictures contain nothing but heads on strings, double bodies and outlandishly elongated limbs. Fleeing spirits crowd together in the air. Demons, curled into balls, surround the hermit.

At the beginning of the fourth volume the apparitions become increasingly grotesque: dramatic situations with enormous dragon shapes, floating spirits and fettered devils.

In the fifth volume, temple spirits guard the sacred shrine and horned figures kneel on the cliffs of an island.

In the sixth volume, demons float on a wild boar over the surface of the water. Two whole pages are covered with a many-armed warrior flying through the air.

In the tenth volume, monkeys and fox demons threaten a princess. Magicians emerge from bottles, or their heads grow huge in the rising steam. A travelling conjuror makes figures appear magically out of the sleeve of his kimono, and a swarm of bees flies out of his mouth. Over a whole series of pages the changing shapes of conjurors are depicted as they saw off one arm, conjure up huge toads or cause water to spray out of a candle. A rider emerges from a bottle-gourd. A huge, one-eyed spectre appears behind a hermit. This volume in particular is filled with numerous depictions of ghosts. Half-human, half-animal hybrids adorn the pages, as do samurai riding on fish or on tortoises. Conjurors and magicians fill the pages again and again. A mouse kingdom is depicted, where the mice go about their business dressed in particularly decorative silk clothes.

In the twelfth volume, turnips appear as human beings and their hanging shapes are demonstrated in an eerie series. Birdmen fight before a large demon, or they appear as conjurors with enormous noses. Huge toads, and alienation effects achieved by holding up magnifying glasses, set the absurd tone of the pictures. Long, scraggy necks and arms extend over double pages. Dreamers are plagued by semi-human monsters. A fox spirit lies in wait behind a bundle of rice straw, and huge tortoises emerge from the sea. Enormous octopuses pursue two wanderers; a dragon spins through the air. Wayfarers are caught in a huge spider's web, and a gigantic worm menaces a falling hero. Ghosts with elongated extremities people the landscape; swimming figures clutch powerful eels.

The thirteenth volume begins with the motif of a fiery dragon which is repeated, suspended in front of the landscape. A carp swims with a goddess, and a huge elephant appears with dwarfish companions.

We encounter in the fourteenth volume an apparition in the sea in the form of a dragon, and a huge octopus which has captured a human being. Beings whose shape is something between an animal and a man intrude into the flow of daily life.
