Following this, over the next four years the factory's chemist Frantz Heinrich Müller (1732–1820) experimented with Bornholm kaolin in an unsuccessful attempt to producehigh-quality biscuitware. Around the same time, the factory's debts were mounting to such an extent that in 1779, King Frederick V stepped in to assume personal financial responsibility for it. The rationale for saving the factory stemmed from his hope of establishing Denmark as one of the leading cultural centres in Europe, and the production of fine porcelain – then known as“white gold” – was one of the surest ways of achieving this. The factory was subsequently re-named Den Kongelige Danske Porcelains Fabrik and a ban was imposed on all imports of European porcelain into Denmark. During the 1780s and 1790s, the factory mainly produced blue-painted services for everyday use, often copying or adapting shapes and patterns from Meissen and other European manufacturers. It also manufactured multi-coloured wares and designs in the Rococo style that were more experimental both technically and artistically. The most notable design to emerge from Royal Copenhagen during this period was the exquisite Flora Danica (Danish Flowers) service, which was commissioned by the Danish court in c. 1789 for presentation to Catherine theGreat of Russia. This famous service, which was ultimately retained by the King of Denmark, was painstakingly decorated with flowers and plants copied from botanical engravingsthat illustrated G. C.Oeder's Flora of the Danish Kingdom. The engravings had been devised by a Nuremberg artist living in Copenhagen, J.C. Bayer, who was employed by the factory from 1776. Impressively, Bayer virtually single-handedly painted all the highly detailed naturalistic floral motifs on the service's pieces, the forms of which were designed by F.L. Bradt. When the Danish court finally ceased production of the service in 1802, it comprised a staggering 1,802 items(1,530 surviving today), making it one of the largest services in the world. During the early years of the 19th century, the patterning of the factory's blue-painted porcelainwas simplified in order to make it easier and less expensive to produce. During this period, the factory put the development of new products on hold and relied on the production of Rococo style dinner, coffee, tea and chocolate services with its famous Blue Fluted and Blue Flower patterns. It was not until the 1820s, on the instigation of the architect and designerG.F. Hetsch(1788–1864), that Neo-Classical wares in the new Empire style began to be manufactured by the company. Apart from these, duringthe 1830sand 1840sthe factory also produced a number of biscuitware figurines and reliefs modelledon works by the Danish Neo-Classical sculptor, Bertel Thorvaldsen (1770–1844). The superlative quality of Royal Copenhagen's biscuitwareproducts led the factory to become one of Europe's most respected porcelain manufacturers. In 1868, the company passed into private ownership, and over the following years the once state-subsidized enterprise gradually became a profitable commercial venture. Royal Copenhagen was subsequently acquired in 1882 by the engineer PhilipSchou, who had previously founded the successful earthenware works Aluminia in 1863. Two years later, the factory was moved from its premises in Købmagergade to a larger purpose-built sitein Frederiksberg, then a suburb of Copenhagen. Having dealt with the production side of the business, Schou set about revitalizing the artistic aspects of the manufactory. To this end, he hired Arnold Krog in 1884 as a designer, and the following year appointed him as the firm's artistic director. The same year, Krog and Schou went on a study tour of Holland, Belgium, England and France, and while in Paris made the acquaintance of Siegfried Bing (1838–1905), who owned an oriental warehouse in Paris that catered to the increasing demand for Aesthetic Movement objets d’art. On his return to Copenhagen, Krog not only oversaw a renewal of the company's Blue Fluted pattern but also introduced a new decorative technique in which cobalt blue pigment was combined with glaze and then applied to unfired porcelain blanks. The pigment was then gently scraped with a knife to reveal areas of the underlying white material. This layered underglaze painting technique was mainly used for designs that were decorated with motifs inspired by Japanese engravings and woodcuts, such as those illustrated in Siegfried Bing's journal, Le Japon Artistique. Krog was also responsible for the introduction of other eclectic and revivalist styles, including Rococo, Renaissance, Viking, Persian, Chinese and Russian. It was, however, the formal simplicity and delicate abstracted decoration of his Japanese-style wares of the 1880s that had the greatest stylistic influence. Apart from soft cobalt blue, other muted coloured pigments, including a dusky pink, a dark green and a misty grey, were developed to withstand the high firing temperatures used for Krog's innovative underglaze painting technique. During his tenure, Krog recruited a number of progressive artists to produce designs for the factory, including Carl Frederick Liisberg(1860–1909), Carl Mortensen (1866-1945), Marianne Høst (1865– 1943), V.T. Fischer (1857–1928), Gotfred Rode (1830-1878)and Bertha Nathanielsen(1869– 1914). During the early decades of the 20th century, Royal Copenhagen established workshops dedicated to the design and manufacture of stoneware products, such as Krog's Cobra vase (1914), and the development of special glazes. Between the World Wars, the factory became renowned for its production of sculptural figures designed by artists such as ArnoMalinowski, Jais Nielsen (1895-1961), Gerhard Henning(1880-1967) and Knud Kyhn (1880–1969). In 1933, Axel Salto joined the design team at Royal Copenhagen and subsequently developed numerous stoneware art pieces with expressive sculptural forms that predicted the post-war Scandinavian fascination with organic shapes. During this period Nils Thorsson (1898-1975) also designed a number of stoneware objects as well as practical tableware for the company. From the post-war period to the present day, Royal Copenhagen has continued to produce its well-known historic designs, such as Flora Danica and Blue Fluted, alongside more contemporary products, such as the Blåkant (Blue Rim)service(1965) byGrethe Meyer, the shell-shaped Konkylle service (1977) by Arje Griegst (b. 1938), and the Ursula faïence dinnerware(1993) by Ursula Munch-Petersen (b. 1937). Having recruited some forty engineers from Boeing, this early manufacturing venture initially produced versions of Northrop and North American aircraft before developing its own designs, such as the Saab 17 bomber, the Saab 21, which featured the world's first ejector seat, and the Saab 29, which was the first European swept-wing fighter. In 1945, the Swedish Air Force stopped purchasing aircraft, so Saab decided to transfer its skills and expertise to the design and production of automobiles instead. The company assigned fifteen of its aircraft engineers to the development of its first car prototype. Because the engineers had never worked on such a project before, they were “entirely unfettered by the conventions and traditions of the established automobile industry”, and as a result, developed a completely different kind of car with front-wheel drive, a transverse engine, a strong safety cage and an aerodynamically shaped stabilizing wing. This remarkable prototype – now known as the Saab 92001 (1947) – formed the basis from which Saab's first production car evolved. With a truly revolutionary streamlined monocoque body that was styled by Sixten Sason and engineered by Gunnar Ljungström, the Saab 92 was wholly influenced both formally and technically by aeronautical design. It was also the first small fourseater “people's car” to be produced in Sweden and reflected Sason's design philosophy that while form should follow function, products should not look too utilitarian. The Saab 92 evolved over the following decades into other Saab models, including the well-known Saab 95 estate car (1959) and the highly successful Saab 96 (1960), which was one of the company's best-sellers with more than 547,000 sold within a twenty-year period. Later, Sason and Ljungström developed a new car, the Saab 99, which was compact yet spacious inside, aerodynamically efficient, and economical to run. Launched around 1968, the Saab 99 introduced a number of innovative features over the following years, such as headlamp cleaning (1970), selfrepairing bumpers, heated seats (1971) and sideimpact door beams (1972). Bjorn Envall, who assisted Sason with the styling of the Saab 99, once tellingly remarked, “It's a very dangerous thing if you're getting too serious. The exterior of the car should tell you something. It should speak to you somehow." This remark reveals a very Scandinavian perception of design – one that understands the importance of imbuing a product with a gentle aesthetic that communicates fundamentally humanist goals. The Saab 99 also became the blueprint for the company's later cars, including the Saab 90 (1985) and the Saab 9-5 SE (1997). During the 1970s the company began producing “medium segment" cars, and later in the 1980s began manufacturing even larger vehicles for the executive market. Having previously merged with the truck manufacturer Scania in 1969, Saab eventually came under the aegis of General Motors in 1990, when the American automotive giant acquired a 50% stake in the company. Typical of a Scandinavian company, Saab is committed to sustainable development – from the reduction of emissions to the minimization of "resource use”. The company also operates a highly ethical social programme, which includes the sponsorship of ![](images/9188e9137ddec05a827db9716d50fbd62ff6476adbafee7b23414cee645a4d09.jpg) various environmental projects and the running of its own rehabilitation centre, known as the Component, for staff who “fall victim to disease, accidents and work-related injuries”. Throughout its history, Saab Automobile AB has combined design, performance and safety in the development of its high-quality and unmistakable vehicles, while its sister company Saab Aircraft AB manufactures both commercial and military aircraft that are based on the continuing application of innovative engineering concepts.
