III Arts, Crafts, and Machines: Reconciling Progress and Fears of Industrialization (1866–1914) n the second half of the nineteenth century in Britain and extending to Europe and North America, the efforts of the early design reform movement dovetailed with other reform initiatives that contributed to elevating the cultural significance of the applied arts. Beginning in the later 1850s, the writings and activities of John Ruskin and William Morris in England placed the design and manufacture of the applied arts within a revitalized ethical framework based upon an almost religious conviction that united craft and art with social, and often radical political, change directed against industrial capitalism. Ruskin and Morris criticized the dehumanizing consequences of mechanization and the increasing division of labor in factories, and their efforts inspired others who established workshops, crafts organizations, and crafts communities on both sides of the Atlantic dedicated to the individual human and social benefits of handicraft as a replacement for, or antidote to, the often alienating effects of an urban, industrialized world. While never unified and often quite varied, the results of Ruskin’s and Morris’s activities are generally referred to as the Arts and Crafts movement. The immediate context for the elevation of craft in aesthetic and social terms was a widening gap between fine and applied art in the West, but a broader cultural perspective included the threat to human rights, sovereignty, and self-determination posed by industrial expansion and imperialism. Only slightly later, and again in North America, Britain, and throughout Europe, a number of architects and fine artists began to direct their creative activities to interior design and home furnishings. These designers called attention to the aesthetic and expressive character of the home and its decoration as a “total work of art,” emphasizing originality and the unity and integration of all the visual arts. At the same time, a number of younger designers sought to reconcile individual expression with issues of manufacturability and competition, forming organizations and publishing journals that highlighted the value of practicality and the shaping of public taste. Their initiatives reached a broader public through participation in the increasing number of well-attended national and international exhibitions both in Europe and in the United States. The continued expansion of commerce and industrial manufacturing hastened the search for new markets and the need for increasing supplies of raw materials. The vast natural resources of the African continent were a target for exploitation through Western colonization in the name of progress, and this is the focus of Part III of the Global Inspiration (see here): Britain, France, and Belgium each competed for control of African territory, accompanied by the often brutal subjugation of Indigenous populations used as enslaved laborers in the mining and rubber industries. The colonization of the Congo by King Leopold II of Belgium beginning in 1885 left its mark on Belgian decorative arts and utilitarian objects through the use of imported ivory and rubber, and imagery that suggested power, violence, and domination. African societies had a well-developed and varied materia culture that included locally produced dyed and woven fabrics for dress, scarification, and ritual, as well as utilitarian objects with distinctive forms and decoration. A small number of European companies designed and manufactured textiles for the African market that are still being produced today (see here and here). In the United States, printed advertising and illustrated mail-order catalogs beginning in the 1880s spurred demand for and access to manufactured goods and furnishings, accompanied by new methods of rationalized and mechanized production as well as branding and national distribution. The success of the department store beginning in the 1860s also stimulated the marketing of manufactured goods to an expanding consumer-oriented middle class. Such democratizing tendencies produced a wide variety of historicizing styles such as the Renaissance and Rococo Revivals as well as more original or “modern” approaches to design recommended by tastemakers such as Charles Eastlake in his Hints on Household Taste, first published in 1868 and directed toward cultivating a discriminating judgment among a female readership (see here). Finally, with Germany and the Austro-Hungarian Empire playing important roles, designers, design organizations, schools, and exhibition organizers sought to address and reconcile what were seen as conflicting attitudes toward technology, standardization, ornament, national identity, and the scientific management of production on the one hand and individual identity and self-expression on the other. This was aimed at maximizing production efficiency and reforming the tastes of middle-class consumers by establishing and reinforcing more standardized and socially responsible forms for modern manufactured goods. Amid various debates and activities on an increasingly global scale, the aesthetic, technical, commercial, and social meanings of the applied arts enlarged the scope of design enterprise activity in the years leading up to World War I. ## Chapter 5 The Joy of Work Ruskin, Morris, and the Arts and Crafts Movement in Britain English writer and art critic John Ruskin (18191900) played an important role in shaping attitudes toward the decorative arts in the second half of the nineteenth century. Like his fellow Englishman A. W. N. Pugin, Ruskin admired the crafts of the Middle Ages and saw the decorative arts as a vehicle for enlightenment, both for the artisans who made goods and furnishings and for the public who might own and appreciate them. The values he attached to the Gothic style, however, concerned the nature of work, an elevated view of labor, which he felt was fundamentally undermined by mechanization, the division of labor, and a capitalist system that increasingly alienated workers from the products of their efforts in the name of progress. For Ruskin, the specialization inherent in the division of labor, and commercial pressures that stimulated cheaper, faster production, deprived workers of the satisfaction of making products from start to finish as he believed they were made in the guilds during the Middle Ages: We have much studied and much perfected, of late, the great civilized invention of the division of labour; only we give it a false name. It is not, truly speaking, the labour that is divided; but the men: divided into mere segments of men  broken into small fragments and crumbs of life; so that all the little piece of intelligence that is left in a man is not enough to make a pin, or a nail, but exhausts itself in making the point of a pin or the head of a nail. Ruskins views on art and design drew in part upon his own travels and careful observations and drawings of architecture and decoration; the sensitivity he developed to the working methods of craftsmen formed the basis for conclusions about the nature of craft and inspired sweeping, if unrealistic, solutions to the social ills of an industrial society. In looking at carved architectural ornament from the Gothic period, Ruskin admired the dialogue between artisan and materials, the imperfections, the differences between one carved capital and another, the struggle to overcome the resistance of stone or wood with the tools at hand in the realization of an idea. The methods of the craftsman were, for Ruskin, the basis for satisfying work and an equality among the arts in which craftsman and artist alike shared in the redeeming act of human labor. Ruskin deplored the machine, because machines made work easy or just plain dull, and because they resulted in uniform products lacking the individuality of their handmade counterparts: All the stamped metals, and artificial stones, and imitation woods and bronzes, over the invention of which we hear daily exultation  all the short, and cheap, and easy ways of doing that whose difficulty is its honour  are just so many new obstacles in our already encumbered road. They will not make one of us happier or wiser  they will extend neither the pride of judgement nor the privilege of enjoyment. They will only make us shallower in our understandings, colder in our hearts, and feebler in our wits. It probably comes as no surprise that Ruskin was no admirer of Joseph Paxtons Crystal Palace, for no cast-iron structure, regardless of its grand proportions or economy of construction, could aspire to being art in the sense of having been the product of the direct manipulation of materials that Ruskin understood to be the essence of all artistic endeavor. Ruskin questioned the equation between technology and progress as the defining characteristic of modern civilization, raising awareness of loss as well as gain, much as critics today debate the role played by robots and artificial intelligence and the ways they impact contemporary life. In addition to his analysis of the cultural meaning of art and architecture, Ruskin articulated his views with a renewed sense of urgency regarding social reform. The only child of a wealthy wine merchant and the beneficiary of a comfortable inheritance, Ruskin was nevertheless sensitive to the levels of poverty, unemployment, and poor working conditions in Britain. He felt a sense of social conscience and responsibility not uncommon among wealthy families of his day, which found outlets in political activism, philanthropy, and other private initiatives aimed at the well-being of the poor and working classes. Rather than applauding the economic expansion and increased productivity of his own time, he blamed industrialization and materialism for exacerbating income inequality and misery, and saw the restoration of meaningful work as a means to alleviate them. Indeed, his exaltation of labor may be seen as a compensation for his own loss of faith, for if religion provided little comfort to humanity in a materialistic modern world, then it might be replaced by some substitute spirituality. For Ruskin, that substitute was the happiness provided by work, and thus the products of work acquired a spiritual dimension. It follows that if labor was its own reward, then the technological and material progress of the nineteenth century represented for Ruskin only a kind of empty materialism that ignored the spiritual values that should inform an ideal society: The foundations of society were never yet shaken as they are at this day. It is not that men are ill-fed, but that they have no pleasure in the work by which they make their bread, and therefore look to wealth as the only means of pleasure. [Italics authors own] Earlier in his career, Ruskin had championed the cause of the British landscape painter J. M. W. Turner (17751851) in his book Modern Painters, vol. 1 (he appears in the 2014 biopic Mr. Turner with Ruskin played by Joshua McGuire), but Ruskin grew to question whether the uplifting qualities embodied in Turners works would enrich the lives of working people. Ruskin then focused his attention on the decorative arts because their cost as well as their utility might have a greater impact upon the workers who made and might enjoy them. Work was a common element in the lives of both the middle and working classes, a basis for social equality linked to mutual respect among people. Unlike Pugin or Owen Jones, it was joy rather than taste that governed Ruskins attitudes: The right question to ask, respecting all ornament, is simply this: Was it done with enjoyment  was the carver happy while he was about it? It may be the hardest work possible, and the harder because so much pleasure was taken in it; but it must have been happy too, or it will not be living. In other words, Ruskin advocated no particular style or set of rules for designers to follow other than that the work be a unique creation reflecting a craftsmans skill, pride, and effort. His views share with designers of the Aesthetic Movement an interest in individuality (see Chapter 6), but Ruskins overriding emphasis was upon the moral and spiritual benefits of handwork, that is, as much upon the doing as upon the finished product. While Ruskin remained primarily a writer and theorist commenting on the relationship between art and society, William Morris (18341896) was more interested in integrating theory with real-world practice. Like Ruskin, Morris came from a wealthy middle-class background and benefited from an annual income from his familys investment in the lucrative copper-mining industry, yet he also possessed a strong sense of social conscience and responsibility characteristic of the Victorian age. As a student at Oxford preparing for a career in the clergy, he was deeply moved by Ruskins ideas concerning the relation between the arts and social reform, and also shared friendship and ideals with a group of young Romantic artists and poets who called themselves the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood (PRB). The group criticized modern materialism and industrialization and was committed to creating a closer relation between art and craft in reaction against the established policies of the Royal Academy in Britain and the elevated status it accorded the fine arts. It was Morriss kinship with the PRB that led him to abandon his training for the ministry and pursue a career as an artist-craftsman, with art equated to any form of making. One can detect the echo of Ruskin in Morriss own writings, which had a lasting effect upon designers in the later nineteenth and twentieth centuries, and which find echoes in phrases such as learning by doing that stress direct skill-based education: Yet I cannot in my own mind quite sever them [the arts of architecture, painting, and sculpture] from those so-called Decorative Arts ... it is only in latter times, and under the most intricate conditions of life, that they have fallen apart from one another; I hold that, when they are so parted, it is ill for the Arts altogether; the lesser ones become trivial, mechanical, unintelligent, incapable of resisting the changes pressed upon them by fashion or dishonesty; while the greater, however they may be practiced for a while by men of great minds and wonder-working hands, unhelped by the lesser, unhelped by each other, are sure to lose their dignity as popular arts, and become nothing but dull adjuncts to unmeaning pomp, or ingenious toys for a few rich and idle men. In addition, Morris was a poet, novelist, publisher, social activist, translator, preservationist, and public speaker, whose myriad interests and activities proved influential in Britain, Germany, Belgium, France, and the United States (in fact, Morriss writings were influential even for those, such as Frank Lloyd Wright, who freely interpreted his views). The spiritualization of craft, its connection with social justice, and skepticism toward the widely held view that equated industrialization with progress characterize Morriss values and became the basis for a number of organizations and related initiatives that are known generally as the Arts and Crafts movement. Morriss design activities began with the interior decoration of his home, Red House, in Kent, beginning in 1858, and designed by his friend Philip Webb (18311915). The house eschewed the tendency toward historicizing period styles as well as expensive (or expensive-looking) materials in favor of straightforward brick and painted wood, modest Gothic pointed arches, and a less formal, asymmetrical plan. Rather than purchase furnishings from a catalog or commission them from a showroom or workshop, Morris and his friends took on the task themselves. At the outset, Morriss wife, Jane (18391914), took an interest in embroidery, while Webb, painter Edward Burne-Jones (18331898), and poet and artist Dante Gabriel Rossetti (18281882, a member of the PRB) designed furniture and stained-glass windows. The ideal of a more direct and self-sufficient lifestyle appealed to Morris, who later wrote, If I were asked to say what is at once the most important production of Art and the thing most to be longed for I should answer, A beautiful House. In 1861 he formed Morris, Marshall, Faulkner & Co., opening a showroom in London, for the design and manufacture of a wide variety of furnishings, including furniture, wallpapers, textiles, stained glass, carpets, woven tapestries, and ceramics. In 1875 Morris reorganized the venture as Morris & Co. The company, or Firm, as it was known, put Morriss ideas into wider practice and might be considered the beginning of the Arts and Crafts movement. An example of the Firms furniture is the Sussex line of chairs with handwoven rush seats using turned spindles for legs and backs. Sussex chairs were designed by Webb and Rossetti, but were based upon country examples found in southeast England (fig. 5.1). Such furnishings established a link with pre-industrial craft traditions only minimally affected by contemporary commercial practice or by the use of more modern machine tools and materials. As a craftsman himself, Morris first relied upon his training as a painter in the design of stained-glass windows and the painted decoration of wooden furniture; from the 1870s onward he primarily designed patterns that were applied to the manufacture of ceramic tiles, embroideries, wood-blocks for wallpaper or fabric printing, and carpets. In many cases Morris relied upon local craftsmen to execute his designs, but he also practiced techniques himself, installing equipment at home, first in London then in the western suburb of Hammersmith after 1878. He also leased facilities for weaving at Merton Abbey, Surrey, where he experimented in the development of vegetable dyes for fabrics rather than commercially available aniline dyes, patented in 1856 and produced as a by-product of coal tar. For ceramics he seems to have entrusted production to artistcraftsman William de Morgan (18391917; see here and fig. 6.7), an independent potter who had worked previously for the Firm, and who also produced his own designs, including tiles for the home of Lord Frederic Leighton (see fig. GI2.1). Whether directly or indirectly involved in actual production, Morris advocated close collaboration between designer and craftsman and refused to use machines even for the printing of blocked wallpapers or fabrics: It is not desirable to divide the labour between the artist and what is technically called the designer, and I think it desirable on the whole that the artist and designer should practically be one. Here Morris describes a fluid working method that differs from the collaborative but hierarchical relationship between artist and craftsman, where the craftsman carries out the creative design of the artist. Morriss own pattern designs seem to follow the general tendency of earlier design reform principles that viewed decoration in the context of architecture, that is, as planar, if often rich, patterns that embellished construction; it was the unity and implied collaboration in his understanding of architecture that guided his approach. He also believed in the importance of individual expression, and advocated original designs in addition to those inspired by the decorative patterns in medieval illuminated manuscripts that he admired and collected. We might think of Morriss patterns as a dialogue, often tense, between a love of the richness and diversity of nature found in plants and flowers and the discipline needed to transform that appreciation into patterns suitable for the embellishment of flat surfaces. An example is his Pimpernel wallpaper of 1876 (fig. 5.2). Against a dark green background, lighter green flowers are symmetrically arranged and repeated, but varied in their positions; each is framed by interlocking spiral vine tendrils in brown loosely entwined by thin green leaves. A secondary motif consists of smaller and flatter green leaves with round blue morning glories placed at regular intervals along the stems. The naturalism is at its height in the windswept flowers and their undulating contours, slightly reduced for the thin leaf forms and becoming more schematic for the blue flowers, tendrils, and smaller oval leaves. The sense of natures luxuriant variety and vitality, and the care with which the designer preserves this freshness within the constraints of pattern for a two-dimensional surface, is remarkable in much of Morriss best work. ![](images/b002320b84b99cf34e632e892666345ff390bb3210998ce2736f6b5682811fbe.jpg) ## 5.2 William Morris, Pimpernel design for wallpaper, block-printed, $2 6 ~ ^ { 3 } / _ { 4 } \times 2 0 ~ ^ { 3 } / _ { 4 }$ in (68 × 52.5 cm), manufactured by Jeffrey & Co. for Morris & Co., 1876. In addition to operating facilities at his residence and leasing separate facilities for production in and around London, Morris & Co. maintained a storefront showroom on Oxford Street, London, where other fashionable retail establishments were located, and the Firm exhibited work at Worlds Fairs, for instance in London in 1862 and at the Centennial Exhibition held in Philadelphia in 1876. ## Morris, Activism, and Socialism Despite the success of the Firm, and projects such as the Green Room that were open to the public, Morris became increasingly disillusioned by the inability of his design activities to effect broad social change and to reach his intended working-class audience. Labor and material costs precluded all but the wealthy from commissioning or purchasing the Firms products, and his views on labor and society were mostly embraced by a well-to-do clientele motivated by conscience to contribute to the wellbeing of the working class. In the 1880s he became actively involved with the socialist movement in Britain as a means of creating social equality and reducing the pressures for production and consumption that fueled the capitalist economic system and an aggressive foreign policy: What I mean by Socialism is a condition of society in which there should be neither rich nor poor, neither master nor masters man, neither idle nor overworked, neither brain-sick brain workers nor heart-sick hand workers ... in which all men would be living in equality of condition, and would manage their affairs unwastefully and with the full consciousness that harm to one would mean harm to all  the realization at last of the meaning of the word COMMONWEALTH. Another recurrent theme in his writings, echoing Ruskin, is the excessive self-interest that was created by competition: Manufacturers are so set on carrying out competition to its utmost, competition of cheapness, not of excellence, that they meet the bargain-hunters half way, and cheerfully furnish them with many wares at the cheap rate. Morris was active in organized socialist activities in London, and his views, delivered in public speeches and in the journal he founded entitled The Commonweal, echo themes of alienated labor, the concentration of wealth and growth of capital in partnership with industry in quest of profit, and the fetishism of commodities found in the writings of Karl Marx (18181883) and Friedrich Engels (18201895). Morriss politics were radical; he sensed that it was not enough merely to make and enjoy handmade objects as a craftsman and socially conscious businessman, and that real change would only come with more direct political activism to foment working-class revolution. But while Morriss ideology was opposed to industrial capitalism in terms of the economic inequality it brought, he could not account for or effectively fight the increasing attraction of consumption (and shopping) across all classes of modern society. Ironically, the success of Morris & Co. depended almost entirely upon an exclusive market, thus in a sense it was supported by the same capitalist system he hoped to eliminate. Eventually Morris acknowledged a role for mechanization in the production of goods and furnishings, particularly if it reduced drudgery. But his acceptance of the machine was narrowly circumscribed and he felt that mechanization should never deprive a craftsman of the opportunity to find pleasure in work. Nevertheless his views appear to have become, not without some irony, the basis for the later equation of machine-made goods with simple designs and smooth surfaces: The more mechanical the process, the less direct should be the imitation of natural forms. Such an approach, an extension of the design reform dictum of truth to materials, was espoused by American architect Frank Lloyd Wright (18691959), whose own attitudes toward the machine are considered below (see here; 142). ## Morris as Publisher William Morriss last design venture was the founding of the Kelmscott Press in 1890, which brought together his attitudes toward art and design with his activities as poet and author. The press printed an illustrated edition of his own poem Story of the Glittering Plain as well as a short volume containing Ruskins Nature of Gothic chapter from The Stones of Venice, which eloquently praised the dignity of work. In Morriss introduction to the Nature of Gothic, he wrote: For the lesson which Ruskin here teaches us is that art is the expression of mans pleasure in labour; that it is possible for man to rejoice in his work, for, strange as it may seem to us to-day, there have been times when he did rejoice in it; and lastly, that unless mans work once again becomes a pleasure to him, the token of which change will be that beauty is once again a natural and necessary accompaniment of productive labour, all but the worthless must toil in pain, and therefore live in pain. So that the result of the thousands of years of mans effort on the earth must be general unhappiness and universal degradation; unhappiness and degradation, the conscious burden of which will grow in proportion to the growth of mans intelligence, knowledge, and power over material nature. Morriss close friend Edward Burne-Jones designed woodcut illustrations for another Kelmscott Press publication, The Kelmscott Chaucer (fig. 5.4), while Morris himself designed border patterns for pages with illustrations, as well as the Latin typeface and block initials of various sizes. The Kelmscott Chaucer used a calligraphic typeface with characteristics of Gothic (Blackletter) letter forms that Morris drew, and which he called Troy, one of three faces he designed for the press. His Roman font, known as Golden, was influenced by fifteenth-century printers working in Venice such as Jacobus Rubeus and Nicholas Jenson, who were responsible for some of the very first Roman (as opposed to Blackletter) types beginning around 1470. Morriss Golden avoided the mechanical precision of modern fonts (see figs. 1.13 and 1.14) in favor of heavier forms with modulated transition from thick to thin strokes and the low x-height characteristic of fifteenth-century Venetian models. Morriss interest in these Venetian types, sometimes known as humanistic, in turn stimulated a revival of letterpress printing known as the Private Press Movement. The richly decorated pages of The Kelmscott Chaucer fulfilled Morriss objective to make the book itself an object of beauty, even if some historians find many of its more elaborate pages less well suited to reading, perhaps a result of the distraction from elements other than type. The Kelmscott Press was located at the artists home in Hammersmith on the bank of the Thames river. The venture emerged at a time when photomechanical reproduction was transforming the character of illustration, and when steam presses were finally being introduced into the bookpublishing industry. Inspired by the example of the nearby Chiswick Press (see here) and the independent efforts of printers and other craftsmen to maintain the craft traditions of the printed book, Morris succeeded in revitalizing fine printing, even if the cost of such efforts limited their readership, generally attracting only a well-to-do clientele and published in short print runs. ![](images/4f16a4ce9a61de40fd2c30ad613ccac6372d9e0ea2eb815ff9197367dc412030.jpg) William Morris (wood-engraved illustrations after designs by Edward Burne-Jones), folio from The Works of Geoffrey Chaucer, 16 <sup>3</sup>/<sub>4</sub> × 11 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>2</sub> in (42.5 × 29.2 cm), printed at the Kelmscott Press, London, 1896. Illustrated books, art journals, and a burgeoning illustrated childrens literature constituted the means by which the Arts and Crafts movement reached different audiences. Publishing is a collaborative enterprise, and a number of printers, founders, binders, and illustrators participated in Morriss Kelmscott Press venture. Emery Walker (1851 1933), for instance, worked as a printer at the Chiswick Press not far from where Morris lived. Prior to the establishment of the Kelmscott Press, Walker had contributed to The Hobby Horse journal, published by the Century Guild (see here). After Morriss death in 1896, Walker and lawyer-turned-bookbinder T. J. Cobden-Sanderson (18401922) founded their own publishing company known as the Doves Press, also in Hammersmith (in fact, directly across the alley from Kelmscott on the Thames riverside). Their efforts combined concerns for the quality of paper and ink, the aesthetics of calligraphy, legibility in type and the spacing of lines and letters, ample white space, harmonious relationships among borders, initials, and illustration, and the overall unity of the page or open double page. Walker designed a Roman font for the press (called Doves), while the calligrapher and teacher Edward Johnston (18721944) designed initials, often printed in red, to enliven the page layouts, as seen in a page from the Doves Bible, printed between 1903 and 1905 (fig. 5.5). The Doves Press embodies the idea of the book as a work of craft and beauty that achieves a unified interrelationship of its parts. Its margins are ample, the initials and title lettering fluid and elegant (recalling a restrained form of the hand-painted initials in early printed books), and the typeface equally refined. It has a taller x-height for lower-case letters and an ample use of white space not found in the darker and shorter forms of Morriss Golden font, but resulting in a purity and restraint in comparison to the busier pages of The Kelmscott Chaucer. A rift between the two partners of the Doves Press resulted in its closure in 1916. Toward the end of his career, Morriss vision for a better society took the form of utopian writings, often published first in serial form in the socialist journal Commonweal. The romance News from Nowhere (1890) takes place in England in the twenty-second century, but its setting is reminiscent of the riverside village community of Hammersmith where Morris lived and worked. In 1887 an Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society was founded to organize annual shows of craft objects. The versatile designer William Lethaby (18571931) established a school in London based upon a close relationship between designer and maker and the practical value underlying and uniting all art: a work of art is first of all a well-made thing. It may be a well-made statue or a wellmade chair, or a well-made book.... Most simply and generally art may be thought of as the well-doing of what needs doing; every work of art shows that it was made by a human being for a human being. ![](images/900c1d441a20f8e3511112a0e7ebe0acc6346859b08f97c0ef6cf4f710b06dc7.jpg) Other craft associations, both in Britain and the United States (see here), followed in the wake of Morriss efforts to elevate the aesthetic appreciation and egalitarian social values of craft production. Charles Robert Ashbee (18631942) was another versatile designer and educator who founded a community of workers with the goal of living and working together in utopian harmony. After graduating from Cambridge University in 1886 Ashbee moved to Toynbee Hall, a residence for university graduates in a workingclass section of London established to rebuild relationships between social classes and improve the lives of workers. In 1888 Ashbee helped to found the Guild and School of Handicraft, based in Londons East End (this section of the city was associated with high-volume piecework that supplied the furniture industry and that was criticized by social reformers), employing as many as 50 workers. Ashbees interest in the preservation of craft traditions stemmed from his experience with materials and process as an artist and a belief that the applied arts provided the means for original, satisfying work: the education of the hand and eye are only fully achieved in the education of the individuality of the workman. Ashbees designs respect and explore the nature of handcrafted materials; his work possesses a striking originality in both its slender, attenuated shapes and its respect for the integration of ornament with construction as seen, for instance, in a music cabinet dating to c. 1895 (fig. 5.9). The cabinets rectangular form and joinery are simple and straightforward, as are the majority of its materials: oak rather than imported or exotic woods, and patinated wrought-iron pullers and hinges (though these are fastened to red leather backings often imported from Africa) and hammered-copper rectangular panels. As early as 1902, Ashbee relocated his guild to the Cotswolds region in rural Gloucestershire (the same general area as Morriss Kelmscott Manor), a move suggesting that the ideals of the Arts and Crafts movement were more compatible amid village rather than urban conditions; instead of posing a threat to the combined forces of capitalism, competition, and industrialization, the ideal of the craft guild became an escape from or antidote to modern alienation, directly for its practitioners and vicariously through those who shared their beliefs as patrons and clients. After meeting architect Frank Lloyd Wright on a trip to the United States in 1901 and following the economic failure of the Guild and School of Handicraft in 1908, Ashbee came to accept the role of machinery and to admire Wrights efforts to reconcile an elevated view of craft with machine production and social responsibility (see here). Alongside the Arts and Crafts communities was the Garden City Movement, initiated in England by the American-born reformer Ebenezer Howard (18501928). Howard sought to remedy the alienation and poor working-class living conditions that accompanied industrial development, envisioning communities of single-family dwellings connected by rail to larger industrial cities. Here residents could cultivate gardens and preserve the satisfaction of self-sufficient living, independence, and the joy of work that they believed existed in pre-industrial societies. The Garden City model provided for trees, neighborhood parks, and residential zoning that separated the office, retail shop, or factory from peaceful domestic life. In separating work from family, however, Howards model also limited the activities of middle-class women to the private sphere, where their primary role was to create a comforting home environment. Letchworth in Hertfordshire north of London was the first garden city, begun in 1903; its designers were the architects Barry Parker (18671947) and Raymond Unwin (18531941). Both were sympathetic to Arts and Crafts ideals, and in 1901 had published The Art of Building a Home, a series of lectures illustrated with wood engravings of interiors and plans for homes they had designed. A photograph of the living room of the Letchworth home known as Crabby Corner (19041905) shows a plain brick wall surrounding a fireplace with a hammered-copper hood, built-in benches and cabinets, and a woven rush-seated chair (at the right below the window) that closely resembles the ladderback chairs produced by Shaker communities and discussed below (see here). The built-in (fitted) furniture maximizes living space for the type of modest, smaller, middle-class home preferred by the architects (fig. 5.10). In their lectures, the architects praised the openness of Japanese interiors as they are depicted in colored woodblock prints, and stressed the ideals of honest living based upon need and convenience rather than luxury and display: Let us have in our houses, rooms where there shall be space to carry on the business of life freely and with pleasure, with furniture made for use; rooms ... plain, simple, and ungarnished if necessary, but honest. Letchworth interiors promote the pleasures of a wholesome family life, removed from the fast pace, distractions, materialism, and pretensions of urban life. Ambrose Heal (18721959) was a London-based furniture-maker whose use of oak and plain, restrained designs was sympathetic to the vision of Garden City planners and architects. Heals furniture used basic joinery (carpentry) techniques, but his attitude toward technology and the use of machines was pragmatic rather than antagonistic. He advocated an accommodation to factory (rather than workshop) production, emphasizing product rather than process; as a result, the manufacturer remained responsible for improving the lives of workers not by maintaining craft skills and a closer relationship between designing and making, but by providing affordable goods that defined and contributed to a modest but comfortable lifestyle. Heals approach to furniture in England has parallels in the United States (see here). It also relates to models for design education, at the time known generally as Industrial Art. Industrial arts education was a compromise, an attempt to preserve elements of the doctrine of meaningful and satisfying work while acknowledging the advantages of time-saving technology, rationalized production, and the promise they might hold for higher standards of living and the quality of life for middle- and working-class families. The shift from craft to industrial production was neither sudden nor total in the manufacture of consumer products, nor were the individual autonomy and creative freedom that united the fine and applied arts entirely sacrificed as new production technologies took root. Leaders of the various permutations of the Arts and Crafts movement devised strategies to empower and protect the value of meaningful work, while business leaders promoted the Industrial Arts in relation to civic responsibility and pride, providing outlets for the development of skills and promoting social activities outside the workplace, such as museums and libraries. While industrial workers were no longer artists in the way Ruskin and Morris may have envisioned, neither were they entirely deprived of agency in a modern culture of design. ## The Arts and Crafts Movement in the United States A number of social critics, writers, and artists were sympathetic to John Ruskins critique of industrialization and its negative effect upon the arts in the United States during the second half of the nineteenth century. In addition to the circulation of Arts and Crafts ideas in print, a display from Morris & Co. was on view at the 1876 Centennial Exhibition in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania. The influence of the Arts and Crafts movement may be seen in the establishment of craft societies and craft education in cities such as Boston, Syracuse, Cincinnati, and Chicago. Reaching a peak in the decades between 1890 and 1910, craft workshops, artisan communities, and journals addressed the challenges and threats posed by mass production, mechanization, and advertising. Gustav Stickley (18581942) is perhaps the best known of many American Arts and Crafts furniture manufacturers in the early twentieth century, in part because furniture bearing the Stickley name continues to be produced, and also because it has been featured in popular films such as A River Runs Through It (1992, directed by Robert Redford). Stickley learned furniture-making while serving as an apprentice at an uncles chair factory in Massachusetts. He became familiar with Arts and Crafts ideology through the writings of William Morris and the published designs of British architect M. H. Baillie Scott (18651945) that appeared in decorative arts journals on both sides of the Atlantic. In 1898 Stickley visited Britain, and in 1900 he founded the Craftsman Workshops near Syracuse, New York, with himself as master craftsman. He published The Craftsman journal beginning in 1901 to market his furniture along with essays that reinforced values of honest construction and the personal satisfaction of handicraft. Stickley hoped his designs would appeal strongly to the directness and common sense of the American people with furniture that was solid, used local woods such as oak and basic (joinery) techniques, avoiding carved decoration and time-consuming marquetry as unnecessary and impractical for modern living. The Craftsman included plans for building your own house, essays introducing readers to the writings of Ruskin and Morris, and designs for furniture, textiles, and other crafts to encourage readers to engage directly in handicraft, hoping to extend the principles established by Morris, in both the artistic and the socialistic sense, equating beauty with simplicity, individuality, and dignity of effect. Other journals, such as California-based The Bungalow illustrated interiors that embodied the Arts and Crafts ideology. Other self-contained craft communities exemplified the Arts and Crafts movement in the United States. Among the most successful was the Roycrofters, founded by Elbert Hubbard (18561915) in 1893 and located in a suburb of Buffalo, New York. The Roycroft workshops concentrated primarily upon the printing press, publishing editions of the founders periodical The Philistine and a series of small paperbound books entitled Little Journeys, as well as metalcraft, leather-making, and furniture resembling the Craftsman style. Some of the designers employed by Hubbard also worked independently for other manufacturers, but the Roycroft community was based upon Hubbards somewhat paternalistic concern with the physical, intellectual, and spiritual well-being of the workers. Like Stickley, he had visited Britain and was familiar with the writings of Ruskin and Morris as well as with Morriss Kelmscott Press. Early books of the Roycroft community were written by Hubbard but also contained verse by American poets such as Ralph Waldo Emerson (18031882) and Edgar Allan Poe (1809 1849). Among the most popular series from the Roycroft press was the Little Journeys, a series of short biographies of famous writers, teachers, and businessmen (the first volume was a biography of William Morris) in a small format. Hubbard used a heavy serifed typeface named Bookman, though with a taller x-height and shorter ascenders and descenders than Morriss Venetian-inspired Golden type (see here), and he included decorated title pages as well as hand-coloring for wood-engraved decorated borders and illustrations (fig. 5.13). The Little Journeys and other Roycroft publications attempted to reconcile the individuality and craftsmanship of the private press movement initiated by Morris with an effort to create a democratic design, if not for the masses, then at least for a broader middle class. As part of his commercial enterprise, Hubbard built a hotel on the premises of the Roycroft village as a retreat for businessmen seeking the therapeutic benefits of the rustic life. Hubbard, formerly a successful salesman for the Larkin Soap Company in Buffalo, was a zealous and tenacious reformer. Henry Chapman Mercer (18561930) founded the Moravian Pottery and Tile Works, located in Doylestown in southeastern Pennsylvania, around the turn of the century to produce molded ceramic tiles and mosaics for use primarily as architectural and mural decoration. Independently wealthy and well respected as an anthropologist and archeologist, Mercer lamented the decline and disappearance of local, pre-industrial crafts in Pennsylvania. To preserve this endangered legacy, he began collecting tools and artifacts from the area and eventually built a museum (more like a warehouse today) to house the objects. He built his private residence and the museum using reinforced concrete with himself as amateur architect and hiring local laborers to help with construction. At the same time he founded a tile workshop and produced tiles using plaster molds with designs based upon historic models primarily from the Middle Ages but also from a variety of other sources. Mercer experimented with a process of two-coat glazing that varied the finish of each tile, and though hand-operated machines were used for shaping the slabs and pressing the tiles, each had an individual character from glazing and the many stages of the process that involved handwork. Although Mercer may not have been directly acquainted with the British Arts and Crafts movement, his lifes work and writings are entirely sympathetic to the movements ideals. Perhaps the consummate do-it-yourselfer, Mercer stated that the value of the products of the Moravian Pottery and Tile Works lay beyond the beauty of the colors and designs and had more to do with the dignity and usefulness of work. Perhaps the most ambitious commission of the Moravian Pottery and Tile Works was for the floors of the State Capitol Building in Harrisburg, Pennsylvania, completed between 1902 and 1907. Even if they were only moderately successful or long-lived, Arts and Crafts workshops, communities, and publications in the United States offered solutions to the dehumanizing aspects of urbanization and industrialization, to the threat that modernity posed to individuality, self-sufficiency, and the meaning and value of work. American Decorative Art 1900 Foundation. ## Printing in the United States For his Kelmscott Press (see here), William Morris drew typefaces based upon earlier approaches to type design that he admired for their even stroke weight and the retention of calligraphic tendencies. His efforts, in effect, gave new life to old faces, reviving interest in the Blackletter forms of the Middle Ages and suggesting variations to the thickness of letterform strokes based upon Roman old-style faces of the later fifteenth century. In the United States, two typographer-book designers based their work upon the example of the Kelmscott fonts, and variations upon it that became widely used in more commercial printing. Frederic Goudy (18651947) and Bruce Rogers (18701956) admired the beauty of Kelmscott books, which they encountered as young men, and embarked upon lifelong careers in type founding and book design. Their interests included type and page design, and their careers testify to a respect for craftsmanship practiced in the broadest sense of the word as celebrated by Morris. The model set by Goudy and Rogers extended to others including William Dwiggins (18801956) and Morris Fuller Benton (18721948), both of whom developed influential typefaces used in modern book printing. Dwiggins is sometimes credited with coining the term graphic designer to describe the varied professional activities that stemmed from craft origins in the printing industry but expanded to include typography, advertising, and page design. Each of these designers studied historical typefaces and created their own types suitable for text and titles. In addition to old-style (humanistic) faces by Caslon, Rogers produced a version of the Bodoni font for modern typesetting. Benton was employed as a punch-cutter for the American Type Founders Company (ATF), where he cut the type for Goudy fonts as well as developing his own typefaces. As noted by typographic historian Alexander Lawson, small variations in these historically inspired typefaces led to their wider use: by adjusting the height of ascenders and descenders, old-style typefaces could be more economically set without decreasing legibility. Will Bradley, whose advertising posters and illustrations are described below (see here), was also employed at ATF, which published The American Chap-Book. Along with other publications such as Inland Printer, The American Chap-Book introduced examples of printing that emphasized new typefaces, lettering, and inks used for chromolithography. Technology contributed to the development of the printing industry in the later nineteenth century, mainly through labor-saving machines that increased speed and productivity. Among the most significant changes was the introduction of automatic mechanical typesetting. As in the first half of the century, it was the newspaper industry, with its high-volume circulation and daily editions, that stimulated invention, leading to a system known as Linotype at the New York Herald in 1886, and quickly spreading through Europe and the United States. Ottmar Mergenthaler (18541899), who immigrated to the United States from Germany, invented the Linotype machine, which released negative impressions of letterforms through the operation of a keyboard, then cast each line of type from molten metal for the letterpress, eliminating the need for hand-setting type. As a result of this invention, the price of newspapers decreased and the number of pages grew, further increasing circulation and space for advertisements. The Monotype machine, developed by Tolbert Lanston (18441913) in the 1880s and 1890s, offered an alternative to the Linotype. It produced multiple impressions of letters from a single matrix containing the entire alphabet, operated by a perforated tape that positioned the matrix to make molds for casting (the perforated tape resembled the cards used for Jacquard looms; see here). While developed for the newspaper industry, typesetting machinery such as the Monotype was also adopted for book printing. In both cases typographers wrestled with the challenges of maintaining consistency and legibility while adopting new and increasingly complex mechanized technologies. Achieving that balance led to interest in the humanist roman letterforms of the later fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. This development was, in some sense, a reconciliation between respect for the highest achievements of craft traditions dating to the development of movable type and the inevitable push toward mechanization as the printing industry and the reading public expanded. Another invention during this period was the pantograph (delineating machine), developed by Linn Boyd Benton (18441932), who worked at ATF. The pantograph was a mechanical device that facilitated the drawing of variations of a single typeface to be produced as punches from a single exemplar, both expanding the range of a font (using extended and narrowed letterforms) as well as its point size with greater precision and speed. Another technical development in printing was the mechanical reproduction of images using photographs. Translating a photographic plate into a mechanically produced image required the use of a screen to reproduce the tonal complexity of the image in a simpler form. Known as half-tone, the process made photography the basic tool of reporting and recording events for newspapers and magazines, and at the same time threatened the jobs of those craftsmen who had met the expanding demand for images using the technique of wood engraving (fig. 5.19). The simplified shadows of the half-tone method of reproduction was adopted by graphic designers in the illustration of products in a larger format, for instance in the posters of Lucian Bernhard in Germany (see fig. 7.6). ![](images/6d10c92796ac89ef03289310bb7ef5d3f97c8c1a9688c84daac010fbeb4261f8.jpg) Enlarged photographic reproduction using half-tone printing process. Photographic reproduction was also used in the field of illustration. While journalism shifted to photography as an eyewitness to faithfully record events, illustration still thrived in the realm of fiction, political and social commentary in the mass media, advertising, and in childrens books, carrying on a tradition of narrative art outside of easel painting, where this tradition was challenged by avant-garde ideas advocating the autonomy of art. Scribner’s Monthly, Harper’s Magazine, Century, and other publications were among the journals that provided a sphere of activity for illustrators. Howard Pyle (18531911) was one of several artists who worked at Harper Brothers under art editor Howard Parsons, providing illustrations to stories that were published serially. Early illustrations were wood engravings. Pyle brought to life stories of danger and adventure, taking readers to distant lands and places peopled by heroes and villains. Pyle lent immediacy to his scenes, often juxtaposing near and distant space and using strong contrast to heighten dramatic effect. An example is a tonal reproduction from Pyles Book of Pirates, originally published in Harper’s Magazine (August 1887), illustrating a shipwrecked sailor on a beach against a backdrop of empty sea and sky (fig. 5.20). The appeal of Pyles illustrations was to escape and fantasy. The gap they filled in the lives of their vast readership was soon shared with a new medium in the early twentieth century: the moving picture, which introduced a set of real-life heroes and heroines in the form of celebrity actors whose portraits adorned the covers of fan magazines and whose offscreen lives, expected to match their on-screen personas, filled the pages of newspapers. A new demand for narrative also arose during the same time through advertising, although not all illustrators were comfortable with the use of illustration techniques to sell products. To some degree a middle ground was achieved in the late nineteenthcentury advertising poster, which managed to appeal to audiences on different levels, that is, promoting the individual expression of the artist, fostering a healthy relationship between art and business to bring high culture to the masses, and generating appeal by linking products and services to a carefree or hedonistic lifestyle (see here onward). ![](images/30877e8145be71d6a1c5a96e7dfafd13e3663fc540e699f8e5ad2bde4f68810c.jpg) ## 5.20 Howard Pyle, On the Tortugas, wood engraving from original illustration for “Buccaneers and Marooners of the Spanish Main,” Harper’s Magazine, August 1887. ## Chicago and Frank Lloyd Wright In 1896 and again in 1901 the British Arts and Crafts designer and advocate Charles Ashbee (see here), founder of the Guild and School of Handicraft, visited the United States, traveling extensively and reporting on the contemporary design activities that he observed. Ashbee, who was a firm believer in hand craftsmanship, the well-being of workers, and a close relationship between designer and artisan, was impressed by a young American architect named Frank Lloyd Wright (18691959). Wright was one of a group of Midwestern architects and designers who created a Prairie School based in Chicago, Illinois. Many of Wrights early houses share features with the Arts and Crafts movement in the United States in their effort to harmonize with their surroundings through low-hipped roofs and horizontal extension, overhanging eaves, a restrained use of ornament, and an avoidance of overt historicism. Significantly, however, Wrights approach to the decorative arts departed from an important tenet of Arts and Crafts ideology, particularly in regard to the role of the machine, and by extension the relationship between designing and making. In a wellknown lecture delivered in Chicago in 1901 at Hull House, a home founded in the city in 1889 by Jane Addams (18601935) and Ellen Gates Starr (18591940) to improve the lives of working-class women by providing a variety of social and educational services, Wright told his audience that the dream for a democratic architecture could only be achieved by embracing the machine, which saved time and reduced drudgery. He stated that the machines ability to produce simple products of high quality and noble beauty had been obscured only by the greed of manufacturers and the ignorance of the public: William Morris pleaded well for simplicity as the basis of all the art. Let us understand the significance to art of that word  SIMPLICITY  for it is vital to the art of the machine! It is not clear what Morris himself may have meant by simplicity, whether a less complicated lifestyle or aesthetically simple forms of furniture and furnishings. In regard to the machine, perhaps Wright was recalling the British designers comment, quoted earlier, that the more mechanical the process, the less direct should be the imitation of natural forms. In any case, as mentioned above, Charles Ashbee modified his own views to acknowledge a role for the machine along the lines envisioned by Wright, admiring the American architects courage in confronting the challenge of industrialization rather than ignoring its possible social benefits in improving the lives of a broader public. Wrights linking of machine technology, aesthetic purity, and a democratic art provided an ethical dimension to modern design that emerged in the interwar period in Europe, although under different historical conditions and often along utopian lines. Wrights views and designs also ran parallel with initiatives in Germany to shape middleclass taste in relation to industrial manufacturing that also took place in the early twentieth century (see Chapter 7). For these designers, decoration was viewed as being dishonest and vulgar if produced mechanically (a variation on the dictum truth to materials), and exclusive or elitist if made by hand: the modern designer should strive rather to create new and original forms designed for factory production to meet the needs and accompany the practical lifestyle of an expanding middle-class audience. Wrights approach to design using restraint in decoration might also be examined in connection with critiques of consumption by contemporary economists such as Thorsten Veblen (18571929), who criticized the purchasing habits of middle-class Americans as a form of conspicuous consumption, linking buying not with comfort but rather with the acquisition of social position that substituted the status acquired through buying with real status, formerly the result of honor and respect earned on the basis of actions and accomplishments. For Veblen, fashion and consumption were menacing threats to the existing social order; for Wright, they were threats to the creation of a true and natural modern taste, appropriate to its time and taking full advantage of new production technologies. Also contemporary with Wrights approach was the theory of Viennese architect Adolf Loos (18701933), who also criticized the role of decoration in design. Loos visited the United States in the 1890s and was familiar with the work and theories of the Prairie School. He equated decoration with cultural decline, arguing that modern urban life demanded a sophisticated but unpretentious style of living, in dress (where he favored the uniformly tailored black business suit worn by English gentlemen) as well as in architecture, where he advocated the use of modern materials such as reinforced concrete, flat roofs, and smooth walls using richly grained woods or marble but without carved, molded, or printed decoration. He expounded his views in a provocative essay titled Ornament and Crime dating to 19081910 and reprinted in later publications. Wright published some of his early designs for domestic architecture in illustrated mass-circulation magazines such as Edward W. Boks (18631930) Ladies’ Home Journal with the title A small house with lots of room in it and a suggested cost of less than \$6,000. But despite the simple elegance of Wrights designs in comparison with the more traditional preference for carved or molded ornament and adaptations of period styles ranging from Gothic to Baroque, Wrights homes were almost invariably custombuilt for affluent clients, and his domestic furniture was neither mass-produced nor achieved broad appeal for consumers, who preferred more traditional expressions of comfort and beauty linked to richness of effect. Even the design published by Wright in the Ladies’ Home Journal may be misleading for a modern reader, since a \$6,000 home in 1901 would correspond more to the median than to low-end price of houses today. Another expression of Wrights preference for simple (but not necessarily inexpensive) forms was the design of colored leaded glass windows, as in the set of bay windows for the dining space of the Susan Dana House in Springfield, Illinois (completed 1904; fig. 5.21). The windows are abstract geometric generalizations of foliate forms. The composition of these and other windows in Wrights Prairie houses suggests they might be assembled from a small number of prefabricated units or shapes (like childrens blocks), but their manufacture was still time-consuming, intricate, and ultimately craft-based. In other words, Wright seems to have created the converse of Morriss recommendation that machines should not imitate hand-work  the windows are handmade yet suggest how machines should make things. Despite the arguments presented in his writings and speeches that the Machine is the great forerunner of democracy, Wright was hardly advocating democracy in the sense of freedom of choice for consumers. Instead he equated an aesthetic preference based upon simplicity with a self-evident but entirely theoretical technical determinism, then assumed that the result shaped and elevated the taste of his intended middle-class audience, that is, an original modern design that the public should want. The concept of design unity not only applied to the open rather than enclosed interior spaces, but also stimulated Wright to design ceramics, metalwork, windows, and lamps, all of which contributed to the warmth and aesthetic harmony of the home, and also to the interrelationship between architecture and furnishings. ![](images/102d7723f8f6a39e83b3f753b8154acdbf566974fe5f781b26569c8da3b5e965.jpg) Frank Lloyd Wright, dining room furniture, Susan Dana House, Springfield, Illinois, completed 1904. As noted above, Wrights career in Chicago should not be considered in a vacuum, for the city was a major metropolitan center, and his ideas took shape in an active artistic environment. This view was based upon an appreciation for nature and the spiritual energy embodied in natural forms as they were integrated into and contributed to the value of the built environment. He designed intricate, original patterns for architectural and interior decoration, used as framed panels (whether stencil-printed, molded in terracotta, or cast in iron) to balance and enliven the repetitive grid-like structure of tall buildings such as the Wainwright Building in St. Louis, Missouri, dating to 1890 1891. William Morriss approach to socialism was avowedly anti-industrial, but efforts to connect design with social justice and equality resonated in a variety of ways and extended beyond nostalgic anachronism. The originality of Dressers designs and patterns also characterizes the work of artists and architects for whom the equality of the arts emerged as a provocative theme pointing to new directions and potential for the history of modern design. The term Aesthetic Movement is often used to refer to these activities, which were labeled as such during the 1870s and 1880s and even satirized in the British periodical Punch and in forms of popular entertainment such as Gilbert and Sullivans 1881 operetta Patience. The movement succeeded in shaping the attitudes of artists, manufacturers, and wealthy consumers toward the decorative arts and in elevating their status. The Aesthetic Movement may be seen as an outgrowth of early design reform while at the same time moving from a preoccupation with standards and paternalism toward a reconsideration of the sources of inspiration, materials, processes, and in particular the importance of individual subjective expression. Designers associated with the movement adopted a freer approach to the use of historical styles, and exhibited greater freedom in their exploration of materials and decoration. They advocated for the essential equality and unity of fine and applied art, in particular as realized in the coordinated interior, in which every element contributed equally to the unified expressive or emotional effect of the whole. Interiors became total works of art, imaginative spaces in which the barriers between art and life ceased to exist. While important aesthetic designers of the period might specialize in a medium such as glass, ceramics, or furniture, many designers and high-end manufacturers sought responsibility and control for all aspects of interior planning and decoration in order to achieve a coordinated and unified result that included fabrics, lighting, wall treatments, furniture, and furnishings. The centers of Aesthetic Movement activities were the growing modern cities of Britain, Eastern and Western Europe, Scandinavia, and North America, and colonial territories such as Australia. These urban centers were also the locations of the numerous Worlds Fairs that took place at the time, including London (1862), Vienna (1873), Paris (1867, 1878, 1889, and 1900), Brussels (1897), Turin (1902), and the United States (Philadelphia in 1876, Chicago in 1893). Aesthetic design was also illustrated in journals and commissioned for boutiques specializing in contemporary crafts and interior design as well as for restaurants, tearooms, department stores and other public spaces. Inspiration for Aesthetic Movement designers was far-ranging geographically and chronologically, drawing upon past Indigenous, as well as recent, national histories, and the decorative arts of non-Western cultures. Irish-born writer and critic Oscar Wilde (18541900), a vocal proponent of the decorative arts, articulated the attitudes that informed the creation of such exotic and personal interior spaces as the Peacock Room. In a passage from his essay The Artist as Critic (1891), Wilde praised the expressive value of abstract form in the applied arts and acknowledged their elevated status: The art that is frankly decorative is the art to live with. It is, of all visible arts, the one art that creates in us both mood and temperament. Mere colour, unspoiled by meaning, and unallied with definite form, can speak to the soul in a thousand different ways. The harmony that resides in the delicate proportions of lines and masses becomes mirrored in the mind ... The marvels of design stir the imagination ... By its deliberate rejection of Nature as the ideal of beauty, as well as of the imitative method of the ordinary painter, decorative art not merely prepares the soul for the reception of true imaginative work, but develops in it that sense of form which is the basis of creative no less than of critical achievement. Dressers comments on the design for a pattern from his 1862 book The Art of Decorative Design, although written in more analytical prose, share the same spirit as Wildes essay: both statements claim that design ennobles the ordinary, but through expressive rather than representational form: I have sought to embody chiefly the idea of power, energy, force, or vigour; and in order to do this I have employed such lines as we see in the bursting buds of spring, when the energy of growth is at its maximum ... Dresser traveled to Japan (his visit was partially underwritten by Charles Tiffany in New York, who asked the designer to return with examples of Japanese metalwork) and lectured widely on the art of that country, praising both the quality and character of Japanese craft as well as noting the ritual purposes different types of household objects served for individuals and families. After the re-opening of trade with Japan in 1854, artistic and commercial exchange was stimulated by objects brought to Britain by its first official representative, Sir Rutherford Alcock (18091897), displayed at a Japanese Court for the London International Exhibition of 1862, and by the acquisition of a collection of Japanese objects by the South Kensington Museum in the early 1870s. In 1875, collector and merchant Arthur Lazenby Liberty (18431917) opened his London shop featuring Japanese craft, while popular interest in Japan emerged with the success of the Gilbert and Sullivan operetta The Mikado (1885). Such activities influenced attitudes toward design and the decorative arts in Europe and in the United States. Japan offered collectors and European artists and designers a highly developed tradition that valued the decorative arts, their role in domestic life, and the creativity of the artisan. In his book The Traditional Arts and Crafts of Japan (1882), Dresser wrote extensively on the practice of Japanese craft, admiring the strength of Japanese craft traditions. The combination of skill with intelligence Dresser noted reinforced his interest in promoting the professional and creative status of designers: I cannot help thinking that the Japanese are right in regarding the man who can make a beautiful pot, a lovely cabinet, a charming fabric, or a perfect netsuki as a being superior to the mere buyer and seller of goods; for while the one denotes his best energies to mere money-making, the other ennobles matter by the impress of his mind, love, intelligence, and skill. It was, at least in part, the high regard for Japanese decorative arts that allowed artists and artisans greater freedom in drawing, permitting them, in the words of art historian Ernst Gombrich, to break the rules, for instance in the uneven spacing of motifs applied to surface decoration or in the irregular forms and textures of ceramic vessels. But the Japanese connection was not a one-way street: just as Dresser was visiting Japan to profit from first-hand knowledge and experience of Japanese decorative arts, so were Japanese artists and government officials traveling to Europe to purchase industrial equipment to modernize the production of silk and ceramics for the export market, and acquire technology and foreign investment in the development of transportation and infrastructure in Japan. These changes were parallel with the installation of the new centralized government beginning in 1868 (Meiji) and the need to replace traditional forms of regional patronage in the arts with commerce in domestic and international markets. Worlds Fairs were one means the Japanese government used to display the nations silk, ceramics, and metalwork, along with establishing technical schools for the training of designers along European models. The appearance of Western merchants and other businessmen and travelers occurred in Japan with the end of a long period of isolationism, providing an opportunity for Japan to communicate on equal terms with the Western world by demonstrating the international appeal of their higher-end material products in porcelain and metalwork, profits from the sale of which might be invested in modern industrial equipment. Their success may be demonstrated by recalling that Dressers Japanese excursion was sponsored by Charles Tiffany, whose New York jewelry company modeled some of its work on examples of Japanese precious metalwork. Thus the phenomenon known as Japonisme, referring to the popularity of Japanese products in Europe and North America, was the result not only of its reception in the context of the Aesthetic Movement that championed the equality of the arts, but also of the efforts of the Japanese government to stimulate economic growth through foreign trade and technology transfer, and creating a modern, competitive, and independent national image. Japan figured prominently in the history of the Aesthetic Movement in Europe and the United States as a source of inspiration for designers and artists as well as providing an example of sophistication and for the elevated place of the decorative arts in visual culture. Japan also offers an exception to the theme of Orientalism that applies so often to Western views of non-Western cultures. Protective of its sovereignty and independence, the nation was receptive to industrialization and adapted industrial technologies and business practices to its own economy under the Meiji government and presented a progressive rather than primitive identity at its pavilion (called the Hôôden) for the 1893 Worlds Columbian Exposition in Chicago, combining traditional crafts and interior decoration with a series of paintings that catered to contemporary Western practices. Other Aesthetic Movement designers in England, including Charles Eastlake and Bruce Talbert (18381881), looked to their nations past, turning to the Gothic style as advocated by Pugin or to the solid forms of the seventeenth century in what is known as the Queen Anne revival, approaches that respected the harmony of utility and beauty advocated by the early design reformers. Eastlakes designs achieved popularity through their publication in his Hints on Household Taste (1868, see here), but while Eastlakes standards in design were linked to reform and education, his concerns were more aesthetic than ethical, and lacked the overt political or moral overtones of Cole or Pugin. Eastlake acted as an advisor in matters of taste and his advice was not free from cultural assumptions about the role of design in society. He directed his comments to female readers whom he presumed took responsibility for creating the ideal domestic environment, one that combined an appreciation for beauty with practical considerations such as a recommendation to avoid furniture with extensive carved decoration so as to reduce dust, require less time for cleaning, and promote healthier living. In this latter sense his and other tastemakers advice books share a concern for efficient and healthy living with American writer Catherine Beecher (18001878), whose relationship to modern design is also considered below (see here). Art Furniture (1882) was the title of a book published by English furniture-maker William Watt (18341885) that featured the work of Aesthetic Movement designer E.W. Godwin (18331886). Watt displayed his companys furniture at the 1878 Worlds Fair in Paris; figure 6.6 illustrates an interior with several prominent art furniture cabinets along with ceramics, window dressing, stenciled decoration on the upper walls, all coordinated stylistically and employing floral motifs whether carved, printed, woven, or painted. The cabinets in the illustration are rectilinear in overall construction but allow ample opportunity for decoration in the panels, using complex techniques of marquetry rather than carving. The illustration also implies a gendered reading: the man (presumably the husband) sits comfortably in an armchair reading a newspaper near the fireplace while the woman, standing to the right, carries a large bowl, perhaps to be placed in an empty center area of the nearby sideboard. Responsibility for creating and maintaining a comfortable, healthy, and aesthetically pleasing home added a creative dimension to household management, but nevertheless maintained the gendered division between domestic and political, the private and public spheres of activity. Out of such practices emerged the field of domestic science (or home economics) in the early twentieth century, providing professional opportunities for women outside the home. ![](images/23db539e2ede7696d9695b5c6c1feac29bf7f1d69326308023573b122b3898d9.jpg) William Watt, Art Furniture, plate no. 7, 1878, from designs by E. Recognizing increasingly commercial circumstances and opportunities in their profession, artists were eager to enter the market for decorative arts as designers and broaden the sphere of their activities. As figure 6.6 implies, aesthetic furniture was directed toward a wealthy clientele. Manufacturers of these products, such as Watt, Gillows, Holland and Sons, or Collinson & Lock in England, or Herter Brothers in New York, were willing to produce items from artists designs for display at international exhibitions in major European cities as well as in the United States, and art furniture was marketed as well through entrepreneurs and collectors such as Liberty in London, and later Siegfried Bing (18381905) and Julius Meier-Graefe (18671935) in Paris (see here, fig. 6.47). Another form of Aesthetic Movement marketing was the studio. Artists such as Whistler were known for the design of the interiors in their own homes and studios, at least in part to attract the attention of potential clients. Another well-known fine artist with an interest in the decorative arts was Lord Frederic Leighton (18301896), a painter who served as President of the Royal Academy of Art and was made a peer in 1896. Clearly there were shared, overlapping elements between the Arts and Crafts and Aesthetic movements; neither movement was unified, and some designers, such as de Morgan, Edward Burne-Jones (see chapter 5), and Walter Crane, seemed to move comfortably between the two. Proponents of both movements believed that the decorative arts were important as vehicles of aesthetic experience and as the products of meaningful labor. But there were differences in terms of emphasis. For Morris, artists with a social conscience could best direct their efforts by being craftsmen, where their products would best address the needs of a troubled society. For the Aesthetic designer, the decorative arts were the vehicle for subjective experience and expression. One might say that the Arts and Crafts movement was directed toward process and reducing the alienation between an artisan and the products of his or her labor caused by technology and competition in a capitalist system. In theory at least, manufacturers and buyers would support such an aim as an expression of social conscience and skepticism toward the facile equation of industrialization with progress. Efforts ranged from advocating for working-class revolution to creating alternative craft communities or making accommodations to the machine compensated by communal activity for workers outside the workplace. Artists of the Aesthetic Movement identified with and gave visible form to subjective experience through the decorative art object or product, often in relation to an aesthetic view of life most often realized in the interior, where all elements of design were united by abstract, expressive qualities of color, shape, line, and texture. ## Books, Illustration, and Type Aesthetic, expressive interests in printing emerged further in Britain with the monthly journal The Studio in 1893. In addition to examples of contemporary furniture and furnishings, The Studio included illustrations by artists with a highly subjective approach to representing nature and the human figure, parallel to contemporary Symbolist attitudes discussed above (see here). The first issue of The Studio featured illustrations from Oscar Wildes poem based upon the New Testament story of Salomé, published originally in French and designed by the young English artist Aubrey Beardsley (1872 1898). Beardsleys The Climax (fig. 6.9) represents Salomés embrace of the severed head of John the Baptist. The scene is divided into two zones, with Salomé floating in the upper half, the faint contour of her body barely visible against the white ground, while her black hair focuses viewer attention on her face as she stares at the head of the Baptist that she holds. Areas of strong contrast attract attention as well, whether the pools of blood in the lower half of the composition (and strange plants that issue from them) or the circular wave patterns in the upper-left corner, a motif that was employed by Whistler in his decoration of the cabinets in the Peacock Room (see fig. 6.5). Interest in childrens literature was strong among early design reformers such as Henry Cole, who saw these books as a means to cultivate taste and instill the values of work and moderation (see here and here). In the later nineteenth century, attitudes about childrens books changed to include a more innocent, playful, in short, a more childlike, less didactic point of view. In attempting to adopt the viewpoint of a child, illustrators entered into an imaginative state of mind that was akin to other work in the fine and decorative arts at this time. The embossed gold seal found on many well-known childrens books today is named for a pioneer in childrens illustration, English illustrator Randolph Caldecott (1846 1886). Caldecott was a self-taught artist who became a professional illustrator only after moving to London from Manchester in the early 1870s. He served as a freelance illustrator for popular weekly news journals in London, and several of his illustrations were reproduced in Harper’s New Monthly Magazine published in New York. Among his bestknown works is a series of books published by George Routledge from color wood engravings by Edmund Evans (18261905). The collaboration of illustrator and engraver is significant, for Evanss skills as an engraver preserved the simple, abbreviated quality of Caldecotts drawings, set off against broad unmodulated areas of lightly tinted color: Evanss efforts were creative rather than mechanical and contributed to Caldecotts success. Eliminating more conventional detail and cross-hatching used in the wood-engraving process, picture books such as Hey Diddle Diddle (1882; fig. 6.11) achieved wide popularity in both Britain and France. Caldecott also earned respect among noted designers such as Walter Crane and artists Van Gogh and Gauguin, both of whom owned copies of Caldecotts relatively inexpensive picture books. The recognition, in artistic as well as popular circles, of Caldecotts illustrations, along with the emergence of exhibitions and galleries devoted to print media, demonstrates the inter-relationship among the arts that animated both artists and the public during the later nineteenth century, and that contributed to respect for the emerging field of graphic design. ![](images/dc55cf1a20d6fc4c09dd9acf773d33586ea34e0d50d36af5c6152d92e908abd7.jpg) 6.11 Randolph Caldecott, illustration from Hey Diddle Diddle, wood engraving, 7 <sup>3</sup>/<sub>8</sub> × 8 <sup>7</sup>/<sub>8</sub> in (18.8 × 22.4 cm), published by George Routledge & Sons, London, 1882. Contemporary with Caldecotts picture books were those of another British artist, Kate Greenaway (18461901). Greenaways father was a freelance wood engraver who had worked with Edmund Evans for the publisher of the satirical illustrated journal Punch, and Evans was also responsible for the engraving and color printing of Greenaways drawings. Greenaway received training at the government-sponsored design schools established around the middle of the century that prepared designers for industry, and Ruskin praised her work for its idyllic recreation of natural beauty in a preindustrial era. Her simplified drawing and frequent use of blank space reveal an affinity to the graphic tendencies of Aesthetic design, and as a woman her creative energies found an outlet in the applied arts rather than in the male-dominated fine arts at this time. Greenaways first popular success was an illustrated book published by George Routledge in 1879 entitled Under the Window. A frontispiece for Marigold Garden; Pictures and Rhymes (first published 1885; fig. 6.12) features high-waisted dresses that recall the simpler fashions of the late eighteenth and earlier nineteenth centuries and a type of idealized childs face (it has been suggested that the artist derived the type from her collection of dolls). Greenaway also used bands of flowers to frame the illustration and serve as a motif to suggest a landscape setting. In this case a garden twists around the playful composition of figures in various poses dancing in a circle. This component in her illustrations grew out of her own sketches from nature as well as the conventionalized approach to drawing she learned at the schools of design and adapted to the genre of childrens illustration. Print technology played an instrumental role in the success of childrens illustration. The illustrations of Greenaway, Caldecott, and Walter Crane were reproduced from these artists original drawings rendered on wood for engraving, and, in the case of Greenaway, translated later onto stone for chromolithographic reproduction as greeting cards for a mass audience. Black-and-white illustrations for more timely publications such as newspapers and weekly magazines used specialized labor to quickly translate drawings into wood, and by the 1880s photomechanical methods of reproducing images were beginning to compete with and eventually eclipse both relief and chromolithographic techniques for the reproduction of images. At first, photographs of original drawings were printed on wood for hand-engraving, eliminating either the artists or engravers role at this stage of production. A more significant development was the use of acid to engrave a wood or metal block printed with a photographic image, eliminating the need for any handwork at all except in the finishing stages. Direct methods of printing involving artists and craftsmen persisted, however, in the genre of book illustration, and flourished in poster design, and in more editorial forms of imagery for political cartoons and caricatures in newspapers and magazines. ## The Aesthetic Movement in the United States In the United States, numerous displays of Aesthetic design as well as a Japanese Pavilion were included at the 1876 Centennial Exhibition in Philadelphia. Five years later, Oscar Wilde made a lecture tour of major American cities where he promoted new attitudes toward the decorative arts. Institutions such as the Metropolitan Museum of Art began to add examples of contemporary furniture and other decorative objects for the home to their collections in an effort to inform and elevate public taste, and schools of design and societies promoting the study and practice of the decorative arts thrived in a number of American cities. In the last quarter of the nineteenth century, American artists turned to the design and manufacture of stained glass and metalwork, achieving considerable success at home and exposure abroad through displays and prizes at Worlds Fairs. In the field of stained glass especially, American painters John La Farge (18351910) and Louis Comfort Tiffany (18481933) received numerous commissions for their work  mostly from private clients but also from public institutions. Both men studied painting in Paris before turning to the decorative arts (La Farge practiced law before becoming an artist). The elevated status of the decorative arts as well as commissions for all aspects of interior design influenced Tiffany, who wrote, I believe there is more in it [i.e. the decorative arts] than in painting pictures. After establishing his own company for interior decoration in 1879, Tiffany became artistic director of the company founded by his father, Charles Tiffany, which manufactured jewelry and virtu in silver and other precious metals. Both Tiffany and La Farge experimented with new materials and techniques to achieve subtle coloristic and textural effects in their works: Tiffany introducing Favrile glass in 1892 for bowls and vases, and La Farge developing opalescent glass for windows. The varied colors and surfaces of La Farges opalescent glass enriched the iridescence of his windows, producing a luminous effect that resembled the thick impasto of oil paint (fig. 6.13). Tiffanys windows achieve the same richness, but the designer also reached beyond unique commissions to produce vases and other household objects aimed at a wider market, including desktop utensils and table and standing lamps with cast bronze bases. In an electric lamp (fig. 6.14) manufactured between 1904 and 1915, the rust and yellow colors of stalks and leaves dominate a few remaining touches of pale green, suggesting an autumnal mood. This mood is echoed not only in the drooping curves of the stalks but also in the fallen leaves and petals casually strewn on the base. The subtle coloristic effects, close relationship between form, techniques of construction, and decoration, lack of reference to revival styles, as well as the mood and expressive unity of the whole, mark the high level of attention devoted to the decorative arts in the United States during this time and express the aspirations of the movement to achieve equality among the arts. Tiffany designed early versions of his lamps for oil, with the fuel stored in the base and a shade to provide ventilation for an open flame. Electric wiring for home lighting gained ground against gas and oil slowly, beginning in the 1880s; in 1907 8 percent of homes in the United States were wired for electricity, rising to 13 percent in 1912. Electricity for home lighting (and later for a wider range of appliances) created opportunities for designers and manufacturers, including modern aesthetic possibilities for Tiffany studio artists, and the company was awarded a grand prize for its lamps at an international exhibition of decorative arts in Turin in 1902 in competition with entries of electric table lamps and chandeliers from Britain, Belgium, and France. One outcome of efforts to elevate the status of the decorative arts in the later nineteenth century was to associate manufactured products with the names of individual designers, much as we associate works of fine art with individual artists. Such identification (or branding), however, ignores or undervalues the collaborative nature of the design enterprise. We freely label as Tiffany the work produced at his studio in Corona, Queens, New York, and yet the products of that workshop involved a large workforce of skilled artisans who were essential to Tiffanys success, many of whom were women. Clara Driscoll (18611944) not only oversaw a group of women who selected and assembled glass shades for electric lamps (the Glass and Cutting Department), but also designed several patterns for the lamps, including shades featuring dragonflies, peonies, and daffodils (narcissus). The influence of the Aesthetic Movement may also be seen in the highly individual approach to architecture by Philadelphia architect Frank Furness (18391912), who contributed furniture designs to a number of interiors for the buildings he designed. Art furniture is also often associated with the Herter Brothers (Gustave and Christian), German immigrants who established a successful business in New York City that provided comprehensive interior design services in period and original styles featuring the highest levels of skilled craftsmanship and expensive materials for the apartments and mansions of wealthy industrialists such as Jay Gould and William Vanderbilt. Herter Brothers regularly exhibited at Worlds Fairs, first with elaborate examples of carving in the Renaissance Revival style, often executed by German and Italian carvers who had emigrated to New York, and then shifting to refined marquetry characteristic of the Aesthetic Movement. The Tiffany Glass Company and other firms occasionally received important public commissions, but the focus of the Aesthetic Movement, both in Europe and in the United States, was the cultivation of the domestic interior as a setting for individual and family life. Rather than the pre-modern concept of household in which generations of parents and children lived or even worked under the same roof, the family home in the later nineteenth century cultivated a new ideal of comfort, intimacy, social status, and individual identity, providing refuge from the anonymity and uniformity of the workplace and opportunities for private, personal experience. Within this private sphere, women were encouraged to participate in an expanded range of crafts traditionally practiced in and associated with the home, including embroidery, woodcarving, and the decoration of pottery. Local decorative arts societies and schools organized exhibitions and offered classes aimed primarily at middle-class women, who might engage in pursuits that were becoming more appreciated as art, while at the same time maintaining their status and respectability in society and not threatening traditionally male-dominated professions. Household furnishings opened up a sphere of creative activity to women. Examples of Rookwood ceramics received medals at the 1889 Exposition Universelle in Paris and a number of them (fig. 6.19) show the assimilation of Japanese-inspired techniques in the use of irregular forms and asymmetrical arrangements of decorative motifs; indeed, the presence of Japanese ceramic artist Kataro Shirayamadani (1865 1948) at Rookwood beginning in 1887 had an impact on the companys production. Adelaide Alsop Robineau (18651929) also explored new directions in ceramics around the turn of the century. Robineau traveled freely throughout her career, working in Minnesota, St. Louis, Missouri, and later in Syracuse, New York, and became aware of aesthetic tendencies in the decorative arts through international exhibitions, particularly the Worlds Columbian Exposition in Chicago in 1893. Her Viking Ship vase from 1905 (fig. 6.20) combines carved relief decoration with smoother wheel-thrown shapes and fascination with the irregularities of surface and overlapping glazes to suggest a wind-tossed sailboat in a turbulent, vast, and forbidding sea. Robineaus work explored the expressive possibilities of her medium in original directions more commonly associated with the arts of sculpture and painting. Despite traditional gender-based limitations, womens achievements in the decorative arts were considerable and their participation more common than in the fine arts. Notwithstanding the emphasis upon the equality of the arts in the Aesthetic Movement, women still moved less easily into the fine arts, and even painters such as Mary Cassatt (18441926), who studied in Philadelphia before settling in Paris in 1874, tended to be confined both in their choice of subject matter and of medium. Cassatt, for instance, worked extensively in pastel, which was thought (by men) to be a less demanding and more appropriate medium for women artists than oil paint. Some women rebelled against the restrictions implied by their traditionally domestic role. Gaining momentum in the early twentieth century, a group of early material feminists looked to apply modern production technology toward liberating women from the drudgery of household work. While books such as Catherine Beechers 1869 The American Woman’s Home advocated a scientific approach to household management  in short a domestic economy or science that would enable women to organize household work more efficiently and provide opportunities for more creative and intellectual pursuits  they did not question the underlying assumption that a womans place was in the single-family home. Other feminist writers and activists felt that a socialized approach to household management, where facilities for laundry, cooking, and childcare were maintained communally rather than within the nuclear family unit, promised greater freedom for women and could marshal technologys time-saving benefits more efficiently. Some feminists saw potential in machinery for institutional dishwashing and laundry services that might be constructed in separate facilities rather than in the home, while others felt that communal facilities for childcare would facilitate womens entry into the workforce. Some of these ideas reached fruition in settlement houses and apartments that redefined a more communal approach to modern living in response to industrialization and urbanization in the later nineteenth century. Such facilities, often open, spacious, and with a factory-like functionality, were meant to replace private kitchens in houses with efficiently designed areas where working women would be able to pick up prepared meals for their families. Such experiments reveal alternatives to the traditional nuclear family and the private home that are part of the context for both the Arts and Crafts and the Aesthetic movements. In focusing upon the decorative arts, both movements recognized and even encouraged the creative participation of women, yet at the same time often reinforced the identification of woman within the domestic sphere. On the other hand, material feminists sought a more radical gender equality based not upon the elevation of craft to fine art but rather upon a shared, communal approach to modern living that questioned the primacy of home and family. In the period between the twentieth centurys two world wars, designs for social housing with communal services also emerged as radical alternatives to the family unit along socialist lines (see here and fig. 9.39). ## Dress Attitudes toward women in the later nineteenth century may also be gleaned through the study of dress and fashion. Another indication of the wider diffusion of ideas about the equality of the arts and the new, elevated status of the decorative arts is the emergence of the high-fashion or haute-couture industry in Paris in the second half of the nineteenth century. The profession of fashion designer is almost synonymous with Charles Frederick Worth (18251895), who worked in London in the textile trade before moving to Paris with his wife and securing commissions for garments from the Spanishborn Empress Eugénie (18261920) as well as other wealthy clients beginning in the 1850s. Worth created the ideal image for aristocratic women in the later nineteenth century  in an age when some fashionable ladies rarely, if ever, wore the same garment twice, and when weekend parties might require several changes of clothing in a single day. Worths wider success in Parisian society would hardly have been possible without industrial technology, in particular the sewing machine, which saved considerable time in constructing elaborate garments through the work of the many seamstresses he employed. Worth also introduced more comfortable surroundings for shopping, invented fall and spring lines to stimulate consumer interest, and designed his clothing in closer relation to the human body, reducing if not eliminating some of the confining undergarments that characterized earlier formal dress. Certainly Worths success grew from an emerging aesthetic interest in and appreciation for the varied textural and coloristic effects of fabrics, as seen for instance in the portraits of French artist Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres (17801867), whose painstaking technique captured the nuances of light falling on the folds of the dress of Madame Moitessier in a portrait from 1856 (fig. 6.21), and whose skin has the appearance of unblemished porcelain. Curiously, as interest in womens fashion grew in the mid-nineteenth century, fashions for men became both more practical and more standardized in the general adoption of trouser suits and a preference for black. While men and women competed for individual distinction in dress through the early nineteenth century, the increasing emphasis upon womens fashion in the later part of the century provides telling evidence of gender discrimination: womens preoccupation with fashion signaled they had no need to work; indeed to do so would be to bring into question their husbands ability to support them. Furthermore, married women were not encouraged to work, and the elaboration and complexity of much womens fashion reinforced inactivity. ![](images/4925a83d27698ca18a46aab7815125904a28ae7c00c45501170378077d3df1b3.jpg) 6.21 Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres, Madame Moitessier, oil on canvas, 47 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>4</sub> × 36 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>3</sub> in (120 × 92.1 cm), 1856. Worths place in the emerging high-fashion industry stems from his sovereignty as an artist-designer, creating an individual look for each patron, reflecting the patrons good taste and the designers creativity and originality. The Hermitage Museum, St. Petersburg. ## The Aesthetic Movement and Shopping While the products of the Aesthetic Movement were often made to order or sold in small shops and boutiques for an exclusive clientele, the newly established department stores benefitted from the movement in creating a unified approach to interior design as a worthwhile investment in marketing to attract the attention of a wider range of consumers. The emergence of the department store in the 1860s revolutionized the experience of shopping for the middle class in Europe and in the United States. These large stores, with a variety of goods sold under the same roof, provided a permanent setting for the spectacle of consumption as experienced in the displays at international exhibitions. Department stores such as the Bon Marché in Paris and John Wanamaker in Philadelphia endeavored to turn shopping into a pleasurable and transformative experience that encouraged shoppers to identify consumption with the fulfillment of an idealized dream of comfort, luxury, self-improvement, and self-realization. Aesthetic design, in elevating goods above utility and function, played an integral role in this commercial expansion. Department stores housed displays for furniture, fabrics and other home furnishings, as well as foods, all ordered in large quantities and sold at fixed prices (rather than by bartering between customer and vendor) with specials and seasonal sales to entice buyers, along with snack bars and even reading rooms. Their success depended upon quantity production of basic types of goods differentiated by a variety of styles of decoration, whether dresses with applied trims of different patterns of lace, or cabinets ordered from manufacturers with decoration in a variety of historical period styles that had begun to emerge around the middle of the century. As an example, see figure 6.23: two plates from a book of furniture designs published in England by furniture manufacturer Henry Whitaker in 1847. The illustrations reveal the strong similarity among the sideboards, differentiated only by carved decoration in the Elizabethan and Italian styles. While perhaps a far cry from the bespoke furniture that aspired to be art by Herter Brothers and other high-end manufacturers, such furniture rose above utility to some higher level of meaning, signifying material progress and discriminating taste for the new middle-class retail market, evidence of art applied to industry, higher levels of increasing production, employment, and channels of distribution. ![](images/1f63c830f0c3b3ecbf5a0a9226ad96d4685c0db706b43a6abff12a4af7468976.jpg) ![](images/3f4ef384141b6260541e7150df3a591dc6ba886fcb6c6b02d164c709d23d51bd.jpg) Henry Whitaker, two illustrations of sideboards in Elizabethan and Italian styles, from the furniture catalog Practica Cabinet Maker’s Treasure, London, 1847. Advice books such as Eastlakes Hints on Household Taste and others demonstrate the close relationship between women, shopping, and individual identity. Rather than emphasizing the making of objects, in terms of labor, commerce, or design, the focus of this expansion of shopping rested upon associations between buyers and objects, as projections or extensions of their individuality. By the end of the century, shoppers visiting department stores such as Harrods in Knightsbridge, London (only a short walk from the Victoria and Albert Museum), were treated to colorfully designed and brightly lit interiors with walls and ceilings covered with enamel-like patterned glazed tiles: an example is Harrods Meat Hall, dating to 1902 (fig. 6.24). In addition to commercial activity, department stores offered free concerts and recitals that provided elevated forms of public entertainment  another demonstration of the link between design and generalized individual and social progress in an emerging consumer society. An example is the Wanamaker Organ, displayed at the 1904 Worlds Fair in St. Louis and installed in the city of Philadelphias Wanamaker department store in 1911 for business-hour as well as weekend afternoon family concerts. ![](images/cdca1562b547f3af112c4732ec76d5d489ae9e9d07958f338a9cd9dec738dbd4.jpg) Meat Hall with tiled walls, Harrods department store, London, 1902. Although the image of Art Nouveau brings to mind the swirling linear style associated with posters, furniture, and applied decoration in the 1890s in France, the term is perhaps best understood in the broader context of later nineteenth-century ideas about aesthetic design and the unity of the arts as they took root on French soil. As numerous Realist and Impressionist paintings of the period reveal, the 1860s and 1870s in Paris witnessed far-reaching changes in the experience of urban life, beginning with the construction of grand boulevards, bridges, monuments, piazzas, and public gardens under Napoleon III (r. 18521870) and civic planner Eugène-Georges Haussmann (18091891), and continuing with the building of the Paris Opéra and the emergence of venues for other middle-class forms of entertainment. There was significant commercial expansion of the city as well, for instance in the opening of the Bon Marché in 1869 and other department stores that soon followed. Middle-class leisure was viewed as a spectator sport, and writers coined the term flâneur to describe that type of gentleman who delighted in strolling the avenues, window shopping or meeting friends at a café. Painter Édouard Manet captured casual slices of this new life of strolling and coffee breaks in his Corner in a Café Concert (18789, fig. 6.26), with a barmaid serving mugs of beer to a crowd of mixed social class customers and a dancer (or singer) performing on stage in the background. International exhibitions of art and industry, the earlier ones modeled on the Crystal Palace, were also part of Parisian public life during the second half of the nineteenth century, being staged first in 1855, again in 1867, and repeated in 1878, 1889, and 1900. ![](images/eb410bce670772d489b91f0f0cedd28d2f75d09f7b0c9272e1053c066297ba6c.jpg) 6.26 Edouard Manet, Corner in a Café Concert, oil on canvas, 39 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>4</sub> × 30 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>2</sub> in (100 × 77.5 cm), 1878–1879. The National Gallery, London. The department stores need for larger quantities of furniture placed inordinate pressures upon the system of workshop production, forcing artisans to increase production while receiving the same or even lower wages, leading to standardization, a decline in quality, and deterioration in working conditions. In part as a response to these circumstances, a private organization of manufacturers and skilled artisans known as the Central Union of Fine Art as Applied to Industry was created as early as 1864 to promote high standards in design based upon the harmony of beauty and utility, standards inherited from the design reform movement in Britain (see here onward). The Central Union sponsored exhibitions and even created a library where artisans and manufacturers might consult drawings as models for production. The silver and gilt beer service (fig. 6.25) by Fannière Frères dating to 1865 embodies the aims of the Central Union at this time. Rather than references to historical styles, the ensemble depicts the planks of wooden kegs, bound with twisted rope and strewn with vegetation, exemplifying the fitness of decoration to purpose. Certainly the members of the Central Union took an interest in elevating the quality of the applied arts in France and in educating consumers and manufacturers, but increasingly the efforts of this organization stressed the renewal of the luxury crafts and the reputation they enjoyed during the eighteenth century, with specialized labor and the use of machine tools to maintain high levels of skill and refinement. After 1889 the organization dropped the word industrial from its title, and concentrated instead upon promoting the strength of the nations artisanal heritage. Reformers generally viewed industrial production with uniformity, poor quality, and unfavorable competition with other nations, and also as a betrayal of a significant national cultural legacy. Contemporary critics debated these issues, often commenting on the displays at international exhibitions. Some critics praised the high levels of technical skill and art as applied to manufacture, while others were critical of the absence of more practical and affordable products and furnishings. ![](images/30296ff2e8f088ad05a4afc157025e33d9eeed6324a75391cfacdc8ee8b21995.jpg) ## 6.25 Fannière Frères, beer service, silver with gilt, pitcher 9 × 5 in (23 × 12.5 cm), 1865. In addition to reviving appreciation for the high quality of eighteenth-century production, advocates for design reform in France also encouraged the combination of high levels of skill together with originality. German-born merchant Siegfried Bing contributed to the activities of the Central Union. Bing collected and sold examples of Japanese decorative arts in Paris, published a journal devoted to his vision of Japanese art and culture (Japon Artistique), and helped to mount exhibitions of Japanese crafts that he admired for their refinement and the craftsmens feeling for nature and for the nature of materials. Bing encouraged contemporary French designers to create new and original work, and visited the United States in 1893 where he saw and praised the work of Louis Comfort Tiffany in stained glass (see here). In 1895 Bing transformed his gallery of Japanese arts into coordinated ensembles of furniture and furnishings displayed in showrooms bearing the name La Maison de lArt Nouveau featuring Japanese crafts as well as contemporary interiors designed by Tiffany and Belgian artist and designer Henry van de Velde (18631957; see fig. 6.47). Despite the eclectic and international character of La Maison de lArt Nouveau, Bing felt that the work was unified by shared levels of refinement and quality, demonstrating the kind of modern renewal of the decorative arts that would inspire artists and artisans and appeal to a discriminating taste. Some critics, however, complained that La Maison de lArt Nouveau showrooms lacked a more obvious stylistic coherence; as a result, Bing realized the importance of appealing to a French audience with a more consistent and recognizably French style that revitalized the legacy of French preeminence in the decorative arts. In this climate of strong national feeling and international competition, a number of French designers emerged whose work demonstrated respect for and continuity with the high levels of craftsmanship in eighteenth-century luxury goods and furnishings, yet without directly imitating their forms. This pride in the national past merged with more modern concepts of artistic freedom and originality seen, for instance, in the works of French Symbolist artists, who saw the arts as the vehicle for subjective expression moving beyond the more optical style of Impressionism in the 1870s and the early interest of those painters in the leisure pastimes of the middle class. Such qualities may be seen in the glass objects designed and crafted by Émile Gallé (18461904), who trained and worked as a glassmaker in the city of Nancy in northeast France. Gallé experimented with color, light, and texture in the medium of glass, exploring effects beyond the traditional associations with the material. Many of his works are highly individual, more like relief sculptures in glass than utilitarian objects. In his vase of 1900 (fig. 6.27), flowers in shades of purple emerge in relief against a light-brown background. Gallé often used an array of colors to accentuate the effects of changing light while mottled textures hinted at reflections. Gallés heightened sensitivity to nature and obsessive manipulation of materials to obtain the most delicate effects of light are contemporary with the series of studies of the same subject by Claude Monet (18401926) as well as similar explorations of translucence and iridescence in glass by Tiffany and La Farge (or in silk by Candace Wheeler). Although less abstract, the feelings for growth and transformation in Gallés works anticipate the automatic techniques of Surrealist artists in the 1920s, and in fact both movements were influenced by a fascination with dreams and the unconscious. Gallés work shares with Symbolism an interest in private, psychological experience, and the primacy of feeling and mood over purely optical experience. Gallé was inspired by the Symbolist poets Charles Baudelaire (1821 1867) and Paul Verlaine (18441896), and phrases from their verse are sometimes etched directly in the glass surfaces of his vases. Such works achieve one of the aims of the later Central Union: to revitalize the arts by obliterating any boundary between them and restore the shared esteem in which French luxury products in several media were held in an increasingly competitive international market. ![](images/29fb0c522a11fd9c74a4b1f0991d47c72c5365da460ee9e5d1ea016772ed139a.jpg) Émile Gallé, Waterlilies, vase, multi-layered glass with filigree, glass inlay and carved details, 1900. In 1901, a group of like-minded artists established the Société des Artistes Décorateurs (S.A.D.) to protect the status of the applied arts against any encroachments by industry and to reinforce the unity and equality of the arts. During this period the decorative arts acquired associations with originality and expression, while the fine arts were recognized for their autonomy from the imitation of nature and an appreciation for their truth to materials or medium. Artists freely crossed the increasingly permeable border between fine art and applied design, among them Odilon Redon (18401916), Pierre Bonnard (18671947), Maurice Denis, and Édouard Vuillard (18681940). Auguste Renoir (18411919) penned essays and letters expressing his strong belief in the unity of the arts. He trained as a porcelain painter before pursuing a career as an easel painter, and lamented the hierarchy between fine and applied art. These artists pursued associations of the word decorative that suggested personal and expressive possibilities in a variety of media beyond easel painting: working, for instance, in lithography, wallpaper design, and painted screens, though often as private commissions rather than more broadly commercial manufacture. Among their efforts might be included those of Lucien Pissarro (18631944), the son of Impressionist painter Camille Pissarro (18301903), who pursued a career in book publishing, but left France for London where an active private press movement held the promise of greater opportunity. ## Art Nouveau in Print and in Public As mentioned above, strengthening the connection between art and industry served many interests, including extending individual artistic activity beyond the fine arts, elevating public taste, stimulating production, creating national identity, and investing useful goods with the promise and satisfaction of aesthetic pleasure. In the increasingly commercial conditions of metropolitan centers such as Paris, the new aesthetic sensibility seen in the decorative arts formed part of a more generalized modernity that extended beyond private ownership and collecting to a broader and more heterogeneous public. At the Exposition Universelle held in Paris in 1900, lArt Nouveau dominated the architectural constructions for the fair, and French pavilions featured coordinated ensembles of decorative art in the new style. French architect and designer Hector Guimard (18671942) was particularly conspicuous in expanding elements of lArt Nouveau formal vocabulary on an urban scale using modern industrial technology. Guimard adapted the elastic plantlike tendrils associated with many products of aesthetic craft to the railings and entrances of the newly constructed Paris underground transportation system known as the Métro, a few of which are still in use (fig. 6.31). Cast iron was used for this vast project, integrating aesthetic invention with modern industrial production. Guimard also used cast iron for window grilles and designs for a series of original numbers to mark the entrances to houses and buildings. In these ways the arts served a socially progressive role, beautifying and creating a visual identity for the city, attracting tourism, stimulating commerce, and suggesting synergy rather than an antithesis between art and industry. ![](images/054e41bd22bd1b751e3253d49376141b3355f3dc35ee4cf08cf9d26c0f34d7e1.jpg) Hector Guimard, Métro station entrance, Paris, cast iron, c. 1900. Elements of Art Nouveau were also popularized via print media, chiefly in the design and printing of publicity posters, made possible through chromolithography along with more lenient laws that relaxed the regulation of public space enacted in France in 1881. Posters were a prominent, colorful means of promoting products, retail businesses, nightclubs, and cafés, and were a ubiquitous presence in the city, enlivening walls, buildings, and kiosks along the citys grand and pedestrian-friendly boulevards. Among the most significant patrons for poster advertisement during this time were the providers of popular entertainment, and the lithographic printers themselves, who produced posters in smaller format for sale to collectors as well as in larger dimensions for viewing on the street. The dance halls, circuses, and cabarets located in Parisian districts such as Montmartre served as a magnet for middle-class urban leisure, activities that allowed for freer, less structured, uninhibited public forms of self-expression. Whether directly as participants or vicariously as spectators, this public found an outlet for playfulness, fantasy, and sensual pleasure in a vibrant Parisian nightlife  in short, posters sold fun. Jules Chéret (18361932) was an early master of poster design, beginning his career as an apprentice to a printer and spending time in England mastering the technique of chromolithography, which had been successfully developed in the printing of color images primarily for illustrated books (see here and fig. 4.13). Chéret often approached the composition of the poster as an integration of lettering and image to create a unified design on a monumental scale, even when the actual size of the poster was not particularly large. In the poster advertising a group of acrobatic dancers known as Les Girard from 1879 (fig. 6.32), for example, areas of bold text at the top and bottom of the tall rectangular format announce the venue and particular act or time of the performance. The placement of the letters deviates from a horizontal base line, and instead connects and interacts with the three silhouetted dancers in the center, almost like an apparatus to demonstrate the flexibility of their elongated limbs. The sense of movement is strengthened by pinwheel-like shapes of arms, legs, and coat tails, and by the virtual elimination of detail and background. Chéret explored the medium of chromolithography in order to attract attention from a distance, working directly on the stone in an abbreviated, seemingly spontaneous manner. ![](images/540b7366511de7e29bc894e82ea5ec0f1a23045ffaedd7feb93262142a754a2b.jpg) ## 6.32 Jules Cheret, Les Girard, poster for the Folies-Bergère, color lithograph, 21 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>4</sub> × 17 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>8</sub> in (54 × 44.5 cm), 1879. An expressive treatment of line, as well as simplified compositional elements derived from Japanese woodblock prints, also appear in the lithographic posters of painter Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec (18641901), which often advertised the performances of popular Montmartre entertainers such as Loïe Fuller and Louise Weber (known as La Goulue). The latter is featured in a poster of 1891 for the Moulin Rouge (fig. 6.33), printed in a large edition of 3,000 for display throughout Paris. In this poster the illustration is simplified by strong contours for the foreground figures of La Goulue and her partner Valentine le Désossé, while the silhouetted forms of the crowd and the typography maintain the planarity of the surface and unify the elements of the composition into a compact whole. Toulouse-Lautrec also worked directly on the lithographic stone, using strong contours as well as a technique known as crachis to splatter rather than to brush ink on broad areas of the stone. The result is a vitality that matches the mood of gaiety and hedonistic abandon that generates appeal for an evening of casual socializing and entertainment. The chromolithographic poster led a double life: as advertisement and as artwork. Designers signed their work and printers issued them in separate, reduced sizes for collectors. They provided commercial printers with an added value as promotions for a new, reproducible form of modern art, made popular entertainment appealing through associations with a carefree lifestyle, and at the same time enabled the expressive ideas of the Aesthetic Movement to extend beyond a narrow, exclusive circle of initiated viewers. They also demonstrated the power of reductive images to attract attention and for the public to identify performers such as La Goulue, Aristide Bruant, or Loïe Fuller, in effect branding them with characteristic gestures, attributes, or signs. ![](images/6294a62ffa2714e99efdcda2e8bce4a7742011fc531ba7c5f7b43857bc3c7945.jpg) Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec, La Goulue (Louise Weber) at the Moulin Rouge, poster, color lithograph, 48 × 33 in (122 × 83.8 cm), 1891. Turning to the Chicago-born performance artist Loïe Fuller (18621928), both Chéret and painter Manuel Orazi (18601934) designed advertising posters featuring this popular entertainer (fig. 6.34). Orazi hints at eroticism in the dancers performance by outlining Fullers partially nude body beneath a sheer garment. Orazis poster also has a stronger sense of abstract and suggestive forms: hair, flowers, drapery become swirling and cascading shapes making it difficult to distinguish representation from more purely expressive, abstract decoration, along with bursting circular shapes that become more abstract as they descend and morph from the bouquet at the upper left. Fullers dance was also the subject of a series of 15 ceramic figurines designed by Agathon Léonard (18411923) for the Sèvres manufactory in 1898 and displayed at the 1900 International Exhibition in Paris. In addition, Fullers performance was captured on moving film in the early years of this new mediums development. On film there appears to be little if any suggestion of nudity. Rather we see the dancers white scarf and circular arm movements combine to somehow transform her body into the form of a flower in bloom as if by magic. The theme of metamorphosis, seen in the transformation of letterforms into representation or hair into flames or pure decoration in the chromolithographic art poster, is not uncommon in Art Nouveau. Historians have noted a relationship between artists aesthetic fascination with the theme of transformation in nature and Darwins theory of natural selection (first published in 1859), which revealed nature in a constant condition of flux rather than permanence, while at the same time guided by a process of selection toward perfection. The dancers appeal certainly incorporated an abstract aesthetic dimension, but also extended to other audiences, who were excited by novelty, fantasy, sensuality, and the surprise of the unexpected. ![](images/26198358163f26ff80bd1ed10150b3698d2e655ed4942f2fda899dbdffe0e92f.jpg) Manuel Orazi, poster for Löie Fuller, color lithograph, 78 <sup>1</sup>/ 1D4 × 25 <sup>1</sup>/ in (198.7 × 64.1 cm), 1900. Eugène-Samuel Grasset (18411917) was an illustrator and advertising artist who developed a style for graphic images sometimes referred to as cloisonné. He employed strong black lines and figureground contrast to reinforce the planarity of the surface, while the linear treatment of drapery folds or strands of wavy hair create secondary patterns resembling the cells of leaded glass, enamel, or jewelry  media in which Grasset also worked. In an advertisement for Encre L. Marquet (Marquet Ink, The Best of All Inks), the diagonal lettering runs parallel to wavy cloudlike shapes that balance the opposing diagonal direction of a woman who sits thoughtfully, pen in hand (fig. 6.35). Grassets figures are less spontaneous and abbreviated than those of Chéret or Toulouse-Lautrec. Nevertheless, female images dominate the art of the poster; they represent the good life when linked to the purchase of products and entertainment, and they offer new models for defining women in more active roles outside the home, as in American artist Will Bradleys advertising poster for Victor Bicycles (fig. 6.36). ![](images/546efbddbe00f3ef54f9777c1402eddcf14e98d504bda3c6a5314ea885a5655f.jpg) Eugène-Samuel Grasset, advertisement for Marquet Ink, color lithograph, 47 <sup>15</sup>/<sub>16</sub> × 31 <sup>13</sup>/<sub>16</sub> in (121.8 × 80.8 cm), 1892. ![](images/33d667f34d3331b351b9081cbb4b5bc7cf605ee817c8b8796f0d176262204cf1.jpg) Will Bradley, poster for Victor Bicycles, color lithograph, 25 $^ { 1 3 } / _ { 1 6 } \times 3 9 ^ { 3 } / _ { 1 6 }$ in (64.4 × 99.7 cm), 1896. Alphonse Mucha (18601939) came to Paris from his home in Moravia (Czech Republic, then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire) after studying painting in Vienna and Munich. His posters advertising the performances of actress Sarah Bernhardt (18441923) are among his best-known works. In the large 1895 Gismonda poster (fig. 6.37), the actress holds a palm sprig that relates to the plays final act in which her character participates in an Easter Day procession. Her garlanded hair is echoed in the shawl and tunic, both inscribed with floral patterns. Unlike other poster artists who sacrificed detail to emphasize bold shapes, Mucha delights in inventive decoration. Yet the semicircular arch above the actresss head, as well as a banner below the ground line, contain the text and help to focus rather than distract the viewers attention. Despite foreshortening and shadow, the simplified modeling and empty area behind the actresss figure emphasize an heroic monumentality and suggest the dreamlike space of the stage. Later posters are freer in the use of abstraction to allude to metamorphosis, especially in the abstract shapes that suggest hair or lush plant forms. ![](images/787f07d0abf0482e996741cdd49dd7ccce6bae26de478e70bf8662dc7774e2ea.jpg) Alphonse Mucha, Sarah Bernhardt in Gismonda, poster, color lithograph, 84 <sup>3</sup>/ × 30 in (215.3 × 76.2 cm), 1895. In the United States, Harper’s Magazine published cover illustrations by Grasset as well as by American designers such as Edward Penfield (18661925), while some of the boldest approaches to the medium appear in the oeuvre of Will Bradley (18681962). Bradleys early illustrations, influenced by Aubrey Beardsley (see here), had appeared in the British journal The Studio, and examples of his work were exhibited at the opening of La Maison de lArt Nouveau in 1895. In the United States Bradley was active in many areas of an expanding print industry. He published illustrated books for his own private and short-lived Wayside Press in Springfield, Massachusetts, and also contributed to a magazine called The American Chap-Book, published by the American Type Founders Company (see here) as well as the trade journal Inland Printer. In an 1896 advertising poster for Victor Bicycles (fig. 6.36), Bradley used four colors to create a bold, reductive image. Repetition in the black silhouettes of the riders and in the elongated floral decoration are both products of simplification, while the use of black ties the border with its lettering to the image. Bradleys posters and illustration create effective figure ground reversal  for instance, in the negative shapes between the arm and torso of the foreground female rider to the left of the composition. The design incorporates a carefully considered hierarchy of information: the red lettering against the black border attracts the viewers eye, first with the larger letters at the top left and then with the company name diagonally at the lower right, while the black silhouettes of the riders serve to connect top to bottom. The placement of white flowers reinforces left to right eye movement that focuses attention on the company name and the head of the larger female figure. The public and commercial face of Art Nouveau extends to the design of interiors and displays related to fantasy and enhancing the experience of shopping. Jeweler Georges Fouquet (18621957) collaborated with Mucha on several brooches, and Mucha designed Fouquets boutique on the rue Royale in Paris in 1900 (fig. 6.38), completed in time for the Paris Worlds Fair of the same year. Patterned tile floors and friezes, plantlike pilasters and other architectural details, relief sculptures of peacocks, and wall-mounted curving glass cases in the shape of bubbles unify wall, ceiling, and floor into a total work of art. Mirrors serve to make the space appear larger, indulging the fantasy of the spectator in an imaginative setting for effectively merchandising the exotic creations offered for sale. ![](images/13776d906147c01d2ef4ea60498c06857b0444882006d956fff18c2560a873a8.jpg) ## 6.38 Alphonse Mucha, interior for boutique, Paris, for Georges Fouquet, jeweler, 1900. Photograph: Musée Carnavalet, Paris. Mucha achieved success as a poster artist, although his posters include more detail than those of contemporaries such as Jules Chéret. To be persuasive, advertising posters had to arrest the attention of the pedestrian viewer, that is, to stand out from a distance with audacious color and broad execution according to the English critic Charles Hiatt, and to project a mood of gaiety, in other words, to sell, often depicting young, idealized, and alluring female figures. Could such objectives have conflicted with rather than stimulated artistic freedom? Mucha left France to return to his native Moravia in 1910. At the conclusion of World War I, his homeland became part of the newly independent nation of Czechoslovakia. Deeply patriotic, Mucha spent the remainder of his career engaged in a series of 26 mural-size paintings narrating the history of his country (The Slav Epic), striving to create a serious national art freed from the commercial constraints of the advertising poster. ## Glasgow: Charles Rennie Mackintosh In Glasgow, Scottish architect Charles Rennie Mackintosh (18681928), his wife Margaret MacDonald (18641933), and a small group of fellow students and colleagues navigated the territory between the aesthetic and broader public context for the decorative arts before World War I. Situated on the River Clyde, Glasgow became an important seaport and international trading hub during the nineteenth century. The construction and outfitting of ocean liners and railway locomotives bolstered the local economy and led to the widespread use of standardized industrial methods of production for furniture and interior decoration. The Singer sewing machine company operated a factory in Glasgow, and the city prospered amid economic growth, new wealth, and a modern, progressive image. In his own architecture and interiors, Mackintosh strived to create a distinctive and original approach to design, combining straightforward construction clearly stated through vertical and horizontal structural elements, integrated with restrained decorative elements that either played upon this geometry (as in the Library of the Glasgow School of Art, 19071909) or incorporated a free interpretation of the tense linear rhythms of Hiberno-Saxon decoration. In many of his projects Mackintosh collaborated with his wife, Margaret MacDonald. The picture frame illustrated in figure 6.39, designed by Margaret MacDonald Mackintosh and her sister Frances (18731921), is made from hammered aluminum on wood. Experimenting with a new metal that was becoming more affordable as a result of cheaper industrial methods of extraction, the frame features highly personal and abstract interpretations of floral and vegetal forms. Moreover, aluminum was a local resource, since Scotland was a center for aluminum production in Britain beginning in the later 1890s. ![](images/2ea4291bb45e8338e538ec4247a33d6906d597ed94862b8697eeec00e6834ae1.jpg) Margaret MacDonald Mackintosh and Frances MacDonald, picture frame, aluminum and oak, 27 ${ ^ 3 } / _ { 1 6 } \times 1 2 { ^ 3 } / _ { 4 }$ in (69.2 × 32.4 cm), 1897. The same vocabulary of an attenuated rectilinear framework forming borders for more organic floral and human forms can be seen in the murals MacDonald designed for Miss Kate Cranstons Buchanan Street Tearooms in 18961897 (fig. 6.40). The tearoom afforded several opportunities for Mackintosh and MacDonald to create unified, original interiors between 1895 and 1903, and was a unique expression of Glasgows original and modern identity. Frequently owned and managed by women, Glasgows tearooms introduced the kind of coordinated aesthetic interior found in privately commissioned homes into a public commercial setting. Mackintosh designed several tearooms for Miss Cranston from 1896 to 1902, where middle-class clientele might enjoy the aesthetic ambience produced by the unity and equality of all the arts. Mackintoshs design activity extended to flatware, dishes, glassware, and tablecloths, as well as furniture, windows, and lighting. He believed that an individual approach to design could be reconciled with efficient industrial production, and that the decorative arts were integral to a satisfying modern lifestyle and not just for the discerning few. His interiors and coordinated design ensembles offered an alternative to historical revival styles and demonstrated that aesthetic experience need not be sacrificed to the demands for standardization in industrial production. ![](images/a5f971343a9d7aae136c17ba2f9b8ee33409128169216e922c552605baa6327f.jpg) Charles Rennie Mackintosh and Margaret MacDonald Mackintosh, interior, Buchanan Street Tearooms, Glasgow, 1896– 1897. ## Austria Mackintoshs interior designs were illustrated in journals, displayed at an international exhibition in Turin in 1902, and stimulated the efforts of a group of artists and architects in Vienna known as the Secession. These artists, like the Pre-Raphaelites in Britain and Les Vingt in Belgium (see here), reacted to the rigidity and elitism of academic practice in the fine arts and sought both freedom of expression as well as embracing the decorative arts. The attitudes of the Secession group appear in the motto inscribed on their exhibition hall, designed by Joseph Olbrich in Vienna in 1898 (fig. 6.41): Die Zeit ihre Kunst, Die Kunst ihre Freiheit (To every time its art, to every art its freedom). Vienna proved fertile ground for elevated attitudes toward the decorative arts, where new ideas resonated with connoisseurs and were directed as well toward a broader, open-minded public. ![](images/7a39ac3bfe12c90942a8a2c7f1cfaf39e45164be0a4e29946b61b9264fcf9cd6.jpg) Joseph Olbrich, façade of the Secession building, Vienna, 1898. Josef Hoffmann (18701956), an architect and leading member of the Secession, and versatile artist-designer Koloman (Kolo) Moser (18681918) created an organization that linked designers, craftsmen, and manufacturers in a shared enterprise to improve the quality and elevate the status of the decorative arts in Austria. The organization, known as the Wiener Werkstätte (Vienna Workshops), was founded in 1903 with the financial backing of industrialist Fritz Wärndorfer (18681939). Hoffmann was inspired by the example of Ashbees Guild of Handicraft (see here) as well as by Mackintosh (whose work was featured in a Secession exhibition in 1900), and wrote to the architect for advice. In his response, Mackintosh encouraged his Viennese colleagues, sounding a note of optimistic liberal idealism in his plea for the role of the artist in improving the quality of everyday life through design: If one wants to achieve an artistic success with your programme ... every object which you pass from your hand must carry an outspoken mark of individuality, beauty and most exact execution. From the outset your aim must be that every object which you produce is made for a certain purpose and place. Later ... you can emerge boldly into the full light of the world, attack the factory-trade on its own ground, and the greatest work that can be achieved in this century, you can achieve it: namely the production of objects of use in magnificent form and at such a price that they lie within the buying range of the poorest. First those who [look down at applied art] must be overcome and taught that the modern movement is not a silly hobby-horse of a few who wish to achieve fame comfortably through eccentricity, but that the modern movement is something living, something good, the only possible art  for all and for the highest phase of our time. Products of the Werkstätte include a range of domestic items from clothing to glass and tableware, to wallpaper and individualized calling cards (presented to domestic servants when paying a social visit), all of which were generally produced on commission from wealthy private clients. Each product bore the stamp of the artist, the manufacturer, and the workshops distinctive interlocked W monogram, as an expression of individual responsibility and the spirit of collaboration between designer and manufacturer. Designers varied in their approaches, but all were committed to originality rather than historicism. Hoffmanns designs parallel those of Mackintosh in incorporating repeated geometric decorative patterns as a distinctive but restrained approach to decoration on otherwise simple, even plain, objects, as seen, for instance, in the chairs manufactured for the exclusive Purkersdorf Sanitarium in 1905 that feature a series of small circular perforations cut in their backs (fig. 6.42). The absence of carved or molded decoration, as well as the light, open interior of the dining room, help to create not only an aesthetically unified interior but also a modern, hygienic one, in sympathy with the views of design reform writers who viewed carved decoration as unhealthy because it attracted dust (see fig. 6.48) and required more cleaning. Other examples of Werkstätte objects include wine glasses manufactured in 1907 by the Meyr Neffe company in Bohemia (now the Czech Republic) but sold in Austria by a Viennese retailer. The longstemmed glasses, based upon traditional techniques practiced in the region but treated here with great subtlety and sophistication, were designed by Otto Prutscher (1880 1949), and are made of clear mold-blown glass overlaid with colored glass and then carefully cut to make the stem appear like links of a fragile, translucent chain (fig. 6.43). ![](images/67c7e78aecd5625f931b48e750efa5ce22f6cb36f20bd0cd3ea97c72e68c4d0f.jpg) 6.42 Josef Hoffmann, dining room of Purkersdorf Sanitarium, 1905. ![](images/feaae26bc31a4b6f05ca21b366f239a01ed17b1e55fa1ddc77202e2b8f2f1e28.jpg) 6.43 Otto Prutscher, wine glass, wheel-cut glass, height 8 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>3</sub> in (21 cm), Meyr Neffe Glassworks, Vienna, 1907. While his early work in graphic design was inspired by French Art Nouveau, Moser turned to severe rectilinear shapes and forms after 1900 in graphic design, exhibition design, architectural decoration, and furniture. His striking poster for a Secession art exhibition from 1902 (fig. 6.44) features three severely abstract bust-length female figures whose circular coiffures intersect with a large central circle. The almost illegible mono-weight letterforms (note the similarity between the odd upper-case R and A), without spacing between words, suggest that clarity of information was not the primary purpose of Mosers visual communication. The grid-like design unifies the composition and subjects the three female heads to geometric simplification, their wing-like black hair silhouetted against blue circles that intersect with a white circle and lozenge shapes filled with small triangles placed on their upper torsos. Moser appears to ignore the compositional reduction and ease of recognition characteristic of successful advertising posters designed to attract attention from a distance, yet at the same time the Thirteenth Secession Exhibition poster might have effectively announced its subject to a reasonably well-informed Viennese citizen of the time: the three women echo the three arts carved in relief above the doorway to the Secession Building (see fig. 6.41), the circles above their heads alluding to the gilt dome atop the building, and the checkerboard designs at the end of lines of text finding their counterpart in the decorative patterns on the same edifice. Among the works associated with the Wiener Werkstätte is the decoration of a home in Brussels for wealthy Belgian banker Adolphe Stoclet, built between 1904/5 and 1911 by Josef Hoffmann, with interior design and furnishings executed almost exclusively by designers and artisans associated with the workshops. No expense was spared for the materials, design, and construction of the home, including different varieties of rare marble and an elaborate series of mural mosaic decorations for the dining room designed by Viennese Secession painter Gustav Klimt (18621918; fig. 6.46). Here Klimt explored his personal vision of the intensity of human love connected with natures fertility and abundance in a unit formed by an embracing couple and female figure on either side of a flowering tree composed of spiraling branches, all treated as flat, simplified shapes. These themes have a particular resonance in fin-desiècle Vienna, where the topic of modern societys alienation from nature and suppression of sexual instinct was brought to light by Sigmund Freud (18561939), through whom terms such as repression and drive have become part of our everyday vocabulary and culture. The Vienna Secession and Wiener Werkstätte were elements of the citys café society or culture, where a rising and diverse middle class met and mixed with the traditional aristocratic elite to produce a vibrant artistic and intellectual milieu outside of the official and more restrictive enclaves of court, salon, or university: more casual, more public, more inclusive, in a word, more modern. For Australian poet and writer Clive James (19392019), the informality of the café and the discourse that resulted from its unofficial status and diverse make-up were keys to the creativity that energized the city. James praised Viennas cultural legacy and lamented its passing after World War I. ![](images/8b01214ec5303ebf1db53afc523d79e19779ac2015f7a3d1fa89cf2b32611a58.jpg) Josef Hoffmann, Palais Stoclet dining room, with murals designed by Gustav Klimt, Brussels, 1905–1911. ## Belgium Adolphe Stoclets choice of the Wiener Werkstätte for the design of his Brussels home was not surprising, for the wealthy Belgian banker spent much time abroad. Yet Belgian artists, in a region experiencing tremendous economic growth through trade and colonial expansion, also embraced modern attitudes toward design and the equality of the arts. One of the countrys most articulate spokesmen and practitioners for the unity and revitalization of the visual arts was Henry van de Velde. As a painter, Van de Velde was affiliated with the progressive Belgian Modernist movement known as Les Vingt. In the early 1890s he began to adapt a Symbolist style featuring abstract shapes and linear rhythms derived from organic forms to the design of furniture, books, and metalcraft. In almost all of his works the decorative elements, characterized by taut curvilinear contours invested with whip-like energy, are integrated with rather than added to structural elements such as the legs of a chair or the branches of a candelabrum (fig. 6.45). ![](images/eb1e5deb14fcc976ed08fcbe1f99f906ce60e32a524244977664ff662bbf6302.jpg) 6.45 Henry van de Velde, candelabrum, electroplated bronze, 23 ${ ^ 1 } / _ { 1 6 } \times$ 20 in (58.5 × 50.8 cm), 1902. Trondheim, Nordenfjeldske Kunstindustrimuseum (Museum Of Applied Arts). Van de Velde attempted to fuse aesthetic expression with efficient production, as if the designer was thinking like a craftsman, or the craftsman as a designer. While deeply concerned with the negative social consequences of industrialization and influenced by the ideals of the English Arts and Crafts movement, Van de Velde found that his aesthetic interests often outweighed his commitment to design as a vehicle for social reform and equality. Invited by Siegfried Bing to install a showroom at La Maison de lArt Nouveau in Paris in 1895, Van de Velde designed a coordinated interior for a study in which murals, fabrics, furniture, and a stained-glass, skylit ceiling create a unified aesthetic effect in which all elements contribute to the overall effect (fig. 6.47). For Van de Velde, individual expression was compatible both with industry and with business practice, at least in theory. He exhibited widely, contributed to publications on modern decorative arts, and was equally interested in the reform and elevation of craft education with stronger ties to the fine arts. In this latter role he was appointed in 1904 as director of a school of craft (Kunstgewerbeschule) in Weimar, Germany, a post he held until the outbreak of World War I. Scholars often use the term whiplash when describing the spring-like energy that Van de Velde and other Belgian Art Nouveau artists employed in their work through shapes and contours. Art historian Debora Silverman (see here) has noted an iconographic connection between the distinctive whiplash motif found in Belgian Art Nouveau and the nations brutal treatment of African workers under Belgian rule in the Free State of Congo in the 1890s, a reference to the human costs of colonial policy under King Leopold II (18351909). Belgian Art Nouveau was on display in the Congo Pavilion at the 1897 International Exhibition held in Brussels and Tervuren, while in other projects Leopold preferred other visual means to communicate Belgian wealth and power, for instance, in the great dome and relief decoration of the 1905 Antwerp Central Railway Station designed by Louis Delacenserie (18381909). The human consequences of industrial development that fueled the Arts and Crafts and related movements in Europe and the United States also emerge in literature and in the early work of Dutch painter Vincent van Gogh (18531890) and contemporary Belgian artists who were sympathetic in their depictions of the difficult lives of miners in the region known as the Borinage. Polish-British author Joseph Conrad (18571924) chronicled the brutality in the Free State of Congo in his novella Heart of Darkness (1899) based upon his experience working on a steamer on the Congo River. Another designer associated with Belgian Art Nouveau was Gustave Serrurier-Bovy (18581910). Serrurier-Bovy designed original furniture featuring expensive woods, carving, and inventive approaches to materials. Beginning just after 1900, he also designed a line of more practical and inexpensive furniture under the name Silex (fig. 6.48) that offered a more utilitarian alternative to the aesthetic Van de Velde interior illustrated in figure 6.47. The design for a Silex dining room was illustrated in a contemporary decorative arts magazine published in Paris in 1904, and features simple construction techniques using inexpensive light woods perforated by a pattern of abstract squares for decoration, relieved by the orange walls and stenciled floral border on the dining rooms upper walls. While individual and original expression remained important elements of design thinking in Belgium, the promise of integrating art into everyday life also remained a goal, realized in advertising art, in commercial interiors, and in the restrained unified domestic interior. As art historian Amy Ogata explained, this aim resonated both with conservative attitudes aimed at shaping middle-class values, and with socialist interests in reducing class difference. ![](images/85b0e44c049501e790cb80ce155aef00b0a3c296038864facd657439d04ec613.jpg) Gustave Serrurier-Bovy, scheme for an interior with Silex furniture, from L’Art Décoratif, Paris, 1904. Dutch merchants brought luxury goods to the West while Dutch artists supplied designs to the Vlisco company, founded in 1846 (and still in business today), for roller-printed dress fabrics and marketed to the local population of West Africa (see Global Inspiration III, here). In addition to museums built in the first half of the century, the city sponsored construction of a large exhibition hall in 1854 inspired by Londons 1851 Crystal Palace. A number of Munich-based artists felt constrained by traditional distinctions between the fine and applied arts in terms of education and the organization of exhibitions. Contemporary with debates in Britain, Vienna, Brussels, and elsewhere in Europe, artists took a keen interest in design, and through their efforts an international art exhibition of 1897 in Munich included two rooms devoted to the decorative arts. In 1896 the journal Jugend (youth) was launched in Munich to promote contemporary arts and entertainment, its circulation reaching 200,000 copies per week around the year 1900, acknowledging the prominent role of the visual arts, including the decorative arts, in contemporary life. The journals title was the basis for the word Jugendstil (youth style), a label for modern ideas in art and design in Germany. A second Munich-based journal, Simplicissimus, also appeared in the same year. A key figure in the practice and promotion of new attitudes toward the equality of the arts was Otto Eckmann (18651902), who, like Van de Velde in Belgium, abandoned painting to pursue a career in design. Eckmann designed woodcut illustrations for the periodicals Jugend and the Berlin-based Pan that depicted plants and flowers with strong outlines and gently undulating flattened shapes emphasizing strong figureground distinction that demonstrated familiarity with Japanese woodblock prints. Decorative forms derived from this approach were developed in distinctive furniture designs, tapestry, and other media, including lettering and even typography. The search for national identity was indeed a creative rather than historical exercise, and was both highly selective and eclectic, supported through government agencies and associations of designers and manufacturers. Through travel and training outside of their respective homelands, the circulation of journals, and attendance or participation at international exhibitions, countries such as Denmark, Finland, Norway, Sweden, Hungary, and Bohemia, each demonstrated the increasingly international scope of design movements that championed the unity, equality, and cultural contributions of the visual arts. In Finland, architect Eliel Saarinen (18731950) used native building materials such as granite to construct a national artistic identity distinct from Russia (of which Finland was then a semi-autonomous province) or Germany, based upon a Romantic vision of Finnish history and mythology. Cultural identity and nationalism were thus linked to political independence, international recognition, and a revitalization of the Finnish economy through manufacturing and international trade. Saarinens Koti chair of 1897, designed for an exhibition, emphasizes simple, solid craftsmanship, with forms and simply carved decoration that recall forms of Indigenous architecture (fig. 6.55). Such designs may be seen as a visual counterpart to widespread interest in oral folk traditions of Karelia (a region of Finland), whose poems and songs were collected and first published in the second half of the nineteenth century. Historians often refer to the concern with a primitive, authentic past as National Romanticism. ![](images/e4a6cbd139487f2ff7b774ed63cd0f199e2a824ff82d6b159d0b737625f48506.jpg) Eliel Saarinen, Koti chair, oak and reupholstered seat, $5 2 \times 3 3 \times 3 1$ in $( 1 3 2 \times 6 8 \times 6 3$ cm), designed 1897. The rug is woven using a native technique known as ryijy, and the abstract flame motif appears in contemporary Finnish stoneware as well as in other textile and wallpaper patterns. Finnish artists looked to the past to create a national style in the arts, but their efforts were less a recreation or revival of the past than a modern, hybrid phenomenon, renewing craft traditions and mining Indigenous decorative motifs while responding to competitive economic conditions and a desire to construct a coherent nationalist sentiment and identity. Thus these emerging national styles were at once nostalgic, selective, and progressive; Finland was successful in lobbying exhibition organizers for its own pavilion, separate from that of Russia, at the 1900 Worlds Fair in Paris. The pavilion was designed primarily by Saarinen, with mural paintings by Gallén-Kallela, and also featured carved display cases with examples of Finnish handicraft. As art historian Bart Pushaw has written, such (architectural) eclecticism was actually representative of the way in which pavilion officials distilled a diverse culture into a single representation, inevitably simplifying, essentializing, and exoticizing an unknown people to gain international acclaim and respect. Indeed, the construction of Finnish identity at the turn of the century excluded both the culture of the long-standing Russian population of Finland as well as the marginalized nomadic Sámi people who had a rich but under-represented role in Finnish history. Conscious of an increasingly international context and market for modern design after the end of World War I, Saarinen participated in design competitions abroad, and eventually emigrated with his wife Loja (18791968, a textile designer) to the United States in the early 1920s, becoming director of the Cranbrook Academy of Art in Bloomfield Hills, Michigan (see here and fig. 10.1). ![](images/1b484ad933affc801dec5f23ff847bebbf767391d866a228c094cdff8e1ff9c1.jpg) Akseli Gallén-Kallela, The Flame, ryijy rug, wool, $1 2 0 \times 6 7 ~ ^ { 3 } / _ { 4 }$ in (304.8 × 172 cm), 1899 (woven 1884). Thorvald Bindesbøll (18461908) was a Danish artist whose ceramics and silver were exhibited at the 1900 Paris Worlds Fair. Until 1918 the present-day Czech Republic was part of the Habsburg Empire with its capital in Vienna. The region, comprising Bohemia to the west (including Prague) and Moravia to the east, developed original approaches to the decorative arts in the later nineteenth century, building upon distinctive local craft traditions in media such as glass, exhibited at Worlds Fairs and benefitting from and contributing to international design trends in Vienna and elsewhere, forging a modern identity and marketing strategy that mingled local and contemporary elements. Glass manufactories in Bohemia, such as Loetz in the town of Klastersky Mlyn or Meyrs Neffe in the south, experimented with new techniques in that medium (see fig. 6.43). From a global perspective, the aesthetic movements examined in this chapter are also contemporary with nationalism, Western imperial conquest, and colonization, the extension of orientalizing attitudes that cast non-Western and non-white populations as less developed culturally than their Western counterparts. The ethnographic displays of Indigenous populations on the Midway Plaisance at the 1893 Worlds Columbian Exposition in Chicago provide ample evidence of such racial and cultural bias, challenged at the time in the work of anthropologist Franz Boas (18581942). Examples from these movements also mirror their complexity and varied expressions in modern design of the era, producing at the same time in the Netherlands, for instance, Berlages Beurs building (fig 6.51) and the rich decoration of Koks porcelain vase (fig. 6.50). The dynamic forces of technology and capital that created wealth and nurtured opportunity also fed disaffection and seething tensions that could erupt in violence, anarchy, and reactive repression in the name of law and order. One is unlikely to gain a sense of social and political tension from advertising posters or home furnishings that are aligned with freedom of expression, promises of the good life, the economic benefits of colonial expansion, and the embrace of progressive ideals, but Toulouse-Lautrecs 1893 chromolithographic poster advertising the conservative newspaper Le Matin presents an unusually grim illustration of a public execution depicting a chained and possibly defiant prisoner approaching the guillotine, silhouetted against an anonymous row of soldiers mounted on horseback (fig. 6.68). Here Toulouse-Lautrec contrasts the intimidating face of authority with the helplessness of the victim (what has he done to deserve the death penalty or, has he been unjustly convicted?) without taking sides. Design occurs in a political, social, and economic context, the product of beliefs and values about individuals and their relationship to the society in which they live and the wider world they inhabit. Dominant views coexist and vie for power with marginal views. Conflicting, resistant viewpoints do not always find expression in design, especially if one relies upon advertising, exhibition displays for evidence. In an era that witnessed the rise of national product brands in the food industry and beyond, however, the workplace offered the architect an opportunity to develop a new paradigm for design, distinct from the more individual aesthetic associations of the private home. In 1906 Wright designed the Buffalo, New York, headquarters of the Larkin Soap Company, a large and successful national, industrialscale manufacturer. There Wright addressed the design of the modern corporate office, organizing the functions of the emerging white-collar professions who functioned as part of a complex business model that manufactured, marketed, and distributed a nationally branded product. In some ways the Larkin Building (fig. 7.1) is the antithesis of Wrights Prairie Style houses, contrasting the horizontality and open flow of space in the former with the verticality and spatial insulation of the latter, with direct light entering only from the ends of the central rectangular workroom and from high above the floor level. In this workroom, the clerks and secretaries anonymity is stated through the repetitive, axial arrangement of desks (and uniform dress) in a vast space that dwarfs the individual and eliminates privacy. The standardization is a visual counterpart to the efficiency and specialization required for the bureaucratized functions of large-scale business, requiring workers to become conditioned to increasingly routinized and repetitive tasks. In exchange or as compensation for conformity to these modern working conditions, Wright offered the security of community, of feeling part of a larger whole, as well as hope for advancement and the rewards for the sacrifice of individuality and selfdetermination to the demands of corporate management: at the top of the workroom an inscribed motto, taken from the seventh chapter of Matthews Gospel in the Christian New Testament, reads: Ask and it shall be given you; Seek and Ye Shall Find; Knock and it shall be opened unto you. Perhaps we will never know if such words and communal spaces provided reassurance and incentive for the clerks and secretaries who toiled in the Larkin Buildings workroom, but Wrights design articulates a managerial point of view regarding the nature of labor and its wider business context of standardization and conformity in the early twentieth century. ![](images/9e13976ee1bc917128a699b22734b0fbc2408b811b3f068ee6b7f3bbcd289ca6.jpg) ## Germany In Germany similar attitudes, fueled in part by large manufacturing and utility companies, took root in the early twentieth century, producing reform initiatives advocating modern design standards based upon practicality rather than luxury and individual expression, and finding support in applied arts schools in Düsseldorf, Breslau, Weimar, and Berlin. German companies and workshops uniting designers with craftsmen also participated in international exhibitions in Paris (1900) and St. Louis (1904), and mounted an exhibition in Munich (1908) that received a positive critical response. In 1907 a group of artists and manufacturers formed an organization to promote the German applied arts, known as the Deutscher Werkbund (German Work Federation or German League of Work). A significant figure in the Werkbund was Hermann Muthesius (18611927), who visited Britain from 1896 to 1899 to study English architecture (published as Das Englische Haus), and subsequently became the Minister of Trade for the Prussian government. Like other advocates of design reform, Muthesius believed in the harmony of utility and beauty, but felt that individuality should be subservient to more practical, durable, and rational forms for manufactured products. Through modularity, interchangeability, specialized and mechanized production, Muthesius and his supporters argued that the benefits of efficiency in design and production outweighed individual expressive, artistic concerns. The benefits of his approach included not only the capital cost savings of standardization to manufacturers, but also the reform of consumer taste, advocating a more modest, comfortable ideal for modern living, stressing moderation rather than luxury and individual identity. Such attitudes may be seen in Muthesiuss criticism of contemporary Scottish design as being impractical in its degree of refinement and out of touch with the habits of modern middle-class living: Once the interior attains the status of a work of art, that is, when it is intended to embody aesthetic values, the artistic effect must obviously be heightened to the utmost. The Mackintosh group does this and no-one will reproach them on this particular point. Whether such enhancement is appropriate to our everyday rooms is another question. Mackintoshs rooms are refined to a degree which the lives of even the artistically educated are still a long way from matching. The delicacy and austerity of their artistic atmosphere would tolerate no admixture of the ordinariness which fills our lives. Even a book in an unsuitable binding would disturb the atmosphere simply by lying on the table, indeed even the man or woman of today  especially the man in his unadorned working attire  treads like a stranger in this fairy-tale world. There is for the time being no possibility of our aesthetic cultivation playing so large a part in our lives that rooms like this could be general. But they are milestones placed by a genius far ahead of us to mark the way to excellence for mankind in the future. The willingness of a number of artists and architects to work within the shared design ethos outlined by Muthesius and like-minded designers and businessmen is an important characteristic of German design in the decade leading up to the outbreak of World War I, stemming at least in part from a desire to construct a national identity in the wake of German unification (1871) and a willingness to compromise expressive freedom in order to achieve common ground through design. Although criticized by the Belgian architect-designer Henry van de Velde for the constraint on artistic freedom and individuality it implied, and opposed by a number of smaller manufacturers for being undemocratic and giving undo weight to larger manufacturing companies, Muthesius had many adherents, especially among Germanys industrialists and a number of leading artist-designers. Among these, Munich-based Richard Riemerschmid (see here) abandoned the abstract rhythmic curves of the Jugendstil for restrained designs in furniture and other household consumer goods. His set of cutlery manufactured for Karl Weisshaupt in 1912 (fig. 7.2) features straight, mildly elongated handles and plain surfaces, contrasting with the engraved decoration and more delicate, asymmetrically curved handles of the Jugendstil (see fig. 6.54). ![](images/dd10f598c632f4176b421201bc2fec7dfa1addeb53869f5ac8f067763ccef691.jpg) ## 7.2 Richard Riemerschmid, cutlery, manufactured by Bruckmann, Heilbron, for Karl Weisshaupt, silver, length $5 \%$ to $6 \%$ in (14.4 to 17.2 cm), Munich, 1912. Peter Behrens (18681940) was another designer associated with the Werkbund and early twentieth-century design reform in Germany. Like Riemerschmid, Behrens abandoned the expressive rhythms of the Jugendstil for more sober forms, seen for instance in his turbine factory for the German General Electric Company (Allgemeine Elektrizitäts-Gesellschaft or AEG) of 1908 to 1909. From 1907 Behrens worked as a consultant for the AEG to develop the companys promotional materials and to work with product engineers to design industrial equipment and electric appliances for domestic use in order to increase demand for electricity. Incorporating standardized forms and parts to reduce labor and equipment costs, Behrens also introduced variety into AEGs line of electric tea kettles through variations in material, finish, and handles to provide for consumer choice. He also preserved associations with handicraft in the use of a molded exterior that resembled a hand-hammered surface (fig. 7.3). The result was an Industry Culture that reimagined the relationship between fine and applied art (the harmony between utility and beauty), advocating reform based upon an equation between efficient production and simple, unified forms with little or no decoration. Although never a national policy in Germany, this practical or objective approach to industrial design and production represented the outlook of large businesses and corporations who benefitted not only from production economies resulting from standardization, but also from products that achieved recognition in the marketplace by possessing a common visual vocabulary, along with a trademark that provided brand identity and recognition. Like most reform efforts, Industry Culture rested upon an architectural paradigm that rendered ornament unnecessary, an ethical belief that valued plain forms as an expression of self-restraint and moderation, and a disdain for the excesses of fashion in a competitive, free-market system. Behrens was a well-known architect whose work for AEG helped to create as well as to promote the companys contemporary image and contribute to the recognition and positive associations of their products. Behrenss graphic design for AEG shares a directness of approach with the advertising posters of self-taught Viennese artist Lucian Bernhard (18831972), who was working in Munich in the first decade of the twentieth century and had joined the Deutscher Werkbund. Bernhard produced advertising posters reduced to the bold company name juxtaposed with the straightforward presentation of its product, in contrast to posters that involved the use of young women, or children, who address the viewer. An example is the 1908 poster for Adler Typewriters (fig. 7.6). Critics have suggested that the simplified treatment of shadow is the result of the influence of photography, where the half-tone method of commercial reproduction (see here) reduced the tonal range of the original image, creating a more abstract effect, especially on a larger scale. Although the typewriter appears in three-quarter view, the simplified tone and justification of the image with the border at the left virtually negate spatial recession. In Bernhards posters rarely are the products themselves distinctive: whether matches, shoes, or cigarettes, these national brands are represented as generic rather than by unique goods, with the letters of the company name itself providing recognition as a sign that generates association and trust, in place of product differentiation or more persuasive advertising techniques employing narrative illustration associating products with lifestyle, or slogans. For some industries, the American System of manufacture continued to wrest control of production from independent workshops and workers in favor of factory owners and floor managers who determined production schedules for goods that could be met only by increasing the division of labor as well as the use of labor-saving, electric-powered machinery and interchangeable parts that required routine assembly rather than skilled labor. To assist companies in reducing labor costs, Philadelphia-born Frederick W. Taylor (18561915) conducted experiments in the 1880s to increase worker productivity. Using a stop-watch Taylor measured the amount of time taken by workers to perform routine tasks. In one experiment he demonstrated that a man shoveling coal would work at optimum efficiency with individual loads of 22 pounds, and even made drawings for the design of a shovel to best hold the load. Once determined, Taylors recommendations were enforced in the workplace by managers and became the measure for labor productivity and rewards (wages), demanding higher levels of compliance and conformity on the factory floor. Taylors analyses are described under the heading scientific management. His findings reinforced Adam Smiths description of a pin factory (see here), but further analyzed in relation to the number of movements necessary to perform a given task in order to save more time and increase productivity. Taylors system required a degree of conformity that might have made John Ruskin shudder, but the sacrifice was part of the bargain (see Frank Lloyd Wrights Larkin Building, here). As described by author Robert Kanigel: There it was, the Faustian bargain in embryonic form: You do it my way, by my standards, at the speed I mandate, and in so doing achieve a level of output I ordain, and Ill pay you handsomely for it, beyond anything you might have imagined. All you have to do is take orders, give up your way of doing the job for mine. Taylors approach to production on a large industrial scale accompanied the emergence and growth of giant corporations, and also may be seen in relation to government- and manufacturing-imposed standardization. Examples of regulation include the United States government uniform standards for the sizes of plumbing and electrical fittings, or standard sizes in the construction industry for wooden beams or windows in order to ensure compatibility among manufacturers without the confusion that might be caused by unfettered competition. Standards also applied to public utilities, for instance, in the telephone industry. The Bell Telephone Corporation exercised a monopoly on the manufacture of handsets, which as a result were only available in a limited number of models. Industrialist Henry Ford expanded upon Taylors scientific management. According to historian Peter Ling, rather than analyzing existing tasks, Ford and his managers saved time and increased productivity by rethinking the location of parts and the sequence of operations in manufacturing, eventually developing the moving assembly line with the help of electrically operated conveyer belts. Through the assembly line, and Fords decision to manufacture only a single model of automobile, labor was defined primarily as a factor of time rather than skill or thought, maximized by demanding production schedules dictated by floor managers and humorously satirized by Charlie Chaplin in a memorable sequence from his 1936 film Modern Times. Fords assembly line required an open and easily expandable interior factory space to accommodate increased production. Through steel-frame construction, inexpensive building materials, and the use of a modular principle to facilitate expansion, Fords architect Albert Kahn (1869 1942) created large, open, well-lit factory spaces to serve the flexible needs of the moving assembly line, first at the Highland Park plant in 1908 (fig. 7.7) and then at the larger River Rouge facility beginning in 1920. ![](images/0b8e6924212a461fb7a7e8efbabe8e6df2968ec15a902bc7ace376eff829b010.jpg) End of the line, Ford Motor Plant, Highland Park, Michigan, c. 1913. From the collections of Henry Ford Museum and Greenfield Village, Michigan. Fords production methods transformed the automobile from a luxury item requiring fine, specialized craftsmanship for details of construction, assembly, and finish into a middle-class form of personal transportation through complete uniformity, standardization, and interchangeable parts for ease of repair and replacement. Between 1907 and 1916 the price of Fords Model T (Tin Lizzie) actually decreased as demand increased. The car, available only in black, remained virtually unchanged and unchallenged in the market until the mid-1920s. The ability of the industry to produce an automobile within the means of many through the application of technology was almost universally admired and contributed to making mechanized mass production part of the ideology of economic progress in the United States and abroad in the period between the two world wars. Until the later 1920s, Ford did not invest heavily in advertising, as the appeal of affordable personal transportation offered by his Model T required little additional promotion or techniques of persuasion. Factory owners addressed the human costs of this system of factory production in a variety of ways, including Fords own Five-dollar Day in 1914 that compensated for the loss of worker independence with higher wages and the promise of putting aside a portion of wages each week toward the purchase of an automobile of their own, and through company efforts to build identity and reinforce compliance among workers through public and corporate relations (see here). Eventually labor unions helped to confront these issues with threats of strikes as leverage for higher compensation and other benefits. Indeed, Fords mechanized mass production demanded conformity, repetition, and strict obedience to managerial control in the workplace, and such practices differed profoundly from the ideals of joyful and meaningful work that animated Arts and Crafts reform and various aesthetic movements in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. As discussed above, the crafts workshop founded by Elbert Hubbards Roycroft community (see here) was an attempt to humanize the workplace by creating a sense of cooperation among employees while incorporating more modern methods of production, provision for housing, and low wages for commercial viability. Ironically, Hubbard admired the success of Ford and other industrialists, whose biographies he published under the heading Little Journeys to the Homes of Great Businessmen. These and other pamphlets were printed in great quantities for corporations who hoped to inspire loyalty among their work force. Such efforts suggest a desire to reconcile Arts and Crafts ideology with competitive market conditions profoundly affected by mass production; to its credit, the Roycroft community remained in business throughout most of the Great Depression, only declaring bankruptcy in 1938. It is also interesting to note that both Stickley and Hubbard opposed the modern labor reform movement: deeply influenced by William Morriss attitudes toward craft, they felt that the concept of organized labor excluded a satisfying role for the individual beyond wage compensation. Fordism did not include a place for design in the general way that we have used the term in relation to craft and manufacture during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, or even the German Industry Culture model that included an aesthetic component based upon the theory of Typisierung: decision-making was directed almost exclusively toward achieving maximum efficiency for production and minimum cost for labor. Only the pressures of competition made design, in the sense of adding value to the utility of a product, an element in the companys approach. In 1927 Ford introduced its Model A in response to competition that made design as well as advertising components of corporate business practice. After all, the Model T retained the basic form inherited from the horse-drawn carriage, and there was little aesthetic concern for decorative detail, harmonious proportions, or a sense of a unified or simplified external form, as seen, for instance, in Behrenss domestic products for the AEG. But then again, Behrens was a designer, and the reform of taste was part of the strategy of Germanys Industry Culture, including product forms that communicated modern middle-class values of moderation and efficiency. Here design appeared to be the appropriate (rather than inevitable) outcome of technical considerations leading to limited standardized types that communicated production efficiency and a shared modern, restrained taste. Following World War I, the moving assembly line, machine production of interchangeable parts, and standardization were adopted by American industries, most notably in the uniform mass production of household appliances such as refrigerators, vacuum cleaners, and washing machines. As in Germany, many appliances were powered by electricity and were marketed by power companies hoping to increase domestic consumption of electric energy. Scientific Management also extended beyond the assembly line: American author Christine Frederick (18831970) studied food preparation along the lines of Taylors time-and-motion studies, applying its principles to the overall coordination of kitchen tasks, contributing to the reduction of drudgery and an increase in leisure and opportunities for other productive pursuits for women. Fredericks Scientific Management in the Home (1912) included diagrams demonstrating the most practical spatial arrangements among various tasks in the kitchen in order to save time, and her efforts became the basis for domestic kitchen design for governmentsponsored public housing in Frankfurt, Germany, in the 1920s (see here). Frederick also emphasized the personal satisfaction of making rational choices for where to store utensils or determine the height of cabinets and sinks, whether for comfort, safety, or improved efficiency. The use of white enamel paint for washing machines and refrigerators connoted personal hygiene, making the ideal kitchen or laundry a combination of well-managed factory and laboratory (figs. 7.8 and 7.9). Advertisements emphasized the practical advantages of electrical appliances, such as toasters, wafflemakers, and irons, requiring less care (just plug it in) and less frequent shopping. Aside from the white enamel finishes, manufacturers devoted little attention to aesthetics, an exception being electric coffee percolators that would have been brought to the dining room table to harmonize with tableware and dishes. Here, as historian Regina Blasyczyk explains, production economies and the division of labor led to flexibility in manufacturing that enabled standardization to co-exist rather than to replace variety and consumer choice. Store owners and managers played an important role here, not as reformers as in Germany or as tastemakers, but as observers who monitored rather than attempted to control or influence consumer preferences, participating in a feedback loop that responded to and stimulated varied and changing styles. Notwithstanding the success of scientific management for the kitchen and its promotion in magazines such as Good Housekeeping and Ladies Home Journal, the reality of the factory and office as primary locations of managed and increasingly routine work helped to create a stronger need to disassociate living space from workplace; if the factory turned men into machines and the kitchen into a scientifically managed space, for many consumers other domestic spaces functioned as a refuge and antidote to the anonymity and uniformity associated with the managed workplace. Communication through print catalogs and mass-circulation magazines extended these attitudes to small towns and rural areas as well. The distinction between home and work has been cited as the primary reason why manufacturers at first found it difficult to successfully market sewing machines for domestic use. Standardized models manufactured by Singer in the mid-nineteenth century were too reminiscent of the factory to appeal to consumers (the earliest models could be mounted on their wooden packing crates), and only by acquiring domestic associations through painted detailing in gold, a more tapered appearance, and a more traditionally carved table (fig. 7.10) did they successfully enter the home as a piece of artistic furniture rather than as an industrial tool. Rather than competing for work in the public world, women were encouraged to cultivate a comfortable and worry-free (as well as efficient) environment for their families  both were components of design, adding value and appeal to products. This role might allow for their direct participation in production via handicraft, but as primary consumers of household goods and furnishings they were increasingly targeted by advertisers. ![](images/8eefd59c5b0e93534bfd171eab30ec2e788d513185b953b5a6d5929e3d2e40f2.jpg) ## 7.10 Singer sewing machine, 1866. In addition to distinguishing home from office or factory through design, manufacturers were able to increase sales by creating variations of products targeting particular markets. These range from the protests and anti-industrial initiatives of John Ruskin and William Morris to the aggressive pursuit of rapid uniform production by Henry Ford. Between these two extremes lie the approaches of aesthetic design and reform (whether art furniture, Jugendstil, or Art Nouveau), Werkstätten, Werkbund, and other associations, and large commercial ventures such as department stores, retail chain stores, and mail-order catalogs that sought to balance the uniformity and rationalization demanded by an emerging system of industrial production with the individuality of the designer and managing motivations of a growing consumer culture. Each phenomenon constitutes a particular reaction to industrialization and mechanization, whether as a threat or a promise, the dream of providing the many with what was once the privilege of the few, of elevating aesthetic awareness of household objects and interiors, of improving the standard of living and quality of life through design allied with technology, of introducing art into everyday life, of defining the products and behaviors of an industry culture. At the same time, technology threatened the very nature of craft production, depriving workers of independence and increasing the disparity among social classes in competitive economic conditions. Various efforts toward creating an alliance of art and industry addressed these complex issues, including the well-being of workers outside of the workplace, and the role of designers, manufacturers, and consumers, all of whom were stakeholders in the design enterprise. In France, as Debora Silverman and other historians have remarked, the political milieu in which design initiatives developed at this time was a conservative (top-down) one in which the cultivation of a refined Art Nouveau style was expected to create economic opportunity as well as to reduce middleclass fears of unrest among an industrial working class. The multi-faceted design enterprise greatly expanded during this period; artist-designers, craftsman-artists, tastemakers, the early professional industrial designers such as Christopher Dresser, manufacturers and retailers such as Woolworth, all believed in the economic, social, and political significance of the decorative arts; all debated the uses and applications of technology, the role of labor, the relationship between fine and applied art, and even more importantly the meanings and definitions of progress. The emergence of new nations (Italy in 1861; Germany in 1870) and efforts toward national independence in Eastern Europe and Scandinavia also contributed to an appreciation of the economic and political benefits of a unified design identity. The social, economic, and political meanings brought to bear upon design activities in the period from the mid-nineteenth century until the beginning of World War I make this period one of great vitality, originality, and tension, in which there emerges a critical dialogue between art, craft, industry, social and international relations and reform, and an ever-expanding culture of commodity consumption. With expanding trade and travel, artists and designers during this time drew inspiration from myriad sources historically and culturally. Their interaction with other cultures, whether in Africa or Asia, was sparked by curiosity, by travel, and by exposure through books, collections, and displays on view in museums and at exhibitions. Western attitudes toward non-Western cultures were complex: on the one hand, appreciating the authenticity of pre-industrial cultures, but, on the other hand, generally revealing bias that cast differences in skin color, religion, and lifestyles as primitive, that is, backward technologically, and at a less well-developed stage of civilization. This cultural bias was contemporary with imperial and colonial aggression, sanctioned to promote progress globally. Part III of the Global Inspiration looks at examples of the decorative arts of Central and West Africa in relation to cultural as well as commercial interactions. Exported African textiles were commodified, disrupting traditional, local patterns of production and consumption, surviving, yet subject to a changing relationship with the modern world. At the same time, Western colonialism fostered varieties of racial and cultural attitudes that combined commercial opportunity with nostalgic longing for the primitive (as displayed, for instance, in ethnographic villages at Worlds Fairs) and revealing prejudices that persist in our own time. # Global Inspiration III Central and Western Africa The third installment of Global Inspiration looks at the decorative arts of Central and Western Africa (the north of Africa was treated in Global Inspiration II). Centuries before Europeans began sailing around the western coast of Africa to trade, they were already in contact with African raw materials and goods and design. There would have been no ivories and very little gold in medieval Europe were it not for the extensive medieval overland trade with West Africa (modern-day Senegal, Niger, and Nigeria) via the kingdom of Mali. People in Ilé-Ifè· were skilled metalworkers, glassworkers, and sculptors, producing highly realistic portraits and glass beads, at a moment when very few Europeans could make anything comparable. Contact between Central Africa and Western Europe began with the arrival of Portuguese traders, merchants, and Catholic missionaries toward the end of the fifteenth century. Scholars describe the early trading interactions between cultures as respectful, and wealthy Europeans (armchair travelers or their agents) collected examples of the continents material culture for their Kunstkammer (cabinets of curiosity) to satisfy their interest and display their worldliness. Many African leaders and the inhabitants of the areas they controlled converted to Christianity while maintaining traditional tribal allegiances and craft practices. Gifts by African leaders played a role in this interaction, demonstrating pride in highly developed local traditions of ivory carving, jewelry, and textile manufacture, while merchants exchanged cloth, metals, and arms for African goods and agreements that provided access to raw materials. Prominent among the material products of Africa reaching Europe were woven and printed textiles worn for dress and as caps; these often employed a central schematized knot or diamond motif that formed the basis of staggered or repeated motifs on square shapes that were sometimes sown together. But European attitudes toward cultural difference shifted from curiosity to exploitation with an active trade in enslaved African people that brought Africans (estimated at 20 million during a 400-year period) to Latin America and the Caribbean to provide labor for sugar and coffee plantations and mining (silver, copper, iron, and other metals) from the later sixteenth century, with human beings (rather than luxury goods) as the objects of exchange. Artistic evidence of the slave trade survives in nineteenth-century carved ivory tusks with spiraling bands of relief depicting various aspects of subservience in the slave trade, produced for export to Europe and displayed at the Kongo: Majesty and Power exhibition at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York, in 2015. Despite the abolition of the transatlantic trade of enslaved African people by the USA and several European states in the early nineteenth century, illegal activity continued until the 1870s. Meanwhile trading in raw materials and manufactured goods continued to accelerate with improved transportation via railways and steamships. Imperialism and exploitation figured prominently in African history during this time, affecting trade (and by extension, design). Over the course of the nineteenth century virtually all of Central Africa was subject to European control as colonies with a minority European ruling class as settlers. In most places imperialism continued until well after World War II, and, in South Africa, the system of racial segregation and inequality known as Apartheid, established in 1948, lasted until 1994. Agreements among France, Belgium, and Portugal, enacted between 1884 and 1885 (the Berlin Conference), divided Central African territories into the arbitrary states of Angola (Portugal), Moyen Congo (France), and the Congo Free State (King Leopold II of Belgium). Increased contact with Europe produced changes in local African craft traditions. In South Africa local weavers (men and women) incorporated glass beads into their woven fabrics, received in trade with merchants from Italy and Bohemia. Entrepreneurial traders increased, for example, Englishman Charles Beving (18581913) collected examples of printed and woven textiles from Africa as well as Indonesia as an agent for Manchester-based (UK) industrial manufacturers (he also co-owned a printing company). His extensive collection, now in the British Museum, London, offers evidence of an active global trade in manufactured goods. A further example of European manufacturers fascination with African textiles, in this case still active, is Dutch company Vlisco, founded in 1846 in the city of Helmond. Vlisco first adapted the patterns and techniques of Indonesian (Javanese) Batik printing (using a wax resist) to roller-printing technology. When Indonesian craftsmen and women improved the efficiency of their own printing techniques to compete with Vliscos products, the company turned to West Africa (Ghana, Ivory Coast, Burkina Faso) as a new market. Imperialism and colonialism rested upon the belief in racial hierarchy that justified the mining of natural resources as cultural progress and a Western, white, and Eurocentric bias that equated civilization with technological development as a universal and inevitable goal. As a result, in some sense to be different was to be inferior, even as the term primitive or savage also connoted a closeness to nature and instinct admired, even envied, in the West for being authentic and in some sense desirable when embodied in craft objects. As noted above, objects of African craft were exported in large quantities to Europe, and geometric textile patterns and other decorative motifs were appropriated by European industrial manufacturers for their own markets as well as directed to the African market. As historian Debora Silverman states, Belgian artist and designer Henry van de Veldes belief in the equality of the arts were in part informed by his interest in the African practice of scarification, in particular ways in which patterns carved into the skin of Congolese natives were naturally adapted to bone structure in the way ornament might be integrated with the construction of a chair or the trunk and branches of a candelabra (see fig. 6.45). ## Appropriation and Cultural Interaction The Bloomsbury Encyclopedia of Design defines the term appropriation as the deliberate act of using preexisting sources such as objects, images, or any cultural element to recontextualize them with little or no modification into commercial products or new artistic creations. This definition is useful, but does not quite fit the examples illustrated here, since original African designs were certainly modified or transformed either through different production processes or to market such products to different audiences. But the important part of the definition is the word re-contextualization, because it affects meaning both for the borrower and for the receiver. The appeal of Vlisco dress fabrics reproduced with Javanese printing techniques and motifs to West Africans, or of African geometric patterns to British consumers, makes light of cultural difference in the interests of commerce as the generator of value. Does appropriation of this kind constitute exploitation? One might argue that African and Indonesian artists were not asked for permission to appropriate their creative effort or given credit or remuneration for their designs when reproduced, and that appropriation and broader availability weakened the political functions and social meanings that African textiles had for their local communities as signs of authority, social distinction, or ritual function. In these cases the engine of appropriation is free trade and competition, which generally (whether East or West) erodes traditional social structures through the less restricted circulation of goods, even at the expense of workers independence and livelihoods, often forcing them to compete amid changing circumstances, altering the conditions in which production, and business, are conducted. A more disturbing instance of the unequal power relationship between Europe and Africa under colonialism emerges in the research of historian Debora Silverman, who examined Belgian Art Nouveau as the visual expression of colonial repression and violence in the Congo Free State in the 1890s (Belgian Congo after 1908; Democratic Republic of Congo since 1960). The Congo was a source of natural rubber, a plentiful and lucrative export used for a wide variety of products including hoses and bicycle tires (bicycle riding had become a popular form of transportation and leisure in the 1890s with the introduction of pneumatic tires around 1890). Abundant rubber was extracted from the forests of the Congo by brutally enforced labor to meet harsh production quotas in lieu of unfair tax burdens imposed upon the native population. What we now call Belgian Art Nouveau was in some instances known at the time as Congo Style. Belgian artists incorporated abstract interpretations of rubber-tree stalks in the furniture, furnishings, and architectural decoration of the Congo Pavilion at the International Exhibition held in Brussels and Tervuren in 1897, and the taut, so-called whiplash linear decoration seen in Henry van de Veldes 1902 candelabra (see above, fig. 6.45) is not only an expressive aesthetic abstraction or exaggeration of a vegetal or floral form, but also a reference to the public floggings that were used to punish and control Congolese rubber workers, the price of progress and the attending wealth it brought to Belgian industrialists. Ivory from elephant tusks was another Central African natural resource, and was regularly employed in sculpture and the decorative arts. A reference to its elephantine source appears in the large central and side panels of a stained-glass window designed by architect Octave van Rysselberghe (18551929) and Van de Velde for the home of Paul Otlet in Ixelles, southeast of Brussels in 1898 (fig. GI3.5). In the panel, one can detect the symmetrical frontal outline of an elephant skull along with its curved tusks in the center and half-heart-shaped ears that extend outward in the side panels. As revealed by Silverman, Belgian Art Nouveau offers explicit evidence of appropriation-as-exploitation, embodied in imagery and tense, dynamic energy, acknowledging power and the violence that lay at the heart of Western imperialism and the objectification of populations in the rush for resources and wealth.
