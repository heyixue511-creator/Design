# INTRODUCTION The Kelmscott Press is probably the most influential and certainly the most famous of all the private presses. Founded in 1891 as a ‘typographical adventure’(Morris to William Bowden, 3 January 1891 [PML]), the Press was the last major undertaking by William Morris in his remarkable career as a designer and reviver of traditional crafts. By emphasising the fundamental importance of excellent ink and paper (the latter hand-made), well-designed typefaces (which he himself created), and handsome ornaments and illustrations, Morris reinvigorated the book arts and produced a series of fifty-two books that are still widely admired and collected. After his death in 1896, most of the books already in production were completed, but the Press finally closed in the spring of 1898. This bibliography describes the published works of the Kelmscott Press, books that were contemplated but were not actually printed, and advertising circulars and other ephemera; a final section supplies the texts of eight contracts and memoranda for Kelmscott books. Since the history of the Kelmscott Press has been told so many times (though not always accurately), in this introduction I will touch only upon certain points of special interest, drawing as much as possible on primary sources such as diaries and letters. ## I.SOURCES The chief source of information about the Kelmscott Press is Sydney (later Sir Sydney) C. Cockerell, second Secretary of the Press. Cockerell wrote the brief official history of the Press, compiled a checklist of its publications, preserved many documents that might otherwise have been lost, and kept a detailed diary during the years in which the Press existed. Cockerell's A Short History and Description of the Kelmscott Press’and An Annotated List of All the Books Printed at the Kelmscott Press in the Order in Which They Were Issued', both of which appeared in the last volume (As3) produced at the Press, are the foundations on which many later writers have built. (That volume also contained A Note by William Morris on his Aims in Founding the Kelmscott Press'.) Another history of the Press with special authority is The Kelmscott Press and William Morris Master-Craftsman (1924) by H. Halliday Sparling, who was (briefly) Morris's son-in-law and the first Secretary of the Press. Though ampler than Cockerell's treatment, Sparling's book does not carry the same intellectual weight and is weakened by the author's eagerness to demonstrate that he is ‘an eager and adoring disciple’of Morris (as he phrases it in his preface). A more obscure, but nevertheless significant, source is a lecture by Frank Colebrook, editor of the Printing Times and Lithographer, entitled William Morris: Master Printer (Tunbridge Wells, [?1897]), much of which is based on the recollections of William H. Bowden, overseer of Morris's printers, and others connected with the Kelmscott Press. Among more recent treatments of the Press, the most important is William Morris and the Art of the Book (New York, 1976), which grew out of an exhibition at the Pierpont Morgan Library of the Morris collection of John M. Crawford, Jr. (The immense Crawford collection is now owned by the Morgan Library.) The volume, however, exceeds one's usual expectations of a catalogue: aside from other riches, it contains essays on Morris as a book collector by Paul Needham, on Morris as a calligrapher by Joseph Dunlap, and on Morris as a typoggrapher by John Dreyfus. Another catalogue of value, with excellent notes by Ronald Briggs, is The Typographical Adventure of William Morris, which records an exhibition sponsored by the William Morris Society in 1958. Harry Buxton Forman's The Books of William Morris (1897) offers, rather unsystematically, a good deal of miscellaneous information about the Kelmscott books written by Morris. William Morris in Private Press and Limited Editions: A Descriptive Bibliography of Books by and about William Morris, 1891–1981 (Phoenix, Ariz., 1983), which is a bibliographical catalogue rather than a true descriptive bibliography, records the excellent private collection of John J. Peterson (Berkeley, 1982). An appendix reprints four interviews with Morris about his work as a printer. The first volume of a three-volume set of Morris's collected correspondence, edited by Norman Kelvin, is scheduled to appear in 1984. Joseph Dunlap, in an unpublished doctoral thesis (Columbia University, 1972), has examined ‘The Road to Kelmscott: William Morris and the Book Arts before the Founding of the Kelmscott Press'. His major conclusions have been published in an article in Victorian Poetry, 13 (Fall–Winter, 1975), 141–57. One of the significant aspects of Morris's pre-Kelmscott Press activities was his interest in calligraphy: see the essay by Dunlap in the Morgan catalogue, cited above, and Alfred Fairbank, A Note on the Manuscript Work of William Morris’ in The Story of Kormak the Son of Ogmund, translated by Morris and Eiríkr Magnússon (1970). In American Book Design and William Morris (New York, 1977) Susan O. Thompson has explored Morris's immense and previously unassessed transatlantic influence. Among other specialised studies one might cite Colin Franklin, On the Binding of Kelmscott Press Books’, Journal of the William Morris Society, 2 (Summer, 1978), 28–30, and John Dreyfus, New Light on the Design of Types for the Kelmscott and Doves Presses’, The Library, 5th Ser., 29 (March 1974), 36–41. On the subject of the Kelmscott Press Press types, Thomas Balston's The Cambridge University Collection of Private Press Types (Cambridge, 195I), pp. 10-19, is also instructive. Morris's correspondence with his papermaker is published in Sydney Ward, William Morris and His Papermaker, Joseph Batchelor', Philobiblon, 7 (1934), 177-8o, and in Henry Morris, A Visit to Hayle Mill (North Hills, Pa., 197o), pp. 22–45. May Morris's recollections of her father's activities at the Press are to be found in an article, Reminiscences about My Father', Philobiblon, 7 (1934), 169–72, and in her introductions to his collected works, which have been separately reprinted (New York, 1973). At his death Morris owned a large collection of incunabula and reference books about printing and typographical design: the fullest list of his books is in the Sotheby auction catalogue, 5-1o December 1898. Similarly, a catalogue of Cockerell's Kelmscott Press collection (Sotheby, 1o December 19s6) provides an invaluable list of proofs, trial pages, and association copies of Kelmscott books that have since been widely dispersed. Unpublished materials relating to the Press are abundant, again emanating mainly from Cockerell. A letterbook of the Press, covering the years 1894–6, is in the William Morris Gallery. The Morgan Library owns a ledger for 1892-6 and a list of customers to whom announcements were sent in 1897; an earlier account book for 1891 is in the private collection of Yoshiaki Morisawa. A volume labelled ‘Specimens'(BL C.43.h.5) is an indexed collection of ephemera, title-pages, and colophons that was used at the Press. The Huntington Library owns a substantial collection of invoices from W. H. Hooper, C. E. Keates, and others addressed to the Press. A group of enlarged photographs of fifteenthcentury types used by Morris as an aid in designing his own types is in the St. Bride Printing Library. TYPES Except for a few passages in Selwyn Image's Greek type in Atalanta (A25), Morris used only three typefaces in the Kelmscott Press books, all designed by himself: the Golden type (a roman English), the Troy type (a blackletter Great Primer), and the Chaucer type (a blackletter Pica). All three were engraved by Edward P. Prince and were cast at the Fann Street Foundry of Sir Charles Reed & Son. The inspiration to design a roman face came to Morris during an illustrated lecture by Emery Walker at the Arts and Crafts Exhibition on 15 November 1888. His chief models were a type used by Jenson in Pliny's Historia Naturale (Venice, 1476) and a similar but somewhat heavier type used by Jacobus Rubeus in Leonardus Brunus Aretinus's Historia Florentina (Venice, 1476). Walker made a series of photographic enlargements of pages from these and other books, which Morris traced and adapted before producing his own freehand drawings. (The photographs, enlarged to a scale of five times the original, are now in the St. Bride Printing Library.) Morris called the resulting design ‘the regenerate type or Jenson-Morris’ and was pleased when Walter Crane complained that it was more gothic than roman (Mackail, ii, 262, 268). The Golden type was cut during 189o, and it was first used in a trial page for The Story of the Glittering Plain (Aī), set on 31 January 189ı. The lower-case g was recut almost immediately. During the latter half of 189ı Prince engraved what Morris described as a‘semi-Gothic type designed . . . with special regard to legibility’(Morris to Bernard Quaritch, I June 1892 [BQ]). Though the Troy type was a freer adaptation of its models than was the Golden type, the St. Bride photographs show that Morris drew heavily upon a type used by Peter Schoeffer in his Bible of 1462 and a type used by Gunther Zainer in a book printed in Augsburg in 1473. Morris's capital I was found to be unsatisfactory and had to be recut. Although early specimens of the Troy type were passages from Chaucer and Froissart, the first book in which it appeared was The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye (A8), issued in December 1892. It was clear to Morris that for the edition of Chaucer he needed a smaller version of the Troy type. Walker therefore made for him a series of photographic reductions of passages from Chaucer and The Story of the Glittering Plain set in Troy type (BL C.102.h.18). The Chaucer type was engraved in early 1892 and was first used for the table of contents and glossary of The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye. It first appeared as a text type in The Order of Chivalry (A13), issued in April 1893. A specimen-chart of the three types (WMA B, Pl. LxxvIII) shows that for each of them Morris designed the following characters: twenty-six capitals, twenty-six lower-case letters, ten figures, seven ligatures, an ampersand, parentheses, seven marks of punctuation, and a hyphen (with an alternative, oblique hyphen supplied for the Troy and Chaucer types). Morris also designed several ornaments for each fount. He did not, however, allow himself the use of italics, small capitals, boldface, brackets, superior figures, or dashes. In November 1892 Morris designed a lower-case alphabet (WMA B, Pl. Lxxvıı) based on the type used by Sweynheym and Pannartz – intermediate between blackletter and roman – in De civitate Dei (Subiaco, 1467), but he did not complete it. After the Kelmscott Press closed, five lectures by Morris were printed at the Chiswick Press for Longmans in the Golden type, and there was a proposal from Longmans, never acted upon, for a uniform edition of Morris's works in the Golden type (Cockerell diary, 3ı May 19oo), though seven volumes in Golden type were published by Longmans early in this century. In 1926 Cockerell, as Morris's Trustee, transferred the Kelmscott types, matrices, and punches from the Chiswick Press to the Cambridge University Press (Cockerell to May Morris, 18 January 1926 [BL Add. Ms. 52740, f. 118]). In 1940, again through Cockerell's intervention, the CUP purchased them (Thomas Balston, The Cambridge University Press Collections of Private Press Types [Cambridge, 1951], p. v). ## 3. For The Story of the Glittering Plain (Aı) Morris used vellum left over from his earlier calligraphic experiments; thereafter the suppliers of vellum to the Press were Henry Band of Brentford, Middlesex, and William J. Turney & Co. of Stourbridge, Worcestershire. William H. Bowden has put on record a detailed description of how the vellum copies were printed at the Kelmscott Press: 'In the first place, it is necessary that the Vellum should have just a little more than a suspicion of dampness (not enough to make the sheet cockle), and this is best got by the following method: – Well wet down half a ream of good stout white paper (equivalent in weight to 6o lb. demy). This should be well turned for about 3 or 4 days, and kept in damp cellar. 'When the Vellums are to be printed, this paper should be brought out, and a skin put in about the middle; a fat board should then be placed on top, and, on top of that, a ream of paper to act as a weight. Only put in one skin at a time. After the skin has been between the damp sheets from 3o to 45 seconds, according to the thickness of the skins, it should be then printed at once, and not waved in the air more than can be helped. After printing, the skins should be interleaved with dry paper, (and not kept damp), and the same process gone through in printing the re-iteration. This allows of perfect register being got. Print the rough side of the Vellum first, on the inner forme. This is important, as the skins spoil on the rough side more frequently than on the smooth. 'Always print one more Vellum than the number required, in case of accident; and if there are any heavy borders or larger initials, two should be pulled. This will be found in the end to be a great saving. As hardly two skins are of the same thickness, the Pressman must use judgment as to the impression. The impression should be rather dwelt on. It is best to work off both sides of the skin in black first, before printing the red, as red ink has a great tendency to run on a damp skin. The red should be printed when the skin is quite dry' (wMG). ## 4. COPYTEXTS Historians of printing and book-design have paid a good deal of attention to the aesthetic qualities of Kelmscott books, but remarkably little has been written about the ambitious publishing programme initiated by Morris in 1891. The books printed at the Kelmscott Press fell mainly into three categories: Morris's own works, mediaeval texts, and standard classics of English literature. Many of Morris's choices were also adopted by later private presses, thus testifying to yet another aspect of his influence. Morris of course proofread his own works (though he probably did not read against copy) and frequently introduced minor revisions. The evidence of the surviving proofs suggests that in most cases he did not proofread the other books, except quite perfunctorily, though he always indicated on the proofs what initials and borders were to be used. Many of the books were edited by F. S. Ellis, who took his responsibilities very seriously and often consulted Morris about textual problems, especially in the mediaeval works. Despite Ellis's strenuous efforts, however, scholars (some of whom are quoted in the NOTEs sections) have generally concluded that the KP texts fall short of the more exacting modern editorial standards. This is hardly surprising, since Morris and Ellis were aiming at a readable text of broad literary appeal rather than literary accuracy. (They did not hesitate, for example, to improve on Caxton's punctuation, though they retained his spelling.) Nevertheless, the care with which they printed literary documents, some of them previously unpublished, is remarkable; the Kelmscott Press was, among other things, a pioneering attempt at intelligent popularisation of literary works that in some instances were drawn from obscure sources. Other publishers such as Quaritch and David Nutt were eager to print mediaeval texts because of their antiquarian appeal, but Morris, to whom the Middle Ages were an immediate reality, published such texts at the Kelmscott Press because of their vivid literary charm. At Emery Walker's suggestion, Morris briefly tried an ink manufactured by Gebrüder Jänecke of Hannover, which, though satisfactory in other respects, was found to be too stiff for the comfort of his printers. However, during 1895 yellow stains began to appear in the sheets of the Chaucer (A4o) then being printed. (See William S. Peterson, William Morris and the "Damned Chemists": The Search for an Ideal Ink at the Kelmscott Press', Printing History, 3, no. 2 [1981], 4-11.) With great difficulty Morris succeeded in eradicating the stains by bleaching the sheets in the sun, but the unhappy experience persuaded him that he would have to switch to the Jänecke ink. According to Walker, . . . Morris read the riot act and said if they [his printers] wouldn't use the German ink he would close the Press. As his pressmen were very well paid they naturally did not want to lose their jobs and gave way. However, they took it out of Morris another way and the number of sheets turned out per day were extraordinarily small' (Walker to John Johnson, 2o April 1931, quoted in WMA B, p. 82; this letter is in JC). On 19 August 1895 Morris wrote to Walker, The Hanover ink was tried on Friday and worked well: I shall use no other now' (The Letters of William Morris to His Family and Friends, ed. Philip Henderson [1950], p. 374). Early in the Press's history Morris carried out an experiment in the application of ink to the formes: in the John Johnson Collection of the Bodleian Library are two proofs of the first page of The Story of the Glittering Plain (Ar), one marked Composition Roller' and the other Litho. roller'. Various experiments with red and blue inks are also extant. Morris bought his red ink, at least initially, from Shackell, Edwards, though Bernard Newdigate claimed – based on Walker's recollections – that both the red (vermillion) and blue (ultramarine ash) were supplied by Winsor and Newton of London (‘Mr. C. H. St. John Hornby's Ashendene Press’, Fleuron, no. 2 [1924], 78). The red ink tended to spread on the dampened sheets at the Kelmscott Press, and the pressmen therefore always worked the red last whenever possible (Colebrook, P. 33). ## 6.ILLUSTRATIONS The principal wood-engraver for the Kelmscott Press was William Harcourt Hooper, Morris's neighbour in Hammersmith, who came out of retirement to work for the Press. He frequently irritated Morris by his principle of exact copying' (Morris to Walker, 2 May 1892 [RC]). For the Chaucer (A4o) and some of the other Kelmscott books illustrated by Burne-Jones, Morris also found it necessary to enlist the talents of Robert Catterson-Smith, afterwards Headmaster of the Birmingham School of Art. In a letter to May Morris, 6 June 1922 (BL Add. Ms. 45347, f. 176), Walker wrote out a detailed account of how the Chaucer illustrations were produced. Burne-Jones, he said, did his drawings in silver point or hard pencil, on approximately the same scale as the woodblocks were to be engraved. These drawings then had to be modified and translated in order to harmonize with the Chaucer type being used in the book. At first Charles Fairfax Murray undertook this translation, but, according to Walker (exaggerating perhaps), it was three months before he completed one drawing. Morris therefore turned to Catterson-Smith. Walker's processengraving firm photographed Burne-Jones's drawings and made two very pale photographic prints (platinos’) of each of them. Catterson-Smith mounted one of the prints on a piece of cardboard and covered it with a slight wash of Chinese white, so that the drawing was just barely visible. He then drew over Burne-Jones's lines with a brush; before this revised drawing was transferred photographically to the surface of the woodblock and engraved by Hooper, Burne-Jones and Catterson-Smith discussed it at length. Walker argued, in fact, that the collaboration was similar to that in which earlier woodengravers had made alterations in a design; the only difference was that in this case a third person had stepped into the traditional role of the engraver. Walker said that Burne-Jones told him the cooperation between himself and Catterson-Smith, especially as the work was so perfect that increasingly Burne-Jones thought of his assistant as a tool in his hand. Catterson-Smith has also left a record of his procedures: Emery Walker made a very pale print of a photograph (a platino) from Sir E.B.J's pencil drawing – the exact size of the drawing – I then stuck the print down on stout card board, and, in order to avoid the expansion of the paper I put the paste on the card board first and then applied the paper print very quickly, so quickly that it had no time to absorb moisture and so expand, then I immediately ran a hot smoothing-iron over it which at once dried the paste. Next I gave the print a thin even wash of chinese white with a little size in it. The result was to get rid of everything but the essential lines: Next I went over the pale lines with a very sharp pencil copying and translating them from the B-J. drawing which was in front of me. The lines of shading were put in pencil. These shadow lines were very difficult as they had to be translations of very grey pencil tones, and they often took a long time to fit into these spaces. When the pencil drawing was finished all trace of the photograph had disappeared. Next came the inking over which was done with a fine round sable brush and very black chinese ink which I bought in bottles. By putting a little size in the Chinese white, as above mentioned, the ink was not absorbed by the white and so remained jet black – otherwise it would have become grey -. To get a good brush for the purpose was not easy. I spread the hairs fan-wise and look[ed] with a magnifying glass to see were there any irregular hairs, if there were I cut them out with a razor and so got an even point. When difficulties arose in the treatment of passages I consulted B-J. Sometimes work by Albert Durer was consulted to see how he had dealt with passages. ORNAMENTS The Kelmscott Press borders and initials, all designed by Morris, were also engraved by Hooper, assisted by C. E. Keates and W. Spielmeyer. Cockerell recalled, in a letter to May Morris, 1o February 1936, how they were drawn: Your father had two little saucers in front of him. He roughed out his design in pencil – then drew it carefully with his black brush, correcting with white & then again with black as he went on, & using white over the black for various little details – but he only, I think, worked on solid black (as sometimes happened) he had to black out a largish part of a design because he was not satisfied with it'(BL Add. All of the ornaments were printed from electrotypes after Walker persuaded Morris that a group of electrotyped initials was virtually indistinguishable from the same initials printed from the original blocks. Morris's normal procedure for designing a border was to draw it directly on a proof of the text or on a piece of paper that was then pasted down on a proof or trial page. All or part of the page was photographed onto the surface of a woodblock by Walker & Boutall, but of course only the border was engraved. The initials were frequently engraved in groups on a single woodblock and were not separated until they had been electrotyped. Morris occasionally experimented with hand-coloured initials, but no initials were so coloured at the Press. In 1897 the blocks of all the Kelmscott Press ornaments and illustrations were given to the British Museum. ## 8. CANCELS The most difficult kind of cancel to detect is that in which an entire gathering, or a pair of conjugate leaves, has been reprinted (see Fredson Bowers, Principles of Bibliographical Description [Princeton, 1949], pp. 234-5). The cancels in the Kelmscott Press books are, with one notable exception (A17), of this type. In the numerous collections of proofs and trial pages assembled by Cockerell, there is considerable evidence of corrections and improvements being made in the Kelmscott books as they passed through the press; and in many instances Cockerell has written‘Cancelled' on leaves that were rejected. Librarians, auction firms, and rare book dealers, enthusiastically following Cockerell's example, have labelled as cancels’Kelmscott Press trial pages, proofs, and retrees. Such confusion is not surprising, since in practice the several categories of discarded leaves and sheets appear to merge almost imperceptibly into one another. At one end of the spectrum is the trial page, of which only a few copies were struck off, used in designing a book. Usually (but not always) a trial page was printed on one side only as a leaf. Once the printing began, Morris inspected the first copy of each sheet (which he regarded as a proof), and if he was dissatisfied, the printers made alterations before any more sheets were printed. An excellent example of this sort of stop-press correction is the different text of 'La Belle Dame sans Merci’which was substituted in The Poems of John Keats (A24). Next, there are the few instances in which we know that a substantial number of sheets (probably all of them) were printed before an error was discovered, and a cancellandum (always a full sheet or at least conjugate leaves) was produced. The reprinting of a pair of leaves (d4.5) in the Chaucer (A4o) is the most unambigious example of this type of cancellation. Finally, in one peculiar case (Ar7), individual cancel leaves were printed, but six weeks after the publication of the book, and I have so far found no copy in which the cancellantia do in fact appear. When confronted with a leaf marked ‘Cancelled’by Cockerell, one wonders whether it is a true cancel (i.e., was it reprinted after all the sheets of that gathering had gone through the press?) or merely a first sheet (i.e., did Morris find something objectionable in it and ininsist upon alterations before the rest of the sheets were printed?). Cockerell did not distinguish between these two categories; yet the first is a cancel, and the second is not. I have therefore chosen to interpret the evidence as conservatively as possible: in only four cases (A2, Aı7, A4o, and A54) did it seem to me beyond any reasonable doubt that cancellation had in fact taken place. BINDINGS Each Kelmscott book was bound in either quarter linen or full vellum by J. and J. Leighton of London. (The Chaucer [A4o] was also available in a special pigskin binding designed by Morris and executed at the Doves Bindery.) One peculiarity of the books may be summarized in this pronouncement by Cobden-Sanderson (whose ideas much influenced the Kelmscott binding style): ‘The end-papers . . . should . . . not be pasted on; they also should be sewn with the rest of the book. And for this purpose additional sections of plain paper should be supplied to the binder by the publisher to be bound up at the beginning and end of the volume’(Bookbinding: Lecture by T. J. Cobden-Sanderson', in Transactions of the National Association for the Advancement of Art and Its Application to Industry [189o], p. 467). Occasionally the first or last leaf of an ordinary gathering was made to serve as a pastedown. The quarter linen style Morris regarded as ‘a satisfactory temporary binding' (Morris to Bernard Quaritch, 25 July [1891] [BQ]), though in fact the majority of Kelmscott Press books have not been rebound. On the spines of the vellum bindings Morris often used enlarged versions of the Golden and Troy types.. . . Mr. Morris has come to the conclusion that the black-letter capitals do not look so well as those of the “Golden"type, & he will not use them for lettering on the covers in future,' Cockerell wrote to Fred J. Hall on 22 October 1894 (LB). The silk ties used on nearly all of the limp vellum bindings were supplied by Morris & Co. (They are, incidentally, the most fragile part of the Kelmscott books and are broken on many copies.) The colour of the ties is not specified in this bibliography, since a single colour was not adopted for all copies of a book. Dull green, reddish brown, and gold were the most common colours. ## IO. STAFF The size of Morris's printing staff fluctuated according to how many books were in production; it probably reached its maximum in 1895 and early 1896 when the Chaucer was approaching completion. A photograph taken at the Wayzgoose in September 1895 shows eleven printers (Fig. 5). The first one hired was William Bowden, a retired master-printer, who was soon joined by his daughter Mrs Pine and his son William H. Bowden, who became the overseer (Sparling, p. 73). In addition there were Sparling (the first Secretary of the Press, 1891- 4), Cockerell (the second Secretary, 1894-8), Mrs Sarah A. Peddie (Cockerell's secretary), and Emery Walker (unofficial adviser). For a time Morris hired a professional printer's reader (Sparling, p. 85). But Cockerell turned out to be an excellent proofreader, and he, Sparling, and F. S. Ellis seem to have carried most of the burden of proofing. One anecdote by Cockerell is worth recording: When I read the proofs for him [Morris] I often used to point out things, which he altered (as a rule) as a matter of course. On one occasion I remember an outburst & a grinding of teeth, & then the alteration after a few minutes, & a confession at Gatti's [restaurant] the same evening by way of amends to myself. But this was over a sentence that he had designed on the first page of the Chaucer, & his irritation was natural as some words had to be taken out & designed again'(Cockerell to May Morris, 19 January 1913 [BL Add. PUBLISHERS Morris was at first content to allow either Reeves & Turner - ‘the most unbusiness-like publishers but extremely honest', as Jane Morris described them in a letter to Wilfrid Scawen Blunt, 2o April 1892 (FM[1]) - or Bernard Quaritch publish the Kelmscott Press books. However, a growing disenchantment with Quaritch's sharp business practices persuaded him to publish as well as print the books. (See NOTEs for A7 and A4o.) ‘Mr. W. Morris wishes to have all the profits of the Kelmscott press,’Quaritch complained in a letter to his son, 8 February 1893; he will retail himself the [“]Godfrey of Bouloyne" [Aı5] – he will be the loser by it, but it will be a nuisance to me on account of the Chaucer'(Bod. The Chaucer (A4o) in particular caused Morris a great deal of financial anxiety until he increased the pressrun from 325 to 425 copies on paper. ## I4. FINANCES The Kelmscott Press was in effect an amusing diversion for Morris (though some very serious ideas lay behind its founding), and he seems to have been astonished that it did not cost him any more than it did. It is difficult to determine whether the Press realised a profit, because Morris was not paid for his contributions, which were essential to the Press's success, and because, especially in the early years, the bookkeeping methods were haphazard. The situation improved slightly when Cockerell took over as Secretary in July 1894: In future, we may be able to keep a closer record of expenditures than itherto,' he wrote to A. J. R. Gooderson, the Press's accountant, on 6 August 1894 (L B). But Morris's attitude towards accountants was one of undisguised hostility. I keep fifteen clerks doing my accounts & yet I cannot find out how much money I have got,' he complained (Mackail notebook [wMG J163], p. 134). When he sent Walter Crane his share of the profits from The Story of the Glittering Plain (A22) on 17 February 1894, he promised another cheque as soon as he could ‘get an account made out which I shall take care is not too much humbugged by the accountant, who (any of them) could easily make out that there was no profit at all. But I will see that it is all fair, as I really know what the proit is effectually’(privately printed by William P. Barlow, Jr., 1975). Hence any generalisations about the Press's finances must be made with extreme caution. Yet the evidence does suggest that on most of the books Morris made a modest profit, though again it must be emphasised that this profit was possible only because Morris contributed his priceless services gratis. ## I5. EXPLANATION OF THIS BIBLIOGRAPHY The bibliography is based upon, and is intended to supersede, Sydney Cockerell's An Annotated List of All the Books Printed at the Kelmscott Press in the Order in Which they Were Issued'(see A53). Certain pieces of information in Section A are taken over intact from the List': the numbers assigned to the borders by Cockerell, the number of copies printed, prices, and dates of publication. To do an independent analysis of the Kelmscott Press borders would be a major undertaking; I have made only a few spot checks on Cockerell's numbering system, and I have found him unfailingly accurate. The number of copies printed was frequently slightly higher than the‘official' figure given by Cockerell, but both this and the date of publication are, in a sense, arbitrary numbers. It seemed better to retain Cockerell's figures rather than to emend some of them very slightly. For the sake of consistency, I have always treated the printed title rather than the engraved one as the ‘real'title. Morris himself is inconsistent: sometimes he seems to regard the printed title as merely a half-title, and at other times he clearly intends it to be the main title. I have not described the engraved titles in each case because they are all fundamentally the same: the lettering is against a floral or vine background and is surrounded by a border. In supplying collational formulas I have tried to follow the rules enunciated in Fredson Bowers's Principles of Bibliographical Description, though I have been reluctant to label certain leaves as cancels except in those cases where the evidence of cancellation is unmistakable (see above). The measurements given of pages and text areas are only approximate and will vary slightly from one copy of a book to another. The collations and measurements given are for paper rather than vellum copies. For each book I have examined a minimum of four copies: those in the Bodleian Library, the British Library, the Library of Congress, and the Library of Trinity College, Cambridge. For books with unusual features I have examined additional copies. Titles and half-titles of books are understood to be in the same type as the text unless otherwise indicated. I have recorded only the bindings in which the Kelmscott books were originally issued. Because of typographical limitations of the machine on which this book is typeset, oblique rather than vertical rules are used to indicate line-divisions. Since Morris never employed the oblique rule as a mark of punctuation, this should cause no confusion. In the NoTES section for each book I have drawn upon unpublished letters and diaries, published sources, trial pages, and proofs. I have not systematically searched for reviews of the Kelmscott books but have cited the few that I know about. For the RELATED MATERIALS sections I have sent a form letter to major British and American libraries and have searched reference works such as Book Auction Records and American Book Prices Current; but of course I am aware that this section can never be complete, particularly since much of the relevant material is probably in private collections. For items located in catalogues, I have given only the last reference found except when I was also able to cite the important catalogue of Cockerell's collection (Sotheby, 1o December 1956). Presentation copies - which are always from Morris unless otherwise indicated – are included in this section because they represent, from Morris's point of view, the reason for the Press's existence.‘Proofs’ means a set of proofs that is within a half-dozen pages of being complete. Proofs of illustrations, ornaments, and engraved titles, because they are so numerous, are not listed unless they are marked or touched or are otherwise distinctive. I have recorded designs for ornaments only when they could be connected with a particular Kelmscott book. Retrees, which auction firms and librarians often mistakenly classify as cancels or proofs, are so abundant that I have not listed them. Since some of the Kelmscott Press materials – once owned by Cockerell – in the Morgan Library, the Clark Library and the Huntington Library are not yet catalogued, I have identified them by using Cockerell's own classification (e.g., PML ‘Morrisiana 11’). Similarly, because some of the collections of trial pages and proofs in the British Library are in multi-volume sets with only one shelf-mark, I have added Robert Proctor's private shelf-mark, which will be found written in pencil on a flyleaf (e.g., BL C.43.c.26 [ΣQQ46]). In compiling the RELATED MATERIALS sections I have encountered certain difficulties which should be mentioned. The most important Kelmscott Press collection to come on the market since Cockerell's in 1956 is the John A. Saks collection, which was sold at Christie's in New York on 2o May 1983. The principal buyer at the sale was Maggs Brothers, acting on behalf of a private collector, very well known in book-collecting circles, who declines to allow his name to appear in print. I was also unable to gain access to another major collection in London, which consists of Emery Walker's set of the Kelmscott books (except for his copy of the Chaucer, which is now in the British Library), most of which are inscribed by Morris.
