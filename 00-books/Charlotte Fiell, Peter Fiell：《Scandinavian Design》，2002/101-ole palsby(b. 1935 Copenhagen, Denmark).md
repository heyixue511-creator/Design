![](images/e3912e4d4012d480a03cbf0ff1ebd170a9859f094a5877cd46870ba0a9a51355.jpg) Ole Palsby, Classic No. 1 thermal jug for Alfi, 1985 # ole palsby (b. 1935 Copenhagen, Denmark) One of the most prolific Danish industrial designers working today, Ole Palsby originally trained in banking and international finance. In 1960, he decided to pursue his artistic instincts and began producing furniture developed in collaboration with several leading Danish designers. Four years later, he established a specialist gallery selling modern design objects and furniture, contemporary painting, sculpture and photography. It was not until 1968, however, when he opened another shop in Copenhagen specializing in high-quality kitchen equipment, that he began to seriously contemplate a career in design practice. Around this time he was invited to design a series of barbecues, which went on to win the prestigious Danish ID Prize in 1971. Bolstered by this success, in 1975 Palsby decided to dedicate himself full time to working as an independent design consultant. Since then, he has specialized almost entirely in the design of household items, from stainless steel cookware and porcelain tableware to nylon kitchen utensils and plastic thermos flasks. Unlike the majority of industrial designers, Palsby has never had a studio or a stable of assistants, but instead works by himselfwherever he happens to be. Although sketching is an important part of his method of working, he has stated that, “no-one can just draw the right solution”. Because of this, he often finds that it is only when he is in a factory handling materials that the creative process really begins to kick in. Guided by his central belief that “Design should be for the eye and the hand”, he evolves essential forms that have an innate simplicity and beauty. In 1977, hewas awarded another Danish ID Prize for his famous Eva Triokitchenware collection. In 1980, Palsby visited Japan for the first time and this had much influence upon his subsequent work. Five years later, he designed his best-known product, the Classic thermal jug for Alfi – the shape of which was reminiscent of a traditional silver creamer used on the Channel Islands. This commercially successful design was followed by the launch of his classic white Eva Trio dinner service, which was born out of his belief that the presentation of food should never be marred by unnecessary decoration. More recently he has designed various products for WMF, most notably his CombiNation cutlery series, which comprises fifty-eight different items. His success, especially in Germany, can be attributed to his use of classical geometry and his logical approach to the design process, in which he attempts to imbue products with a “look as natural as if the design had just occurred of its own accord”.
