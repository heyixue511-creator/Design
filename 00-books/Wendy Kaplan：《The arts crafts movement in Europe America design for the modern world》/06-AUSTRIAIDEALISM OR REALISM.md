# AUSTRIA: IDEALISM OR REALISM ![](images/45bfab0fa94373b32f66e3a6b9b6ceef66d2c6e10958094151c8c9cc543fca64.jpg) ![](images/868126270852e9e795230984345d9610d364dca91e2050cd1526eb91dc8b1c1f.jpg) group of progressive Viennese artists broke away in 1897 from the city's established exhibiting body, the Künstlerhaus, and formed their own society, commonly known as the Vienna Secession (4.1). The Secessionists—led by the group's first president, the painter Gustay Klimt—were outward looking. Believing the culture of their own city to be parochial, they wanted to bring in the best of modern art from the rest of Europe. This was a key moment in the history of modern Viennese art. Six years later in 1903, two leading members of the Secession, the architect Josef Hoffmann and the painter and designer Koloman Moser, set up a craft workshop in Vienna called the Wiener Werkstätte with the backing of a textile industrialist, Fritz Wärndorfer. Its sophisticated products stand at the center of any account of the Arts and Crafts movement in Vienna (4.2 and 4.3). The Werkstätte was partly modeled on the Guild of Handicraft, the workshops of the English Arts and Crafts designer C. R. Ashbee, and it is often seen as evidence of the remarkable influence of the British Arts and Crafts movement in Europe and America around 1900, Modernity in Viennese architecture and applied art is thus intimately connected with its response to the outside world. While accepting the importance of these influences in the formation of the Arts and Crafts movement in Vienna, this essay aims to interpret the Werkstätte and other Arts and Crafts work in the context of the city's own history and culture. Outside influence, particularly from Britain, was strong, but Viennese Arts and Crafts were distinctly Viennese. AUSTRIA. (NDUSTRY, AND URBAN LIFE The circumstances of the Arts and Crafts movement in Austria were different from those of the movement in Britain. First, the country had experienced little industrial development. In Britain, the birthplace of the Industrial Revolution, the Arts and Crafts movement was in part a reaction against industrialization, one stimulated by the mid-nineteenth-century writings of A. W. N. Pugin and John Ruskin, which criticized the quality of British everyday life in aesthetic and moral terms. The economy of the Austrian Empire, by contrast, was still dominated by agriculture and handcrafts. The making of everyday useful objects was to a great extent under the rigid control of guilds, which were opposed to free trade until after the mid-nineteenth century.' Industrial production, when it did develop, was concentrated almost exclusively in the crown lands of Bohemia and Moravia. Major industrial cities like those in Britain and Germany did not exist. As a result, Arts and Crafts ideas in Austria focused on aesthetic matters rather than on society at large, and that was only toward the end of the nineteenth century. Second, the origins and the leading players of the Arts and Crafts movement were confined to a single city. Vienna was the capital, the imperial seat, and the administrative and cultural center of the Hapsburg Empire. It was a city shaped by the Hapsburg monarchy and centuries of Catholicism. And it was large. By 1828 Vienna and its suburbs had a population of 290,000, ![](images/0f3a6ad75875ebbf45968b7b989e9d8cd892922849a74ad42d1c413b0c97d60d.jpg) ![](images/fee8e366f9da29b00055217ffd294801e3d804d1cf3ce6b19f3c398029166753.jpg) making it the third largest city in Europe after London and Paris. Between 1857 and 1869 the population grew from 476,000 to 607,000, and by 1891 it had reached 1,356,000. Vienna was the center of a multinational empire, and a magnet for those seeking work and a career, but because it had almost no industry, it did not have an industrial proletariat. Thus people in Austria did not necessarily associate the city with poverty and ![](images/c261567cf8c254e923885df4ca1e5c7113636d902817046d5a8be29b407da2ed.jpg) moral depravity. The city in itself was never demonized and there was no tendency to flee urban life and establish artists' colonies in the countryside, as was the case in England. The people in Vienna who had the wealth and progressive taste to buy objects that reflected an Arts and Crafts ideology—and after 1903 this means almost exclusively the products of the Wiener Werkstätte—were the rich, and mainly Jewish, haute bourgeoisie. These were, for the most part, families that had moved to Vienna from Germany or the Austrian crown lands and had prospered in the second generation. The new style of turn-of-the-century Vienna was connected much more with local traditions, such as folk art and the solid, unpretentious furniture of the early nineteenth century known as Biedermeier, than with the conventional Baroque vocabulary of the imperial court and the old Austrian aristocracy. Having these associations, it offered the haute bourgeoisie cultural assimilation and social differentiation. It encouraged an independent, contemporary, and Viennese form of expression at the same time that it fostered the haute bourgeoisie's traditional, prestige-oriented culture, which was based on an aristocratic lifestyle. The Viennese critic Berta ZuckerkandI saw this in 1905 when she reviewed an exhibition of work by the Wiener Werkstätte at the Galerie Miethke (4.4): "We strongly recommend collectors, the connoisseurs of the beauty of the past, to see this exhibition.... They will rediscover the delight of refined enjoyment.... From the solid ground of functionalism [Sachlichkeit] has sprung the fullblown flower of Celliniesque art, in other words, of an aristocratic taste." BEFORE THE ARTS AND CRAFTS: PROGRESSIVE ARCHITECTURE AND DECORATIVE ART IN VIENNA, C. 1860-C. 1895 In their study of the Arts and Crafts, Elizabeth Cumming and Wendy Kaplan identified the four principles of the movement as unity of the arts, joy in work, individual artistic expression, and emphasis on regional identity.3 Between about 1860 and the early 1890s, these ideas were only partly present in the progressive architecture and decorative art of Vienna. In some ways these decades anticipated the movement, and in other ways they did not. The unity of the arts was mainly understood as a relationship between architecture, painting, and sculpture, rather than between the fine and applied arts, as William Morris, the leading figure of the Arts and Crafts movement in Britain, had recommended. Architecture was much more artistically advanced in Vienna than sculpture and painting, so the unity of the arts implied that painting and sculpture could reach the same artistic quality. Thus, Heinrich von Ferstel's Italian Renaissance design for the Museum für Kunst und Industrie (Museum of Art and Industry; now the MAK, Museum für angewandte Kunst—Museum of Applied Arts) of 1868 was a model for the integration of these three fine arts. At the same time, the applied arts were not completely forgotten: the museum was also the first building in Vienna to achieve coherence in every detail, starting from the architectural proportions and materials right through to the design of the door handles. In the 1870s and 1880s the unity of the arts was seen as a principle to be observed in the artistic handling of a particular task. This was the view of Jakob von Falke, the second director of the museum and one of the first in Vienna to attempt to break out of the aesthetic cul-de-sac of historicism.' Later, about 1900, Viennese designers approached artistic unity more ambitiously, as a creative principle in its own right, the starting point for a Gesamtkunstwerk, or total work of art. The moral dimension of the Arts and Crafts had a part to play in progressive Vienna, but it remained at a general, social level, unconnected with the personal notion of joy in work. It was a question of the relationship between designer, producer, and consumer.⁵ This view is a constant in Vienna, We find it as early as the lecture at the opening of the Museum of Art and Industry in 1871 by its founder and first director, Rudolf von Eitelberger, and then several decades later in the 1905 Arbeitsprogramm (Work Program) of the Wiener Werkstätte. Both deplore a society in a state of radical change, with its culture of superficiality and false luxury brought about by economic speculation, cheap mass production, and false artistic expression.6 Artistic individuality was strong, especially in architecture. It was much more marked in progressive Viennese architecture than in the equivalents in Berlin and Paris, and for the first time, an issue was made of the naming of designers responsible for handmade goods. As in England, though, individuality was seen to be hindered by its expression in historicist terms. In his Aesthetics of Handicraft, von Falke wrote that historical models restricted the quest for a contemporary style. They were at best an interim, if necessary, stage on the road to creative freedom.8 On the whole, the Viennese were negative about national or regional identity in these decades. Although the German Renaissance style was available as a way of expressing the authority of Austria within an empire of many nations and 54 million people, it was too closely identified with the power of the newly unified Germany for political comfort. In the end the Viennese preferred the Italian Renaissance style, because its tectonic clarity helped them with the problem of structure and ornament. The decision to build the new Museum of Art and Industry in an Italian Renaissance style confirmed this choice. It was only later, around the turn of the century, that a regional spirit developed. The leading progressive architect in latenineteenth-century Vienna was Otto Wagner. He trained in Vienna, and at the beginning of his career his work was inspired by the Renaissance. By 1877 he had reinterpreted this into a more personal form of expression— in his apartment block at Schottenring 23 (4.5). In 1889 he called this transformation a "free renaissance that has absorbed our genius loci." Wagner, however, did not identify him- ![](images/f6762805f4be92afdbfb6d370c735ea79b254332d2946a5f7944b24505c0f755.jpg) self with any particular style. He thought that the future of architecture lay in a natural, creative drive, out of which would evolve a "Nutz-Stil," a functional, or utility, style. His concept was thus defined in terms of function, not aesthetics, and in this sense he can be seen as a precursor of the modern age. If we think of Viennese architecture at this time as hovering, intellectually, between functionalism on the one hand and artistic expression on the other—in other words, between realism and idealism—then Wagner decides the matter in a practical, inclusive way. He thought that a good architect should have an acquired practical knowledge, but he should also have individual creative talent. Only the artist who could actually build deserved the title of architect.'o Two of his favorite sayings were, "Anything impractical cannot be beautiful" and "Artis sola domina necessitas" (Art serves only one mistress, function)." Wagner's idea of a functional style also had an economic dimension; it implied the use of artistic resources.'2 In this respect he was like von Eitelberger, the leader of the Viennese designreform movement, who thought that local creative talent could compete economically on the international market. This idea became effective at the turn of the century, when the Viennese art critic and biographer of Wagner, Josef August Lux, described the city's artistic avant-garde as "that invaluable economic commodity TALENT."3Design became a marketable commodity. In the Salonecke Entwurf Professor Joseph Hoffmann bentwood industry and elsewhere, it was no longer the manufacturer's name that sold a product, but that of the designer, as demonstrated in an advertisement for Kohn factory furniture that emphasized Hoffmann (4.6). From 1903 onward, the Wiener Werkstätte was to make the elevated status of the designer an important part of its marketing. In 1893 the Viennese art historian Alois Riegl published his standard textbook on the history of ornament, Stilfragen: Grundlegungen zu einer Geschichte der Ornamentik. Riegl interpreted works of art as the product of a struggle between their material properties and a creative drive in human nature, which he called the will to form. (Here is another example of the tension in Viennese thought between realism and idealism.) This view places great faith in individual artistic expression, and because its most direct manifestation is ornament, Riegl's interpretation promotes the value of the applied arts. This book was influential in progressive art circles devoted to the demolition of traditional hierarchies. In 1899 the art critic Ludwig Hevesi described the new methods of teaching drawing at the Kunstgewerbeschule (School of Applied Arts), which was attached to the Museum of Art and Industry. Under Hoffmann, it was no longer pictorial representation but ornament that was most important. Nature was broken down into its elementary formal components, and the capacity for abstraction was encouraged in this way.⁴ We can see Riegl's influence in the role of ornament in the Vienna Secession's monthly journal, Ver Sacrum (The Sacred Spring), which began publication in 1898. In Ver Sacrum, ornament becomes an art form in its own right, and the distinction between the fine and applied arts has been eliminated, in accordance with the tenets of the Arts and Crafts movement. Decorative vignettes form a striking part of the page layout; they are given their own captions citing the name of the designer, and their status is acknowledged as equivalent to that of painting, sculpture, or architecture. THE DEVELOPMENT OF THE ARTS AND CRAFTS MOVEMENT IN VIENNA The ArtS and Crafts movement in Vienna developed rapidly in the second half of the 1890s, and the impulse for this came, in part at least, from Britain. The quiet penetration of Arts and Crafts ideas can be traced in the Museum of Art and Industry's library acquisitions. Before 1885 the library's British holdings included only technical works on art by Ruskin and two picture books by Walter Crane. In the late 1880s and early 1890s, under the directorship of von Falke, it bought two works by Ruskin that were seminal for the Arts and Crafts, The Stones of Venice and The Seven Lamps of Architecture, In the late 1890s and early 1900s, under the Anglophile director Arthur von Scala, the library purchased more books by Ruskin, picture books and Arts and Crafts essays by Walter Crane, and several books by William Morris in German translation, including his early lectures, Hopes and Fears for Art. In 19o0 it also bought two books printed and bound at Elbert Hubbard's Roycroft Press in East Aurora, New York, suggesting that Vienna was beginning to take note of the Arts and Crafts movement in the United States. This suggestion is strengthened by the fact that there were copies of the American magazine The Craftsman in the library of the Wiener Werkstätte, and also by the comment of the critic Hermann Bahr, made as early as 1898, that the United States played an important role in the development of Viennese Arts and Crafts, especially in the understanding and artistic treatment of materials.'⁶ The new artistic aspirations of the movement became public in 1897, when the younger and more progressive artists from the Künstlerhaus left to form the Vienna Secession. The Secessionists were progressive in their modernism and in their internationalism. Over the entrance to their newly built exhibition rooms, they inscribed "Der Zeit ihre Kunst, Der Kunst ihre Freiheit" (To every age its art, to art its freedom), and they displayed work by artists from France, Germany, Belgium, and Britain. They were also progressive in embracing the decorative arts. In the first issue of their journal, Ver Sacrum, the editors proclaimed their mission: "And here we appeal to you all, without any distinction of class or wealth. We know no difference between 'higher' and 'lesser' art, between art for the rich and art for the poor. Art is for everyone."' Thus Vienna, without actually naming its English models, adopted the ideas of William Morris and the Arts and Crafts movement. The first manifestation of Arts and Crafts ideology in Viennese applied art can be seen in Josef Hoffmann's early furniture designs. Honesty of expression was embodied in the plain softwood boards, sawn so that their outlines also acted as ornament. Hoffmann designed the bedroom suite for the house of the painter Ernst Stöhr, a founding member of the Secession who was receptive to this kind of simplicity and averse to furnishings that expressed convention or prestige. The suite includes a cupboard (in this exhibition), a double bed, night tables, and a chest made of softwood formerly stained green (4.7). The combination of bed and chest is medieval in type and so is the plank construction, but there is no specific medieval source, and the plank construction itself has been transformed into a decorative motif. The idea of a useful object designed in an artistic spirit was also adopted in a competition to design table glass for an ordinary household, organized by the Museum of Art and Industry in 1899. The design by Koloman Moser that won the competition was executed in several decorative variants and successfully sold by the glass distribution firm E. Bakalowits & Söhne (4.9). In the same year as the founding of the Secession, 1897, Arthur von Scala became director of the Museum of Art and Industry and the Kunstgewerbeschule. Von Scala was an Anglophile and a personal friend of the director of the South Kensington Museum in London.18 In the tradition of English design reformers like Henry Cole and Owen Jones, he encouraged art teaching that was focused on draftsmanship and designing for industry; he did not fully support the Viennese avant-garde. Under his directorship, the museum's new official publication, Kunst und Kunsthandwerk, begun in 1898, was richly illustrated and embellished with English-style typography. In the first issue, art historian Franz Wickhoff called the new Viennese style "English," though he stressed that what was needed was "indigenous accomplishments" along similar lines.'Wickhoff described the commercial English furniture that von Scala promoted, as well as the revived interest in local Empire and Biedermeier work, which, like the English work, was traditional, functional, and perceived, at that time, as made for the middle class. Joining forces with these English sources of inspiration, which were supported by, among others, a traditionally Anglophile Austrian aristocracy, were the ideas of the Arts and Crafts movement. Reports in Kunst und Kunsthandwerk in 1899 included reviews of modern English bookbindings and an excellent overview of the various English craft guilds.20By 1900 the dam seems to have burst and the English Arts and Crafts movement became the leading topic of the journal. In 19o0 Kunst und Kunsthandwerk included a detailed article about the craft reforms of C. R. Ashbee and a review of the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society's sixth exhibition in London by Count Vinzenz Latour.² In 1901 it published a long article on M. H. Baillie Scott.²2 Von Scala's direct influence on art education came to an end in 1899, when the Kunstgewerbeschule became independent under the directorship of the painter Felician Baron von Myrbach. In the same year Josef Hoffmann, then twenty-nine, and Koloman Moser, then thirty-one, joined the school. They were appointed professors in 19o0. The arrival of von Myrbach, Hoffmann, and Moser was one of those strokes of luck that brings the right people together in the right place at the right time, giving them the opportunity to put their ideas into practice. Von Myrbach was not important as a painter or draftsman, but he understood the signs of the times. He carried out a thorough reform of the curriculum, setting up workshops and reorienting the school toward practical training, of the sort introduced into some British art schools in the 1890s.23 The pottery workshop, started in 1899, was the first of many. A potter was employed and two wheels purchased for the ceramics class, which, until then, had only produced designs on paper (4.10 and 4.11). For the first time in the history of the school, the students were put directly in touch with materials. Design and execution were united once more. Josef Hoffmann, Wiener Werkstatte THE WIENER WERKSTÄTTE In 1903 Hoffmann, Moser, and the textile manufacturer and art patron Fritz Wärndorfer established a cooperative workshop in Vienna, the Wiener Werkstätte. Wärndorfer was to finance the project, and Hoffmann and Moser were to be artistic directors. They set up a metal workshop first, perhaps because they were concerned about the lack of contact between artists and metal craftsmen—there was no such workshop at the Kunstgewerbeschule (4.20).29 Later in 1903, they moved to larger workshops at Neustiftsgasse 32-34, where they also created departments for bookbinding, leatherwork, cabinetmaking, and lacquerwork, as well as an architectural office. Later still, departments for printing, fashion, and textiles were added. For glass and ceramics, they used their own designs or those of craft students, which were fabricated by outside manufacturers. Hoffmann and Moser must have been aware that their new role as businessmen gave them a chance to develop a convincing artistic enterprise, and they aimed to cover all the crafts necessary to create artistic unity in interior decorating. They aimed, that is, to create a Gesamtkunstwerk, using their own workforce and workshops. ![](images/07053243432c9b4319ff170fac4743e397fa92bc4dc8aea52dc089ee72b2ac6a.jpg) The idea of the Gesamtkunstwerk originated in the belief that humanity could be redeemed through art; therefore, the separation of art from life must be eliminated. Morris had first taken up this challenge in his attempt to restore the unity of fine and applied arts. The artists of the Secession responded to the challenge in their exhibition design and organization. For example, their fourteenth exhibition in 1902 was conceived as a Gesamtkunstwerk to showcase a single work of art—Max Klinger's monumental sculpture of Beethoven. The Kunstgewerbeschule professor and Ver Sacrum editor Alfred Roller created the mural on the wall behind the sculpture (his poster for the exhibition [4.21] was a detail of this mural); Gustav Klimt painted the famous Beethoven frieze in another room (4.22); and Hoffmann was the principal designer of the exhibition, which unified not only all the visual arts, but also art with music. The most famous examples of Gesamtkunstwerk associated with the Wiener Werkstätte were the schemes for two large buildings—one public, the other private. The Purkersdorf Sanatorium (1904-5) was designed by Hoffmann, with interiors by Moser and Hoffmann. It combined white walls, clean-cut functional furniture, a dominant motif of geometric forms, and a light, clinical austerity (4.23 and 4.24). The other example, designed in 1905 and completed six years later, was the sumptuous family home of the Belgian banker Adolphe Stoclet in Brussels. The classic designs of the early years of the Werkstätte, when Hoffmann and Moser were doing most of the designing, were mostly in metalwork, leather, and furniture. Although the commentary on these designs often focuses on the square as a decorative motif (it was used so frequently that it became almost a signature of Viennese Arts and Crafts), the quality of early Werkstätte designs often depends on a delicate and creative interplay of squares and circles. Consider Hoffmann's Sitzmaschine of 1908, made by the bentwood firm of Jakob & Josef Kohn, an austere assembly of squares and rectangles on a large scale and in detail, which is, so to speak, qualified by the massive curve at the back, the balls at the joints, and the general softening of corners (4.32). In Moser's 1904 plant stand of perforated and painted metal, the circle is a controlling element of middle size—in the curves of the central stem and the circles of the plant holders—whereas the square is a tiny dynamic unit of the design, working at its own miniature level as a network of tiny voids that mysteriously make up a structure (4.33). A similar interplay can be seen in Hoffmann's designs for jewelry, in which the fat, curling leaves are gracefully contained by a fine, rectangular frame (4.34 and 4.35). In the early work of the Werkstätte, the square was a wellspring of invention, even though it seems so closed and formal. It had the same role in the designs of Mackintosh and Macdonald, despite their sensuousness. These designs belong to those years when Hoffmann and Moser were the firm's main designers and Wärndorfer's backing was secure. All these things changed. The austere and substantial forms of the early work gave way to a playful and decorative style of fanciful objects, spriggy ornament, and allover patterns. The designers changed: Hoffmann was there for the whole life of the Werkstätte, subtly adapting his style, but Moser left in 1907; the new, decorative manner came in with Carl Otto Czeschka, who joined in 1905, and with Dagobert Peche in 1915, Peche was the principal source of decorative fantasy. A critic wrote, "In Josef Hoffmann's case everything rests firmly on the ground, in Peche's all is shooting upwards."3s In 1916 the Werkstätte set up the Künstlerwerkstätte (Artists' Workshop), which provided studio space and equipment for decorative artists and gave the Wiener Werkstätte itself an option on their work. This greatly increased the number of the firm's designers, now mostly women who had studied at the Kunstgewerbeschule. Their work was more various than before the war, but no less fanciful. And the backers changed. In 1914 Wärndorfer left Vienna for the United States under awkward circumstances. His place was taken by the banker Otto Primavesi, but the new situation was never stable. There were attempts in the 1920s to involve the Werkstätte with industry, but the artistic individualism of its prewar years was too strong for that, and it passed through a series of managers and financial crises in the 1920s, sustained less and less by its reputation. Given that the original purpose of the Wiener Werkstätte was to create modern, functional, and well-crafted objects for daily use, the question of how well it implemented its own credo must be addressed. Indisputably, the Werkstätte holds a position of international importance in the history of modern design. It is also well known that there were two important protagonists of the modern movement in Vienna at the turn of the century, and that they held opposing views. One was the Werkstätte's Hoffmann and the other was Adolf Loos. Hoffmann stood for renewal through form: the worId of design was to be made new, and modernity achieved, through the style of the Werkstätte's work. Loos, on the other hand, was an architect and cultural critic who concerned himself not with form but with content, not with the appearance of things but with the things themselves, with the way people live and use objects. He objected to the creation of new ornamentation for its own sake, and he was a critic of the culture as a whole.36 History has shown that Loos held the more profound and enduring view. Questions of content cannot, over time, be solved through the polishing of surfaces. The Werkstätte's superb handicraft production of objects designed by individual artists is incompatible with the needs of a modern society. This is a lesson of history. In early-twentieth-century Vienna, however, the issue was not at all clear. What was clear was that the traditionally authoritarian, Catholic culture of the city made it very difficult for any individual to express himself artistically, or even in the circumstances of daily life, and both Hoffmann and Loos experienced this. Vienna had the good fortune to bring forward two such gifted, controversial spirits at one time. Their different starting points offer two possibilities: to decree modernity through the artist's work, and especially through his control of the Gesamtkunstwerk; or to understand modernity as a social process in which the artist can play an enabling, but not a controlling, role. In this Viennese scenario, everyone must set his own priorities, must choose either form or content. SHAPING A NATIONAL CONSCIOUSNESS THROUGH THE CRAFTS Following the Ausgleich (Compromise) of 1867, which accorded Hungary a relatively privileged position within the Hapsburg Empire as a partner in the "Dual Monarchy," the country had undergone a period of rapid industrialization.⁵Despite the economic advantages and degree of political autonomy this entailed, the partnership was doininated by Austria and overshadowed by memories of a protracted, at times bloody, independence movement. This situation drove the repeated calls for a national style in design. The Tulip movement, for example, expressed support for Hungarian industries through the wearing of badges and jewelry in the form of this vernacular motif. Led by prominent members of the upper classes and supported by the cultural authorities, the initiative for patriotic consumption was part of a wider economic and cultural program to resist Austrian domination that continued until World War I. In a global context, this entailed ambitious and sustained participation in international expositions, starting with the Exposition Universelle in Paris in 1900, when Hungary exhibited independently of Austria. National archetypes were also changing. Increasingly, the Hungarian peasantry rather than the aristocracy was perceived by politicians, artists, and writers as the embodiment of the national spirit, and the related focus on vernacular crafts reflected this changing concept of "nation." In particular, the search for a cultural identity was tied to the ethnographic exploration and recording of folk arts in ![](images/25fe1f127913075f0fb8a77d4bf506bf6e9d408c0698e09cd344b27815c7e1fb.jpg) remote Transylvania, where traditions were believed to have survived in their purest form, relatively free of Turkish and Hapsburg "contamination." Traditional crafts provided a common ground, bolstering the concept of nation and offsetting the destabilizing effects of rapid and uneven socioeconomic change by referring to a natural, spiritual order. Sustaining those crafts that survived while simultaneously developing a modern idiom became central to the selfconscious invention of tradition and nationhood. Above all, vernacular embroideries and dress became symbolic referents of national identity that all Hungarians—aristocrat or peasant, man or woman—could identify with on some level. More than any other craft, the textile arts in Hungary also had the potential to communicate the values of social interconnectedness and continuity central to Arts and Crafts ideology. In every rural district distinct stitches and patterns were elaborately embroidered on pillow and table covers, and national costumes were still worn in many regions. In particular, the szúr, a richly decorated, woolen overcoat worn by male peasants and shepherds, was widely discussed as an icon of the new peasant consciousness, and it was studied as a source of inspiration for modern design (5.2).6 Its simple structure and bold decoration were reworked in the elegant coat designed by Júlia Zsolnay (5.3). Following the examples of London and Vienna, the National Museum of Decorative Arts was established in Budapest in 1872 "to gather the most precious remnants of the decorative arts of the past, and by using them as teaching material, to preserve and improve ancient techniques in today's decorative arts...and further, through the education of mass taste, to create a larger public for these pieces of art." Under the auspices of this museum, the Magyar Iparmúvészeti Társulat (Hungarian Decorative Arts Association), its magazine Múvészi Ipar (Artistic Craft), and the Országos Magyar Királyi Iparmúvészeti Iskola (National School of Applied Arts) were established by 1885. Outside Budapest the state encouraged the development of rural crafts, or home industries, as a means of countering deep-seated problems of agrarian decline and seasonal unemployment. Government ministries worked in tandem with local manufacturers and private organizations to arrest the falling away of skills and markets by providing training and equipment, assistance with the collection and modification of old patterns, price regulation, and improvements in retailing and distribution. ![](images/10f2613444f250a8ea6a37859e9a0aab81527e11f2186dc259a67f75d1370a33.jpg) One of the earliest and most successful home-industry centers was run by Mrs. Gyarmathy (born Etelka Hory), who organized the production and sale of textiles embroidered by peasant women in the Kalotaszeg region of Transylvania (now part of Romania) (5.4). Already famous as a novelist and writer of short stories about rural life, she helped popularize the region, and by the 1890s she had agents in Berlin, Munich, Brussels, Paris, London, and New York." (It was through her London agent that Walter Crane was invited to Hungary in 1900.) The involvement of upper-class and aristocratic women in such enterprises conformed to a tradition of female philanthropic patronage that operated increasingly through an international network. As they became more successful, such organizations faced the perennial problem of steering between the commercial imperatives of the marketplace and the desire to preserve the integrity of the genuine folk culture on which the home industries drew.° Calls for a national style and fascination with vernacular culture had been accelerated by the prominent position given to crafts and peasant life in exhibitions that brought ethnographic research into the public domain. From the 1870s the Zsolnay company raised general awareness of regional patterns by improving the body and glaze of local ceramics marketed at national fairs." At the Budapest National Industrial Exhibition in 1885, the most popular pavilion was the Kalotaszeg House, reconstructed and furnished with traditional artifacts by Mrs. Gyarmathy and based on her research into rural traditions. Thereafter home-industry products from around the country were continually represented and marketed in the capital, initially through the new Museum of Commerce, and from 1895 to 1899 through a special bazaar, thus encouraging the rising taste for peasant-style crafts.'² Paradoxically, as idealization of the country's folk culture became more prevalent among the urban bourgeoisie, conditions among the rural poor worsened. Bridging the growing socioeconomic divide between Hungary's urban and rural populations became a major issue that exponents of the Arts and Crafts movement sought to address both practically and symbolically. The key event in the cultural landscape of Hungary at this time was the Millennial Celebration of 1896 commemorating the arrival of the Magyars in the Carpathian Basin one thousand years earlier. Not only indicating the political maturity and economic progress that Hungary had achieved as part of a major imperial power, the millennium celebrations (especially the exhibition that opened in May) also marked a critical moment in the crystallization of Hungarian nationhood. Due to its landlocked position at the crossroads between East and West, Hungary had long been a melting pot of different ethnic and religious groups. To meld and assimilate this heterogeneous collection of peoples into a recognizable nation entailed the "magyarization" of many minority groups, some of whom, like the Croats and the Slavs, were seeking political independence from Hungary, just as Hungary had done from Austria. Among the symbolic representations of a united autonomous realm with secure foundations was A Millenniumi Falu, a "village" organized by the Ethnographic Association. Budapest also laid claim to the heritage of Transylvania in a full-scale reconstruction of the Vajdahunyad Castle, associated with the Renaissance Court of King Matthias, which was to become a permanent feature in the urban landscape. An equally prominent but more controversial addition to the cityscape was the new building designed by Ödön Lechner to house the collections of the Museum of Decorative Arts (5.5). An influential statement about modern crafts and the need to establish a new Hungarian style, it challenged the prevailing historicism of landmark projects like the Neo-Gothic parliament building. Just as the Hungarian language, distinct from all Slavic and Romance tongues, was a unique and immediate signifier of national identity, a comparable "design language" was seen as crucial to the shaping of a national consciousness.'5 Lechner's design gave monumental expression to the complex debate around the origins of Hungarian folk ornament. On a more abstract level, the design of the museum established dress and textiles as the paradigm for a modern architectural style.
