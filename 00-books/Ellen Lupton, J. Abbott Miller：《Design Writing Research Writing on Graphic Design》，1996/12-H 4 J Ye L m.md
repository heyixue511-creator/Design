Mu30555 Illustrators commonly have looked to fine art as a stylistic source, particularly for promoting cultural events and luxury products such as fashion, design, and travel. American designers and illustrators had access to advanced European art through multi-lingual magazines such as Gebrauchsgraphik (published since 1925) and Graphis (published since 1944). Such domestic fine arts periodicals as ARTnews also featured the work of an international art community. Graphis in the 194os was primarily a trade journal, but also included articles on Picasso's illustrated books and on medieval, folk, and children's art—a range of sources that contributed to the visual culture of progressive commercial artists. While Cubism and Purism informed much of the advertising and poster art of the 1920s and 193os, Expressionism and Surrealism moved to the foreground in the 1940s and 1950s, replacing a geometric machine aesthetic with organic, gestural, “naïve," and playful styles. Paul Rand and Bill Bernbach employed humorous collage and personified typography in their campaign for Ohrbach's department store during the 4os, and Alvin Lustig incorporated biomorphic shapes inspired by Klee, Arp, and Calder in his designs for New Directions Books, which would commission work from Warhol in the 50s.14 The first-generation Expressionism of Klee and Arp was a more established reference for commercial artists than the Abstract Expressionism of de Kooning, Pollock, Kline, and others. As a 1958 trade article suggested, Abstract Expressionism was deemed inappropriate for everyday advertising: “Certainly a shoe ad neither could nor should be heightened with the emotional intensity that characterizes de Kooning's Women."15 Illustrators who adopted the painterly techniques and gestural draftsmanship of Expressionism included Bob Gill, Robert Peak, Ben Shahn, and Robert Weaver.16 Shahn was the most prestigious of the group, moving easily between the worlds of fine and commercial art. His work appeared in numerous advertisements and magazines and was also exhibited in galleries and at the Museum of Modern Art. The exhibition inaugurating the founding of the Art Directors Club of New York in 1920 was typical: illustrations were presented as autonomous works of art in the exhibition and catalogue, displayed apart from the printed ads for which they were commissioned.2o ![](images/5365d51620f9f38013591181eddbc01c6bf23e70a1d55ea88e2987c4cec980d5.jpg) In contrast, art historians have considered illustration to be a degraded form of painting, not only because of its commercial application, but also because of its literary or anecdotal content. A standard criticism of a work of art is that it is “illustrative."21 While the Museum of Modern Art has admitted graphic design into its collection, it has not legitimated illustration. Within popular media, “artistic" illustration carried connotations of high culture that were denied it by the more exclusive discourse of museums and art historians. Art director Lou Dorfsman, who commissioned Shahn, Warhol, and other progressive illustrators during the 4os and os, used them only selectively, when an “artistic" appeal was appropriate. In 1951 Dorfsman hired Warhol to illustrate a newspaper ad for a CBS radio feature on drug abuse and violence called The Nation's Nightmare. The program served as a public relations tool for CBS; the ad aimed not so much to attract an audience for the presentation as to alert the government and the public that CBS had directed its resources toward an important social issue. An artistic illustration like the one produced by Warhol for The Nation's Nightmare served to advance the image of CBS as a humanistic, enlightened institution. Dorfsman explained that he "wouldn't give Andy Warhol or Ben Shahn the Dick van Dyke Show or Mary Tyler Moore Show to do....Warhol was at a higher level than prime-time entertainment."22 CBS was a visible and influential force in the advertising and design community of the 1950s. Its president, Frank Stanton, was an early advocate of"corporate identity." Stanton encouraged art directors William Golden and Lou Dorfsman to lavish attention on every aspect of the company's communications, from trade ads and press kits to wallpaper and matchbooks. CBS designs won numerous awards and were published frequently in design magazines. The leaders in the period's editiorial design were art directors Henry Wolf (Esquire and Harper's Bazaar), Otto Storch (McCall's), Sam Antupit (Esquire), Bradbury Thompson (Mademoiselle), Cipe Pineles (Charm and Seventeen), Alexander Lieberman (Vogue), Art Kane (Seventeen), Tina Fredericks (Glamour), and Will Burtin and Leo Lionni (Fortune). Art directors during this time gained increasing power in the editorial process, using typography, photography, and illustration to actively shape—rather than passively reflect—the content of magazine features. Richard Avedon and Irving Penn brought “signature” styles to photography that challenged the province of the illustrator. Art Kane and Henry Wolf were both skilled photographers, able to set up conceptual" scenarios that combined the realism of the camera with the inventiveness and plasticity of illustration. While Chwast's and Glaser's work of the 5os was intentionally rough and expressionistic, during the 6os it employed bright colors, bulging, voluptuous forms, and decorative styles borrowed from art history.25 Rejecting the modernist attempt to reduce messages to their geometric essences, Glaser and Chwast have looked to popular culture as a warehouse of available imagery. Commanding the full resources of illustration, typography, and art direction, their work became internationally influential during the 6os and 7os. Glaser has called Andy Warhol “the perfect commercial artist." Looking back at Warhol's illustrations of the 5os, Glaser has noted that his uneven, expressive line placed him firmly in the commercial arena dominated by Shahn, and followed by himself and many other younger illustrators. Warhol's later Pop Art and the work of the Push Pin Studio both issue from what Glaser has characterized as “a prevailing ethos of banal forms, comercial art, and comic strips." The carnivalesque style of the Push Pin Studio departed both from conventional realism and from more highbrow adaptations of modern art and design. Likewise, Warhol brought what Glaser has called “the doctored, photostatic vernacular of commercial art" into the domain of the gallery.26 Nevertheless, while the material employed by Warhol and the Push Pin Studio is comparable, the functions it served were distinct: Push Pin revived and revised such discredited source material as Victoriana, art deco, and comic books in order to create polished and urbane graphics, appropriate for promoting cultural events and packaging jazz and classical music. Warhol, on the other hand, brought the graphics of supermarkets and advertising into the gallery, refraining from the stylistic interventions of the Push Pin Studio and celebrating the banality and anonymity of his sources. Pop Art appropriated material that had attained an almost nostalgic status by the early 6os. The well-worn packaging of Wonder Bread, Coca-Cola, and Campbell's Soup supplied reference points for a stable, suburban, middle-class America. The graphic identities of these products had been firmly established before World War II, and the Campbell's Soup label had been updated in only minor ways since the early twentieth century. Warhol's personal library included a design textbook which asserted that the familiarity of the Campbell's image was so valuable that the company could afford to alter it only in “subtle, scarcely perceptible ways."27 In contrast to such guarded conventionality, the advertising and design that was considered progressive during the early years of Pop Art was inventive; it was derived from a largely urban milieu, and gave expression to an ethņically diverse, culturally sophisticated design community. The predictable headlines, celebrity endorsements, and slick airbrush illustrations of the immediate postwar period were the creation of an , entrenched, homogeneous Madison Avenue ad industry. In the 192os, ad agencies had sought to create a “science" of advertising by engaging psychological theory; this scientism continued to expand with the rise of marketing and demographic studies, which encouraged conformity and caution.28 This conservatism was bolstered by a largely upper-middle class, male, Ivy League work force. In the late 5os the new agency Doyle Dane Bernbach (DDB) began to produce art and copy whose humor and sophistication challenged its consumer audience, exchanging hard-sell tactics for an unprecedented self-consciousness about advertising. DDB's campaign for Volkswagen became the exemplar of the “New Advertising,” a style made possible by a closer working relationship between the art director and copywriter. The new advertising strategies also reflected a wider representation of Jewish, Italian, Irish, Greek, and female art directors and copywriters.29 In contrast, the realm of communication that served as source material for Pop Art did not come from the vanguard of advertising and design, but from the tried-and-true vocabulary of the “affluent society." Warhol's success as a commercial artist coincided with a gradual shift in the professional standing of illustrators, prompted by the increasing use of photography. This continuity is what makes Warhol problematic as a “fine” artist. For example, while Lou Dorfsman admired the artistry of Warhol's illustrations, he remained skeptical of his art-world stardom. For Dorfsman, commercial techniques produce commercial art. He commented in 1988, “Silkscreening Monroe in 12 different color breakdowns is fine, but to me it's a graphic design schtick."3o In his transition from the business of illustration to the business of art, Warhol made use of the skills, both manual and social, that he had learned in the commercial art world. While illustration continued to be an important profession within graphic design, Warhol might have sensed photography's encroachment, and surely he sensed its power. The May 1969 cover for Esquire, created by George Lois and Carl Fischer, demonstrated the flexibility and impact of photographic illustration: a “highconcept" image was given the force of realism by a seamlessly crafted photomontage. Warhol is shown sinking in the commercial mire he celebrated. The fact that his descent is depicted photographically indicates the rise of the camera and the diminishing prestige of hand-drawn illustration in a commercial art world that was increasingly self-conscious about the boundaries between art and design.
