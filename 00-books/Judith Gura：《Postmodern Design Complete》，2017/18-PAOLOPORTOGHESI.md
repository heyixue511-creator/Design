# PAOLO PORTOGHESI b.1931 ITALIAN ARCHITECT WHOSE HISTORICIST DESIGNS ARE DISTINCTIVE FOR THEIR INCLINATION TO THE BAROQUE. BELOW Mosque of Rome, completed in 1994. OPPOSITE Conference Hall in Termi di Montecatini thermal baths, Italy, 1987. From the earliest years of his long career as architect, theorist, and educator, Paolo Portoghesi rejected the idea that Modernism could deny the influence of history, a position that put him at odds with the leading figures of Italian architecture, including the influential critics Leonardo Benevolo and Manfredo Tafuri. According to Portoghesi, Postmodernism was “a refusal, a rupture, a renouncement, much more than a simple change of direction."34 He believed that a focus on tradition could stimulate innovation rather than stifle it, and that an examination of even the most modern works would reveal their foundations in historical architecture. Portoghesi's interest in organic forms of Modernism, shared with his Rome colleague Bruno Zevi, can be seen in his architecture, which uses sweeping, curved forms for dramatic effect. As president of the architectural section of the Venice Biennale from 1979 to 1992, Portoghesi was responsible for Postmodernism's public debut: an exhibition at the i98o Biennale called Strada Novissima, for which twenty architects were invited to create building facades along the theme "The Presence of the Past." The participants included luminaries such as MICHAEL GRAVES (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), HANS HOLLEIN, ROBERT A. M. STERN, and ROBERT VENTURI and DENISE SCOTT BROWN (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design"), all of whom designed spaces that were unambiguously classical. The exhibition was a showcase of virtuoso styles with an underlying message: it stressed the importance of history to modern architecture. Generating controversy as well as accolades, it brought Portoghesi's ideas, and the new historicist strain of Postmodernism, before an international audience. Portoghesi had another influential role, as editor of the journal Controspazio from 1969 to 1983, and was dean of architecture at the Politecnico di Milano in the significant years from 1968 to 1978, when modernist dogma came under fire. His own architectural studies were at the University of Rome, from which he graduated in 1957. He began teaching there in 196ı and established his Rome-based architectural practice with Vittorio Gigliotti in 1964. His most celebrated architectural project is probably the Mosque of Rome (i994), the largest mosque outside the Islamic worid. The structure incorporates a traditional silhouette in a clearly modern structure that nevertheless evokes a grand classical temple framing a traditional mosque form. The dramatic interior combines a contemporary framework with traditional elements such as Islamic tilework, and accommodates up to two thousand worshippers. The interior conference hall he designed in a section of the famed Terme di Montecatini baths in Pistoia (i987) is a soaring. skylight-topped space framed with wooden pillars. The best-known Italian architect of his generation, Aldo Rossi was also influential as a designer, artist, writer, teacher, and theoretician, and the leader of one of Italy's most important architectural movements-one that fed into the phenomenon that became Postmodernism, but is barely remembered today. His distinctive architecture used a spare vocabulary of primal forms drawn from the vernacular structures of his native country. Designed with little extraneous ornament, his buildings are visually striking and often uncannily evocative Born in Milan, Rossi studied architecture at the Politecnico di Milano, and on graduation in 1959 began to work at the magazine Casabella, continuing to do so after opening his architectural practice the same year. Italy was then engaged in the challenges of rebuilding a country after the destruction of the Second World War, and a group of young architects, including Rossi, Carlo Aymonino, and Manfredo Tafuri, were concerned that the functionalist, modern buildings then in fashion were not only incompatible with existing older structures but insufficiently connected to either history or modern society. Neo-rationalism, or La Tendenza, as their movement was called, sought to solve the problem using the existing vocabulary of architectural forms instead of seeking avantgarde, utopian solutions. These basic forms, developed over history and retained in the collective memory, would respect the existing fabric of the city and relate to those living in it. Rossi and his colleagues in La Tendenza ![](images/c041b48329705a08fc91c949e9848ceccb0115df1842c2f60b5f6999f319c250.jpg) produced exhibitions, proposals, and theoretical discussions, and were influential as teachers, though they built relatively little during the subsequent two decades. Rossi's most important book, L’architettura della città, was published in ı966, the same year as Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture by ROBERT VENTURI (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"). Indeed, he expressed similar ideas to Venturi, focusing on the need to reject functionalism and recapture meaning in architecture, and viewing the city as a dynamic entity that evolved over time and was capable of absorbing change without losing meaning. To Rossi, architecture was more than something to look at: it could elicit an emotional response. Rossi's first built project was the Teatro del Mondo, for the 1979 Venice Biennale. A yellow octagonal tower on a barge that floated off St. Mark's Square, it was a fanciful and ephemeral reminder of the eighteenth-century Venetian tradition of floating theaters. After the Biennale, it was rowed across the Adriatic to Dubrovnik, where it remained until 1981. Probably his best-known project is the austere San Cataldo cemetery (i971) in Modena, which used the limited vocabulary of basic geometric forms that he repeated throughout his career. Hotel Il Palazzo (ı987) in Fukuoka, Japan, used a similar architectural language, as did his later Bonnefantenmuseum (i995) in Maastricht, Netherlands. Rossi designed only four private homes, including one in Florida's planned community Seaside\*, and his final project, an elegant residence in suburban Michigan that was completed after his death by members of his Milan office. The three-story home, of limestone, brick, cedar, and glass, is a sophisticated and mature work that is both classically referential and supremely modern. Rossi's teaching began early in his career, at the Politecnico di Milano (ı965), continuing later at the University of Venice as well as in appointments in Switzerland, Spain, and the United States. Rossi's superb draftsmanship led to museum exhibitions that featúred his drawings and color renderings.
