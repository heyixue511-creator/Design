# AMERICA: THE QUEST FOR DEMOCRATIC DESIGN ![](images/3dc66b663b9de395545e71f5821cf1a48735ef23809019cac9093dc07d33f873.jpg) illiam Morris asked, "What business have we with art at all unless all can share it?"He especially wanted individual fulfillment for craftspeople and believed this was best achieved by making things by hand. But since he also wanted the objects to be affordable, his declaration reveals a fundamental paradox of the Arts and Crafts movement: craft is labor-intensive, so a fair wage for handwork usually precluded large-scale production. Morris defined art as "the expression of man's pleasure in labour," but the goal of earning a living by its creation while making objects widely available remained elusive. "Democratic design" was the term commonly used around 1900 for simple, good-quality objects made for a broad audience. This is what Europeans perceived as America's contribution to the Arts and Craft movement, as did Americans themselves. Design reformers on both sides of the Atlantic saw the country's triumph as one of technological innovation and the rational use of the machine. Like their counterparts in Britain and elsewhere, American advocates had vowed to change society through the transformation of the work process, but their focus was on democratic design. While the term was used in European countries, it had a more complex definition here, because the United States had the most democratic political system. All the essays in this volume have demonstrated the inaccuracy of a common perception about the Arts and Crafts movement—that it was monolithically anti-industrial and antimodern. In fact, even the British movement accommodated the needs of commercial production. America (closely followed by Germany) was the least ambivalent about marketing the Arts and Crafts movement, and anti-industrialism was far less rooted in the country's culture. Consequently, the Arts and Crafts reached the broadest section of the populace here, with far less of Britain's ideological component. Historian Eileen Boris succinctly summarized the basic difference by observing that the American protagonists "democratized even as they diminished the legacy of Ruskin and Morris." The American story, however, is not a simple one about how the country diluted the movement's ideals by not adhering to its most profound aspirations. Among its many manifestations here, the movement adopted more than its fair share of Arts and Crafts idealistic paradigms: efforts to improve the lot of urban immigrants and the rural poor through craft; design schools that emphasized manual training rather than rote copying; groups of socialists (in cities such as Chicago and New York) committed to the creation of truly oppositional production methods; utopian yet unprofitable art colonies. C. R. Ashbee, a protagonist of the British Arts and Crafts movement, extolled the American art colonies for their success in placing joy in labor above wide accessibility or even quality (which was the British movement's most heralded characteristic). Summing up a series of visits, he asserted, "We cannot measure the output of these personal shops by the high standards of the very best. That is what we in England have been doing—we of the A&C—and have thus made of a great social movement a narrow and tiresome aristocracy working with high skill for the very rich." Furthermore, the United States had the largest middle class in the world, and thus the best conditions for the "do-it-yourself" aspect of the Arts and Crafts to thrive. Americans had the leisure necessary to pursue crafts as personal fulfillment—a domestic hobby apart from the white-collar world.5 Embroidery kits, blanks for china painting, and classes for woodworking were widely available, as were instruction books such as Handicrafts in the Home." Architects could reach a far broader clientele by publishing model-house designs in mass-market journals. The most famous and longest-running series (lasting more than fifteen years) was sponsored by Edward Bok, the progressive editor of the Ladies' Home Journal. In 1895 he began commissioning architects (Frank Lloyd Wright among them) to design plans and elevations for moderately priced houses. Bok also asked Will Bradley, America's best-known graphic artist, to contribute ideal interiors, all with the goal of disseminating good design to middle-class consumers (8.3). A Hungarian critic was one of many in Europe who observed that "the Americans are adapting to modern developments in their arts and crafts. Their purpose is to abandon handcraft and to work only with machinery.... This is the realization of democracy in art." While this statement about complete mechanization is as exaggerated as Ashbee's wistful musings about communities of individual happy craftsmen, both represent how Americans adapted the paradox of British ideals to American circumstances. After looking at the reform efforts in America, which were modeled closely on British precedent, this essay will explore how the achievement of product accessibility and American regional variations made the movement here distinct. The moral aesthetics of American reformers derived from Britain, most notably from John Ruskin and William Morris. Their writings were best-sellers; reading groups met throughout the United States to discuss them; American leaders invoked their names with an almost religious fervor. Arts and Crafts ideals and designs were disseminated via hundreds of organizations modeled on London's Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society. British art journals such as The Studio were widely available; an American edition, The International Studio, began publication in 1897, four years after the magazine was founded in London. Although neither Ruskin nor Morris ever visited the United States, many British leaders did make the journey. In addition to Ashbee, others—including Walter Crane, Oscar Wilde, T. J. Cobden-Sanderson, and Morris's daughter May—either gave lectures or accompanied exhibitions of their work. All met with American designers and critics, offering advice about what the country could do to further the agenda of design reform. Friendships were made during these visits; for example, Ashbee maintained close contacts with Frank Lloyd Wright and recorded visits to many other important Arts and Crafts figures, such as Will Price (the founder of the Rose Valley art colony outside Philadelphia) and Charles Sumner Greene in Pasadena. In turn, American leaders went to Britain to participate in settlement houses and art colonies, or just to meet their colleagues and often role models. Charles Eliot Norton, Harvard's first professor of the history of art, had a long-standing friendship with Ruskin and became the first president of Boston's Society of Arts and Crafts. Ernest Batchelder, who founded the Handicraft Guild in Minneapolis (and later, an important tile company in Pasadena [8.1]), traveled to Ashbee's art colony in Chipping Campden in 1905. Ellen Gates Starr Jane Addams visited the pioneering settlement house Toynbee Hall in East London and returned to Chicago in 1889 to found Hull House, which became America's most important social settlement. There, immigrants demonstrated craft activities at the Hull House Labor Museum to affirm their native traditions as a bulwark against the alienation of life in a new country, often in a hostile urban environment. Programs were also initiated to provide the economically disadvantaged with new skills, not just recreational relief from the tedium of factory work or psychological solace. Ellen Gates Starr, cofounder of Hull House, spent more than a year as an apprentice to T. J. Cobden-Sanderson at the Doves Bindery and then established a hand bindery at Hull House in 1898 (8.4). Her bindery was decorated with framed photographs of Ruskin and Morris as well as pages from the Kelmscott and Doves presses. Starr was a committed socialist who believed in the redeeming power of art and in changing the nature of labor. Like many rueful radicals, however, she would later concede, "If I had thought it through, I would have realized that I would be using my hands to create books that only the rich could buy."8 The mixture of charity and design training proved a potent one in many other media, such as ceramics, metalwork. and furniture. The designers tended to be middle- and upper-class women, as were the patron-philanthropists who sponsored their efforts. Financially supported by Mrs. James Storrow, the Paul Revere Pottery exemplifies this relationship. Established in 1908 to produce and sell breakfast and tea sets, lamps, tiles, and vases, the pottery developed out of the Saturday Evening Girls' Club at the Boston Public Library's North End branch. Pottery decoration gave the daughters of Italian and Jewish immigrants an alternative to factory employment and an opportunity to earn money while receiving artistic instruction. Professional designer Edith Brown oversaw all the work; decorators like Sara Galner executed it (8.5). Many other art potteries furthered a social agenda. The Marblehead Pottery in Massachusetts was founded in 1904 to provide occupational therapy for "nervously worn-out patients" recovering from tuberculosis." The following year, when the work proved too onerous for the patients, the pottery was separated from the sanatorium. It soon became one of the leading art potteries in America, known for its subtle glazes and abstract geometric decoration (8.6). The original intentions of Marblehead were followed by Arequipa in Fairfax, California, where the decoration of ceramics was to provide "art as a tonic" for tubercular patients, and its sale a subsidy for their stay at the pottery's associated sanatorium (8.7).'º Textiles—embroidery, rugs, weaving, and lace—were the most important media for arts and crafts in the service of philanthropy. Educational institutions such as London's Royal School of Art Needlework (8.9) inspired the establishment of American associations devoted to design training for women. As Candace Wheeler, the first professional woman interior designer in America, pointed out, the goal of the groups she was instrumental in founding in the 1870s and 1880s, like the Society of Decorative Arts in New York, was to turn "the common and inalienable heritage of feminine skill in the use of the needle into a means of art-expression and pecuniary profit." In 1879 Wheeler joined Louis Comfort Tiffany (and others) to design interiors as Associated Artists. When the successful firm disbanded four years later, Wheeler adopted the name, and she and her family operated the new company for twenty-five more years—her daughter Dora was one of the chief designers (8.8). Wheeler's interests lay in helping gentlewomen fallen on hard times or in encouraging the creative strivings of people like herself, for whom the purpose of designing was not to "prevent starvation of the body, but to comfort the souls of women who pined for independence."² New opportunities for design training and product distribution did much for women's advancement; the greater fluidity of American society opened more possibilities than were available in Europe for a woman to establish an identity as an artistic professional. The preceding discussion represents the two distinct but often overlapping types of craft activity that Juliet Kinchin describes in her essay in this volume as "home industries" and "artistic craft": the former were more numerous and less documented; the latter, far better documented and consisting of (rather than mentored by) professional artists. Each country discussed in this book had many groups that represented these two types of craft, both emphatically part of the Arts and Crafts movement. Wheeler was the cofounder in 1878 of the Women's Exchange, devoted to helping women sell products made at home regardless of their aesthetic merit, but most of the organizations she led fit the category of "artistic craft." The next decades—from 1890 to 1910—would see the establishment of hundreds of these organizations. The Newcomb Pottery, for example, was part of a women's college at Tulane University in New Orleans. It was founded in 1895, to fulfill "a dual purpose—to create pottery (and soon after other hand-crafted objects) of high artistic quality, and to provide young women at the college an opportunity to earn money. It was to be a'model' industry."13 The goal of the "home-industry" groups was slightly different—to provide impoverished immigrant or rural people (largely women) with instruction from those more economically privileged and formally trained in design (often at schools for "artistic craft"). In the American South, more than one hundred settlement schools (usually operated by Protestant missions) were established, mostly by designers from the northeast states. In North Carolina, for example, professional artists supplied local women with designs based on Appalachian weaving. These designs were modified—either to standardize production or to appeal to particular notions that northerners, who were a primary market, had about the rural South.'⁴ The mission of these home industries was to encourage the survival, or in some cases revival, of design traditions. Their efforts reinforced Arts and Crafts aesthetic values about regionalism and reflected the contemporary nostalgia for an idealized preindustrial past. The artistic crafts also drew upon a reverence for place. According to the director of the Newcomb Pottery: "The whole thing was to be a southern product, made of southern clays, by southern artists, decorated with southern subjects!"5 Consequently, magnolias, live oaks, hanging moss, cypress trees, and other southern vegetation were the primary motifs on the pottery and other crafts made there (8.11 and 8.12). The drive to preserve a national past that also informed the present could provide as strong a motivation as philanthropy. Margaret Whiting and Ellen Miller founded the Society of Blue and White in Deerfield, Massachusetts, in 1896 to preserve the techniques of eighteenthcentury embroidery they had admired in their local historical society. Whiting later explained their creative adaptation of these designs: "Since it was the spirit of the Colonial work that called for revival...the quarry of drawings and specimens of old work were used with discriminating freedom."6 The embroidery Mayflower pays homage to the American past, but it also freely departs from the colonial model (8.10). Within a few years, Whiting and Miller were successfully marketing the society's textiles throughout the United States. Trained at art schools in New York, Whiting and Miller designed all of the society's patterns, which were usually made by local women as a way of supplementing their incomes. Welloff women who summered in Deerfield also participated in the execution of the work. Passionate about quality and the preservation (as well as the updating) of a threatened craft, Whiting and Miller were not among those whose primary concern was the improvement of social conditions. This British concept of the central importance of the domestic vernacular had two manifestations in America. The first, and more significant, was the doctrine of fidelity to place and traditional techniques, which encouraged designers to look to the American landscape and past. The material culture of colonial New England and the mid-Atlantic states appealed to the moral aesthetics of Arts and Crafts movement enthusiasts. They saw in these historical precedents the "natural" and straightforward use of materials that they were advocating for modern building and design. The other manifestation, especially prevalent in the Northeast, was the direct borrowing of British styles. Partners Bertram Grosvenor Goodhue and Ralph Adams Cram provide a good example. Leaders of design reform in Boston, they helped found the Society of Arts and Crafts there in 1897, and they drew heavily from the English Gothic for their architecture. Goodhue designed the type, borders, and decorative initials for the best-known American Arts and Crafts publication, The Altar Book (8.13), which is also the one most influenced by Morris's Kelmscott Press (see fig. 2.21). Goodhue's later work embraced Spanish and Byzantine design, as well as any other vocabulary he deemed appropriate for a commission.'8 ![](images/ea17e829537800e87fea406ba52c3b3e25056deea75701a571b3ee0b13d247e8.jpg) This approach was especially characteristic of American architects and craftsmen—for them, the Arts and Crafts was not so much a style as an attitude toward the making of objects. The Philadelphia workshop of Samuel Yellin, who championed the revival of architectural wrought iron, demonstrates eclectic sources—he borrowed from medieval France and Germany as well as Renaissance Italy. His prototype for a ceiling grille is Renaissance in inspiration, but the motif of facing birds indicates that sixteenth-century Florentine artists were also borrowing from Islamic sources (8.14). Yellin might also have been looking more locally for this motif—to eighteenth-century Pennsylvania German pot tery and furniture. No matter where his sources came from, the important thing for Yellin was that the work be forged by hand. He declared, "There is only one way to make good decorative iron work and that is with the hammer at the anvil."9 In 1925 he evoked the by-then sacred name of William Morris to emphasize the ![](images/0f498727ca29959e46cf1d9ee6a2ca93227e5973b9ce8fea602f33ccd7e7cb80.jpg) absolute necessity of drawing inspiration from the past. The key was never to fall prey to "a slavish imitation or repetition of old things, which were created in a different age, under entirely different circumstances." If American Arts and Crafts ideology is most distinguished by efforts to make design more democratic, then its aesthetic is most distinctive in regional expression, as seen in the architecture and decorative arts of the West Coast and Midwest. The Prairie School was the most ahistoric of American regional styles. With Louis Sullivan and Frank Lloyd Wright as its leading lights, the Prairie School was a group of mostly Chicago-based architects (including George Grant Elmslie and George Washington Maher) who in their search for original American design "had raised their standard of revolt against the disciplined ranks and array of custom."i The architect Irving Pond wrote about this shared quest for indigenous expression, one particularly suited to the Midwest, stating, "horizontal lines...appeal to the disciples of this school as echoing the spirit of the prairies of the great Middle West, which to them embodies the essence of democracy."²These architects shared the conviction that a building must respond to its surroundings: it must be horizontal, like the prairie, and tied to the earth through low-hipped roofs, broad eaves, and extensions—porches, terraces, and pergolas. The ideal of an "organic" architecture originated with Sullivan, who beginning in the 188Os advocated the creation of a new American architecture through the simplification of mass and ornamentation based on the growth of plants. Through his voluninous writings, as well as his innovative buildings, such as the Carson Pirie Scott department store, Sullivan became the philosophical leader of the Prairie School. The members of the group were linked in many ways: some shared office space and some were instrumental in founding organizations such as the Architectural League of America and the Chicago Society of Arts and Crafts. Organic ornamentation was integral to George Grant Elmslie's work, a legacy of the twenty years (1889–1909) he spent in Sullivan's office (fifteen as chief designer). The rug Elmslie designed for the Henry B. Babson House (8.16) serves as a unifying device; its central medallion is repeated in many elements of the décor, including other rugs, stained-glass windows, andirons, and a tall-case clock. When Elmslie left Sullivan's office and began a partnership with William Gray Purcell (much of the interior furnishing for the Babson House was designed after the two became partners), they explained the function of these repeating geometric motifs abstracted from nature as "an organic part of a structure, an efflorescence of the idea represented in the building itself."3 ![](images/e12e1293a6cf3d96adca336651976694a5565c9ca54bf01937c3e652f6a67bbf.jpg) George Washington Maher followed this ideal of design unity even more fervently. He developed his own version, the "motif rhythm theory," which involved the constant repetition of a motif drawn from nature, preferably one unique to the site, or one particularly meaningful to the client. This motif would "bind the design together."4 The stylized thistle on the fireplace surround from the Patrick J. King House in Chicago, for example, is found throughout the interior (8.17). Maher embraced the notion that democracy in architecture "mean[s] that buildings should be erected to fit the actual needs of the people." The editors of a periodical devoted to the Midwest, The Western Architect, saw this as analogous to the fundamental principles of a democratic government, which they defined as "one conducted for all the people with equal opportunities and no special privileges."s This multifaceted definition of democratic design, which included not only availability to the middle classes but also an organic relationship to the landscape and an inclusive political agenda, is a particularly American interpretation of Arts and Crafts principles, most fully realized in the Midwest. ![](images/410fcef5fa28e6843f1bdacf3c1500a34d2e51b6a31170f30448d208f6ff8669.jpg) While Sullivan provided the philosophical underpinnings of the Prairie School, it was Wright who, after leaving his position as draftsman in Sullivan's office in 1893, evolved a design language that made him the preeminent member of the group. As H. Allen Brooks originally pointed out, Wright's major development was to greatly expand upon the design of interior spaces that were not enclosed in the traditional sense.26 In the innovative Ward Willits House of 1902, the corners between the dining room, living room, and porch were abolished, creating interpenetrating spaces and freeing the walls to define distinct areas rather than enclose them (8.18). The house was thus opened from within, and like the Francis W. Little House (8.19), its long horizontal lines, low-pitched roofs, overhanging eaves, and expansive porches tied it to the environment. Maintaining their traditional protective function, the walls also serve as screens, open at the corners to allow a continuous flow of space. Wright intended that the leaded-glass windows he designed for his houses also function this way, as "light screens."7 A second-story window, now known as Tree of Life, from the Darwin D. Martin House is among Wright's most revered designs (8.20). Wright's client Mr. Martin, however, had objected to using it on the ground floor as well, "because of the density of the design, which would limit the light and prevent anyone standing in the room from seeing out."8Wright insisted that his window designs did not interfere with their function and instead "add[ed] a higher architectural note to the effect of light itself."29 His furnishings were not meant to be seen as autonomous works of art, but only in the context of the Gesamtkunstwerk, the total work of art. Clients were expected to restrain their own taste and preferences to achieve the greater good of design unity. ![](images/206d8375f489e5807a9a06bcca71958c9e7431ce65c9be027e8fce54b5b788aa.jpg) ![](images/0e9f594ab4c75cdd33ba2395a6a3bbcc1762ef297e4aafd131c542363c43cdd1.jpg) ![](images/bd441336e328d6add67641411689a5b1f55ffb8ddd2d5232704d3b31c3d59019.jpg) Although few Prairie architects went as far as Wright's "destruction of the box," all shared with him the Arts and Crafts goal of design unity, and all at least paid lip service to their particular ideal of democratic design. Anthony Alofsin succinctly summarized the concept of organic architecture that Wright had stated in his "In the Cause of Architecture" (1908).3° All six principles enumerated are fundamental to the Arts and Crafts movement as well as to the beliefs of the other Prairie School architects: truth to "the nature of materials"; conventionalized (stylized) design; honest structure; simplicity; a multiplicity of styles; and the essential dictate, that the building be tied to the landscape through the use of color, local materials, and the site's own geography. The pitch of the lampshade for the lamp Wright designed for the Susan Lawrence Dana House, for example, echoes the slope of the roof of the house, and the earth tones of the glass reflect the colors of the surrounding prairie (8.22). Despite his commitment to invest individuality in specific commissions, Wright did not hesitate to reuse a design if he particularly liked it. His copper urn and weed vase reappear in several Wright residences, among them the Dana House, the Avery Coonley House, and Wright's ![](images/685a879d7bc82b26532e545d0785f145f471a78bbeb901a03ed86e876bfef603.jpg) own home and studio (8.21 and 8.23). Wright designed a chair composed of intersecting planes about 1904, and he used variations of it in his studio (8.24) and in many buildings, such as the Unity Church, the Hillside Home School, and the Larkin Office Building. Such repetition was permitted, even desired, because Wright defined organic composition as the geometric relationships that form the basis of all structures. All objects should be reduced to their basic geometry—the circle, the triangle, the square, the octagon, and the cube. These forms were easily executed by machines, so Wright believed the machine was a crucial tool for the realization of his artistic and social goals, as will be more thoroughly discussed later in this essay. Like the Midwest, California is a region that is associated with particular design characteristics. Sleeping porches, pergolas, and patios (features almost unknown in inclement Great Britain) were certainly used throughout America, but never more often or to greater effect than in California, where the mild climate and lush vegetation were most conducive to the Arts and Crafts ideal of living close to nature. The work of brothers Charles Sumner and Henry Mather Greene epitomizes the high end of Arts and Crafts architecture and design. Ashbee deeply admired their Pasadena architecture as "among the best there is in this country." Henry Mather Greene and Charles Sumner Greene The Greenes represent the Arts and Crafts at its most refined and elite; however, California also provided the movement with its most potent symbol of the democratization of artthe bungalow (8.29). Although built throughout the country, the bungalow has always been associated with California; its open interior, one-story plan, prominent porch, and low-pitched, overhanging roof offered the ventilation and protection from the sun suitable to the state's climate, and its rapid assembly, affordability, and informality made it particularly advantageous for the state's mobile society. The wood-framed bungalow was one manifestation of the local vernacular in California; other architectural styles were inspired by the state's Spanish, Mexican, and Native American traditions, as well as by the Mediterranean region. Bertram Grosvenor Goodhue, who had many commissions for buildings in California, received his most influential in 1911, when he was appointed consulting architect to the Panama-California International Exposition in San Diego. Much of the Spanish Colonial Revival was a response to his work there (8.30). As Kevin Starr points out, "Goodhue was just at that stage where he could remarkably fulfill San Diego's needs: poised between past and present, glimpsing the modern but filled with nostalgia for an imagined past."4 On the West Coast, the Spanish colonial past was idealized in a way analogous to how the English colonial past was perceived in the East: a simpler time when a spirit of community prevailed, and people lived in harmony with nature and took pride in working with their hands. This is especially true of the furniture and architecture inspired by the eighteenth- and early nineteenth-century Spanish adobe missions. In this case, the philosophy espoused by a Swedenborgian minister was embodied in a simple chair, an entirely appropriate design for a religion with the central tenet that "all things in the natural world had a corresponding meaning in the spiritual world."38 Such a belief fit in well with the Ruskinian ideals that Worcester embraced; therefore, he chose architects linked to the Arts and Crafts movement to build his church. It supported the satisfying American myth that the Spanish missions were a "distinguished chapter in the history of American colonization," instead of one characterized by the shameless exploitation and decimation of a native population.“ Equally important was how neatly the term dovetailed with the ideology of the Arts and Crafts movement. The trade journals (as well as the "shelter" magazines) capitalized on this fortuitous combination of morality, marketing, and ease of manufacture. One declared that "furniture of the mission type...is furniture with a mission, and that mission is to teach that the first laws of furniture making should be good material, true proportion and honest workmanship."The term adhered with such tenacity that it soon came full circle and was reapplied to the church's chairs. In February 1901 House Beautiful described the chairs of the Swedenborgian Church as "beautifully modeled, suggestive of the primitive old mission furnishings."2 As the United States assumed a new position of global power at the turn of the century, the need for a national American art—inextricably tied to ideals about democracy—became conflated with a need to compete economically with the major European powers. An aesthetic of simplicity was well suited to mass production and mass consumption. Straight lines and unornamented forms were far easier to achieve using machine technology than were curvilinear and carved ones. This is not to deny the philosophical idealism of the American Arts and Crafts movement or its efforts as part of the larger Progressive movement to ameliorate the great injustices and economic dislocations resulting from unprecedented urbanization and industrialization. The life of Oscar Lovell Triggs—socialist, professor at the University of Chicago, cofounder of both the William Morris Society of Chicago and the Industrial Arts League—embodies these reform principles. Triggs was devoted to the establishment of the "New Industrialism," which would "encourage the founding of...manufactories upon the basis of artistic craftsmanship" and build on Morris's belief "in the value of industrial education [as] a factor in the modern arts and crafts movement."⁴3He responded to the call for organic wholeness, convinced it could be achieved by combining proper design training with a transformation of the factory system: machines would do the soul-deadening repetitious work, allowing individual expression to flourish. Higher quality work would be done on a smaller scale in a pastoral setting, where the factory would include resources for continuing education, sports, and some farming. Architects of the Prairie School also subscribed to the ideal that the machine could be an ally rather than an adversary of human creativity. Ironically, it was at a meeting of the Chicago Society of Arts and Crafts that Wright publicly declared his allegiance to the machine as the natural extension of the movement's principles: "William Morris pleaded well for simplicity as the basis of all true art. Let us understand the significance to art of that word—SIMPLICITY—for it is vital to the Art of the Machine." Wright maintained that if machines were used correctly, they would be agents for the promotion of a democratic art as well as the simple life: "The machine, by its wonderful cutting, shaping, smoothing, and repetitive capacity, has made it possible to so use it without waste that the poor as well as the rich may enjoy to-day beautiful surface treatments of clean, strong forms."4 But professors such as Triggs and architects like Wright were not the American harbingers of the Arts and Crafts movement. This was the achievement of the entrepreneurial manufacturers, who would spread the Morrisian ideal widely, if not deeply, among the country's broad middle classes. If in Britain many Arts and Crafts protagonists were architects, and in Germany, they were most often painters who had converted to design, in the United States they were businessmen. Gustav Stickley has been mentioned earlier in the context of his role as the hugely influential founder and publisher of The Craftsman magazine, which featured articles by or about the leading practitioners of design reform in Europe as well as in the United States. In the pages of The Craftsman, Stickley, with his combination of commerce and passion, emerges as a uniquely American leader of the Arts and Crafts movement. He used the magazine as a vehicle to promote ![](images/24fa06a20c3e90c68a758822eaf564b9f685513912a37d7e38af884b0ed11aca.jpg) the furniture, textiles, and metalwork made by his company, and he published low-cost "Craftsman Homes" in every issue."5 Complete working plans could be ordered from his Craftsman Home Builders' Club, free of charge to all subscribers to the magazine, or his company could be engaged to actually construct the houses. Although he took full advantage of the economic synergy resulting from such interrelated efforts, he was genuinely devoted to improving public taste, working conditions, and the quality of American domestic life. Although Stickley published the first issue of The Craftsman as a panegyric to Morris and socialism, and devoted the second to Ruskin, he was also a pragmatic businessman. He introduced his New Furniture line in 1900, not at an exhibition society, but at the furniture fair in Grand Rapids, Michigan, the very center of the trade. What has become known as "standard Stickley," while never really inexpensive, was affordable to the middle class. Seats and upholstery were also made of natural materials—leather or rush—although for a brief period he experimented with the kind of metal and wood inlays that would seem to violate the prohibition against decoration not required for actual construction (8.35). The Roycroft community in East Aurora, New York, is another example of economic viability due to leadership provided not by an artist-designer but by a commercial proselytizer. The community's founder, Elbert Hubbard, had been a partner at the Larkin Soap Co., where he pioneered mass-marketing techniques that he later applied at Roycroft. After visiting Morris's workshops in London in 1893, he returned to America determined to establish a hand-printing enterprise along the lines of the Kelmscott Press. The Roycroft Press was immediately successful (8.38) and led to the establishment of a bindery and a leather shop. Within a few years, furniture, metalwork, and leaded-glass studios were added (8.39 and 8.40). All the goods were available through catalogues and many furnished the Roycroft Inn, where guests could stay while observing life at the community. At its height, Roycroft had more than four hundred workers. Hubbard's system was one of benevolent capitalism rather than the socialist democracy he claimed.48 Working conditions were excellent by early-twentieth-century standards, but Hubbard paid relatively low wages (albeit supplemented with gifts of food and coal) and exercised autocratic control over the community. Roycroft was financially successful; its products sold throughout the country. Furniture Shop of the Roycrofters The "World of Commerce" also ensured that the products were widely available, although at the expense of the craftsman's joy in labor and control over quality. Arts and Crafts furnishings grew so popular in the first decade of the century that many manufacturers (including Sears Roebuck) responded by focusing merely on style, without heeding any of the movement's philosophy. These companies sold cheap imitations of Stickley's Craftsman furniture, using veneers to imitate quarter-sawn oak, tenons applied to appear structural, and cast-brass and copper hardware instead of hammered materials. In silver, hammer marks could be added by hand to medieval-looking forms after the piece had been spun on a lathe (8.42). Triggs's dream for the New Industrialism was best realized by quality products and enlightened working conditions within a factory context. In 1902 Triggs published Chapters in the History of the Arts and Crafts Movement, in which he describes the Rookwood Pottery of Cincinnati as an "Ideal Workshop" (8.41).5° Rookwood was the most successful American art pottery and the first to receive international acclaim, winning major awards at expositions such as Paris (1900) and Turin (1902). In concluding this study of which aspects of the American Arts and Crafts were variants of the British movement and which were particularly characteristic of the United States, the art pottery industry serves as a useful paradigm. Just as America had emulated Britain in the establishment of settlement houses, design training, home industries, and artistic crafts, the United States also had its "pure" artists committed to creating every stage of manufacture themselves. The inability to actually earn a living in this way, or to produce in any quantity, was also analogous to British precedent. Although renowned on both sides of the Atlantic, Adelaide Alsop Robineau sold only about six hundred pieces of porcelain during her career; she supported herself instead by teaching and publishing the popular journal Keramic Studio (8.43). George Ohr worked outside the mainstream of even the most progressive art potters at his studio in Biloxi, Mississippi. He single-handedly created virtuoso vessel forms out of local clay—working them until they were as thin as porcelain, then crushing them into the desired shapes before applying equally unusual glazes—but he could not support himself through their sale (8.44). As a journal observed in 1909, "If it were not for the housewives of Biloxi who have constant need of his flower pots, water coolers and flues, the family of Ohr would often go hungry."s2 What distinguished "American" Arts and Crafts most, however, was its success with largerscale production. In the case of art pottery, this came from established commercial potteries that, recognizing a market for greater individuality, added special lines to their regular output. ![](images/f8c1cd6f99b0a7af84bc8b525ae7bc033812e0eae2443464d37af230eb313205.jpg) 8.43 Bowl, 1917. Adelaide Alsop Robineau ![](images/4ba618745cbdc827013c3abe4988279b04920d61838ceb16837a01d47ecd8365.jpg) In 1909 the Fulper Pottery Co. added the Vasekraft line, characterized by flambé and crystalline art glazes, to their production of food containers and other completely utilitarian stoneware. And in 1908 Gates Potteries' Teco Art Pottery line was described by The Studio as demonstrating that "America is coming to her own in pottery." William Day Gates commissioned designs from Prairie School architects, such as Elmslie and Hugh Garden (8.45), as well as from well-known sculptors. The successful company was already equipped for the production of architectural terra-cotta, and Gates used molds instead of hand throwing. Thus he was able to achieve his artistic goals while offering his pottery at a price the average consumer could afford. The Studio also wrote admiringly of the picturesque location of the pottery, forty-five miles from Chicago, "where the artists are in close communion with nature," and of an atmosphere infused with "the enthusiasm of the workers."53 Here is as close a description of the New Industrialism as Triggs could wish, complete with the required bucolic setting. In medium-sized shops, such as the widely imitated Grueby Faience Co. in Boston, the vases were not one-off designs and the patterns were prescribed, but each piece was thrown, not molded, and the decorators were allowed some individual variation (8.47). However, molded wares, like those in the Teco line, were not beyond the pale of full consideration as Arts and Crafts products. Some designers, such as Artus Van Briggle, who left Rookwood to start his own pottery in Colorado, believed work was best realized by creating a master mold of the original, from which slip-cast pottery was produced in plaster molds (8.46).
