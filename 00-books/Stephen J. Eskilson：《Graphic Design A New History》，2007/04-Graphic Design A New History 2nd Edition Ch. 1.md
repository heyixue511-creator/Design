The Industrial Revolution is the name given to the period when European economies shifted from a mainly rural, agricultural base to one focused on the mass production of goods in large factories. One direct result of the Industrial Revolution was the increasing concentration of the population in large cities and the consequent rise of mass culture, as merchants of all sorts of products—including artistic ones such as theater and the visual arts—sought to reach out to the millions of inhabitants of the modern metropolis. While the Industrial Revolution is usually defined in terms of the components of industry such as the steam engine, the railroad, coal, iron, and steel, it is important not to overlook how the rise of inexpensive, mass-reproduced printed materials contributed to life in the new urban setting. The steam-engine-driven press that was first developed in 1814 furthered this phenomenon. The expansion of the printing industry was immense during the nineteenth century, as, for example, the British public witnessed the establishment of over two thousand periodicals during this period—some of these inexpensive publications were printed in runs of over 200 thousand. Meanwhile, the demand for printed advertisements of varying types exploded as the purveyors of mass-produced goods sought to reach as broad an audience as possible. In 1888, John Ashton published his satirical ballad "The Bill Poster," which gives a sense of the proliferation of printed matter that covered the urban byways of London: Round Nelson's statty, Charing Cross, when any Thing's the go, Sirs, You'll always find me at my post, a sticking up the Posters, I've hung Macready twelve feet high,—and though it May seem funny, Day after day against the walls, I've plastered Mrs. Honey! With my paste! paste! paste! All the word is puffing, so I paste! paste! paste! The situation was similar in other Western cities, especially in France, Germany, the Austro-Hungarian Empire, and the United States. The expansion in printing that accelerated throughout the nineteenth century had a profound liberalizing effect on the urban populations of Europe. Taken in concert with the fifteenth-century invention of movable type, these advances that allowed for mass literacy and communication of an altogether new scale enabled the emergence of Western democratic society. The Industrial Revolution, which brought unexpected prosperity and excitement to Western societies, had a profound impact on everyday life, especially the lives of European and American women. The new urban milieu offered women and girls opportunities for economic independence through factory and retail work that would eventually lead to more dramatic political and social change such as their right to vote and to own property As women gained new, transformative roles in urban life, their engagement with design as both producers and consumers was equally transformed. While the Industrial Revolution wrought enormous positive change to Western society, it also led to discouraging developments, particularly in terms of the often brutal living and working conditions of the urban poor. Throughout the nineteenth century, reformers both inside and outside government demanded change as the lives of the workers who fueled the urban economies were often beset with unspeakable miseries, with injury, disease, and death their constant companions. In 1848, the German philosophers Karl Marx (1818–1883) and Friedrich Engels (1820-1895) produced their famous critique of the abuses of industrial capitalism, Manifesto of the Communist Party. In this essay, Marx and Engels asserted that class struggle lay at the core of Western society, and that capitalism as an economic system was inevitably to be replaced by what they saw as more just socialist and communist societies. Influenced by Marxism, an acknowledgment of the hardships of urban life for the poor led many designers to hope that their work could serve not only as a visual antidote to industrial ugliness, but also in some way as an avenue that would lead to the rectification of the many social ills of the Victorian era. While these varying attempts at social change through design often seem to have had only a minor impact on the world at large, the concept of design as a catalyzing, beneficent force for social change is one that has endured through to the present day. ## New Technologies During the nineteenth century the continuing Industrial Revolution and the rise of cities stimulated a demand for mass media and advertising on an altogether new scale. In order to meet this demand, new technologies such as steampowered presses, mechanized letterpress, lithography, and chromolithography enhanced the range of possibilities for the mass production of printed materials. Photography, perhaps the most significant invention of the century for graphic design, gradually made inroads during this period. The German inventors Friedrich Koenig (1774-1833) and Andreas Bauer (1783-1860) sold their new power press to The Times newspaper of London in 1814. It could produce over one thousand pages per hour. Koenig and Bauer's platen, a type of flat-bed press, was later superseded by the rotary steam press invented in 1843 by the American Richard Hoc (1812–1886), which could print eight thousand sheets per hour. On a smaller but no less influential scale, Stephen P. Ruggles of Boston developed what he called his “Card & Billhead" press, the first of many treadle-powered platen machines. This press, mechanized but not powered, eventually had a transformative effect on jobbing printers, who were able to increase their productivity enormously. These less glamorous workhorses of the nineteenth century allowed for the continuing expansion of letterpress printing even in the face of competition from commercial lithography later in the century. Lithography had been invented late in the eighteenth century by Alois Senefelder (1771-1834), a German playwright who had sought an inexpensive way of reproducing theatrical scripts. The chemical process he devised allowed for an image to be drawn directly onto a block of limestone and then reproduced in large quantities at low cost. Compared to the relief printing techniques used in letterpress, lithographic stones did not wear down quickly, and lithography gradually would become a mainstay of commercial printing. However, the laborious transfer of the image to the stone slowed the process considerably, and it was not until the 1880s perfection of the halftone screen process that photolithography, whereby an image could be transferred photographically, greatly speeded up the process. Early in the twentieth century, commercial lithography finally came into its own with the invention of rotary offset presses that made the technology much more competitive with letterpress printing. While Senefelder had experimented with colored inks in the early 1800s, it was other printers who in the 1830s discovered stable ways of reproducing color images. As was the case with photography, a series of competing inventors filed patents for chromolithographic technology in varying countries as the process matured. In the latter half of the nineteenth century, chromolithography, which had largely begun as the province of specialists, became a commercial mainstay. Finally, the invention of process color printing made the accurate photographic transfer of color images more feasible, if not yet commonplace, by the turn of the century. The pictorial newspaper was one of the most influential types of nineteenth-century publication. Making full use of steam-driven letterpress technology, these newspapers could be inexpensively mass produced in runs of upward of 100 thousand. Pictorial newspapers collectively provided a key conduit for the production and consumption of urban mass culture as they were replete with a mix of news, entertainment features, fiction, and poetry. The newspapers depended on wood engravings to reproduce the work of professional illustrators in order to augment and dramatize cach week's news. In England this repression came about mainly through indirect means, notably the enforcement of so-called stamp duties. A stamp duty, a kind of tax, is a system for different types of publications and legal documents whereby a small fee is required for each and every copy of the work. The first British stamp duty had been imposed on newspapers as early as the eighteenth century with the intention of not only raising revenue but also adding a barrier to the newspaper industry's expansion. The stamp duty and related advertisement duty were increased several times over the ensuing decades, eventually reaching a sum of 4d per copy in 1815. Because most nineteenth-century newspapers cost only 2d or 3d, the stamp duty more than doubled the final price. The implication of this pricing is clear: it limited the ability of publishers to create commercially successful newspaper businesses while at the same time keeping the supply of information a province of the wealthier segment of society. Few working-class people would have been in a financial position to pay 7d for a newspaper. The inexorable changes wrought by the Industrial Revolution—steam presses capable of printing upward of thousands of pages an hour and railroads to facilitate distribution, combined with a growing, increasingly sophisticated urban population that demanded information and entertainment at affordabie prices—led to the eventual cancellation of the stamp duty. It was relaxed in 1832 and completely abolished in 1855. Historically, this seems especially to have been the case with the work of women such as Lady Filmer, whose contributions to visual culture were confined to the private sphere, leaving the public arena as an exclusive province for male achievement. ## New Design Theories The idea that the Victorian age lacked significant new design ideas and instead relied solely upon revivals of historic styles is arguably the greatest of the misinterpretations visited upon the Victorians. It is of course true that the first half of the nineteenth century witnessed a wealth of revival movements as designers successively embraced diverse styles including Greek and Roman Classicism, Egyptian, various medievalisms encompassing both the Romanesque and Gothic, and Islamic design, as well as ornamental European trends ranging from the Cinquecento to Rococo and Louis Quinze. These revivals should not be reviewed as the desperate flailings of a degraded design culture, but rather as embodying the spirit of empirical inquiry pioneered during the Enlightenment (see Introduction). Each successive nineteenthcentury revival was analyzed with an almost scientific mindset, as designers sought to understand how the styles had functioned both visually and conceptually. That being said, it is true that as mass culture came to the fore, less estimable design workers often mixed historical styles in a haphazard and disharmonious fashion that was also theoretically unsound. As noted above, the nineteenth century has often been dismissed by critics as a lackluster era for design not only in the practical realm, but in the theoretical one as well. In this narrative, Victorian design theorists, blissfully unaware of the visual chaos engendered by the Industrial Revolution, practice a sort of undisciplined eclecticism, lacking a governing set of universal principles. In contrast, twentieth-century design theorists are celebrated for the depth and breadth of their theoretical commitment. A more reasonable view should take into account the manner in which nineteenth-century designers and critics— especially in England—developed most of the fundamentals of modern design theory, setting the stage for the extrapolations of the twentieth. An understanding of how nineteenth-century designers applied deeply considered theoretical principles to their work should start with the architect and designer Augustus W.N. Pugin (1812–1852), who is perhaps best remembered for his contributions to the rebuilding of the Palace of Westminster in London during the 1840s (fig. 1.7). Though not the primary architect of the building that houses the British Parliament, Pugin designed the overall ornamental system in accordance with his embrace of Gothic revivalism. The key point here is that for Pugin the Gothic style was not just some convenient visual sourcebook, but rather provided a rigorous conceptual framework for design. His oft-quoted idea from the book The True Principles of Pointed or Christian Architecture (1841) neatly summarizes his belief: The Gothic is "not a style, but a principle." This aphoristic statement shows how Pugin was attuned to the idea that design should signify on more than the most superficial visual level, and prefigures modern designers' commitment to design as a tool of social reform. Pugin also sought to evaluate buildings based on functionalist principles, and for him the Gothic still provided the best examples of rationally designed, intuitively clear, and honest structures. ![](images/bc1fb786031f6f66688fc81ce67db5ec8beb76d4acfcb6d687bb73a25cd4bb44.jpg) 1.7 A.W.N. Pugin, et al, Palace of Westminster, 1840s. Despite his progressive and pragmatic tendencies, it is true that Pugin was unable to grapple effectively with the industrial world, and his Gothic revivalism in many ways served him as an escapist dismissal of the city around him. Like many design reformers of the nineteenth century, he was unable to focus on what beauty could be found in modern life, and rather romantically yearned for the stability of the past. Furthermore, Pugin viewed architecture almost strictly through the lens of his conversion to Roman Catholicism. Therein lies the greatest deficiency of all Victorian revivalisms: past styles such as Gothic or Classical were freighted with centuries of cultural baggage. It is difficult to imagine how the complexities of the urban experience could be vitally captured by styles that already had engendered so much history and so much conflict in the European psyche. Like Pugin in England, the architect and theorist Eugène Viollet-le-Duc (1814-1879) promoted the Gothic revival to his contemporaries in France. Viollet's comprehensive restorations of many French monuments, including the Cathedral of Notre-Dame in Paris, brought him many accolades and considerable influence, although historians today often disdain Viollet for the ahistorical manner in which he worked, often favoring his own romantic view of what a medieval monument should be over an accurate reconstruction. Like Pugin, Viollet interpreted the Gothic style as embodying universal design principles that were relevant to modern society. Viollet perceived the Gothic style as one that was structurally honest—the forms of the buttressing, for example, expressive of their load-bearing function. This notion of functionalist honesty, as well as Viollet's close consideration of what forms were most appropriate for each building material— stone, iron, and so on—would have a profound impact on architects and designers in subsequent decades. In addition, Viollet's process of seeking inspiration from organic forms had a decisive effect on the Art Nouveau style of the late nineteenth century. In contrast to Gothic revivalists such as Pugin and Viollet, the influential British architect and designer Owen Jones (1809– 1874) sought to define a new modern style that did not rely so completely on the past. Jones excelled equally at theory and practice, and his The Grammar of Ornament (1856) demonstrated his critical inquiry into a multitude of historical styles—many from non-European sources—as the basis for developing a set of design principles that could be utilized by his contemporaries. This elaborately illustratedstyle manual called for designers to recognize the functionality of both objects and ornament, and to integrate them in a harmonious fashion. The Grammar included a list of 37 axioms that Jones had worked on formulating for years, the first of which advised: "The decorative arts arise from, and should be properly attendant upon, architecture." It is important for the study of graphic design to recognize that the contemporary specialization of the field is a quite recent phenomenon. Most of the history of graphic design was made at a time when cross-fertilization of diverse design arts and architecture was not exceptional, but the norm. One should also be aware of the hierarchical nature of this history; as Jones's axiom relates, it is architecture that should set the standards upon which the other design arts should rely. Jones was an architect himself, and his statement was not born out of arrogance but reflected a reformer's call for unity in the arts. This need for unity was especially important in the face of the confusion wrought by the multiple design solutions of the Victorian age. As an architect, Jones was committed to the promotion of the element of color in his work, for example in the polychromatic interior scheme he developed for Joseph Paxton's Crystal Palace in 1851. Jones also is credited with bringing a level of sophistication to color printing that was almost unheard of in the mid-nineteenthcentury. His seminal work, Plans, Sections, Elevations andDetails of the Alhambra (1836-1845) greatly advanced the technology of chromolithography. It was in the early 1830s that Jones, having completed his architectural training, completed the Grand Tour, which led him to an appreciation of polychromy in Islamic architecture, a personal discovery that would set the stage for his later career in England. In an era of numerous influential designers and critics, Henry Cole (1808–1882), because of his central role in planning and managing the 1851 Great Exhibition of the Works of Industry of All Nations, stands out as the most influential figure of the ![](images/471e093bddab68ff05623edf29cc59df1a55e66cb3a970b664a3d6ab9acf42bc.jpg) Victorian age. The Great Exhibition was held in Hyde Park, London, between May 1 and October 15. A grand financial and political success, it hosted almost six million visitors from around the world and aptly demonstrated the industrial might of the British Empire. Best remembered today for the Crystal Palace, Joseph Paxton's enormous iron and glass temporary building that housed over 100 thousand exhibits in a space totaling close to a million square feet (fig. 1.10), the Great Exhibition mesmerized the public with its displays of machinery. In the judgment of most design professionals, the exhibition mainly demonstrated how low standards had sunk in the industrial age. Recall Ralph Wornum's condemnation in his widely read article "The Exhibition as a Lesson in Taste"—quoted at the beginning of this chapter—"there is nothing new in the Exhibition in ornamental design; not a scheme, not a detail that has not been treated over and over again in ages that are gone; that the taste of the producers generally is uneducated." Wornum, a lecturer at the Government School of Design, was by no means the only critic who saw the exhibition as an aesthetic failure, but he was one of the most vocal. His sense that mid-century design had become more and more degraded, as exemplified by many works in the Great Exhibition, became the basis for his popular book of 1856, Analysis of Ornament. This book saw Wornum taking a position on one of the ongoing questions of British design: how much should it rely on natural forms? Wornum felt that ornament derived from natural forms was often recklessly and amateurishly applied to a whole range of objects. These “ornamental abominations," as he termed them, lacked any coherent visual message in regard to the function and context of the object. For Wornum, making a gas jet into a flower did not make the object beautiful but rather subverted the clarity of its function. Wornum felt that the beauty of natural forms should serve only as a model and inspiration for designers who would also grapple with the intrinsic qualities of the object that they were creating, an idea that would build momentum throughout the succeeding decades. One of the most visually engaging publications that was published as a result of the Great Exhibition was Matthew Digby Wyatt's The Industrial Arts of the Nineteenth Century (1851-1853), printed in two volumes and featuring 160 chromolithographic plates. Wyatt (1820–1877), architect and later art historian, was part of the circle of design reformers centered on Cole, and had served as secretary to the executive committee for the Great Exhibition, whereby he helped supervise the construction of the Crystal Palace. Industrial Arts represented an enormous undertaking by the printing firm of Day & Co., which was determined to assert its leadership in the field of chromolithography and to demonstrate that the technology was capable of producing reproductions of the highest quality. In order to produce 1,300 copies of such an extensively illustrated book, Day & Co. employed a team of upward of forty artists, lithographers, and administrators. Howard Leathlean has asserted that Wyatt's oversight of almost all aspects of the artistic production and printing of Industrial Arts makes him a nineteenth-century precursor to the modern art director. Once again, this is an effective rebuttal to the notion that Victorian printing was strictly the province of tasteless amateurs. The chromolithograph shown here, "Group of Indian Objects Principally Enamelled" (fig. 1.11), features a selection of decorative works from India. The subcontinent, as it was then known in Britain, was at that time part of the British Empire. Wyatt—as well as Jones, Richard Redgrave, and Cole—had expressed special praise during and after the Great Exhibition for the objects displayed in the Indian Courts. It is important to recognize the enormous effect that British colonialism had on Victorian design. While many designers such as Pugin explored historical styles that had European roots, there was an almost equal fascination with the arts of non-Western societies then controlled by the British. The Great Exhibition itself had more than anything else been designed to show off the wealth and size of the British Empire. In this manner, the Victorian immersion in global art and design styles demonstrates how the contemporary fascination, even obsession, with global cross-fertilization does not represent as new an idea as many of its proponents may think. Of course, the Victorian celebration of the fruits of its colonies today is viewed mainly as a reminder of the brutal domination, exploitation, and enslavement practiced by Europeans upon so many of the world's peoples during this era. ![](images/d6a901dfed6bea07d28e363e6f1d64f2d7c2a3c2b2ff9f522e0f87cdfd458922.jpg) 1.11 Matthew Digby Wyatt, "Group of Indian Objects Principally Enamelled' from The Industrial Arts of the Nineteenth Century, 1851-53 For critics such as Wyatt, the art and design of India was viewed through a romantic lens that stressed its preindustrial purity and natural style. THE NINETEENTH CENTURY. AN EXPANDING FIELD ## Advertising Agencies Advertising g Another important stimulus to the rise of the graphic design field was the establishment of advertising agencies. Volney Palmer of Philadelphia is generally credited with the origination of the industry around 1840, when he began working as a middleman between newspapers, from whom he bought space, and companies, to whom he sold it. The first advertising agency, N.W. Ayer & Sons, was established in Philadelphia in 1869, while prominent New York firms such as J. Walter Thompson formed their businesses in the 1870s. By 1880, there were literally hundreds of advertising agencies in all of the major cities of the United States. In 1888, an advertising executive named George Rowell founded the journal Printer's Ink which proved to be the most influential example of a new type of magazine, devoted to tracking the print industry. Printer's Ink also acted as an arbiter for the advertising industry and its clients, as it published the first reliable circulation rates for periodicals and newspapers— numbers that were the key to setting consistent rates. Journals such as Printer's Ink shone a spotlight on the successes and failures of individual agencies and their campaigns, offering for the first time a way in which the value of certain strategies could be determined. While this evidence was largely anecdotal, it paved the way for a consideration of the value, or lack thereof, in using advanced design aesthetics to lure customers. By the 1890s, most large firms employed an art director as part of a separate department, which enabled the firms to take over the design of advertisements as well as their marketing. In terms of graphic design, under the American model the art director, sometimes called an "art editor," was king. Art directors were responsible for the overall design of the advertisements, and devoted much of their energy to finding and hiring the best illustrators because representational imagery was the cornerstone of the American market. ## William Morris One Victorian design reformer who made a fundamental contribution to the establishment of the graphic design profession was the multifaceted theorist and practitioner William Morris (1834-1896). Born into a wealthy British family, Morris was one of the first to recognize that the flood of goods produced because of the advances of the Industrial Revolution all too often lacked artistic merit. From furniture to books, Morris decried the lowly state of the design arts, and contended that the urban environment need not be filled with such downright ugly objects. Propelled by his beliefs, Morris dedicated his life to improving the quality of British design. Beginning in 1861, he founded the first of a series of firms that would engage a variety of different design problems over the ensuing decades. An important influence on Morris's attitude toward the arts was thc work of the English writer John Ruskin (1819–1900). In two books, The Seven Lamps of Architecture (1849) and The Stones of Venice (1853), Ruskin asserted that industrial society had squashed the independent creativity of workmen. In the future, designers would not just reject the historical model that Morris embraced, but they would also contend that Morris's use of historicist styles was inappropriate for a new, modern urban society. ## The Advent of Graphic Design The proliferation of the mass media during the nineteenth century came initially without a complementary expansion in the design profession. Rather, the vast majority of printed materials suchas posters and books were not “designed," insofar as scant attention was paid to artistic issues of composition, drawing, and color. Instead, most books, posters, and the like were drawn up by the jobbing printers who worked on miscellaneous projects with a vast range in terms of quality. Their primary work was to operate the industrial machinery of publication. The focus of advertisers and the printers who worked for them was on simple functionality—getting a message out to the urban public. In the headlong rush to promote the latest theatrical event, circus, or soap, little attention was paid to the artistic quality of printed posters that were intended to last for only a few days, or perhaps a week before they would be covered by a new layer of advertising. In fact, one of the major concerns of advertisers during the heyday of the poster in the 1890s was the question of whether an artistically designed image would create more business than a less accomplished one. Intense competition among the numerous poster, magazine, and newspaper publishers of the day caused many to seek cost savings at every turn; most printed materials offered little in the way of quality typesetting or elegant page layout. Therefore, it is important to remember that in a narrow, technical sense much of the work discussed in the early chapters of this book does not offer examples of "graphic design” per se, but is rather part of a more generalized history of visual communication. It is hard, for example, to think of a figure like Honoré Daumier or Owen Jones as a graphic designer in the contemporary sense. The term “visual communication" is, in fact, preferred by many design historians and practitioners for this reason. Furthermore, be aware that the term “graphic design" is commonly used anachronistically by historians. It was coined by book and type designer William A. Dwiggins (1880–1956) only in the 1920s, and did not become common parlance as an encompassing term for the artful creation of visual communication until decades later. In addition, throughout this book we will review instances of people who practiced graphic design in the broad sense without pursuing it as a profession. This situation allows today's historians, using hindsight, to offer a straightforward answer to one of the most basic questions about the history of graphic design: when did the field come into existence? The answer is that the profession was established when the task of designing mass-produced material was separated from the task of printing it; this was more of a conceptual separation than an actual one, as many pioneering graphic designers produced their own work. Of course, this process did not happen all at once in a single month, year, or even decade, but was the cumulative effect of a number of different market and cultural forces at work. Also, this separation of functions was never absolute by any means, as even today there is considerable interplay between the design and actual production of visual communication. What makes the study of graphic design history so exciting is the fluid nature of the subject; throughoutits history designers have needed time and again to define and redefine themselves and their profession in the face of cultural and technological change. In this regard, the changing status of the graphic design profession will be a major theme of this book, and will reappear in a number of later chapters.
