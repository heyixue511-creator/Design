The Zenkai house project was reconstructed in 1997 in the Japanese prefecture of Hyogo, two years after a severe and traumatic natural disaster. The house was severely damaged in the Great Hanshin-Awaji or Kobe earthquake which claimed 6,300 lives. One of the hardest-hit areas was the cities and suburbs of the Hyogo Prefecture, including Takarazuka. One in fi ve homes was destroyed or

rendered uninhabitable by the shock, and a quarter of public buildings were damaged beyond repair. Any buildings constructed after 1981 mostly survived the earthquake as they were built to a new set of codes that ensured they were shock resistant. However, those that were not constructed to those standards suffered serious structural damage or complete collapse. Many of the older traditional houses in the district had heavy tiled roofs that weighed around two tonnes, structured in order to resist the frequent typhoons that plagued Kobe and the surrounding areas. These tended to be structurally overloaded, and

usually only supported by a light timber structural frame. In the earthquake, when the wood supports gave way in the tremors, the roofs collapsed the unreinforced walls and fl oors and fl attened the building, resulting in the loss of many lives and the devastation of the area.

## Analysis

Zenkai house is a document of my journey as an architect. I didn’t change the existing building at all; I just put new elements on and in it. We can see two generations of buildings now.

– Interview with architect in Zenkai House,18th August 2016. <sup>54</sup>

Constructed in the 1900s, the traditional house that Katsuhiro Miyamoto was born in was so severely damaged in the disaster that the local authorities deemed the house uninhabitable and decreed that it should be demolished. The sentimental values that Miyamoto felt for the building, the family memories, and the histories of the spaces imbued within it prompted a different response to the authorities’ desire to demolish and start again. Mixing the words for Zen (totally), with Kai (collapse), the designer did not want to forget the disaster by removing all signs of the tragedy. Instead he wanted the urban environment to remember and in some way memorialise and refl ect this tumultuous period in its recent history. Rather than lose the character of the site and demolish the row of houses in which his home sat, Miyamoto proposed to intervene upon the ruin with a new structure, one that would impact upon the existing building in such a way as to render it a hybrid of new and old structures and stand as a monument to the recent past. The new building would also serve the pragmatic function of bringing the house not only up to current earthquake resistant

codes, but also going beyond what was needed as structural reinforcement. Analysis of the existing building was deepened by Miyamoto’s knowledge of the house that he grew up in. Site is important in all of his work:

I study the site in each project for a long time. Always I can fi nd something that sits behind the ‘scenery’ of the surfaces that I am looking at. I fi nd vestiges of history, from the site or from a picture of it, anything. I analyse what has accumulated over time, looking for meaning and a way forward as a guide for the site and the new project.

– Interview with the architect, 18th August 2016. <sup>55</sup>

In Zenkai House, form and structure, context, environment and new use have all been analysed and reused, especially in relation to the designer’s close connection to the place. Most of all memory and the history of the building have played an important role in acting as a guide for the reuse of the family home and for it to be made a design offi ce for Miyamoto’s architecture studio. The familial ties were so strong that Miyamoto planned the new uses of the building to be positioned where the existing ones were housed. For instance, the main meeting room was located in the old living room. The kitchen, accessed through a small double-height enclosed courtyard, would now become the printing room and archive. Miyamoto’s offi ce was located in what was his old bedroom, complete, by his desk, with a set of notches on the surviving timber frame, documenting his growth as a child.

It is the analysis of the building, combined with the sentimentality of the memories and histories of the family that the building embodies, that has ensured that the designer has reused the building in such a

## Strategy

## Intervention

I suppose that the loads will be gradually transferred to the steel framework. The wooden framework will retire from the structure, and then it will exist just as a fi xture which retains the memory of the habitant’s lives and the earthquake disaster.

– Katsuhiro Miyamoto. <sup>56</sup>

Miyamoto chose to reuse and stabilise the domestic building by intervening with the existing wooden structure with a new steel frame. The frame was designed to act as a support and brace for the old timber beams and columns, ultimately, in time, taking over the role of the new structure from the old timber beams and columns. The designer likened this process to a patient in a hospital, wrapped in a plaster cast after an accident where they have broken their limbs. The frame was designed to be a support not just for the existing building but it was also a metaphor for the healing of the house and the city after the disaster that befell it.

The new steel frame was unceremoniously placed back and in amongst the structure of the house. Then the wooden beams and columns were lashed to the new frame. The effect upon the interior ensured that the new intervention was clearly distinguished from the old, and in some places leaving the junctions between new and old brutally exposed, enhanced its dynamic quality. The new structure was evident in each room, a constant reminder of the history of the city and the unfortunate disaster that befell it. As Miyamoto stated:

![](images/966134a7bf9f226c0ebeb541e70a97fe93f7008cf86827039517ad830ae9f403.jpg)

Usually architecture is constructed from the bottom to top. In this building it is done the other way around and we have grafted the new into and amongst the old. <sup>57</sup>

In this instance intervention was used as a commentary on the existing building. Intervention is a robust and sometimes brutal way of reusing existing space but, in this building, this vigorous intervention also expresses a sentimental dimension. Whilst the building was forcefully imposed upon, with steel work unceremoniously grafted onto and through the timber frame, columns and beams attached brusquely to the existing structures and diagonal bracings pierced through screens and walls, the new structure acted as a scaffold or a frame, supporting and nurturing the relationship between the new and the old. Instead of trying to replicate what was once there, a strategy that could have suffocated the memories of the place and instead museumifi ed the interior, the radical new intervention offered a composite construct of meaning, clearly delineating a new generation of use for the old family home.

## Tactics

The earthquake disaster exposed hidden elements of the building. When it removed the shattered walls, the frames were revealed and exposed. I left as much as I could. The plaster, the wires, the usually hidden away innards of a building I exposed to the outside world. – Katsuhiro Miyamoto. <sup>58</sup>

## Surface

Traditionally, designers will fabricate new spatial identity by imposing new surfaces, often in the form of new materials and

coverings. In a building so replete with existing memories and narratives, each surface would have exemplifi ed specifi c memories through particular materials. Miyamoto kept as much of the shattered materiality of the existing building as he could: the extant walls, surfaces and materials were retained in order to provoke a deep and profound connection to the past of the building.

## Plane

Where possible, existing screen walls, fl oors and ceilings were retained and kept. The new structure was placed in, around and through them. ## Light

A small central double-height covered internal courtyard was retained in order to allow natural light deep into the plan of the building. This also afforded a space for some of the unusual steel structural members to be positioned in the building. The courtyard offered some respite from the intensity of the structural gymnastics of the interior, in the top-lit interior space.

Sometimes an event takes place that changes a city and its urban infrastructure forever. In this context the process of rebuilding the house through the retention and reuse of its existing buildings and spaces was reliant on a series of selective and also dramatic choices. The retention of any existing buildings that are salvageable allows them to become instruments enabling both new and old to coexist in order to narrate history and chart progress, especially in a diffi cult or distressing context.
