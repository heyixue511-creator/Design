# MEMPHIS INTERNATIONAL DESIGN COLLABORATIVE WHOSE ICONOCLASTIC DESIGNS MADE POSTMODERNISM A MEDIA SENSATION. ![](images/2ad9dec4d953cb682de667925634494daf2d2870aafd748e3574f18b416febe1.jpg) Architecture might have been postmodern design's first expression, but for many people the term Postmodernism is synonymous with Memphis, whose shamelessly attention-grabbing objects flew in the face of convention. Making costly pieces from inexpensive materials, they redefined the concept of luxury products, with assertive forms and colors that defied expectations of what furniture should look like. Memphis upset the equilibrium of the design world—and the aftershocks continue to reverberate three and a half decades later. The collaborative was born in December 198o in the Milan apartment of ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design"). Bemoaning the sad state of current design, sixty-four-yearold Sottsass and a group of younger architects decided to produce and market a collection of objects unhampered by restrictions of formn, functionality, and mandated “good taste." In an oft-told story documented by journalist Barbara Radice, they chose the Memphis name from a Bob Dylan song playing on the phonograph. The initial group of twenty-somethings included ALDO CIBIC, MATTEO THUN, and MARCO ZANINI (see all under “Design"), then working for Sottsass Associati, as well as MICHELE DE LUCCHi, GEORGE SOWDEN, MASANORI UMEDA,MARTINE BEDIN, and NATHALIE DU PASQUIER (see all under "Design"). ANDREA BRANZI (see under “Design") joined soon after, and ALESSANDRO MENDINI (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), who had been involved in Studio Alchimia\* with Branzi and Sottsass, contributed to the collection. The roster of international participants ultimately included PETER SHIRE (see under "Design” and "Living with Postmodern Design"), JAVIER MARISCAL (see under "Design"), and SHIRO KURAMATA (see under “Design"), as well as architects ARATA ISOZAKI (see under “Architecture"), MICHAEL GRAVES (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design"), HANS HOLLEIN (see under "Architecture"), and ARQUITECTONICA (see under "Architecture"). The first collection of fifty-five pieces by twenty designers included Sottsass's Carlton bookcase, which would become an icon of postmodern design, as well as Bedin's appealing four-wheeled Super Lamp. In truth, it was not so much a collection as a series of unrelated objects— Memphis pieces were intended to clash with their surroundings, and with one'another. Instead of materials generally associated with quality furniture, the designers used bright plastic laminates in kitschy patterns, printed glass, celluloid, neon tubing, and plated sheet metal. Although each item had a function, practicality took second place to visual impact. Furniture pieces were initially named for luxury hotels; lamps and fabrics for exotic locales. Despite its fun-loving facade, however, Memphis design was rooted in deeply serious ideas. Its members rejected the commercialization of design and the constraints imposed by self-appointed arbiters of taste who dictated the form and function of objects. The Memphis project was an outgrowth of Alchimia's experiments in transgressive design but though Alchimia's designs were prototypes, Memphis objects were actually produced. A primary supporter of the project was ABET LAMINATI, whose products were used in much of the collection; Memphis objects were virtual billboards for the firm's candy-colored, riotously patterned laminates. Lighting manufacturer Artemide\* was the other major backer, agreeing to produce and market the collection. Most production was contracted to a variety of small, specialist firms that were willing to devise new techniques to make the designs, most of which were not compatible with conventional production methods. The Memphis collection made its debut in the Arc '74 showroom at Milan's Salone del Mobile in September i981. The launch itself was a major event, drawing some two thousand people who waited in long lines, creating traffic jams that delayed the arrival of the principal designers. Memphis was an instant phenomenon, its impact eclipsing anything else presented at the fair. The New York Times called it "an outrageous collection of bizarre colors and shapes," but acknowledged that it was “an effervescent, seductive and undeniably sympathetic group."24 The founders referred to their project as the “New International Style." Whatever their designation, however, Memphis's extravagant, extroverted creations were impossible to ignore. The media-savvy group took pains to be noticed by the press, and their designs drew unprecedented coverage, both favorable and unfavorable, in the international press; De Lucchi, in a 2oo9 interview, recalled seeing the work on fifty-two magazine covers in its first six months.26 The design community was equally polarized. In a much-quoted comment, British designer Jasper Morrison\*, a student at the time of the Memphis launch, confessed to being “repulsed by the objects but also freed by the rule-breaking.' And Museum of Modern Art chief curator Paola Antonelli, then studying in Milan, thought it atrocious."²7 But others were captivated; fashion designer Karl Lagerfeld furnished an entire apartment with Memphis, as did photographer Helmut Newton. The collection made its American debut in October i982 at Memphis at Midnight, an event at Furniture of the Twentieth Century that according to the New York Times drew more than three thousand people to a Chelsea loft, where it met with a similarly polarized response. Architecture critic Paul Goldberger wrote that it was "brash, exuberant, loud and bears roughly the relationship to classic modernism that punk rock bears to Stravinsky"²8 but acknowledged it as a sign of vitality in modern furniture design, noting the appeal of objects that, notwithstanding their subversive elements, could not fail to elicit smiles. Memphis designs were sold through galleries like Art et Industrie\* and Max Protetch\*, both in New York. Distributed nationally through Urban Architecture, primarily to architects and designers, they developed a dedicated following of avant-garde collectors. Following fashion industry practice, Memphis made special events of its annual product introductions, generating attention from the media and a public becoming more familiar with, though not universally enamored of, the styles. Later collections modified the initial exhibitionism in more accessible objects, particularly in ceramic and glass. An exhibit of Memphis design, including objects and original architects' drawings, opened at the Brooks Museum of Art in Memphis, Tennessee, in 1984,29 traveling to ten American museums before arriving in i986 at the Cooper-Hewitt Museum in New York, its first major exposure in the city. Exhibitions were also staged in London and Tokyo. The designs were intended to make a point, rather than a profit. This was fortunate, since limited production, by hand, meant that the pieces were costly,3' and therefore affordable only for a small market of adventurous consumers. With few exceptions, even the well-received items sold no more than fifty pieces. Memphis published a new catalog every year, introducing a total of some 65o products, including furniture, lamps, jewelry, tableware, fabrics, even neckties and underwear. By the third collection, the designs were less adventurous, and many were factory-produced in larger quantities, diluting the original concept. Weakened by Sottsass's departure in i985, the group disbanded in 1988. The collective's name and design copyrights were subsequently sold, and though many of the pieces are still being produced, the original Memphis became a historic artifact, representing a moment in design whose impact reverberated well beyond its brief life. In terms of commercial success, Memphis was a failure; its importance lies in its influence.
