Artists' books became a major form of creative expression in the 1960s. Although books were part of earlier movements and had been an important aspect of early twentieth-century avant-garde poetics and art, the 1960s witnessed a new era of book enthusiasm in conceptual, minimalist, and pop-art circles. The ideal of the book as an almost neutral, massproduced, anticraft. and quasi-industrial form was epitomized in the work of Ed Ruscha. Books like his were meant to circulate outside the gallery system and reach a mass audience—a dream that did not come to pass in quite the way it was conceived. 13.10c The Censored Review, edited by Ron Padgett, 1963. Set on a typewriter and produced on a mimeo machine, this journal exemplifies the activity of independent literary presses intent on self-producing small editions that circulated outside of mainstream publishing networks. Writers and poets whose work was later widely acclaimed were first published in such formats. In San Francisco, posters for major bands and music clubs became vehicles for cutting-edge graphics by talented designers. Many eschewed the use of photography, producing instead intricate and suggestive drawings with interlacing designs. Vivid Day-Glo effects, many produced through skilled manipulation of standard process inks, made for a hallucinatory quality in the posters that papered the streets. In California, psychedelic style also epitomized the bands whose sound came to symbolize the era—The Grateful Dead, Country Joe and the Fish, Frank Zappa and the Mothers of Invention, Jefferson Airplane, Big Brother and the Holding Company, and many more (Figs. 13.11a and 13.11b). Revolutionary culture and protest In the United States, protests against the Vietnam War intensified in the late 1960s. Organized resistance to official policies and military actions produced an enormous output of graphic images. Turning mainstream imagery against itself was one approach of protest art. Designing posters in the passionate spirit of classic works was another. An eighteenth-century painting by Francisco Goya or an Expressionist print by Käthe Kollwitz might provide the inspiration for a quick graphic response to events.
