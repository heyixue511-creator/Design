# TOMÁS TAVEIRA b.1938 VERSATILE PORTUGUESE ARCHITECT WHO MOVES EASILY FROM MONUMENTAL BUILDINGS TO WHIMSICAL OBJECTS. RIGHT Interior of Torres das Amoreiras, Lisbon, Portugal, 1986. Sometimes described as the enfant terrible of Portuguese architecture, Tomás Taveira has enjoyed a career combining local notoriety with international renown. As a major star in a country not generally known for innovative design, he has designed buildings that changed the landscape of a city, and witty collections of objects that reveal his whimsical side. Taveira was born in Lisbon to a poor family, and at the age of seventeen began working as a junior assistant in the architecture office of Nuno Teotónio Pereira, where his drawing talent was noticed and nurtured, and he enrolled in the Universidade Técnica de Lisboa to study architecture. He worked with the firm of Francisco da Conceição Silva before establishing his own office, and later traveled to America, receiving a graduate degree in urban planning and design from Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1978. His sometimes flamboyant architectural style was not readily accepted in Portugal, a country once restrained by dictatorship and coming late to modernization. Taveira's designs were a challenge not only to the bland Modernism of the time, but to a population unaccustomed to color and ornament. Impossible to overlook his bold postmodern buildings brought him both celebrity and criticism. Opened to great fanfare, the lavish project of aluminum, glass, marble, and concrete was a catalyst that transformed an area formerly used for parking buses into a thriving cosmopolitan center in downtown Lisbon. The shopping mall, a lavish facility of more than 430,000 sq. ft (40,000 sq. m) framed by three huge towers, has become a major attraction. Nevertheless, Taveira remained enamored of his native culture, and was not averse to including references to it in his work; a major development project for BNU bank (i989) produced a cluster of imposing office buildings with a bicolor facade and arch-topped silhouettes suggesting a supersize Portuguese guitar. Other buildings, in a variety of forms, use sherbet-bright color and lively, patterned tiles that reflect his Mediterranean heritage as well as a postmodern aesthetic. Reflecting their shared sensibility, ALESSANDRO MENDINI (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") wrote the introduction to a book on Taveira's work.37 Taveira's collection of decorative pieces, which he called Transfigured Objects, is a totally unexpected change of pace, evoking traditional Portuguese crafts but with a personal touch and considerable wit. It includes striking cabinets, wooden chairs with saw-tooth backs and psychedelic patterns, and a series of traditional ceramic pieces that he transformed into exotic, polychromatic works of art. With his provocative personality and maverick style, Taveira has seen many of his most original proposals remain unbuilt, but his idiosyncratic approach has attracted like-minded clients in many countries, and he has worked in locales as wideranging as the Middle East, East Asia, South America, and Africa, as wel as his native country, on projects ranging from office buildings and urban complexes to airports, stadiums, hotels, and hospitals. He was also able to indulge a lifelong interest in film direction by working on television and concert set designs, and in Lisbon he designed three important stadiums for the 2004 Summer Olympics.
