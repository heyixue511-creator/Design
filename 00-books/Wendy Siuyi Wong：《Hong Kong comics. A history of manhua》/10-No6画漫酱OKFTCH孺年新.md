## 1-8.

## The Sketch (Bun-gok Manhua), 1929 Editor: Ye Yin-quan

A year after China's first cartoon magazine, Shanghai Sketch, was published, this competitor appeared in Guangzhou. Six issues of The Sketch were published each month; twelve issues made up a single volume. Although published in Guangzhou, it was widely available in Hong Kong, where it had been printed during its first six months. The main attraction of the early volume of The Sketch were the stories of Brother Ho and Mister Tai-shi, by Ye Yin-quan. Beginning with volume four, the cover was printed in full color, and the magazine took on a more cynical tone. From this point on, there were two types of content inside: First, there were realistic stories of Guangzhou life, including scenes of markets, pawnshops, funerals, and even singing on the street. Second, there was the more risqué side of The Sketch; pictures of women were used to attract readers. The Sketch was targeted at readers in southern China, which meant its style was quite different from Shanghai manhua. Its main contributors were Ye Yin-quan, Lee Fan-fu, Li Maoji, and Huang Meng-tian.

## 9-12.

## Modern Miscellany, 1930

## Art: Various artists

The bulk of this magazine was devoted to news, society and tourist photographs, and other editorial features. However, manhua were very important to its popularity. Each issue's inside back cover carried a twelve-panel comic strip, Ye Qian-yu's Mr. Wang. (Mr. Wang was the first Chinese manhua character; he originally appeared in Shanghai Sketch.) Modern Miscellany also included regular contributions by Zhang Guang-yu, Zhang Zhen-yu, Huang Wennong, and Shao Xun-mei.

## 13.

Chau Yat-ching, 1946 Art/Script: Yu Ming

Chau Yat-ching was one of the first manhua published in Hong Kong. The main character, Chau Yat-ching, came from a wealthy family, but his life consisted of a series of humorous blunders. Chau also represented the modern young man, wearing Western suits rather than traditional Chinese clothes.

## 14.

Fong Tong-geng Teases Chan Mung-gat, 1946 Art/Script: Ng Fa-pang

This early comic followed the two title characters' competitive and collaborative adventures. Their costumes and bald heads dated from the Qing dynasty.

## 15–19.

Wu-lung Wong (King of Blunders), 1946 Art/Script: Lui Yu-tin

Wu-lung Wong first appeared in 1946 in Ghuangzhou's Chum Daily newspaper, and it soon became one of the most popular serial comics in southern China. The story, about life in postwar Guangzhou, criticized the unfairness of society and the incompetence and ugliness of Nationalist Party officials. After it appeared in the newspaper, Wu-lung Wong comics were collected in book form; sometimes several issues would be released in a month. Circulation soon jumped to 50,000 to 60,000 an issue, and distribution widened to include Hong Kong, Macau, and other locations in the Guangzhou province. After the 1949 Communist takeover of mainland China, Wu Lung-wong continued to be published in the Hong Kong newspaper Chung Sing Daily. From the comic’s position on the front page of the paper, it is clear that the series was very popular. It was also later made into a film.

## 20–21.

Man in Suit, 1947

Art/Script: Tou Jo-wai

The main character of this comic always wore a Western-style suit, which symbolized the “modern man” in late-1940s Hong Kong. The comic was a sarcastic critique of the well-dressed man, who took advantage of his friends and played around.

## 22.

Sporty Aunt, 1947

Art/Script: Sze-to Dip

Created in 1938 as a newspaper strip, Sporty Aunt related the romantic affairs of the pinup girl Third Auntie, the beautiful Ah-ling, Director Sheung, and Uncle Chan. The strips were collected in this book after the war.
