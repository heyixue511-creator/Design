Without the need to produce more than one of each object, there was little risk in exploring a different aesthetic. Though postmodern furniture required audacious buyers, smaller objects proved less problematic. Producers including ALESSI, CLETO MUNARI, SWID POWELL, and ACME commissioned leading architects to design products that generated considerable publicity, though not necessarily considerable sales.'5 The most prominent Italians of the Memphis group, as well as others including Hollein, Botta, Venturi, and Graves, designed silver coffee services and tableware, porcelain dinnerware, and jewelry that enabled them to be creative in different media on a smaller scale. Although the producers recruited an international roster of talent, Postmodernism had definite geographic limits: Italy and the United States were its touchstones, with major architects in the United Kingdom and Japan, and a few elsewhere in Europe. Creating postmodern interiors proved more challenging than designing objects: elements like classical columns and pediments could be used to sculpt backgrounds or reconfigure space, but postmodern furniture tended to dominate an interior and was generally incompatible with other styles. Relatively few interiors apart from those of committed collectors adopted postmodern design, and the few that did were more likely to be designed by architects than by interior designers, who generally gave a wide berth to the style. Notable exceptions were design showrooms, which, being frequently redecorated, were committed to keeping au courant, and spaces by ARATA ISOZAKI and Graves for Sunar-Hauserman\*, Stern for Shaw-Walker, THOMAS BEEBY and STANLEY TIGERMAN for FORMICA, and MICHAEL VANDERBYL for Bernhardt were among the most striking interiors. None, of course, survive. Commercial interiors in postmodern style—office buildings, restaurants, theaters, and such—required to be more enduring, were understandably more conservative. A noteworthy exception was Disney, whose Swan and Dolphin hotels were designed by Graves to make the flamboyant most of Postmodernism's double coding, projecting symbols to which the general public could immediately respond. In textiles and wall coverings, Postmodernism produced spirited patterns in sparkling hues by designers like Sottsass and NATHALIE DU PASQUIER, providing welcome relief from the blandness of most contemporary offerings. Some patterns recalled New Wave graphics and Russian Constructivism, but the most original of them combined Memphis-like clashing shapes and colors. Like most postmodern furniture, however, they were too extreme to make major inroads in the marketplace. It produced kaleidoscopic designs in posters, album covers, and magazines with pop, cartoony or abstract-geometric motifs. It also encouraged exciting new approaches to typography. American designers, including David Carson\*, APRIL GRIEMAN, ED FELLA, and KATHERINE MCCOY, contributed some of the most successful of these. No matter how widely accepted in the marketplace, any style will begin to fade, yielding over time to reassessment or reinterpretation. Postmodernism was no exception, as inferior iterations at mass-market levels eviscerated its appeal to the design cognoscenti. In December 1986, Interior Design reported that Postmodernism had been “diagnosed as seriously ill"'7 when students in London walked out of a Graves lecture, and three months later the New York Times speculated about a change in attitude that was either a “backlash" against Postmodernism's kitschy interpretations or a sign that the style had simply run its course.8 By the final years of the decade, Postmodernism was being referred to as a movement in decline, if not altogether moribund. It was, most probably, to be expected. A highintensity style marked by rejecting convention without defining new principles was difficult to maintain. The political message of the radicals, the refnement of the architecture, and the exuberance of Memphis had been diluted by low-budget copies and kitschy trinkets in formulaic motifs and colors. Its cutting edge dulled, Postmodernism became as repetitive and predictable as the Modernism it sought to replace.
