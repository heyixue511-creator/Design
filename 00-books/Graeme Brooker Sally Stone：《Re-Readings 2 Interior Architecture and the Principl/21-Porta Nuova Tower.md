# Porta Nuova Tower

Location Arsenale, Venice

Former function Naval building

New function Exhibition and conference centre

Date 2011

Designer MAP studio

![](images/7aa0580225a10738c3197797c2952d8be71aa4fba5fbe8882f3737f86f410387.jpg)

The concept of an echo or ghost of an original element can be the generator for the adaptation of a building, especially if the original object is invested with particular meaning. The architect can choose to engage with the concept of replication, reproduction or imitation. The copying of simple objects can reveal ambiguity, it can shift their identity and introduce an element of uncertainty. The act of duplication is invested with shared meanings, in that the memory of an object is often a distortion of the original.

Figure 2.22 The tower from the edge of the canal.

This approach of creating a copy, a simulacrum or impression of an existing object is the methodology that MAP studio took to the remodelling of the Porta Nuova Tower in the Arsenale area of Venice.

The Arsenale was the heart of the Venetian naval industry from the 12th century and is a great symbol of the ancient maritime power. It was once the supreme shipyard of the world and held the most formidable navy. Jan Morris, in her book Venice, describes the war-readiness of the Venetian Navy. “As soon as the alarm was sounded the first warships were towed out of the Arsenale and an almost Fordian succession of activities were rapidly completed to make the boat ready for sea. First supplies, then cordage, small weapons, oars, and fi nally at the entrance to the Arsenale, the crew leapt aboard, and thus the galley sailed away ready for action. The Porta Nuova Tower, which stands at the lagoon of the north side of the dock, was built at the beginning of the 19th century, and was constructed as a building to fi t the masts onto the ship.”<sup>11</sup>

The Arsenale area is no longer used by the military, and this project is part of a wholesale remodelling of the workshops and warehouses. The incredibly solid and necessarily strong stone building was divided into three, both horizontally and vertically. The interior is tripartite and contains three full height spaces, while the exterior is classically a tower, with a base, shaft and top. The project recognises and interprets this; the new elements are as visually strong and robust as the original building, although notably of the 21st century. The architects have inserted an exhibition area, conference space, laboratories and library into the building. The materials used are exposed concrete, fi bre-cement fl oor panels, waxed COR-TEN steel. These contain the same magnitude of expression, but contrast quite simply with the existing stone and timber.

The waxed COR-TEN staircase is a most magnifi cent single element within the space. It sculpturally rises through the whole open interior space and is a dramatic insertion in the heart of the building. It doesn’t touch the edges of the walls, but is specifi cally designed to fi t tightly within the existing alcove. It is an extraordinary echo or impression of the still existing but structurally unsafe original timber staircase, which sits immediately next to it. The new imitates the old.

The solidity of the staircase, the sense of its completeness, and the repetition of the structure echoes the language of the Minimalist sculptures or works of the

mid-20th century. A constant play between the experience of perception and the understanding of the insertion has been created. The staircase contains the qualities of wholeness produced by the simple geometric forms, thus there is a comprehension of the complete object. The staircase is not perceived as a collection of risers, treads, and landings but as a simple geometrical undivided object. MAP studio have created a timeless insertion, something that is both sculptural and useful, dramatic and solid, and yet speaks of the future for the building and the area.

## Installation

## Introduction

To install something is to place it in position and for it to become established in that location. The exact position of the object is composed, in that it is deliberately situated in relation to the things around it, whether these are other objects, or the surrounding context. It is a particularly useful remodelling strategy. The architect can curate the interior; they can select, organise and present precise and specific objects or works in a particular manner. They can design structures that are self-contained and completely self-supporting. They can create an installation that is demountable, reversible, and exchangeable. Because an installation is maybe cheap, it can be investigative, clever and therefore more experimental. It can ask questions that maybe more permanent architect can’t, and it can be magical, controversial, awkward, provocative, emotional and wonderful.

The term Installation Art first came into use in the 1960s, and was used to describe the way in which an exhibition was laid out. Consideration was given to adjustments of scale, appropriateness, placement, style, materials etc. It was particularly popular with Minimalist artists who were acutely aware of space in which they were exhibiting, and the ways in which their work related to it. Their work was often sensitively arranged, or installed, to create a dialogue with its surroundings, so viewers could take in both the work and its environment as one overall, immersive display. An installation was and still is a total, unified experience.

The relationship between the installed object and its immediate context is always somewhat tenuous. The installation is not necessarily permanent and therefore it often has a transient or fleeting quality. It may be deliberately unsettling within the space, too big, too bright, the wrong

shape, and this ephemerality creates tension: tension between the objects and the context, and tension between the individual objects themselves. This anxiety can heighten awareness of the objects or elements, and also of the container or situation. The fugitive qualities accentuate the impermanent relationship.

Installation is reversible. The objects, works or elements can be removed and the host space will revert to its previous incarnation. The carefully conceived and placed elements will have no lasting impact upon the building; they will not have caused any damage. Within a remodelled building, the strategy of installation is often used when the host building is particularly precious, when it is of great conservation value, or is exceptionally fragile.

In 2007, Merkx+Girod architects created a contemporary bookshop in a former Dominican church in Maastrict, in the very south of the Netherlands.

The Gothic church, which was consecrated in 1294, had been considerably changed over the years, but still retained its huge somewhat romantic quality. In order to preserve the character of the church, while also creating the necessary commercial space, the architects installed a multistorey, black steel structure to contain and display the books. This was positioned off-centre in the length of the nave, thus creating usable shop space within the resultant larger space and a reading area within the smaller.

The café, which is situated in the apse, contains a huge cruciform table, which is lit by an enormous suspended corona, or huge circular light fitting. This is a somewhat audacious act, and had the installation been perceived as a permanent feature, then it might have been badly

received. But the ephemerality of the design allows for this impertinence.

The church was fully restored, and the utilities and other equipment have been housed in the extended cellar. The installation did not affect the integrity of the building; should the need ever arise, all the elements of the bookshop can be removed. The architects have created an extremely dramatic, atmospheric and completely reversible installation.

## Kiang

Location Vienna, Austria

Function Café

Date 1985

Designer Helmut Richter

![](images/b5a13efad0ee6c1b80e02d3a93b849eaf25e744a0c04e25f47721bdec2c47aac.jpg)  
Figure 2.26 The spatial progression through the restaurant is controlled through the placement of a series of carefully designed objects.

In Vienna there exists a collective certainty about the spatial connection between eating and communication. There is a pronounced tradition of the coffee house. It is somewhere that is much greater than a place to consume coffee, it is part of the cultural fabric of the city. Indeed the Viennese coffee house or Wiener Kaffeehaus is so important to the culture of the place that it is listed by UNESCO as part of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Austria. The Viennese have a name for the spatial harmony that exists between eating and communicating: Gemütlichkeit. This is one of those words that refers to sensation, and so is somewhat difficult to translate, but really means that feeling of cosiness, contentedness, comfort and relaxation that is experienced when in a comfortable chair, drinking good coffee surrounded by friends. In Vienna a system of semipublic hospitality has evolved, one in which the acts of eating and drinking, seeing and being seen are overlaid within the coffee house.

The Kiang Restaurant has been inserted into the ground fl oor of an apartment block in the city centre. A series of independent skins or linings divorce the restaurant from the building and thus these, rather than the containing masonry, form the space. This use of cladding to create space is very much in the tradition of the Viennese architects such as Otto Wagner or Adolf Loos, but Kiang lacks the formal geometry of these earlier buildings. Kiang is at once both much more relaxed and more dynamic. The linings act to create a space that is at odds with the original building. The architect Helmut Richter uses a diagonal and a curve to instil a slight sense of unease within the traditional confi nes of a Viennese café.

The fi rst skin is made of slightly obscured glass and placed in front of the façade of the building, deliberately upsetting the conventional relationship between the exterior and the interior of the building. This new plane contains full-height sliding windows with fl uorescent lighting tubes placed immediately above each opening. This semi-transparent external skin signals the new use, while also providing additional entrances and seating alcoves when the weather permits.

Figure 2.27 The surface-mounted proud window frames subvert the usual organisation of the building.

The stainless steel counter has been swivelled away from the orthogonal geometry of the interior walls. This not only provides suffi cient space for the small kitchen, but also directs the visitor towards the rear of the café and the enormous free-standing, bright red, stretched plastic screen. This curving stretched plane of lorry tarpaulin sits slightly forward of the end wall of the interior space and defi nes the room while also discretely shielding the route to the WCs. Another much smaller perforated steel screen, which is situated at the point where the interior has a single step, helps to control the extent of the eating area and acts as a coat stand. The Wiener Kaffeehaus has been dragged into the modern era. Richter has created an interior that subverts and updates the established nature of the traditional coffee house through the installation of a series of dynamic elements made of contemporary materials, deliberately breaking the conventional rules of the existing building and by extension the tradition of the coffee house.

## Harbour Bath

Location Copenhagen, Denmark

Designer Bjarke Ingels Group (BIG)

Date 2003

![](images/81a12407537a3c8573e662b8204ebb81e8b4c49384642466cf9d910df0927db0.jpg)

The revitalised front is a common feature of many post-industrial port cities. These landscapes of production have become obsolete, and their adaptation and reuse has become an important element in the revitalisation of the urban environment.

Figure 2.28 The diving board and the lifeguard’s seat are sculptural objects within the horizontality of the harbour.

For example, Liverpool in the UK has transformed a once bustling port into a thriving cultural area. Museums, galleries, concert halls and shopping now fi ll the magnifi cent port buildings and the harbour frontage.

Of course, this wasn’t achieved without considerable anguish; there were decades of empty and forgotten despondency before the metamorphosis was instigated.

The harbour in Copenhagen has been transformed from an industrial port and traffi c junction into a cultural and social centre for the city; a development which is an integral strategy for regeneration of public life along the water’s edge. The harbour was active until the 1980s, and had always acted as a barrier between the residential area and the water. It was horribly polluted, not just from the waste of the industrial area, but also from the effl uent of the city, which at one time drained directly into it. The city has invested heavily in sewage treatment to improve the quality of the water, and also in the transformation of the water front area into a park: a facility for local people to use.

The idea of leisure has evolved in the 21st century: from self-improvement, through tourism to therapeutic recreation. Leisure is reinvented by succeeding cultures and by the values and preoccupations of each. The link between leisure and health is very much a contemporary preoccupation, and so it is common for people to want to exercise during their non-working hours. But this too has also progressed; the sterile environment of the gym is no longer attractive to many people who want to exercise outside. They want to take advantage of their own environment, to be able to enjoy themselves in a sympathetic landscape, conducting different activities while still being able to appreciate the benefi ts of fresh clean air.

Figure 2.29 The baths are part of the revitalisation of the whole quayside, and as such belong to both the land and the sea.

The public transport strategy for the Copenhagen Harbour Front project transforms the water into a virtual street, with continuous pedestrian and bicycle circulation along both sides of the harbour. To further add interest and experience, a series of independent interventions were strategically placed along the promenade. The most dramatic of these is the Harbour Baths designed by BIG. This is a contained timber platform or terrain on which to play and to rest. The wooden frame contains fi ve seawater pools of various types, including a wading pool for children and people with mobility challenges, a 50-metre lap pool, and a diving pool. The most dramatic and extraordinary architectural feature is the stair-like structure that serves as a diving platform and performance area. The complete baths complex is movable, so can be transported along the quayside when necessary. It is also sustainable; the timber boards were grown locally, so that replacement timbers are readily available and easily sourced.

The adaptation of the rotting and obsolete landscape into a vibrant playground makes it possible for the citizens of Copenhagen to go for a swim in the middle of the city. The Harbour Bath has created a new type of public space for the city, it offers an urban harbour landscape with dry-docks, piers, boat ramps, cliffs, playgrounds and pontoons. Copenhagen has a direct relationship with the water, it is said that no citizen is more than 15 minutes from the sea. This intervention exploits this to create a progressive contemporary playground for the 21st century population. By cleaning the water in the former harbour area and building fl oating interventions, an unused space of the city is transformed into a space of recreation.

## World

Location BBC, London

Function Public installation

Date 2015

Designer Mark Pimlott

![](images/91999a3c30cf1b11d02ca73f89ced9f9a06189b1bb3c090af5eb3dc3f889891a.jpg)  
Figure 2.30 The names are grouped together by personal association rather than location.

The term public art refers to art that is in the public realm, regardless of whether it is situated on public or private property, or whether it has been purchased with public or private money. Generally public art is commissioned to occupy a specifi c site, and of course there are many examples of monument, memorials and civic statues that inhabit important civic locations within the urban environment. During the latter half of the 20th century, when art began to be removed from the rarefi ed atmosphere of the gallery and placed within the public arena, the nature of public art began to change. It could be transitory, in the form of performances, dance, theatre, poetry, graffi ti, posters and installations. Public art could also be subversive, antagonistic and political. The nature and form of permanent public art also changed: traditional statues and sculptures are now generally avoided while more abstract notions of art are employed. A public installation can represent the ethos of a company or organisation, in that it can translate and make solid the principles or ideas that the organisation was built upon. A public installation can communicate the character of those that commissioned the piece in a positive and creative manner.

World is a pavement artwork created by Canadian-born artist and architect Mark Pimlott. It is a permanent installation at BBC’s Broadcasting House in London, and expresses the global dimension of the

BBC, whose motto is ‘Nation shall speak peace unto nation’. The artwork has been installed in the U-shaped public space between the buildings and acts to connect them to each other, to the streets of London, and to the world beyond.

World refl ects the global dimension of the BBC and consists of 750 stone fl ags that have been inscribed with the names of places from around the globe. These are connected together with elegant steel rods that act as lines of latitude and longitude. This ‘fragment’ of a much larger globe is enhanced with inset lights that can be imagined as constellations of stars or cities, and speakers, which are set into the surface of the installation and each of which whisperingly transmits live world service broadcasts in different languages.

There is no global accuracy to the positioning of the names; rather they are grouped together in the hope that lateral associations will be made, that personal connections will occur and accidental images will emerge as visitors stroll over the pavement towards the entrance of the building. The selection of names was informed by the artist’s own experience, memory and knowledge. Thus places were selected because they represented something to him, as well as having a wider bearing within the wider public consciousness.

![](images/3a5a5268bff83f5761b24d9a5bea1f402f085d786942df849bcb03805c33c6dd.jpg)  
Figure 2.31 ‘World’ is an installation that fi lls the courtyard of the BBC with murmurs, obscure connections and dreams of distant lands.

So for example, ‘Coney Island’ is featured in a little cluster of other islands, some remote, others famous. The notion of the island is what connects these names together: just the word island. Pimlott revealed that “Coney Island appeared because of its evocation of ‘everyman’ relaxation, Luna Park, photographs of the great hordes on the beach made by Weegee, the hot dog eating competition, a particular conception of summer. I visited only once, many years ago, a rather melancholic affair, not uncommon to all seaside resorts”. <sup>11</sup>

World is a strangely beautiful and quite emotional installation. It expresses the importance of the organisation it represents, not by bravely shouting or exclaiming its importance, but through whispers, shadows, engraving and association.

Former function Opera House

Original date 1875

Remodelled 2011

The Palais Garnier is perhaps the most recognisable opera house in the world, not only for the opulence of the architecture and the magnificence of the setting, but probably due to its use as the setting for Gaston Leroux’s 1910 novel The Phantom of the Opera. The building itself is highly decorated, sumptuous and classical. It is designed in what is described as the Second-Empire Beaux-Arts style, which was quite prevalent in France towards the end of the 19th century. The building is rather excessive and over exaggerated, the sculptures are slightly overscaled, and thus the building appears to be somewhat larger than life. The interior is equally lavish; it is richly Baroque with velvet, gold leaf, chandeliers, cherubs and nymphs. Leroux’s Gothic love story of the Phantom is said to have been inspired by the dramatic qualities of the building, plus the rumours of the never-ending fl ow of water that continually bubbled onto the ground that had been cleared for the building. This great lake is said to still exist, despite great efforts to drain it, beneath the building.

Garnier had originally planned to install a restaurant in the opera house, but given the already enormous fi nancial outlay, this was abandoned. The idea was resurrected as a competition in the early 2000s. Given the historical importance of the building, the design restrictions were quite extreme: the restaurant was to be reversible, that is it had to have the ability to be completely installed with the possibility of later removal, without causing any damage to the building. All of the elements of the design had to integrated into the site without touching the original structure.

The restaurant is situated in a once open pavilion that is slightly separate to the opera house. It was previously known as the ‘Subscriber’s Pavilion’ and was used to shelter the carriages of theatregoers as they arrived and departed. The restaurant is formed by the installation of a voluptuous undulating white mezzanine that fl oats under the ceiling of the pavilion. It is like a cloud formation that slips between the existing elements of the room without touching them. This curvaceous structure has a geometry of its own, which actively works against the strict axial symmetry of the original building. ## DESSA Architectural Gallery

Location Ljubljana, Slovenia

Former function Retail

New function Gallery

Date 1985

Designer Boris Podrecca

![](images/d3d8043e2dd47751bd253a1c9dbe844cff50582e8082c4542e8d961bb13b1665.jpg)

Figure 2.35 The tiny and painstakingly designed timber library is carefully fi tted into the circulation area.

Within much Modernist architecture, the interior was merely the product of the exterior walls. The position of the openings, the ingress of light and the shape of the rooms were completely dictated by the

strict requirements of the external envelope. The pursuit of transparency within much of contemporary architecture has taken this reduction in the importance placed upon the integrity of the interior

even further. It means that the division between the inside and the outside of the building all but disappeared. The interior is a continuation of the landscape, with just the structural columns acting as almost insignifi cant interruptions. The theoretician, Fred Scott, describes this as the interior having escaped from the building.

In direct opposition to this, the interior of a building can be vastly different to the exterior. It can offer distinction and contrast, and have a defi nite and particular identity. The remodelled building offers an opportunity for the new to be individually distinct, to be complete. It will always have a relationship with the container in which it is placed, but the interior can also have an identity of its own.

The DESSA Architectural Gallery is located in the old Jewish quarter of Ljubljana in Slovenia. The site had already undergone a number of transformations before its current use. It was once a synagogue, but in the 16th century, after Caesar Maximilian deported the Jews from the Carniola region, it was converted into a Catholic Chapel. This was demolished in the 19th century to make way for an apartment block with retail at ground level. Even that had several changes; a restaurant in the 1930s, a music school following the Second World War and after that a workshop.

Figure 2.36 The new cladding for the wall has been pulled slightly away from the original walls of the space to create a perfectly proportioned room.

The minute and beautifully detailed gallery is composed of just four rooms: a library, a reading room, an offi ce and a meeting room. The approach that the architect took was to clad each of the rooms with a different material. This cladding, or lining, was placed slightly in front of the original walls, leaving a small gap all of the way round. This device allowed the rooms to become geometrically pure. The new walls were positioned so that the resultant interior space was exact. Thus, the irregularly shaped gallery became an impeccable rectangle, the offi ce plan was based on six squares, and the tiny reading room was a perfect circle. Each of the rooms is devoted to a different architectural material which refl ects the activity that happens within the space. The gallery marks the moment of arrival within the building, so it is lined with stone, the library is articulated in warm timber, effi cient plastic for the offi ce and the acoustic deadening qualities of textile line the meeting room. The fl uctuations in the spaces behind the new walls allowed the architect Boris Podrecca to insert storage, such as cupboards and shelves.

This use of cladding to identify space, and the extreme level of attention to detail places Podrecca in the great tradition of Mitteleuropean Architecture. Semper, Loos, Plečnik and Wagner are all obvious precursors or forerunners of this approach. Much of Podrecca’s later work is within the urban landscape and he has created many delightful city squares which contain charming but useful outdoor furniture.

## Introduction

Often described as the details of making interior space, tactics is a term we use to describe a more substantial way of creating particular identities utilising a number of spatial devices. These instruments, used in the construction of interior space, range from the everyday elements that designers are familiar with, such as floors, walls and ceilings, through to more esoteric materials such as light, surface and movement. All of these elements of space may be utilised by the designer in a variety of ways in every project. This is in order to create a very distinct and particularised set of environments for a variety of different uses, often for numerous occupants, some of which will not even be known.

## Plane

## Introduction

Derived from the Latin Planum, plane denotes a flat or level surface, usually placed either horizontally or vertically in or outside of a space. It is one of the most recognisable elements in the built environment, and is one that the occupant of any space will usually have a direct relationship with. The four planes described in tactics share some commonalities. They will often connect in some way, such as wall to floor, but it is when they are combined to create confined space in the form of a room, that they exemplify the control available to a designer.

## Façade

When it came to prominence as part of architectural discourse, the decorous façade stood as an architectural super-element, coordinating the door, windows, and walls, united into a singular and signature composition.

– Alejandro Zaera-Polo and Stephan Truby. <sup>15</sup>

The façade is often described as the plane endowed with the most signifi cant meaning in architecture. As Zaera-Polo suggests, throughout history it has provided the

opportunity to create a prominent plane, one that is replete with aesthetic, cultural and political signifi cance. This is still the case, but technologies have impacted upon its role as an exterior-facing wall. Its responsibility for controlling both interior and exterior environments, excluding inclement weather, and retaining interior temperatures, has been radically altered through the development of glass and curtain walling systems. Because of the signifi cance that the façade of a building will consist of, and combined with the complex planning laws of most cities, the façade is often one of the few remaining planes left standing after a building is demolished. Therefore the aesthetics, and subsequently the meaning of a good façade, are often highly prized. In the remodelling of the iconic Everyman Theatre in Liverpool, Haworth Tompkins utilised the signifi cance of the façade to project a meaningful image of the building and its occupancy back to the city.

The main west-facing façade of the building was adapted to portray a large-scale public work of art consisting of 105 moveable metal sunshades, each one carrying a life-sized portrait of a Liverpool resident. Typographer and artist Jake Tilson created a special font for a new version of the iconic red ‘Everyman sign. Collaborating visual artist Antoni Malinowski made a large painted ceiling piece for the foyer, to complement an internal palette of brickwork, black steel, oak, reclaimed Iroko, deeply coloured plywood and pale in situ concrete.

As well as signifying what a reused building can mean to the city, the façade of a building can provide a direct view into what the interior has become. Brinkworth were commissioned to rethink the All Saints brand’s identity. The designers created an unforgettable façade display for the retail spaces through which they wanted to refl ect the All Saints image of ‘elegant decay’. The interior of the fi rst store in Spitalfi elds, London, was partly ruined, with walls and bare structure exposed through a process of creative demolition and the intuitive stripping back of the layers of accretions in the existing interior space. The exterior façade was enclosed in rusted steel and then the shop window was fi lled with Singer sewing machines, projecting the tailoring background of the brand’s founders and the function of their clothing retail space to the street outside whilst enticing the shoppers in.

## Wall

The wall is a plane that both divides and unites space. Whether permanent or temporary, a wall will organise and demarcate what is public, private, formal or informal in a space. It is a spatial device that will provide protection, separating various visual, acoustic and environmental situations. For instance, in the Casa De Musica in Porto, opened in 2005, both the visual and acoustic properties of the main hall were enhanced through the utilisation of corrugated glass screen walls, allowing light and view into what is normally designed to be a sealed off black-box performance space. The wall is an element that can be designed and made to work very hard in an interior, containing storage in the form of cupboards and shelves upon which items such as books and possessions can be placed. Walls can also be less matter-of-fact, and instead, when made very thick, can contain surprises in the form of secret passages, stairs or libraries. Poche, or pockets, is a wall tactic where spaces such as rooms can be ensconced in the thick walls of what are often historic buildings. Utilising this wall tactic means that whole rooms can often be found secreted in the walls of historic palaces and castles.

As well as being secretive, walls can be used to impart information through the display of objects in an exhibition. In the Palazzo della Ragione, Milan, the design of the exhibition Convoy RSHA January 30, 1944, designed by Morpurgo de Curtis Architetti Associati, utilised a series of dynamic walls that were placed amongst the historic space of the existing building. Each wall told the story of the movement and deportation of Jewish people through this time. Walls were used to not only impart information but were also placed dynamically against the rigid structure of the building, in order to symbolise movement and denote the terror and sadness of these enforced transitions across space and time. Whether thick or thin, permanent or temporary, hard or soft, static or in motion, as Corbusier stated, walls can call forth emotions.

## Floor

The fl oor is a surface upon which our daily lives are performed. It is a plane that negotiates the space between the body and the substance of the ground. In an interior context, it is sometimes found to be a highly decorated element, patterned and embellished in order to make a distinct feature of a space. In other interiors it is reduced to invisibility, abstracted to form a continuous unbroken surface upon which use can take place unguided and without distraction. It can be textured, smooth, elevated, moveable, transparent, fl oating or solid. Whatever form it has been manipulated to take, it always establishes conventions, cultural habits and the techniques for territorialising inside spaces.

In the Towada community centre in Japan, Kengo Kuma designed the floor to be an undulating surface, providing a playful surface for children to roll around on inside the building when inclement weather kept them indoors. The timber floor was constructed from different shaped sheets of timber, stacked to form a contoured interior landscape, a series of rolling timber hills, where the children can roam and play.

In essence, in all projects, the floor is an instrument of territory making, an overlooked surface that is arguably a support player to the dominance of the vertical plane to which it is often attached – the wall, the surface of endless scrutiny in architectural history. The floor is often a discrete surface, yet one that is fundamental to our daily lives.

## Sofit

If the fl oor anchors us to the earth, the ceiling allows our spirits to aspire to the sky.

– Edwin Heathcote, The Meaning of Home.<sup>17</sup>

Usually opposite the floor and atop the wall, the soffit, or ceiling, provides an enclosure for a space in the form of a lid or a cover. It is a plane that can be mundane and banal, merely a cover for hiding the services of a space. The ceiling or the soffit – a plane installed on the underside of the floor above – has become synonymous with the contemporary requirement to artificially heat, cool, light, power and also to protect interior spaces from dangerous phenomenon, such as fire.

Throughout the 20th century, the space between the ceiling and the soffit became a deepening cavity that held all manner of pipes, ductwork and cables, detectors and alarms, neatly secreted behind a plane of modular tiles suspended in a metal grid. This has not always been the case and in some projects the ceiling can be afforded a vital role in the design of the interior, making an expressive surface in which even with the suspended ceiling removed, the exposed ductwork, cables and sensors are organised and used to delineate movement or lighting in a space.

When fully considered the ceiling can become expressive to such a degree that it becomes the dominating interior element of the space. The Camper Store by Atelier Marko Brajovic in São Paolo in Brazil has been fashioned from thousands of red shoelaces, hung from the ceiling. The ceiling plane undulates through the space and is atmospherically carefully controlled with lighting inside it. In carefully chosen spaces the laces hang lower right down to the floor and connect the ceiling with the floor plane.

A soffit can be designed to perform its practical duties, such as lighting and climate control, but rather than hiding these elements away it can be configured in a way that celebrates their existence. Lazzarini and Pickering’s design for a villa in Positano, Italy, included a table whose surface continued up exuberantly to the ceiling, fulfilling the duty to light a particular piece of furniture as well as creating a sculptural element in the interior space.

Whichever way a plane is utilised in a space, and for every example where it has been deployed without thought for its expressive potential, there are a multiplicity of ways of realising dramatic walls, floors, ceilings and the façades of interior spaces.
