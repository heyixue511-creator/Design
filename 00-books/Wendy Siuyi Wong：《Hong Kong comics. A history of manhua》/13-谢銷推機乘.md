## 56.

## Caricature Weekly, 1961

## Editor: Lee Ling-hon

The same artists who had made the best-selling Manhua World created this tabloid-size weekly. Known as a “penny paper” (because it cost one cent), it became one of most successful comics of its time. Its eight pages contained mainly illustrations and comics, some in single panels and some in four-panel strips, as well as some fiction and a page of works by students. Caricature Weekly proved so popular that it began to publish every day, as Caricature Daily.

## 57.

## New Comics Weekly, 1962

## Editor: Tsui Ngai

This weekly penny paper was modeled after Caricature Weekly but was drawn by different artists, including Lee Yu, Lui Yu-tin, and Sung Sam-lung. Its contents were similar—cartoons, serial comics, and entertainment news——and like its predecessor, it quickly became a daily. The most popular strips in New Comics were Little Couple, by Lee Yu, Wu-lung Wong, by Lui Yu-tin, and Uncle Fatty, by Sung Samlung.

## 58–61.

## Entertainment News, 1963

## Editor: Fung Tai-wai

This two-color tabloid-size paper sold for ten cent and essentially started “penny paper fever” in Hong Kong. Among its eight pages was a gallery of Western (mainly American) comics, which were simply reprinted with the text in Chinese, as well as local comic strips and columns. Entertainment News also encouraged readers to submit their own comics——-and paid for them—which added to the paper's popularity. A supplement to the paper, Cartoon Daily, was published in 1963; its main appeal was adventure comics. Although artists were only paid about fifty cents per comic, the paper's insatiable demand for art—enough to fill four pages every day—kept many of them in business. The publisher supplied the story, and artists only had to draw the illustrations. Although the quality of the comics was poor, the constantly changing narratives kept readers buying the paper. The comics were later reprinted in a four-by-six-inch book that sold for fifty cents.

## 62.

## Epoch Comic Weekly, 1962

## Editor: Leung Tak-wah

This penny paper followed the typical pattern: eight pages oflighthearted and humorous stories, many with uplifting themes designed to help readers temporarily escape their problems. It became a daily paper in 1965.

## 63-64.

## Manhua Daily, 1963

## Editor: Mak Ching

This eight-page penny paper included work by local artists and one page of translated Western cartoons. One of its main attractions was that the front and back pages were printed in color. Martial arts and adventure comics were inside, and single-panel cartoons filled up the remaining space.

## 65.

## Radar Pictorial, 1965

## Art: Mok Kwan-ngok and Man Yong

A popular Japanese samurai movie, Blind Knight, inspired local artists Mok Kwan-ngok and Man Yong to create this eight-page daily paper, which featured a ongoing sword fighting comic. Like the movie, it was a hit, although the demands of drawing eight pages a day meant that the quality was very crude. Still, it remained popular until the mid-1970s, making it one of the longest-running penny papers. Mok and Man started another penny paper, Ultraman Pictorial, an eight-page daily based on the Japanese manga of the same name. However, this paper was short-lived since the artist could not draw enough for two dailies at the same time.

## 66-67.

## Companion Manhua, 1963

## Editor: Unknown

This penny paper ran imitations of work by famous contemporary comic artists, including Hui Guanman, Chan Chi-dor, Mak Ching, Lee Ling-hon, Wong Chak, and Wong Sze-ma. However, it also helped create a new genre of manhua when it combined cartoon strips with adventure stories.

## 68-70.

## The Cartoon and Comics Garden, 1964

This penny paper was another eight-page tabloid with color covers. It included the work of Lee Singcheung, Ng Gei-ping, and the rising female artist Lee Wai-chun.

71-103.

Old Master Q, 1964

Art/Script: Wong Chak

Old Master Q is the most popular manhua character in Hong Kong as well as in Chinese communities all over the world. Almost four decades after the comic's debut, Old Master Q is still being published, each issue eagerly read by a worldwide audience. Old Master Q was even the subject of popular live-action and animated films in the 1960s and 1970s. The comic itself is a four-panel strip in which Old Master Q and his sidekicks (“Big Dumb” or “Big Potato” and Mr. Chun) get involved in many different kinds of adventures. Whether he's traveling through time, visiting Japan, or marrying one of many wives, Old Master Q frequently finds himself in trouble. The character of Old Master Q was greatly influenced by Ye Qian-yu’s Mr. Wang, who first appeared in 1928 in Shanghai Sketch. It was also possibly influenced by Peng Di’s Lao Fu Zi, who was popular in the 1930s and 1940s in mainland China.

Old Master Q's Crazy Comics, 1965 Editor: Wong Chak

Following the success of his character Old Master Q, Wong Chak and a few other young artists published this variety magazine, which was inspired by American comics. Each issue featured eight pages of sixpanel Old Master Q comics. The remaining four to six pages had short comics by Ching Ho, Ng Hocheung, and Sze-to Yong, among others.

## 109.

Crazy World, 1966

Editor: Wong Chak

The main lure of this thirty-six-page magazine was Wong Chak's Old Master Q, although it also included comics by Heung-shan A-wong, Tsui Shing-on, Tou Sam, and Ma Chung. It was never as popular as Old Master Q’'s Crazy Comics, and its short run ended with the 1967 riots, which disrupted Hong Kong society and caused huge setbacks for the manhua industry.

## 110–112.

Fat Chan, Gentleman, and Brother Ho Manhua, 1965 Art/Script: Lee Fan-fu

This comic was created by Lee Fan-fu, a manhua artist of the older generation who was trying to be part of the 1960s comics boom. A collection of Lee's prewar work, this publication was targeted toward adults and thus had difficulty competing with all-ages comics such as Old Master Q. Only about ten issues were published.

## 113–114.

Dumb Detective Manhua Series, 1965

Art/Script: Sung Sam-lung

Each issue of Dumb Detective revolved around stories about the title character and his assistant, Fat Chu, as they try to solve crimes, with often humorous results. The series ran for more than 200 issues.

## 115–116.

First published in Sun Daily as a four-panel strip, this serial followed the adventures of Dummy Seven, a not-so-bright character who lives among smart people. The other character, A-ling, or “Beauty” (although her name means “beauty," she is not beautiful), has a quick, sarcastic tongue and likes to take advantage of people, especially Dummy Seven. 13-Dot Cartoon, 1966

13-Døt was the first major comic created especially for female readers. Drawn by a woman and featuring female characters, the comic helped project a modern, Western image to young women in Hong Kong. Some of Miss Thirteen Dot's appeal was her fashion sense; young women sometimes copied her clothing style.

## 133-134.

Careless Young Man, 1966 Art/Script: Ng Sheng-gwan

This comic followed the life of a wealthy young man. He is handsome, wears modern clothes, and has a perfect rich father, a caring mother, a maid, and a girlfriend. However, his absentmindedness leads him into many comical adventures and provides fodder for many jokes.

## 135.

Dai-wah and Siu-wah, 1966 Art/Script: Lee Shek-cheung

This manhua strip was originally published in a pro-China newspaper. Its stories—about two brothers, one mature and considerate and the other cute and energetic—had positive messages but also carried Communist ideas and anti-American propaganda.

## 136.

Movie Stars and Comics Magazine, 1967 Editor: Youth Culture Committee As its title suggests, this manhua magazine integrated entertainment news with comics. It is one of the first comics tied to TV and movie stars.

## 137.

Handsome Dak, 1967   
Art/Script: Hui Guan-man   
First published in Joke Comics and Sun Wan Daily, this comic included light, humorous stories about its handsome main character and his various girlfriends.

## 138.

MCC Cartoons, 1968

Editor: Fung Siu-ho

The title of this comic, which lasted for only one issue, was slang for mung cha cha (“uncertain of anything”). Its artists were trying to capitalize on Hong Kong popular culture, with an emphasis on new fashions and dancing.

## 139.

New Mr. Wang and Mr. Chu, 1968

Art/Script: Ching Fu and Mak Man-chung The main character of this comic, Mr. Wang, was named after the character created in 1928 by Ye Qianyu for Shanghai Sketch. Its two artists hoped that recreating classic manhua characters would let them compete with Old Master Q.

## 140-141.

Mr. Chu and Aunt Eight, 1970 Art/Script: Mak Man-chung

T'he format and style of Mr. Chu and Aunt Eight was similar to Old Master Q with six-panel strips. Although the events, jokes, and tricks involved were all related to Hong Kong, they were rather ordinary.
