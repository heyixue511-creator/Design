In Italy the challenge of postwar recovery stimulated a burst of design innovation that helped polish an image that had been tarnished by the country's recent Fascist regime. Aided by America's Marshall Plan funds, the country repaired its infrastructure and retooled factories, making what would prove to be foresighted investments in research and technology. Italian producers began collaborating with major designers to create innovative, export-worthy products, and American museums showcased this renaissance, helping to reestablish Italy as a center of enterprise and creativity.6 Italian designers had been exploring the applications of materials developed during wartime, particularly plastics, using injection molding and other processes to reposition synthetics as acceptable design media, rather than as stand-ins for natural materials. In i963 Richard Sapper\* and Marco Zanuso\* designed the first piece of polyethylene furniture, a child's chair for Kartell\*, and designers like Joe Colombo, Vico Magistretti, and Zanini\* used both rigid and flexible foam plastics in ingenious furniture designs, including the first modular seating. Design became a market stimulus and a public relations tool, catapulting Italy to the forefront of postwar consumer culture. Meanwhile, the austere Modernism that had emanated from the Bauhaus and crystallized in the glass-walled International Style skyscrapers was falling from favor, and a design rebellion was emerging. Disillusionment with Modernism's failed promises—it had not made a better world, much less the utopian ideal sought in the aftermath of two world wars—led to splinter groups that challenged the status quo.7 In parallel to the pessimistic protests, a wave of utopianism generated visionary pro posals for social reforms that would transform cities and the lives of their inhabitants. These ideas were woven into the conceptual underpin nings of postmodern thought, and would later be implemented in the New Urbanism movement. In i96os Europe, a number of groups rebelled against the state of design and the consumer-driven culture, hoping to recapture the social relevance abandoned by Modernism. These included Archigram\*, Archizoom\*, Superstudio\*, Studio 65\*, and the best-known and most productive Studio Alchimia\*, whose participants included ETTORE SOTTSASS,ALESSANDRO MENDINI, and ANDREA BRANZI, and whose designs often prefigured those of the MEMPHIS collective later founded by Sottsass. Despite having become static, Modernism provided fertile soil for the flowering of anti-modern thinking; though they produced little more than ideas, the activities of the radicals provided the spark that would ignite Postmodernism. The Museum of Modern Art in New York celebrated Italy's resurgence in 1972 with the exhibition “Italy: The New Domestic Landscape," curated by Emilio Ambasz and showcasing 18o household objects by more than ioo designers. The most influential part of the exhibition, however, was a display of twelve specially commissioned conceptual environments by Archizoom and Superstudio as well as individual designers including Gae Aulenti\*, Joe Colombo, GAETANO PESCE, ALDO ROSSI, and Ettore Sottsass. By highlighting Italian creativity in designs for the home—even though some of the concepts were unrealistic or impractical—the exhibition brought international attention to the country's accomplishments. Italy became the undisputed star of the design firmament. In self-defense, producers in other countries were motivated to invest in avant-garde design, and there were key contributors in the United Kingdom, Japan, Germany, Spain, Portugal, and elsewhere. Following the social and political instability and cultural change that had characterized the i96os and ı97os—with protest and countercultural movements building across the United States and in Europe, the Vietnam War, economic recession and rising inflation—the i98os brought change of a different kind. There was good—the fall of the Berlin Wall--and bad: the AIDS epidemic and the first signs of global warming. The United States perhaps exemplified the era, with the rise of the conservative New Right, opposing big government and high taxes and supporting a free market. Perhaps in response to the economic uncertainty, affluent “yuppies" ushered in an era of conspicuous consumption.8 the archetype of Thorstein Veblen's society fixed on the need to acquire objects as symbols of status. Technology changed everyday life, with personal computers, Swatch watches, the Walkman, and cell phones becoming commonplace. The world of popular music responded with Michael Jackson, Prince, and Madonna; and the newly launched MTV gave an outlet to hard rock, heavy metal, rap and hip-hop artists. Art went anti-establishment, using found materials or idealizing everyday objects, introducing new forms of expressionism in painting, assemblage or photography by rebels like the Arte Povera groups in Italy, or Jean-Michel Basquiat, David Salle, and Robert Mapplethorpe in America. Anyone could make art, on any subject, of any material, challenging the commercial attitudes of elitist galleries.9 The radicals rebelled against the rampant consumerism of the time, and the postmodernists mocked it. A defining event in the birth of the postmodern movement was an exhibition in the Venice Biennale in ı98o—the first year that architecture had been included in what is still one of the most important international art fairs. Under the direction of PAOLO PORTOGHESI, the Strada Novissima was a papier mâché street of 23 × 3ıft (7 × 9.5m) building facades following the theme "The Presence of the Past." The twenty invited architects from nine countries included Robert Venturi, ROBERT A. M. STERN, MICHAEL GRAVES, LÉON KRIER, and HANS HOLLEIN. Acknowledging that Modernism was a failure, the designs suggested a return to academic order and historical tradition. In the book published in conjunction with the exhibition, however, Portoghesi, Vincent Scully, Charles Jencks, and Christian Norberg-Schulz put forth divergent views of Postmodernism, an indication that the movement was still struggling to define itself. Despite the controversy generated by the Strada Novissima, it was adapted for showings in Paris and then San Francisco, drawing broader attention to anti-modernist ideas. Modernism had essentially denied the role of history in shaping design, but for many postmodern architects and designers, looking back instead of forward could provide a solution to Modernism's shortcomings by linking with the established past instead of the undefined future. The most overt expressions of historicist-based postmodern design were in architecture. Now iconic buildings by architects such as Graves in America, Hollein in Austria, JAMES STIRLING in England, Rossi in Italy, and MARIO BOTTA in Switzerland were antithetical to the strict geometry of the International Style, introducing non-rectilinear forms, lively color, and ornament adapted from classical structures. Not every architect of the i98os was a postmodernist, but all those practicing in that era were influenced by its concepts—even if it was merely to denounce them. Many avowed followers of the established modernist aesthetic broadened their vocabulary to include elements reprising designs of the relatively recent past, if not the ancient world. Prominent architects like Richard Meier\*, despite making incursions into historicist-influenced design, held fast to Bauhaus and International Style tenets. Others, like IAMES WINES, Peter Eisenman\*, and Daniel Libeskind\*, have been classified as Deconstructivists, which many historians consider an offshoot (or after-effect) of Postmodernism.'º But they and many of their contemporaries are variously designated as modern, late modern, and postmodern, underlining the fact that design involves personal values and commercial interests as much as a philosophy, and that labels are not always as accurate as they might appear. As a radical new style, Postmodernism was more easily acceptable for public buildings and commercial structures than private residences. A commitment to living space is more personal, and more long-term: faint-hearted clients need not apply. And a little bit of historicism goes a long way, as became evident in the planned communities that emerged in the 198os with the New Urbanism movement, whose primary theorist was Krier. In its efforts to standardize the design of cities in an industrial society, the International Style version of Modernism had created soulless, homogeneous environments. "It can be said that there is not a single urban model that has not failed or is not on the road to failure," Branzi commented in ı984." The New Urbanism, seeking the diversity prescribed by critics like Jane Jacobs, sought to remedy that, recreating the ambience of European towns or nineteenth-century villages with walkable configurations of historicist-influenced homes clustered around town centers. DiSNEY'S Celebration in Florida, with buildings by Stern, Venturi and DENISE SCOTT BROWN, Johnson, and others, was designed with sometimes ostentatious renditions of postmodern style. The result, many critics felt, was a cartoon-like interpretation of an impossible utopian vision. A less exaggerated application of the concept was devised by Andrés Duany\* and Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk\* for Seaside\*, Florida, with public buildings by major-name architects including Rossi and Krier, and private ones individually commissioned by their owners according to a set of design standards intended to encourage diversity, recalling the vernacular design of a traditional Southern town. Architecture was the public face of the new aesthetic. but it was the i98r launch of the Memphis group in Milan that opened the contentious dialogue within the design community and captured the attention of the consumer public. With Sottsass as its organizer and prime spokesperson, the collaborative showed his designs and those of compatriots including MICHELE DE LUCCHI, Alessandro Mendini, and Branzi, and non-Italians including PETER SHIRE, Hollein, and SHIRO KURAMATA. The pieces were illogical configurations of furniture forms in jazzy colors and with laminate patterns that played with viewers' expectations, upending accepted ideas of appropriateness and mocking established criteria of good taste. The audacious exteriors belied the serious ideas underlying the designs, and though many critics called them superficial, Memphis intrigued the international press, which found photogenid fodder for unprecedented media coverage. Although embraced by a cult of enthusiasts, most Memphis designs failed to reach a wide consumer market; the high prices and limited quantities were as much a cause as the exhibitionistic designs.¹² Memphis made more headlines than actual sales, and the collaborative disbanded in i988.'3 In the consumer marketplace, avant-garde designs normally inspire adaptations or blatant copies by lower-priced producers, but the trickle-down effect of the Memphis look was undermined by careless manufacturers and poorly designed, pseudo-postmodern objects.'4 It did, however, stimulate a move to more adventurous and original design—perhaps its most important legacy. Roughly concurrent with Memphis was the emergence of a new category in furniture: limited edition, not-necessarily-functional objects later given the designation design-art. A coterie of London-based designers played with industrial materials and detritus, adding historical references to furniture that prioritized concept over comfort and were light years away from mass-produced goods. In New York, the innovative Greenwich Village gallery Art et Industrie commissioned conceptdriven designs, and European galleries like Néotù\* in Paris, Aram in London and Design Gallery Milano in Milan engaged in similar efforts.
