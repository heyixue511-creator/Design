# WHAT IS DESIGN CULTURE?

Many people have asked what characterises ‘design culture’, and what if anything makes it different from other cultural fields. However, no single theory about it is complete in itself. One reason for this is that in practice, design phenomena prove difficult to observe, because they are experienced in a way which bridges people and things so that the difference between ‘thinking persons’ and ‘non-human objects’ is often blurred. Imagine trying to undertake a cultural study of your own hand. Since it is a part of you, while it is possible to produce interpretations or ways to approach your hand, it is not possible to explain finally why it is as it is. Perhaps, you can only really say how your hand ‘seems’. In other words, gaining an objective viewpoint on design is difficult. By definition, it involves people, and those who try to observe it find themselves involved in design’s agendas and projects. Nonetheless, there are a range of wonderful interpretations which can help give us insight into what distinguishes it as a social and cultural phenomenon. This chapter is about some of these anthropologies of design (Gunn and Donovan 2012).

The two images on the next page (Figure 2.1) are different examples of things which can be called design. On the left, we have a whiteboard from a brainstorming session in which I participated in a design lab in a company. On the board is a small sketch of a kind of digital artefact which the company could have made. The sketch manifests an idea, a thought, a concept and a possibility, and it comprises evidence of work, the work of thinking. But it is not a product or service which is widely produced in the world.

In the image on the right meanwhile, we can see a piece of furniture in a home in Stockholm. Many thousands of these objects have been sold internationally. This thing is heavily used and very functional. It is not a foregrounded and obvious object, rather it is very much in the background. It is an ‘IKEA’ piece, and by this, we know that it is not only sold by the company but also designed by the company as well. At some stage in the past, the IKEA designers in Älmhult developed a concept in the form of sketches and modelling. Their work was passed on to manufacturers in other locations, who used ‘the designs’ to mass-manufacture the furniture. The furniture then passed on to retail outlets in which people were looking for not only a particular kind of object but to buy a design which would be suitable for them, because IKEA is known for selling ‘design’, not only selling material stuff. As the object passed into the home, it then was deployed with particular aims, or to put it another way, we could say that it enabled peoples’ own domestic designs: their aims, the aesthetic or look they wished to achieve, their sense of organisation and order, and the simple ‘fit’.

These two images illustrate two different but complementary perspectives. For decades, anthropologists studying design have paid particular attention to sketching, graphics and patterns (see, for example, Gunn 2009; Ingold 2007), and one version of the origins of design lies in the development of sketching techniques in Europe. Sketching became an increasingly popular way to think about the material world from the Renaissance. At that time, visual culture changed from a medieval understanding, where most images were deciphered for their underlying religious meanings, to a human-centred understanding by which many images were read as human observations. Sketches such as the one in the image illustrate that there are particular professional work processes which characterise design and involve ‘conceptualisation’, or working with concepts (Schön 1982, Lawson 2005). If you have ever been to a design show, you will have seen many things which are designs because they are understood as human thoughts: clothes, furniture, models, videos, interiors, colour palettes, textiles and samples of materials, prototypes and shapes. There are an infinite number of these things, because the existence of design itself presumes that material possibility and development has no end, but they are all manifestly ‘concepts’. Sketches epitomise this perspective on design better than anything, and anthropologies of sketching, envisioning, imagining and hypothesising are well established as approaches into design.

The second image illustrates a different account of design culture, this time as an industrial phenomenon. The Industrial Revolution in Europe and the United States necessitated an artistic and intellectual response, and the development of a set of professionals capable of directing what all of these new factories would build. When you have a production line of hundreds of people and machines making thousands of identical objects, then somebody else needs to very precisely design the things they will produce. Movements such as Arts and Crafts in Britain, the Bauhaus and Werkbund in Germany and the Mingei movement in Japan marked the separation of a separate professional sphere of design professions. In this account of design, the relation of thought and thing is also a relation of workplace roles: those people who design are separated from those who manufacture, but they are related. The industrially manufactured object then is evidently designed insofar as people can recognise the sequences of work involved in its production and the separation of the conceptualisation stage from the manufacturing stage in the mind of the person who owns and uses the object. The sense of design around many everyday objects is wholly anonymous, not so much an individual designer as a result of a collective mode of organising society around material goods.

So in these small windows into design, we can see that it can be understood as work processes or as a particular professional group. Both of these definitions can be indexed by certain material things, and popular understandings of design are that it comprises in a category of objects or goods. By taking only two examples, the sketch and the bookcase, we can witness that design is a sociocultural phenomenon. It is cultural because it involves collective acts of ‘making sense’ of the world, and it is social because it is about how people relate to one another.

As a sociocultural term, the word ‘design’ becomes an analytical term which social scientists find useful to account for their observations about how people culturally make sense of the world and also about their social relationships. Consider, for example, the use of the term ‘marriage’ to describe the many different ways humans organise their relationships in a wide range of communities and through history. Rituals of marriage differ, the number of people involved differs (one, two, three, four, etc.), understandings of gendering differ and the marriage lasts for very different periods of time. In some marriage traditions, a couple’s children can be closely related to a biological father and mother, while in other traditions, they may be more closely related to adults who are not their parents, for example people who look after them. Understandings of responsibilities, rights, work within a marriage, economic exchanges and political arrangements differ. What a marriage ceremony does to people, spiritually or physically or legally, varies. Marriage is not even consistent in a community, so if you return after one generation, you witness different customs. And yet in spite of all of this variety and change, cultural observers find the term ‘marriage’ very useful, recognising not only that these infinitely diverse arrangements can be compared, but also that the significance of these relationships as marriages is partly based in the fact that they are variable and specific. The logic of ‘this is a proper marriage because we do it in this particular way’ often applies. There is such a thing as ‘our’ way of marrying, as opposed to ‘theirs’, and ‘they’ can mean the next country, the next village, your neighbours, your parents or your children. Cultural phenomena are often constituted in such patternings of meaning, in being different as well as similar.

Design is likewise culturally and socially constructed, infinitely variable but also constituted in certain kinds of phenomenon. Thoughts are one of its common characteristics, the work of thinking and how it relates to actions and material things. In designs and designing, thoughts travel. Thoughts are not limited to a single individual’s consciousness or brain but become knowledges and form wider relationships (Henare et al 2006). I mostly call these thoughts ‘concepts’. Being relational, and depending on perspective, design also can seem ubiquitous. Seen in the right light, anything can appear designed, because humans have thought about everything. But in any particular context, certain things or aspects may typify design better than others. Designed things, whether they are similar to the sketch or the bookcase, are a bit like social jigsaw pieces. They appear as things which seem to ‘fit’. So they evoke the social spaces of possibility, into which they fit, and the journeys and intentions which brought them there.

This chapter proceeds with a review of some approaches to design culture and how to understand it. Social scientists have been fascinated by the topic for many different reasons. How do different theorists recognise design, what is it that they feel needs to be explained? and what accounts have they given of its core features? I have separated this overview into four kinds of approach: design as about finding our place in the world, as about wildness, as care, and as the problem of belonging in a globalising world. Having set out a range of views, the second half of

Chapter 1 argues that design anthropology needs a more engaged perspective than any of those, one more suitable for somebody who is experiencing design as it happens, which recognises it as a fundamental part of industrial modernity and acknowledges the potential social problems which can arise from that. To advance this argument, I argue that we first have to understand design’s mystifications, the reasons why it is difficult to perceive objectively. I then suggest that we can understand the design anthropologist’s perception as existing in tension between two viewpoints. On the one hand, there are understandings which emphasise the work and intentions of designers (the ‘push’ theories), and the understandings which emphasise everyday use of designs (the ‘pull’ theories). A design anthropologist should really be interested in things which help them understand the social space in between production and consumption of concepts. That framing suggests material culture theories are an especially good way to explore design, and that is the topic of the following chapter.

## Approaches to design and why it matters

## Discovering our place in the world

In a study of domestic objects and arrangements, Csikszentmihalyi (1991) argued that design appears as the imposition of a sense of order on a world which appears otherwise in disarray. Yet this sense of order is most of all subjective, acts of ‘making sense’. Many people may see a pattern but gain a different sense of ordering from it. On the one hand, professional designers intend designed objects to self-evidently make sense, to stand for themselves, but such objects are re-adapted into the ‘symbolic ecology’ (Csikszentmihalyi 1991: 27) of many private worlds. Through objects, people organise conceptions of their different motivations, projects, memories and relationships. So while, for example, a designer might think first about function, about how something gets a job done, for the people using it, these functions are actually more likely to be conceived of as part of an ordering of their social commitments, responsibilities and purposes. Where a designer sees ‘cooking food’, a user sees ‘providing a meal’; where a designer sees ‘a wonderful photograph’, a user sees ‘memories of a social event’. Social ordering prevails. These multiple parallel acts of organisation are a part of a grand project of designing and purposing: ‘the creation of private meaning is no less miraculous than the accomplishments of Rembrandt or Michelangelo’ (Csikszentmihalyi 1991: 34).

Design can be witnessed through a sense of order, because it is the best evidence of human intention and is a sign through which two human minds can recognise one another’s presence. In a design relationship, mutual recognition occurs in the production of a pattern: not only in books but more in their arrangement in a bookcase, not only in the wallpaper but more the way it matches other furnishings and not only in a piece of clothing but more in a costume or style. In such examples, design acquires a kind of fractal quality such that it is possible to recognise human intentionality ‘in between’. Let us consider the example of a path which runs across grass, which could be a tarmac path or a muddy route flattened by many feet (Ingold 2007). When we follow a path, we can relate to different aspects of it as designed. Perhaps, it is the route of the path, a particular curve in its course, which is designed. One landscape designer produces a practical but inelegant path, cutting directly from one point to another, and somebody in a hurry pursues this with purpose and efficiency, and it works perfectly. Another landscaper takes the approach that it is important to relish the experience of the path itself and ensures it meanders a little, deliberately looping indirectly through greenery. People walking this path appreciate instinctively from the curve that they have the option of appreciating the path and the journey itself. A different landscape designer may produce a visually stunning pattern of paths, if seen from the windows of surrounding offices, but an impractical one such that people cut across the grass, producing worn informal earth tracks fanning out.

Informal paths (Figure 2.2) have inspired a lot of user-centred design work. Called ‘desire paths’ in the United States, they have been recognised as important by town planners since the 1950s, and co-opted into design research work. Software designers talk about the shortcuts people make online as ‘desire paths’ and often turn them into formal functions of apps and websites (some of Twitter’s functions originated as ‘desire paths’). Following Csikszentmihalyi, we might see the collective production of such muddy tracks by many feet as design, the result of thousands of repeated, deliberate intentional actions which shape the world, give sense and purpose to experience and create a sense of organisation, albeit an alternative one to the individual landscape designer.

So in this example, we can see the wide range of ways in which a sense of order is perceived: in the mere existence of a path, in the materials used (tarmac or mud), in the inflective sinuous curves of the edges of the path, in the sharpness of the finish and the edges, in the route it takes or in the purposive action of walking. Design may be perceived in any or all of these. One of the fascinating cultural properties of design which is revealed is its sense of depth. In describing design, then, one can ‘zoom in’ or ‘zoom out’, to examine the significance of the smaller and smaller elements, and how they build up into larger entities. Thousands of small acts can be seen as one large act of design, and vice versa. Thoughtfulness can be seen in the overall route, or in the details (finishes and edges). A sense of order which has potential personal relevance is constituted in this property of depth, the capacity to read ‘between’ elements, which seem to form a pattern, and perceive that somebody at some point has conceptualised a connection.

It is not then that every attempt to order the world should be called design, nor every act of planning, rather it is the ways in which such attempts manage to engage with one another to create a recognised sense of normality. For DiSalvo (2011: 16), among the characteristics of design work are that the ‘practice of design extends the professions of design. … The practice of design is normative. … The practice of design makes ideas, beliefs, and capacities for action experientially accessible and known’. For DiSalvo, this means that it is possible to build a space where different senses of social order can coexist, and hence produce alternative political and social possibilities. Rather than rebel or resist against power structures, DiSalvo envisages coincident normative orderings in design work.

The history of the Swedish ‘Lily Blue’ dinner set illustrates how a design exercise can aspire to appreciate others’ ordered social worlds, but unsuccessfully. Lily Blue was a blue-and-white dinner set which drew on traditional Swedish colours and motifs, designed by William Kåge in 1917 for a Home Exhibition in Stockholm. It aspired to be a ‘well-thought’ object which would celebrate the democratic and inclusive tendencies, by making good, beautiful, functional and Swedish home goods available to people from working-class backgrounds. However, this democratic design intention proved mis-founded, and the dinner set did not prove as popular as expected. Zetterlund (2011) argues that the designers had a rather overdetermined view of the people who they thought comprised the ‘working class’. The people they were designing for actually lived in different ways, had different domestic circumstances, priorities and aesthetics and might use a range of materials (including woods and metals) at meals. Some preferred alternative, more obviously imitative ‘traditionalesque’ (Zetterlund 2011: 114) rustic styles of dinnerware, in which being traditionally Swedish was more evidently at a conceptual level. In this example, the aspiration to include people and build a more democratic society was undermined by the over-assertion of its own vision, when it needed to recognise many parallel acts of social ordering.

In examples such as this, it is not so much that design asserts a normative social order, as that through design people find ways to locate themselves in society. Graphical patternings and styles are a primary way by which such self-locations happen. Anthropologists working with diverse communities across the world have observed how style, pattern and graphical design can relate directly to how people see themselves in society, and indeed what constitutes a person. There is an extensive literature about the graphical traditions and productions among Kuna communities in Panama, for example. Fortis (2010, 2013) observes how Kuna patterns are often produced by repetitive actions such as weaving, building patterned surfaces which can be admired. He argues that these sorts of processes inform how Kuna people think people are formed. So a foetus inside a mother may be understood not as ‘grown’ but more as ‘made’. When babies are born, a pattern can often be observed on their heads, resulting from the various ways the blood of the amniotic sac adheres to them. The surface skin of a newly born baby is thus read in these terms, and one can argue that people here are designed in a very literal sense. This is a cultural argument, and it is one example of how we can advance very local theories of design, not only global ones.

Design does not mean in this instance being inducted into particular knowledges, skills, and work processes in a design college, so as to work within industrial production processes. Instead, design means a cultural understanding of how people and things are created, and how this creation is perceived and can be understood using local knowledges, skills and expertise. We can contrast a consumption and design paradigm here: the idea of consumption might suggest, for example, ‘I am the sort of person who uses blue and white tableware’, by contrast with other types. A design paradigm instead suggests, ‘I am the sort of person who uses tableware which has been thought-through, because I also am the sort of person who thinks’. Where industrial design paradigms predominate as ways of understanding creativity, people are also understood as normally planned and intended.

## Design is wild (and so are people)

While some theorists see design as a sense of order underlying appearances, whereby design establishes how people and things ‘fit’, others emphasise the exact opposite, its capacity to diverge from expectations. At the Glasgow School of Art (GSA) product design show in 2014, the designer Basra Santini exhibited the project ‘Monster Tourism’ (Figure 2.3). This project outlined a model for a consultancy which would conceptualise a tailor-made monster, for clients such as local tourist authorities, companies or community organisations. This kind of work is usually categorised as service design. At first sight, this proposal seems very improbable, and more of an artistic commentary than a serious design. The consultancy, Cryptozoological Marketing Solutions, has not materialised. However, the work was based on pretty solid social research conducted around Loch Ness, Scotland, in which Santini talked with a wide range of people whose lives and businesses are affected by the myth of the monster. The work could clearly demonstrate and calculate the potential economic benefits of having a local monster. It also developed that work in an entirely realistic and appropriate way, by directing it towards branding, tourism and heritage. The realistic possibility was further demonstrated in the project by applying the consultancy’s methods to the Isle of Arran. The agency developed ‘Orran’, a monster for Arran, outlined Orran’s characteristics and developed physical artefacts to emulate him, including a hoaxer’s kit to produce his footprints suited to the landscape of Arran. I myself found the proposal very plausible based on my own family’s experience, since my wife supported herself through college by working for a branding consultancy which developed branding ideas based on the characters and monsters in Greek myths. In short, if monster design is a fantasy, it is a real fantasy.

Good design work often combines aspects solidly grounded in existing experience with aspects which are wholly new and improbable. At the extreme end of the spectrum, as in academic design shows, this work comprises in provocative art. Work such as that at the GSA, and even more so the London work of Dunne and Raby (2013), epitomises this tendency. Yet the development of everyday products also routinely involves thinking of radical alternatives and often, as with Monster Tourism, alternatives which emerge from watertight observation of ordinary life and the status quo. What is really admirable about this kind of design work is that it has one foot in the artistic aspiration to ‘think big’, and the other in the mundane world of everyday branding, marketing and consumption. Like Alice in Wonderland, it finds wormholes in normal life leading to alternative realities.

One of the key thinkers who has explored the ‘cultural divergence’ of design culture is Judy Attfield (2000), who characterises designed things as ‘things with attitude’ or ‘wild things’. The attitude referred to is not as simple as the attitude of a designer who intends change in the world. Rather, design is often wild because it actively works with subject/object relationships, and hence challenges the integrity of the subjectivity of the designer per se. Just as importantly, design has the frankly unnerving capacity to utterly change high and low culture. Expensive, sought-after, rare, privileged things (Design with a capital ‘D’) can be quickly rendered as ubiquitous mass-market goods (design in the lower case). Likewise, designed environments can make everyday life beautiful and sought after, such that what is ordinary becomes special. The attitudinal quality of designed things refers to their social trajectory, in other words, they embody primarily social qualities (more than personal, psychological or functional, which are secondary), and they manifest normal temporality as change rather than stasis:

having placed ‘design’ in the lower case in the more generalised context of material culture, the task is to reconstitute it as a particular type of practice objectifying sociality rather than reduced to a particular type of artefact in a recognisable style. The characteristic which distinguishes design in this sense, is the practice that produces ‘things with attitude’, the material culture of innovation driven by a vision of change as beneficial.

(Attfield 2000: 32)

When concepts such as change and innovation become central to popular conceptions of social life, epitomised in peoples’ material culture, then design comes to characterise society in general (Barry 2001). The wildness of design reflects a particular historical moment, a modern moment, within which people are alive to the possibility of social change as normal, and the mutually understood signification of that possibility. In this regard, the aspect of design’s wildness which has fascinated some thinkers is its contagious evangelism. Design spreads, so when people come into contact with design things and processes, they become involved with its projects. Since design challenges and works with distinctions of subjects and objects, its wildness becomes both contagious and generally framed as positive. Experience and knowledge of design can be a oneway track.

The consumption of IKEA goods in Sweden provides a good example of a moment when design seems contagious (Garvey 2017). Within Swedish society, there is an underlying awareness of IKEA as Swedish, not only having historical roots there, but also that much of the design work is undertaken locally. In the showrooms, physical engagement with various goods is much appreciated. The company’s marketing approach is not to ‘advise’ people about good design, so much as to offer people an awareness of their own thinking about the goods, and their capacity to plan their own home interiors. Garvey (2017: 3) describes how this is experienced by people at IKEA as a kind of ‘benign intervention’, meaning that there is a sense of ‘agency’, of helping people to transform their lives. One of the implications is that the nature of home and domesticity in Sweden is inflected with design properties, domestic spaces having theatrical and open qualities which in other countries might be associated with public spaces. Locales such as IKEA meanwhile can have for some Swedes very intimate and reassuring qualities, which in other societies might be private. The production of cultural fluidity, which Attfield characterises as wildness, is referred to by Garvey as ‘design dispersed’ (Garvey 2017: 149).

Hence there are a range of social theorists who find themselves driven to explain the wildness of design. Many of these are particularly concerned with trying to produce accounts of creativity, of change, of agency, of social movement and of why design’s effects are largely sociocultural ones: ‘to fulfil a particular task, to make a statement, to objectify moral values, or to express individual or group identity, to denote status or demonstrate technological prowess, to exercise social control or to flaunt political power’ (Attfield 2000: 120). A central question for such thinkers is not only what design ‘is’, but what it ‘intends to do’.

## Unbounded care

The third set of approaches place their emphasis not on order or agency, but on the implied goodness of design. This links to the quality of ‘forness’ which designed things are seen to have. Designed things carry with them a sense that they are ‘for’ people and situations, that the people who use them and experience them have been ‘considered’. That consideration means design comprises a culture of ‘care’.

Returning to Scandinavian design, there is a long-standing tradition of social responsibility at the heart of much design work. Murphy (2013, 2015), in one of the most wide-ranging and comprehensive ethnographies of design, places the idea of ‘care’ at the heart of Swedish design, exploring a specific Swedish concept which infuses various dimensions of life, material forms, services and infrastructures:

everyday goods are made to simultaneously reflect and enact a social democratic ideology. These are not just things, they are ‘just’ things, serving a sociopolitical purpose beyond their surface functions and operating independently of the neoliberal political climate in which they are created.

(Murphy 2013: 120)

People read various senses of care into the ‘cultural geometry’ and visible styles of designed things in Sweden, according to Murphy. The patterned object language of curves and lines is read as caring because of being variously ergonomic, economical, uncomplicated, honest and usable. The world begins to appear as something which was created with somebody just like me in mind.

It is important to remember that the care in design is cultural, not natural or inevitable. There are many examples of harmful design. A huge amount of design work involves military applications and armaments, for example. While such things kill people more efficiently, the justification given for them is generally peace and maintaining social and global order. Hence they could also fall into Murphy’s category of ‘just’ things. There are an increasing number of examples of how social media design can be detrimental socially and psychologically, but again a lot of this design was motivated by good intentions.

The most disturbing recent study of recent years in this vein is Dow Schüll’s (2012) work on design in the gambling and casino industry. Schüll has spent years talking with many different people involved in gambling, including gamblers, architects, casino owners, the designers of gambling machines, workers and others. The combined efforts of designers of gambling machines, casino interior designers, and financial services, each with due consideration of improving things for gamblers, have produced environments capable of destroying lives in every aspect but breathing. The environments are intended to usher people in and help them literally lose themselves in the fascinating maze-like interiors. The machines can stimulate and sooth the senses and help people to lose themselves and enter the ‘zone’ of the machine. The monitoring of peoples’ gambling, their losses and financial algorithms can help flag up when people are at risk of losing too much; at that point, the casino can help them to take a break and survive to gamble another day. Gambling businesses can earn more in the longer term by just skimming off most of gambling addicts’ money, but not bankrupting them too fast. So a host of good design activities, each one user centred in the sense of empathising with and considering the gambler and their comfort, collectively amounts to a system highly destructive of people. Yet none of the design professionals, each one specialised in a particular physical aspect of the casino (a great game, a great interior, convenient financing), is actually involved in designing the system overall, somebody else is concerned with bringing the elements together.

The mantra of these actually detrimental designs might be the same as for gifts, ‘it’s the thought that counts’. More interesting to think about design culture are examples of dystopian conceptual design (Dunne and Raby 2001, 2008). The idea of Unpleasant Design (Savić and Savčić 2013) makes us aware of, and in some ways even celebrates, designs which are intentionally uncomfortable in some way, but developed in the name of a larger or alternative good. An example of unpleasant design which has been much in the news in London is when spikes are installed on ledges in public areas to prevent homeless people sleeping on them. Other examples can be, for example, litter bins with sloping tops to prevent people from leaving bags or coffee cups on top. Savić and Savčić also provide wonderful examples of graphic designs which seem to leave the eyes watering. Many of these examples work to promote the function of the object which was intended by a designer, through disrupting any attempts at alternative uses, and remind us of the cultural contingencies of seeing design as caring.

In spite of these contrary examples, designers do consider their ‘users’, and there is a lot more to this. If design is care, then it potentially becomes many other things: a modern mode of building relationships, an ethical field and an emotional one. All of these phenomena underpin care. By contrast with Attfield’s characterisation of the social trajectories of design as being about intentions, or purposeful thoughts-as-actions, framing design as care involves framing the thoughts behind it as considerations, or empathic thoughts about people. In anthropology, the study of relatedness and kinship is very clear that close relationships can be founded upon either the idea of shared flesh and blood, or shared spirits and souls, or upon looking after one another. In instances where material things such as food, shelter and warmth are the primary metaphors of relatedness (Carsten 2000), care becomes the main social glue by which relationships are constituted and begins to bind people together not only because they choose to consider one another’s interests and needs, but also because they are responsible for one another. Transcending the study of intentions, purposes and change, the study of care is concerned with the social fabric. Designers are not related to the people they design for (unlike parents caring for children, who often become parents because they look after children), indeed the relationship is generally anonymous. And yet this anonymous consideration is crucial and works by thinking about cultures of similarity and difference, about the likeness and unlikeness of people and situations. One designs for ‘other’ people, almost by definition. Consequently, design is one way by which society works with concepts of alterity, social difference, and with ideas of significant cultural others.

When design distinguishes itself as a form of care, it potentially becomes a form of ethical practice (Held 2007), and designed things can appear as ethical products. As a form of ethical reasoning, care refers to a mode of justification for actions and products based on empathy and the capacity to put oneself in another’s shoes, more than by reference to principles. This is a hugely influential way of thinking, drawing on generations of feminist scholarship (Gilligan 2016 [1982]; Noddings 2003) and becoming a normal mode of conduct in global institutions such as the UN (Robinson 1999).

Hence when Murphy describes designed objects as ‘just’ things and ‘just things’, some would choose to see behind this a process by which a weight of ethical reasoning is brought to bear in ordinary human experiences. Exploring the sense of ‘forness’ appreciates how so many things in the world appear to have trajectories by which they arrive specifically in this moment for particular people and communities to experience. Design has a sense of mobility in its goodness, arriving across distances from anonymous professionals and institutions through the industrial economy and binding societies and culture together in particular moral ways.

## Belonging in globalisation

The last set of paradigms by which social scientists explore design culture concerns its role in establishing belonging as a very modern phenomenon, connected to contemporaneity, progress, and the most up-to-date industries. In this set of ideas, long-standing communities and nations appear to be founded upon their modernity before their traditions. It seems singularly odd that a phenomenon which is so rooted in global industrial capitalism, which is generally seen as alienating, can not only help people feel more in control of their lives but give them a redeeming sense of belonging (Figure 2.4).

Modern, industrial, metropolitan and globalised cultures present their own sources of intrigue for anthropology. The social thinkers who founded the various social sciences during and in the wake of the Industrial Revolution each placed a theory of the problems of modernity and capitalism at the heart of their work: Marx, Weber, Durkheim and Simmel, for example (the thinkers we know most about from this period are all bearded, white men). Design, as we have seen, is often argued to be intrinsically modern because it comprises a set of skills which become necessary, not simply creative choices, within an industrial manufacturing process. The relationship of designer to design user is generally commoditised, a matter of monetary transactions, and frequently anonymous. Developing principles by which to create can in effect be seen as one implication of the social problems of industrialisation.

Local design histories (Fallan and Lees-Maffei 2016) are one way of critically exploring the dialogues of industry and design, as things which are globally imposed or locally inspired. By taking a regional approach, design historians can throw a critical eye on modern design culture by revealing the diversity of human creativity across the world, and the infinity of modes of conceptualising and of making. Forms, materials and styles can be understood through particular local awareness of how designed things came to be this way.

The history of Japanese design offers a wonderful example of how in design work, modernity can emerge locally and have a sense of local ownership, and not necessarily seem to come from some distant studio or laboratory. Many Japanese craftspeople are revered as national treasures, embodiments of the nation and its heritage. One of the reasons for this is a man called Yanagi Sōetsu. During the 20th century, Yanagi placed his irrevocable stamp upon design by promoting Mingei, a ‘folk art’ movement based on an array of ways of thinking about design. Mingei celebrated inexpensive objects made by hand, locally, and used in everyday life. New kinds of products and forms arise from the repetitive and practical experience of makers who have a physical experience of producing things over and over again. Moeran (1997) argues that these ideas reflect Japanese spiritual principles derived from Shinto religious beliefs, but also that (contrary to what Yanagi himself said) the movement was partly inspired by William Morris’ Arts and Crafts movement in Britain. This point is important, because artists, craftspeople and designers across the world have been inspired by Japanese traditions, forms and ideas, and it becomes a point of accuracy to understand their work as inspired by Japanese ideas, or as comprising an unconscious reimporting of European Arts and Crafts ideas. Alternatively, asserting the uniqueness of Japanese traditions risks the sin of ‘orientalism’, that is the risk of presenting them as an exotic counterpart mirroring European and Western ideas.

Kikuchi (2004) unpacks in detail the development of ‘Japanese Modern’ design styles and their roots in Mingei. What she highlights is how this is not simply a local history, nor a set of colonising ideas, but an ongoing and complicated set of global cultural conversations and transformations. Unpacking the development of Mingei as a form of ‘reverse orientalism’ (p. 127), she shows how Japanese design unfolded through celebrations of one’s own history, and an adoption and manipulation of how other people across the world saw Japan and people in Japan. Rather than accepting Mingei as a pre-written traditional package, she separates out the theory, the material styles and the social movement, as important in different ways at different historical moments, and as elements which could travel or could remain local. Consequently, Japanese design traditions have not so much been promulgated, as constantly ‘rediscovered’ by people in Japan. Yanagi was emphatic about how good design is discovered as much as made. In extensive lecture tours in Europe, Yanagi promoted Mingei, which he himself had developed by adapting various Japanese and other sources, as an authentic long-standing Japanese and Asian movement which he had discovered and set out on paper. In doing so, he offered up a type of exoticised, more spiritual, more communitarian and more traditional image of Asian design, superior in its ideology to industrialised, mechanised and individualised modes of designing and appreciating design in the ‘Occident’. While the founder of Mingei saw it as an Arts and Crafts movement, rather than design, generations of Japanese designers after the Second World War found inspiration in Mingei for developing products that were modern and global and Japanese. An incredibly strong and influential global tradition was established within which Japanese people are able to be surrounded by designed, industrial, internationally exported goods and make the

claim ‘we did this’.

## Critiques: how design deceives

According to these various approaches to cultures of design, it can be characterised as intrinsically about ordering (e.g. as ideological), as fundamentally untamed (e.g. as distributed agency), as basically considerate (e.g. as care) and as about using global goods and services to establish local rootedness. Each of these kinds of interpretation involves different ways of looking at and defining design. In places they are contradictory, but each also has its merits for social scientists trying to observe, study and interpret design from an objective perspective. They do show us what design can achieve in society and culture.

However, only the final approach (Kikuchi’s) really accounts for the potential problems of design. There are many reasons to criticise design, because it ‘flatters to deceive’. In other words, it can produce fascinating things which can nonetheless be exploitative. Many design anthropologists (and other design researchers) are in fact implicated in exploitative processes, generally unintentionally. They also do not often achieve an objective analytical stance on design itself because they are people whose role involves moving between design sites. They therefore have two fragmented perspectives, sometimes looking towards the design studio and sometimes towards their informants and the communities where designs are to be used. This leads to fragmented definitions of design and to a need for viewpoints which are serviceable during their motile work.

To consider how we may think more critically about design culture, we need to ask about how design culture fits into the workings of modern, global political economy, and account for the changes in perception and perspective which happen as people are drawn to be more ‘involved’ in design. Three particular critical approaches are as follows: design as fetishising (following Marxist thinking), as colonising (e.g. Escobar 2018) and, third, as based on misrepresenting creativity (there are long-standing anthropological critiques of this kind, e.g. Wagner 1981).

The idea of design as a fetish arises if you consider it to be ultimately part of a system which exploits labour. It can be interpreted as a system which obscures how goods and services are produced, such that one person can take credit for another person’s labour. As we have seen, design itself is a form of labour, thinking work, which is usually coordinated and even banal. Most design teams have a set of familiar procedures which they can follow and apply with some confidence in their own skill. At the same time, the design industries are a primary way of organising other peoples’ labour in a global industrial system. As we saw in Chapter 1, recent years have witnessed its rise as a fundamental organising principle for human lives, economics and politics. In this contemporary economy, sites of work are separated, and design, manufacturing, sales and other roles happen in different places. The manufacturing capacity of many factories across the world is essentially at the disposal of companies who produce designs for them to respond to. So design concepts, and their transmission, enable production and consumption across many sites and are how most products and services happen, for most people in the world.

Because of this relation to labour, from this perspective, designed things can be analysed as the material manifestations of contemporary capitalism. One can compare design culture today to how, during a very different era, Marx saw ‘commodity’ culture as typical of the society he lived in, and as an engine of the structures of Victorian society:

A commodity is … a mysterious thing, simply because in it the social character of men’s labour appears to them as an objective character stamped upon the product of that labour; because the relation of the producers to the sum total of their own labour is presented to them as a social relation, existing not between themselves, but between the products of their labour.

(Marx 2007 [1867]: 81)

Of course, this historical analysis of the relation of a commodity to labour has been analysed and debated for over a hundred years and has many dimensions to it, some of which we will explore in Chapter 6. While the contemporary economy is utterly different from Victorian capitalism, nonetheless in design, we can witness new forms of the fetishisation of value. A design-based economy not only has the capacity to ensure the separation of sites of conception, manufacture and use, it also has the capacity to make it seem as if the value of goods and services lies in the thought behind them, rather than in their production or delivery. Many computing products, for example, might be designed in the West coast of the United States. The plans are sent to China where products are assembled and then sold back in the United States. Many designs follow this kind of pattern, when designers live relatively close to people who use designs, but manufacturing happens at one remove. Designs, in the sense of sketches, or plans, develop an existence independent of the people and products involved and come to be registered as ‘intellectual property’, things to be bought and sold whoever initially worked on them. One effect is what Harvey and Krohn-Hansen (2018) call ‘dislocated labour’, by which people are no longer so connected to places by their work and jobs, because jobs are moveable. Ultimately, it is goods and services which are known to have been ‘designed’, by somebody and at some point, which are more valued.

Although a great deal of thinking work goes into designing goods and services, often this work is itself deliberately obfuscated. The presentation of goods and services suggests that design has been done, but the details and complexity of the work should not, in good design, be obvious. For example, contemporary computing design often focuses on making the interface or screen as simple and intuitive as possible. The programming, computing hardware and infrastructures are fantastically complex and materially vast, but design work aims to make the immensity of this reality, and the many peoples’ work involved in different locations, less visible, so that no thought of the complexity intrudes on the moment when a computer function is used. Similar principles also apply to other goods and services. Most of the design aims at apparent simplicity so as not to confuse people and to enable them to use the thing smoothly as if using it were their own idea. Sometimes, the greater the design work, the more simple a thing will seem. These things therefore generally carry no obvious sign of any individual designer (unlike art, technology or heritage).

The point of this is not that we should take a Marxist approach. In fact, most Marxist thinkers have been very bad at producing any decent account of design, partly because of a failure to appreciate thinking as a form of work. Designing is not manufacturing. Rather, the point is that we should be asking similar questions of design culture as Marx asked of commodity culture. The notion of a fetishised design directs us to take very seriously how it is that thought can operate as the half-visible substrate of social relationships within capitalism today. As with commodities, the perception and sensoriality of designed things, their ‘mysteriousness’ is an important factor in how design culture operates in the macrostructures of the global political economy.

The second set of critiques of design culture emphasise how design is a colonising force. These approaches also argue that design can deceive, but it is not labour which is obscured.

Instead, design culture suppresses and hides the immense cultural diversity of ways of creating, imagining and making. Rather than use my own words, those of Escobar (2018) express the problem much better. In this quotation, he is describing the implications of the 20th century modernist design tradition, which saw its project as global ‘development’:

A new design dream was overtaking the world; we are still engulfed by it, even though, for many, as for the Earth itself, the dream is increasingly turning into a nightmare. What the United Nations envisaged was a sweeping “elimination design” (Fry 2011) of its own, aimed literally at eliminating the vernacular design and endogenous practices that for centuries had nourished, for better or for worse, the lives of millions throughout the centuries. Almost overnight, a diverse range of rich and vibrant traditions were reduced to being worth, literally, nothing: nondescript manifestations of an allegedly indubitable fact, “underdevelopment”. Yet this dream made perfect sense to millions and was embraced by elites worldwide. Such was the power of this design imagination.

(Escobar 2018: 6)

Most new approaches to design in the 21st century appreciate the problems of the monolithic, global design vision of the previous generation of designers. Design can be colonising, not by occupying territory, but by occupying minds, identities and culture. We shall discuss in Chapter 4 how many design anthropologists see their work as ‘decolonising’ design. While in some ways they build on the roots of de-fetishising approaches, in other ways they are profoundly different. The project of decolonising places immense emphasis on the power of the design imagination, as a social paradigm, an image of society itself. It values peoples’ capacity for, and right to, cultural reproduction and growth. It pays less attention to the work of thinking up solutions to actual problems, using material goods, in a global and cross-cultural economy.

A third, related, critical approach to design culture emphasises how it shapes our perception of the culture concept itself. By emphasising the importance of a core idea of ‘creativity’, design can manipulate how we see culture and see people. In short, some design can fetishise creativity as a quality which is embedded in some people more than others. This is a point which has been developed in critiques of the anthropological concept of ‘culture’ itself. As with any discipline, culture is a paradigm created by social scientists in order to usefully think about human issues in a certain way, just as economists created ‘the economy’ as a useful subject and psychologists ‘the mind’. To demonstrate the basic idea, rather than cite recent work, I will go back to one of the original thinkers on this point, Wagner (1981), and his work The Invention of Culture. Wagner places ‘creativity’ and ‘otherness’ at the centre of his argument, pointing out how they are linked. The idea of culture, he argues, becomes useful for people experiencing very different lifestyles, because they experience a clash between their expectations of how creativity works, as a mode to reinforce routines and to develop novelty. Robbins (2002) summarises the argument:

‘We’ assumes that differentiation and novelty are innate, part of nature, and we take the creation of convention as the paramount human project. ‘They’ assumes that convention is innate and that differentiation and novelty must be produced. In a shorthand way, we might put it that we assume the individual and make society a problem; they assume society and make the individual a problem. If we accept this scheme, one question that arises is how people might move from one position to another.

(Robbins 2002: 7)

Culture does not simply exist as something a person has, or possess an integrity in itself. Culture cannot be expressed fully as a set of rules, principles or values. Rather, it involves a way of thinking about and working with the problems of similarity and difference which are actually experienced in a globalised world. ‘The anthropologist cannot simply ‘learn’ the new culture and place it beside the one he already knows, but must rather “take it on” so as to experience a transformation of his own world’ (Wagner 1981: 9). Cultural understanding here comes through ‘culture shock’, by which the anthropologist’s interpretations of culture are creative analogies, understanding difference in terms one is familiar with. It is by experiencing ‘otherness’ then that we can put ourselves in a position to consider culture. Wagner’s arguments have a resonance with design work and design culture. While in his ‘us and them’ contrast, Wagner was thinking particularly of the 20th century contrast of Euro-American anthropologists and other communities across the world, we might take his arguments as being about people who design and people who are designed for. The ‘us and them’ fragmentation, important for thinking about culture, is also important for designing at all.

Design, I argue, has an intrinsic ‘otherness’, because seeing an object as ‘design’ really only makes sense because there has been an attempt to consider or anticipate an ‘other’ person, community or place where it may be used or have effects. On the one hand, there is the design ‘us’, in which we design as a part of being human and creative. On the other hand, there is the design ‘them’, the images of socially invested, often habitual, people who are imagined as beyond the work of design itself. Design and anthropology both necessitate confronting this fragmentation and finding ways to bridge it. To have an understanding of design adequate for a design anthropologist, we need a conception of culture as problematic, one which can negotiate between their orientation towards professional studio work as design and their orientation towards their informants as creative people. This is to acknowledge the existence of radical human alterity and difference and also acknowledge the very real possibility of misrepresenting other peoples’ experience when the divide of experience and understanding is so great.

## Anthropologies of design: push and pull

My argument here follows on from what we saw in Chapter 1, that design anthropology is not only concerned with designing for people, but also understanding the social and cultural problems of the ‘designed world’ we inhabit. In the last part of this chapter, we look at the problems of perspective posed for design anthropologists by their fragmented perspective on design. As design has grown in importance, so also have the claims on creativity across global society, from professional institutions to everyday life. Design constructs a pervasive sense of a boundary between people, through the constant pressure to place creativity at the heart of one’s own identity. Rather than the broad selection of interpretations of what design achieves in culture which I examined previously, we need a more muscular engaged politics of the operations of design. Design anthropologists confront two design processes, the ‘push’ and ‘pull’ ideas of design. ‘Push’ or ‘impact’ perspectives see it broadly in terms of extended agency and are typified by Latourian approaches (e.g. Yaneva 2009A, 2009B). By contrast, ‘pull’ or ‘consumption’ perspectives see design as created in popular culture and emphasise how cultural creation happens in using designed things (e.g. Appadurai 2013). In the next chapter, we will then examine how ideas from material culture about the process of objectification enable us to carve out alternative ways of thinking in the contested territory between the ‘push’ and ‘pull’.

What makes a computer, a pair of jeans, or a pair of chopsticks ‘designed’? From a ‘push’ perspective, it is professional designers who are best equipped to decide what counts as ‘design’. That is, it is not value, its social role, or functionality which makes it designed but rather the acts of thought and consideration by a designer or designers. The details of the world of things and services hence are interpreted as manifesting qualities of mind, and those things become either person-like or become ways of distributing the intentions of people through the world.

The most extreme tradition of considering design as the manifestation or materialisation of the thoughts of the designer dates back to the 20th century. Pevsner’s (2005 [1936]) Pioneers of Modern Design presented the origins of design as a history of visionary, even revolutionary individuals. He aspired to frame design as the equal of, but distinctive from, high art. This narrative resonated within the political frameworks of the mid-20th century, drawing heavily on the 1920s modernists, and their very conscious aspiration to achieve social revolution through design. Design could work to put aside the tragedies and inequalities of the past, such as the Great War of 1914–18, and the Great Depression of the 1930s, and usher in a brave, new, designed world.

Other interpretations of design as extended agencies are more subtle than Pevsner’s. Yaneva (2009A, 2009B) produces one of the most finely observed accounts and is one of the few ethnographers to have managed to put herself in a position to really explore professional studio environments. For example, she draws on Latour’s Actor–Network Theory to unpack the everyday architectural environments of the university. There are thousands of designed ‘things’ present, but instead of looking at discrete objects, she begins to analyse the range of modes of engagement, sentiments, uses and characteristics of existence experienced. There are at least two ways to move between floors, the stairs and the elevator, but they are very different. The same people using the staircase will tend to talk-and-walk, while the elevator is often very quiet, private even tense. The choice of socially different modes of acting is not accidental, but a result of design decisions:

If we communicate in a different way in the elevator and on the staircase it is because they offer two different modes of distributing agency with the environment. Designers have chosen between two ways of delegating action to the nonhumans: elevators and staircase, corridors and rooms, handrail and keys, walls and doors – of two ways of reassembling the social.

(Yaneva 2009B: 276)

In this analysis, design acts as a kind of social glue, materialising and concretising a framework of sociality which extends far beyond individual lives or isolated experiences. Through being designed, the material world and world of services embody cerebral acts of consideration by designers that enable a range of ways in which individual people may achieve their aims, make choices, and achieve goals.

The vision of distributed agency imagines the world as very ‘art-like’, reminiscent of the 1920s Modernists’ vision of the world as a ‘total work of art’ (Gesamtkunstwerk). As the 1919, Bauhaus Manifesto (its earlier version, from its years in Weimar) outlined,

“Architects, sculptors, painters – we all must return to craftsmanship! For there is no such thing as “art by profession”. There is no essential difference between the artist and the artisan. The artist is an exalted artisan. Merciful heaven, in rare moments of illumination beyond man’s will, may allow art to blossom from the work of his hand, but the foundations of proficiency are indispensable to every artist. This is the original source of creative design.

(Source: website of Bauhaus Foundation Dessau, accessed April 2016)

One of the implications of an art-like world is that people become potentially more aware of the nature of their relationships. So if pushed, the person experiencing a building interior might be able to begin to sketch out the range of people involved in the development of the place. Designed things then manifest social histories in the form of relationships. Gell (1998) proposes that art-like objects are an ‘art nexus’ of compounded agencies. His analysis differs from Latour’s in several ways, including how thoughts are manifested. In a Latourian network, diverse thoughts and intentions can often be anonymous and impersonal, loose but impactful threads of purpose and planning. Gell’s nexus, by contrast, is a web of cognition, so users of art-like objects encounter them as many compounded ‘minds’, in which intentions are understood as clustered and potentially forming human identities. We will look at Gell’s ideas in more depth in Chapter 3.

In either case – Latourian or Gellian – engagements with design are also engagements with prospectively hierarchical social frameworks. In practice, some people design better than others, usually through acquired skills (Grasseni 2007) and education, but sometimes also through personal ability. The political understandings of ‘impact’ approaches see some thoughts and minds as more influential than others. As we have seen, most design skills are not wholly exclusive, whereas, in many professions, there are practitioners and non-practitioners, in design, everybody who is human has some degree of skill, so the cambered hierarchies of expertise are in constant tension.

By radical contrast, ‘pull’ perspectives examine everyday experience and consumption and see design as determined primarily by context. This treatment emerges from nearly a century of studies of design history, examining shifts and trends in popular culture through the kinds of objects and material culture which surround people and are seen to typify their lives. A prominent anthropologist arguing in this vein is Arjun Appadurai:

Design and fashion, in this changing world, become the infrastructure through which the demand of objects for contexts becomes channeled and stabilized to some extent. This is a vital point, for it allows us to recognize that objects (designed things, by definition) demand contexts that can never be rigidly derived or deduced in advance from any inherent property of the object.

(Appadurai 2013: 262)

Appadurai’s argument is derived from his research on globalisation and ideas of locality. He has observed that local communities and places do not just happen, but in order to exist as stable and relatively predictable places, need constant effort and re-imagination. ‘Human history,’ he writes, ‘from this perspective, could be re-written as the history of design’ (Appadurai 2013: 254). In this framework, designers and design work do not determine if something is rightly design, the designers as persons are contingent rather than defining factors. So although, of course, products and services are intended to be used in a certain way, they may or may not succeed in that respect. Within social lives, potentialities, hopes and requirements open up. It is the ‘desire’ of products and services for such contexts, their appropriateness and fitness, which gauges them as design.

Seeing design as determined by context appreciates its opacity in that it is often a retrospective phenomenon, and one which must constantly adapt. As a diversity of examples of design have shown, it is commonly ‘discovered’ when something works, or becomes a success, rather than being ‘invented’ with some kind of clear foresight. In many projects, I have experienced a sense of retrospection and discovery: it is not uncommon for a design team to go through past work, pick over some elements, make new associations and realise that there is actually ‘a design’ in it.

Design thinkers have questioned their own authority and exclusivity since at least the 1970s. ‘All men are designers,’ Papanek (1971: 3) famously wrote, ‘All that we do, almost all the time, is design, for design is basic to all human activity’. Julier (2013) refers to this as an ‘open’ interpretation of design culture. Ingold (2012) echoes some of these themes in a discussion of design at the breakfast table, a pervasive presence shaping all of everyday life, and how it is done and learned through forms and materials. From sitting on a chair, to opening a cereal packet, to using a spoon, all of these actions are as they are because of generations of human intentions, not because they have to be this way. Why do we need spoons when we can drink straight from bowls? Why do we need chairs to sit when we can squat? Because of centuries of design infusing culture, not because any object is intrinsically necessary.

The planning and patterning of any act toward a desired foreseeable end constitutes the design process.

(Papanek 1971: 3)

Papanek’s statement about the universality of design was a critique of the prevailing mid-20th century view of design as high culture. He made the very valid point that any attempt to consider design as a ‘thing by itself’ must fail, because of the way in which it is intertwined with so many aspects of everyday life and culture. Contextual approaches enable us to critique monolithic understandings of design, appreciate design in human diversity and see many design cultures in many places and times. So a movement such as Mingei in Japan, self-declared as an art and crafts movement rather than design, can be framed as an alternative mode of creative making, and effectively a design culture. As Otto (2015: 58) notes, in his work comparing Danish design practices and Papua New Guinea ritual practices, ‘it is useful to widen the definition of design to capture the aspect of intentional cultural and material change across societies in time and space’. Anthropologically, this openness to cultural diversity is crucial and democratising, avoiding the trap of narrating design as a single tradition.

While a contextual approach helps us respect cultural diversity, it also has the effect of rendering many individual designers invisible. In consumption, when a design works well in context, it is no longer evident who made it. The kinds of hierarchies and aspirations in design are appreciated, not as hierarchies of skills, but more as hierarchies of taste (Bourdieu 1984). Rather than professional thinking work, the inequalities implicit in design emerges as popular thinking about arrays and associations of products and services, constantly debated and evaluated.

Push-and-pull perspectives are good for different kinds of exercise. Push perspectives are very good for thinking about professional design, and about the constitution of design as different kinds of groups of people, communities of practice constituted by a range of skills (Farías and Wilkie 2015, Yarrow 2019). They help us to think about effects in design, not only what a certain designed thing achieves, but also the effects which one community can have upon another. These are the cultural ‘us’ whom Wagner identifies, consciously involved in design and looking out, working to change a seemingly conservative world. Pull perspectives, by contrast, help us to scrutinise the engagements of design, to think about the relative anonymity of most designers, and how and why their labour is often rendered invisible. Pull viewpoints appreciate a much broader canvas of the temporal and global dimensions of different kinds of design. Rather than think about politics as impact and effect, we can think about questions of acceptability and evaluation. This is the equivalent of being Wagner’s cultural ‘them’, existing in a constantly changing and fluid cultural world, within which the work of stability, reliability and predictability happens.

Neither of these stances is adequate in itself, but together they help us think about the range of options of the mechanics of cultural change itself and the position of the design anthropologist within it. As we have seen, a great deal of design anthropology work concerns making innovation and change reliable, predictable and comprehensible. Involved in that exercise is a necessary sense of alterity, a sense of us and them, negotiated constantly in design work and designed things.

## Consequences: design as cultural glue or cultural oil?

Like Janus, design looks in two directions at the same time: as a silent quality of all massproduced goods it plays a generally unacknowledged but vital role in all our lives. As a named concept within the mass media it is, however, much more visible and generally recognized.

(Sparke 1986, xix–xx)

The first half of this chapter outlined some of the distinctive phenomena which social scientists have seen in design and sought to explain. These researchers have asked questions such as what distinguishes design culture, what constitutes design and what design does. Some people (including myself) have examined certain kinds of material objects and things, such as a Billy bookcase, a conceptual sketch, or Lily Blue Swedish chinaware. Other researchers focus on praxis, or certain kinds of a skilled work process. Others looked at how discourses and meaningfulness help constitute design, when people understand it as political. Widening their scope, there are also studies of design as certain kinds of social movement or tradition, such as Mingei or Bauhaus, each involving certain groups of people, kinds of work, ways of thinking and styles of object.

Most anthropologies of design only focus on certain manifestations in order to reveal a much larger sociocultural framework. Design only exists because of multiple sites and times, such that a product, to be designed, must have been planned previously, at another time and in another place. Planning work exists as design in relation to its anticipations of usages and effects. Diverse design sites and times are connected by relations of ‘forness’, of consideration and of purpose, which mean design is pervaded by alterity and an awareness of significant others. There are a wide range of ways in which we can think about this necessary interstitiality of design, its existence in between. What matters is how we think about the tensions in this chain of existence, the pushes and pulls, and how design anthropology can work within this gap, to extend it or collapse it.

It is by examining the material culture of design, as I will do in the following chapter, that anthropologists can most fruitfully address these issues. It is impossible to read minds, and that means that understandings of design as constituted in thinking are inevitably subjective. However, in design culture material things, substances and places are the closest indication we have for other peoples’ thinking. They are opaque and mystified, but they are often in different ways intentional. When design work takes account of material culture, engages with the people whose lives it represents and considers peoples’ own verbal accounts of their own material culture, it is one way of working with the interstitiality of different human contexts.

## Summary for students of anthropology

In this chapter, I have outlined a wide breadth of possible approaches to design culture, with examples, and I have summarised the implications are of taking that position. I have argued that a design anthropologist needs to build points of view between what I call the ‘push’ and ‘pull’ perspectives.

Design is a difficult topic to study as a cultural phenomenon. Practically any aspect of human life can be perceived as design when viewed in the right way such that design is relational. Encountering and observing design involves experiencing transformations between objective and subjective perspectives.

Design can be compared with other cultural categories such as art, technology, heritage or technology, but it also has its own distinctive characteristics. Cultures of design are not only about functional capacities or meaning but also about relationships between people. Design is profoundly social in its effects, directly concerned with belonging, identity, care, appropriateness, legitimacy and inequality.

In the contemporary world, the growth of design has been accompanied by a common starry-eyed admiration, but design culture can be problematic. It is especially important for a design anthropologist to have an understanding of design culture which is capable of unpacking its potential for social division, alienation and inequality.

Design can be theorised as a ‘push’, in terms of the ways that agency is distributed and plays a role across human life. There are several frameworks for doing this. When we take this approach, we reveal how it is people who decide what is or is not ‘designed’. It is an approach which reveals the necessary skills, professionalisms and hierarchies involved in design whereby design professionals set themselves apart.

Design can alternatively be theorised as determined by the ‘pull’ of contextualisation, by the rightness of how something fits. This places the responsibility for determining what is design more on non-human and material things, such that for people design is often more characterised by an experience of emergence, discovery and retrospection. Contextual approaches are capable of recognising and respecting diverse vernacular creative traditions and professions as design and emphasise the global cultural diversity of responses to the question of what makes something designed.

Design is, therefore, fundamentally involved with materiality and human environments. Approaching design as a form of material culture is one way to develop more clarity in ways of thinking about it.
