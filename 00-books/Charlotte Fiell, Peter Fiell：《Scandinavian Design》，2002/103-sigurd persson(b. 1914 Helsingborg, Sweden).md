![](images/f204fc7048d42c4cf05b3402b1cdf8e7c1ba2ae7ed819f66ed206fb621a33314.jpg) ▶ Sigurd Persson, gold and amethyst bracelet, 1963 # sigurd persson (b. 1914 Helsingborg, Sweden) The renowned Swedish designer Sigurd Persson trained under his father, Fritiof Persson, as a silversmith in Helsingborg from 1928 to 1937. He gained a journeyman's diploma in 1937 and subsequently studied at the Akademie für Angewandte Kunst (Academy of Applied Arts), Munich, from 1937 to 1939, and at the Konstfackskolan (University College of Arts, Crafts and Design) in Stockholm from 1939 to 1942. He established his own workshop in Stockholm in 1942 and began designing cutlery and jewellery. Seven years later, he founded an industrial design practice in Stockholm and designed most notably sculptural stainless steel vegetable dishes (1953) for Silver & Stål, and the Jet Line range of cutlery (1959) for the Kooperativa Förbundet (Co-operative Society of Sweden). In 1959, he won a competition to design in-flight cutlery for SAs, and later designed matching grey plastic tableware for the airline. Between 1968 and 1982, he worked as a freelance consultant to the Kosta glassworks producing brightly coloured minimally decorated designs. During the late 1960s and early 1970s Persson also worked as an independent sculptor, establishing his own gallery in Helsingborgin 1970. In 1976, he designed uniform badges for the Swedish Army, and together with nine other silversmiths opened the Argentum Gallery in Stockholm. He also designed a range of strong and boldly formed saucepans for the Kooperativa Förbundet in 1978, which exemplified his approach to the design of practical, everyday objects. Successfully bridgingthe worlds of handcraftsmanship and industrial design, Persson believed that design had to be truthful in order to be successful, and that, “In every well-designed object, it is not just the function that is described in the form. Something else is there, too – we might call it an expression of the times." Whether designing a saucepan or a silver candlestick or a piece of jewellery, Persson imbued the object with sculptural monumentality and economic functionalism. His silverware was a source of inspiration to many American silversmiths and as such, assisted in the revitalization of metal smithing as a craft at numerous American colleges and universities. Like so many other Scandinavian designers, Persson proved extremely versatile, being able to design mass-produced utilitarian products alongside exclusive limited-edition pieces. Around this time Zedigmet Hans-Christer Ericson(1945), who subsequently created a total graphic identity for the company, which included a new font. In 1985, Playsam was awarded a prestigious Excellent Swedish Design Award for its eye-catching graphic programme. The toys manufactured by Playsam were similarly innovative and were designed by Ulf Hanses (b. 1949), who had previously apprenticed to the well-known industrial designer Sigvard Bernadotte. Hanses had also designed a range of toys for handicapped children during the early 1980s, the rights to which were acquired by Playsam. His later well-known Streamliner car(1984) for the company, with its clean lines and bold use of colour, was conceived while Hanses was sketching freely, and its “obvious” aesthetic and functional purity has meant that he has never had to alter the design. As Hanses noted, “Playing with shapes, volumes and proportions is the basis of my design work. I am constantly returning to geometry. ... When I work with toys I can concentrate on expression without being disturbed bysome complicated technical idea." Hanses' toys revived and updated the age-old tradition of wooden toy making in Sweden, and it is not surprising to learn that he comes from the Dalarna region, which is famed as the place where the colourful wooden toy horses originate from. Acknowledging the importance of craft-folk heritage, Hanses' toys have a stripped-down and essential aesthetic that is akin to that of Shaker design. Hanses believes that it is the ability to convey “soul” and "expression”within a product that differentiates design from draughtsmanship, and certainly his diminutive Streamliner has an endearing character that is as engaging for children as it is for adults. As Carl Zedig has observed, “Most important is the simplicity. When you look at a Streamliner, your imagination adds details."Apart from hisStreamliner range, which includes cars, boats and planes, Hanses has also designed a simple rocking horse for Playsam that possesses a similar elemental quality.
