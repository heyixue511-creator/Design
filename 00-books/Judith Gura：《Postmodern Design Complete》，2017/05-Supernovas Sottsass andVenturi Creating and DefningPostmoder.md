ETTORE SOTTSASS and ROBERT VENTURI (see both also under “Living with Postmodern Design") were central figures in creating and defining Postmodernism in the ı98os but in fundamentally different ways, for in their work they showed that Postmodernism was not a rigid, monolithic movement but one capable of great diversity. In charting these new pathways they would profoundly change the course of twentieth-century design by redefining the concept of what constituted modern design. Sottsass embraced an approach that can perhaps best be called radical Postmodernism, whereby designers “investigat[ed] the spiritual and sensual qualities of an object," consciously blurring the line between art and design; “the outer skin of a piece of furniture...[was also] interpreted completely as image carrier."' Sottsass, moreover, believed strongly that design should have a social and cultural mission, and “the emotional nature of the relationship between people and the things that they use in daily life" was a central concern of his.² While Sottsass did create important designs for mass production, for companies like Olivetti and ALESSI (see under “Edited Design”), it was the work done primarily as limited editions or one-of-a-kind pieces, sold through groups such as MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") or high-end design galleries, that would perhaps prove to be the most seminal. This design approach came to be viewed as an “Italian version” of Postmodernism and was quite pervasive not only in Italy but in Europe. Venturi, conversely, advocated an approach that is now called historical Postmodernism. These designers overtly used and reinterpreted historical motifs and placed a decided emphasis on the aesthetic experience. There was a strongly philosophical and theoretical basis to Venturi's work, and his designs were surprisingly, unlike Sottsass's oeuvre, largely mass-produced by American companies such as Knoll\* and SWID POWELL (see essay under “Edited Design").3 Venturi thus became a symbol of a distinctive “American version" of Postmodernism. To place them in a larger context, Sottsass and Venturi were part of a generation of artists born between the world wars, though Venturi was the younger by eight years. By the i98os, they were in their late fifties and sixties and at the peak of their careers. Both were striving to break the hegemony of Modernism and consequently rewrite the history of design.4 Both designers had also trained as architects, but their careers evolved quite differently. Sottsass worked primarily as a designer, and his major architectural work came late in his career after the completion of a residence (i986-9o) for the American collector Daniel Wolf in Ridgway, Colorado. Conversely, Venturi worked primarily as an architect, and his major work as a designer was done in a relatively brief period in about 1978–93.5 Thus Sottsass's output as a designer was enormous, while Venturi's was limited in comparison; both nonetheless became international leaders of the star architect-designer movement that arose in the i98os. What is of the greatest importance are the conceptual and aesthetic models that they established for the design field. A critical analysis of their work reveals remarkable similarities as well as contrasts. Their approaches to design were quite different in the ways they handled form, materials, color, pattern, technology, and even the terminology used for naming their pieces. ![](images/2d06bf365e15b7c2b5aeb3ee656506b8eefe1104dce711843eec43763b3d01fd.jpg) ![](images/29684e14e24fb7e5569beb2bd49739a1ee5a0516ed9a608edb83db7892ac59c2.jpg) Like other postmodernists, Sottsass and Venturi rebelled against Modernism, a driving force throughout much of twentiethcentury design. They wanted to create a new conceptual approach that was not focused on function, mass production, new materials and technologies, or abstract forms. Sottsass developed a more intuitive but strongly sensory approach to design. He was an extremely articulate man but not one to write treatises.6 He worked from the micro to the macro, from details to the larger wholé, in a design process he called percorsi (paths), which was used primarily for his interiors and architecture.7 Sottsass was also a great draftsman, working in a spontaneous design process whereby objects were developed relatively quickly, producing as many as ı5o new designs in a year.8 There were also clear aesthetic influences on Sottsass. It is important to note that Sottsass was not born in Italy: he was Austrian by birth and thus came out of a decorative tradition going back to designers such as Josef Hoffmann, as exemplified by the latter's neoclassicizing work after 191o. Sottsass knew the history of neoclassical architecture and design; but he knew it so well and was so adept that his work never featured the overt historical references that were so consciously an element in Venturi's work. Then again, there was an almost neoclassical sense of form in Sottsass's work of the early i98os; his designs have a base, a shaft (or center section), and a capital. He was very conscious of the importance of the base in his objects, and its relationship to the ground.9 But, like Venturi, Sottsass was also fascinated with the flip side of the coin: everyday pop and vernacular culture, as well as kitsch. Sottsass had traveled widely, and absorbed and adapted aspects of the art of non-Western countriesparticularly India, which Sottsass visited with his first wife in i96ı: “He later described the journey as a search for origins and an exploration of the sensory (rather than the rational) terms of existence; India expanded his understanding of the connections between objects and the rituals of daily life. The colours, forms, language and mystical references of Indian culture reverberated in his work for decades to come."1o Venturi had a far more rational and philosophical approach to design." He was one of the most brilliant theoreticians of the second half of the twentieth century. Learning from Las Vegas (1972; co-authored with DENISE SCOTT BROWN, also see under “Living with Postmodern Design", and Steve Izenour) shows his fascination with the pop and vernacular of the everyday and the appeal of bustling American urban centers such as Las Vegas. Both books became important manifestos for Postmodernism and among the most influential publications of the twentieth century, Venturi looked primarily to Western design for inspiration for his forms, which he would then abstract and simplify. His work was cerebral and high-style, with layered references to historical styles, displaying little of the sensuality or spontaneity of Sottsass. Scott Brown herself has called it a "Quaker Puritanism," a term that could perhaps only be considered as an attribute in a city like Philadelphia.'² Venturi, like his Italian counterpart, liked to mix opulent and vernacular forms and materials for sensory impact; he even delighted in brashly juxtaposing different historical styles. The influence of pop was also evident in Venturi's designs, in which "cartoon graphics, flatness, large scale, references to commercial art, and the 'contradictions of scale and context' he championed in Complexity and Contradiction" can be seen.'3 An analysis of Venturi's work reveals a pronounced two-dimensionality: in his forms, patterns (stenciling, silk-screening, enameling), and materials (plywood and plastic laminate). In contrast to Sottsass, Venturi worked from the macro to the micro, starting out with a larger concept and then refining every detail as the design progressed. He was also a great and tireless draftsman, but his design process was more deliberative, taking time to develop and refine objects. It is worth noting that the three designs discussed here were Venturi's first serious experiments in furniture;'4 he thus succeeded in the enormous challenge of producing major works on his first try—no small feat. Sottsass's and Venturi's approaches are best exemplified by looking at examples of their furniture designs done in the early i98os, when Postmodernism was in its formative years. Looking back more than three decades later, it is easy to forget how radical these designs were when they first appeared to challenge Modernism. Sottsass preferred case furniture as the form with which to develop his ideas, as opposed to the chair; in his complex compositions he would increasingly blur the lines between cupboards and tables, combining elements freely. Case furniture allowed Sottsass to play with oversized, bold, and exploding forms—highly original designs without historical precedent. He was not interested in new technology or innovative materials; rather, his pieces were handcrafted by skilled artisans employed by Memphis, the company he created to produce his designs and those of his likeminded cohorts. Sottsass's designs for Memphis could be production or limited edition. He was prodigious and produced new collections for Memphis annually. His initial pieces from the early i98os were quite planar or two-dimensional in form and pattern; these designs were not about function but “the forms, colours, materials and volumes used."'5 Sottsass employed vernacular plastic laminate for the finished surface of his pieces but embellished them with bold patterns of his own design or vivid colors; he would on occasion use multiple patterns on objects. There is also a consciously kitsch aspect to many of these designs. Sottsass's work is meant to be over-the-top, bordering on the vulgar;6 there is a Promethean explosion of exuber ance, in contrast to Venturi's rarefied, intellectual energy. It is also important to note the exotic names that Sottsass chose for early designs: Casablanca, Ashoka, Murmansk, and Euphrates, for example.'7 The influence of Postmodernism on the contemporary design field has been so pervasive over the last three decades that it is now hard to imagine the sheer shock and consternation that ensued when Memphis debuted in i98r. Pieces such as Casablanca (or Carlton) were the perfect proclamations of this new radical approach to design, for, at 7ft (2.im) high and 5ft (ı.5m) wide, their forms were like giant, exploding exclamation points. In the early years of Memphis, Sottsass's forms were characteristically planar and were covered in two-dimensional patterns (in this case Spugnato) or sheets of brightly colored plastic laminate. In contrast, his compositions were often symmetrical and traditional tripartite forms with a base, shaft, and capital, which is especially evident in his sketches, which bristle with energy. Perhaps most importantly, in designs such as Casablanca, with its flaring arms and monumental scale, Sottsass was “arranging objects or volumes...giving a...dynamic sense" to his work.'8 Four years later, a dramatic change could be seen in designs like the Tartar console (i985), one of Sottsass's first attempts at creating more plastic or three-dimensional forms.'9 He was also beginning to play with asymmetrical shapes, making his compositions even more dynamic. Tartar would prove to be an important harbinger of later furniture designs by Sottsass.² In contrast to Sottsass's use of case furniture, Venturi preferred to develop his ideas using the chair form. He began by drawing antique furniture to learn how to assimilate and reinterpret historical styles.22 His designs are cerebral and highly original adaptations and reinterpretations of historical forms. Indeed, in approaching the Knoll project, Venturi set as his task—with his customary intellectual bravura—to reinvent and reinterpret the history of the modern chair from the eighteenth century to the ascendancy of Modernism in the i92os: an astounding feat.23 Venturi, like Sottsass, was also using objects as "symbols," but in different ways; Venturi treated the elevations of chairs like the facades of his buildings: "the design and construction of the Knoll chairs was equivalent to the decoration of a modernist shed."²4 Regarding this historical imagery, Venturi himself said: “This kind of symbolism involves exaggerated scale, color, and pattern to promote the sense of representation, to promote tension among the elements with the design and to accommodate the exaggerated stimuli our modern sensibilities are attuned to."25 He was not using new technology or innovative materials, though his designs were mass-produced by Knoll. His basic form was a two-dimensional molded plywood chair, recalling the 193os work of Alvar Aaito, a designer he much admired. The construction was simple: a back panel joined to a bent seat panel.²6 For his finished surfaces, he most often chose plastic larinate, preferring mundane and everyday materials; but the profile and detailing of the chairs was complex, Venturi playing with the two-dimensional undulations of the molded plywood and the curving profiles of the chairs. Using a simple "blank," Venturi was able to achieve enormous variety by employing a multitude of forms, colors, patterns, andtextiles. There were four models used for the Knoll series: chairs covered in the Grandmother pattern plastic laminate; chairs finished with solid-color plastic laminates (black, surf, yellow, burgundy); chairs veneered with solid-color plastic laminate highlighted with elaborate painted decoration to simulate carved or applied ornament; and chairs laminated with veneers of light, naturalgrain maple or dark gray-stained bird's-eye maple, an exception to his general avoidance of rich materials. The chairs could also be embellished with an upholstered seating pad, but both his forms and textile patterns were highly two-dimensional. In the end, it took Venturi some six years (1978-84) to refine the design and achieve his exacting details.27 The form of Venturi's Queen Anne side chair is a visibly literal adaptation of an eighteenthcentury chair, with its curved stiles, central splat, and subtle undulations in the front legs to evoke cabriole legs. TheGrandmother pattern—here in a plastic laminate adapted from an identical textile—is one of Venturi's most astute designs. It was inspired by “a flowered tablecloth that had belonged to an associate's grandmother, over which a pattern of black dashes inspired by the conventional lining of business envelopes was superimposed."28 In this design, Venturi combines historicism and the vernacular with great élan. The Art Deco chair is slightly smaller in scale but a more original design. Here Venturi focused his attention on the design of the back panel, an extraordinarily intricate composition employing solid and void, geometric and floral patterns, and subtle and bold colors—again, complexity and contradiction. Preliminary drawings show his arduous design process, in which he used black and colored markers to create patterns on full-size profiles of the chairs. The Chippendale chair is one of Venturi's most luxurious designs. Here Venturi's attention is on the form of the back, with its sculptural crest rail and perforated splat. It is its materials, however, that give the design such elegance: gray-stained bird's-eye maple veneer (sumptuous color with an intricate surface) is contrasted against his Tapestry pad, a colorful and dramatic oversized floral pattern “overlaid with an abstract geometric pattern of rotated white squares in purple ones."29 Again, drawings by Venturi show his painstaking search for the perfect interplay of subtle profiles in multiple dimensions. This brief overview reveals the extraordinary breakthroughs made by Sottsass and Venturi in the early [98os. In creating and defining two distinct aspects of Postmodernism, they caused a seismic shift in twentiethcentury design, for they redefined modern design so that it was no longer just Modernism but a much more inclusive and multifaceted movement. Their designs, moreover, still resonate today in the work of later generations of designers who have built on and extended their philosophical, conceptual, and aesthetic legacies.
