She began her training at just 16years of age in the ceramics department of the Taideteollinen Oppilaitos (Institute of Industrial Arts) in Helsinki. After completing her studies in 1952, she worked briefly as a designer forthe Wärtsilä-Arabia ceramics factory, and in 1953married the Finnish furniture and interior designer Antti Nurmesniemi. The same year, the founders of Marimekko, Viljo and Armi Ratia (1912-1979), “hand-picked”her to become the company's new artistic director. During the immediate post-war years there were shortages ofall kinds in Finland and the lack of new clothes meant that women's dresses, which tended to be made of dark and drab coloured textiles, were endlessly adapted in the cause of “make do and mend”. Around this time, however, theRatias other firm Printex had begun producing colourful and vibrant furnishing textiles designed by Maija Isola that were printed on inexpensive cotton sheeting. The concept for Marimekko was to use similar screen-printed textiles for clothing, and Vuokko Eskolin-Nurmesniemi was entrustedwith creating the first line of youthful, fun and off-thepeg garments, which were the very antithesis of the expensive and rather staid collections emanating from the well-known Paris fashion houses. Eskolin-Nurmesniemi's textiles, such as Tibet and Ram, were patterned with large geometric motifs, and often used two overlapping colours so as to create a third colour – a technique that became a key feature of Marimekko fabrics. The design of Eskolin-Nurmesniemi's clothes was as radical as the patterning ofher textiles. In her search for greater universality of appeal she eliminated traditional features such as tucks, darts and button-holes and replaced them with zippers and touch-and-close fasteners such as Velcro and press-studs (up until then only ever used on men's clothing) so as to make her garments more user-friendly and less physically restrictive. The first Marimekko show was held in Stockholm in 1957and Eskolin-Nurmesniemi'sground-breaking designs were widely acclaimed by critics. The bright colours she used were especially appreciated in the United States and her clothes soon became highly fashionable there after Jacqueline Kennedy purchased a number of them. Her clothes and textiles were favoured in particular by designers, architects and other creatives who preferred her unconventional look, which reliedon structural essentialism rather than on gimmickry or showiness. In 1964, Eskolin-Nurmesniemi founded her own Helsinki-based company, Vuokko, and continued to create loose-fitting clothing that predicted the future work of designers such as Issey Miyake (b. 1938), who regards her as his “design mother". She has received numerous awards including the Lunning Prize (1964), the Finnish State Design Prize (1969), the Prince Eugen Medal (1986), andthe Kaj Franck Prize(1997). In 1977 her printed cottons and jacquards were exhibited at NewYork's Metropolitan Museum of Art, and in 1988 she was made an Honorary Royal Designer in London. Throughout her career VuokkoEskolin-Nurmesniemi has created trends rather than followed them. Having accumulated much wealth as a result of his dealings in Turku, Thorwöste subsequently loaned some money to the owner of the local Antskog ironworks for its expansion. The loan could not be repaid and so by default Thorwöste obtained full proprietorship of the works in 1647. He subsequently gained permission to extract iron ore from the Ojamo mine and in 1649 was officially allowed to manufacture cast iron and forged items (except cannon). The same year he was also granted permission to establish a blast furnace and a bar hammer at Fiskars. This new ironworks was highly productive, being able to produce over 1,000 kg of iron a day: The Fiskars ironworks manufactured all kinds of products such as nails, wire, knives, tools, and reinforced wheels, which were transported to Stockholm and the provinces in the south of Finland. During the mid-18th century, the ironworks began producing small amounts of copper that was rendered into utensils by Fiskars' smiths. In 1822 the ironworks was acquired by John Jacob Julin, who during the 1830s established at Fiskars the first fine forging workshop and the first machine workshop in Finland. By now the ironworks was producing various household utensils, including cooking pots, frying pans, high-quality tableware, kitchen knives, scissors, and the famous puukko hunting knives. By the turn of the century Fiskars was manufacturing some forty different models of horsedrawn plough (in total over one million ploughs were produced at the ironworks between 1860 and 1900). During the First World War the ironworks was modernized and its product range expanded, but its further growth was impeded by the global depression of the 1930s. The post-war period saw Fiskars focus its attention on the developing American market. To this end the company produced a stylish, export-led range of flatware, known as Triennale (1957), which was designed by Bertel Gardberg and reflected the growing internationalism of Finnish design during this period, In 1967 the company launched its famous orangehandled O-series scissors (1963), which had been developed by the engineer and wood-carver Olof Bäckström (b. 1922). The prototype for the ergonomically conceived ABS handles of these scissors was carved by him from solid wood.
