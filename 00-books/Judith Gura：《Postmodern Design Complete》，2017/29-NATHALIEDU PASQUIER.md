# NATHALIE DU PASQUIER b.1957 BORDEAUX-BORN DESIGNER WHOSE DECORATIVE PIECES DISTILL AFRICAN PATTERNS THROUGH A GALLIC SENSIBILITY. It is not surprising to learn that Nathalie Du Pasquier began her career as a painter rather than a designer. Most of her patterns could easily be framed and mounted on walls; in fact, she refers to her work as "decorated surfaces." Du Pasquier is known for exuberant designs for textiles and floor-coverings whose irresistible appeal mixes vivid Mediterranean colors with patterns inspired by non-Western cultures. She and her friend and compatriot MARTINE BEDIN were the youngest members of the MEMPHIS (see under "Edited Design") collaborative, drawn to Italy by the appeal of a new, more colorful approach to design than that of Bauhaus-inspired Modernism. Born in Bordeaux, France, Du Pasquier was fascinated by the intensity of the textiles she saw as a teenager on visits to Africa. She began to translate them into her own designs, naming many after the countries she had visited. Her patterns are not African, however, but an ingenious melding of ethnic inspiration and her own sensibility. They were a radical departure fromn the formal imagery that was popular at the time, and indeed from any of the historicist patterns embraced by others who rejected the modern aesthetic. Distinctive for both their originality and the impossibility of being labeled with a particular style, her textiles and carpets are unmistakable in their extravagant pattern and saturated colors. In 1978 Du Pasquier moved to Italy, where she met GEORGE SOWDEN. who became her husband and frequent collaborator. In i98ı both became part of the initial group drawn into ETTORE SOTTSASS's (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") circle to establish Memphis. The Mamounia chair, in Memphis's first collection, was a joint effort: Sowden's design and Du Pasquier's fabrıc. She also designed textiles for the collaborative, as well as bold area rugs that complemented the furniture in deliberately unmatched effects—a kind of compatible clashing. "It was probably the beginning of a new era," she said in 2o14, "Form did not have to follow function any more, and design was about communication."²2 Du Pasquier and Sowden continued to collaborate on special projects as well as designing independently; they showed a series of one-off ceramics in Lucerne in i986, and "Objects for the Electronic Age" (i983), an exhibition of twentyfour colorful small pieces such as clocks and lamps, brought them considerable attention. In fact, ABET LAMINATI (see under “Edited Design") produced patterns designed by Du Pasquier specially for the collection. In the peak years of Postmodernism, retailers Esprit\* and Fiorucci found that Du Pasquier's designs were ideal companions for their trend-setting clothing, and she also designed small objects for ARC74 and timepieces for Lorenz. Except for the timepieces, she classified everything she did as "decorated surfaces"—two-dimensional designs that were almost endlessly varied, and distinctively her own style. When Memphis disbanded in 1987, Du Pasquier left the design world and returned to painting and sculpture in her Milan studio, expanding her aesthetic to explore a dialogue between European and Chinese painting traditions. She began to show her work in galleries and abroad. In more recent years she has returned to design after being rediscovered by the fashion world, where her 198os patterns are being translated into decorative accessories. Iames Evanson (also see under “Living with Postmodern Design") has successfully negotiated the delicate balance between art, design, and craft in several decades' worth of furniture and lighting designs, while pursuing a parallel path as an architect and builder. Montana-born Evanson applied his several skills as a participant in the avant-garde art movement that developed in New York as Postmodernism was emerging in Italy. He was one of the group of young designers, artists, and craftspeople converging around Art et Industrie\*, the influential gallery opened by Rick Kaufmann in i977 in SoHo. In shows that defied the conventions of furniture, rejecting stylistic labels and prefiguring the twenty-first-century category of design-art, Evanson and his cohort presented furniture in tune with the hip-hop and graffiti culture of the time. In addition to introducing innovative American designers and artisans, Art et Industrie was also a pioneer in showing avant-garde work from Italy. Evanson's furniture, assembled using traditional craft techniques, combines abstract forms in unconventional combinations of materials with historical iconography. It straddles the dividing line between fine and decorative art; he is concerned with art that functions, rather than art as an end in itself. This can be credited to Evanson's early training with his father, in carpentry and cabinetmaking, which led him to enroll in the industrial design program at ArtCenter College of Design in Los Angeles. After interrupting his studies to serve as an army helicopter pilot in the Vietnam War, Evanson returned to school, switching first to illustration and then to fine art. He left to open his own storefront studio, and gradually began to apply his skills to making hybrid objects combining art and cabinetry. In i975 he was invited to participate in a show by the influential dealer Ivan Karp, and moved to New York. Setting up a workshop in the developing NoHo neighborhood, Evanson began to make artwork as well as design/build projects to support the artmaking. Deciding to complete his education, Evanson enrolled in the architecture program at Pratt Institute. After graduating in i977, he continued to refine his approach to functional art, using fragmented forms and mixed materials in a direction that paralleled the work of the Italian avant-garde. His best-known designs are probably the Lighthouse lamps (i984), a series of eight pieces suggesting tall buildings with rays of light and color streaming from the window openings. They were, Evanson explained, designed as pleasuregiving art objects: “I wanted them to reflect the spirit of the i98os, not the international modernistic style of the 1950s and 196os."23 They brought him media attention and an invitation to exhibit with MEMPHIS (see under "Edited Design"), after ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") attended an Art et Industrie opening and admired the work. In the years when Italians were being lauded for design leadership, Godley-Schwan were among the relatively few American practitioners of innovative, unconventional furniture design. Their spirited shapes and comic-book colors were strikingly different from anything then being produced in a country that came late to Modernism. Their colorful and appealing pieces drew considerable media attention, one reporter calling them "children's furniture for adults."24 Lyn Godley originally planned to be a fine artist, receiving a BFA from Ohio State University and an MFA from the University of Wisconsin (ı98o). Lloyd Schwan's training was also in art, but when he began to work with Godley, the pair decided to take a more practical direction, designing objects that satisfied their desire for creative expression but would be saleable as well. This led them to design and make pieces that could have some actual function, even if that function was second to their ornamental value. Godley and Schwan started off small, establishing their business in Chicago in 1984, but decided the following year to move to New York and set up a studio in Brooklyn to produce their handmade artworks. Their first success was a collection of jewelry that mixed geometric-shaped beads with unusual materials like rubber rings and colored wire. The pieces were fanciful combinations of Pop art and atomic age imagery, and an immediate success with fashion-forward consumerslooking for something new and different. These were accessible artworks, proclaiming that fashion need not be stodgy and that jewelry need not be made of precious materials. ![](images/574f600abfaa3b4c412c892cf7412cbb44b43ecc688e78d99433e82502a19947.jpg) From jewelry, Godley and Schwan moved into designing furniture, like the Otto cabinet, a bicolor hourglass shape set sideways and mounted on slim legs, and the sculptural Dancer cabinets, which could be siblings to some Studio Alchimia\* designs. They also made lighting and accessory pieces, selling the works through galleries or directly to clients. But that was to change as they looked for new ways to showcase their work. Taking an ambitious and unprecedented leap in 1988, Godley-Schwan became the first American firm to exhibit at the Salone di Mobile in Milan. The timing was perfect; although MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") had just disbanded, Postmodernism was still in the ascendant, and the move brought them considerable attention and retail distribution. Unlike most frms making limited-production objects, Godley-Schwan began to show their designs at trade exhibitions in America, broadening their own distribution and helping erase the elitist image of designer products, almost all of which were then being sold exclusively through specialist galleries. The partnership ended in ı998, and Lyn Godley has continued to design under the Godley-Schwan name. Her primary focus has been on lighting, working with different light sources and most recently with fiber optics, in fine art and public art projects. To her, design and art are inseparable.
