McMug was the most popular comics character in Hong Kong in the 1990s, with a devoted following among children and adolescents. ## 246–250.

Art/Script: Lai Tat-wing

With this work, Lai Tat-wing broke away from the manga that dominated comics in the 1990s to create something original. Even the title is fresh: the words form a sound without any real meaning. His strips left much to the reader's imagination. For example, the 160-page comic Dan-dan Ha, Go-go Nut contains no dialogue at all. Lai’s highly controlled line drawings and composition, and his originality and enthusiasm were well received by readers. Today it is considered one of the most original Hong Kong comics of the 1990s.

## 251–254.

## Three Times Seven Equals Twenty-One, 1995 Art/Script: Au-yeung Craig

This alternative comic was a mixture of illustrations and comics drawn in untraditional formats. Each page of the ten chapters contains just one frame; the frames are broken into action sequences to tell the story. The book was printed in both Chinese and English, reflecting the bilingual culture of Hong Kong. Auyeung later published more highly original work in the series My Own Private Heaven, which maintained the bilingual format while experimenting more with traditional comic forms.

## 255–256.

## Mom's Drawer Is at the Bottom, 1998 Art/Script: Lau Lee-lee

This self-described feminist manhua was the first of its kind in Hong Kong. The artist challenged social and cultural norms by engaging feminist humor, and outlining the relationships amoong gender, humor, and feminist social critique. The 228-page collection included such topics as sex and sexuality, sex roles, gender politics, life, media, home, the nation, and the earth. Lau, however, did not draw her manhua in an explicit way, nor did she directly attack patriarchal ideology.
