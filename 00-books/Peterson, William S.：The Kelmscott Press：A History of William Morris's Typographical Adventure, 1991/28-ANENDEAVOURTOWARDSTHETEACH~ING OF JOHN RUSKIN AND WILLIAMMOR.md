The Doves Press, founded in 19oo by Cobden-Sanderson and Emery Walker, was the most distinguished successor of the Kelmscott Press and was patterned after the Kelmscott model very closely indeed. Given the close relationship of both its proprietors to Morris, the similarities are of course hardly unexpected. Cobden-Sanderson brought his partnership with Walker to an end in 1909, and the Press itself closed in 1916; in order to prevent Walker from using the Doves fount in future books, Cobden-Sanderson melodramatically tossed the punches, matrices, and types into the Thames under cover of darkness. The antagonism that erupted between Walker and Cobden-Sanderson is, however, only a partial explanation of the ultimate collapse of the Doves Press: a deeper reason lies in the rigidity of Cobden-Sanderson's vision of what he termed the Book Beautiful' (a phrase sometimes erroneously attributed to Morris but presumably derived from Oscar Wilde's House Beautiful'). The unhappy truth is that the Doves books are boring in their cold perfection, each one an immaculate clone of the other. Albeit stunningly lovely, they disclose none of the vitality that one finds in even the least satisfactory Kelmscott books. Yet this extreme simplicity of the Doves Press volumes mediated between the ornate pages of the Kelmscott Press and the more austere tastes of twentieth-century typographers. By demonstrating that Morris's principles of book-design could be applied with equal success to a completely undecorated text, Cobden-Sanderson and Walker translated the Morrisian Ideal Book into terms acceptable to printers and publishers who found Morris's own style intolerable. Giovanni Mardersteig, whose Officina Bodoni has been regarded by some as the most outstanding private press of the twentieth century, is only one notable example of a young man who first became excited about the possibilities of fine printing through the example of the Doves Press.² What we must always bear in mind, however, is that the Doves Press could not have existed without the theories and practice of Morris lying behind it. The Press had a long, illustrious history, much of it falling outside the boundaries of this book; but what Hornby's career as a printer especially demonstrates is that it was (and is) possible to be a disciple of Morris yet to chart one's own course in book-design. The Ashendene volumes convey the impression that Hornby still speaks Morris's language but has translated it into a new and unexpected dialect. The Vale Press (1896-1904) of Charles Ricketts offers another instructive example of how the Morrisian influence could be diluted by other forces. Ricketts admired Morris's books immensely, but he recognized that it was only too easy for the Kelmscott style to be debased by less talented designers. In an essay entitled A Defence of the Revival of Printing (1899), Ricketts commented on the ornamental tendency' in Morris which led him to create books ornamentally conceived and of ornamental use. With such pomp of circumstance', he observed, they became more and more large, necessitating ornamental type such as the Troy type; certain books like the Chaucer should be perused at a lectern.' When the designing of such a book fell into the hands of a typographer who only half-understood Morris's principles, the results were disastrous: deprive ornament of its excellence, relate it to some casual fount, throw in with this a photogravure frontispiece, badly blocked boards, various papers, &c., and the new usual ornamental book becomes unbearable; instead of a revival in the shaping of books we have a new cumbersome trade article.' The type that Ricketts designed for the Vale Press is a bold, slabserifed Roman which openly proclaims its indebtedness to the Golden type, and Ricketts's borders also owe something to Morris's, though the general effect of a Vale page is much lighter, more delicate and open. Ricketts, like Cobden-Sanderson, found the dark coloration of the Kelmscott books unattractive and sought inspiration instead in the less cluttered pages of the early Venetian printers. I would define the page of a fine Kelmscott book as full of wine, an Italian book as full of light,' he declared. Yet if Hornby veers off into a different dialect of the Morrisian language of bookmaking, Ricketts seems to be speaking in a different tongue altogether: the earnestness and solidity ofthe Kelmscott style have, through some strange sea-change, become mannered and precious. Ricketts's deepest links, despite his professed emulation of Morris, are in fact with the art-nouveau book that coexisted with the Morrisian style during the final decade of the century. John Russell Taylor, in a lengthy study of the art-nouveau fashion in typography, has offered a useful and concise distinction between the two traditions: Arts and Crafts [i.e. the Kelmscott-inspired style] is essentially, as the label suggests, direct, unaffected, simple (or at least simpliste); it is fairly solid and robust; and it is solidly traditional, often with a sort of revivalist fervour. Art nouveau, on the other hand, is essentially sophisticated and eclectic, accepting and assimilating influences from here, there and everywhere – Japanese, Greek, Celtic, Pre-Raphaelite. . . , Rococo, Dutch, Blake, Victorian industrial design, etc., etc. Moreover, art nouveau is above all highly mannered."7 The origins of art nouveau are obscure and complex; suffice it to say here that Aubrey Beardsley was responsible for introducing the style in book illustrations and ornaments, and, as we saw earlier, this came about as a direct result ofhis failed attempt to illustrate a Kelmscott book. When Morris peremptorily refused his frontispiece for Sidonfa the Sorceress, Beardsley saw Dent's edition of Le Morte Darthur (1893-4) as a wonderful opportunity to achieve revenge by parodying the Morris-Burne-Jones conventions and modifying them in a sensual, decadent fashion. The Morte Darthur was a great commercial success, and it effectively launched Beardsley's career as an illustrator.
