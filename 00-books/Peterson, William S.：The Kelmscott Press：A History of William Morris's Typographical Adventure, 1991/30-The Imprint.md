There is the danger in habitual excess of language that the plain man is likely to be frightened by it. It may be feared that much current exposition of the place and purpose of art only widens the gap between it and common lives. A proper function of criticism should be to foster our national arts and not to frighten timid people off with high-pitched definitions and far-fetched metaphors mixed with a flood of (as Morris said) "sham technical twaddle." It is a pity to make a mystery of what should most easily be understood. There is nothing occult about the thought that all things may be made well or made ill. A work of art is a well-made thing, that is all. It may be a well-made statue or a well-made chair, or a well-made book. Art is not a special sauce applied to ordinary cooking ; it is the cooking itself if it is good. Most simply and generally art may be thought of as THE WELL-DOING OF WHAT NEEDS DOING. If the thing is not worth doing it can hardly be a work of art, however well it may be done. A thing worth doing which is ill done is hardly a thing at all. Fortunately people are artists who know it not—bootmakers (the few left), gardeners and basketmakers, and all players of games. We do not allow shoddy in cricket or football, but reserve it for serious things like houses and books, furniture and funerals. Naturally Morris had his defenders after the war, chief of whom was Bernard Newdigate, the accomplished printer at the Shakespeare Head Press, who wrote a regular colunn entitled Book Production Notes'for the London Mercury between 192o and 1937. Newdigate repeatedly reminded his readers of Morris's achievement at the Kelmscott Press, as in this representative passage from a column of 1934: A few years since it came to be the fashion to disparage Morris's work as a printer, or, at best, to dann it with faint praise; and this notwithstanding that in the eight-and-thirty years which have passed since his death, no books have surpassed his in excellence and sheer beauty, and none have been printed anywhere save at the Doves and the Ashendene presses which are worthy to rank with then.'9 Newdigate was one of several typographical enthusiasts — the others were Francis Meynell, Stanley Morison, Oliver Simon, and Holbrook Jackson – who met in London during 1922 in order to establish a publishing society that would issue one book a year, set and printed by mechanical means yet produced to the highest standards. The would-be founders immediately discovered themselves divided ideologically: There were disputes on the texts suggested and the manner of printing them and Newdigate was felt to have too strong a tendency towards the ideas and practice of William Morris,' Simon recalled.3° The society was disbanded before it was even organized. Though no more than a minor episode, the stillborn Fleuron Society represented emblematically the struggle between Morrisian handicraft methods and the modern mechanization of printing. This was in fact the very issue that rendered Morris's accomplishments so problematic during the second and third decades of our century. Among the intelligentsia of the day there were urgent calls to harness the machine to aesthetic and hunane ends, emphasizing the importance of design in inustrial production, and Morris's Luddite tendencies now seemed distinctly embarrassing. In England the most eloquent expression of this viewpoint came from Aldous Huxley, who wrote in 1928: It has become obvious that the machine is here to stay. Whole arnies of William Morrises and Tolstoys could not now expel it. . . . The sensible thing to do is not to revolt against the inevitable, but to use and modify it, to make it serve your purposes. Machines exist; let us then exploit them to create beauty - a modern beauty, while we are about it. For we live in the twentieth century; let us frankly admit it and not pretend that we live in the fifteenth. . . . The printer who makes a fetish of hand-work and medieval craftsmanship, who refuses to tolerate the machine or to make any effort to improve the quality of its output, thereby condemns the ordinary reader to a perpetuity of ugly printing.' Huxley thus transforms Morris's profoundly moral rejection of industrialism into a conspiracy against the reader of modest means; the same point was often made during Morris's lifetime (Talbot Baines Reed, for example, said something very like this in a letter to Emery Walker written in 1888³²), yet Huxley's polemic is striking both because ofits cleverness and because ofits assumption that the benefits of mechanization and mass-production are so self-evident that they no longer require defence. Even those whose fundamental attitudes were close to Morris's acknowledged the supremacy of the machine in the contemporary world. Eric Gill's brilliant but eccentric Essay on Typography (1931) illustrates how Morrisian (and Ruskinian) ideas could, with the passing of time, be metamorphosed into mysterious new shapes. Gill, a master of reductio ad absurdum, argues that, since industrialism and handicrafts represent two separate worlds of human experience, machine-made goods should be plain and simple, not imitations of craft artefacts. Once we have decided to make the machine our master, we must learn to accept the only kind of beauty it can create. On the Continent the Bauhaus movement had been advancing the same case, but what sets his reasoning apart from theirs is that it is coloured by intense hatred of our industrial civilization. When one probes beneath the surface of Gill's writings, his unlikely advocacy of functionalism proves to be not very different from the position of Morris, whose medievalism Gill claimed to despise.³ Morris's understanding of machinery was certainly more complex than Huxley's caricature of him as a regressive escapist might suggest. The confusion about his perspective was compounded by the simpleminded propaganda of some of his disciples, particularly Sparling, who offered the world a one-dimensional version of Morris's ideas on handicrafts: Sparling could not even bring himself to admit that an Albion press is a machine.34 When the restoration of Morris's reputation came about in the following decades, therefore, it signalled a growing realization that Morris had anticipated many twentiethcentury notions of functionality and faithfulness to the nature of materials. The fullest exposition of this revisionist view of Morris is to be found in Pevsner's Pioneers of the Modern Movement (1936), which was retitled in the second edition Pioneers of Modern Design: From William Morris to Walter Gropius; but one can see the same pattern of coming to terms with Morris in the public and private pronouncements of Stanley Morison, who was largely responsible for the important programme of historic type revivals at the Monotype Corporation. Morison, like many of his contemporaries, viewed Morris's printing with distrust and delivered a steady stream of criticism of it during the 192os and ı93os. In a 1944 lecture delivered in Edinburgh (published in Morison's The Typographic Arts: Two Lectures, 195o), however, he adopts a more Pevsnerian view of Morris, arguing that he was not really opposed to the machine-made book. Morison is able to make a persuasive case because Morris does indeed refer occasionally in his typographical writings to the application of good principles of design to commercially produced books. Yet to make Morris a cryptomodernist who had the misfortune to be born in the nineteenth century, as both Pevsner and Morison imply, is a form of special pleading that brings us no closer to the core of Morris's ideas than do Sparling's purple passages attacking machinery. Another issue on which Morris was seen to be in the wrong was the ornamentation of books; one of the most widespread manifestations of anti-Victorian sentiment in the 192os was the conviction that ornament, especially of the historicist kind, was morally and aesthetically inappropriate for a machine-made product. The application of this doctrine to book-design led to what one French critic later described, in a different context, as astyle calviniste',3 characterized by a puritanical fear of any decoration of the text. Holbrook Jackson offered a trenchant summary of the doctrine in 1924: ‘Decadent printing is always ornamental printing. When typographical imagination fails it becomes decorative.' It was the Bauhaus school that adopted the idea most zealously, eschewing all ornamental patterns except for the functionalist structure of blocks of plain sanserif type. In England the hostility to ornament rarely reached such a pitch; even Morison, who espoused a different variety of typographical austerity, admitted that the taste for ornament is an essential human need.37 So long as ornament was seen as a symptom of moral weakness, however, it was impossible for Morris's contributions to fine printing to be acknowledged fully. Whereas Morris's contemporaries had been startled by his rejection of typographical orthodoxies, in the early decades of this century the Kelmscott books were seen as the final, overripe expression of Victorian ornamental eclecticism. Under these circumstances, how can one claim any connection at all between Morris's theories of bookdesign and the renewal of English printing that took place after 1918? There is indeed a direct line of descent from the Kelmscott Press to the 192os and 1939s, but, because Gill's and Morison's generation found it psychologically impossible to acknowledge the ancestry of their typographical views, the line is not always easy to trace; we can glimpse the connection, however, by looking briefly at the careers of two men who, with Emery Walker, were largely responsible for bringing the Morrisian vision of the Ideal Book into the market-place. J. H. Mason, an admirer of Morris and compositor at the Doves Press, began teaching evening classes in printing at London's Central School of Arts and Crafts in 1905, and four years later, on the recommendation of Walker and Cobden-Sanderson, he was appointed the superintendent of the Printing School.38 In that position he was responsible for training many of the next generation of British printers. The typographical style that he imparted to his students was a simple adaptation of the Kelmscott/Doves manner: closely spaced Caslon Old Face (almost the only type of which he approved) printed on handmade, dampened paper on an Albion press. Mason was aware that such conditions could not be duplicated in most twentiethcentury printing firms, but he argued that, in order to operate complex modern machinery, a young printer first had to master the fundamental elements of his craft — a principle of which Morris would surely not have disapproved. Leonard Jay, who enrolled as Mason's first pupil and in 1912 became his assistant at the Central School, eventually grew restless under Mason's regime of Morrisian orthodoxy and in 1925 left to become head of the School of Printing in Birmingham. There he continued his master's tradition of exacting craftsmanship and careful design, but his students in Birmingham were also encouraged to study advertising typography and to make use of the Linotype and Monotype machines. Jay regarded these innovations not as a lapse from the purity of the Kelmscott/Doves faith but as an attempt to prepare students for a world in which handicraft methods no longer prevailed. In a lecture delivered in 1929, Jay declared, ‘The product of machinery can only be improved when its producers are imbued with the same spirit which produced fine work in the past, and every effort should be directed towards breathing life and beauty into the products of machinery. This is a vital necessity in this mechanical age; for the more machinery that is introduced into the printing trade the greater the need for more alert, intelligent, and enlightened craftsmen. Perfect machinery is available everywhere; our saving grace lies in better quality of production.'9 In the differences between Mason and Jay one sees the process by which Morris's ideas were adapted to mechanized printing and stripped of their Ruskinian implications. Jay and his students were just as fastidious in their search for high standards of craftsmanship as Morris was, and they nearly always followed his advice on such crucial matters as wordspacing, leading, and margins; but Morris's fear of the dehunanizing effects of machinery upon workers is no longer in evidence. Francis Meynell's highly successful Nonesuch Press is another example of how the Morrisian principle of hand-production could be gradually assimilated to more modern methods, though Meynell, unlike Mason and Jay, thought that Morris was something of a calanmity' to the development of printing.4° Meynell recognized the relationship between the Nonesuch Press and Jay's work in Birmingham, because in both instances there was an abandonment of the handicraft ideal, accompanied by an abiding belief in the importance of painstaking design. In 193o Meynell wrote, My previous experience had shown me quite clearly that, in order to avoid monotony and to produce desirable editions at a reasonable cost, one must intelligently exploit the best mechanical equipment and the highest technical skill available. To-day there is more fine typographical material to be had than even the largest printing house in the country could possess; and the various “mercenary" presses have developed technical skill and variety along various lines. There was therefore no good reason, we thought, for a new private press in the old style, arrogantly selfcontained, and with but one type and obsolete “hand" machinery. . . . In this faith we began to mobilise other people's resources; to be designers, specifiers, rather than manufacturers; architects of books rather than builders. In this faith we proceed to compel the fully mechanical processes to serve for the first time the end of Fine Book making.'41 Though this stridently anti-Morris blast sounds like a complete rejection of all that the Kelmscott Press stood for, in reality Morris's principles of book-design had simply been detached from their original intellectual context and were now being claimed as their own by a new generation which believed that machinery could be tamed and civilized. ## 上安 Morris's impact on printing and book-design outside England was, for at least a decade or two, unparalleled, with the force of his influence being felt most dramatically in northern Europe and English-speaking countries. In the United States imitating the Kelmscott books became a temporary craze towards the end of the century, as Professor Susan Thompson has shown in her exhaustive study American Book Design and William Morris (New York, 1977). Roberts Brothers of Boston published a pirated facsimile of The Story of the Glittering Plain a few months after it was issued by the Kelmscott Press in 1891, and within a few years the New York periodical the Bookman (November 1896) was complaining that ‘Kelmscott-Jenson type, more or less accurately reproduced, has spread from Portland to San Francisco, and seems especially dear to the heart of advertisers of bicycles and malt extracts’. The more responsible American printers and book-designers were alarmed by this commercial degradation of Morris's ideals. Everyone who cared for William Morris must regret the extent to which his name and work are being used in the matter of printing,’ wrote Theodore L. De Vinne. ‘Tasteless imitations of his types and ornaments threaten us on every side, and no one seems able to start a new press or get some type without taking Mr. Morris's name in vain.'4² Bruce Rogers, looking back on the phenomenon in 1912, recalled that for a period of ten years the work of William Morris had an enormous influence on American printing - and not, on the whole, a beneficial one, except in its insistence upon sound craftsmanship'.43 The Kelmscott style in the United States was initially promulgated by a handful of periodicals and publishers of anglophile or Arts and Crafts tendencies. The most ambitious of the periodicals was the Knight-Errant of Boston, which survived for only four issues during 1892-3; set in Old Style type on handmade paper and ornamented with initials and tailpieces designed by Bertram Grosvenor Goodhue, the Kuight-Errant was self-consciously Morrisian and aesthetic. In the July 1892 issue an essay by Francis Watts Lee, ‘Some Thoughts Upon Beauty in Typography, Suggested by the Work of Mr. William Morris at the Kelmscott Press', provided facsimiles of three Kelmscott pages and the printer's mark designed by Morris, and described his books as the crowning glory of the nineteenth century'. Another Boston periodical was Modern Art, a quarterly published between 1893 and 1897. Its design, though more satisfactory than that of the Knight-Errant, was equally Morrisian (Kelmscott-style borders and initials, a replica of the Golden type, shoulder titles and notes in red), and it had the distinction of publishing A Note by William Morris on His Aims in Founding the Kelmscott Press', an essay reprinted in the last Kelmscott book. A considerably less imposing journal was the Craftsınan, produced by a crafts guild called the United Crafts of Eastwood, N.Y., the first issue of which (October 19o1) was devoted entirely to Morris, with vaguely Morrisian borders and indifferent typography. The publishing houses that fell under Morris's sway in the 189os displayed his influence at a relatively sophisticated level, since they were responding to a variety of styles in book-design that floated across the Atlantic. Copeland and Day of Boston, for example, occasionally imitated the Kelmscott manner, most conspicuously in their edition of Rossetti's House of Life (1894), but many of their books possessed a strongly aesthetic and decadent quality reminiscent of John Lane's characteristic style.
