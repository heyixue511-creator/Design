With this brisk introduction William Morris began writing on 11 November 1895 a brief explanation for an American admirer of how and why he had established the most famous of all private presses, the Kelmscott Press. The essay first appeared in Modern Art, a Boston magazine, in 1 896 and was later reprinted, under the title A Note by William Morris on his Aims in Founding the Kelmscott Press, as the last publication of the Press in the spring of 1898. Morris's formulation of his purposes was characteristically terse: it was the essence of my undertaking', he wrote, to produce books which it would be a pleasure to look upon as pieces of printing and arrangement of type.' The rest of the essay was devoted to a discussion of paper, type-design, spaces between words and lines, margins, and ornaments. Morris's Note is an undeniably important document. As one of his few public statements about the Kelmscott Press, it has been quoted frequently since 1898 in histories of the Press; indeed, in 1924 Bernard H. Newdigate went so far as to suggest that the Note was one of the three books that every student of English book-production ought to read.' Yet one might argue that the essay is cryptic, even evasive, leaving a host of questions unanswered. How did Morris develop an interest in book-design and production? Why did he turn to fifteenthcentury German and Italian books for his models? What was the relationship between the Kelmscott Press books and the best work of other nineteenth-century English printers? How exactly did Morris rediscover some of the fundamental principles of fine printing? How did he find the craftsmen to carry out those principles for him? We need the answers to these and other questions, for there is nowadays general agreement - even among those who dislike his style of typography - that Morris is one of the most important printers of the modern era and that the establishment of the Kelmscott Press was an event of major cultural significance. Throughout northern Europe and the English-speaking world, particularly in the United States, the impact of Morris's typographical theories and of the Kelmscott books thenselves was pervasive. Historians are still arguing about the nature of that influence (whether, in particular, it was always a good or enlightening influence), but no one disputes that the forces set in motion at the Kelmscott Press eventually spread round the globe. Fron a merely quantitative point of view, the achievement of the Kelnscott Press does not seem especially impressive. Between 1891 and 1898 it produced fifty-two books and a set of specimen pages of another book that was never completed. The books were designed by Morris, printed on hand-presses, and issued in limited editions averaging about 3oo copies. All were ornamented with initials and borders by Morris, and some were illustrated, usually by Edward Burne-Jones. So why are these few Kelmscott books famous everywhere today, and why have they profoundly affected twentieth-century theories of book-design? One explanation of their potent appeal lies in the fact that Morris's ideas, like his ornamented books, are richly suggestive. When he appears to be speaking about typographical matters, he is in reality examining how we ought to live. IfI were asked to say what is at once the most important production of Art and the thing most to be longed for, I should answer, A beautiful House; and if I were further asked to name the production next in importance and the thing next to be longed for, I should answer, A beautiful Book,'he wrote in an unfinished essay. ‘To enjoy good houses and good books in self-respect and decent comfort, seems to me to be the pleasurable end towards which all societies of human beings ought now to struggle." That is a most characteristic passage by Morris: not content with treating typography as ‘a minor art' (to quote Stanley Morison's phrase),3 Morris insists upon enlarging the context and seeing the beautiful book as an essential part of his attempt to redeem the late-Victorian world from dreary mechanical ugliness. It is surely this passion for moral, aesthetic, and social reform which lends resonance to Morris's typographical pronouncements and which makes the history of the Kelmscott Press a matter of some importance even to non-bibliophiles. Morris, like many of his contemporaries, had grave misgivings about a world that was increasingly being shaped by industrialism. He looked about him at the Victorian city — smoky, polluted, crowded, and gloomy — and asked himself whether the human race was really in a better state now than it had been in the Middle Ages. In an essay published in 189o Morris took his readers on a tour of an unidentified but typical English industrial town (apparently Peterborough) and reyealed, street by street, building by building, the sheer horror of the urban landscape. With great eloquence Morris described the dreary terraced houses, the small, shabby shops, the ‘ugly modern iron bridge’, the depressed and underfed workers in the streets, the wilderness of dirty brick and stone.⁴ This was the grimy present that Morris found about hin; the past which he then immediately conjured up was a medieval world of simplicity, purity, and cleanliness. Morris's Hamlet-like injunction to Look here, upon this picture, and on this' reminds one of that famous pair of plates which had appeared in A. W. N. Pugin's Contrasts (second edition, 1841): Catholic town in 1440' and ‘The Same Town in 184o'. In the lower plate we see a serene city of spires pointing towards heaven; in the other plate the multitude of lovely churches has been supplanted by factories, belching smokestacks, and a particularly grim Victorian workhouse. Pugin's pictures, in turn, evoke Carlyle's Past and Present (1843), the very title of which vibrates with the fervour of Victorian moral idealism, and its implied contrast, like Pugin's, between a lost Golden Age and the bleak world of nineteenth-century England may help us to grasp the passionate intensity of Morris's rejection of the present. We may perhaps wish to protest that Pugin, Carlyle, Ruskin, Morris, and others of their outlook idealized the Middle Ages and ignored the material benefits of our mechanized civilization; yet we fail to do them justice unless we recognize that the Gothic Revival was not ultimately an attempt to resuscitate a period style of architecture or art but rather a morally powerful protest against the psychological and socil disintegration produced by the modern world. The Kelmscott Press was, in effect, the final phase of the Gothic Revival. Burne-Jones compared the Kelmscott Chaucer (1896) to a cathedral; by the same analogy, the smaller books can be regarded as parish churches, each faithfully rendered in quasi-medieval style. But such a sweeping rejection of contemporary life brought with it apparently insoluble problems. How, for example, could one reproduce the general appearance of a fifteenth-century book and still escape the dangers of mere imitation? By turning one's back on contemporary printing machinery, how could one improve the appearance of the ordinary book? In reverting to methods of hand-production, how could one print books that readers with modest incomes might afford? Why, it was asked repeatedly in the press, did Morris, a committed socialist, not print books for the poor? Why did he insist upon producing anachronistic books whose glamour derived primarily from their resemblance to medieval volumes? Morris was conscious of such accusations, as we shall see, and he offered persuasive answers to some of them. The dilemmas he faced at the Kelmscott Press are of more than technical interest today, because they illustrate both the rewards and dangers of a reversion to handicraft methods. If, as Morris argued, our complex technological civilization bears within it the seeds of its own destruction, then the story of the Kelmscott Press — possibly the best-known example in the Western world of a serious, thoughtful attempt to revive a traditional craft — becomes a fascinating parable of the extent to which we may be able deliberately to turn the clock backwards. One suspects that Morris has been written about more extensively than any other printer except Gutenberg. Even before the Kelmscott Press was officially opened, enthusiastic reports about Morris's ‘typographical adventure’ (as he described it in a letter) began to appear in British and American periodicals. By 1893, in distant Chicago, the Inland Printer was prepared to proclaim that ‘a new era has dawned in English printing'. Reporters flocked to Kelmscott House, Morris's home in Hammersmith, for interviews with the Master Printer'. The Kelmscott edition of Chaucer was widely described as the most beautiful printed book since the fifteenth century. In the last volume produced at the Press, Sydney Cockerell supplied A Short History and Description of the Kelmscott Press' andAn Annotated List of All the Books Printed at the Kelmscott Press in the Order in Which They Were Issued'. In 1924 H. Halliday Sparling, who had been Morris's son-in-law and, like Cockerell, a Secretary of the Press, published a book-length history entitled The Kelmscott Press and William Morris Master-Craftsman. Since then many other more specialized studies of the Press have appeared in print.7 How, then, can one justify still another book on the Kelmscott Press? The short answer is that there is really only one extended history, Sparling's, which is by now very much out of date; since 1924 a great quantity of manuscript material relating to the Press's history - including letters, diaries, manuscripts, and corrected proofs of Kelmscott books – has become available to scholars. The dispersal of Sir Sydney Cockerell's collection in 1956 and the acquisition by the Pierpont Morgan Library of the Kelmscott materials of John M. ## Introduction Crawford, Jr., are only two notable instances of the emergence in recent decades of important new documentary evidence. With these fresh records in hand, and with the calmer perspective that the passing of time usually brings, it ought (one hopes) to be possible now to write a history of the Kelmscott Press that avoids both the embarrassing proselytizing tone of Sparling's book and the biliousness of certain anti-Morris polemics published in the past. The Kelmscott Press, despite — or perhaps because of — its fame, is a subject encrusted with myths and misconceptions. It is surely worth the effort to try to set the record straight, if only because Morris's curious typographical experiment may force us to reflect on the meaning of that fascinating yet morally ambiguous technology which threatens today to envelop the world.
