changes in US mainstream popular culture. A theoretical line connects Roddenberry’s Western Have Gun – Will Travel to the science fiction/ humanistic drama of Star Trek, to Marvel Comics’ Black Panther superhero, to The Black Panther newspaper’s version of everyday black life, to the 2018 Black Panther film. All of these texts deviate from standard US hero narratives and hegemonic cultural mythology to challenge embedded ideas about race, justice, heroism, and morality. Through their images, Emory Douglas and the other BPP artists visually armed a conceptual revolution, creating more space for African Americans in the 1960s and 70s to imagine a global liberated future.

After working in graphic design, she became an educator, artist and writer, exhibiting her work internationally. Her work ranges from digital prints and artist books to websites and interactive installations. An essay on former Black Panther artist Emory Douglas’s work appears in Black Panther: The Revolutionary Art of Emory Douglas (for which she wrote a new introduction to the 2014 edition), and West of Center: Art and the Counterculture Experiment in America, 1965–1977. Since 2004, she has continued to write about Douglas’s work including his current international human rights activism. An essay on Cuban artists in The African Americas: A Collaborative Project on the African Diaspora in the Cultures of Latin America, the Caribbean, and the United States reflects her visits to the island to study art and culture.
