The ukiyo-e masters developed a way of grouping different formats which they referred to as the contest of framed pictures'(kibori gakuawase sanzu). This phrase (which appears on a surimono by Ryūryūkyō Shinsai, Picture of the Kugutsume Kaneko),¹ is used of a compositional style in which the most disparate pictorial forms – circle, oval, fan-shape, double circle, gourdshape, and the more usual rectangle and square - are used in conjunction, one format within another. The style was more subtle than this, however: the secondary formats contained within the principal pictorial structure could also be cut-off, overlapped, or ârranged in distinctive progression as desired.²

Japanese painters saw in their immediate surroundings related pictorial themes which inspired them to elaborate this unusual compositional technique. The circular window, a common feature in Japanese houses (xo32) which also recurs sometimes as a wall recess (tokonoma), afforded a direct 'detail' view outdoors.³ Seen together with interior objects such as kakemono, pillar pictures, ikebana vases etc., these could create combinations of shapes that lent themselves to pictorial depiction. Hiroshige's colour woodcut View of Sekiya village from Masaki (588) is an example of this circularwindow view. On the right a sliding wall is clearly depicted, and our eye is taken past branches with blossom in the foreground, away into the distance of the landscape. There are numerous Japanese painters' manuals which deal with the theme of the picture within a picture’ or the contest of pictures’, as it was a much loved theme for lacquer box decoration. Connoisseurs and art lovers developed particular affection for the genre because of its potential for somewhat bizarre effects.

Mixing formats became popular in Europe, and for the most part compositional forms originating from the Far East were used.

In his instructions on panel painting, which aimed at detailing how Japanese lacquerwork could be copied, Franz Lanek in 1903 brought out a series of almost 400 bound volumes of prints, which went through many editions. Each one measured 25×53.5 cm. (10×21 in.) and consisted of so-called artists' sketches. Here we see absolutely plainly the relationship to the Japanese woodcut. Examples of techniques for combining colours within a compositional field were given, and these became very widely adopted in their Europeanized versions. The decorative plates produced by the Paris manufacturer Th. Deck (589) and by the English firm Brown-Westhead, Moore & Co. of Hanley, Staffordshire (59r) are just two examples among many which either faithfully reproduce purely Japanese-style herons and plants or give a European harvest-time landscape set against Japanese’ flowers.

The circular portrait format within a rectangle was a noble genre used in Japan for illustrating tales of heroism (592, 595). The usual combinations were an oval or circle inside a rectangular or square surface, rather like a mon emblem, containing a portrait, a landscape or a figure. Many painters made use of this highly distinctive format grouping, including Shumman, Hokusai, Kuniyoshi, and above all Yashima Gakutei. Most commonly portraits formed the subject matter. These would be accompanied by a brief written text. On the whole the people portrayed were successful actors or actresses. Europe took up this format: Vuillard, for instance, chose a slightly flattened circle as a pleasing, almost mobile picture surface, in which to place a seated woman (598). He treats it experimentally, introducing deliberate verticals to counteract the ‘squashed' effect of the flattening of the circle (598).

The circular format inside a rectangle also had a functional use in that it would arrest and lead the eye forward, as if through a window. The second-generation Impressionists were especially fond of this glimpse effect, and in this departed from their great mentors. Vuillard, one of the leading lights of the Nabis, goes his own way in his picture The négligé (594). Not only does he compress the figure within the overturned oval like a wound-up spring, but he gives the bedstead and chair such clear-cut treatment, as if seen through the eye of a telescope, that they seem to assert themselves even on the space outside the picture. The closeness of the painter to his subject, conveyed by the immediacy of the picture plane, reflects the approach of the Japanese ukiyo-e artists. Hokusai, who for the Symbolists and Nabis was an outstanding, intensely original master of composition, produced a series of A Hundred Stories (Hyaku monogatari) (596), in which he uses an extremely bold layout with rectangles bitten out by quarter- or semi-circles in a highly individual way. The ghost who eats up little children could pop up anywhere – and does, in the most extreme of picture formats.

Art Nouveau artists showed à liking for circular pictures within a long horizontal format. Walter Leistikow, Richard Grimm, Ernst H. Walther, and T. T. Heine often employed this combination in their illustrative work. In Leistikow's vignette The Müggelsee near Berlin (1o31) the broad-angle view and the circular telescopic close-up are bound into a single pictorial plane.

Because it was so generally popular, this compositional form also recurred several times in the magazine Die Gartenlaube. The wide circulation of this magazine sent out ripples which, ironically enough, even made themselves felt in the painters'manuals in Japan. Themes from Gartenlaube were translated back into the Japanese idiom - as the Prussian eagle apparently glaring at a bee in one instance illustrates. The combined format technique was taken up by the printers of ephemera, and picture postcards even today sometimes testify to this inclusion of other forms within a rectangle.

The outside walls of Japanese houses are sometimes equipped with sliding grille walls known as shoji (981, 99o). The rooms inside are defined by similar, lighter walls (fusuma). The grille structure consists of closely joined, flattened strips. These strips are so arranged as to produce a sort of trellis with rectangular individual sections (1029).

Every metre (3 ft) or so there are stronger vertical strips, thicker in diameter than the inner ones. These serve as a framework. Covered with paper, the screen appears through a light, bright wall surface (984, 1023, 104o). The pattern is clearly visible during the day (990), and also at night with lights on inside (69o). The feeling for clear articulation in the Japanese colour woodcut impressed me the very first time I came across it. Horizontal lines always stand in interesting relation to the verticals. Japanese painters have devised subtle and complex methods of creating interplay between picture surface and pictorial space ... They have even developed a grille-motif of posts or trees in the foreground, middle distance, or background to specific artistic effect .. .

This passage is from a letter from Emil Orlik to Julius Kurth,¹ both important collectors of ukiyo-e works. Orlik, who is considered to have been partially responsible for the revival of the colour woodcut in Germany, was in Japan between 190o and 1901 and then again in 1911 and 1921. We owe him a debt of gratitude for his practical investigations into the techniques of the Japanese colour woodcut. His close association with the leading Western ukiyo-e specialist Julius Kurth was very fruitful, and encouraged avid collecting, which was of vital importance to practical artistic experimentation.

A remarkable feature of the Japanese woodcut is its variety of formats – from the square of the surimono to the long, thin strip form – which can all be used at the artist's discretion. However, there is an underlying system that is far from arbitrary. The traditional formats available to the artist had their own exact dimensions. Yet while the outside measurements of the pictorial field could not be determined solely by the artist's wishes, the ways in which the picture was spatially divided up remained extremely flexible. Imaginative diagonal depth-lines alternate with step-like arrangements similar to flats in a theatre, and the results are sometimes the most superb impressions of space in landscape. Placing a grille motif in the foreground or middle distance is another technique of Japanese art, which strove to portray the endless diversity of nature. Nevertheless it is important to realize that here too deinite conventions· however flexible - governed the various methods of artistic composition. For the late ukiyo-e masters, inclusion of a grille across the pictorial space carried special significance. On the one hand it was intended to cut the foreground off from the landscape behind, and on the other to create a spatial unity with often startling visual progressions. Hokusai's landscapes provide à good example of this technique, for example Mount Fuji in a bamboo grove (616). The Japanese colour woodcut changed European principlesof composition directly with this system. It created new methods of depicting distance, new sightlines and a new illusionistic portrayal of the momentary and the direct. Here too it stimulated a trend which culminated in modernism.

A similarly positioned cut-off is provided by Hiroshige in his woodcut Cartwheel on the sea-shore (66r). In the years 1877 to 188o this woodcut caused great interest among Parisian painters and was probably on show in Paris at the international exhibition of 1878.16 Degas was influenced by this composition in his Race course (66z). Here the situation is captured as though in passing and the figure of the man with the top-hat is so cut off by the right-hand edge of the picture that only a third of him is visible.¹ Almost universal is the truncated head which is developed by the Europeans almost to the point of deformity.18The group of shoulder-length figures, positioned side by side and one behind the other, was a compositional form taken up by Maurice Denis from the Japanese colour woodcut. The translucent colouring of his lithographs, which he combines with compositions influenced by Far Eastern models, was the ideal of the Pont-Aven School, a school which retained its influence for over a quarter of a century. As with many other artists, the stimulus for Maurice Denis's lithographs came from Kitagawa Utamaro.

Soon the truncated portrait was to be carried even further. Paul Gauguin's Portrait of Meyer de Haan goes to the extreme. In an almost violent manner he places the head of his subject in the upper left-hand corner behind a table-top running diagonally to the right. Though looking down onto the table-cloth, one sees the face irrationally from below. The language of the picture, which avoids any realistic concept of space, disturbs the point of view of the observer so much that the truncated object becomes deformed. Although Pierre Bonnard gives us a greater depiction of depth in his picture Meal under the lamp – so that the lamp gets into the picture as a vivid still-life in itself – the main figure is cut off so that the portraits of dog and man form a macabre unity. This deformed character is accentuated by the disturbed proportions elsewhere in the picture, and by the colour harmony.

In Japan the post or pillar has great importance, especially the toko-hashira post which divides the tokonoma (prayer niche) in the house from the chigaidana (storage niche with shelves). This post is connected with the religious tradition. In the tea houses it can, therefore, be a specially chosen tree-trunk which still bears the bark on its outer surface. It has a symbolic meaning as the centre of the primeval hut. It is often strangely gnarled or weathered, still possesses the beginnings of branches or is only roughly hewn. At the spot where the post joins the lintel, a bronze nail-head in the form of a branch is usually placed.

A very narrow picture scroll (kakemono) is often hung on this post. This narrow strip, or pillar picture, emphasizes the post and marks it out.

Posts were important in the tea house or in temple buildings, but they were also used in private houses, where stake-like posts support the roof. They rest on the earth on roughly-hewn stone blocks (dodaishi) and, as load-bearing architectural elements, divide up the whole structure.² The post, therefore, was the direct vertical focus for its immediate surroundings (989–1010).

The Japanese woodcut is a representation of everyday life, and therefore the post takes on a strong significance within this art form. In the early horizontal picture scrolls, one can already see the post as a compositional element. Whole series of posts are often represented from high up in order to animate the flow of the picture – the track of the eye – and yet spatially to divide up each pictorial unit as it is unrolled. The colour woodcuts, especially those of the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, develop the post as a pictorial element in itself, that is, the post is accented, thereby attaining an important thematic significance.³

Among the early woodcut artists, Hishikawa Moronobu especially emphasized the post in the composition of his illustrations for an ukiyo-e-zōshi, as did Isoda Koryūsai.

The Japanese painters – like the Chinese — saw the tree as something to be revered, a fundamental element of nature. The highly developed nature symbolism of the Japanese still saw the tree in the post, as the ritual treatment of the toko-hashira shows. When, therefore, the living tree is depicted in a natural environment it can exhibit a post-like character. In Hiroshige's woodcut The Izumo Shrine in the mist (679) the pine trees are a symbol of long life. The Japanese cedars have been placed in the foreground like large posts. Their trunks stand out darkly against the wraith-like mist. Typical of the religious significance of these tree/posts is the introduction of a torii, or entrance gate to the temple, at the right-hand edge of the picture. The post of this sacred gate can hardly be distinguished from the tree-trunks.

The trunk of an old pine can also be accentuated by placing it close to the eye in the foreground, almost on the central axis of the picture. This representation of posts or tree-trunks for their own sakes (668, 67o, 683) which, in the woodcut and in the picture scrolls, often went much further, particularly influenced the French Impressionists. Monet saw trunks of trees in a similar way to the Japanese painters (613); in the great Waterlilies (68o), it is clear what importance he gives to the tree as a cut-off load-bearing pillar'. In a wide circle the strongly overlapping tree-trunks stand like posts in front of the panorama-like wall so that a curiously asymmetrical vista is opened to the observer. In its logical, new, optical conception of space the work evokes the vista seen from the opened sliding door of the Japanese house.ª Because of the large size of the whole area the excerpt becomes monumental and the context no longer to be guessed at, especially since the closeness to each other of the few cut-off tree-trunks dominates the picture. The tree/posts, because of their foreshortening, give the impression of being large-scale fragments. What is fleeting and accidental, therefore, becomes paradoxically a monumental experience.

Van Gogh too, who saw the tree as a living being and said that one must allow trees to grimace, shows in his picture The sower (669) a gnarled trunk which divides the picture in the foreground. The trunk roughly corresponds to the ritual toko-hashira post in Japanese art, and is connected with the content of the picture, for the sower in front of the huge setting sun is the expression of the situation the artist has arrived at in his own life; the old windswept tree adds an expressive statement, for the primeval tree against the setting sun is a symbol of life coming to an end.³

Štylized tree-trunks are very important for the Pont-Aven School. We find them in the works of Paul Sérusier, Armand Séguin, Henry Moret, Georges Lacombe and Emile Bernard. In European Symbolism and Art Nouveau the tree-trunk is often stylized in a bizarre manner , as for example in the works of Bradley, in late Maurice Denis, in Félix Vallotton, Willy von Beckerath, as well as Carl Thiemann and others (170, 172, 605, 607, 609, 681).

In general one can state that the Japanese painter, when he looks out from his house, includes the post as a significant component in his picture. This vertical caesura then becomes an ever more highly developed element in the composition. Extremely diverse themes were developed in a highly varied manner in which the post could rise up out of a formal group of women as a central vertical line, as we see in a woodcut by Kiyonaga (674). This single support fulfils the task of dominating the picture.

The post also had the function of dividing up the picture or of overlapping with various objects (672), when deep and flat composition had to be combined, as the Kiyonaga print shows (674).6 The European painters knew this problem too, and above all it was Degas who studied the overlapping of flat foreground and spatial depth (678). The task Degas set himself is the choice of an unprecedented, or at best extremely unusual, pictorial excerpt. By dividing the picture by means of posts he usually achieves in the one composition several such fragmentary vistas.

The post is very suited to this, for itș form can be continued mentally by the observer. Degas does not want to depict a comprehensive whole as the Far Eastern painters do; he is rather concerned with introducing indications of direction, or sight-lines, into the observer's imagination, and so doing away with the constraint of the frame imposed by European Renaissance illusionism. Degas, therefore, begins to unify the exaggeratedly cut-off motif by using posts as structural devices (678). In his picture The café-concert of 1885 (675) he tries to give a limited vista which is blocked off from below by a balustrade and in which two vertical posts at the right-hand edge of the picture radically divide it. The figures are depicted as though in passing'. The version in the Havemeyer Collection, New York, of 1875/77 (The song of the dog, 677) shows the further development of this, for the pillar is brought into conjunction with the singer, and the vertical caesura runs down over the light-coloured post to the brightly-lit figure. Women in front of a café, of 1877 (676), cuts the left foreground by means of vertical posts in the Japanese manner.

In The café-concert (673), Degas depicts the singer in a red dress, with a wide pillar behind her which, together with the figure itself, divides the right-hand portion of the picture. In the left-hand portion a similar process is repeated, and the foreground is filled with the heads of the audience and the orchestra; a picture which exhibits the most diverse Japanese influences. However, the most effective feature is probably the vertical diversion by means of pillars which here, also, extend the figures.

Degas draws the most extreme consequences from this structural principle. His pictures of dancers (678) are also linked to this type of structure. By means of posts the painter cuts off groups of figures seen daringly close to the eye. By this means he often arrests the flowing movement in the picture. The vertical division by means of posts also leads to asymmetry in the Far Eastern sense.

The post as a vertical caesura was introduced into Western painting through the Japanese model, and painters were also receptive to other similar motifs, above all the ship's mast or the flag-pole, which as vertical divisions provide equally surprising compositional effects - as one can see in the racing picture Jockey before the race by Degas (684) or The harbour at Honfleur (685) by Vuillard. Among the Nabis, and especially in Vuillard's work, the scaffolding-like framework gains in importance, and the human figure is no longer the most important thematic element. The ship's mast helps to divide up the spatial depths of the landscape, which are no longer calculable since the view from above has given way to a quicker movement of the eye. What is fragmentary, although depicted in isolation, becomes haphazard, the momentary is conceived in a new way as standing between stasis, hesitation and vanishing: contrasting ideas which will have a decisive effect on twentieth century painting.

The Finnish painter Akseli Gallén-Kallela, in his Winter landscape with five crosses of 19o2 (686) combines Symbolist characteristics with Japanese influence, a concept which the Symbolist painters in Belgium also took up. Rich possibilities opened up for the new method and the innovatory way of seeing, which were further developed by European Expressionist painting.

The cloisonniste style makes use of that technique which, as cloisonné enamel work, was already brought to a considerable flowering in the Middle Ages; In this process, strips of wire are soldered onto a base, to create areas into which the coloured enamel is placed, and then fired. The result is coloured surfaces with black borders. The visual similarity to the leaded stained-glass windows of the Middle Ages is obvious.1³ The printing techniques of graphic art, which also favour outlines, tend in certain areas, for example in the Japanese colour woodcut, to use internal contours which correspond optically to cloisonnisme, since in the woodcut process bridges are also used to separate colour areas.

The silhouette technique using solid areas of colour was employed by the early woodcut designers in many ways. It was constantly refined until the late ukiyo-e, when the solid colour could also appear without a border, and in this way formed an even stronger coloured silhouette than when it was delineated by means of bridges. Bernard extended the cloisonniste technique, within the coloured silhouette style, by making it into a theoretical ideal.¹ He demanded autonomous colours which should approximate to local colour: solid colour decoration as an uninterrupted aesthetic effect, in contradistinction to the Impressionistic differentiation of colour nuances (695).

Toulouse-Lautrec, on the other hand, is concerned first of all with the precise recording of the outline which also leads on to the solid shape (471, 698). The characteristic moment in the fleeting movement is depicted by a vibrant or oscillating outline.2º In his lithographs Toulouse-Lautrec develops that cogent, condensed silhouette style which carries Bernard's cloisonnisme to its conclusion. The aim was to introduce the sharply cut solid in a modulated and clarifying manner: ‘suggesting instead of stating'(also termed ombres chinoises). The silhouette style develops in two ways. The shadow is removed from the colour and is set free as a homogeneous black; the colour can therefore exist autonomously - as a solid coloured silhouette (129).

This style, which appears in European art even within the depiction of landscape at the end of the nineteenth (7oz) and the beginning of the twentieth century, undergoes various changes outside France. Because of its lack of shadow it is tied more strongly to the solid area and thus paves the way for a process of abstraction which leads by means of schematic coloured areas to a pattern of solids, which then become diagrammatic and ornamental. Here, too, composite forms again come into being, linking the linear effect of silhouette and solid black area with other, coloured components.²1

Solid black is used in the most diverse variations by Aubrey Beardsley (689, 706, 709), who intensifies this silhouette style into a hybrid, memorable and unique form which in its extreme subtlety tends towards that decadence which can already be seen in the late Japanese ukiyo-e masters. Long curving lines, dramatically silhouetted black expanses in front of a light background, through patterning and free-standing individual ornaments constitute, as the points of emphasis of the composition, the individual character of this art. In this way Beardsley achieves a unique stylizątion and symbolism in his figures. This capricious form of the autonomous silhouette (433, 704, 707, 71x) is then developed creatively by the Vienna Secession artists, who use the coloured solid silhouette even more.²²

Vuillard, by using white as a contrasting element in his lithographs,makes the silhouette look as if it is suspended in the air. But this white, floating brightness remains secondary compared to the black. Thus Vuillard makes the dark silhouette into a primary compositional element which can be seen clearly in his Two sisters-inlaw (69z). The painter thus manages to represent that inaccessible space which is peculiarto the Nabis' pictures and which often allows red to be added as an intensifying feature. The black figure here has become a definite arabesque. The crouching woman is as though caught in mid-motion; she and her position, as well as the internal drawing, are blurred. The eye is led in leaps over the arm of the chair and the white dress to the black arabesque and back again. The yellow wallpaper in the background is only a foil for the suspended, reposing, black silhouette. The angle from which it is seen is slightly raised and corresponds to the bending woman who determines the flow of the movement. The bent posé of the figure in the silhouette was often carried by Vuillard almost to the point of deformity. It is noticeable that the Nabis in general do not choose a sharply contoured silhouette but a more broken contour with transitions to other coloured silhouettes. The contrasting aims of these opposites are in accordance with Bernard's original theory; in his cloisonnisme he developed coloured silhouettes on the Japanese model.²³

In the further development of the idea, Art Nouveau takes the Japanese black silhouette as its model. Extreme examples can be found in landscapes above all. Concentrating completely on the outline of the object, William Bradley creates moving shadow forms which are open in all directions and transcend the Japanese model (7or). There are certainly similar compositions in Japan, but these are always confined by the frame. In 1893 Charles Guilloux completed an ornamental landscape for L’Estampe originale (7oz) in which the trees take on silhouette-like forms. The light penetrates from the background, atmosphere is created by means of a spray technique which is unusually combined with the black silhouette. By making the landscape abstract, though still entirely comprehensible in a realistic manner, the way is paved for abstractionism.²⁴

Henri Jossot too (7o3) and the Viennese Josef M. Auchentaller (7o4) develop this even further by means of the perfect silhouette style.²⁵ The style in Europe had reached its highpoint. The black silhouettes create pictorial abstractions which have nothing to do with the European cut paperwork of the early nineteenth century. By means of its enormous enlargement, Franz Wacik's poster for the Silhouette Ball of the Vienna Secession on 22 February 1912 (707) created a mysterious effect which corresponded to the mood of the period and made the ball in the Secession building in Vienna into a silhouette garden', as the critic Ludwig Hevesi remarked.²⁶

The silhouette, after the Japanese model, as the theme of a carnival party was a fantastic idea which made the artistic event of Japonisme appear alive and real. This party, as Hevesi remarks, was comparable to the Chinese shadow plays which in the Far East entertained the popular masses for centuries . . . The huge silhouettes flitted ghost-like over the walls ..." In the condensed, large, black solid form the human figure is given an abstract expression which was to influence modern painting and illustration in Europe most strongly. The confining of the sharp, dark contour line in an abstract structure of solids was to be a task for the Expressionist painters of the beginning of the twentieth century.
