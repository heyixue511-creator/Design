To create an impression of personal friendliness and the absence of oppressive management, landscaped offices used furnishings that suggested domestic interiors (albeit those of the British and American commercial élite) and relied on the image of home as the place in which people are authentically themselves and as the source of all sincere personal relationships.\* Domesticity in the office was not a discovery of the 1950s. Half a century earlier, the offices of some directors and managers had been furnished as domestic interiors, usually in a traditional style, Georgian or Louis XV; what was new was the provision of a high quality environment for everyone below the managing director. The tastefully furnished director's office in the early twentieth century said, 'this is a place in which personal relationships and gentlemanly behaviour matter more than purely commercial ones,' with the implication, 'I am a man of culture and good taste with whom you may be proud to do business.'
