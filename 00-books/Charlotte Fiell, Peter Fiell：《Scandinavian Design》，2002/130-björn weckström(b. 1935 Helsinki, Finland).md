![](images/1287fd51666c5fa9025490c06b2bbe4ac74a46d5ae707bad33101ed3bd6927b9.jpg) Björn Weckström, Planetaariset Laaksot (Planetoid Valleys) necklace for Lapponia, 1969 # björn weckström (b. 1935 Helsinki, Finland) ![](images/3d80ab1194c434d0e8a9b699034c0049bc8e6f7af110e614243749a80fbeab93.jpg) As a child Björn Weckström dreamt of becoming a sculptor, but his family insisted he pursue a more practical career and so he studied at the Kultaseppäkoulu (Goldsmith's School) in Helsinki, graduating in 1956. Following this he studied in Europe, America, Mexico and the Far East prior to working as a freelance designer from 1956 to 1963. He established his own workshop and gallery in Helsinki in 1958 and a year later received second and third prize in a cutlery design competition arranged by Hackman and Sorsakoski. In 1961 he received a challenging commission from a client who asked him to design and execute a piece of jewellery using raw Lemmenjoki gold nuggets. The resulting piece emphasized the “true, primordial nature” of the material and came to be seen as the first example of so-called “jewellery of Lapland” – highly sculptural jewellery inspired by the raw and rugged landscape of Weckström's native land. The same year his Fairy Castle ring made of gold and smoky quartz was purchased by the Victoria & Albert Museum in London, and in 1962he was awarded second prize in a jewellery design competition held by the Finnish Goldsmith's Association. The following year he was appointed principal designer for Lapponia Jewelry, which had been established in 1960 as a manufacturer of art jewellery. Like his fellow countryman Tapio Wirkkala, Weckström was captivated by the romanticism of Lapland and took annual trips there so as to capture the “northern mystique and its beauty” in his work. Using the ancient “cire perdu” method of casting from wax, and inspired by the sculptures of Henry Moore (1898–1986), Weckström has produced new organic forms that celebrate the inherent characteristics of gold ("a hot material” that has a dramatic and charged character) and silver ("a cool material" that is soft and requires tranquil design). Freeing jewellery from its traditional forms, Weckström's pieces, such as the Flowering Wall pendant (1965)and the Planetoid Valleys necklace (1969), which was worn by Princess Leila in Star Wars, not only function as “sculpture in miniature”, but also produce a sense of “complete landscape”. In 1968, Weckström was awarded the Lunning Prize and shortly thereafter made a trip to Uganda, where he found much new inspiration. During the late 1960she began incorporating acrylic elements into his jewellery pieces, and from 1974 also began designing glass and large bronze sculptures and domestic glassware. With his Lappgold and Space Silver collections, Weckström brought a new expressive sensibility to jewellery design. While his informal, modern and pioneering designs are primarily aimed at the luxury market, the manufacturer Lapponia has explained the nature of their exclusivity: “Aesthetics which are not based on the principle of fulfilling social needs remain open, forever seeking new possibilities. Theyare the free aesthetics ofan intermediate zone. A process."
