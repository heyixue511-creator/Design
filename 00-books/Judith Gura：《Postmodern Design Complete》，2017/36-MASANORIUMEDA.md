# MASANORI UMEDA b.1941 JAPANESE DESIGNER WHOSE INVENTIVE FURNITURE HAS PERSONALITY AND BROAD APPEAL. Like many of his colleagues in Postmodernism, this Japanese-born designer transitioned easily from commercially produced objects to esoteric, avant-garde designs. He was responsible for what is probably the most photographed—though certainly the least marketable—object in the panoply of MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") collections, the Tawaraya, an overscaled seating unit in the form of a boxing ring. Famously, in the year of the group's founding the entire Memphis crew was photographed in one; that image of a group of revolutionaries reclining on a completely impractical piece of furniture has been reproduced countless times in publications all over the world. Apart from the Tawaraya, Umeda's designs were only moderately functional, though consistently imaginative and enlivened with straight-faced humor. Ginza (i982) is a bookshelf shaped like a robot, an appealingly decorative form perhaps best used for its ornamental value. since it holds relatively few books. He also contributed ceramic objects to the Memphis collections, including the Orinoco vase and Parana bowl in ı983. Trained at the Kuwasawa Design School in Tokyo, where he graduated in i962 with a diploma in design, Umeda then went to Italy, which was by then becoming the nucleus of design innovation. He worked from 1967 to 1969 in the office of Achille and Pier Giacomo Castiglioni, brothers who were among the most respected of the postwar generation of Italian modern designers, and from 197o to 1979 as a design consultant for Olivetti, where he met ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), who invited him to participate in Memphis's first collection.3º Tawaraya was the happy result. Umeda left Italy and Memphis in ı986, returning to Japan and opening his firm U-Metadesign. He designed several flower-shaped seating pieces for Edra\* in i99o, which reveal the same ability to infuse objects with personality as do his Memphis designs—but these later works, for a producer requiring saleable goods, are comfortable as well as eye-catching.
