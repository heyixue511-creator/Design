![](images/ec900cfc268c41216f059071f6162477bda371b21a5c224c8873a77443dd6d35.jpg) Knud Holscher, d-line door handle, 1972 ▶Knud Holscher, d-line staircasesystem, 1999 # knud holscher (b. 1930 Rødby, Denmark) ![](images/c3301063d596216c25b5ecd1044503136a471d745d82d8fcc8afad4f62e506b7.jpg) The internationally renowned industrial designer Knud Holscher studied architecture at the Kongelige Danske Kunstakademi (Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts), Copenhagen, between 1949 and 1957.From 1959 to 1964 he worked for Arne Jacobsen, initially as a draftsman and then later as an associate participating in one of Jacobsen's most important building projects, St Catherine's College, Oxford. In 1964, Holscher established a design practice in Copenhagen in partnership with the British product designer and architect Alan Tye (b. 1933). Together, they co-designed the Modric range of sanitary fittings, which was “the first comprehensive range of ironmongery manufactured in Britain to an integrated design system”. The Modric range subsequently received a British Design Award and the British Aluminium Industry's Prize in 1966. The following year, Holscher became a partner in the Krohn & Hartvig Rasmussen architecture practice. In 1970, he won another British Design Award for an office signage system and two years later, designed his highly successful d-line range of stainless steel door furniture, which has won numerous awards and has grown to become the world's most extensive range of architectural ironmongery and bathroom fittings. The d-line range with its soft-edged functionalism is a powerfui manifestation of Holscher's “striving for a humanist interpretation of the rational and the presumptive”. In 1975, Holscher designed stainless steel salad servers and tongs for Georg Jensen within a similar vocabulary of form. Today, Knud Holscher Industrial Design is one of the leading Scandinavian design consultancies and numbers among its clients JCDecaux, Erco, Philips, Fritz Hansen, and Facit. The practice has also won numerous competitions for street furniture and signage systems. Holscher's classically Scandinavian humanist approach to industrial design synthesizes the “rational with the irrational” so as to “provide an answer to problems that are so complex that their formulation is found only in, and with, their solution”. Holscher has also designed numerous public and private buildings, including the Royal Danish Theatre in Copenhagen(1978), Næstved Town Hall (1981), and Copenhagen Airport (1983), and withothers has published a book entitled Culture in a Crisis Time (1980). ![](images/4f80d41b859b8ce74f9077d3cb343d254f818ddce5a2b5aa39d67662028474e3.jpg)
