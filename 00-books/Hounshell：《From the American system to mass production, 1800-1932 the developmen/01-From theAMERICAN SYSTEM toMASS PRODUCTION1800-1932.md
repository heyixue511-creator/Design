# From the AMERICAN SYSTEM to MASS PRODUCTION 1800-1932

# The Development of Manufacturing Technology in the United States

![](images/04f5605d30093705e310b3e2e9bf41b48f55215e85a7c149883aace0dfcd28f0.jpg)

DAVID A. HOUNSHELL

Americans, wrote Pulitzer Prizewinning historian Daniel J. Boorstin, possess “a genius for mass production. In his richly illustrated From the American System to Mass Production, 1800-1932 David Hounshell explores the development of the “American system" of manufacturing in the half century before the Civil War and the transition of this system into the “mass production" of the twentieth century.

Previous writers on the American system have implied or argued directly that the technical problems of mass production had been solved by antebellum armsmakers. Hounshell argues, however, drawing upon the extensive business and manufacturing records of leading American firms— including Singer Manufacturing Company, McCormick Harvesting Machinery Company, Brown & Sharpe Manufacturing Company, Pope Manufacturing Company, and Ford Motor Company—that the diffusion of arms production technology was neither as fast nor as smooth as has been assumed. Both the expression mass production and the technology that lay behind it were developments of the twentieth century, attributable in large part to the Ford Motor Company.

From the American System to Mass Production explores the important role of individuals in the diffusion and development of production technology and the central place of marketing strategy in the success of selected American manufacturers.
