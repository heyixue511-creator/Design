# MICHAEL VANDERBYL b.1947 SAN FRANCISCO DESIGNER WHOSE WORK CROSSES DISCIPLINES. FROM PURE MODERNISM TO TRANSGRESSIVE AVANT-GARDE. Though some of his work seems to follow the tenets of classic Modernism, Michael Vanderbyl's career has seen him move effortlessly from its rigorous principles to less restrictive avant-garde design. He refers to himself as an interdisciplinary designer, explaining, "I like to do it all."28 A native Californian, Vanderbyl graduated from the California College of Arts & Crafts (now California College of the Arts) in i968, and opened his own office in San Francisco in 1973 San Francisco had given birth to a youth culture that spawned underground graphics in comic books and psychedelic art, and a new generation of graphic designers who would bring it into the mainstream. Vanderbyl was one of those who made the Bay Area a center of innovative graphi design in the postmodern years In moving from graphics and print into broader territory, he enhanced the public profile of his profession, helping to put it on a level with other disciplines more customarily classified as art. He also helped to expand graphic design beyond its New York-centered base, as California New Wave concepts migrated east, introducing color and humor to serious, Swiss-based practices Although he was not the only designer to move in this direction, Vanderbyl was distinctive in his use of three-dimensional effects, something that may have evolved from his early interest in architecture.²9 He used the technique in work for Esprit\* and later in brochures and posters for Simpson Paper that featured objects by SWID POWELL (see essay under “Edited Design") and MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") and postmodern iconography, with a depth of imagery rarely seen in two-dimensional work. Vanderbyl went on to design furniture, showrooms, decorative accessories, and fabrics, all with a consistent sense of style that draws on the principles of graphic design. He often set bold images against fields of strong color, using type for titles and letting the illustrations tell the story. His work is more classic than transgressive; always legible, crisp, and with a sense of humor that is subtly clever without being raucous. This ability to bring an air of establishment substance to avant-garde style brought Vanderbyl commissions from major corporations to develop brand identity programs. Vanderbyl was Dean of Design at his alma mater for more than thirty years, encouraging students to follow his example by thinking of graphic design as extending far beyond the printed page. He has also taught at Cranbrook Academy of Art, ArtCenter College of Design, the School of the Art Institute of Chicago, and other prestigious colleges, and lectures frequently on design and branding. In addition to winning awards, his work has been acquired by several important museums. Probably his most out-of-the-box project was created in 2ooı, when he designed the graphics for an America's Cup yacht—a most rewarding project for a longtime sailing enthusiast.
