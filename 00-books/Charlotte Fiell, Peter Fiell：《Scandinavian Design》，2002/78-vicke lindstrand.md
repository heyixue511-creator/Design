![](images/e05774256373595c4401d53f27c3070903443683c5986581dbb704479dfc6be9.jpg) Vicke Lindstrand, Fishing Net vase for Kosta, 1951 # vicke lindstrand ## (b. 1904 Gothenburg – d. 1983 Småland, Sweden) The renowned Swedish glass designer Victor Emmanuel (Vicke) Lindstrand studied graphic design at the Konstindustriskolan (School of Arts &Crafts) in Gothenburg from 1924 to 1927. From 1922 to 1928, he also contributed cartoons and illustrations to two local newspapers and briefly studied in France and Italy. In 1928, he began working for the Orrefors glassworks designing glassware that was classical in form and often engraved with stylized figurative motifs in the Art Deco style. Objects such as these together with other painted glass pieces by Lindstrand were exhibited at the 1930 Stockholm Exhibition andreceived much critical acclaim. Several of his designs were adorned with muscular female nudes, which reflected the continuing and pervasive influence of Cubism. He later designed a window for the Swedish pavilion at the 1937 Paris “Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne” and a glass fountain for the 1939 New York World's Fair. Around this time, he began developing a softer and more fluid style, with his engraved decoration becoming more rhythmic. During this period his modelling of figurative motifs also became increasingly gentle and more naturalistic. Although he continued producingdesigns for Orrefors until 1941, Lindstrand also worked for the Karlskrona Porslinsfabrik from 1935 to 1936 and Upsala-Ekeby from 1936 to 1950, where he was art director from 1943 to1950. Between 1950and 1970, Lindstrand ran his own studio in Århus and was also the design director of Kosta Boda. During the 1950s, the glassware he designed for this company had a strong and identifiable character and predicted the bold forms that became so fashionable in Swedish glass the following decade. While at Kosta, he produced some of his most sculptural work, much of which was influenced by the work of contemporary Venetian designers such as Fulvio Bianconi. Unlike the Italians, however, Lindstrand's designs were distinguished by less expressive organic forms and by a more restrained use of colour. During the early 1960s he experimented with engraving polished cullets of waste glass, the results of which possessed a raw and icy character that suited his motifs of Laplanders and reindeers.
