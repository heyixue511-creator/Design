# FOUR MORRISAS BOOKDESIGNER ![](images/68a9a59fd0eceb733f9398cfd956b51fbc4531ed4a818575c134e863f9c66b98.jpg) VERYONE knows (or thinks he knows) what a Kelmscott Press book looks like: it is a massive folio printed in dense black type with large, overpowering wood-engraved initials, borders, and illustrations. Such at least is the popular notion of a typical Kelmscott product, a stereotype reinforced by the innumerable photographs of the most heavily ornamented pages of the Chaucer that seem inevitably to appear in every history of printing and book-design. In reality, though Morris did, like most designers, develop a general typographical formula, he applied it to individual volumes with great subtlety and skill, so that there is no such thing as a single completely representative Kelmscott Press book. Notwithstanding their apparent uniformity of manner, one can find evidence of Morris's growing sophistication and self-assurance as a book-designer as the Kelmscott books gradually came off the press. The ornamentation slowly became richer and the pictures better integrated with the words. Morris showed more daring in his use of red ink: at first employed abstemiously, it eventually lent colour to colophons, notes, and his printer's mark. In Psalmi penitentiales (1894) the Latin text of each verse in red was followed by the English text in black; in Sidonia the Sorceress (1893) the red notes flowed all round the edges of the black text. Even those elements we think of as Morris's typographical signature — the shoulder-note, the engraved title-page, the all-caps, unleaded title (frequently unjustified) — evolved from a surprisingly prolonged process of trial and error. Because the Chaucer is the most famous and widely discussed work of the Kelmscott Press, many readers have assumed that it is also typical. The Chaucer's design represents a solution (a triumphantly successful solution, some would argue) to a particular set of problems of typography and illustration; but had Morris lived to complete the Froissart, another double-column folio of similar proportions, we know that it would have been fundamentally different because of a new style of borders and initials that make their first appearance in the Froissart trial pages. And of course both the Chaucer and the unfinished Froissart are, in their amplitude, unrepresentative Kelmscott books: most of the works issued by Morris's Press were octavos, and the majority of them were unillustrated. (Even the Chaucer contains many pages, seldom reproduced in photographs, with no wood-engraved illustrations and only a few small initials.) Morris undoubtedly had a great fondness for folios — he argued, rather unconvincingly, that they are easier to read because folios lie submissively and quietly on a table — yet at the other extreme he also produced four of the Kelmscott titles in the format known as 16mo (with a page size of $5 _ { 4 } ^ { 3 } \ \times \ 4 _ { 8 } ^ { 1 }$ inches). These are wonderfully delicate little books which belie the stereotype of ponderous, overweight folios flowing in rapid succession from the Kelmscott Press. Another way of circumventing the usual misconceptions about the Kelmscott typographical style is to recognize that Morris's principles could be — indeed often were — applied to commercially produced books as well as limited private-press editions. Morris contended that any book, even the most cheaply manufactured shilling reprint, could be improved by the adoption of a few simple rules: the use of old-face types, the reduction of white space between words and lines, and the careful adjustment of margins. As for ornaments and illustrations, it was better to eschew then entirely than to adopt mass-produced or unsuitable ones. Despite his ill-concealed misgivings about the printing press as an instrument for producing universal literacy, Morris was realistic enough to recognize that newspapers, magazines, and the majority of books had to be manufactured as economically as possible. Only the occasional volume could fit his prescription for the Ideal Book': ‘a book not limited by commercial exigencies of price." We can, therefore, see Morris the book-designer in another light when, from time to time, we find him wrestling with the problems of commercial printing in the 189os. At the very moment when he was about to found the Kelmscott Press, for instance, Morris was also carrying on negotiations with Bernard Quaritch about the Saga Library' series that Quaritch was to publish under Morris's editorship. In an undated memorandum to Quaritch (probably written in 189o) Morris emphasized that I should wish to have some controul [sic] over the printing and general “get-up"of the books. . . . It would, if I may say so, be of some use to the sale of them as my public expects from me a certain style.'Specifically hc suggested that the books should be printed in old-style type', preferably either Caslon or Miller and Richard's Old Style. ‘If Caslons were used the size ought to be pica, as his letterș have long ascending and descending strokes and consequently look rather smaller for their size. The position of the p[age] of type on the leaf ought to follow ancient precedent, and not be plastered right in the middle as modern printers treat it.' Morris proposed supplying wood-engraved initials if necessary, but all ‘rules" and little twiddledy printers ornaments I should do without'.² (Morris's admiration for Caslon's type was not unqualified,3 but, like Walker, he endorsed it because it was the only typeface then widely available that even approximated his ideal.) There are other letters from Morris — for example, one written to the publisher Swan Sonnenschein in 1893 about the design of the pamphlet Socialism: Its Growth and Outcome⁴ — in which he offers remarkably sensible advice about printing books under ordinary trade conditions. In the same vein, when approached in 1894 about producing a syllabus at the Kelmscott Press, Morris dictated to Cockerell the following reply: Mr. Morris suggests that you should have the syllabus set up in the plainest possible manner, without rules or ornaments, (& if possible in Caslon's Old faced type) & it will then in his opinion be certain to look well.' As Morris said on another occasion, ‘whatever the subject-matter of the book may be, and however bare it may be of decoration, it can still be a work of art, if the type be good and attention be paid to its general arrangement." Books have a tendency to become beautiful objects unless we deliberately and perversely make them ugly. I lay it down . . . that a book quite un-ornamented can look actually and positively beautiful, and not merely un-ugly, if it be, so to say, architecturally good,'he declared.7 Yet however much Morris might praise the plain, unadorned book, he in fact produced at the Kelmscott Press volumes that were anything but simple and functional; and this discrepancy between his theory and practice has led to interminable disagreements about the nature of his contribution to modern book-design. Some critics, fixing their gaze on the Kelmscott books, have seen him as the advocate of a regressive, pseudo-medieval style of bookmaking that is irrelevant to contemporary needs; others, emphasizing his writings on design and typography, have identified Morris as the unacknowledged source of the great revival of English commercial printing that took place in the 192os. Was Morris, then, an escapist who misled several generations of private-press printers into believing that they could turn the hands of the clock back, or was he rather, in Nikolaus Pevsner's famous phrase, one of the pioneers of modern design'?8 There is no doubt, as Morris never tired of arguing, that choosing an attractive typeface and arranging the block of text carefully on the page add little to the cost of a book. What made the Kelmscott books so expensive to produce, however, was Morris's insistence upon handicraft methods, lavish ornamentation, and high-quality materials. It is not surprising if we are sometimes baffled by the nature of his Ideal Book', for Morris himself harbours anbiguous aims: even as he enunciates eminently sane proposals for reforming the commercially manufactured Victorian book, we sense that he is secretly hankering after the illuminated French manuscript of the thirteenth century. In the presence of such myriad-mindedness, we need not be embarrassed by our own confusion when trying to interpret the meaning of the Kelmscott Press and the ideas that lay behind it. In examining the techniques that Morris as a printer actually adopted, it is important to bear in mind that his primary intention, when he established the Press, was to print a few copies of books for his own pleasure and that of his friends; under these circumstances, he had little choice but to make use of the hand-press, which was ideal even for the somewhat larger pressruns (on an average, between 300 and 5oo copies) he eventually settled on for the Kelmscott books. The slow pace of Morris's Albion presses made it possible for his printers to inspect each sheet individually for imperfections, and, by pausing a split second when the forme came into contact with the paper, the pressman could ensure that Morris's heavy ornaments printed solidly and blackly. Indeed, all those wood-engraved initials and borders, so characteristic of the Kelmscott volumes, would have been out of the question had Morris selected a power-driven press.º He was of course well aware of this issue: in 189o, writing to Quaritch about the Saga Library, he urged that the large-paper copies should be printed with the hand-press, so as to get the impression black enough'.1º The adoption of the hand-press was also closely connected with Morris's decision to print on dampened handmade paper, which would have been impossible on a cylinder press. At present there is very little good paper made, and most of it is very bad,’Morris declared in a lecture in 1893. Our ideal book must, I think, be printed on hand-made paper as good as it can be made. . . .'" Joseph Batchelor's attractive (and durable) paper was a primary ingredient in the success of the Kelmscott Press books; one consequence of this is that any photographic reproduction of a Kelmscott page invariably fails to convey the superb tactile quality of the original. Towards the end of the nineteenth century it became fashionable to produce éditions de luxe and facsimile reprints in pseudo-handmade papers, nearly always with ragged, untrimmed edges. The earliest Kelmscott books reflect this ‘deckle-fetishism'(as John Carter has called it), but it is significant that Morris assumed his books would be eventually rebound and the deckle trimmed: in a letter written in November 1892 which probably refers to The Golden Legend, he explained, ‘The edges could be easily trimmed when the book is bound, so as to get more uniformity in the size. Of course the book is issued “done up", and not bound: and under the circumstances it would have been a mistake I think to have trimmed the edges."² But Morris very soon changed his mind, perhaps because he realized that most Kelmscott books were not after all being rebound by their owners, and Biblia innocentium, which was issued on 9 December 1892, was the last volume to come from the Kelmscott Press with untrimmed edges. A few collectors of more conventional tastes found this a distressing decision. As to the cut edges of the K.P. books W.M. followed the custom of the early printers, & had them trimmed,' Cockerell wrote to Harry Buxton Forman. I am sorry that you think this such a blemish.'3 Even Forman, however, may have found comfort in the fact that the Kelmscott volumes were trimmed only very slightly. Several of Morris's pre-Kelmscott books had also been issued in large-paper editions, but by the time he set up the Press he realized that this practice ruined the proportions of the margins and introduced too much white space on to the page. 'I am clean against [large-paper editions],'Morris announced in 1893, though I have sinned a good deal in that way myself, but that was in the days of ignorance, and I petition for pardon on that ground only."4 For the sake of collectors, he printed a handful of copies of most of the Kelmscott books on vellum, though Morris and Walker agreed that they personally preferred paper copies. Finding good vellum — which is made from the skin of young calves — was not easy. Morris in 1891 still owned a small supply of Roman vellum which he had imported for his calligraphic experiments two decades earlier, and this he used to print six copies of The Story of the Glittering Plain, the first Kelmscott book; unfortunately he could not obtain more from the same source, because the Vatican bought up all the best Italian vellum. Sparling offers the amusing information that Morris briefly entertained the thought of appealing directly to the Pope for some vellum on the ground that the Golden Legend was a book in which he ought to be interested'; ' such a violation of Morris's agnostic scruples proved to be unnecessary, however, when he found an English manufacturer of vellum, Henry Band (of Brentford, Middlesex), who was capable of meeting his exacting standards. In contrast with paper or vellum, ink attracts little attention from readers; even Morris had nothing to say about it in his lectures and essays on the book arts. Nevertheless, ink is a surprisingly important element in printing, and the range of choices available is determined to a large extent by the kind of press being used. As the speed ofrotary and cylinder presses increased throughout the nineteenth century, printers were compelled to use progressively thinner inks which produced pages always more greyish-looking. By selecting the Albion hand-press, Morris was able to turn to inks that were much thicker and slower-drying — and presumably closer in consistency and appearance to the inks employed by fifteenth-century printers. Indeed, when an English publisher recently tried to match Morris's ink for a facsimile reproduction of the Kelmscott Chaucer, it was discovered that ‘the original ink has never completely dried - suggesting that Morris himself never found the perfect answer to the problems which beset him in finding an ink to meet his requirements'.'9 The ink difficulties proved in fact to be more intractable than the paper problem (which had been solved as soon as Morris found Joseph Batchelor), and, although it constitutes only a minor episode in the history of the Kelmscott Press, the story of Morris's search for an ideal ink is a small but illuminating example of the smoothed-over version of events given to us by earlier historians of the Press. Cockerell, typically, offers an admirably concise but incomplete account: ‘The black ink which proved most satisfactory, after that of more than one English firm was tried, was obtained from Hanover. Morris often spoke of making his own ink, in order to be certain of its ingredients, but his intention was never carried out.'° Sparling, on the other hand, devotes more than two pages to the matter in his book on the Kelmscott Press, quotes Morris on the ‘damned chemists'who have ruined nineteenth-century inks, supplies an excellent description of the ingredients and character of Morris's ink, and in general treats the episode as still another instance of Morris's rejection of modern technology in favour of the sound, simple materials associated with traditional craftsmanship. The German ink which Morris finally adopted, he admits, was rather thick, and in the average press-room of those days - or in these? — a little soft soap would soon have got into it . . . ; [but] quickness of working was not asked for at the Kelmscott Press'.² (Sparling, in his tribute to the purity of Morris's arts-and-crafts ideals, fails to mention that the German ink was made in an enormous factory with smoke billowing from a multitude of smokestacks.) Contrast with this the recollections of Emery Walker, who first discovered the superiority of the ink manufactured in Hannover by Gebrüder Jänecke, having tried [he wrote] every ink maker in England without success. Many years afterwards when Morris was starting up the Kelmscott Press the question of ink came up and I advised him to use the same ink. It was tremendously stiff and very hard work for the pressmen, and the introduction into the office produced something very near a strike, and Morris[,] anxious to get on having a vast prospect of books before him which he wanted to print, rather weakly gave in and used the best ink he could get in England. This turned out to be unsatisfactory, so Morris read the riot act and said if they wouldn't use the German ink he would close the Press. As his pressmen were very well paid they naturally did not want to lose their jobs and gave way. However, they took it out of Morris another way and the number of sheets turned out per day were extraordinarily small."² Morris's outburst to his pressmen sounds in character (though the threat of plant closure was perhaps not entirely consistent with his socialist principles), and Walker's version of the Kelmscott Press as a tense, accident-prone operation is a useful corrective to Sparling's lyrical praise of the spiritual harmony of its pressroom. In fact, Morris as a printer was guilty of occasional artistic and technical bungling, especially in the early stages of the production of books; Sparling and Cockerell, in their solemn, reverential accounts of the Press, tell us almost nothing about Morris's habitual tendency to muddle through, and to extricate himself in the end from alarming (and sometimes amusing) predicaments. Morris never found a convincing explanation of what had gone wrong with the Shackell, Edwards ink, nor (being incurious about theoretical questions) did he seek one once he had found a pragmatic solution of his problem. There are faint yellow stains in several of the early Kelmscott Press books (notably The Golden Legend), usually behind heavy ornaments, and, as Morris's friend Jacobi pointed out, similar stains occasionally appear in incunabula: perhaps, Jacobi suggested, the phenomenon stemmed from an imperfect fusion of the two chief ingredients of ink: carbon and varnish.2 Whatever the reason, Morris was content to have solved the immediate dilemma, yet the price he paid in adopting the Jänecke ink is evident in a Kelmscott Press announcement of February 1896 which informed customers that, owing to the strength and slow-drying qualities of the ink used, the Chaucer will not be fit for ordinary binding, with the usual pressure, for at least a year from its issue'. After Morris's death, when the production of Sire Degrevaunt (1897) was rushed so that it could be issued at the same time as its companion volume Syr Isambrace, his binder found it necessary to insert a flimsy between the frontispiece and the first text page because the ink was not yet dry; one assunes that this was done with reluctance, since it violated Morris's principle that the two pages of an opening must be seen together. Morris also used red and (less frequently) blue inks at the Kelmscott Press. The red he employed extensively for titles and shoulder-notes, though only rarely for initials; two Kelmscott books, Laudes Beatae Mariae Virginis (1896) and Love is Enough were printed in three colours. For both the red and blue inks he turned again to conmercial sources. The blue (made from ultramarine ash), supplied by Winsor and Newton to Morris's own specifications,26 is beyond criticism. On the other hand, the proofs of the Kelmscott books, as well as the books themselves, reveal that Morris experimented with several reds, and the evidence suggests that he was never fully satisfied with any of them. He objected to red inks that were too pink or orange (the ink used in Blunt's Love-Lyrics (1892), for instance, is distinctly carroty), and Shackell, Edwards's red, which he adopted for a time, showed a tendency to spread on the damp paper. For this reason, the Kelmscott pressmen always worked the red ink after the black when possible. Behind the scenes at the Kelmscott Press there were frequent experiments with inks. Proofs of the engraved title-page of Reynard the Foxe (1893) were struck off in red ink, a practice Morris never adopted in any of the Kelmscott books; more astonishingly, a set of proofs of the illustrations for The Earthly Paradise and Love is Enough was printed in gold ink, thus lending new meaning to the phrase ‘Golden type'.27 Morris bought brown inks (which he did not in the end use); he printed blue inks from various manufacturers on both wet and dry papers in attempting to achieve the effects he sought;28 in 1891 he compared the results from applying ink to the formes with ordinary composition rollers and then with litho rollers;29 for several years he tried many combinations of hand-coloured and printed ornaments, none, evidently, to his satisfaction. This sort of restless curiosity did not extend to the Kelmscott bindings: here Morris at once found an answer — admittedly a compromise — to his requirements and never departed from it except in the Chaucer, some copies of which were issued in a special pigskin binding designed by Morris and executed at the Doves Bindery. Though all the other Kelmscott books were bound by an ordinary (but conscientious) commercial firm, their structure and appearance were much influenced by Morris's friend Thomas J. Cobden-Sanderson, who achieved fame as both a binder and a printer. Cobden-Sanderson had been an unhappy young barrister in 1883, with suppressed artsand-crafts longings, when Jane Morris turned to him after dinner and said, why dont you learn bookbinding. That would add an Art to our community, and we would work together.'º Two days later he apprenticed himself to Roger de Coverley, a leading bookbinder, and, with encouragement from the entire Morris family (including May, who said that his new vocation was much nicer than being a barrister''), Cobden-Sanderson soon set himself up in business. 'I think it would be a very good thing for you to get a bookbinding business together,' Morris wrote to him. I would be very glad to give you one of my Editions to do if we could get it done at a possible price.’³² Because the Doves Press was to be the most direct and distinguished successor to the Kelmscott Press, the names of Cobden-Sanderson and Morris have always been linked in the minds of bibliophiles; but in fact their fundamentally differing personalities fixed a great gulf between them. Cobden-Sanderson was visionary, idealistic, and humourless, and it is apparent that Morris, a craftsman of more energetic and pragmatic temperament, often found his neighbour on Upper Mall a source of irritation. Cobden-Sanderson's sober accounts (in his journal) of his encounters with Morris are filled with unintentional humour: in March 1885 Morris launched into an attack upon Cobden-Sanderson's prices and suggested ‘that “people would go to the cheapest market," and almost as if he approved; thought my work too costly; bookbinding should be “rough": did not want to multiply the minor arts(!); went so far as to suggest that some machinery should be invented to bind books'.3³ (Though of course Morris was deliberately exaggerating in order to annoy him, there is a ring of authenticity to these remarks: the style of bindings he adopted for the Kelmscott books was exceedingly plain, if not rough.) The tension in their relationship never dissipated. Though in the early stages of planning The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye Morris speculated that he might have it bound in morocco,3⁶ he worried that goat and calf skins available through the usual trade sources in nineteenth-century England were inferior and not likely to prove durable. None of the Kelmscott books was therefore issued in a conventional leather binding. Morris instead persuaded J. and J. Leighton, an old and respected London firm,37 to provide for each of his books one of two simple bindings: full vellum or quarter linen (with blue-grey papers on boards), the latter of which he described to Quaritch as a satisfactory temporary binding'.38 The Story of the Glittering Plain was issued in a stiff vellum binding, with washleather ties and the title stamped in the Golden type on the spine. 'I have just received from Morris the “dummy",' Cobden-Sanderson wrote in his journal on 4 April 1891; ‘it is a dummy binding by Leighton, to show how he proposes to bind The Glittering Plain.'9 Both Morris and Cobden-Sanderson were still searching for an appropriate binding for the Kelmscott books at this point, so that the first appearance of the vellum version displays two features that were later abandoned: Morris soon decided that he preferred limp to stiff vellum (though the stiff vellum binding is in some respects more satisfactory, as it does not wrinkle and curl), and in his second book, Poems by the Way, he substituted silk ties for washleather ties. Despite these subsequent modifications, the Kelmscott Press vellum binding from the beginning possessed a highly distinctive appearance and was widely imitated by other private presses and commercial publishers. One reason it caught the attention of readers and collectors immediately is that vellum as a material for binding books had long gone out of fashion. Joseph W. Zaehnsdorf, in the second edition of The Art of Bookbinding (189o), declared, 'This art of binding in vellum seems to be entirely lost at the present day; its imperishable nature is indeed its only recommendation. It has little beauty; is exceedingly harsh; and little variety can be produced even in the finishing' (p. 147). The simplicity and ‘imperishable nature' of vellum were precisely what appealed to Morris (who, curiously enough, was especially fond of the occasionally darker vellum binding with untrimmed hairs and always reserved such Kelnscott books for his own library). Another characteristic feature of Morris's books, one that was not generally imitated or admired, grew directly out of a dictum of Cobden-Sanderson: 'The endpapers . . . should . . . not be pasted on; they also should be sewn with the rest of the book. There was at least one contemporary protest against this dubious practice.4 Another deficiency of the vellum-bound books is that vellum hardens with age, making it now nearly impossible to open fully some of the Kelmscott volumes. The Kelmscott quarter-linen binding, though more unabashedly an expedient, also set a fashion among other publishers, so that for a generation or more after Morris's death English readers were afflicted with a plague of books issued in boards covered with blue or grey paper. The paper used on the Kelmscott books was a laid, handmade stock that varies slightly in thickness and colour from volume to volume; it seems to have been supplied by Leighton,4² though the advertising of the Kelmscott Press never mentions it, presumably because Morris did not envision the quarter-linen binding as providing anything but short-term protection for a book. As in the case of vellum, the Kelmscott quarter-linen bindings were slightly modified and improved in the later books: the binding of The Golden Legend, for example, is unmistakably inferior to its successors. One small but annoying problem whose solution eluded Morris was the proper form for titles on bindings. As a rule, the title was gilt-stamped in one of his three typefaces (sometimes enlarged or reduced) on the spine of the vellum bindings. On the quarter-linen bindings the title was printed in black ink on the front cover if the book was slender; for larger books it was printed on a paper label which was pasted to the linen spine. Surprisingly, Morris did not always use Batchelor paper for the spine labels, perhaps because it was too thick and inflexible. The label on the Chaucer, for example, is printed on the tissue-thin laid paper watermarked ‘Silver Linen'which Morris habitually used for stationery and proofs, and as a result the label has disintegrated badly on nearly every copy of the book. Even on the vellum bindings there were difficulties with titles. For The Defence of Guenevere, and Other Poerms (1 892) Morris arranged for Herbert Ellis, F. S. Ellis's son, to hand-letter the title in Gothic script, and, although he praised it in a letter to the younger Ellis (I think they are very well written & look very well indeed, and for that size and shape of book look decidedly better than the stamped gilded letters'43), it is significant that he did not repeat the experiment. Likewise Morris came to realize in 1894 that titles in Gothic capitals were exceedingly difficult to read, and he thereafter used the Golden type rather than the Troy or the Chaucer type for that purpose.⁴⁴ As usual, even though Morris had settled on two standard forms of binding, he found pleasure in carrying on various curious experiments. A few of the vellum bindings were stained: after calling at Kelmscott House on 5 November 1891, Cockerell wrote that evening in his diary, Several copies of Poens by the Way on the dining room table with covers stained red, yellow blue & green.'These books, destined for Morris and his immediate circle offriends, were not offered for sale. There were no silk ties on the vellum binding of The Poetical Works of Percy Bysshe Shelley (1894-5), provoking an indignant letter from one of Morris's most faithful customers, the Revd Stopford Brooke. The ties were omitted from the Shelley as an experiment, as some people don't care for them,' Cockerell explained to Brooke, but except in the 2 other volumes of this work, they will appear in all future books that are bound in vellum.'4 In the summer of 1894 Tregaskis exhibited in London seventy-six copies of The Tale of King Florus and the Fair Jehane (1893) that had been sent to the world's leading bookbinders. ## Paper, vellum, ink, and bindings were all matters of much concern to Morris, and we have seen that he struggled, sometimes heroically, to find the highest-quality supplies available; Morris's fame as a bookdesigner, however, rests less on his use of fine materials at the Kelmscott Press than on the typographical theories he enunciated in a series of lectures and articles during the 189os.4 Those theories have had an important, albeit indirect, effect on the physical form of the twentieth-century book, and they have provoked much debate among typographers, yet it is essential to recall that Morris was not temperamentally drawn to abstract theorizing. Robert Catterson-Smith, who worked closely with Morris in the preparation of the Kelmscott Press illustrations, offered this analysis of his employer's mind: He hated being asked questions which meant an effort at analysis. He appeared to be unable or unwilling to separate his feelings from anything he thought about. The beauty of the outside of things was enough for his mind, he did not wish to probe the unseen.'47 By instinct Morris distrusted any attempt to codify a set of rules for designing books. Still, he did formulate and lay down his own laws in splendidly dogmatic fashion. It is obvious that his conclusions were based directly on his earlier attempts at bookmaking (including the calligraphic manuscripts), conversations with Emery Walker (and perhaps secondarily Charles Jacobi), and his firsthand experiences at the Kelmscott Press, which was still in the early stages of its development when most of Morris's typographical lectures and essays were written. Had Morris lived beyond 1896, it is likely that some of his principles of book-design would have been modified, or at least that his ex cathedra tone might have softened; for those principles could not always be reconciled with what he actually did at the Kelmscott Press. One wonders, for example, whether he might eventually have realized that no one typographical style is suitable for all books. Morris of course subtly modulated the Kelmscott manner for his individual volumes, but the family likeness is very strong, and even friends most in sympathy with his aims were aware that such a reductionist approach to book-design fails to do justice to the complexity and variety of literary texts. In a characteristic piece of whimsy, Burne-Jones wrote in 1893 that ‘Morris vows he will print Dickens (if he lives) at the Kelmscott Press. Oh, ifI could see the words “Mrs Gamp" in floriated initials — perhaps in red — thick — [here he draws a witty parody of her name as rendered in Morris initials] . . . for to be printed so finely is equivalent to beatification from the Vatican. . . .'48 When a journalist put this very question to Morris — whether Dickens might not appear odd in Kelmscott dress — he replied tartly, I fail to see the incongruity. That arises simply from preconceived notions of the right thing, established by vicious custom.'49 He then launched into a slightly irrelevant attack upon shoddy workmanship in the modern world; but the question is a pertinent one, and Morris's response is not persuasive. The Kelmscott formula would not work for every book, and only in his less combative moments did Morris allow himself to admit this. If the chief defect of Morris's views on bookmaking was their intellectual rigidity, his strength resides in a tough-minded pragmatism and sensible grasp of first principles. There can be no greater contrast than that between his typographical writings and Cobden-Sanderson's The Ideal Book or Book Beautiful, first published at the Doves Press in 19o1.5° Whereas Morris is interested in how books are actually made now and how they were made in the golden age of printing (the fifteenth century), Cobden-Sanderson constantly loses his way and strays into this sort of cosmic nonsense: ‘Finally, in its wholeness, symmetry, harmony, and beauty, without stress or strain, the consunmated BO OK BEAU TIFUL is one in principle with that whole of life which is constituted of the world and of ourselves, that complex and marvellous whole which, amid the strife of competitive forces, supremely holds its own, and in the language of the ages writes upon the illuminated pages ofthe years through the infinitudes of time and space moves rhythmically onward to the completion ofits astonishing story, the true archetype of all Books, beautiful and sublime.' Morris, fortunately, was incapable of such rhetorical fights. The tone and underlying assumptions of his writings are in fact closer to those of Stanley Morison's First Principles of Typography (193o, revised 195 1): both Morris and Morison see typography primarily in historical and ethical terms; both offer closely reasoned principles that are founded, so far as possible, on the best traditional practices of earlier printers; both seem to make annoying claims to omniscience because their attitudes towards book-design are rooted in an interpretation of Western culture as a whole. Morison of course frequently disparaged his predecessor (this appears to be a classic example of what Harold Bloom has called the anxiety ofinfuence), and Morris would no doubt have found Morison irritatingly puritanical. ‘Typography', Morison wrote, ‘is the efficient means to an essentially utilitarian and only accidentally aesthetic end, for enjoyment of patterns is rarely the reader's chief aim.'One can hear the ghost of Morris sputtering vigorous dissent to that proposition. Yet, if one reads between the lines of Morison's brilliant little pamphlet, there are distinct echoes of the founder of the Kelmscott Press as well. It is possible to trace a direct (if not always visible) line of descent from Morris's exposition of typographical principles in the 189os to the most influential statement of the twentieth century. Nowhere is Morris's rigidity more evident than in his insistence upon the reduction of white spaces between words and lines of type. The rich effect of a dense, black, heavily ornamented Kelmscott page is too often achieved at the expense oflegibility and light: such a page may create, in some readers at least, a feeling of overwhelning claustrophobia. Morris's explanation ofhis preference for darker coloration sounds entirely plausible; he praises the sturdier typefaces of the early printers, reminds us that they used leading much inore sparingly than their Victorian counterparts, and makes an effective case against the spindly types that disfigured modern books. But beneath this sweetly reasonable line of argument one suspects the presence of an irrationaí, atavistic yearning for darkness on the printed page that cannot be accounted for in purely typographical terms. The same preoccupation with deeper tones and the exclusion of light is evident in the later stained-glass windows of Morris and Company. The immediate source of Morris's strong convictions about compact word-spacing was Emery Walker, who already in the 188os was arguing for a return to the practice of the fifteenth-century printers. Both the theory and practice of Victorian printing favoured placing what were known as‘thick spaces' or (at the very least) ‘middle spaces' between words, with even larger spaces after colons and full stops.² Insisting that such white patches destroyed the texture of the printed page, Walker advised compositors to remove the thick spaces from their cases completely and replace them with thin spaces', which he thought should be adopted as the norm. By the time The House of the Wolfings was published in 1889, Morris vowed to enter into the conflict on this side also', yet he found it surprisingly difficult to alter the deeply engrained habits of his printers. One can see evidence of this in the series of trial pages of The Story of the Glittering Plain which were struck off at the Kelniscott Press on 31 January 1891, a few days after the Golden type had arrived from the foundry. The first of these trial pages⁵⁴ is, except for the use of the Golden type, a typical piece of Victorian book-composition: the word-spacing is extremely loose, and indeed after full stops there are em quads (the largest space normally used by a compositor, the width of a capital M'). Only in the third and fourth versions does the trial page begin to look like an authentic Kelmscott page with smaller intervals between words. Morris, in over-reacting to the splotchiness of the usual Victorian printed page, sometimes huddled the words together so tightly as to interfere with legibility; English commercial printers, meanwhile, continued in their bad old ways until the 192os, when there was a gradual but general acceptance in the trade of the Walker-Morris attitude towards word-spacing. Morris's crusade for the elimination or drastic reduction of leading was less successful. Nineteenth-century books are leaded far more generously than most readers today would wish, but once again Morris went to the opposite extreme by trying to abolish spaces between lines altogether, thus making some (though not all) of the Kelmscott volumes difficult to read. Apart from word-spacing and leading, the question which most engaged Morris's attention as a book-designer was the proportions of margins. The usual practice of Victorian printers was to place the text very nearly in the centre of the page; Morris, with much righteous indignation, argued that the two pages of an opening, since they are always seen together, must be regarded as a single unit, and that therefore the two adjoining inner margins should be narrower than the outer margins. I think you will very seldom find a book produced before the eighteenth century, and which has not been cut down by that enemy of books (and of the hunan race) the binder,' declared Morris, ‘in which this rule is not adhered to: that the hinder edge (that which is bound in) must be the smallest member of the margins, the head margin must be larger than this, the fore [i.e. the outer] larger still, and the tail largest of all. I assert that, to the eye of any man who knows what proportion is, this looks satisfactory, and that no other does so look.'8 As an historical description of the habits of early printers, Morris's statement is more or less accurate; however, real difficulties arise when, on a later occasion, he attempts to formulate the proportions more precisely: ‘A friend, the librarian of one of our most important private libraries, tells me that after careful testing he has come to the conclusion that the mediaeval rule was to make a difference of twenty per cent from margin to margin.'s9 In an article published in 1933, Alfred W. Morris's own practice at the Kelmscott Press is not entirely consistent with his public pronouncements on margins. For one thing, Emery Walker had convinced him that headlines (or running titles, as they are sometimes called) weakened the unity of the printed page. Morris therefore always used shoulder-notes instead ofheadlines. This meant, however, that his outer margin had to be much larger than his theory might indicate, and that in turn necessitated an unusually broad expanse of blank paper at the bottom of the page. On the other hand, the inner margins of the Kelmscott books are frequently so slender that they create problems in rebinding. The meagreness of the inner margin was not accidental: Cobden-Sanderson reports that Morris wanted the margins of his borders to meet in the crease leaving no white'.6² Another source of inconsistency in Morris's application of his formula to margins is that he was reluctant to continue a line of poetry on to a second line, and in order to avoid what printers call a turnover' he would allow a long line to drift far into the margin. Poetry, in fact, provided a severe challenge to several of Morris's typographical theories. Though a poet of some reputation himself, he obviously — as a book-designer — preferred to print prose, because it lent itself more readily to that solid, dark block of type which Morris always sought. However, his habit of placing an enormous decorated initial at the beginning of a poem (or sometimes at the beginning of each section of a poem) produced headaches for the Kelmscott Press compositors. Since Morris preferred to set the opening lines next to the initial in capitals (and yet also wanted to avoid turnovers), his printers faced an insoluble dilemma. Morris's usual formula was to run the opening lines of the poem together, separated only by small leaf ornaments. In Tennyson's Maud (1893) he discarded most of the leaf ornaments and simply printed the first page of the poem as prose, a decision that seems to have satisfied no one, though Morris followed the practice in several later volumes as well.3 Nowhere in any poem did he indent turnovers, in disregard to usual typographical convention; for that matter, he was also reluctant to indent paragraphs in prose. Morris as Book Designer It comes as a surprise to discover that Morris had nothing whatever to say about the designing of title-pages. The explanation of this anomaly is that the fifteenth-century printers whom he most admired did not supply title-pages, or at least nothing beyond a simple label on an otherwise blank page. The Kelnscott Press title-pages are sometimes slightly more elaborate than that, but one can see that Morris's heart is not really in them.
