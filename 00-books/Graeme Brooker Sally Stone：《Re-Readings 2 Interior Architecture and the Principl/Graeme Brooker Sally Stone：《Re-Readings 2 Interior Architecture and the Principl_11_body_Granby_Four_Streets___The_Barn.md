# Granby Four Streets

Location Liverpool, UK

Date 2013 – ongoing

Designer Assemble

![](images/23393f137da330bb899c1119bc23c66de83aecf4371621bebadaf12d43c682f6.jpg)  
Figure 1.50 Conceptua image of Granby Winter Garden, courtesy of Assemble.

Art activism can be seen as a key element of social protest; art-making can often be regarded as subversive in its very nature as it can challenge existing and established systems. The artist who works outside the system chooses to make art and precludes what conventional wisdom would deem to be productive work. Political art with a social conscience can be regarded as having a sense of responsibility or concern for the problems and injustices of society. It is not uncommon for artists, designers and architects to produce work that questions collective wisdoms and attitudes. It could be argued that one of the main purposes of art is to convey an idea and open the mind of the viewer to seeing the subject in a

fresh, provocative way: it is the responsibility of the artist to shake people out of their complacency.

Over the last generation, the position of the designer or architect has radically changed. Just as the house is considered to be much more than a machine for living in, so architecture and design is far more diverse than the Corbusian model of the ‘masterly, correct, and magnifi cent play of masses brought together in light’. Architects and designers are people who can solve problems, and these problems have expanded beyond the traditional expectations of the profession. Nontraditional models of practice mean that designers fi nd their own projects, they can act as client/developer/user all in one, they use processes of self-organisation to make things that subvert traditional hierarchies.

Assemble are a collective based in London who work across the fi elds of art, architecture and design. Their practice seeks to address the typical disconnection between the public and process by which places are made. Their architectural spaces and environments promote direct action and embrace a DIY sensibility. By employing unconventional means, their projects are self-initiated, self-made or self-organised: they subvert the typical hierarchies and procedures that surround the realisation of architectural spaces. Assemble, regard the built environment as important. They feel that they can make a difference, not through the traditional craft of architecture and design, but through non-antagonistic, small, local interventions, whether are social, physical and/or temporal. As custodians of the built environment, they use nontraditional models of practice to generate a feeling of civic responsibility within the local population of urban users and dwellers.

Assemble worked with the local community of the Granby Four Streets project to transform a corner of Toxteth in Liverpool. A small collective of local residents refused to move out of this row of red-brick terraced houses which were scheduled to be demolished. The houses were originally constructed for artisan workers in the 1900s, but had become dilapidated after a long period of deliberate neglect. Initially the residents of the neighbourhood worked on their own to once again bring positive life and character to the area. They set up a community land trust which was reinforced by quite small scale but productive ideas such as organising a monthly market selling cakes, vintage clothes and Caribbean food, and planting fl owers with a guerrilla gardening project.

Assemble were invited to help improve the houses and neighbourhood. They worked very much with what was there. The project did not include massive new structures but was based upon a clear understanding of the quality and worth of the existing. The sustainable and incremental vision for the future of the area included the refurbishment of the housing and the public space, and the provision of new work and enterprise opportunities by recruiting young residents to work on site, training them in job skills.

An environmentally sound approach was taken. Rather than refurbish every home to the same specifi cation, each property was carefully examined and the best possible solution for the individual structure was pursued. So for example, one house, which was so dilapidated that the ceiling had collapsed, was provided with a doubleheight living room. Another pair of adjoining houses which were completely uninhabitable were converted into a winter garden and fi lled with palm trees and ferns.

The project was awarded the highly prestigious 2015 Turner Prize, which is an annually presented British art award to celebrate new developments in contemporary art. This is the fi rst time that the prize has been won by an architectural and design collective, and they were commended for offering an alternative model for how society can work. The project and the practice refl ect an international movement where socially engaged art or social practice is recognised.

## The Barn

Location Herefordshire, UK

New function Studio

Date 2016

Designer Connor and Darby

![](images/72ed5f42256df2fc7ae2b2179fcbff2b76adbd712f83ad4b1b8d227a63a5dc3c.jpg)  
Figure 1.52 The derelict barn was completely encased in locally sourced matte-black corrugated metal.

David Connor was one of the most expressive of the postmodern interior designers in 1980s London. He revolutionised design with a small collection of domestic and retail projects. These remodelling projects were as anarchic as the clients that they were designed for and included the Seditionaries shop for the ultimate punk couple, Vivienne Westwood and Malcolm McLaren, and an apartment for the pop punk, Adam Ant. The liberation of money meant that interior design was at the time on the cusp of changing from a very proper pursuit for classically trained designers into something considerably more fashionable, provocative and at times outrageous. Connor’s enormous and expressive freehand drawings for these interiors

epitomise this approach. Thirty fi ve years later he has completed, with Kate Darby, a much more serious project: an ecologically sound architect’s studio.

There are a number of options of what to do with an existing building that is in quite an extreme state of disrepair. These range from demolishing the building and then reconstructing it to look somewhat like an identical but pristine version of the original, to simply accepting the condition of the structure and attaching new elements to complete the whole. Another approach is to construct a completely new and independent structure around or within the original building, thus preserving the existing but getting the benefi t of a new structure. The new can contrast strongly

with the old, so each is different and strongly contrasts with the other, and yet are intrinsically linked by virtue of occupying the same site, responding to the same environmental conditions and being dictated to by the same parameters.

The approach that Connor and Darby have taken is to very carefully preserve an utterly dilapidated farm building and then sublimely wrap this ruin with a pristine black corrugated metal skin. The conservation of the original was somewhat obsessive. The tumbledown structure was indeed ruinous. There was no roof, and just three walls had survived, but everything was saved in exactly the state that it was found, down to the much too low doorframes, the broken glass in the windows, and even the cobwebs.

The new structure sits precisely around the original building and responds intensely to its form. The doors and windows are positioned immediately in front of those of the original cottage, so the user has to peer through the gaps in the cracked and uneven glass, past the old and dusty cobwebs and through the new triple glazed windows to view the outside world. The only place where this acute relationship is broken is in the location of the missing fourth wall. Here the architects have installed an enormously tall window, which faces east and provides a fantastic view past a tremendously old oak tree in to the surrounding pastoral landscape.

The new structure, which is almost childlike in its simplicity, is very sustainable. The corrugated metal was locally sourced. The nearby market town of Leominster has a long tradition of working with sheet metal. By virtue of its position on the border between England and Wales, the town was once highly prosperous mainly due to the woollen trade, but also because of linen

and leather. As this dwindled during the Industrial Revolution, it began to specialise in agricultural machinery and, most importantly for this study, corrugated metal for farm buildings. The minimally detailed black corrugated cladding of the studio is supported by a simple steel frame and hides almost 40cm of insulation. Thus the studio has a relatively constant temperature, which is supplemented when necessary by a wood-burning stove, the fuel for which is collected from the woods behind the building. The remodelled building, though obviously highly contemporary, doesn’t look out of place among the historic collection of farm buildings: its scale, colour and form are all sympathetic to the surroundings and the view from the huge east-facing window allows the occupier to feel at one with the landscape.

## Architecture Archive

An archive needs a particularly stable atmosphere. The life expectancy of a collection is signifi cantly affected by the environmental conditions in which it is stored, and if problems within buildings occur they can cause signifi cant damage to that collection. A range of environmental factors can affect the preservation of artefacts, including light, pollution, movement (including vibration), insects, temperature and humidity. Of these factors, a number are relatively easy to manage. Radiation in the form of light, which can cause paper to yellow and ink to fade, can be controlled with covered windows and enclosed storage. Dust and atmospheric pollutants, and vibration damage which

can produce decay, need to be monitored; and insects defi nitely need to be eliminated. But it is temperature and humidity that can have the most signifi cant impact upon the condition of archive collections. Relative Humidity (RH) is a ratio expressed as a percentage of the amount of water vapour held in a specifi c amount of air compared with how much it could potentially hold, so for example, when completely saturated the RH of the air is 100% and condensation becomes inevitable. The RH is directly linked to the temperature; the higher the air temperature, the greater amount of water it can hold, so when air cools it can hold less water and thus there is a greater chance of condensation. So, day-to-day fl uctuations in temperature, caused by natural factors such as sunlight or artifi cial reasons such as heating can lead to a fl uctuating RH and make condensation unavoidable. Thus within all archives (and also libraries and museums) it is important for the temperature and humidity to remain constant. The RH can be controlled with the use of air-conditioning, but this is not always a desirable answer. A natural solution, one which is more sustainable and has less environmental impact, is often required.

Hugh Strange Architects have inserted a perfect little self-contained architectural archive within the derelict walls of a disused farm building. This is just one of a collection of highly considered, environmentally sound new and reused buildings on the working farm. The new sloping structural insertion is actually in two parts, linked by a tiny courtyard. The upper area is an offi ce or studio with casement doors opening out onto the small terrace; the lower part houses the architectural archive. It contains necessarily clean and plain walls, and is lit by roofl ights. The structure is set back from the retained walls and rises above them, in a sort of simulacrum of the form of the original building.

The most prominent feature, as seen from the farmyard, is the fi brous cement roof that extends for the full length of the building;

the ridge and eaves drop from end to end in parallel with the gentle fall of the ground.

This gesture maintains the impression of a unifi ed form but the fabric supporting the roof actually comprises two independent enclosures framing the covered entrance yard between them. The new and complete insertion was constructed from locally sourced cross-laminated timber panels; these range in depth from 300mm for the wall panels to an even thicker 420mm for the roof. This simultaneously provides the building with insulation and thermal mass, thus creating the environmentally stable internal atmosphere necessary for an archive.

The insertion is deliberately very pure and simple; everything else required of the building is expressed as clearly additive; conduits, curtain rails, even windows are all attached to the face of the structure. Underfl oor heating, powered by an air-source heat pump, is set beneath inset wide boards in the middle of the fl oor to provide constant temperature; this is supplemented by a black wood-burning stove which acts as a centrepiece to the space.

For prolonged study, a bespoke display system has been installed on the long, windowless wall to the farmyard; it is this area that benefi ts best from the provision of toplight. Designed by the architect in collaboration with upholsterer Jude Dennis and joiner Lars Wagner, it comprises a series of large hinged plywood panels that open to reveal a surface of packing fabric on which selected artefacts from the archive are displayed. In a manner characteristic of the project as a whole, these workaday materials have been elevated through the attention paid to their assembly.

A sustainable environment can foster good health and positive attitudes through ideas of community, fellowship and social structures. The architect or designer can deal with the physical aspects of the environment, but without also addressing the social relations, the project may not fully succeed. Lefebvre discussed this in The Production of Space when he commented that when ‘no heed is paid to the relations that inhere in social facts, knowledge misses its target’.<sup>5</sup> Social structures are important, whether recreational or within the workplace, and the designer can facilitate these within the commercial environment. Contemporary ideas about community and cooperation

can be installed within the remodelled building. By deliberately encouraging interaction, and discouraging excessive solitary working, the offi ce environment can become a productively healthy place. The nature of the interior can ensure that knowledge workers will focus more upon inherent communal activities such as communicating, sharing and developing ideas. Over the last 30 years, the proportion of internal offi ce space given over to individual workspaces has dropped signifi cantly in comparison with the increase in spaces for informal collective activities such as gathering points, social hubs and meeting places. (It is interesting to note that this increase in spaces for

social interaction has actually spilled out from the offi ce, and it is now quite normal for meetings to occur offsite in a coffee bar or park.) Thus the architect can help to create empowered and self-reliant people who are equipped with powerful information technology, enjoy their work and are more productive.

Figure 1.59 The circulation route is a dynamic force within the corporate atmosphere of the atrium.

When it opened in 1928, Number 50 Martin Place in Sydney was not only the city’s tallest building, but it also contained the world’s largest banking chamber. It could be described as a rare Australian example of the American-infl uenced inter-war Beaux-Arts style. The 12 storey façade was constructed from a solid red granite base, topped with four soaring Ionic columns and pilasters clad in pink-glazed ceramic tiles. It was originally crowned with a double attic and a most extraordinary rooftop rifl e range. The interior was equally triumphant, with richly veined marble columns placed at strategic points within the grid of the immense decorated banking hall.

Johnson Pilton Walker and BVN Donovan Hill remodelled the building; it is still a bank, so the primary function has not changed, but the methods of work have dragged the building into the 21st century. The fi rst job was to strip the extraneous accretions from the building, including the plant room that had been installed in the 1980s and completely blocked the atrium. This ensured that natural light once again illuminated the huge banking hall. The building was constructed in an era when offi ces depended on natural ventilation and light to work. This was exploited even further: the width of the atrium was increased by about 50% by cutting the fl oor plates back from the internal supporting columns, thus ensuring a massive increase in the natural light fl owing into the building and the air circulating throughout it. A very contemporary insertion was placed within

![](images/fb81540c7bb50e2572eb68a699ce467e80d9b1f19ebb6261aa15b29e94c74e68.jpg)

the conserved atrium: two futuristic, circular glass lifts, which whizz visitors up to a two-fl oor stainless steel canopy on top of the building. This dome was designed as a fi fth façade; it can be viewed from neighbouring buildings as a shimmering lantern. Another contemporary technological addition with a sustainable approach was the replacement of the 1980s air-conditioning ducts and their massive ceiling voids, with water-based chilled beams that take up much less space.

The building could be described as being deliberately functionally inconvenient: that is, the building is organised in a slightly disruptive manner, so that the users have to navigate their way around the building in a less streamlined manner and therefore meet each other. There are a series of strategically placed, specifi cally designed destinations that the users of the building have to pass through. These meeting points encourage staff to speak to one another, to share ideas, to have spontaneous conversations. This reduces the need for formal meetings and can help solve problems more quickly. For example, there is a staircase that rises through the atrium, and like a department store that forces shoppers to take a roundabout route through different fl oors of merchandise to reach their destination, this interna circulation route runs through the fi fth fl oor coffee station. Likewise the kitchens: instead of the previous three per fl oor, there is now just one every other fl oor, thus increasing the chance of people just bumping into one another and the serendipity of meeting: giant water cooler moments.

And so the remodelled structure has symbolically been updated. It has embraced ideas of sustainability not just in the reduction of the amount of energy used, but also in the creation of a community among the users of the building.

## Chapter 2: Strategy

“I am for richness of meaning rather than clarity of meaning; for the implicit function as well as the explicit function. I prefer ‘both-and’ to ‘eitheror’, black and white, and sometimes grey, to black or white. A valid architecture evokes many levels of meaning and combinations of focus: its space and its elements become readable and workable in several ways at once. But an architecture of complexity and contradiction has a special obligation toward the whole: its truth must be in its totality or its implications of totality. It must embody the dificult unity of inclusion rather than the easy unity of exclusion. More is not less.” <sup>6</sup>
