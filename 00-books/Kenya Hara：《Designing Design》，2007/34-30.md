The right-hand photo is the "human Othello" scene from Sato's film, "Kino." ## Masahiko Sato and Exit/Entry Stamps Masahiko Sato has directed a number of widely viewed ad campaigns in Japan, proposed the game IQ, and made the film KINO. As a professor, he gives a very popular seminar at Tokyo National University of Fine Arts and Music. A song he produced for a kids' TV program, Dango-San-Kyodai (Three Dumpling Brothers), became a huge hit, and helped confirm that Sato's message reverberates even in the hearts of children. The common thread among all of his projects, in all of these fields, is that he coolly pursues the rules of communication and applies them in an exceptional way. KINO is an anthology of short stories. Among them is one entitled “Human Othello." Three men are standing side by side at a bus stop, facing right. A fourth man comes along. For whatever reason, he joins their line but facing the opposite direction. The man on the right end, who should have been standing at the front of the line, glances at the fourth man and contagiously turns to the left. Soon, the other two in the middle notice something wrong about their direction and slowly turn as well. Consequently, the entire line changes direction. It's an interesting story portraying an Othello-like phenomenon that affects human psychology. Sato has always looked for something like the seeds of communication. Around these seeds in the human mind, tiny movements occur. Let's call them the germs of inspiration. Sato discovers the principles of their germination. In everything he does, we recognize his sharp observation and the sophisticated precision of this principle. The subject I asked Sato to work on was exit and entry stamps for passports at international airports. The Japanese stamps indicate the difference between exit and entry with a circle and a square.
