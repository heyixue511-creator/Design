![](images/e636c273f18989310f97e6b1c27c808635a1edbe1e405d0e662cf0feb1b0ca5f.jpg) Henrik Bull, Plate with Nordic motifs for Porsgrund, c.1900-1905 # porsgrund (founded 1886 Porsgrunn, Norway) In 1886, Johan Jeremiaessen establishedthe Porsgrund ceramics factory in Porsgrunn with the financial assistance of his wife's family, the Knudsens. Shortly after this, he appointed the German ceramicist Carl Maria Bauer as the company's first technical director, and the following year the first firing of ceramics took place. Initially, the factory produced underglaze-painted and transfer-printed wares using traditional patterns originating from the Royal Copenhagenand Meissenfactories. By the turn of the century, however, the factory was fully operational and making a profit on the sale of its ceramic wares, allowing Porsgrund to begin producing its own Nordic style patterns. Products such as these were not only conceived as a means of differentiating the company's output from that of more established Scandinavian manufacturers, but were also intended as a material expression of Norway's desire for independence from Denmark, and as a means of asserting its own wholly autonomous artistic identity. It is ironic, therefore, that in 1901 the factory appointed a Dane, Rose Martin, as its new technical director, who then went on to introduce a form of underglaze painting that had been developed earlier in Denmark. The Nordic style wares produced by the factory during this period were designed by, among others, Gerhard Muntheand Henrik Bull (1864–1953), who worked in a Neo-Viking style using intertwined and intricate strap-like patterns. Around this time the illustrator Theodor Kittelsen(1857–1914), who was famed for his depiction of fairies and trolls, also designed ceramics for Porsgrund that were embellished with scenic Nordic landscapes, as did Thorolf Holmboe(1866-1935), whose Art Nouveau style designs featured simple floral motifs painted in underglaze blue. Following this early flowering of artistic excellence, the factory did not produce anysignificant work until the appointment of the Danish designer Hans Flygenring(1881– 1958)as artistic director in 1920. Flygenring had previously studied under JohanRohde as well as under Arnold Krog at Royal Copenhagen. He remained at Porsgrund for seven years, during which time he produced an extensive number of decorative and functional wares that were characterized by Oriental-inspired floral and linear motifs. Her geometric and rather architectural product designs reflected her interest in the work of the international avant-garde, and in particular the Russian Constructivists and German Functionalists. The colours she used, such as those for her well-known banded designs – hot orange, gold and black – were very often influenced by the Art Deco style. By the late 1930s, however, Gulbrandsen's work became less hardedged and began to embrace a softer and more humanizing modern aesthetic. After the Second World War, Tias Eckhoff designed a number of ceramic product lines for Porsgrund, including the Det Riflede (The Fluted One) range (1949) and his oven-to-table Glohane range(1951), which were awarded gold medals at the 1954 (X) and 1957 (XI) Milan Triennale respectively. Another wellknown design from Porsgrund's post-war “golden era”was the Spire tableware range (1953)designed by Konrad Galaaen (b. 1923). During the 1960s,70s and 80s, the factorymanufactured porcelain and stoneware products designed by Eckhoff, Eystein Sandnes (b. 1924), Leif Helge Enger (b. 1934) and Grete Rønning(b. 1937)that were mainly distinguished by soft functional forms.
