While Le Corbusier, Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, and Walter Gropius were propagating the principles of modernism in Europe, architects and designers in the United States, led by Frank Lloyd Wright, were developing their own version of a modern architecture and interior design. By the end of the nineteenth century, Americans were in advance of architectural developments in Europe. The growth of their major cities, and Chicago in particular, with its embryonic skyscrapers, was setting stylistic challenges and their cities were new, development and infrastructure were not inhibited by medieval street patterns and memories of architectural pasts. In the first decades of the twentieth century, European radicals agitated to destroy the past and write new rules for the future; Americans were content to let the future shape itself. ## ARCHITECTS AND DESIGNERS ## Wright After Wright returned from Europe in 1911, he started to build himself a home on land belonging to his mother in Spring Green, Wisconsin, which he called Taliesin (1911–59; 20.2). The three houses and studios that form Taliesin take their cues from the surrounding countryside. He broke up exteriors to reflect and add to the natural irregularities of the sites. Sloping ceilings, often with exposed roughly cut rafters, suggest quite primitive dwellings, closer to pioneering homesteads than the suburbia of the Prairie houses (see here). He subscribed to the view that the hearth was the heart of the home—as he had in the Prairie houses—and piled up roughly cut local stone to make monumental fireplaces. ![](images/8dd9bac4dcbefda17c5e529e20fe470bb23891de3bc630a2cf361910888755ed.jpg) Frank Lloyd Wright, Taliesin, Spring Green, Wisconsin, photographed in 1925, 1911–59. The complex of buildings that forms the Taliesin estate expanded over time, with buildings to house its various functions as a home, a studio, a school of architecture, and a working farm. Taliesin, like all the homes that Wright designed for himself, continued to evolve over the nearly five decades that he lived there. It includes many dramatic interior spaces, including this living room, with its pitched ceiling planes and massive stonework for walls and fireplace. Most great architects are identified by a single style that they evolve and to which they remain faithful, but after the Prairie houses, which exist as a distinct body of work, Wright’s buildings shared few stylistic characteristics, as is illustrated by two projects of the 1930s. In 1935, he was commissioned to design a weekend house near Bear Run in the Laurel Highlands of southwest Pennsylvania. He designed Fallingwater, a house of cantilevered concrete slabs that perches above a waterfall and seems to slot into the rugged rock striations of its site (20.3). It is the antithesis of the buildings for the Johnson Wax Company headquarters in Racine, Wisconsin, designed and built between 1936 and 1939, with its smooth round-cornered research tower and the top-lit forest of tapering columns with their lily-pad capitals in the administration building’s large and lofty general office (20.4 and 20.5). Wright was a modern architect but not a modernist as understood in Europe, nor was he an adherent of the International Style (see here) in the United States. He preferred to think of himself as unique. His Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum in New York (1956–9; 20.1) attracted criticism because of its unsuitability for displaying paintings, and in its defense, Wright adapted Louis Sullivan’s dictum and pronounced that “form and function should be one—in a spiritual union.” He was already in his fifties when modernists were publishing manifestoes regarding form and ornament and, by then, had already embraced horizontality and expressed his own reservations about ornament. During his long career he produced nothing that might be aligned with what Europeans or indeed other Americans, increasingly influenced by European immigrants, were doing. Gropius arrived in 1937 and Mies in 1938. Both carried on resolutely in their European ways and most American architects accepted their formulae, apart from renegades like Wright and his acolyte Bruce Goff (1904–82), whose instincts were American and who responded to its landscapes with organic forms and local materials. ## INSIGHTS The Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum Frank Lloyd Wright began to design the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum in New York in 1943. Its sweeping, spiral curves ignore the constrictions of the four-square grid of Manhattan. However, the exterior, and therefore internal, walls lean away from viewers, leaving a gap between flat pictures and the curved wall behind them. The Guggenheim could be said to be a machine for processing visitors who are transported by elevator to the highest point and left to follow the ramp that leads them systematically past exhibits of artworks, a forced march that discourages spontaneous diversions into secondary rooms. The Guggenheim has spawned a rash of galleries—including its namesake in Bilbao, Spain (1997)—which sacrifice the interior in the cause of the exterior. A building, particularly one associated with high culture, is permitted to be grand but not attention-seeking and should not set itself above or inconvenience its contents. Yet this conflict of interests between a building’s self-regard and the experience of its visitors does make an argument for the role of the interior designer: architects are too invested in their exteriors, too locked into the scale and materiality of construction, to deal objectively with the spaces they enclose (see Gae Aulenti, here). A fresh eye can refine scale and materiality. ## Gill Californian architect Irving Gill (1870–1936) is regarded as a pioneer of modern American architecture. He was born in Tully, New York and briefly worked with Wright in the office of Adler & Sullivan in Chicago. He was aware of Sullivan’s thoughts on ornament, although, like Adolf Loos in Vienna (see here), he interpreted the exhortation to banish ornament literally and relied on natural finishes to decorate his interiors. His best-known works are the Dodge House in Los Angeles (1915–6; 20.6) and the Scripps House at La Jolla (1917; 20.7). Both houses have flat roofs without eaves and flat, white-reinforced concrete walls with irregularly shaped windows. Other than occasional obvious allusions to Mission style (see here) in their arched doors and perimeter arcades, they are as starkly simple as anything produced by Loos. Gill carried a similar simplicity into his interiors, with white walls and openings all free of moldings. In place of traditional baseboard he introduced quadrant covings that allowed horizontal and vertical elements to meld together. Internal walls were paneled wood and floors were concrete or Sorel cement, which could be colored to imitate marble. ![](images/ab8477f1666c6149d9504d934e2f373fdefd432c76d54e3742688c3b853fdb2c.jpg) 20.6 Irving Gill, Dodge House, Los Angeles, 1915–6 (demolished 1970). Gill was the first American architect to simplify his interiors by rejecting architrave and cornice moldings, but perhaps his most radical innovation was to replace the traditional timber baseboard with a quadrant molding that created a smooth transition between floor and wall. ![](images/62ec7e4328a67e19bcf03d6fee9dc136d404a54a706a0548472c529c047caddc.jpg) Irving Gill, Scripps House, La Jolla, California, 1917. Gill was an early enthusiast for building houses in concrete, perhaps because it allowed him to eliminate dust-collecting junctions. In this view of the Scripps House, the potential for creating long flat planes is clear—but the owners appeared to have trouble arranging the incongruous furniture in that unfamiliar context. In 1908 Gill designed and built eight small houses for rent, which he used as testing grounds for new materials and construction methods. Each house had a small entrance court or porch to provide privacy. The interiors had smooth continuous surfaces, flush paneled doors, and generous storage, with raised closet floors to ease cleaning. Architecture critic Joseph Giovannini (b. 1945), author of Irving J. Gill: Architect 1870–1936 (2006), suggests that “the desire for an easily maintained sanitary home drove Gill’s aesthetic toward purity.” Gill shared with European modernists an ambition to improve the lot of working people. Yet his work featured frequently in Craftsman magazine and most of his tenants were from the small section of the middle class who were excited by homes designed to utilize the domestic advances of the new century. ## Schindler Rudolph Schindler (1887–1953) was born in Vienna and trained there before emigrating to the United States in 1914. He worked with Wright but, having concluded that Wright was not crediting or paying him enough, moved to California in 1920 and opened his own practice. In 1922 he built the Schindler Chace House in West Hollywood. It was an architectural and social experiment, a living and working place for four creative individuals: Schindler and his wife Pauline, a composer, and Clyde and Marian Chace, an engineer and a dancer. Clyde had worked with Gill and brought experience of concrete construction to the project. Two L-shaped apartments, each with two studios, were connected by a communal kitchen and utility rooms. There were no designated bedrooms but two sleeping baskets on the roof with redwood fourposted canopies and canvas sidings. In 1926 Schindler completed the Lovell Beach House in Newport Beach, California, for the Lovell family (20.8). It is considered a milestone in the development of North American modernism. Raised high above the beach on raw concrete columns, the two-story house has a white-walled living and dining area with full-height windows on the east and south sides facing the ocean. The kitchen and breakfast area lie on the north side and share a terrace overlooking the garden. ![](images/3e2d6f44ede8d1f6bd69b82c360b2a7998e18ce7892b5ed4231ccb8319acf3a4.jpg) 20.8 Rudolph Schindler, Lovell Beach House, Newport Beach, California, 1926. A view from the access balcony to the bedrooms. It is modernist but built with timber technology rather than concrete. It may be that the exposed roof joists led to its exclusion from the International Style exhibition in 1932 (see here). ## Neutra Richard Neutra (1892–1970) knew Schindler in Vienna, and followed him to the United States in 1923, and also to Wright’s office. In 1926 he and his wife moved into the Schindler House when the Chaces moved out. While Neutra was there, Schindler’s former clients, the Lovells, commissioned him to design the a new house in the Hollywood Hills (1927–9), which became known as the Lovell House, or Lovell Health House because Philip Lovell was a physician and naturopath who advocated sun and exercise. Neutra’s house is similar to that of Schindler, with ribbon windows, white walls, cantilevers, pilotis, and a flat roof. The interior walls are white and unornamented, and the floor is carpeted gray (20.9). ![](images/b84bcbf4ccb03f8d7f32d17bf4a33c8939c8762e08eb0356347f662091261967.jpg) 20.9 Richard Neutra, Lovell House, Los Angeles, 1927–9. The panoramic windows and bare white walls provide a California-informed interpretation of European modernism. Inevitably, the Lovells’ switch of architect from Schindler to Neutra caused tension between them. Schindler was the more volatile of the two while Neutra responded meticulously to his clients’ wishes and was, consequently, the more successful. He provided an architecture shaped by the Californian climate and European modernism. In 1935 he designed a house (demolished 1971) in the California desert for another Viennese, the film director Josef von Sternberg. It introduced curves to animate the public areas and perhaps to integrate the small building more discreetly into the empty expanse of its 13-acre (5-ha) site. The walls were steel-framed, clad in painted aluminum sheet, generally white, and free of decoration. Large windows and screens were assembled from industrial components. A double-height living area was surrounded by a balcony that served as an art gallery. The only rooms on the upper floor were von Sternberg’s bedroom, with its view of a rooftop-reflecting pool, and a generously mirrored bathroom. ## Lescaze In his native Geneva, William Lescaze (1896–1969) had been a pupil of Swiss architect Karl Moser (see here). He emigrated to the United States in 1920 and opened a practice in New York in 1923. His first important commission, in 1929, was for the Oak Lane Country Day School, in Bluebell, Pennsylvania, in which he downsized stairs to suit children and specified cork floors to protect their knees. The same year, he entered a five-year partnership with American architect George Howe (1886–1955), whom he had persuaded of the virtues of modernism. Howe was a successful designer of houses and banks but Lescaze took the lead in designing the building for the Philadelphia Saving Fund Society (1932). It was the first tall modernist building in Philadelphia. Its great size, sternly geometric mass, and black stone-clad base disconcerted the Philadelphian public (20.10). 20.10 ![](images/6a55a5dabe5497ed8772f1206525d66808098d03ba41857f0a744b662c7ef60f.jpg) William Lescaze and George Howe, Philadelphia Saving Fund Society Building, Philadelphia, 1932. Large windows flood the tall banking hall, augmented by concealed artificial light from lowered ceiling panels. The columns are faced with polished black marble. Lescaze’s own house on East 48th Street in Manhattan, New York (1933–4), was a refurbishment of a four-story brownstone. His office was on the ground floor and his home above. He gave the house a horizontal emphasis in contrast to the verticality of neighboring buildings. The traditional brownstone façade was replaced by story-high glass blocks, within a concrete frame, which were intended to improve sound insulation and give privacy. The interior had a neutral palette of yellow, blue, gray, and white. The floors were covered in gray rubber tiles and indirect lighting was used throughout. He also designed bespoke furniture for the house, including upholstered chairs with metal tube frames and a rosewood table (20.11). ![](images/9b9ad1fc86bda41e1fdafdc20093684981398f8410e7314ba357d904774cf363.jpg) William Lescaze, Lescaze House, New York, 1933–4. He introduced a glass block façade that filters light and made his blue, gray, and white color scheme subtler. ## INTERNATIONAL STYLE The vibrant American economy had created a need for, and ways to construct, new building types, and the tall commercial tower in particular. American cities were not stifled by revered ancient centers that demanded to be preserved but, despite the United States having its own version of an architectural future, news of radical change in European architecture aroused interest and, in 1930, Alfred H. Barr, Jr., the director of the Museum of Modern Art in New York felt it was time to acknowledge developments in Europe. The subsequent show, Modern Architecture: International Exhibition, was mounted in 1932. Curated by the historian Henry-Russell Hitchcock and the museum’s director of architecture and design Philip Johnson, it was a far from comprehensive overview and ignored De Stijl, among other modernistic strands. The buildings shown largely conformed to the modernist dicta, with horizontal massing and plain planes, unlike skyscrapers. A catalog, titled The International Style: Architecture Since 1922, gave a label “International Style” to the American architecture that had less to do with the public and private housing that dominated the exhibition and more to do with steel and glass corporate towers in the manner of Mies. Exhibitors were grateful for publicity but some objected to having their work categorized to suit the curatorial theme. Because Wright had undergone a two-decade-long career hiatus since 1909, Hitchcock and Johnson had originally omitted him from the exhibition. His late inclusion rested on a quickly prepared single exhibit of an unbuilt proposal for a large house in Denver. Wright objected to being in the company of Raymond Hood (see here), whom he described as a “self-advertising amateur.” He also denounced Neutra as a high-powered salesman. Hood’s Gothic Revival entry had won the Chicago Tribune Tower competition but he had largely rejected ornament in his designs for the Daily News building (1928–30) and the Rockefeller Center in New York (see here) and so qualified for inclusion. Neutra’s Lovell House conformed to European modernist ideals. Schindler was a notable exclusion despite his work on the earlier Lovell House. Thirty-seven architects from fifteen countries exhibited at the show but only seven were from the United States. Seventy-five projects were shown; sixteen were from western Europe, with the remainder from the U.S.S.R. whose Constructivism was clearly aligned with modernism, and Japan, which was preparing to integrate into the modern world. Scandinavian designers were omitted. Their work had romantic, organic leanings and Hitchcock and Johnson had defined the International Style as having abstract, cubistic, and mechanistic qualities that responded to the twentieth century’s “machine age.” Exhibits shared flat roofs, smooth white walls, large areas of glass, asymmetrical plans and, inevitably, an absence of historic or ornamental detail. They were “functional” in the modernist sense and that principle determined the nature of the eleven interiors included in the exhibition. Afterward, the International Style became visible in magazines and books but it did not find general favour. In 1934 Kenneth Clark, the director of London’s National Gallery, described modernism as “The architectural revolution that is familiar to everyone—at least it ought to be because no aesthetic movement has been more relentlessly publicized.” He offered an explanation for its unpopularity: “We have abandoned the conventions of architecture before abandoning the conventions of life.” ## CONTINUITY AND MODERNITY While architecture schools were increasingly in thrall to functional modernism, decoration schools continued to teach students to work in historical styles even though clients were not necessarily concerned with historical consistency or accuracy. Male decorators were more likely to sail close to modernism but tended to treat modernity with a light hand. ## Robsjohn-Gibbings Terence Harold Robsjohn-Gibbings (1905–76) was born in the English west coast port of Liverpool, which may have encouraged his visits to the United States in 1929 and 1930, when he settled in New York. In 1936 he opened a decorating shop on Madison Avenue in 1936 and became one of the most successful American decorators of the 1940s. His furniture clearly showed the influence of ancient Greek forms (20.12). No original Greek furniture survives but his work was inspired by the furniture depicted on the painted Greek ceramics he drew and studied in the British Museum. He incorporated original antique artifacts into his interiors but the influence of Art Deco is obvious in his interpretations of original sources. For one of his most important commissions, Casa Encantada in Los Angeles, finished in 1938, he designed more than two hundred pieces of furniture but his rooms in this and other projects were sparsely furnished and, because of his enthusiasm for the original sources, his new pieces coexisted comfortably with the antique classical artifacts he added. In 1960 he met the Greek cabinetmakers Susan and Eleftherios Saridis and with them created the Klismos line of furniture (“klismos” is ancient Greek for armchair). In 1966 he moved permanently to Greece where he continued his work as an interior designer. ![](images/0a99d0bb096f5ec998ad1951fccc294f7d67b340a7be8408ecd9b6dd5f12056d.jpg) ## 20.12 T. H. Wormley (1907–95) was born in Oswego, a small town in rural Illinois. After a correspondence course in interior decoration he studied at the Art Institute of Chicago then worked as an interior designer at the Marshall Fields & Company department store. In 1931 he traveled to Europe where he met Le Corbusier and Emile-Jacques Ruhlmann (see here). His work aligns with the latter’s. When he returned to the United States, he met the chairman of the Dunbar Furniture Company from Indiana, which had built horse-drawn carriages until the popularity of the automobile forced them to change products. Their high level of craft skills adapted easily to producing Wormley’s designs and he created an average of one hundred new pieces each year for more than thirty years. His furniture was simple and designed for comfort (20.13). He favored wood, generous upholstery, and craft elements such as tambour drawers, woven-wood cabinet fronts, and caned seatbacks. He said, “Furniture is needed for practical reasons, and because it must be there, it may as well be as pleasant as possible to look at, and in a less definable psychological way, comforting to the spirit.” 20.13 ![](images/748a86da4a15575e275ce07bfa91ff42e6b35054110946f6da4df1834326406b.jpg) Edward J. A relaxed interpretation of a traditional chaise longue, modern but not aggressively so, having a light structure and soft upholstery with lines of stitching across the width to ease and express the curve. He set up his own design practice in 1945 and created the highly successful Precedent collection for the Drexel Furniture Company while remaining as Dunbar’s director of design. In 1957 he designed the Janus collection for Dunbar which combined new and antique elements— Japanese woodblocks and vintage ceramic tiles were set into the tops of wooden coffee tables. (Janus was the two-headed Roman god who could look to the past and the present.) He said, “Modernism means freedom—freedom to mix, to choose, to change, to embrace the new but to hold fast to what is good.” ## RESIDENTIAL AND INDUSTRIAL DESIGN The Great Depression that followed the 1929 Wall Street Crash blighted the American economy throughout the 1930s. However, it did not inhibit the nation’s consumerist instincts. American designers from a variety of creative backgrounds coalesced into what became the new profession of industrial design. They worked principally for the manufacturers of household appliances and while they approved of functional objects, design was ultimately, for them, about styling. The new profession really owed its birth to clients starting to understand that styling sold products, whether it was a railroad engine that represented the speed and glamour of the company that ran it or a pretier coffee pot. it seemed logical to ask these creative polymaths to improve the interiors in which businesses met their customers. ## Streamline The new designers’ imaginations were fired by the Supermarine S.6B, a seaplane created by Reginald Joseph Mitchell (1895–1937) in Southampton, England, which in 1931 broke the world air-speed record, with a then astonishing 400 mph (644 km/h). Its smooth, curved body, with reinforcing corrugations to allow the use of thinner metal, was shaped on aerodynamic principles but perceived to be extremely beautiful. What began as an objective solution became a styling inspiration for furniture, light fittings, and the most mundane household gadgets. The romance of speed that had inspired the Italian Futurists to crave mass destruction was now shaping the domestic interior. The resulting design style was the American equivalent of French Moderne. It became known as Streamline and shared Moderne’s taste for elegant styling over raw function. It was long, low and horizontal. It began a with technical innovation, and became a commercial success. Public taste appeared to accept modern design when it ventured beyond the utilitarian and became glamorous. ## A New Breed of Designer Design, especially industrial or product design, likes to promote itself as a problem-solving activity but these first practitioners came from different backgrounds, the theater, film, and particularly what was then called “commercial art”—the making of illustrations for magazine advertisement pages. All had personal style—commercial artist turned fine artist Andy Warhol (1928 –87) specialized in drawing shoes. ## Loewy Raymond Loewy (1893–1986) was born in France but began working life as a window designer for New York department stores and an illustrator for Vogue and Harper’s Bazaar. He designed the Coldspot refrigerator and many of the other machines that became emblematic of the United States and would help establish it as the trailblazer in popular culture—shark-nosed locomotives, and long, low Studebaker automobiles. For an exhibition in 1934 he created a Streamline office, a room of curves and stylish light fittings (20.14). As his firm grew, Loewy designed interiors that were accessible to large sectors of the population including passenger cars for the Pennsylvania Railroad, offices, and retail stores. ![](images/769a2f34a9395be9e1cabd372d9b4a1657ea0acb88d06677d2848adf9d52b2c5.jpg) 20.14 Raymond Loewy, mock-up of Streamline office, New York, 1934. He insisted on working with the company’s engineers to evolve ideas, a process which he said led to more attractive products and better sales. While an illustrator for New York department stores he designed Moderne interiors and furniture for private clients. In 1929 he opened his own studio and, in 1930, designed a bentwood chair in an attempt to match the modernistic pieces being produced in France. A desk, in 1934, marked his switch from wood to tubular metal and a confident use of the curved corner. He revitalized the then struggling Herman Miller Furniture Company by introducing cautiously modernistic pieces. He pioneered modular domestic furniture with a range of storage pieces for which customers could specify finishes, hardware, and internal organization (20.20). In 1942 he brought the modular principle to office furniture: his Executive Office Group system had 137 components including pedestals, table tops, drawers, cupboards, and subdivision options. The enormous success of the range helped convince American industry that modern design could sell to the general public. ## Liebes The textile designer Dorothy Liebes (1897–1972) was born in California and established her reputation as an innovative designer and successful businesswoman from her studios in San Francisco, before moving to New York, the center of American design, in 1948. She created modern fabrics for public and commercial interiors and was known for daring bright colors, extraordinary textures, and mixes of materials, which included metals, leather, and bamboo. Her use of vibrant color set her work apart from the modernists who preferred monochromes. She exploited the new plastics that became available after World War II and her pieces were popular with architects, including Frank Lloyd Wright, whom she met in 1935, when he influenced her design thinking. From 1934 the tall Coldspot refrigerator sold through the Sears, Roebuck catalog, and therefore, thanks to the retailer’s national network, was available in the remotest corners of the country. The modern kitchen, as it had developed by the 1930s, made use of a continuous counter with overhead cabinets modeled on the practice of scientific-laboratory design. Ease of cleaning and efficiency of work patterns are implied, while the visual impact relates to the streamlining typical of industrial designers’ work of the time. Streamline seemed particularly appropriate for electrical goods and a new power source deserved new fittings. Indirect light sources were concealed in cornices or sconces, washed walls, the ceilings, and the fluorescent tube became standard in kitchens and the norm in public, commercial and institutional interiors. For Europeans, who were still largely deprived of modern conveniences, those household goods represented a promised land of consumer durables. However excited householders were by household gadgets, they were more conservative in living rooms and bedrooms. The radios and phonographs that replaced the piano as the source of entertainment were required to match the gravitas of the instrument they usurped and were encased in carved wooden cabinets with doors to conceal dials and mechanical parts. Caution prevailed in the choice of carpets and fabrics, and geometric or abstract patterns were seldom found in homes. ## Eating Out The archetypal vernacular American public meeting place was the diner, the ubiquitous shortorder restaurant that took its inspiration from railroad dining cars, and it locked Streamline into popular culture. Diners appeared in the 1920s with Moderne detailing that evolved into Streamline in the 1930s. Repurposed railroad cars were narrow but later, purpose-built alternatives persisted with railroad references. Typically, a counter, with rotating backless stools, ran the length of the plan with a narrow preparation-and-serving area behind and booths against the other long wall. Colors were brash and materials metallic and plastic. Floors were usually black and white checkerboard tiles, upholstery was usually red and plastic, and ceilings were often coved, to suggest a dining car on a railroad locomotive (20.26). For Europeans, diners symbolized an egalitarian culture; their interiors would become familiar in Hollywood films. But in the Great Depression of the 1930s, Hollywood also offered a less egalitarian environment that was just as seductive, in the bright white Art Deco apartments, restaurants, and nightclubs that were the haunts of the socialite protagonists of mysteries and musicals. ![](images/4df49b11fa02d90bbca34f2e137b82ec8ebdcb3eb1a6a72ca75aa461a77646b1.jpg) 20.26 Supreme Diner, Boston, Massachusetts, 1946. The diner, a short-order restaurant that set out to recreate the dining car of a railroad locomotive, became a popular feature of the American roadside. As real railroad dining cars adopted Streamline and Moderne traits, so the diner followed adopting rounded corners, chrome trims, and strong, bright lighting. When attempts to dress up skyscrapers in Greek and Gothic fragments were abandoned and the later, even taller, towers were allowed to evolve their own identities, an accessible modernistic, American, style emerged. By the end of the 1930s, despite reverberations from the International Style exhibition of 1932, skyscrapers that are now classified as Art Deco demonstrated how popular modern exteriors and interiors, could find an appreciative audience. World War II began in 1939 and Europe and much of Asia were forced to fight on their home fronts but, for the United States, it was an overseas war that resuscitated its economy and allowed it to build what seemed, to the rest of the world, a template for the future.
