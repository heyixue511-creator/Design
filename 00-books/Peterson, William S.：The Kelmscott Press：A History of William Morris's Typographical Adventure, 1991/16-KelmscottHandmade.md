Morris gave his permission, and thereafter the Batchelor paper was used by many of the major private presses (beginning with the Doves Press) and by a few commercial printers for de luxe editions. The Kelmscott Press also, as Batchelor's letter suggests, inspired a fashion for thick, coarse, machine-made papers which mimicked the most obvious qualities of the Batchelor product, but such palpable imitations misled only the least sophisticated reader, and most have long since discoloured and crumbled. The Kelmscott Press paper, on the other hand, was not a ‘quaint', pseudo-antique sham but a serious recreation of the finest papers that had ever been used in books. Morris's search for a good handmade paper was directly linked to his decision to print books on a hand-press. The Golden Legend contract (11 September 189o) specified merely that Morris was to have control over the ‘selection of the printer', and Sparling asserts that Morris's original intention was to have only the composition of the Kelmscott books done at Hammersmith, with the presswork to be supervised by Emery Walker at his office, No. 16 Clifford's Inn, near Fleet Street.⁵² But by January 1891 Morris owned his first press, a Deny Albion Press, complete with Cylinder Inking Table', for which he paid W. H. Bowden, subsequently his foreman, £25. When work on The Golden Legend fell behind schedule later in the year, Morris bought One Super Royal Genuine Albion Press’, for £42 1os. on 26 November 1891 from Hopkinson and Cope.s4 Before his death he purchased two additional presses: a small Albion which was used for printing leaflets and proofing, and a full-size Albion which was acquired from Hopkinson and Cope for £52 1os. at the end of 1894. The name was symbolically important to Morris, for it linked his Press with Kelmscott Manor, the beautiful sixteenth-century house in south-western Oxfordshire of which Morris had been a tenant since 1871 and to which he was deeply attached. Sydney Cockerell, while visiting Kelmscott, recorded in his diary on 10 August 1892: ‘W.M. speculating as to whether the Kelmscott Press could not be moved from Hammers[mith] and set up in the meadow here!' The exclamation point always appears in Cockerell's diary when he is reporting Morris's most outrageous proposals: in this instance both he and Morris must have known that shifting the Press to a remote village on the edge of the Cotswolds was not really possible. C. R. Ashbee was to attempt something like this with his Essex House Press, and the result was not encouraging. The Kelmscott Press, then, would have to remain in Hammersmith, on the western outskirts of London, but the pleasant riverside atmosphere of Hammersmith's Upper Mall offered an attractive alternative to rural life which had the important advantage ofproximity to the craftsmen who would be essential to the success of the Press. In fact, three of them — Walker, Cobden-Sanderson, and W. H. Hooper (the chief wood-engraver of the Press) — were only a few steps away from Morris's home, Kelmscott House; to no one's surprise, therefore, Morris chose to establish the Kelmscott Press at 16 Upper Mall, a brick Georgian cottage merely a few yards from his own front door, which he rented from 12 January 1891. Sparling's description of the building makes it clear that, in this earliest phase, the Kelmscott Press was bursting at the seams: ‘One upstairs room of the cottage was fitted with racks, cases, imposingstone, etc.; another housed the single press, bought secondhand; the rooms on the ground-floor were utilized for stores.'7 The surviving rent book discloses that Morris paid a rent of 8s. 6d. per week and that he gave up his tenancy on 13 June of the same year.5® In the meantime Morris had acquired the lease of Sussex Cottage, 14 Upper Mall, which was attached to No. 16, Sussex House, used by Walker and Boutall.s9 Cockerell, by coincidence, visited Sussex Cottage for the first time on 4 April 1891, when the colophon for The Glittering Plain was being printed, and left this account in his diary: Emery Walker took me to see the Kelmscott Press, in which the Glittering Plain, the first book to be issued from it, is now being printed from Morris's new type. . . . Soon Bowden's daughter (Mrs Pine) and son (William Henry Bowden) joined their father in Upper Mall, Hammersmith, and the staff gradually grew as Morris's plans for printing expanded. We have Sparling's word that Morris himself never set a line of type, but, as in his earlier craft revivals, he asked questions constantly, peered over the shoulders of his printers, and familiarized himself with all the technical aspects of printing. Despite increasing ill health, Morris was in exuberant good spirits during the early months of 1891 as he watched his old dream of producing beautiful books begin to take substance. W. H. Bowden has left this lively picture of Morris at the time: When the type came in from the founders, he was very anxious to help lay it in the cases; but not having served his time to the business, more often than not put the type into the wrong box. It was very amusing to hear him saying to himself: "There, bother it; in the wrong box again!" But he was perfectly good-humoured, and presently ran off and came back, bustling up the path — and in my mind's eye I can see him now — without a hat, and with a bottle of wine under each arm, with which to drink the health of the Kelmscott Press.' The first delivery of paper from Batchelor arrived on 27 January, and on the last day of the month the elder Bowden printed for Morris several versions of a trial page of The Story of the Glittering Plain. Enclosing one of the trials in a letter to Ellis a few days later, Morris exulted, I don't know what you will think ofit; but I think it precious good.’² He was now impatient to get a book from his Press into print as quickly as possible. Morris had originally intended that The Golden Legend should be the first title, but the small sheets supplied by Batchelor seemed more suitable for his Glittering Plain, a book of modest size. Unwilling even to wait for some illustrations that Walter Crane had promised for The Glittering Plain, he plunged ahead with an unillustrated quarto. Meanwhile, rumours about Morris's Press began to find their way into print. One of the earliest notices was this item in the Literary Gossip' column of the Athenaeum on 21 February 1891: ‘Mr. William Morris is getting his press into working order. The printing of‘The Golden Legend" will be preceded by that of the romantic story first published in the English Illustrated Magazine under the name of‘The Glittering Plain."A very limited number will be printed as the first issue of the Kelmscott Press, by which name Mr. Morris calls his new enterprise.' There was a further reference to The Glittering Plain in the Athenaeum on 4 April, with surprising results: ‘The fact is on the first announcement in the Athenaeum there was a rush to Reeves & Turner and they sold every copy long before the price was announced, according to Walker.63 So the Kelmscott Press was to be, after all, a commercial success; not even Morris, in his most sanguine moods, had anticipated that. Yet it always remained a small, intensely private enterprise, infused with Morris's own distinctive personality and conducted in a slightly improvisational style. The casual tone of the Press's operations was evident from the beginning. The official publication date of The Glittering Plain was 8 May.
