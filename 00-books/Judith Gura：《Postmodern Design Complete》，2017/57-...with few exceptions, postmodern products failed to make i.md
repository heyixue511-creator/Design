Postmodern styling eventually reached almost every area of the home and leisure market and, Memphis, in particular, inspired countless new products. Lamps, many by the same architects who had created postmodern furniture, appeared in unconventional forms and lively colors, though were not always efficient for illumination. Glassware sported quirky silhouettes and bright hues, dinnerware introduced vivid, if sometimes distracting, pattern, and clocks or containers often took the form of miniature buildings. Postmodernism surfaced in a Hannukah menorah display at Jerusalem's Israel Museum in i986 in which ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design"), Peter Eisenman\*, and Richard Meier\* participated. Michael Graves even designed a set of tools for DIYers. In many of these cases, the objective was to 'package" products with fashion and eye appeal, but without consideration for function or production process. Certainly the most successful venture into mass-market goods were the designer housewares commissioned by Target, which made Graves's Alessi tea kettle and PHILIPPE STARCK's (see under “Design") curvy toothbrush ambassa dors for avant-garde style at an affordable price.
