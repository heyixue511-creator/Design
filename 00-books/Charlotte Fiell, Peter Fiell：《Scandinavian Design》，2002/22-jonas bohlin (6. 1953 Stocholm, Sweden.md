At his degree show in 1981 heshocked the Swedish design establishment with his Concrete chair – a tubular steel and concrete construction conceived more as an art piece than as usable furniture. The Concrete chair, which made reference to Bohlin's earlier life as an engineer working on bridge building projects, completely countered the tenets of Scandinavian good design and came to epitomize Swedish Post-Modernism. Bohlin subsequently worked as a freelance designer for Källemo, which produced theConcrete chair in 1982 in a limited edition. Källemo also made available his Concave chaise longue (1983), another Post-Modern design with a segmented form and highly graphic profile. Idiosyncratic designs such as thesewerewidely publicized throughout the 1980s and helped to establish Bohlinas one ofSweden's most avantgarde designers. Bohlin founded his own design practice 1983, and two years later established a Stockholm-based art and design gallery, Stockholm Mobile, exhibiting hisown work, whichplayed with form, function and emotion. In 1988 Bohlin became a professor at Beckman's School of Design, Stockholm, and was later appointed head of its FormCourse(1992–1996). The same year he was awarded the prestigious Georg Jensen Prize. During the 1980s and 1990s, Bohlin exhibited his numerous limited-edition designs in Scandinavia, Japan, America and Europe. One of his most notable projects known as “LIV"(the Swedish word for “life” and the Roman numeral for “54”), involved him and 53 friends of his rowing from Stockholm to Paris over a period of 116 days. This extraordinary exercise, marked by the publication of a book documenting the journey, inspired a design collection that included lighting,furniture, a vase and a rug. From 1991 to 1993, Bohlin was chairman of SIR(National Association of Swedish Interior Architects) and since then has remained one of the leading figures in the contemporary Swedish design community. ![](images/1d2f7c2f80ebe84101e06d257a2e9a1fcaf2bddc5bd0a3355586fc46b163a2e1.jpg) One of the most important pioneers of Danish design, Kay Bojesen initially trained as a silversmith at theGeorg Jensen workshop from 1907 to 1910. He continued his studies in 1911 at the Fachschulefür das Edelmetallgewerbe (Technical School for PreciousMetals), Schwäbisch-Gmünd, and subsequently worked for a short while as a craftsman in Paris. After returning toCopenhagen he established his own workshop there in 1913. His early work reflected the rich decorative influence of the Georg Jensen studio, but by 1931 he had adopted a modern style that emphasized simple forms, undecorated surfaces, and suitability for mechanized production. From 1930 to 1931, Bojesen designed ceramics for the Bing & Grøndahl factory. As a leading figure of Scandinavian Modernism, he was also thedriving force behind the opening of Den Permanente in Copenhagen in1931 – one of the first design centres to be established in Europe – which acted as a permanent showcase for the best products of contemporary Danish design. During the 1930s, Bojesen also designed numerous silver flatware and hollowware pieces, including his influential Grand Prix cutlery (1938), which was notable for its elegant pared down form and flowing line. Many of his silver designs were later translated into stainless steel and industrially produced by Motala Verkstad in Motala and the Universal Steel Company in Copenhagen. Bojesen was also one of the first modern designers to begin exploring the formal potential of the three-pronged fork. Apart from manufacturing his own designs, Bojesen's workshop also produced metalware by other designers, including Finn Juhl, Ole Wanscher and Magnus Stephensen. In 1938,the Danske Kunstindustrimuseum in Copenhagen staged a one-man show of Bojesen's work and he later exhibited at the 1940 (VII) Milan Triennale. During the 1940s and 1950s, Bojesen continued producing metalware items which brought him widespread recognition. He was awarded a Grand Prix at the 1951 (IX) Milan Triennale, and the following year was appointed silversmith to the King of Denmark. During this period he also receivedinternational acclaim for a number of landmark wooden designs, including his characterful monkey, rabbit, elephant, bear and puffin toys (which he had begun to make as early as 1935), and his functional yet sculptural teak salad bowls, suchas his spherical double ![](images/b5abb46cf5c8ce52f1bd1e01374ef1746d1f33bf67c68b86aacbab893adc6d73.jpg) bowl – a gold medal winner at the 1954 (X) Milan Trienale. Bojesen's workshop, now overseen by his son, continues to produce some of his best-known designs, which are still widely seen to exemplify the sensual yet practical style synonymous with Danish design.
