## Notes

The material that is used in this paper was compiled in large part during a study leave in New York in October and November 1989. Due to other obligations, the final version could only be completed in December 1992. I wish to thank Professor Vera Zolberg of the New School for Social Research and Professor Richard Sennett of New York University for their hospitality and support during the time I spent in New York. I also want to thank the Belgian National Fund for Scientific Research (NFWO) for the grant that made my study leave in New York financially possible.

1. I only criticize the very inspiring, very well-written study of Richard Sennett (1976) The Fall of Public Man for repeating the Boucicaut myth and the related myth concerning the pivotal role of the early department stores in the breakthrough of new retailing methods. The 'hard core' of his study is certainly not put into question by this critique. Intellectual honesty even obliges me to say that my own interest in the history of the early department stores and, more generally, in the history of modern Parisian commerce, was aroused by Sennett's perceptive book. Walter Benjamin's by now well-known Passagen-Werk, also a hidden source of inspiration for Sennett's study, did the rest.

2. I base this assertion on a comparison of the historical studies and materials cited.
