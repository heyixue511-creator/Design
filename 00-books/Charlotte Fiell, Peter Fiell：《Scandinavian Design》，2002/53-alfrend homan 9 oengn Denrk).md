From 1972 to 1979 he was employed in the design practice of Professor Vilhelm Wohlert (b. 1920), who like others of his generation approached architecture holistically, becoming intricately involved in all aspects of the work from the construction process to the details, inventory, and lighting, so as to ensure the highest level of quality. While working in Wohlert's office Homann designed light fittings for the first time – an area of design for which he would later become particularly well known. By the time Homann established his own architecture and industrial design practice in 1978, the holistic approach had become central to his work. Both his Homann wall lamp (1981) and Strata light series (1988), for example, were originally designed site-specific for a major building renovation he undertook in Nyhavn. Whether designing an outdoor lamp or a piece of furniture, Homann seeks “simplicity, clarity and logic”, and believes that the best products are those that are initially designed for a specific context and then later converted into standard manufactured products (Louis Poulsen subsequently put both the Homann and Strata lights into production). In 1992, Homann won widespread recognition – including Germany's Red Dot Award – for his multi-purpose Ensemble chair designed for Fritz Hansen.Theundulating single-form seat shell of this chair, which is injection-moulded in a specially developed recyclable polymer, incorporates an innovative construction technique that produces air pockets in the shell so as to reduce the amount of material used and lessen overall weight. TheEnsemble's seat shell not only affords good lumbar support and resilience, its surface provides a pleasurable tactility. As an efficient stacking chair designed specifically for the contract market, the Ensemble is appropriate for a number of uses including cafeterias, conference rooms, offices and waiting areas. Like his lighting products for Louis Poulsen, Homann's furniture for Fritz Hansen, Rudd International USA, and Labofa benefits from refined detailing and high-quality engineering – attributes that are synonymous with Danish industrial design.
