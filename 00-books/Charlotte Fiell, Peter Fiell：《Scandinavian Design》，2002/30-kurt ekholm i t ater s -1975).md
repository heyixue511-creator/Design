While training in Sweden he gained an insight into the moral and social philosophic basis of modern design and was greatly influenced by the progressive “funktionalmen”(functional)movement there. On his return to Helsinki in 1932 he joined the Arabia ceramics factory and that same year set up its prestigious art studio. Around 1934 he designed a number of stoneware vases with simple forms and mustard yellow or celadon green glazes. More importantly, Ekholm also designed the practical, attractive and inexpensive AH service (1935). This warm yellow faïence range of tableware was followed a year later by his Sinivalko (White-Blue) service, which was undoubtedly inspired byWilhelmKåge's revolutionaryPraktika service (1933) for Gustavsberg. With its softened practical forms and simple banded decoration, the stackable and inexpensive Sinivalko service was similarly utilitarian and aesthetically austere in nature. Like Praktika, the design of the Sinivalko service was primarily motivated by social concerns and in particular the idea of “good design for all”. In 1949 Ekholm moved to Sweden and worked for a year as a designer at the Rörstrand ceramics factory. He was subsequently appointed director of the Konstindustriskolan (School of Industrial Arts), Gothenburg, in 1950 and two years later obtained Swedish citizenship. The ancestry of Electrolux can be traced back to the AB Lux company, which was founded in 1901. In the same year the company launched a kerosene lamp designed specifically for outdoor use, which was known as the Lux lamp. Later in 1912, Axel Wenner-Gren begana collaboration with the company that resulted in his invention of one ofthe world's first household vacuum cleaners, the Lux 1. The same year this revolutionary design was launched by AB Lux, the noisy car-like vacuum cleaners that trundled along the streets plyingtheir business from one house to another were rendered virtually obsolete over night. In 1915, the company introduced the first of its revolutionary cylinder vacuum cleaners, which came with a range of tools that could be individually attached to the end of a flexible hose. Four years later, Lux merged with another Stockholm-based company, Elektromekanista, to form Elektrolux (later changed to Electrolux) and Wenner-Gren was appointed general manager of the new entity. In 1921,the easy-to-useMode/V vacuum cleaner was launched with its sleigh-like runners designed by Wenner-Gren – an innovationthat featured on Electrolux cleaners well into the 1960s. The following year, Swedish engineering students Baltzar von Platen and Carl Munters invented a cooling machine which converted heat to cold by applying the absorption process and which could be powered by electricity, kerosene or gas. This highly innovative refrigerator was subsequentlymanufactured in limited quantities by their company, AB Arctic. In 1925, Electrolux purchased the company, increased production of the refrigerator, and over the next decade grew rapidly, establishing manufacturing plants in France, Britain and America. In the late1930s, Electrolux recognized the competitive advantage offeredby high-style design and commissioned Raymond Loewy(1893-1986) to design several streamlined products, including a cylinder vacuum cleaner, afloor polisher, and the sleek L3000 refrigerator. 1940 saw the launch of Alvar Lenning's revolutionary labour-saving streamlined Assistent (Assistant) foodprocessor, which could mix, grind, grate, squeeze and chop. Later in the 1940s and early 1950s, Swedish designers SixtenSason and Ralph Lysell (1907–1987) produced other streamlined designs for Electrolux, including Sason's famous bullet-shaped Model 248 vacuum cleaner(1943). In 1963, Electrolux established its own in-house design department under the leadership of Hugo Lindström. Over the following decades, Electroluxincreased its share of the white goods and household appliance markets with various company acquisitions, including Eureka, Husgvarna, Zanussi and White Consolidated, which owned brands such as Frigidaire, Kelvinator and White Westinghouse. One of Electrolux's design directors, Michael Green, notes: “In today's marketplace, appliance manufacturers that don't make a serious commitment to design very quickly turn into commodity producers ... this company includes design in the Values and Vision statement." Like many other Scandinavian manufacturers, Electrolux has been more concerned with offering innovative value-added design solutions than with attempting to predict fashion trends.
