10. The new study of Bing by the art historian Gabriel Weisberg suggests that he was influenced by a second Brussels center of art nouveau, the Maison d'Art housed in Edmond Picard's Hôtel de la Toison d’Or. Picard's residence provided the opportunity for the Brussels avant-garde members of Les XX, which included Henry Van de Velde, to exhibit art works from many different media as a single integral environment, modeled on the Gesamtkunstwerk. As I noted in chapter 12, Picard and the members of Les XX explicitly related their artistic innovations to political radicalism, stimulated partly by the volatile character of Belgian politics in the 188os and 189os. Weisberg does not indicate whether Bing responded to the social and political goals underlying the Picard art nouveau initiative, but he notes a fundamental and revealing difference between the Maison Picard and the Maison Bing: Picard's Maison did not offer any works for sale but rather created an exhibition space and social center for the entire Brussels avant-garde. Bing's establishment was created as an exhibition center cum boutique-gallery—all items were for sale, and the selection criteria were determined partly by commercial imperatives. (For example, Bing could not include works by artists who were represented by other art dealers or galleries.) See Gabriel P. Weisberg, Art Nouveau Bing: Paris Style 1900 (New York: Abrams, 1986), p. 56, and n. I1, p. 273. Thus Picard's center expressed the ideals of an oppositionist culture, for whom the integration of all the arts implied the return of harmony to society and the individual; Bing divested the Brussels art nouveau of its social idealism, transposing the Gesamtkunstwerk into an aesthetic model for making beautiful houses for the refined and elegant consumer.
