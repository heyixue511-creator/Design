## VI Progress, Protest, Pluralism, and Responsibility (1961– 2020) ulfilling the promise of the partnership between technology and progress through design in the post-war period has proven to be a challenging, often elusive goal. The ethical, racial, economic, and political tensions that erupted during the turbulent 1960s continued to be felt if not intensified in the third millennium. Among the challenges facing designers today are our relationship to the environment and climate, not only affecting the development and choice of materials for manufactured goods, but also impacting international relations and the scale of concerns for global health and safety. The specter of terrorism in the wake of the attacks of September 11, 2001, resulting wars, and displacement continue to threaten the safety, security, and freedom of movement we take for granted and often view as the bedrock of our confidence in shaping a progressive and inclusive global future. Combating terrorism results in stronger degrees of vigilance and raises ethical and legal questions about the balance between surveillance and privacy. Accompanying these third-millennium concerns has been the rise of nationalism, protectionism, and authoritarian regimes that replace pluralism and dialogue with xenophobia, polarization, and tense international relations. While it is difficult to assess the effects of climate change, terrorism, and counterterrorism upon the design enterprise, a renewal of efforts by ethically motivated designers has been one recent development, along with continued innovation in digital devices, fashion, and related products. Global health during the COVID-19 crisis engaged designers in meeting the needs of healthcare professionals with shortages of protective equipment and the monitoring of policies that assessed information and helped to ensure public safety, part of a coordinated, multi-disciplinary, and multinational effort to save lives. Whether infected or not, citizens relied upon information designers’ timely interactive charts and graphs to track the virus and inform decisions by both governments and individuals regarding appropriate actions and behaviors to reduce cases and deaths while efforts to develop and distribute vaccines took shape. While pharmaceutical companies and the medical community of front-line doctors and nurses took the lead in confronting the pandemic, in its broadest sense, that is, “changing existing conditions into preferred ones,” designers contributed but did not own change in fields as complex as healthcare, involving myriad stakeholders. Amid the complexities and challenges involved in shaping our individual and collective futures, opportunities for designers have never been broader nor more urgent than at the present time. Food processors, microwave ovens, compact disks, personal and handheld computers, MP3 players, interactive videogames, cellular phones, digital imaging, and global positioning systems and devices (GPS) are only the most obvious examples of technologies impacting contemporary lifestyles over the past 60 years, extending the range of our powers and redefining the relationship between the human and the artificial in the industrially developed nations of the world. Personal computers and the Internet have not only stimulated the marketing and merchandising of these and countless other products, but also have expanded the sphere of design activity that encompasses all fields of information and communication. The growth of the Internet is a clear indication of an emerging postindustrial age in which information, intellectual property, and a wide range of human services, including e-commerce and sophisticated target marketing, are increasingly the sources of economic power, while manufacturing often takes place in the less-developed nations of the world. Since the 2010s “smart” technologies, centered around Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Human–Computer–Interface (HCI), inform the design enterprise and connect it with fields far beyond art and engineering. The expansion of markets for commodities has been further stimulated by the deregulation and privatization of communications industries, by the growth and sophistication of credit buying and e-commerce, by computerized techniques of product development and 3-D printing, and by sophisticated online business practices that track and carefully monitor consumer behavior, providing such “big data” to advertisers. The breakup of the Soviet Union in 1989 and the emerging independence of former Eastern bloc countries have extended the hegemony of market-based capitalist economies and their dependence upon innovation and expanding the production and consumption of commodities both domestically and internationally. China is perhaps the most recent and explosive example of the effects of free enterprise, and the 2008 Summer Olympic Games in Beijing as well as the “Design China Now” exhibition (also in 2008) at the Victoria and Albert Museum in London and other venues attest to the role of design in promoting tourism and a modern image for the country based upon rising incomes and consumer spending. While design consultancies such as IDEO are often identified by the products for which they are responsible, products generally account for a relatively small percentage of the company’s design activity, which includes corporate identity and organizational systems to facilitate communication and commerce. The visible form of products today is often the result of a lengthy process of observation and databased research, dialogue and compromise among viewpoints representing client interest, user experience, development and production costs, aesthetics, materials, and market research. Design practice appears to be as much a model for nonhierarchical organizational behavior generally as a strategy directed toward producing physical objects; the term “stakeholders” is often used when describing contemporary design practice to indicate the dialogue among differing points of view. For better, or worse, designers continue to contribute to the creation of new or “improved” consumer goods, an ever-increasing flood of products that raises questions as to whether we are the victims rather than the beneficiaries of both technology and the information age, defining us perhaps too narrowly as little more than machines for programmed consumption, reducing the exercise of freedom to the choice between endless varieties of products that complicate and fragment our lives. Concerns with environmental as well as social responsibility on a broader global scale have become more urgent, undermining at least some of the confidence in the material future we are designing. Sustainability has certainly shifted from the fringes to the mainstream, and is now the focus of corporate advertising strategies and interdisciplinary programs in universities, presenting tremendous challenges to designers that extend beyond innovation to rethinking basic assumptions about our relationship to the goods we produce and consume. Green design triggers a broad-based consciousness that informs shared attitudes toward new products in relationship to the environment, but for some interest groups, to be effective that consciousness needs to extend to patterns of consumer behavior that are difficult to break or alter, especially when consumer spending remains a basic indicator of economic health, and when polarized politics pits short-term profits against ethics stemming from longer-term dangers. Awareness of these contradictions and uncertainties, of the combination of hopes as well as fears for the future of design, forms the basis for the final part of this study. In the final section of the Global Inspiration, we turn again to Asia and to the People’s Republic of China, whose rise as a world economic power since the opening of relations with the West in the 1970s in no small measure involves design, whether in terms of the domestic market, trade, technology, tourism, or the rapidly changing patterns of living that mirror social, political, and environmental conditions. ## Chapter 13 New Materials, New Products Material technology continued to stimulate original industrial product designs in the 1960s, reinforcing the ideology of good design as it had emerged in the first post-war decade. A significant part of this growth revolved around research and development in synthetic plastics and production processes. Like Charles and Ray Eames in the United States, Robin (19232000) and Lucienne (19172010) Day were Britains designing couple. Robin Day used a new flexible plastic known as polypropylene to compete with fiberglass in the design of seating. Days 1963 Polyprop chair (fig. 13.1), manufactured by Hille, shares much in common with the Eameses and Saarinens earlier molded chairs (see fig. 11.5), but offered refinements in ease of assembly and a variety of thin steel supporting elements. ![](images/6a42ea9a94a7326e2a1d07d338bab11e7b0b694b781f96b1c26f9d3e348ae482.jpg) Designed by David Rowland (19242010), the 40/4 chair (fig. 13.2) is named for the economy of space it occupies when stacked: 40 of these chairs occupy but 4 feet of vertical space using a specially designed tilted trolley. In 1963 the Chicago-based architectural firm Skidmore, Owings, and Merrill (SOM) needed 17,000 chairs to furnish new buildings for the University of Illinois at its Chicago campus. Aware of Rowlands design, SOM selected the 40/4 and the designer licensed production to the General Fireproofing Company of Youngstown, Ohio, at a cost of \$16 per unit. Developed independently by Rowland through many models and prototypes over a period of eight years (195563) amid rejections from Knoll Associates and other manufacturers, the stacking chair consists of a thin steel-wire frame with vinyl-coated molded steel plates for seat and back. A horizontal steel rod at the front of the chairs frame adds stability and strength, while the flanges at the back of the frame connect one chair to another for seating in close rows. Although aluminum and plastic were explored for the seat and back, the final design used molded steel plates and simulated wood veneer finishes in four basic colors. Rowlands 40/4 may be seen both as an exercise in progressive problem-solving in the tradition of post-war good design for flexible, spacesaving, compact institutional seating (as well as persistence on the part of the designer), and for its relation to the more boxy and restrained aesthetic characteristic of much international modern industrial design in the 1960s. The chair received a grand prize as best single object at the XIII Triennale in Milan in 1964, and was used to accommodate guests in St Pauls Cathedral in London for the wedding of Prince Charles and Lady Diana in 1981. Joe Colombos (19301971) 1965 side chair, manufactured by Italian company Kartell beginning in 1968, was designed as a single-piece molded seat and back into which plastic legs were inserted (fig. 13.3). Less organic than most of its predecessors, Colombos plastic side chair was a unified design where legs, seat, and back were parallel or perpendicular to each other as well as to the walls, floors, and ceilings of the rooms they occupied. We often think of plastics as cheap, even dispensable alternatives to other materials (plastic knives, forks, spoons, for instance), but, as argued by design historian Catharine Rossi, Italian manufacturers such as Kartell maintained high standards in prototyping and production that competed with, rather than undercut, wooden or metal furniture, and kept prices and exchange values high. Philadelphia Museum of Art. Not every original design from the 1960s derived from newly developed materials or processes. Aluminum emerged in the interwar period as a constructive material for lightweight industrial furniture in aircraft and outdoor use; after the war it was used in the fluid cone-shaped pedestals that support Saarinens Tulip furniture for Knoll (see fig. 11.12). Knoll also replaced steel with aluminum in manufacturing Mies van der Rohes Barcelona chair, designed in 1929 and re-released after World War II. In 1962, Charles and Ray Eames used aluminum for the Tandem Sling, manufactured by Herman Miller for seating that continues to be used in airport terminal gate areas (fig. 13.5). The design responded to a variety of requirements. Easily assembled in flexible units from a small number of parts, with seats made from nylon (like plastic, another synthetic polymer) contoured to lessen pressure on the back of the legs, this seating system also facilitated maintenance by airport custodial staff, who could operate vacuum cleaners more easily to reach underneath the seats. As noted by designer and design historian Craig Vogel, armrests provided additional support for waiting passengers as they stood up, and prevented them from stretching out along the length of several units (airports and airlines felt this provoked negative associations of lengthy waiting times for departures or loitering). Less playful and organic than a number of earlier Eames chairs for the home or molded designs for public spaces, this aluminum and nylon seating exemplified design as a creative problem-solving activity, integrating aesthetic, practical, and technical determinants for responsible industrial design. ![](images/4ac2af00271a213594efb1fa0070abca04291662c900194d246a5819b4d929f9.jpg) Charles and Ray Eames, Tandem Sling multiple seating (five-seat unit), aluminum, black steel bars, padded vinyl, 33 <sup>3</sup>/<sub>4</sub> × 117 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>4</sub> × 28 in (86 × 298 × 71 cm), manufactured by Herman Miller Company, Zeeland, Michigan, 1962. Modern furniture of the period, and in particular domestic (rather than institutional) seating at the higher end of the market, was often closely allied to tendencies in the fine arts. While the biomorphic forms of the 1950s related to the improvisational forms of Automatic Surrealism, Colombos side chair and other designs shared suggested similarities with the hard-edged abstraction and precision of New York artists such as Frank Stella (b. 1936) or Ellsworth Kelly (19232015). The audience for some of the most original examples appears to have been limited mostly to readers of international journals and visitors to exhibitions and museums rather than to the domestic or even contract markets favored by architects and interior design professionals. Some industrially manufactured furniture of the 1960s seems more at home in a museum or art gallery than in a living room, better suited to reflection, comparisons with earlier examples, or as evidence of a designers creative imagination, as applied design for arts sake. An example is French designer Pierre Paulins (19272009) 1966 Ribbon chair manufactured by Artifort, made of upholstered rubber and wood on a metal base (fig. 13.6), or Chilean Surrealist painter Roberto Mattas (19221990) Malitte seating system for Gavina (1966), constructed of fabric-covered foam rubber units that fit together like a puzzle (fig. 13.8). The work of both designers has much to do with originality and the appreciation of the restrained organicism of contemporary abstract painting and sculpture; Mattas seating system also emphasizes flexibility in possibilities for alternative arrangements of its components as well as building a novel room divider or screen. ![](images/e9142df333ae4ada5515000b6482cc398735b3179358ab066fbac71713e4e146.jpg) Pierre Paulin, Ribbon chair, metal, rubber, and lacquered wood with fabric-covered foam upholstery, $2 7 \mathrm { ~ } ^ { \circ } / _ { 1 6 } \times 3 9 \mathrm { ~ } ^ { 3 } / _ { 8 } \times 2 9$ $^ { 1 } / _ { 8 } \mathrm { i n } ( 7 0 \times 1 0 0 \times 7 4$ cm), manufactured by Artifort, Maastricht, the Netherlands, 1966. ![](images/820f53853be0c45a527b074783b23d47a48b377539bbafc5f5830be2a52b1f80.jpg) Roberto Matta, Malitte multi-function seating system (closed), fabric-covered polyurethane foam, height 63 in (160 cm) when stacked, manufactured by Gavina (Italy), 1966. Hard-edged aesthetic qualities apply to the Eclisse (Eclipse) lamp designed by Vico Magistretti (19202006) and manufactured by Artemide in 1966 (fig. 13.7) in a variety of colors. The lacquered aluminum lamp consists of two concentric semicircular shades attached to a semicircular base; the shades rotate, allowing users to adjust the amount and direction of illumination. Again, the smooth shapes and compact design recall the purity of geometric abstraction, as well as its cool precision. Both the name and the forms allude to the solar system. In the 1960s, industrial designers played an important part in strengthening Italys international reputation for original and sophisticated products, supplying new designs and name recognition for furniture, machines, lighting, and household products manufactured by companies such as Venini (glass), Kartell (plastic), Pirelli (rubber), Flos, Arredoluce, and Artemide (lighting), and Gavina (furniture). ![](images/cd4e641cd0888a8968af6b7fc2d03852ad8797ef9b2224780269749d7421e225.jpg) Vico Magistretti, Eclisse table lamp, lacquered aluminum, $7 \times 4 ~ { } ^ { 1 } / _ { 2 }$ in (17.8 × 11.4 cm), manufactured by Artemide, Milan, Italy, 1966. ## Product Housing Housings constitute a broad category of industrial design activity, but generally refer to the shells enclosing mechanical products ranging from domestic appliances to office and laboratory machines and audiovisual equipment. During the 1960s, the bulbous and even streamlined forms of many appliances and office machines in metal housings were replaced by more compact, rectangular shapes in both metal and plastic, while transistor and other electronics technologies also tended to result in more compact, portable products. ![](images/e5ef3b4b6ffe43afece6bce581e34c8ed96af45cc40af2370587ebc3a62bc926.jpg) Egidio Bonfante, poster advertisement for Olivetti Valentine typewriter, 1969. A revealing comparison might be between Marcello Nizzolis 1948 Lexikon 80 typewriter for Olivetti (see fig. 11.45), and the tapered rectangular Praxis 48 electric typewriter (fig. 13.10) designed for Olivetti in 1964 by Ulm graduate Hans von Klier (19342000) and Ettore Sottsass, Jr. (19172007). Sottsass worked as a consultant to Olivetti to provide visual unity in the design of its Elea 9003 office computer system. A further state of refinement and compact form may be observed in Olivettis sleek and rectangular manual portable typewriter known as the Valentine, also designed by Sottass beginning in 1969 and housed in a bright red smooth plastic sleeve (fig. 13.11). Its color and product name also provided the machine with personality for added appeal directed toward an upscale and fashionable youth market, as seen in a poster advertisement by Egidio Bonfante (19222004; fig. 13.9). Disappointed with the price point of the Valentine, Sottsass later questioned some of the negative social implications of modern industrial design, and his later practice explored new, independent directions. Miniaturization continued to be a determining factor in the design of product housings such as televisions, tape recorders, and cameras. Japanese company Sony pioneered the design of portable televisions using transistors as early as 1959, employing a thin metal casing around the irregular and slightly tapered picture tube, a carrying handle, and a steel track to support a visor that reduced glare. A similar design, with plastic housing, was introduced in 1962 in Italy by Brionvega, designed by Richard Sapper (19322015) and Marco Zanuso (see here), also emphasizing lightness and portability. These designs contrasted with conventional, wooden cabinetlike housings (often including hinged doors to hide the screen) in a variety of period furniture styles. Perhaps the most widely accepted compromise between the appeal of traditional furniture and the advantages of portable television was the 1965 Sony Trinitron, rectangular in form, adaptable to built-in shelving or moving stands, with a wood-grained plastic laminate surface that harmonized more readily with furniture and paneling in the domestic interior (fig. 13.12). Further miniaturization of the speakers and picture tube, and the use of remote-control operation, led to even more compact and minimal designs, characterized by ever-thinner rectangular integrated housings in flat-screen televisions that can be directly mounted on the wall. Sonys approach to consumer electronics acknowledged that miniaturized technology dictated a degree of aesthetic conformity that was acceptable to consumers. Instead of cockpit or dashboard-like features, Sony electronic products favored attention to user-friendly operation and frequent new models without significant changes in styling aside from color or lighter and more durable materials. One example of this strategy was the replacement of reelto-reel tapes with cassettes in the 1970s. Handheld mini-cassette recorders appeared in Japan in the mid-1960s, while Sonys portable cassette player, known as the Walkman, was introduced in 1978 (fig. 13.13). In the early 1970s, Sony advertisements for its newest high-end reel-to-reel audio tape deck suggested that the product had reached a final, perfected form: This could be the tape deck youll leave to your great grand-son. The balance between novelty, innovation, continuity, and brand dependability reveals ongoing dialogue between competing perspectives that defined the expanding practice of industrial design for many electronics and information technology-based companies. The 1960s also saw the increased use of digital rather than analogue clocks, with numerical readouts that implied greater precision and accuracy (fig. 13.14). Digital clocks reduced the digits of the Arabic numbers to a system using just seven dashes displayed electronically, as in the Visotronic tabletop clock manufactured by Braun in 1979 (fig. 13.15). A similar mechanical read-out system that included the letters of the alphabet was employed for the Split Flap Board Information System in train stations, designed by Italian manufacturer Solari di Udine beginning in 1962. Whether digital or mechanical (hence the name Flap), traditional clock hands contained in rounded forms were abandoned in favor of more compact housings. In lighting, the development of high-intensity bulbs spawned a generation of minimal designs for table lamps, including Michael Laxs (19291999) metal Lytegem table lamp of 1965 for Lightolier, with a collapsible, antennalike arm, making the lamp into little more than a 6-inch (15-centimeter) tall paperweight when not in use (fig. 13.16). Lightolier also developed systems featuring modular cylinder-shaped housings for lights, attached to ceiling tracks, integrated with architecture and permitting lateral and rotational flexibility and reducing the need for table or floor lamps. Track lighting is compact, but still rather substantial in comparison with the more recent types of illumination using halogen lamps. Halogen technology for lighting has further reduced the size while increasing the intensity (as well as the heat) of illumination, and has led to a variety of aesthetic possibilities as well. The almost weightless appearance of halogen bulbs suspended from barely visible wires is seen, for instance, in Ingo Maurers (19322019) YaYaHo lighting system from the early 1980s (fig. 13.17). This uses a vocabulary of standardized components to explore three-dimensional space, providing the experience of a work of installation art as well as interior illumination. In 1964, Robert Propst (19212000) and George Nelson (see here) introduced the Action Office system for modular furniture, manufactured by Herman Miller (fig. 13.18). Available in a combination of surfaces including hygienic white laminated plastic, the Action Office system was adaptable to large areas of open floor space, with the possibility of flexible configuration for group clusters or individual offices to manage workflow. Dimensions were based upon the determination of average space needs and proximity between elements of individual components for the efficient and optimum performance of office-related tasks. The plastic laminates of the Action Office system provided smooth and durable work surfaces that could be easily cleaned and maintained. The appeal of this material also extended to table and cabinet surfaces for kitchens, manufactured and widely marketed by the Formica Company. Originally developed as a form of plastic insulation and a coating for machine gears, the applications for Formica as a laminate for furniture grew after World War II. This is not simply because new materials may be linked to better performance, but also because small changes following the initial development of a product provide the basis for frequent replacement of existing models, blurring distinctions between progress and fashion. A case study might be the tennis racquet industry, where metal racquets began to compete with traditionally crafted wood-framed racquets in the mid-1960s, with the T-2000 model aluminum racquet, first introduced by the Wilson Sporting Goods Company in the United States in 1967 (fig. 13.20). The adoption of the new racquets, first among younger audiences, was stimulated through endorsements by younger tennis professionals such as Jimmy Connors (b. 1952), and increased exposure of professional tennis tournaments on television. Wilson had used endorsements prior to the advent of television for its top-of-the-line Jack Kramer model  still a standard long after Jack Kramer retired from active tennis in the early 1950s, an almost unthinkable circumstance with todays marketing emphasis upon technology, change, and the current generation of star athletes. A resulting tennis boom, beginning in the later 1960s, accompanied a series of equipment improvements, such as lighter racquets with larger heads, followed by the replacement of steel consecutively by aluminum, graphite, lightweight ceramic materials, even lighter titanium, and nanotubes combined with more traditional carbon fibers. It becomes difficult to separate the development of new technologies from their relation to the tennis stars who market particular brands or whose personalities, both on and off the court, are linked to the product and by extension to the consumer who purchases it. Once again, the complexity of the design enterprise during the 1960s makes it increasingly difficult to distinguish between technological progress, performance, and commercial competition, or more simply between improvement and novelty. Conrans Habitat stores expanded the marketing for international modern design beyond contract commissions from architects for institutions and sophisticated clientele who constituted much of its audience during the later 1950s. Habitat obtained rights to manufacture and distribute contemporary designs from Italian and Nordic designers and manufacturers and offered practical mass-produced furniture recalling period styles, as well as imported craft items such as Japanese utensils. Habitats advertising and merchandising, featuring displays filled with home furnishing products and accessories, also were beginning to blur the lines that had distinguished good design from mass-marketed commodities during the 1950s (fig. 13.21). Increasingly the array of goods, changing merchandising displays, and variations 13.21 of new but similar products resembled marketing strategies such as obsolescence and relied upon rising levels of consumer spending aimed at design-conscious upwardly mobile consumers. Habitat offered modern design as a refreshing lifestyle choice, a matter of taste rather than to the more broadly ethical, humanistic motivations of good design in the immediate post-war years. At the 1964 New York Worlds Fair, the USs major automobile manufacturers sponsored large pavilions as in 1939, but the earlier fairs vision of suburban living, personal mobility and interstate highways, labor-saving appliances, increased leisure, and individual home ownership, had for some (at least the white middle class and upwardly mobile blue-collar workers) largely been realized. The future promised plentiful and peaceful atomic energy, exploring the exciting new frontier of outer space, and a dawning information age that would usher in a variety of benefits to an interconnected humankind. These themes were symbolized by the Unisphere designed by Gilmore David Clarke (now the site of the National Tennis Center and United States Open Tennis Tournament), a tilted spherical stainless-steel (provided by the United States Steel Corporation) sculptural representation of the Earth, encircled by three rings evoking orbiting space satellites as well as electrons surrounding an atom. Rather than displaying office products such as typewriters and calculators, IBM used multimedia displays and film to present their mainframe computers as powering highspeed information technology to solve complex problems in space travel, in short, to construct and manage the technological future. Eero Saarinen and Charles and Ray Eames designed the IBM Pavilion, and the Eames office coordinated all aspects of the visitors experience. Their goal was to humanize the computer, emphasizing the dizzying speed of its calculations, shifting attention away from Cold War fears of the destructive power of hi-tech missile systems and the specter of a nuclear Armageddon. Presenting computers as sophisticated data-driven problem-solvers, the Eameses defined their expanded view of design as a series of informed decisions within an everyday context. In their film Think (the IBM motto), the Eameses compared design to planning the seating arrangement of a dinner party (fig. 13.22). The problem-solving metaphor of decisions that lead to a desired or preferred result constructed an understanding of design as a natural human capacity applicable to the widest possible range of activities. By choosing a dinner party, the Eameses and IBM emphasized the relationship between design and everyday living and the ubiquity of innate human creativity; while the Eameses dinner party in figure 13.22 may look a bit formal to us today, comparing computing to dining, or including puppet shows in the companys pavilion, allowed visitors to relate to, accept, and feel comfortable with complex subjects such as the flight paths of jet planes or the management of highway and street traffic. As explained by architectural historian John Harwood, the Eameses incorporated the playful aspects of problem-solving, creating a circuslike atmosphere with a variety of display strategies to suggest the magical as well as the logical and technical aspects of computers. ![](images/fdb9c19f4c4d2389dc55e3300eb13a1ddd42d884fdfaafaeb881989f01049d54.jpg) Charles and Ray Eames, still shot from the film Think showing the “design” of a dinner party, from the IBM Pavilion at the New York World’s Fair, 1964. Before the advent of personal computers, tablets, and smart phones, the 1964 IBM Pavilion offered an encouraging response to information technology, not unlike presentday viewpoints about artificial intelligence or genetic design. The idea of thinking machines was greeted with a combination of optimism and fear; whether through product housings, or promotional displays at Worlds Fairs, designers helped to define, and to promote an understanding of technology and how it related to human experience. This included hiding their complex circuits in visually attractive and understated forms, and communicating with a broader public via films suggesting that artificial intelligence was both natural and full of excitement and promise. Such efforts supported positive attitudes toward the close relationships between government and business, not only in manufacturing but also in the growing information and energy (service) sectors of the economy. Through exhibitions and other forms of media, corporations engaged in public education and the promotion of pro-technology values: at the 1964 Worlds Fair, General Electrics space-age pavilion, for instance, featured moving theaters that presented the history and promise of a future of inexpensive electricity through the use of nuclear power. Lubalins creative efforts in graphic design aligned more generally with freedom of expression, even if that freedom conflicted with conservative attitudes politically and censorship in the publishing industry. Journals such as Avant Garde and Eros were part of a broader movement in design and politics that placed individuals in conflict with the conservatism of both large businesses and government. Lubalin also produced the journal U&lc beginning in 1970, devoted to typographical experimentation using filmsetting. The shift to filmsetting, and eventually to computer-generated typefaces, gave more control to designers in developing new fonts to meet a wider range of personal and broadly political expressive needs. Milton Glaser (19292020) also explored more intuitive approaches to graphic design during the 1960s and beyond, through perhaps less controversially than Lubalin. His oeuvre owes much to testing the limits of traditional representational illustration through a variety of techniques and media as well as reductive and subjective approaches to drawing and typography. Results emerge in a remarkable series of posters, book jackets, and logos, as well as in books of poetry and childrens book illustration. A series of book covers for paperback editions of Shakespeare achieved unity through the use of ample white space, heavy black borders, and delicate wash drawings featuring a central character or grouping from each play. Abbreviating both technique and narrative promoted the viewers active response, and distinguishes the series from other editions. Suggestion and active response characterize Glasers childrens illustrations, as well as his poster for the Olivetti Valentine in 1969 (fig. 13.41), in which the small red portable typewriter is juxtaposed against a verdant landscape. The work of Glaser, Chwast, and Lubalin, along with the journals they art-directed or contributed to such as Avant Garde and Eros, raises questions about visual communication in the 1960s, in particular whether the challenges such work presents to authority were directed toward the profession of graphic design or whether they also were politically motivated and activist in nature. While references to racial inequality or to the anti-Vietnam war posters in Avant Garde were in sympathy with protest movements and grassroots resistance during the 1960s, their appearance on the pages of magazines with limited circulation rather than posted on the streets as propaganda or calls to action may suggest a limited impact outside of the professional design community. Freedom of expression in graphic design does not always equate with or translate into active political dissent and the risks of direct confrontation with authority. What emerges from the study of such work is that it operates outside of the professional parameters of the International Typographic Style and the New Advertising, whose practice is less able to accommodate fresh, and at times unsettling, approaches to visual communication. As a result, appliances could also more readily appear built-in, part of a unified and space-saving ensemble for the laundry or kitchen (fig. 14.1). At the 1964 New York Worlds Fair, the Formica Company, owned from 1957 by the giant chemical corporation American Cyanamid, created a series of model kitchens for the companys Worlds Fair House. Formica, a form of hard plastic laminate attached to plywood backing for tables and countertops, was manufactured in an ever-increasing array of colors, patterns, and textures to suit a wide variety of tastes, including simulated natural and traditional finishes such as wood, marble, and other types of stone. Formicas success was contemporary with a similar expansion of varied textures and patterns for vinyl flooring suitable for game rooms, kitchens, and enclosed patios, and touted for the ease of cleaning and maintenance with household products such as Mr. Clean and Spic and Span. Moreover, vinyl flooring, Formica, and plywood paneling appeared in popular magazine advertisements and continued to be featured in articles as prefabricated do-it-yourself products, manufactured in sizes to conform to the standardized dimensions of suburban housing tracts for ease of installation, maintenance, and remodeling projects (fig. 14.4). More futuristic was the Monsanto House of the Future, built in 1957 in Disneyland and displayed at the 1964 New York Worlds Fair. The Monsanto House was constructed almost entirely of molded plastics, with curved walls and large expanses of glass, raised above ground level to emphasize the materials lightness and suggest a connection to the space age. The kitchen included plastic dishware and push-button kitchen controls to activate lighting, cabinet doors, and automated appliances. ![](images/1efa3ff57551ae2bac8d78521e120465f3e437f1339b66629bc20370c3e52db9.jpg) Advertisement for masonite wall paneling, Masonite Corporation, Chicago, Illinois. Better Homes and Gardens, January, 1962. A backlash against synthetics in fabrics and products also emerged during the 1960s, part of a countercultural reaction to the environmental dangers of pesticides and the use of chemical weapons (such as Agent Orange, also manufactured by Monsanto) during the Vietnam War. With at least some of the health and environmental risks of chemicals addressed and monitored through EPA standards and other regulatory agencies beginning in the mid-1970s, the link between chemistry, new materials, and design remained strong. Today, synthetic products continue to fuel the mass homeimprovement and do-it-yourself markets, contributing to the nationwide success of large outlets such as Home Depot, Lowes, and more specialized suppliers. Wall-coverings, paneling, cabinets, ceramic and lapidary tiles, and window blinds are only a sample of the myriad products used in home remodeling and decorating that offer variety and a steady stream of new variations that appeal to consumer tastes in home as well as other self-improvement industries. Rising thresholds of comfort, convenience, and privacy, new materials and techniques of application that narrow the gap between professional and amateur, and the satisfaction of participating in the process of selection and installation combine to make home improvement an integral component of middle-class homeowning, and an example of the role design plays in our ordinary lives. Former negative associations between synthetic products and kitsch tend not to apply so strictly to such products: increasingly industrially manufactured marble or leather can simulate their natural counterparts without obvious detectable difference, undermining both the aesthetic and social distinctions between real and fake, and rendering less obvious the meanings of authenticity and truth to materials. As seen in figure 14.4, the advertising for many of these products during the 1960s often featured women either marveling at their new products or dressed to play hostess, notwithstanding challenges to traditional gender stereotypes that emerged for instance in Betty Friedans 1963 book The Feminine Mystique and in the womens liberation movement. During the 1960s, women debated and protested sexual discrimination and actively rebelled against gender stereotypes socialized through education and the mass media. Certainly the marketing of home products at the time may be seen as a form of social control, tied to traditional associations with womens roles in the nuclear family and personal fulfillment within rather than outside of the home. Toward the end of the decade the playful, inclusive, and creative element in mainstream consumer culture was also being recognized in books such as architects Robert Venturi, Denise Scott Brown, and Steven Izenours Learning from Las Vegas (based upon travels and teaching beginning in 1966, published in 1972). These authors, in addition to coining the phrase less is a bore (appropriating and refashioning Mies van der Rohes Modernist encomium to reduction less is more), embraced the existing man-made, eclectic, artificial, and blatantly commercial billboards, neon signs, glitz, historical references, and sensory immediacy of the Las Vegas strip and its casinos. Formica, vinyl flooring and tiles, avocado-colored electric frypans, and plywood paneling also provided a creative outlet and promised self-fulfillment for many middle-class homeowners, and represented a popular form of the expansion and democratization of leisure in post-war America. Learning from Las Vegas questioned the aesthetic and ethical pretensions of international Modernism in the post-war period and argued for a more sociable understanding of design that probed hierarchical distinctions between good design and recognized the attraction of the products of popular culture. ## Capitalism as Deception? In addition to racial discrimination and class inequality that called into question the beneficial relationship between design, technology, and democracy, Marxist critics continued to argue that the increasing comfort associated with the domestic interior amounted to self-deception rather than liberation. In their view privacy and comfort do little more than hide an often empty, insecure, and unsettling existence, reinforcing social control and conformity, all outgrowths of business practice in a capitalist system that exacerbates income (and racial) inequality and is increasingly closed off to avenues for self-realization other than consumption and materialism (see here). Even the family is not immune from this critique, since the hegemony of the nuclear family is a twentieth-century (rather than universal) phenomenon that constructs a separation between work and leisure, private and public, which is itself artificial and normative only within advanced capitalism. Marxist critics claimed that the advent of electronic media such as television and film only erodes the publics ability to discern the capitalist agenda at work amid increasingly seductive forms of communication and advertising, and as the alliance of big business and government consolidates rather than diffuses political and economic power. For these reasons the apparent material and social progress of post-war Western democracies as promoted at Worlds Fairs and in advertising concealed inequities in the distribution of wealth and economic opportunity, not only within Western societies but globally as well. Within their professional community, some designers questioned the partnership between art, business, government, and the promise of social as well as material progress that characterized more than a decade of expanded production, exhibition, and consumption following World War II. In 1964, and with the signatures of several colleagues, graphic designer Ken Garland (19292021) published the manifesto First Things First that was reprinted in the journal Design and received notice in the British newspaper the Guardian. Garlands text documents the self-examination and search for alternatives within the profession: We, the undersigned, are graphic designers, photographers and students who have been brought up in a world in which the techniques and apparatus of advertising have persistently been presented to us as the most lucrative, effective and desirable means of using our talents. We have been bombarded with publications devoted to this belief, applauding the work of those who have flogged their skill and imagination to sell such things as: cat food, stomach powders, detergent, hair restorer, striped toothpaste, aftershave lotion, beforeshave lotion, slimming diets, fattening diets, deodorants, fizzy water, cigarettes, roll-ons, pull-ons, and slip-ons. By far the greatest efforts of those working in the advertising industry are wasted on these trivial purposes, which contribute little or nothing to our national prosperity. In common with an increasing number of the general public, we have reached a saturation point at which the high-pitched scream of consumer selling is no more than sheer noise. We think that there are other things more worth using our skill and experience on. There are signs for streets and buildings, books and periodicals, catalogs, instructional manuals, industrial photography, educational aids, films, television features, scientific and industrial publications and all the other media through which we promote our trade, our education, our culture and our greater awareness of the world. We do not advocate the abolition of high-pressure consumer advertising: this is not feasible. Nor do we want to take any of the fun out of life. But we are proposing a reversal of priorities in favour of the more useful and more lasting forms of communication. We hope that our society will tire of gimmick merchants, status salesmen and hidden persuaders, and that the prior call on our skills will be for worthwhile purposes. With this in mind we propose to share our experience and opinions, and to make them available to colleagues, students and others who may be interested. signed: Edward Wright, Geoffrey White, William Slack, Caroline Rawlence, Ian McLaren, Sam Lambert, Ivor Kamlish, Gerald Jones, Bernard Higton, Brian Grimbly, John Garner, Ken Garland, Anthony Froshaug, Robin Fior, Germano Facetti, Ivan Dodd, Harriet Crowder, Anthony Clift, Gerry Cinamon, Robert Chapman, Ray Carpenter, Ken Briggs First Things First was tame, even gentle, in comparison with later condemnations of industrial design by Victor Papanek and others (see here). Nevertheless it demonstrates a renewal of ethically motivated initiatives in design, suggesting that corporate communication and the responsible attitude toward advertising championed by International Typographic Style advocates no longer automatically qualified as worthwhile purposes, and that voices within the design community were rethinking the relationship between designers, technology, business, and progress. But while a consumer culture certainly has its dangers and excesses, it has not precluded alternative approaches and strategies for design, including efforts aimed at social and environmental responsibility, and numerous forms of resistance and subversion. Indeed, many recent discussions of mass culture focus upon an active and independent rather than passive role for both designer and consumer and a rethinking of the dynamics of production and consumption. Responses to popular culture cannot always be programmed, as noted by Venturi, Scott Brown, and Izenour in Learning from Las Vegas: You can like billboards without approving of strip mining in Appalachia. Whether in the late nineteenth century or in the later twentieth century, and whether as a response to early or advanced industrialization, the appreciation for and creative use of materials and processes by designers, craftspeople, and ordinary renters or homeowners remains a tribute to the dynamism, diversity, and inclusiveness of consumer culture. Without this complex, multi-layered character, such design forms could not exert their mass appeal nor provide avenues of resistance. Some of these alternative readings and interpretations of the phenomenon of mass culture are considered below. ## Mass Design: The Fringes Stimulated by the mass media of television, radio, and film, European countries, and in particular Britain, first absorbed and then contributed to and even dominated the expanding youth market for commodities and an insatiable appetite for the products of the music industry. Economic recovery and growth during the later 1950s and 1960s in Europe challenged traditional class boundaries as well as social norms of behavior, finding expression in clothing, dance, film, and music, and emphasized freedom of expression predominantly through taste in fashion and popular music. Occasionally reaction to the new youth culture resulted in condemnation and even censorship, but such initial threats to established social norms were often followed by tolerance and wider imitation, acceptance, and commercial exploitation. Subgroups formed on the fringes of a generalized youth movement directed toward greater permissiveness and less inhibited self-expression. As argued by British historian Arthur Marwick, an increasingly heterogeneous youth culture was joined by other marginal groups in the 1960s, including racial minorities, women, and the gay community, each with a strong desire for acknowledgment and recognition. Rebellions against inhibitions and prohibitions during this period ranged from skirt lengths and marijuana use to support for abortion, freedom of speech, lowering the voting age, and opposition to the death penalty, racial discrimination, homophobia, and national foreign policy  for instance the United Statess involvement in the Vietnam War. Media coverage of fringe or subculture activity in mass circulation magazines and television accompanied public demonstrations throughout the 1960s, increasing awareness. Recognition of difference not only applied to popular culture, but could also be seen in the fine arts, where the splintering of the avant-garde and the emergence of galleries on the American West Coast contrasted with modern arts identification with abstract art and the dominance of the New York-based art world during most of the 1950s. ![](images/1969307fa6d6a615a494926edd7185049cfff08e2b4a88b2695bf898071d6fcf.jpg) Mustang convertible, Ford Motor Company, Dearborn, Michigan, 1965. New attitudes and behaviors were easily commercially exploited or commodified as fashion products and accessories, taming to a certain degree the threat of activism and confrontation with authority. Even in the increasingly conservative and homogeneous United States automobile industry there were some popular successes stemming from a hedonistic youth culture, for instance the Ford Mustang (fig. 14.5), manufactured toward the end of 1964 and appealing to an expanding audience with features such as tapered concave cutaway door panels derived from earlier sports cars such as the Chevrolet Corvette. The legendary appeal of the Pontiac GTO, introduced in 1964, was based more upon a powerful V-8 engine than its conventional rectangular body, and the exhilaration of pushing the speed limit was much celebrated in popular youth culture, embellished with customized flamelike pinstriping in the work of Kenny Howard (19291992; aka Von Dutch) or as the subject of the rock music hit Little GTO, recorded by Ronny and the Daytonas in 1964. Other examples of a non-conformist, youth-oriented popular culture in the period emerged early on in the world of fashion in England. British designer and entrepreneur Mary Quant (b. 1934) designed and sold clothing aimed at young buyers, based upon simple, even traditional pinafore patterns but featuring higher hemlines, bold colors, and fashion accessories such as dark fishnet stockings and knee-length leather boots, creating a look associated with an active and rebellious lifestyle (fig. 14.7). Quant has been credited with inventing the miniskirt and with helping to launch the careers of international models such as Twiggy in the mid-1960s who exemplified the new style, as well as with influencing the trend toward more youthful approaches to fashion among some Parisian haute-couture designers. Such crossovers in taste occurred as well in fine art with the success of Pop Art and celebrity artists such as Andy Warhol (19281987). Warhols oeuvre was quite varied, ranging from serial images of mass-produced Campbells soup cans or Brillo boxes that challenge notions of originality, to more politically charged work such as his series of stark, black-and-white silkscreen images taken from press photographs of suicide victims, fatal car accidents, and the electric chair chamber that provide a grim commentary on violence in the United States, where the grainy quality of the image and its repetition also demonstrate a combination of sensationalism with the often numbing fatigue of mass media exposure (fig. 14.6). From general sexual permissiveness and self-expression among youth and those older groups wishing to identify with a more modern, open lifestyle, to non-conformism and rebelliousness by some subculture groups finding an outlet in hairstyles and fashion or advocacy for drugs, to political and social activism, this most accessible medium of rock music involved an active, engaging role for the audience. In short, the 1960s maxim do your own thing did not mean the same thing to everyone. Amid growing racial tensions in large American cities such as Los Angeles and Detroit, reaction to the escalation of US involvement in the Vietnam War, worker strikes and student protests both in Europe and the United States, the politics of Pop Art and rock music appeared as part of a wider struggle of artists and designers to engage the mass media and public opinion in the interests of personal expression or wider political and social activism. ## Undercurrents: Graphics and the Underground In addition to rock music concerts, festivals and happenings, protest movements and youth gatherings produced a number of distinctive cultural expressions in the field of graphic design outside of the parameters of mainstream professional practice. The demonstrations and disruptive confrontations between police and students on college campuses over issues of free speech and the United Statess involvement in Vietnam were high-profile media events, performances, and provocations akin to art-world happenings and hard-rock concerts, a world where guitars were occasionally smashed and American flags burned, culminating in the 1969 gathering at Woodstock in upstate New York and in the currency of the term counterculture. Posters were a part of popular communication and protest in the later 1960s, accompanying the public sitins, demonstrations, and concerts in cities and on pedestrian-friendly university campuses throughout the United States and Europe. Poster forms were also adopted for album covers for rock music groups. Many poster designs eschewed precise, hard-edged letterforms and gridded layouts in favor of a more calligraphic and elastic typography inspired by Art Nouveau and blending lettering with illustration. An example is Robert Wesley (Wes) Wilsons (19372020) poster for the Otis Rush/Grateful Dead/Canned Heat concert held at San Franciscos Fillmore Auditorium in 1967 (fig. 14.8; see also fig. 13.39). Wilsons posters employed strong figureground reversals that reduced legibility and mirrored the experience of stroboscopic lightshows that often accompanied live concerts. Psychedelic posters were often printed in small format and were equally found on the walls of dormitories or apartments as on the street. Moreover the lettering, while inventive, also at times verged on the illegible, ignoring one of the functions of the poster medium for immediacy in identifying a product or service quickly and directly. Whether in two or three dimensions, in still or moving images, such psychedelic effects were meant to intensify the underground drug experience popular among youth during the later 1960s and 1970s. Most of the designers were graduates of art schools rather than technical or industrial arts institutions, and they brought with them highly individual sensibilities to poster design. The Polish poster phenomenon is yet another example of the varied nature of design under Soviet communism (see here). Rather, artists exercised considerable personal freedom without specific commercial pressures or restrictive oversight. Protests, strikes, and sit-ins took place in many Italian cities during that year: on university campuses student demonstrators rebelled against authoritarian administrations and biased admissions policies, and in factories workers organized for higher wages in response to frustrated expectations for higher standards of living. In solidarity with students and workers, a number of Italian industrial designers began to view their role in the creation of sophisticated and expensive domestic objects as part of a repressive collaboration between government and corporate interests that reinforced class distinction, fueled commodity consumption for the wealthy, and betrayed working-class hopes that the benefits of industrial design and increased production would accrue to the many rather than to the few. In sympathy with unrest on campuses and in factories (at Fiat and Pirelli, for instance), designers looked to alternative strategies that might reshape their relation to society (on Ettore Sottsasss role in this movement, see here). The theme of protest figured in the design exhibition Italy: The New Domestic Landscape held at the Museum of Modern Art in New York in 1972, curated by Argentinian architect and designer Emilio Ambasz (b. 1943). It included both new and familiar examples of post-war modern Italian design in furniture, lighting, and product housings featuring molded plastics and abstract sculptural forms (see figs. 13.8, 13.14), as well as a section devoted to flexible modular designs for apartment seating and storage. An additional category, however, was titled objects selected for their sociocultural implications, which Ambasz defined as follows: The second ... attitude is motivated by a profound concern for the designers role in a society that fosters consumption as one means of inducing individual happiness, thereby ensuring social stability. Torn by the dilemma of having been trained as creators of objects, and yet being incapable of controlling either the significance or the ultimate uses of these objects, they find themselves unable to reconcile the conflicts between their social concerns and their professional practices. They have thus developed a rhetorical mode to cope with these contradictions. Convinced that there can be no renovation of design until structural changes have occurred in society, but not attempting to bring these about themselves, they do not invent substantially new forms; instead they engage in rhetorical operation of redesigning conventional objects with new, ironic, and sometimes self-deprecatory sociocultural and aesthetic references. In addition to individual designers represented in this section, a number of design groups also were included, such as Archizoom and Superstudio, both founded in the later 1960s and devoted to experimental ideas in architecture and design, with collaboration replacing manufacturers marketing of individual star industrial designers. Ambaszs essay and displays of experimental industrial design in the 1972 MoMA exhibition suggest that the meaning of designed products lies not only in their physical form but also in their relationship with users; in this context appropriating references to American consumer culture engaged the critical distinction between high and popular culture and might also be seen as an affront to the refined international Modernist sensibility. Paolo Lomazzi, Donato DUrbino, and Jonathan De Pass Joe chair (1970/1), made of polyurethane foam covered in leather in the shape of a baseball mitt (fig. 14.14), eschewed the sophisticated abstraction of much contemporary Italian furniture and referenced images of banal consumer products associated with post-war American affluence and leisure. According to Andrea Branzi (b. 1938), a founding member of Archizoom, one of the intentions of such objects was to appropriate and invert the message of popular culture, to create a design situation in which consumption coincides with opposition. Blatantly replacing the aesthetics of nonobjective abstraction with exaggerated decorative or representational elements drawn from luxurious Art Deco or American popular culture such as the model dream beds and interiors designed by Archizoom, these designers, whether individually or as members of a group, were operating outside of accepted parameters for industrial design as they developed in post-World War II Italy. It is not difficult to see parallels for such subversive interpretations of banal objects in fine art of the same period. For instance, Claes Oldenburg (b. 1929) also produced installations or environments of everyday objects and furniture, a strategy employed in the contemporary art world to move outside the white walls of conventional New York galleries and to more actively engage the spectator. Such projects included his Bedroom Ensemble (1963; fig. 14.15), which featured diagonal rather than square beds and dressing tables, constructed of wood and covered in lively, clashing surface patterns of manufactured Formica, not unlike a walk-in Pop Art assemblage by Tom Wesselman (19312004; fig. 14.16). Again, the acceptance of popular culture and open-mindedness to heterogeneity, playfulness, and multiple readings of cultural artifacts provide the basis for a new kind of artistic freedom involving greater participation on the part of the spectator. One form of industrial design criticism targeted large corporations on behalf of the consumers whose interests the corporation pledged to support. A pioneer in this effort was Ralph Nader (b. 1934), whose 1965 book Unsafe at Any Speed: The Designed-In Dangers of the American Automobile attacked the General Motors Corporation for neglecting significant consumer safety issues in the design of the companys aluminum rear-engine compact Corvair model, introduced by Chevrolet in 1960. Nader extended his investigations of corporate business practice to include chemical food preservatives in popular American products such as hot dogs; in this and other cases the evidence was convincing and the result undermined consumer and public confidence in corporate responsibility. Such efforts also eroded confidence in governments that failed to adequately regulate business and protect its citizens. Eventually such efforts resulted in legislation requiring truthful and informative product labeling as a measure of consumer education and protection, as well as other regulatory laws and agencies. More recently, Nader played a role in the investigation of the Boeing Corporations 737 Max passenger airplanes after two horrific crashes in 2018 and 2019 killed all 346 passengers and crew and led to the temporary grounding of all 737 Max planes. Austrian-born and British-educated Papanek (19261998) was an outspoken critic of styling and obsolescence in industrial design, advocating instead an approach that took account of limited natural resources and addressed needs as opposed to wants. His views were contemporary with those of economist Ernst Schumacher (19111977), author of the book Small is Beautiful: Economics as if People Mattered (1973) who lobbied for a fundamental change in the direction of scientific effort, away from production and toward conservation. Less optimistic and utopian than R. Buckminster Fuller, who supplied the preface to Design for the Real World, Papanek tended toward the apocalyptic: whether at the macro- or micro-level, both designers envisioned a world in which governments, individuals, and grassroots organizations directed design projects for housing and products in the broad public interest for safety and the efficient or appropriate use of technologies and resources, and warned of the global effects of unlimited production and consumption in terms of population, pollution, the distribution of wealth, and environmental hazards. Fuller also lamented the lost opportunities for designers to play a more active role in meeting basic needs in developing countries. The gravity, and the relevance, of Papaneks hopes and fears for design emerge again in this passage from Design for the Real World: Isnt it too bad that so little design, so few products are really relevant to the needs of mankind? Watching the children of Biafra dying in living color while sipping a frostbeaded martini can be kicks for lots of people, but only until their town starts burning down. To an engaged designer, this way of life, this lack of design, is not acceptable. Papanek attempted to restore or to redirect a sense of purpose to design as an activity integral to the survival of the planet in environmental as well as in social terms. Papaneks urgency reminds one of Le Corbusiers provocative and polemical question at the conclusion of Towards a New Architecture: Architecture or Revolution? Papanek also allows us to recognize that terms like progress are relative and invite alternative definitions and courses for future action. Both designers believed in the ability of design to alter the conditions of life on a global scale. Le Corbusier believed that the machine and mechanized manufacturing held the key to realize his utopian vision, whereas Papaneks efforts were directed more toward available or low-tech applications of technologies and a do-it-yourself approach that begins with individual commitment. He provided numerous examples of socially and environmentally directed projects that explored the problem-solving context of his approach to design and took into account local conditions and circumstances, quite independently from aesthetic considerations. Papanek directed student projects at a number of universities. Rather than being antitechnological, Papanek encouraged designers to consider local materials and energy sources, and available rather than hi-tech solutions to design problems  solutions that enable people to help themselves rather than reinforce dependence upon massive external technological investment and the environmental, economic, and negative political consequences of such investment. His rhetoric, much like that of Modernists before him, was militant in tone, a battle cry directed as much against the current practices of professional industrial design as toward the grassroots alternatives he proposed. Papaneks concerns also resonated with a countercultural group of younger designers who staged protests at the design conference in Aspen, Colorado, in 1970, critical of the industrial design professions lack of political, social, and environmental engagement. While Fullers geodesic domes failed to attract attention in the building industry and Habitat 67 remained a one-off model for urban housing, there was a boom in the construction of low-cost, high-density social housing projects and neighborhood redevelopment initiatives in the later 1950s and 1960s in a number of American cities as well as in the Soviet Union. In the United States inner-city housing projects were built with the aim of stemming the exodus of families to suburban communities and providing modern if modest residential housing that replaced decaying and mostly minority neighborhoods and improved the quality of life for their low-income residents. There has been widespread agreement that these massive, well-intentioned governmentsubsidized building and redevelopment projects destroyed existing structures and deteriorated due to cost-cutting in the process of their construction, and that they also, and perhaps more importantly, failed to engage residents in their design or to build a sense of neighborhood community and shared responsibility among their inhabitants. British historian David Harvey commented that the demolition of the 1955 Pruitt-Igoe housing development in St. Louis in 1972 signified the demise of the belief that largescale high-rise social housing in line with Modernist principles could help advance the cause of social or racial justice. As architectural critic and urban activist Jane Jacobs noted in her 1961 book The Death and Life of Great American Cities, housing standards based upon Modernist aesthetics or social determinism were not able to effectively address the challenges of creating safe and economically viable neighborhoods and communities. In retrospect the design process must take into account a variety of factors that include the perspectives of the population being served, the psychological effects of the environment, and zoning considerations that affect the nurturing of community. Despite the progressive and democratic intentions of slum-clearance and the war on poverty in the 1960s, Modernist design was judged harshly by many critics as a solution for working-class urban living, representing the viewpoint of architects and planners without sufficient input from the users whose lives were affected. While some critics attribute the failure of high-rise social housing to the arrogance of elitist urban planners out of touch with those whose needs they are supposed to serve, more recent historians acknowledge the difficulties urban planners encountered and offer a degree of respect for the wellmeaning intentions that motivated such projects. Today the revitalization of urban neighborhoods often entails maintaining existing local businesses and attracting new ones, preserving the character of residential housing, converting warehouses to lofts or other purposes, and creating appeal for young professionals and families. But appeal creates demand which in turn raises housing prices, making such housing affordable only to wealthier and less diverse buyers, a process usually referred to as gentrification. Housing was a concern in the communist East as well as in Western Europe and the United States. Modernist housing blocks were constructed in the Soviet Union, East Germany, Yugoslavia, and elsewhere in communist Eastern Europe. As argued, however, by British historian and author Arthur Marwick, the challenge to authority from under-represented and marginalized minority voices, whether women, Black people, LGBTQ+ people, or students, produced more open-minded attitudes toward difference and inclusion, leading to reform through progressive legislation establishing and protecting individual rights. In the United States, federal enforcement of the 1964 Civil Rights Act, the 1965 Voting Rights Act, and widely broadcast demonstrations such as Martin Luther Kings 1963 March on Washington, DC brought issues of racial inequality and opportunity to national attention. Legislation created the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) in 1970, while the passage of laws aimed at reducing highway deaths through seatbelt laws and mandatory manufacturer recalls on defective parts and systems eventually resulted from Ralph Naders spirited challenge to unquestioned faith in corporate power. For almost 20 years, the oil crisis of the early 1970s and concerns for fuel efficiency and automobile safety produced lower speed limits (55 mph) on many of the nations highways. Other legislation legalized abortion (Roe vs. Wade, 1973; since overturned in 2022 by the Supreme Court), required the reading of rights to suspects placed under arrest (Miranda vs. Arizona, 1966), and made divorces easier to obtain. Universities abolished quota systems for admission, and the percentage of minority students able to attend college began to rise through need- and merit-based scholarships, loans, and affirmative action laws. Universities and colleges developed new courses and programs for the critical and historical study of film, photography, jazz, race, womens studies, and forms of popular music. The United States Cold War policy of communist containment and soft power (see here) continued, while broad-based pressure at home among youth and in the media helped to bring an end to the drawn-out war in Vietnam, a response to the questioning of policies regarding threats to American power and influence abroad. The same questions attended US military intervention in Iraq and Afghanistan in the wake of the attacks of September 11, 2001. The 1960s signaled recognition of ethnic, racial, gender, and generational subcultures with distinct identities in fashion and graphic design, the latter often in connection with protests and demonstrations seeking inclusion, recognition, or social justice. The coexistence of alternative voices is often referred to as pluralism. This shift also produced a backlash against multiculturalism and globalism that simmered and began to gain ground in the twenty-first century in response to the attacks of 9/11 and with the backing of more authoritarian, nationalist governments and renewed political division and polarization. Aggressive, belligerent, destructive, and uninhibited, punk culture in music, fashion, performance, poetry, and graphic design relished provocation, celebrating graffiti, personal tattooing, and body piercing, and making exaggerated, garish use of highly commercialized products such as cosmetics, hair gels for spiked hair styles, and hair dyes to achieve unconventional effects in personal appearance that consciously triggered resentment and outrage. The punk phenomenon belongs within the framework of pluralism, complex in its relationship to popular culture, acknowledging rather than rejecting the multiple signs and messages inherent in our consumer culture. An example of adapting the rebellious counterculture aspects of the punk movement to advertising for products otherwise characterized by brand standardization was the Philips Tracer electric shaver, sold in a variety of colors and targeting a youthful market (fig. 15.6). The reutilization of industrial products and materials for public and domestic spaces and the repurposing of abandoned warehouses and factories for studios and apartments emerged under the term hi-tech in the later 1970s. The Castiglioni brothers Mezzadro stool, first designed in 1954 and manufactured by Zanotta beginning in 1957, prefigured this phenomenon, reinterpreting a metal tractor seat in a domestic setting with little transformation of its utilitarian associations. Ron Arads Rover chair, made from tubular metal attached to a second-hand automobile seat, also exemplifies aspects of a hi-tech aesthetic. Hi-tech interiors featured exposed ventilation ducts and riveted I-beams, pipe-railings, prefabricated industrial building materials, and perforated spiral staircases, seen, for example, in the waiting room of the Occupational Health Center in Columbus, Indiana, designed by Hardy Holzman Pfeiffer Associates between 1974 and 1977 (fig. 15.7). Using color and emphasizing unfinished textures of concrete, unglazed brick, and metal, the interior exposes the new post-industrial aesthetic, acknowledging honestly rather than hiding the plumbing and HVAC systems that are an integral part of their design. A more celebratory tone appears in the loud and busy exterior of Richard Rogers and Renzo Pianos Pompidou Center in Paris (completed 1977). The exposure of industrial materials and fittings extended into a wide variety of household accessories, including plastic-coated wire shelving units manufactured by the Elfa company in Sweden, appearing first in the later 1970s (fig. 15.8). Furniture was another focus for hi-tech design, seen in Japanese designer Shiro Kuramatas (19341991) How High the Moon chair (fig. 15.9), dating to 1986 and manufactured by Vitra. Surprisingly, the metal mesh construction is both strong enough for support and flexible enough for comfortable sitting (the mesh conforms comfortably around ones body when seated). Yet How High the Moon does not seem to have been designed with sitting as its main or sole intention, as the lightness and transparency of its material, together with the precision of its curves and joints, exert an especially strong aesthetic appeal that allows us to experience the material independent from or in addition to its function. ![](images/f08bb67321fe4b01e73c212ffef7cb96f0326f47a4c9faf3ec77b62fe8695195.jpg) 15.7 Hardy Holzman Pfeiffer Associates, interior, Occupational Health Center, Columbus, Indiana, 1974–1977. 15.9 ![](images/3869ca2fe2246e0a6aa9132abe461f31a1caf12d539790259cd3346fe4b258a9.jpg) 15.8 Wire shelving, plastic-coated wire, Elfa company, Sweden, late 1970s. ![](images/af3c1915a6f6b118b9efb0ef3470ac6b25818052f5f09e0a3e0ed6693f41b14e.jpg) Shiro Kuramata, How High the Moon armchair, nickel-plated steel, 28 $^ 1 / _ { 4 } \times 3 7 ~ ^ { 3 } / _ { 8 } \times 3 2$ in $( 7 2 \times 9 5 \times 8 1$ cm), manufactured by Vitra, Switzerland, 1986. Museum of Modern Art, New York. ## Postmodernism and Taste Capital investment in business on an increasing international scale following the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1989, the formation of the European Union in 1993 and trade negotiations between the United States and the Peoples Republic of China (beginning with President Richard Nixons visit to China in 1972), all demonstrated that economic expansion and free trade consistently dominated international relations despite political differences, with human rights serving as a bargaining chip to ensure the cooperation of governments on behalf of their broad-based constituencies. Today, in general, the press and public it represents readily accept that US foreign policy is governed by balancing concerns for human rights and the protection of the nations intellectual property with the exploration of new markets for raw materials and goods, including designed products, technologies, and services. Even the Peoples Republic of China, which celebrated the 70th anniversary of communist rule in 2019, is closely connected with global supply-chain networks for expanding international trade and competition. Chinas economic growth and role in contemporary design is treated in Part VI of the Global Inspiration. Within a framework marked by the hegemony of capitalism and the equation of economic and social progress with technology, innovation, and economic expansion in the later twentieth century, pluralism continued to dominate the modern production and consumption of design. This generally healthy heterogeneity reduced the dichotomy between reform-based initiatives grounded in shared aesthetic and ethical values, and approaches to design governed by free enterprise and addressing a diverse market. In the context of inclusiveness and relativism in taste, notions of reform, resistance, and subversion relinquished a measure of their power to define a vital current in the history of modern design, namely a desire to shape not just the experience of products and spaces but also the values of a future society that included ethical concerns and addressed the many strands of social justice. More recent concerns regarding migration, displacement, racial and ethnic equality, and climate change in the twenty-first century have renewed a sense of urgency in directing the attention of the design community toward deteriorating environmental and social conditions at both the professional and grassroots levels. These efforts are discussed in Chapter 16. Toward the end of the 1970s, the popular and subcultural challenges to modern architecture and good design were becoming more widespread. The catalog for the 1983 exhibition Design since 1945, held at the Philadelphia Museum of Art and curated by Kathryn Hiesinger and George Marcus, included essays advocating rational standards and abstract aesthetics in design by Dieter Rams, Max Bill, and George Nelson, but also contained an essay by University of Pennsylvania sociologist Herbert Gans, who argued that post-war international modern design was a treasure trove of upper middle class culture appealing to a particular class and reflecting particular (and exclusive) taste rather than universal democratic values. At the same time, the more rhetorical Anti-Design initiatives in Italy and elsewhere in the later 1960s (see here) acquired an international high-end commercial cachet underwritten by manufacturers such as Alessi and Swid Powell. In Milan, for instance, organizations espousing new directions in design were initiated in the later 1970s. Alchymia (1976) and Memphis (1981) included the work of not only Italian but also Japanese and Latin American designers. Memphis (see here) and other groups or individuals designing ambiguous appropriations of historical styles and popular culture were described as Postmodern, a term commonly used among critics and historians (although rejected by some designers) that filtered into more general usage. Postmodern design encompasses projects and forms that signal a welcome end to the critical dichotomy between aesthetically or ethically directed design on the one hand and commercially motivated design on the other, an opposition that emerged in the early nineteenth century with the design reform movement and that dominated design theory through the two decades following World War II. It may also be described even more broadly as an attitude through which various tendencies of Modernism in design are deprived of their reformist or revolutionary utopian pretensions. For design, Postmodernism shares with mass entertainment and culture a user-oriented approach that emphasizes multiple interpretations and meanings, and embraces ephemeral criteria of evaluation for the design enterprise. ![](images/14ad5dfc78b325e27b67e8111c889d9e32490e8b13ffff49df946d78247dcce5.jpg) ## 15.10 Paula Scher, Swatch advertising poster, offset print, 29 <sup>7</sup>/<sub>8</sub> × 20 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>8</sub> in (76 × 51 cm), 1984. The term Postmodernism is often found in conjunction with other concepts such as post-industrialism and late capitalism, all referring to a culture that equates economic growth with progress and with individual freedom, strengthened in the early years of post-war affluence in the United States and spreading to Europe, Japan, and other industrialized nations. Late capitalism signifies investment directed toward increasingly segmented audiences (rather than Fordist mechanized mass production), and the readiness of businesses to design, manufacture, and market products with increasing speed, responsiveness, flexibility, and sophistication in a highly competitive and constantly evolving commercial environment. The design enterprise is also accelerated through the increased speed of batch rather than mass production, target marketing, and more recently communication and marketing through e-commerce (in 2021 IKEA ceased to publish its print catalog, see here). Batch production focuses upon the assembly of limited quantities of varied products using similar components for flexible yet efficient manufacture. One example is in the fashion industry, where a limited number of bodies are expanded through variations in trim to permit more individualized consumer choice. As another example, British design historian Nigel Whitely cites the success of the Swatch wristwatches first in Britain and then internationally in the 1980s, the result of inexpensive plastic materials and production, and an especially fashion-oriented approach to wristwatches to complement clothing styles (fig. 15.10). The ephemeral, virtually disposable Swatch (the name combines the words Swiss and watch, but also brings to mind the word switch), manufactured by Swiss company Eta beginning in 1983, targeted a young audience less likely to be influenced by the emotional attachments sometimes associated with personal objects and heirlooms (such as a grandfather clock). Graphic designer Paula Schers (b. 1948) 1984 Swatch poster advertisement appropriates elements of Herbert Matters 1934 travel poster for the Swiss Tourism Bureau. Schers photomontage technique and juxtapositions suggest a broad range of associations connecting wristwatches and Switzerland (snow-capped mountains, skiing, and the red Swiss flag emblem that forms part of the company logo), and timepieces, leisure, and with the history of Swiss graphic design. The range of meanings here is open-ended, communicating at different levels to those with or without an insider knowledge of graphic design history. As product advertising, Schers poster deviates from the emphasis upon the straightforward objectbased approach (without addressing figure) found in Swiss advertising design in the post-World War II era (see here and fig. 11.64). ## Postmodern Products Postmodern design also emerged in organizations such as Memphis, composed of industrial designers liberated from their contracts with particular companies, enjoying the freedom to pursue directions beyond the parameters of good design. Postmodernism or pluralism was also the overriding theme of the exhibition Design Now: Industry or Art, held at the German Architecture Museum in Frankfurt in 1989. ![](images/5aad0597eb628b7f9153200bea83dca612568258acca12fa15ca45672b0d6d87.jpg) 15.11 Ettore Sottsass, Jr., Tartar table, reconstituted veneer, lacquer, and plastic laminate, 30 × 75 <sup>3</sup>/<sub>4</sub> × 32 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>4</sub> in (76 × 192 × 82 cm), manufactured by Memphis, 1981. ![](images/3d27c86d94d99ad9921f7cbaeecf366a66c8e68fea310dd853d2e3e7397276d4.jpg) Alessandro Mendini, Proust armchair, painted wood with hand-painted upholstery, height 42 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>8</sub> in (107 cm), made by Studio Alchymia, Milan, 1978. Ettore Sottsass, Jr., who worked under contract to Olivetti in the 1960s in the design of office machines, mainframe computer housings, and personal typewriters (see figs. 13.10 and 13.11), independently designed prototypes for furniture in the 1980s using commercial industrial materials such as plywood and plastic laminates in bright colors and decorative patterns. These examples appropriated or exaggerated Hollywood-style historical set design, the sensory excitement of billboard advertising and rock music (Memphis is the home of rock icon Elvis Presley, immortalized in Andy Warhols silkscreen images and in the furnishings of the singers own Graceland mansion; it also figures in the title of Bob Dylans rambling 1966 song Stuck Inside of Mobile with the Memphis Blues Again). One product is Sottsasss 1981 Tartar table (fig. 15.11), whose laminated surfaces and patterned decoration recall Oldenburgs 1963 Bedroom Ensemble or Wesselmans (19312004) Still Life Number 30 kitchen-table assemblage (see figs. 14.15 and 14.16). Memphis organized group exhibitions in Milan, and the Formica Corporation invited members of the group to design furniture using its new ColorCore product, a solid variation of the material that could be molded and cut. The conflicting, overlapping interpretations are typical of Postmodern forms and their eclectic appropriation of the past in a reflective and complex way. Alessandro Mendinis (1931 2019) Proust armchair (1978; fig. 15.12), named for the introspective French writer Marcel Proust (18711922) and produced for Studio Alchymia, is another example: the Rococo Revival-upholstered armchair references mid-nineteenth-century comfort and artisanal craftsmanship, while the brightly colored upholstery cover is a clear appropriation of pointillism, a style of painting associated with Georges Seurat (1859 1891) and concerned with reproducing the harmony and luminosity of contrasting colors. The materials and carving assert mass, while the flickering upholstery patterns deny it: the chair both affirms and dematerializes physical form at the same time, as if to illustrate the coexistence of multiple, even opposite perceptions. Such interpretive dissonance was characteristic of many Postmodern design initiatives; in addition to welcoming the return of decoration and historicism in objects as a jab at the universal pretensions of international Modernism, such works also emphasize the shifting and complex relationship between objects and users in late twentieth-century design thinking and practice. A similar explosion of complex pattern and saturated color appears in the printed cotton fabric designs of French artist Nathalie du Pasquier (b. 1957), also a member of Memphis. Du Pasquiers Gabon textile dates to 1982 and was manufactured by the Milanese company Rainbow. Gabon combines tightly packed angular shapes, strong color contrasts, and irregular spatial dislocations in a bold, almost dizzying composition (fig. 15.13). The pattern was one in a series Du Pasquier designed following her travels to Africa, and indicates the varied and eclectic appropriations of the Memphis group, alternately inspired by Hollywood glamour, history, and travel. In the catalog for the Victoria and Albert Museum exhibition Postmodernism: Style and Subversion, 19701990 Jane Pavitt and Glenn Adamson likened the eclectic borrowing in Postmodern designers work to the theme of the road trip in literature or films such as Easy Rider (1969), whether thrill-seeking, soulsearching, or aimless wandering, an escape from the routines and responsibilities of ordinary working life. Du Pasquiers travels to Africa, or Sottsasss fascination with India (seen in stupa-shaped ceramic vessels he designed in the later 1960s) incorporated oblique references to non-Western cultures, freely mediated by the designer, combined with other references to popular culture or history, and fabricated with modern industrial materials or experimental methods of production. In some ways the global meanderings of Sottsass and Du Pasquier anticipate or accompany the accelerated flow of goods internationally, and increasingly broad awareness of the interconnected nature of design activity, whether to expand commerce, create interdependent supply networks, or address shared awareness of sustainability. Despite museum exhibitions, commissions from companies such as Alessi or Formica to individual designers and groups such as Memphis, and the emergence of small galleries and boutiques that promoted the self-conscious complexities of contemporary experiments in furniture and other household commodities, the majority of furniture and other domestic products in the broad mass market initially owed little to the clever ironies of much Postmodern design. Yet there was increasing evidence in the later 1990s that the forms of many Postmodern designers were reaching a wider market, such as Michael Gravess houseware designs for retail chain Target (see fig. 16.22). Much of this activity demonstrated the belief that Postmodernisms eclectic sources encouraged a healthy receptivity to diversity, novelty, and difference in society, further eroding taste hierarchies in a tolerant, global society. This view, however, was not without its critics. Skepticism toward Postmodernisms inclusiveness was ongoing, and the issue of tolerance remained (and remains, especially in a climate of identity politics, nationalism, and polarized politics) a matter of debate: critic and author Robert Hughes, in Culture of Complaint: The Fraying of America (1993), found little cause for optimism, seeing instead a Balkanization of subcultures in the products of a fragmented culture obsessed with the delineation of difference rather than common ground or shared notions of quality. Marxist critics lamented the absence of art and design that might nurture the seeds of resistance to capitalist hegemony. In Postmodernism, or the Cultural Logic of Late Capitalism (1991), Fredric Jameson defined Postmodernism as a cultural dominant, the integration of a formerly autonomous aesthetic discourse with commodity production. For Jameson the result of such an integration undermined true opposition in political terms and only demonstrated an aesthetic depthlessness, with mass media substituting for direct understanding, producing an experience rich in immediacy and association but essentially superficial and alienating. Meanwhile, a gap in income levels and the standard of living between industrially developed and underdeveloped nations, or racial inequality in the United States, continued to signal, for some, the need for a more thorough commitment to social and ethical responsibility in design on a global scale, a need whose urgency has increased in the twenty-first century. Cultural and technological disparity and inequality remain undercurrents in envisioning the future practice of design: lobbying efforts for Internet access throughout the schools and households of the United States are typical of the ideology of progress, yet ignore the fact that a majority of the population in many underdeveloped countries lacks the basic services many of us take for granted and call into question the meaning or reality of the Canadian educator and philosopher Marshall McLuhans (19111980) global village in the information age. ## Beyond the Object: Expanding Design’s Reach Pressures both from within and outside the design professions led to new, expansive directions in practice, education, and promotion. Global markets spurred businesses to adjust to increased competition and acknowledge more sophisticated, aware consumers who demanded products differentiated less by superficial styling than by a broad range of considerations focusing upon how well products communicated or interacted intuitively with users. A human-centered design began to emerge, including new approaches to product design but also expanding to the design of services and other aspects of business practice, with hierarchical decision-making giving way to collaboration, and user experience as common denominator. In mechanical products, historian Jeffrey Meikle suggested the 1988 Ford Taurus as an example of successful design in the American automobile industry from this period. The Taurus (fig. 15.19) broke with industry practice by having designers, engineers, and marketing departments work together from the outset, ensuring that different points of view were considered strategically during development. Such practice resulted in greater sophistication in styling, and generated excitement for consumers and profits from increased sales for Fords American division. Such an integrated approach has also been cited as an explanation for the success of the Japanese automobile industry, where factory workers are included in discussions of quality and production efficiency. Inclusion gives workers a stake in an expanded design process, encouraging loyalty, responsibility, initiative, and innovation, qualities generally subsumed under the term empowerment. The fact that many contemporary consultancies have group rather than individual names (Pentagram, Smart Design, IDEO, Frog, Droog) also suggests the effectiveness of the paradigm of creative collaboration in design. ![](images/81d2e8106abd62e3ec724e2fbfe235e906310e5ac93bd7f6bba6ef2cf02e222a.jpg) Ford Taurus, sedan or station wagon, Ford Motor Corporation, Dearborn, Michigan, 1988. Design consultancies that emerged in the 1970s, such as Pentagram, with offices first in London and expanding to locations in the United States and Europe, offered a wide range of integrated services to corporate clients that transcended boundaries of product design to include typography, graphic design, and office and retail interiors. This involved regular meetings among partners to present and receive feedback on concepts, in close communication with the client. Other international consultancies, such as Frog Design (founded 1969) offered a similar approach, describing themselves on their 2009 website as a global innovation firm. Miniaturization in the electronics industry (see here) reduced obvious differentiation in terms of styling, leading to increasing homogeneity among similar competitive products. Responding to these circumstances, designers shifted attention toward creating a closer relationship between products and users. Mario Bellinis (b. 1935) design for the Divisumma 18 portable calculator for Olivetti in 1972 used a soft synthetic rubber housing and keypad, enclosing the miniaturized components in a tactile, skinlike shell with rounded edges and a warm color that connects emotionally with consumers (fig. 15.20). This soft, anthropomorphic approach to product design has grown with newer materials and production technologies, and has been an essential component of the working methods of contemporary design consultancies. The consultancy IDEO assembled examples of design from the collection of the Cooper-Hewitt National Design Museum for a 2007 exhibition, and Bellinis Divisumma 18 was among the objects selected, demonstrating the consultancys focus upon intuition, inspiration, and empathy. Theorists began to refer to contemporary approaches to design as being soft, implying several related concepts, including the designers manipulation of digital imaging in a more refined and flexible interactive process, or emphasizing the interface between users, products, and services. Soft design is linked as well to software and to the information age, that is, the virtual instructions that run our computer programs; and soft may refer to increasingly and pliable user-friendly materials, or to the complex layering and sensory richness that characterizes the work of a number of contemporary graphic designers. ![](images/e89fffa7585901fdd0fdb5718807318158c20c4ebae1f84e8efc55a916c05eea.jpg) 15.20 Mario Bellini, Divisumma 18 handheld calculator, ABS plastic, melamine, and rubber, 1 <sup>7</sup>/ × 10 × 4 <sup>3</sup>/ in (4.8 × 25.4 × 12.1 cm), Olivetti ING, Ivrea, Italy, 1972. User experience not only applied to user-friendly materials, but also to less visible means to foster a natural relationship between human beings and a broad range of mechanical and electronic devices. Engineer and author Donald Norman (see here) considers user-centered design in a number of books such as The Design of Everyday Things, published in 1988. Using a wide range of examples drawn from personal (yet common) experience, Norman describes the often frustrating relationships we have with objects whose increased functionality comes at the expense of transparent, intuitive controls and operation (such as programmable telephones with messaging and retrieval options or portable video cameras and players), and argues for the need to consider user error and physical interaction in designing products that are safer, easier, and more natural to operate. While Bellinis Divisumma 18 remains within the familiar category of product housings and new industrial materials, computer technology introduced new ways of connecting products with users that reach beyond the object. Apple Corporations Macintosh personal computer, introduced in 1984 (fig. 15.21), offered an integrated monitor and hardware that saved space and reduced assembly time. More importantly, the Macintosh design included the companys Graphical User Interface (GUI), which used a small hand-operated mouse that visualized the screen as a desktop and the mouses arrow-shaped cursor as a pointing device permitting access to documents, folders, and programs rather than having to type in a series of instructions, thus reducing learning time for users and removing a barrier to operation. The Macintoshs distinguishing design feature was not just its portable compact form but the way in which the GUI created a direct link between personal computing and the familiar, reassuring physical act of organizing, manipulating, and accessing information visually and spatially, reducing the distance between machines and users and making personal computers, indeed, more personal: an extension and amplification of our human capacities. Touch Screen Interface (TSI), in development since the 1960s but used extensively since the early 2000s for personal laptop computers, smartphones, gaming, and point-of-sale transactions, eliminates the need for pointing devices or cursors to access content or typing messages. The role of design in TSI may best be described as facilitating navigation in the digital realm through touch rather than the intermediary of a mouse or touchpad. ![](images/6e0742b3cae34187e1424b9597caa0ac091c6acb0f571585f25bfb097d66d0e5.jpg) 15.21 Macintosh computer, plastic housing, 13 $^ 5 / _ { 8 } \times 9 ~ ^ { 5 } / _ { 8 } \times 1 0 ~ ^ { 7 } / _ { 8 }$ in $( 3 4 . 5 \times 2 4 . 4 \times 2 7 . 7$ cm), Apple Corporation, Cupertino, California, 1984. The expanding framework for design in the information age is parallel and contemporary with the emerging field of artificial intelligence. Herbert Simon (1916 2001), the Nobel-prize-winning economist and computer scientist, offered an inclusive definition of design in his 1969 book Science of the Artificial: Everyone designs who devises courses of action aimed at changing existing situations into preferred ones. The intellectual activity that produces material artifacts is no different fundamentally from the one that prescribes remedies for a sick patient or the one that devises a new sales plan for a company or a social welfare policy for a state. Design, so construed, is the core of all professional training: it is the principal mark that distinguishes the professions from the sciences. Simons definition encompasses the act of form-giving in industrial design but acknowledges new territories for design activity that emphasize interaction between technology and human beings directed toward an imagined future, with information and users as the common denominators in such activity. Simon also raises the possibility that the designed (or artificial) world becomes less and less distinguishable from nature itself as design draws humans and machines closer together. We commonly refer to our handheld phones as being smart: they are extensions of ourselves, responding to our desire for information and expanding the range of our possibilities for future action, whether finding our way to the nearest gas station or letting a friend know we are running late. Certainly the design of prosthetic limbs to enable mobility and independence exemplifies the sophistication and possibilities of smart design, along with wayfinding technology that leads to the promise of self-driving vehicles. Simon expressed his vision in a spirit of optimism and confidence in innovation and progress with an expanded range of possibilities for areas of preferred results. Others have voiced misgivings, raising doubts as to whether the promise of advanced information technology is benevolent and humanizing or dangerous and even dystopian, especially in relation to the use or misuse of surveillance technology and increased role of social media as a source for the dissemination of information. Just as the industrial age unleashed a mixture of hope as well as fear, so has the information age produced its share of advocates for the benefits of AI as well as skeptics who reflect upon its limits and dangers  prefigured over four decades ago in the 1982 film Blade Runner, directed by Ridley Scott. As designers engage with fields beyond products and services, their efforts become less specialized but also more diffused, less easy to define or clearly explain. It is perhaps our job as historians to navigate the path from past to present, supplying the context that gives shape to the future and identifying how best to think meaningfully about the expanded role design plays in our lives. ## Chapter 16 # Contemporary Design in Context: Innovation, Responsibility, and Balance Somewhere between shared, increasingly urgent concerns for safety, health, social justice and sustainability, and a democratic embrace of more inclusive and creative innovation in combination with technology that stimulates the seemingly insatiable desire for individual fulfillment through commodity consumption in a competitive global economy, there lies a middle ground that provides hope for the future of design, a balance between the permanent and ephemeral, between nature and the artificial, between individual and society, between creative freedom and ethics, between what design has been and what it may be. The shape of that future will depend upon the manner in which a number of competing and even conflicting viewpoints and approaches to the rapidly expanding field of design develop and also upon the degree to which such viewpoints and practices may be reconciled, regulated, and shaped. Designs future includes the increasingly dynamic role of technology and production and the political and economic circumstances (local and global) in which they develop, consumption and commercialism, craft and aesthetics, as well as the ability of design to shape not only our products and services, but also our global societys individual, social, and environmental values. Each of these considerations is increasingly interwoven and interdependent in a synthesis that is certainly challenging and yet may remain vital, enriching, and optimistic. With the increasing reach of design activity and recognition of the ongoing economic, health, and environmental challenges we face, there is no better time, or perhaps no more urgent time, for designers to exercise their skills. Twenty-First-Century Political, Environmental, and Social Motivations for Design Increased awareness of the effects of global warming, natural disasters, the migration or displacement of populations due to political instability, terrorism, natural disasters, dangers posed by air and water pollution, and the build-up of plastics and other synthetic waste materials, all pose threats as well as opportunities for the design community and the societies whose needs it serves. What is remarkable is the wide variety of broadly defined design efforts that respond to environmental and social concerns, ranging from the futuristic and visionary to the mundane, from new technologies and materials to traditional craft and re-use to meet challenges and stimulate further effort. Such dangers are hardly new, but climate and the environment have assumed renewed importance amid extreme weather conditions and catastrophes, and the reduction of government regulations to protect the environment from commercial exploitation whether in the rivers and streams of the United States or the rainforests of Brazil. Conferences, symposia, the formation of organizations, and the media direct attention to sustainability, disability, safety, and the survival of displaced and Indigenous populations globally. In 2005 the Cooper Hewitt National Design Museum in New York created the position of Curator of Socially Responsible Design to help develop the museums first international (as opposed to national) design triennial exhibition, titled Why Design Now? in 2010. The international scope of Why Design Now? reinforced the importance of global attention to eight ethical themes: Energy, Mobility, Community, Materials, Prosperity, Health, Communication, and Simplicity. While some well-known names in industrial and fashion design appeared (for example, Issey Miyake and Tord Boontje), many projects were community-based. And while some projects were visionary (for instance, a futuristic plan for San Francisco  HydroNet  that included clusters of twisting plant-like, white-and-green, energy-collection structures popping up incongruously to protect the city from the rise in water levels and threat of flooding), the majority were realized objects or well-developed prototypes for production. The definition of design offered at Why Design Now? was understandably inclusive, ranging from industrial design and crafts to graphic and information design, fashion, healthcare, and architecture. Among the exhibitions themes, only one involved aesthetics (simplicity), and even here the projects encompassed compact space- and material-saving forms, flexible components, and easy production or assembly, implying ethical motivations and problem-solving as well as aesthetic purity. There was an effort to include lo-tech and local projects rather than focus solely upon the most recent technological innovations in materials technology. For instance, alongside a lightweight aluminum folding bicycle was the Samarth bicycle trailer, designed in the US for use in India by Radhika Bhalla (b. 1983), made from locally sourced bamboo, rattan, jute, and iron (fig. 16.1). The message here was not only empowerment (that everyone can make a difference through design), but also that design innovation can be compatible with local practices and existing patterns of living. In addition to state-of-the-art solar panels, other projects included food packaging (AgroResin) made from biodegradable plant fibers such as wheat, rice, and corn stalks. The compatibility of ethical design and existing commercial practice related to the exhibitions themes of prosperity and communication. Rather than the polemical, oppositional tone found in the writings of Victor Papanek (see here), Why Design Now? displayed Amazons Kindle tablet reader (fig. 16.2), noting the savings in paper (and recycling costs) along with the typeface, digital ink, and reflective illumination (like paper and unlike a typical computer screen) for a comforting interface that reduces eye fatigue. ![](images/4c85be60c61df6bd528c95c38bfe79f55eb60d88c9476a876336b9fad924f2ee.jpg) 16.1 Radhika Bhalla, Samarth bicycle trailer, locally sourced bamboo, rattan, iron, jute, coconut fiber, wheels, USA/India, 2008. ![](images/eb07a9570a513ef5f1ff4fe33592f5422621b70f35c0eaf1e03b3ae9474fcc58.jpg) Amazon Kindle tablet Reader, plastic, acrylic, aluminum, E Ink®, 6.7 × 4.6 × 0.36 in (17 × 11.7 × 0.9 cm), 2009. The Cooper Hewitts 2016 By the People: Designing a Better America exhibition was national in scope and focused upon community-based design efforts to address poverty, homelessness, responses to natural disasters that often affect the poor, urban redevelopment, voting rights, and even a redesign for a cheerful, welcoming waiting room for parolees, all accompanied by wall texts and an introductory documentary film chronicling the almost 50 million Americans living below the poverty line, the historical loss of manufacturing jobs, the elimination of government support for low-cost social housing (1973), and the devastating effects on low-income Americans caused by natural disasters such as Hurricane Katrina in 2005. While projects were limited to the United States, By the People embraced and addressed with concrete examples the Millennium Development Goals of the United Nations from the year 2000, which included reducing child mortality, improving maternal health, ensuring environmental sustainability, and promoting gender equality. Akin in its DIY approach to Stewart Brands Whole Earth Catalog (see here and fig. 14.21), but more community- rather than individually based, By the People brought socially responsible design from the margins of design history closer to a more inclusive mainstream understanding of design activity, extending beyond the counterculture into the subject of museum exhibition or media treatment of design. That an holistic approach to sustainability includes public awareness and a participatory process is also demonstrated by efforts in South Korea, where 95 percent of food waste is recycled. The program began at the grassroots level with neighborhood organizations working independently, and became nationwide with government investment and support. The program includes mechanical waste bins for depositing food waste, which is separated and reprocessed to manufacture animal feed, compost, and biofuel. Citizens separate their familys food waste into biodegradable bags (including used cooking oil) for deposit into the bins, which weigh the bags, and residents are charged a nominal monthly fee automatically. The program has reduced the amount of food waste per family along with South Koreas carbon footprint. The system is a paradigm for an integrated design effort that involves an awareness of the shared ethical values and responsibilities of citizens and governments, and the use of smart technology to manage and facilitate operation, resulting in economic and social benefit. Other twenty-first-century catalysts for ethically responsible design have been global climate change and refugee crises. In 2018 the theme and title of the Design History Societys annual conference (held in New York at the Parsons School of Design) was Design and Displacement, and in 2017 the Museum of Modern Art featured an exhibition entitled Insecurities: Tracing Displacement and Shelter, whose introductory text stated: For over 60 million persons in the world today, shelter is defined through constant movement or escape. Bringing together projects by architects, designers, and artists, working in a range of mediums and scales, that respond to the complex circumstances brought about by forced displacement, the exhibition focuses on conditions that disrupt conventional images of the built environment. In addition to urgent responses to violence or catastrophe, longer-range design thinking and initiatives address climate change by proposing a circular model, one in which recycling lies at the middle rather than at the margins of possible options. Circular design seeks to redirect the linear trajectory for designed goods that proceeds from making and buying to disposal. Instead, manufacturers are encouraged to design and produce goods that consider the sustainability of the materials they use, including waste materials, think in terms of products that might be repaired, components re-used in successive versions of personal electronics, or shared rather than owned. The success of circular design also depends upon shifting consumer attitudes toward buying secondhand items, thinking beyond products and services toward wider economic systems that make sense economically as well as ethically. Examples include Thomas Leechs Shoey Shoes, a line of childrens shoes using discarded leather and based upon the premise that children grow out of their shoes approximately every three months, resulting in a tremendous amounts of waste that ends up in landfill (fig. 16.4). Interestingly, while circular design may be a novel term and designers have received funding support from foundations, many of the ideas it proposes have historical precedent. Some, such as the ChargePoint Networked Charging Station, and on display at the Cooper Hewitts 2012 Why Design Now? triennial, were driven by environmental considerations, while others were motivated by economic rather than overtly ethical concerns: the parts of the Model T Ford could be easily replaced because the car remained relatively unchanged for well over a decade (see here); and at the height of the Cold War Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev proposed fleets of staterather than individually owned automobiles to meet the needs of Soviet citizens, and ride-sharing today offers an alternative to personal car ownership. Despite what appears to be a combination of common and economic sense, changing corporate and consumer practices remains a challenge: since its debut in 2007 the Apple iPhone has gone through many iterations with considerable media fanfare, but without buyers being able to upgrade functions without purchasing the newest release. 16.4 ![](images/d5036fdd8d2b6e21f3656e38b7c5a393f7a9310e32e70cbf2ef52159bec5c841.jpg) Thomas Leech, “Shoey Shoes,” display of children’s shoes made from leather waste from the fashion industry, 2016. While not specially directed toward climate change, the Cooper Hewitts triennial exhibition, titled Nature (20192020), included displays dealing with how we view our relationship with nature and the environment, using the study of nature to inspire and direct design activity. One example is a collaboration between French aircraft manufacturer Airbus, architectural firm The Living, Autodesk software company (both based in the US), and German-based AP Works fabrication and 3-D printing company, to produce the partition that separates an aircraft cabin from its galley within very specific airline-industry safety requirements. Designing for disability constitutes another area of socially responsible awareness in the expanding design community. In the United States, the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) of 1990 prohibits discrimination against persons with disabilities, adding momentum to rising interest and legislative directive in providing access to buildings and services for that population as a civil right, whether motivated by a belief in social welfare and the collective value of a caring society or by a desire to enable people to function independently in the world. Italian custom design company Snaidero designed a wheelchair-adaptive kitchen, with easy access to sinks and storage using indented rather than right-angled counter surfaces, with resulting circular areas used for swiveling storage (fig. 16.8). ![](images/60811aef9358702b26abd2931d398df51e15797fcd230a7b70552fadcef3f00a.jpg) Marketed successfully beyond people with disabilities are the soft, rubber-handled kitchen utensils created to require less effort to hold and grip and manufactured by the OXO corporation, founded in 1990 by houseware-industry entrepreneur Sam Farber (19242013), with the consultancy Smart Design (fig. 16.9). Farber designed his Good Grips peeler for his wife, who suffered from arthritis that made it difficult for her to hold the traditional standardized stainless-steel kitchen peeler without pain. While they were initially designed to address the needs of specific populations, the wider public appeal of OXO utensils addresses how products communicate with different groups of users and satisfy needs beyond those originally targeted. Not surprisingly, the redesign of a comfortable and appealing (forgive the pun) kitchen utensil, found in the displays of retail chain stores, exemplifies this approach and the added value Universal Design brings to our daily lives. That environmentally and socially motivated design has become compatible with rather than opposed to a consumer-based economy (both-and rather than either-or) is also seen in products promoting healthy living and ethically conscious shopping. Nigel Whitely notes, for instance, that the Body Shop stores equate the purchase of their cosmetics with promoting diversity in procuring ingredients for their products and adopting sustainable guidelines for packaging. The Body Shop was one of the first companies to devote a link on their website to sustainability and trademarked the phrase Enrich not Exploit. Their promotional material asserts that The world is our source of beauty, but its facing devastation. We actively help enrich the biodiversity where we grow our ingredients. We act and campaign to enrich and support threatened areas of outstanding natural value to the planet. Ethical consuming promotes an awareness of the broader consequences of our buying habits, but many critics remain unsatisfied, arguing that the appropriate response to environmental concerns posed by the cosmetics industry lies in curbing consumption, not in making people feel better about the products they buy. Global responsibility is also part of the mission of Starbucks Coffee chain, originating in Seattle, Washington, in 1971 and now operating more than 33,000 stores worldwide (in this books second edition, published in 2010, the number was 17,000). Design communicates the companys mission to support a sustainable environment. Although it claims its cups are recyclable, and aims to preserve elements of original architecture in many of its stores, using natural colors of green and burlap brown, is committed to ethical and responsible growing, and to fair trade in the interest of small-scale independent coffee growers, not all of these ideals are practical. Attaching ethical values to a product through design also helps to offset the impersonal nature of big businesses in comparison and in competition with locally owned shops offering similar items or services. Like circular design, other sustainable design efforts encourage designers to address the afterlife of manufactured products, whether using recycled materials in the production process or looking for plant and other substitutes for unsustainable materials. Israeli-American designer Neri Oxman (b. 1976) has spent her career developing biodegradable alternatives to plastics, only roughly 10 percent of the more than 300 tons of which are recycled globally each year. Her imaginative research hints at a (albeit more distant) future in which products might age and decay naturally without harming the environment. The interconnected political and environmental crises of the twenty-first century have motivated many in the design community and the community at large to address social and ecological responsibility in a variety of ways. Efforts are increasingly global: widespread migration, whether from Syria toward Eastern Europe, from North Africa toward Italy and elsewhere in Western Europe, or from Central America to the United States, is indeed an interconnected challenge, in which expansive, interdisciplinary design and global thinking play active roles. Yet the challenge is complicated politically, producing resistance amid widespread fears of the loss of national identity and flamed by autocratic leadership and protectionist policies that undermine a pluralism that embraces difference, tolerance, and the hope of mutual understanding and benefit. ## Peering into Possible Futures Neri Oxmans research was included in the 2019 to 2020 Designs for Different Futures exhibition at the Philadelphia Museum of Art, where many displays seemed to belong more in a museum of science than in an art gallery. The expansion of design into fields as diverse as food, shelter, biomimetic materials, personal cosmetics, robotics, and surveillance is as clear an indication as any of the growing territory design now shares with engineers, chemists, biologists, and computer and climate scientists. As design becomes more collaborative and interdisciplinary, so the paradigm of the individual designer/creator, and with it the connection to fine art, fashion, or designer household products and furnishings, becomes but one way (rather than the primary method) of understanding how designers function in society. Designs for Different Futures focused less on consumer goods and services than upon experimental and rhetorical projects and displays, encouraging us to think rather than to buy, seeking to increase awareness that might influence behavior. Catalog essays and wall texts suggested that looking far ahead rather than at the next seasons fashions yielded the realization that present global levels of production and population growth, resulting carbon emissions, and natural resource depletion are environmentally unsustainable, and that many of todays (and tomorrows) designers should address how best to prepare in the long term for the threat of extinction, looking to develop a closer relationship with nature as a guide to sustaining our planet and directing efforts to improve the human condition. The curators noted that our unbridled faith in the success of the traditional paradigm of progress measured in relation to industrial technology, rising production, and consumption as presented at Worlds Fairs beginning in the mid-nineteenth century, has been shaken. Not surprisingly, the authors reminded readers of Victor Papaneks (see here and fig. 14.20) call to arms to designers in light of global conditions, but the tone as well as some of the displays in Designs for Different Futures were less polemical and generally more tolerant of technology and of consumer culture, inclusive of but not confined to radical change. As noted, displays included Neri Oxmans material explorations (see fig. 16.10), vases made from natural rather than synthetic polymers such as shellac, hi-tech wheelchairs that can climb or descend stairs, lifelike three-dimensional and virtual robots that explore the margins of intimacy, or mechanical robot arms to bottle-feed babies as prototypes for future living. Possibilities range from mutual-help groups to care communities, from smallscale distributed production to local food networks, and from community gardens to new forms of communal living. With the word different in the title and the plural rather than singular futures, the exhibitions organizers incorporated design for people with disabilities, signifying inclusion of populations marginalized from activities that enrich their, and our, lives. While the Cooper Hewitts Nature exhibition was in its final days, and the Design for Different Futures was en route to Chicago from Philadelphia, the coronavirus pandemic (COVID-19) forced schools, restaurants, and museums worldwide to close, changing patterns of life for millions in a matter of days, prohibiting the freedom of travel and movement many of us take for granted. Early responses to the virus involved containment, testing, and an urgent need for critical medical equipment, along with support for vaccine research. In a polarized political climate in the United States and globally, media attention focused upon preparedness and balancing concerns for overall health with troubling economic fallout. Neither politicians, nor health officials, nor journalists used the word design very often, and yet designers played an active role in addressing the crisis. Perhaps most visibly, public awareness was critical in communicating in real time the spread of the virus and providing information to alert the public and suggest the importance of social distancing, hand washing, and protection provided by wearing masks in public. Newspapers printed instructions for making your own masks at home, and those with time and a sewing machine used their skills to make masks for themselves, their families, neighbors, or local hospitals in a spirit of community-mindedness to combat fear and isolation. Messaging depended in large degree upon graphs and color-coded maps that charted the viruss spread, hotspots, numbers of people affected, and mortality rates locally, nationally, and internationally. Interactive information design, including myriad phone apps, was an essential communication tool, and graphic designers drew, consciously or not, upon decades of design history to create effective means to organize timelines and statistics clearly and hierarchically, promoting comprehension and guiding reader behavior, whether for testing or the need to stay at home or to take other protective measures. In Britain, newspapers reported that James Dyson (see here) had designed a ventilator that could be quickly manufactured, addressing a shortage for critically ill patients in intensive-care units of hospitals. Other manufacturers, including an Italian company that re-tooled its scuba mask as a Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) device in lieu of ventilators that were in short supply, offered solutions demonstrating a sense of shared responsibility in the face of crisis. However, such efforts also required tutorials to train medical staff to operate the equipment effectively, in short, proving the need for coordinating design at many levels, involving engineering and the interface between machines and their users. Temporary shelter, mobile ICUs, sanitation, and air-filtration products constitute another area of health-related design whose importance grew in response to the pandemic. ## Contemporary Design and Economic Growth As environmentally conscious design efforts have become more numerous, more varied, and more persistent in the twenty-first century, so economists have begun to question the paradigm of associating progress with growth in production and consumer spending (as measured, for instance, by gross domestic product, GDP), at least in more industrially developed nations where consumption not only exacerbates environmentally related problems, but also may result in greater income disparity and attending social ills (see here). Nevertheless, new or redesigned products and furnishings, stimulating increased levels of production and advertising to spur consumption in the retail market, endure at, or close to, the center of our general public understanding of the design enterprise, with consumer spending and attending job growth, both national and international, remaining for business leaders and politicians a prime indicator of employment and economic health, with communication design and software related to e-commerce accelerating the flow of goods domestically and globally. The gross domestic product of the United States comprises of business and government expenditure, exports, and personal consumption expenditures for goods and services. Design operates in each of these categories, but personal consumption constitutes 70 percent of the US GDP. Over the past 70 years, there has been a shift from expenditure on material goods to services such as healthcare and childcare, both of which include considerable investment in products and the design of interior spaces (see here on IDEO): in 1950 spending on services represented 40 percent of GDP, while in 2018 it was 70 percent, in part the result of higher levels of efficient manufacture in goods-producing industries that reduces labor investment, steering the design professions as well as consumer spending toward the expansion of services. ![](images/42bf594d6a12acd3dfb9f531b65736b44b4cd5e3455a3307a5fdc103060f57ae.jpg) Minotti, Lawrence Seating System, Rodolfo Dordoni Design. A related, recycled approach to contemporary fashion and other fields of design emerged in the successful career of African American celebrity designer (and trained civil engineer and architect) Virgil Abloh (19802021), who was artistic director of menswear for French fashion house Louis Vuitton as well as head of his own line of streetwear called Off White. Abloh transformed gritty, urban streetwear such as T-shirts, hoodies, and Nike sneakers into collections for young, affluent professionals who disdain conventional office attire. His clothing incorporates eclectic references to history (a collection of tie-dyed windbreakers suggests the counterculture of the 1960s), while a series for Off White features bold diagonal stripes of black and yellow resembling crosswalks or street signs. Social media was among Ablohs sources of inspiration, providing images captured on iPhones and posted to Instagram, a rich and limitless sampling of experience and meaning, extending to interiors and furnishings. Abloh partnered with IKEA, for instance, for one of its furniture lines that includes a rug printed as an IKEA receipt. Drawn to fashion and to shopping as a teenager growing up in Rockford, Illinois, and as a student in Wisconsin and later in Chicago, Abloh admired the success of fashion designers Ralph Lauren and James Jebbia (founder of the skateboarding shop and clothing brand Supreme New York) as minds and spirits that show that clothing can be much more than just the tangible, physical garments for the human body. Race also enters into the meaning of some of Ablohs collections. Although better publicized, Ablohs is but one of countless lesser-known stories of design history hidden beneath racism. At the middle of the retail market for consumer goods stand electronic products such as smart phones and personal assistants as well as furnishings manufactured by large international companies such as IKEA and Japanese retailer Muji. The IKEA Corporation of Sweden, originally founded in 1943, has successfully expanded and marketed a warehouse approach to Nordic modern design in many large European cities as well as (since 1985) in affluent North American suburbs and in Asia. IKEA operates stores in more than 63 countries globally (the largest is in the Philippines). Online catalogs of merchandise, off-the-shelf availability, flat, carry-home packaging, as well as do-it-yourself assembly instructions and tools for fastening standardized bolts and fittings within product lines all contribute to the appeal of IKEA and are integral to the companys design policy, together with the affordability of products ranging from furniture and lighting to fabrics, household, and kitchen wares, and storage systems. Another reason is the idea that the study of design history has grown out of art history, where individual artists (mostly male, European or North American, and white) play an outsized role. Increasingly, however, observing and analyzing the needs and experiences of users has become an acknowledged part of the product and service design process. One example is Dutch architect Rem Koolhaass Seattle Public Library (completed 2004; fig. 16.24). This large steel and glass structure, constructed on an entire city block, lacks the unified profile of much modern architecture; moreover, the design of its interior was based upon suggestions from different groups of users to facilitate a variety of services rather than an over-riding scheme or single idea or system. Relinquishing ownership can be difficult, but critics have applauded the buildings ability to cater to different types of users, and the library has generally been welcomed by Seattle residents, who feel they had a share in its design. Koolhaas has seen a parallel for his approach to design not in traditional urban planning but in the haphazard growth of megacities with populations of upward of ten million such as Lagos (Nigeria) or Mumbai (India). While such chaos conflicts with many of our associations between design and order, Koolhaas finds the fragmented aesthetic energizing and (somehow) navigable for many of those who live and work in such places (less so perhaps for tourists). When the user-centered approach shifts from the realm of product and direct service design to virtual web-based applications or phone apps, the term user can take on other connotations. Mining and analyzing data on user habits (with or without their knowledge) allows companies to chart and to introduce content that maximizes the continued attention of app visitors. As reported by New Yorker staff writer Amy Weiner, the more time visitors spent on a site or an app, the more effective the companys efforts; users became addicts, drawn toward a subtle form of coercion to produce a desired result for marketers. ![](images/d80a747e637d5f54b091d813067f04a3996522954c477124aa55bbe8e2bf3d21.jpg) Rem Koolhaas, interior, Seattle Public Library, Seattle, Washington, 2004. A related approach to contemporary design process is known as product semantics. Designer and critic Guy Julier has described the Dyson electric vacuum cleaner along these lines. The underlying concept of product semantics is the analysis of consumer values and behaviors in order to break down barriers between ourselves and the products or services we use, and may consider purchasing. Even James Dyson himself is part of the product semantics equation  he presents himself as part of the lore of invention itself, appealing to the tinkerer in each of us who has the capacity to question the status quo, think of something better through imagination, patent it, and profit from our insight. Diagrams of the contemporary design process tend to assign equivalent roles to designers, manufacturers, and users/consumers, often using a triangular model with arrows emphasizing communication or feedback loops that acknowledge multiple stakeholders and facilitate dialogue among them involving values and beliefs, marketing, distribution, manufacturing practice, and user research. ## Service Design There is no single formula for success in the expanding design enterprise. Consultancies such as Pentagram rely upon oversight from its various partners who attract customers, while others such as IDEO do not assign credit to individuals. Both approaches engage a broader range of expertise to the user experience not only of products but also to navigate services toward creating preferred experiences. An example of service design addresses wait time for travelers in the screening of luggage in large airports in response to the threat of terrorism in the wake of 9/11. Screening luggage involves the use of cumbersome plastic bins into which travelers place their bags, coats, and personal accessories on a conveyor belt to be scanned by TSA (Transportation Security Administration) personnel before walking through screening metal detectors. Long lines during peak travel times create delays, exacerbated by a shortage of available bins because airport staff have to move them from one end of the screening process to the other while also attending to their other responsibilities. At Londons Heathrow airport, the MacDonald Humfrey Automation design consultancy developed a service system, Mach-Lane, that eliminated such delays: a double-tiered conveyor shuttles plastic bins automatically from the end of the line back to the beginning where travelers access them to fill with their belongings; and it works! ## Artificial Intelligence, Smart, and Autonomous Products Digital technology paved the way for personal computers, including desktop, laptop, and handheld devices. Pioneered by the Intel Company in 1971, the processing of digital information on microchips reduced the mechanical space requirements of earlier computers. Beginning in the mid-1980s, personal computers eclipsed and eventually replaced typewriters, books, the Rolodex, and calculators for business, research, and home use. With the growth of the Internet, computers have also vastly expanded the possibilities of information and data processing as well as banking and business transactions. Like many contemporary electronic products, phones have become mobile resources of information tailored to our needs: appendages rather than accessories. They are not only personalized through the apps we choose to download, but also quite literally become an extension of ourselves, a generally well-meaning but not terribly invasive shadow-self or even better self. For instance, my phone tells me how many steps I take in a day, what my step goal is in relation to my age and weight, the number of calories I have burned; it congratulates me when I reach my goal, and gently chides me to get up and do something when I fall short. As such, smart design means a level of interaction between machines (the artificial, human-made world) and the living, breathing world of nature. In addition to phones and personal assistants like Amazons Echo or Alexis, designed artifacts provide us with information, save time, make us more efficient or productive, and move toward a seamless interface between human and machine worlds, mediated by buttons, by touch, or by voice. Apples watch (2015) transferred functionality from the hand to the wrist, maintaining its association with the milestone iPhone but acknowledging that the further miniaturization of its hardware and the dexterity required to access its functionality might be limited to more tech-savvy users: Apple sold 2.4 million watches in the second quarter of 2015, while 75 million iPhones were sold in a single quarter of 2014. The design of such products is complex, involving teams of individuals with diverse backgrounds in product design, engineering, biology, anthropology, ethnography, marketing, service design, and data management, with the boundaries among such fields increasingly fluid and interconnected. With its expansion into software as well as hardware design, Apple operates in a horizontal way at all levels of production development, a voice in decisions from idea to final product. Once the stuff of science fiction, the idea of self-driving cars is now becoming a reality, and similar to nineteenth-century reactions to mechanization, gives rise to fears as well as hopes. For some designers as well as writers and theorists, the key to the design of robotic devices is reducing barriers to our interaction with them. The term uncanny valley refers to robots whose resemblance to human beings is so seamless that we feel threatened and repelled rather than comfortable interacting with them, impeding rather than encouraging interaction. For critics, artificial intelligence poses a threat to workers, for instance to the millions of people employed as truck drivers, cabbies, Uber and Lyft drivers who fear losing their livelihood with the advent of selfdriving vehicles that no longer require the service they provide, or operators who watch machines do the tasks they used to perform manually and from which they derived satisfaction. Indeed, the history of design demonstrates that new technologies entail risks as well as advantages whose results may not in many cases be predicted; an improvement may benefit some but not others, and designers may embrace or attempt to reconcile the viewpoints of multiple constituencies or stakeholders. ## Materials Technology and Softness New synthetic materials and new uses for older industrial materials also continue to blur boundaries between the natural and artificial worlds. Part of this criticism is at the structural level; that is, the interactive digital technology that powers our iPhones, guides us with reasonable confidence by global positioning technology to unfamiliar places, and allows us to program and personalize our digital devices, may also be used for unauthorized surveillance, the dissemination of disinformation, or remote, destructive military strikes. Such uses raise questions about the risks that technology poses to safety, and make us think about whether the progress we associate with information in cyberspace does in fact mask more dangerous relationships between political authority and those businesses that design and provide information technology. It is hardly surprising that skepticism about the information age was stimulated by global terrorism and responses to the attacks of September 11, 2001, when the Pentagon and World Trade Center towers became powerful symbols and targets for destruction. While advocates of Postmodernism optimistically connected pluralism with tolerance and an embrace of difference, 9/11 and the war on terror introduced a renewed and destructive ideological and political dualism that appears to have grown rather than subsided. The media presentation of the World Trade Center attacks elicited strong patriotic feeling in the United States and a shared sense of sympathy for human suffering worldwide. At the same time, subsequent global events, such as the Arab Spring (20102011) and refugee crises, stirred further, often hostile nationalist fervor and civil war. The relationship of these political events to the history of modern design is at times direct, for instance in the development of devices to improve personal security or respond promptly to victims of violence and displacement. Indirectly, politics affects international relations, for example, taking the form of protective trade policies that disrupt supply chains or impose tariffs and threaten free trade and the interdependence and sharing of information that maintains the design enterprise. ## Graphic Design in the Digital Age Digital technology has made a major impact upon the practice of graphic design. As the varied methods of graphic designers extend into the virtual space of the computer screen, user experience shifts from turning pages or unfolding pamphlets to triggering links by click or by touch to reveal multiple windows filled with content that is seen, read, and heard, often simultaneously. The result is inherently more robust and interactive than traditional print media, riveting and distracting all at once. While adapting to the new media seems almost natural for younger generations who grew up with personal computers, for older populations the interface can be challenging. The rapidly evolving technologies that make such new explorations possible provide designers with flexibility and choices beyond those of photomechanical processes and allow them to produce typography and manipulate images with increased speed and an expanding range of possibilities. Graphic processing and digital manipulation provide opportunities for designers to unite photography, filmmaking, and more traditional typography, illustration, and layout. While the term digital media is sometimes used to refer to such activities, the degree of overlap with graphic design in undergraduate education and in practice makes discipline-based thinking appear narrow and out of touch with constantly evolving graphic design industry practice. Such media convergence was at the root of the Media Lab developed at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology beginning in 1984, which continues to explore interrelationships and interactivity in the digital realm (on the Mediated Matters group at the Media Lab, see here). At the same time, the technologies that equip the designer with new means for communicating information require an ability to balance versatility with specialized technical skills, resulting in challenges for traditional discipline-based university curricula and professional practice alike that yield discussion and an array of future educational models. Using computers to generate digital type and manipulate digital images for graphic presentation began in 1984 with the operating system and user interface developed by the Apple Corporation for its Macintosh computers. The translation of type and images into electronic code on a low-resolution computer screen was not entirely new. However, creating and controlling those impulses with a computer mouse on a virtual desktop with pull-down windows rather than programming them with typed instructions on a keyboard was unique at that time to the Apple Corporation and catapulted the growth of personal computing. This graphic interface used terms like cut and paste for functions familiar to graphic designers and amateurs alike (developed by computer scientist Larry Tesler, 19462020). Amateurs used the new interface to democratize the printing and graphic design industries through desktop publishing. Manipulating fonts and using a range of effects with the click of a mouse became commonplace, and office assistants could produce and edit reports and other communications with logos, borders, and images, generating a wide variety of materials formerly produced by professional printers and graphic designers. In completing these tasks, amateur designers borrowed from among thousands of symbols and popular graphics (clip art) stored on five-inch compact disks or downloaded from the Web. The Macintosh also appealed to a small group of enthusiastic professional graphic designers, not as a tool to replace existing camera-ready design or printed output, but as a distinct form of communication offering new possibilities for expressing a contemporary sensibility. A pioneer in these explorations was the German-born and trained typesetter and graphic designer Wolfgang Weingart (19412021), who taught typography at the Basel School of Applied Art beginning in the early 1960s. Looking beyond the more limited range of typefaces, grid layouts, and photomechanical techniques associated with Swiss Style (or International Typographic Style), Weingart expanded (and subverted) the techniques of letterpress and offset lithography through complex layering (overlapping) of photographic images and an ambiguous, suggestive use of symbols and letterforms, as seen in his poster announcing a 1984 exhibition of Swiss posters (fig. 16.30). Developments in technology during the 1990s made desktop computers the industry and educational standard for the graphic design profession. Students entering the field began to generate projects using software packages such as QuarkXpress and Adobe Photoshop, and universities replaced drafting tables and light tables (the latter used to cut and paste images and type for photographic reproduction) with computer workstations, scanners, and high-resolution printers. Hand-drawn calligraphy gave way to computer-generated typography on the Macintosh operating system, many of whose features were incorporated into the Windows system, developed by the Microsoft Corporation for IBM and IBM-compatible personal computers. Students began to carry packages of floppy disks (later Flash or keychain devices; now virtual cloud-based storage eliminates the risk of losing or forgetting even a portable device) in addition to (or rather than) toolboxes filled with X-Acto knives and felt-tipped markers, while administrators looked to the computer industry, foundations, and rising tuition fees to help underwrite the tremendous cost of software licensing and workstations. Increasingly, computer interface also permits a great deal of experimentation, emphasizing the playfulness inherent in process rather than forming a shortcut to a predetermined result, as with 3- D printing and rapid prototyping and the flexibility they introduce into mechanical production. Experimentation, tinkering, and intuition often exist in the very nature of the craft process, and even in front of the computer screen. It was fear of the elimination of this aspect of process that aroused the indignation of John Ruskin and William Morris now well over a century ago. Despite the sophistication of an expanded, collaborative design process as practiced in modern consultancies, it is interesting to note that when the forward-looking IDEO design consultancy selected objects from the permanent collection of the Cooper Hewitt National Design Museum for a small exhibition in 2007, the themes of the exhibition were intuition, empathy, and inspiration, rather than, for instance, research, analysis, testing, problem-solving, or even HumanComputer Interaction (HCI). ![](images/c0616f464b0e15e5c2ab1c60a57b80b5877bd3c7666dc228fcdd3695bf756809.jpg) Sally Bowen Prange, Barnacle Teapot, stoneware, silicon carbide, $6 \times 1 1 \times 6 ^ { 1 } / _ { 2 }$ in (15 × 28 × 16 cm), 1991. Design, History, and Common Ground Every day we encounter designs power and its effects upon our daily lives. In 2018 I participated in a relatively small interdisciplinary conference in Philadelphia titled Common Ground. My paper was on a somewhat esoteric subject in the history of type, but throughout the days events and wrap-up I met older and younger scholars from near and far, listening, speaking, asking questions, and sharing knowledge. Throughout my career I have enjoyed being part of such events, yet less than two years later such gatherings were being held remotely, if at all, in response to the spread of coronavirus. Zoom and related remote-conferencing technologies, and the design they entail that facilitates our ability to navigate their platforms and communicate with one another virtually, allowed many of us to function professionally and personally during the health crisis, even to virtually attend conferences, symposia, and webinars that would have otherwise been inaccessible due to travel costs or conflicts in scheduling. As noted above, design also contributed to our understanding of the virus with published charts and graphs (interactive via the Web) that informed our responses through protection and social distancing. And yet remote conferencing, like remote travel, hardly substitutes for direct experience, whether personal or cultural, reminding us of how much we take for granted in our daily lives and of the limits of technologies upon which we depend when things do not, or cannot, go as planned. Crises also increase our awareness of disparities in income and opportunity that limit access and create hierarchies to the acquisition of knowledge. Designs rich and evolving history, and strength, as well as its challenge, lie in its breadth, its interaction with so many sources of inspiration and expanded areas of practice, disciplinary interdependence, and the importance of the cultural context for its activities. The difficulty, perhaps, is that with the promise of expansion comes diffusion and the fear of losing the common ground that connects one aspect of design practice to another, in turn making the expanded notion of design difficult to pin down and to instrumentalize toward desirable, and tangible results. History may provide some understanding and appreciation for such common ground, or at least continuity. Through technology, designers remain in constant contact with new materials and processes that stimulate ideas for original products or transformations in existing ones. Through production, designers work with manufacturers, investors, taking into account markets, costs, and sales. As artists, designers are able to integrate self-expression and experimentation that contribute to the value we place in goods and services. With consumers in mind, designers increasingly work to incorporate human factors, user-friendly interfaces, and appealing forms for different tastes, along with a wide range of personal meanings and interpretations. By observing actions as ordinary as peeling an apple or vacuuming a carpet, designers investigate new possibilities and alternatives to what exists. And since the publication of this books second edition, the historical thread of ethically motivated solutions to shared concerns for safety, sustainability, response to crisis, accessibility, and inclusion, with a long track record of reform efforts motivated by concerns with social justice upon which to build, have been renewed and strengthened, even emboldened through events of the recent past, including climate change, migration, and movements such as #MeToo and #BlackLivesMatter. In todays world, design can be team-based, anonymous, individual, or heroic; ephemeral and self-indulgent, durable, or circular; negligent, hedonistic, or fueled by renewed interest in social and environmental consciousness and commitment. Throughout this history, with its dynamic range of possibilities, designers remain actively engaged with the world of nature, with technology, and with the man-made environment in all their excitement, complexity, responsibility, and ambiguity. While the increasing range and variety of the design enterprise can be overwhelming in its scope, its history helps us to provide some continuity with what design has been with what it is, and with what it might be, revealing relationships that offer a sense of assurance or reassurance and a context for ongoing change and shared discourse. Moreover, design continues to be responsive to all of us who use its myriad and ever-expanding products and services, to all of us whose lives it transforms, hopefully, in the long run, for the better and common good. ## Global Inspiration VI Revisiting China The history of China during the twentieth century included the end of the imperial Qing dynasty (16441911), the founding of the Republic of China (19121949), and a civil war ending with the establishment of the communist Peoples Republic of China (PRC) in 1949. The PRC carried out massive state-controlled economic planning under the leadership of Chairman Mao Zedong (18931976) in two stages: the Great Leap Forward (19581962) and the Cultural Revolution (19661976). Private property was abolished, industries nationalized, and a largely rural population organized collectively into communes under state control. Improvements in infrastructure under centralized control were offset by massive mismanagement of resources and distribution leading to widespread famine between 1958 and 1961. The Cultural Revolution saw continued improvements in transportation and infrastructure, the rejection of Western material products and fashion, and the molding of collective values through education, dress, communication, and devotion to Chairman Mao. In design, the period under Maos leadership is most easily recognized in the standard uniforms worn by party officials and workers, and by heroic propaganda posters promoting individual sacrifice and collective solidarity through work and patriotism. Beginning with United States President Richard Nixons official visit in 1972, China ended its isolation from the West and pursued an Open Door policy that permitted foreign investment in manufacturing, private enterprise, and trade. The period from 1978 (two years after Maos death) to 2000 witnessed massive economic growth, the rise of a middle class, and the integration of China into an increasingly global economy. Measured in GDP, the Chinese economy expanded seven-fold between 1989 and 2019 and thirty-six-fold between 1978 and 2019; in 2022, China was the second largest economy in the world. But what does this mean in relation to design history? Perhaps the most familiar link to design is the outsourcing of labor from other Western industrialized nations to China in the manufacture of consumer goods, creating jobs for Chinese workers and maintaining competitive prices for consumers. However, there are also downsides: the manufacturing sector has hollowed out in other nations (including most notably the USA), and China and its workforce are now suffering environmental and health and safety consequences. Furthermore, a lot of these cheap goods are made by the forced labor of Uyghurs and other ethnic minority groups, in a country often criticized for its human rights practices. Cheap products can come at a very steep price. A related phenomenon is the extraction of raw materials and manufacture of parts for a wide variety of products from electronic devices to clothing accessories that contribute to supply chains in an increasingly interdependent global economy. While we might refer to an iPhone as a product of the United States-based Apple Corporation headquartered in Cupertino, California, the phone is made from hundreds of parts, with Chinese companies playing a large part in the complex process of production, although the Foxconn Corporation that performs much of the iPhones assembly is located in Taipei, on the island of Taiwan. In addition to fashion and fast-food brands (the first McDonalds restaurant opened in mainland China in 1990), IKEA opened its first home goods store in China in 1998, increasing to nine stores by 2012, and 36 in 2021. With rising per capita incomes and a population of more than 1.4 billion in 2022, Chinese citizens participated in an elastic consumer culture, while private enterprise inside the PRC led to Chinese companies competing domestically and internationally to market their products. The open door policy was indeed a revolving door, permitting retail businesses to open stores in Chinese cities, and Chinese entrepreneurs to manufacture and sell their own products worldwide. Fashion and product designers in China today are part of an international contemporary design scene for the luxury market, merging elements of traditional Chinese imagery with experimental materials, or traditional materials such as porcelain or lacquer with an abstract or Postmodern aesthetic. An example is figure GI6.2, a highly polished stainless steel stool or coffee table designed by Shi Jianmin (b. 1962) whose organic form recalls the rock formations and rock sculptures found in Chinese gardens and public spaces, contrasting here the irregularity of natural materials with the smooth steel finish. One might also consider design in modern China in relation to soft power, the term that describes how design functioned during the Cold War to promote individual and social values on both sides of the Iron Curtain. Hosting the Summer Olympic Games in Beijing in 2008, the 2010 World Exhibition in Shanghai, and the Winter Olympic Games in 2022 provided the stage for presenting or constructing the image of modern China. The events drew upon a long and active national tradition of spectacular display and pyrotechnics for visually exciting celebrations. The summer Olympic Stadium, known popularly as the Birds Nest, was built by a team of Swiss architects, with little if any direct reference to Chinese identity. The Chinese Pavilion at the 2010 World Exhibition, designed by Chinese architect He Jingtang (b. 1938; the pavilion is now a museum), is an inverted pyramid exemplifying sustainability through natural ventilation, thermal panels, and solar energy (fig. GI6.3). Its traditional Chinese deep red color and supporting interlocking bracket construction (known as Dougong) navigate elements of a national identity within a contemporary sustainable global context. An anonymous exhibition poster from 2000 also exemplifies the results of Chinas opendoor policy: the poster advertises an exhibition of Danish modern design (The Danish Wave +) that toured three of Chinas largest cities (Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Beijing  the average population of the three cities is 20 million) and was sponsored by the Chinese Ministry of Culture, the Royal Danish Embassy in China, and other cultural institutions from both countries. The material culture of modern China is both a product of and a contributor to the globalization that characterizes the later twentieth and our current twenty-first centuries and that frames the study of design in our time. Communication and interdependence provide for the exchange of ideas, technologies, and materials that propel innovation and growth. In some areas of design the results create a degree of homogeneity, for instance in smart phones or laptops, recognized designer clothing and accessory brands encountered in high-end shopping malls, fashion districts, and tourist destinations worldwide. There are other aspects of the design enterprise that relate to China alone. For instance, the governments use of artificial intelligence for facial recognition in order to identify and oversee citizens behavior, or the contrast between the use of environmentally responsible building practice for the 2010 China Pavilion in Shanghai and the air pollution and congestion that has choked the city of Beijing intermittently over the past ten years. With stronger centralized political control and decision making in fewer hands, the future of design activity appears in flux, and it remains to be seen whether industrialization and increased production will stimulate ethical alternatives to the standard equation of progress with growth.
