Art Chantry has been called the "poster boy of poster art,"'7 since perhaps his most provocative and attention-getting work has been in the form of posters, which he has been making since he was a teenager. Most closely associated with the underground music and theater scene in Seattle, his work reflects a variety of influences ranging from outsider art to comic books, psychedelic culture and avant-garde European design. Born in Seattle and raised in Tacoma, Washington. Chantry received his bachelor's degree from Western Washington University in Bellingham, graduating in i978, and returned to Seattle, which was then the center of a burgeoning subculture of punk rock music and avant-garde theater. Though his degree was in painting. he rejected fine art in favor of graphics, building a reputation in the i98os with his album covers and posters for Washington-based record companies Sub Pop and Estrus as well as desięns for books and publications like the Rocket, a newspaper covering the local music scene. For the latter, where he was art director in i985, his pages had a chaotic look that belied their meticulous planning, and for one issue he moved the masthead from its usual position, turning it upside down at the foot of the page. Over a career of more than forty years, he has studiously avoided the mainstream, in his techniques as well as his design. While many of his contemporaries use computers in tandem with low-tech tools, and others have adopted the computer exclusively, Chantry does neither. He rejects computers altogether in favor of an entirely hands-on approach with old-fashioned studio tools and techniques. Often, his work does not seem "designed," but simply assembled from an assortment of unrelated elements brought together in chance encounters. That is far from the case, and the precisely planned results are always original, often provocative, and sometimes disturbing in their in-your-face assertiveness. He puts his designs together by assemblage and collage, combining type, photos, drawings, and colors to make a statement that suits the project, and the client. ![](images/64112278520c89e941538e63a7d5a64536c531a03e838ac917ec53d44814d746.jpg) ![](images/fbd28783e29be9e6ab2e6bca2bf71e9457020dd0f728e80f12a1746d7eb0de79.jpg) Though his work is not definably postmodern in style, he has embraced the designation, believing that all designers are essentially postmodernists in that they appropriate ideas from the past: "We put those ideas into new contexts and juxtapositions to create new ideas...none of the ideas we toss around are new."18 Chantry refuses to equate his profession with fine art; indeed, he believes that design is more influential, more powerful, and ultimately more dangerous in its ability to manipulate consumers. A provocative spokesman for his profession, he has written and lectured extensively about his ideas. His career was documented in a monograph, Some People Can't Surf: The Graphic Design of Art Chantry (2ooi),'9 and his often controversial opinions are captured in Art Chantry Speaks: A Heretic's History of 20th Century Graphic Design (2015).
