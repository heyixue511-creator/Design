# CHAPTER 11 # Celebrity and “Good Design” Raymond Loewy's fascination with automotive design extended into his private life. In 1946 he took two 1940 Lincoln Continentals and custom redesigned them for himself and a friend. The result was a modern version of the traditional brougham style car body in black with the front seat fully visible beneath a Plexiglas roof, as if for a chauffeur, and an enclosed cabin with round opera side windows for passengers in the rear seat. The custom hood ornament was a Frigidaire door handle. Loewy often used the car for the showmanship that was his trademark. One of Loewy's student apprentices at the time, Budd Steinhilber, relates the time when he was asked to take Loewy's leather presentation portfolio down to a parking garage on 6th Avenue, and put it in a designated Ford convertible. Loewy was scheduled to make a presentation in Hartford, Connecticut. In the meantime, Loewy's personal chauffer was instructed to drive in from Long Island in his customized Lincoln Continental, and park it at curbside in front of the office building on Fifth Avenue. Large crowds began to gather, and soon Loewy, his wife, and her French poodle emerged ceremoniously from the building, stepped grandly into the Lincoln and drove off accompanied by the rumble of its 12-cylinder engine. However, they only drove around the corner and down 46th street to the parking garage where the Loewy's then transferred to the rental Ford convertible. The chauffer was dispatched back to Oyster Bay. Pure and simple theater!¹ Meanwhile, Loewy's office in South Bend had been busy during the war with the first newly designed postwar cars for the Studebaker Corporation, introduced in May 1946. Recall that all automotive companies were required to devote all their manufacturing capability to produce military equipment during the war and to suspend work on consumer goods. General Motors and Chrysler made M-4 Sherman tanks at the rate of 2,000 per month. Ford produced 18,000 B-24 Consolidated Aircraft Liberator bombers. Plymouth made Bofors antiaircraft guns and aircraft landing gears. Packard made V-12 engines for PT boats and aircraft. Willys-Overland, Bantam, and Ford made Jeeps. Studebaker made trucks, aircraft engines, and personnel carriers. After the war, a few prewar-designed cars, such as Packard, Hudson, and Nash, were quickly introduced in 1946, using old tooling, and with only minor changes. Norman Bel Geddes designed a new grille and dashboard for the prewar Nash. Willys-Overland began production in 1946 of civilian jeeps using military Jeep tooling, the Model 463, 4WD, Jeep station wagon, designed by Milwaukee industrial designer Brooks Stevens to look like prewar “woodies." But Loewy's office in South Bend, being independent of the Studebaker Corporation, was exempt from the war production restrictions of the automotive companies, and thus, starting in 1939, it was able to begin to develop Studebaker's postwar, 1947 models of the Champion, Commander, and Landcruiser during the war. They were designed by Virgil Exner, head of Loewy's studio in South Bend, who was hired by Loewy in 1937, and Robert E. Bourke, who was hired by Exner in 1940, both members of Loewy's Studebaker team. But Loewy had fired Exner in 1944 for conspiring with Roy Cole, Studebaker vice president of engineering, to bypass Loewy. Exner went to work as an employee of Studebaker under Cole, and Loewy replaced him with Gordon Buehrig, who had been hired as head of Loewy's Studebaker studio. Since he left Auburn in 1936, Buehrig had served with the Budd Company until 1938, after which he had worked as a freelance designer. Although an excellent designer, Buehrig was a shy and unassuming person and simply did not have the take-charge disposition needed to manage the studio. Bourke was soon put in charge, and both worked on the 1947 models, which were ready for tooling as soon as the war was over. When they were introduced in late 1946, design professionals praised the innovative design, with its distinctive wraparound, "greenhouse" rear windows on two-door models; its low-slung, flush side styling; and the flat-back trunk instead of the traditional tapered look of the time. The trunk prompted a joke at the time that one could not tell if the car was coming or going. But it was new and quite different from any Detroit models. Eliot Noyes said it was "so refreshing it made other cars look out of date." Life magazine published a ten-page article on Loewy, Studebaker, and its new models. Over the next three years, Studebaker would break all its peacetime records for sales and profits. ![](images/7f0fd3285aca7b5f2fba96e1c337969d495d6ff099398d627abf71e2c35218b3.jpg) 1947 Studebaker, by Raymond Loewy Associates. Other design activities in 1947 included work by Raymond Loewy Associates (RLA) in its New York office. As consultant to Coca-Cola, RLA designed the red, streamlined dispensers made by Dole Deluxe that sat on soda fountain counters across the country, the work of Carl Otto and Clare Hodgman of Loewy's office. RLA also slimmed down the hourglass shape of Coca-Cola's iconic bottle and designed open-sided delivery trucks to expedite stock delivery and handling. Tucker Madawick joined RLA in 1947 and with Carl Otto reopened Loewy's London office on Grosvenor Street, which had closed down in 1940 as the bombing of Britain began. Madawick handled the accounts of Electrolux of Sweden, Austin of England, Unilever, Gestetner, Lyon's Tea House, the Rootes Group, and Allied Ironfounders. In Chicago, John Morgan took on Mel Boldt as a partner in his consulting office, and Dave Chapman designed streamlined sewing machines for Montgomery Ward. Lurelle Guild was engaged by Alcoa to design hardware/aluminum furniture, which included chairs, tables, consoles, vanity desks, bed headboards, nightstands, and chests. Alcoa sold over 400,000 Kensington and Wear-Ever chairs before discontinuing the line in 1952. Guild had a long list of clients and was so well known that many manufacturers featured his name and photograph on marketing materials. In New York, Norman Bel Geddes had hired Elliot Noyes, who had returned from military service after the war, to be his design director. The office designed a secretarial desk for International Business Machines (IBM), a cigarette vending machine for Rowe Manufacturing Corporation, and a jukebox for the Lion Manufacturing Company. Bel Geddes was soon commissioned by IBM to design a new typewriter and had started working on it, but Bel Geddes's office closed in 1947 due to financial mismanagement. Noyes opened his own office that same year and completed the typewriter design started under Bel Geddes, which would be introduced in 1948 as IBM's Model A. Thomas J. Watson, Jr., president of IBM and a glider pilot friend of Noyes, retained him for product design at IBM. Nathan George Horwitt designed a wristwatch in 1947 with a round, black face without numerals, symbols, or lines to mark hours or minutes, and with only a single gold dot marking the twelve-o-clock position, intended to be evocative of a sundial. Vacheron & Constantin-Le Coultre, a Switzerland firm, originally manufactured it. But Zenith Movado would proceed to produce the exact same design in 1948, without any credit or compensation to Horwitt. A prime example of design piracy, it would take 27 years in the courts until Movado finally settled with Horwitt for \$29,000 in 1975. The watch would be included in the permanent design collection of the Museum of Modern Art in 1960 and become known as “the Museum Watch." A wall clock of the classic Horwitt design was produced in 1960 by the Howard Miller Clock Company and is still being produced today. In June 1947, the Chicago, Milwaukee, St. Paul and Pacific Railroad introduced its streamlined flagship, the Olympian Hiawatha, which traveled from Chicago to Seattle/Tacoma, Washington. Brooks Stevens had designed it from stem to stern. The dramatic orange and maroon train was pulled by a massive Fairbanks-Morse diesel engine topped by a high, forward cab with automotive/aircraft-type split windshield, and a nose like a vertical cylinder with chrome horizontal bands. Many described the Skytop solarium observation cars as the most distinctive railway cars ever built. The rear lounge had glass windows, not just at eye level, but also in three tiers overhead like a greenhouse, to afford an uninterrupted view of scenery. In the forward area were sleeping compartments. The separate ladies' and men's lounges were designed with distinctly feminine and masculine character, respectively. That year, Stevens also designed a Trav-ler portable plastic tabletop radio, an Evinrude Sportwin outboard motor, a line of luggage for the Wheary Company, and an aviation plant in California for McCulloch Corporation's aviation division. The Society of Industrial Designers (SID) in 1946 had initiated a massive effort to promote the work of its growing membership. Prominent designers were busy writing articles, lecturing, and granting interviews. The October 1946 issue of Electrical Manufacturing published an article by Walter Dorwin Teague titled "Industrial Design in Partnership with Engineering," which stressed coordination between the two disciplines to build better appearance into and not applied to a product. The design and arrangement of Teague's offices and studios were featured in a May 1947 issue of Architectural Record. The June 1947 issue of Coronet magazine featured a biography and personal interview with Henry Dreyfuss. By 1947, the American Designers Institute (ADI) had several hundred members, while the Society of Industrial Designers (SID), a more exclusive organization, had only 78. For many years, SID was criticized by some as being an exclusive club, and compared to the ADI, it was. This was obviously the intention of the founders. Ten years after its founding, in 1954, SID membership would total 153, a tenfold increase over its original 15 founders. At that same time, membership in the original organization, the American Designers Institute (ADI), would be 200. Unlike the ADI, the SID did not offer recommendations for a postwar industrial design education curriculum. Instead, in February 1947, it announced a program of evening courses at New York University for industrial designers, both professional and nonprofessional. Four courses consisting of 15 lectures each were scheduled for each semester of the 1948-1949 academic year. Various industrial designers would lecture on a variety of technical and philosophical subjects. Also in 1947, the SID organized a photo exhibition of 32 of its members, which opened in Philadelphia and traveled to 12 other cities. Harley Earl, the dominant force in automotive design, started the 10-year Detroit battle of "tail fins" by adding modest but noticeable tail fins on the rear fenders of the 1948 Cadillac, which prominently elevated the brake light housings. Reportedly, Earl was inspired by the Lockheed P-38 warplane's twin tails. Already, Harley Earl's postwar use of excessive chrome and iconic streamlining details were coming under criticism by leading industrial designers, who generally favored simpler and cleaner designs after the war. "Streamlining" had become a derogatory term. In his 1940 book, Industrial Design, Harold Van Doren had explained how the unfortunate term of "streamlining" had been associated with industrial design since about 1934: Streamlining has taken the modern world by storm. We live in a maelstrom of streamlined trains, refrigerators, and furnaces; streamlined bathing beauties, soda crackers, and facial massages. True streamlining is not nearly so important, nor so frequently met with in problems of industrial design, as the general public probably thinks. The manufacturer who wants his laundry tubs, his tvpewriters, or his furnaces "streamlined" is in reality asking you to modernize them, to find the means for substituting curvilinear forms for rectilinear forms ... to conform to the current taste, or fad if you will.... He expects, too, that unnecessary exposure of mechanical parts will be eliminated, that buttons will be substituted for levers, and control panels and dials will be organized into simple and easily read groups, tied in wherever possible with related elements of the principle forms. In 99 cases out of 100) this procedure is utterly unrelated to genuine streamlining. Then why does the client refer to it as such? ... Simply because, in the unbelievablv rapid growth of the American language, words soon lose their specific and restricted meanings and assume a general significance embracing far wider fields than originally intended.² The original term "streamlining" correctly referred to automobiles, aircraft and trains, which moved at high speed and therefore were made more efficient with streamlining. But when used on stationary objects, it was understandably criticized as unnecessary, and postwar designs generally abandoned the practice, except for Detroit and the automotive industry in general, and of course it is still used there today. But in the late 1940s, Detroit was the recipient of criticism, even though automobiles had reasonable justification to use streamlining under the original meaning. But streamlining was no longer the problem. Simply put, it was more a matter of an excessive amount of decorative chrome and extraneous details such as tail fins, elaborate hubcaps, and hood ornaments that Raymond Loewy would call "jukeboxes on wheels" in a 1955 speech he would deliver to the Society of Automotive Engineers. The same critics also did not approve of the Detroit term "styling," which by now had become almost synonymous with decorative surface embellishment, rather than the ergonomic consideration and convenience of passengers. Cars were so low that entry was made difficult even without a hat. There were no seat belts or air bags. You may recall that Harley Earl had changed the name of his group at General Motors from the “Art and Colour Section” to the “Styling Division” in 1937. The term “styling," for many, suggested cosmetic decoration, excessive streamlining, and insincerity, rather than the more comprehensive practice of industrial design, which would have considered safety devices and user convenience. Had Earl used the term “industrial design” instead of “styling," he could have avoided such criticism, but he did not. It seemed that he and his colleagues envisioned their work as something different than designing toasters and refrigerators. It was the beginning of a sustained split within the industrial design community, with three more or less independent groups: the single-product designers, who worked mainly, but separately, in the home furnishing industry (American Designers Institute); the automobile industry (Detroit); and the multiproduct/multiclient designers who had founded the Society of Industrial Designers in 1944. As the number of industrial designers began to increase exponentially after about 1948, when the first wave of new graduates arrived in the field, these three groups would slowly blend into the single identity of industrial design over the next 20 years. In 1948, Ray Patten at General Electric passed away while heading GE's Appearance Design Division. Arthur BecVar, who had joined GE in 1944 after returning from military service, was appointed to fill his vacated position. BecVar changed the name of his group to the Industrial Design Division. Industrial designers like BecVar, who were employees and heads of corporate industrial design organizations, were in the minority at the time, representing only 15 percent of the members of the Society of Industrial Designers (SID). They were informally referred to as “in-house" designers. And no, they were not referred to as “out-house” designers, but “consultants." As 1948 progressed, Alfonso Iannelli became a consultant for appliance manufacturer John Oster, designing mixers, hair dryers, knife sharpeners, and hair clippers. Walter Dorwin Teague Associates designed the first Model 95 Polaroid camera, produced by the Polaroid Land Company, which became an instant success. Brooks Stevens designed a refrigerator for the Coolerator Company and the iconic Willys-Overland Jeepster phaeton (a roadster with four seats rather than two), a sporty convertible that retained the familiar fenders and vertically slotted grille of the wartime Jeep. Ten thousand were sold the first year. In 1949, Stevens would design Harley Davidson's Hydra-Glide motorcycle and a Roadmaster bicycle for the Cleveland Welding Company. By 1948, Barnes & Reinecke had a huge staff of 375 and a shop of 50 machinists, but Reinecke sold his interest in the partnership and established his own office, J.O. Reinecke & Associates, specializing in industrial design and product planning, with offices on Ohio Street in Chicago, taking with him designers Jon Hauser, Harold Hart, Don Lowe, and Jack Knight, as well as a number of accounts. Clients included Caterpillar, Emerson, AT&T, Johnson & Johnson, 3M, McGraw-Edison, Westinghouse, and Zenith. In Cleveland, Viktor Schreckengost had returned from military service to teach industrial design at the Cleveland School of Art (now the Cleveland Institute of Art). He also continued to design many consumer products, such as for his client Murray Bicycle Company, for whom he designed many children's transportation products, including pedal cars, tricycles, and banana-seat bikes well into the 1960s. Schreckengost also produced work in fine art, sculpture, and painting. In the early 1950s, he created monumental ceramic sculptures for the Cleveland Zoo (now Metroparks Zoo). Ford introduced its first postwar car design in 1949 that would become an automotive classic. As mentioned earlier, Henry Ford II had in 1946 selected the design concept of industrial design consultant George W. Walker over that of Bob Gregorie, the head of Ford's styling studio, causing Gregorie to resign. Walker had actually submitted three quarter-scale clay concept models to Henry Ford II, one each by three of his staff members: Elwood Engle and Joseph Oros, Walker's key designers, and a model maker, Dick Caleal, just hired by Walker because of the clay model he had made at Walker's request. It was Caleal's model that got Walker the job with Ford. How Caleal, a lowly model maker, created a concept that won out over two of the best automotive designers (three, if you include Gregorie) in the industry was unknown at the time. But a story of intrigue would come out, essentially because of Robert Bourke, then head of Loewy's Studebaker team in South Bend. Studebaker's chief engineer had ordered Bourke to cut Loewy's staff because of budgetary reasons. Bourke had to fire several people, including one of his model makers, Caleal. Now unemployed, Caleal had approached Walker for a job and was told by Walker that he would be hired if he built an acceptable quarter-scale clay model of a concept proposal for the 1949 Ford, for which Walker was trying to get a contract, within a week. Caleal excitedly called Bourke with the good news and asked if he could help him plan the model. Bourke, wanting Caleal to get the job with Walker, gave him a wooden “buck” or armature for the quarter-scale clay model, and some wheel models. Bourke and three other designers agreed to help Caleal with the model on evenings in Caleal's Mishawaka, Indiana, home when he had the model up to rough size, in order to help him meet the deadline. They did so on Caleal's kitchen table, heating the modeling clay in the kitchen oven. Based on Bourke's rough sketches, they all worked on the model at the same time, Bourke handling the front grille and motif. ![](images/5efd8040e5330ca993d3add1a02bdce801e07a98164f75adcb9d165bcf284f76.jpg) 1949 Ford, by George Walker staff member Dick Caleal. Bourke had been inspired since 1940 by aircraft propeller hubs that looked like the tip of a bullet and had sketched numerous ideas with such a spinner hub on the nose of concept sketches (see also the 1950 Studebaker, described later). So it was not unexpected that this device would appear in the center of the clay model's grill. When it was finished, the model was 99 percent like the Ford that would be produced in 1949, with the spinner centered on the middle horizontal grille bar as its most distinctive feature. Caleal took the model to Walker on the backseat of his car; Walker liked it, and Caleal got the job. Bourke was silent, since he had secretly used Loewy's staff and materials to help Caleal and didn't want to jeopardize Caleal's job. As a result, Walker, in the tradition of the day as head of his office, received full credit for the design, By 1978, the design would be hailed as "The Car That Saved an Empire—The 1949 Ford." But Bourke would spill the beans in 1985, and the story would be out.³ In 2002, Ford would introduce a retro concept model called the Forty-Nine, which emulated the original, and in 2003, Ford would officially credit Caleal with the design of the 1949 Ford. Gordon Buehrig left Loewy in 1949 and went to Ford, as head of the body development studio, and would work on the 1951 Victoria coupe and the 1952 Ranchwagon. George Nelson, who had opened his own office in 1947, was by now producing a flood of highly acclaimed furniture designs as design director of the Herman Miller Furniture Company, including his 1949 Basic Cabinet Series, his 1945 Storage Wall concept, and his 1949 Atomic Clock for the Howard Miller Clock Company, named because of individual balls marking the hours, which radiated from the center hub on thin sticks. It was the first of many of his clock designs. Nelson, who had been the anonymous author of the influential 1934 Fortune magazine article “Both Fish and Fowl," now wrote another article in the July 1949 issue of Fortune titled "Business and the Industrial Designer," this time under his own name. He retraced the brief 20-year history of the profession and acknowledged that increasing sales is an integral objective of industrial design, but that following fashion trends or fads can be detrimental to small manufacturers. Using the example of automotive design, he explained, The general tendency of the little manufacturer is to tag along with the big three in the apparent hope that this course is the safest, although in point of fact it is the most risky because it means competing on the most unfavorable terms possible. He then cited the 1947 Studebaker Champion designed by Raymond Loewy (with a lot of help by Bob Bourke), which demonstrated with great clarity the one way open to the small manufacturer: design leadership, [and which] ... established itself as a distinct entity in the public mind in the only effective way it could—through the appearance of the product itself. In other words, for most manufacturers in the lower brackets, the radical approach to design may be the safest business approach. Nelson also challenged business to "get the most out of their designers” by suggesting that industrial designers of ability and integrity be established as a member of a company's policy-making group, with freedom to make his influence felt not only on product design but on all matters of general policy that affect design.⁴ In other words, he wanted designers to have an influential managerial role in industry, not simply function as artistic stylists. Nelson himself was a living example of design's future beyond “styling" as an influential director of design for Herman Miller. Over the next decades, many designers would climb the corporate ranks to become vice presidents with significant influence in business strategies. About this same time, the Society of Industrial Designers (SID) published U.S. Industrial Design, 1949–1950, with 190 pages and 389 photographs, showing the work of members Egmont Arens, Robert Davol Budlong, Dave Chapman, Donald Dailey, Donald Deskey, Henry Dreyfuss, Gordon Florian, Lurelle Guild, Raymond Loewy, Peter Müller-Munk, Joseph Platt, George Sakier, Brooks Stevens, Viktor Schreckengost, Walter Dorwin Teague, Harold Van Doren, Russel Wright, and many others. SID also conducted a Product Design Seminar at Lehigh University in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania, from July to August in 1949. Speakers included Walter Dorwin Teague, who spoke on “Rightness Resurgent”; Donald Dailey, who presented a case history; Carl Sundberg, who spoke on “Designing for Thermoplastic Materials”; Egmont Arens, who spoke on “Low Pressure Molding of Plastics”; J. Mcleod Little, who spoke on “Product Design"; Henry Dreyfuss, who spoke on “Design Analysis”; and Harold Van Doren and Gordon Florian, both of whom also spoke on “Design Analysis." The seminar fee was \$500. By 1949, Raymond Loewy Associates was incorporated and became known as the Raymond Loewy Corporation (RLC), which at that time had a staff of 200 working under partners Loewy; William Snaith (managed all department store work); A. Baker Barnhardt (in charge of all packaging, product, and transportation); and John Breen (business manager). Raymond Loewy Inc. also included many soon-to-be-famous designers who worked for Loewy without any public recognition for years, since only Loewy was allowed to receive design credit. Among them were Richard Latham, Peter Thompson, George Jensen, Clare Hodgman, Robert Bourke, Virgil Exner, Robert T. Tyler, Franz Wagner, Joseph Parriott, Jay Doblin, Gordon Buehrig, Carl Otto, Don Defano, Tucker Madawick, and countless others. In 1949, Loewy's organization designed a complete line of electrical home appliances, including refrigerators, ranges, automatic washers, ironers, and dryers for the Frigidaire Division of General Motors. The refrigerator was a simple white box, with only the handle end accented by a delicate chrome circle, a visual “target” to open the door. But the most successful result of SID's publicity campaign was when Loewy pulled off the greatest postwar industrial design public relations coup by appearing on the cover of Time magazine on October 31, 1949, with the caption, “He Streamlines the Sales Curve." On the cover was a color drawing by Boris Artzybasheff (1899–1965), the famous artist who created many Time cover portraits of world personalities. This one featured a large image of Loewy's face, surrounded by ghostly images of Loewy's designs, including his 1950 Studebaker, S-1 locomotive, Greyhound bus, aircraft, boat, furniture, and appliance packages, all floating in a bright, blue sky background around Loewy's head. "Blue sky," ironically, is a common design term describing imaginative but often impractical ideas, the opposite of Loewy's creations. The article, just over five pages in length, was in the “Business and Finance" section on page 68, titled “Modern Living: Up from the Egg" (Loewy had often described the egg as a perfect design), and reviewed a number of Loewy's current designs in progress, which included the new two-level Greyhound Scenicruiser, designed in 1946 but just being road tested, a face-lift for Manhattan's Gimbel Brothers department store, and models for three new ocean liners for American President Lines. Included in the text was a brief history of industrial design and references to Loewy's pioneer colleagues who brought art to the Machine Age, including Teague, Dreyfuss, and Van Doren. Egmont Arens was cited as making chairs more comfortable by taking clay impressions of fat, skinny, and in-between posteriors to make a one-piece plastic chair; Dreyfuss for his form-fitting modern toilets for the Crane Company; Teague for Boeing Stratocruiser interiors, and Bel Geddes for transforming 10,000 square feet of lobby, dining, and storage space into a modern theater that extended the stage into the audience. Loewy was the Steve Jobs of the day, and for similar reasons. They didn't invent the technology, but they took it and turned it into life-changing physical products that consumers loved and valued. There was a colorful description of Loewy as a person in the Time article: The Shy Salesman. Suave, grey-haired, medium sized (5 ft. 10 in.), Loewy talks in a subdued voice that is, at the same time, apologetic and compelling. His face is reposed, gentle, sad, and as inscrutable as a Monte Carlo croupier. Obsessively shy, he is always “Mr. Loewy” to his associates.... Despite his shyness, he is a crack salesman who throws no artistic tantrums. Far from turning out designs with offhand sureness, he works them over painstakingly until the client is satisfied. He also has an almost hypnotic power to impress, persuade and convince the toughest tycoon.⁵ The Time article also described Loewy's extravagant lifestyle, and what he did with his annual gross income that exceeded \$3 million (equivalent to \$29 million in 2013 dollars), compared to \$1 billion in yearly sales of Loewy-designed products (equivalent to \$9.77 billion in 2013). In addition to his Manhattan apartment atop a building on 58th Street, in 1946 he had designed and built a \$100,000 (\$977,000 in 2013) dream house, Tierra Caliente, in the desert near Palm Springs, California, complete with an outdoor swimming pool that curved into the living room. Summer found him in France, where he had three homes, one a red-tiled villa, Uriane, in St. Tropez, purchased in 1930; another a 16th-century manor, La Cense, outside Paris, built by Henry IV, and purchased by Loewy in 1933; and an apartment on Paris's Quai d'Orsay.6 Not mentioned was the Loewy home in Tetelpan, Mexico, built in the 1940s and also called Tierra Caliente. Loewy had just married his second wife, Viola, 30 years his junior, in 1948. The use of the term “streamline” on the cover of Time magazine with Loewy's picture illustrated the persistent popularity of the term in 1949 to describe industrial designed products. By this time, the surviving holdout for 1930s “streamlining” was the automotive industry, led by General Motors. Most design professionals were abandoning the term, as well as the practice, on stationary products. Nevertheless, by coincidence, that same month, Harold Van Doren, then president of SID, who was also part of SID's promotion campaign, wrote a feature article, “Streamlining: Fad or Function?" in the October 1949 issue of Design, the monthly journal of the British Council of Industrial Design (CoID, established in London in 1944), which had just begun publication of its professional journal. Van Doren defended streamlining, which had been under fire by both the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) and Europeans: "Much so-called streamlining is imposed on the designer by the necessity of obtaining low cost through high-speed production." Using the refrigerator as an example, he compared the traditional costly process of bending sheet metal at sharp angles and assembling multiple components, versus the large metal stamping presses adopted by most manufacturers since about 1936: Since steel stampings techniques make it impossible to produce hard, 90 degree edges of pointed corners, any designer engaged by a refrigerator company was already committed to these large radii and softer contours. He concluded his defense: “Is it fair to damn the designer to eternity because his designs echo the one really new visual phenomenon of his age?"7 Van Doren essentially was saying that most of what critics were calling streamlining was the result of manufacturing requirements for economy, which was exactly the explanation he gave in his 1940 book. He was correct, but critics of industrial design had a tendency to be more interested in artistic criticism than facts. The AT&T 500 telephone designed by Henry Dreyfuss Associates (HDA) was introduced in 1949, a sleeker and more compact version of the original 1937 model. The unsung designer was Robert Hose, an associate of HDA. In 1954, the 500 would be the first telephone to be offered in colors other than black, and would still be the most commonly used phone in the United States in 1995. In December 1949, contributing to the Society of Industrial Designers' (SID) ongoing campaign to promote industrial design, Dreyfuss made an address to the American Society of Engineering Educators. He suggested a revision to the traditional three elements manufacturing relied upon for success: “utility, quality, and price," which he said could be easily duplicated by competitors. He then spelled out the five “vital factors the indus trial designer must consider”: 1. Convenience of use (including utility and safety). 2. Maintenance. 3. Costs (consideration of tooling, general manufacturing costs, and distribution problems). 4. Sales (merchandising knowledge, public acceptance, and awareness of competition). 5. Appearance (last but not least, the designer must add his knowledge of form, line, proportion and color to integrate the product into a pleasing whole). These principles would become the credo of HDA, repeated in magazine interviews and lectures, and were picked up by many industrial designers well into the future. In his address, Dreyfuss then criticized the traditional use of extraneous decoration, imitation of materials other than those used in manufacture, and sentimental icons: With the application of this formula by the industrial designer, the extraneous decoration which the manufacturer had previously thought sufficient attraction to sell his product, began to disappear. Sullivan, that great forerunner of modern architecture, said that form should follow function. The industrial designer picked up that watchword, and rose buds, griffen's heads, and other doo-dads became increasingly conspicuous by their absence. In short, the industrial designer permitted the function of the product to dictate its appearance. Mindful that he was speaking to engineers, Dreyfuss tactfully added: "I should like to underscore here the fact that no industrial designer worthy of the name could contemplate this work without the close cooperation of engineers."8 In the fall of 1949, the 1950 Studebaker was on the market, priced at \$1,487 (\$14,500 in 2013) and designed by Loewy's South Bend Studebaker studio, with a staff of 30 under Robert Bourke. T'he body was basically the same as the successful 1947 model for cost-reduction purposes, so the design needed a dramatic front end to make it obviously different than the 1947. Bourke was competing for its design with Virgil Exner, who was in 1947 working directly for Studebaker's vice president of engineering, Roy Cole. Bourke won the competition and secured the Studebaker account for Loewy, Inc., over the next seven years with his famous aircraft nose spinner (see the 1949 Ford, described earlier) to create the now classic “bullet-nose" Studebaker. The first month, Studebaker sold more cars than any in its history. During its production life of three years, over 500,000 would be sold. Studebaker would become the largest independent carmaker, fourth under the Big Three, and the only serious challenger to Detroit styling. In 1948, Exner had left Studebaker and returned to Detroit to work for Chrysler, where he would become director of styling. Ever since 1940, when it created its own separate department of industrial design, the Museum of Modern Art, led by architects, had been trying to establish its influence on the field ofindustrial design, which had been monopolized by nonarchitect Machine Age pioneers. MoMA had consistently criticized the designs on the market as “modernistic and streamlined aberrations." In 1941 it had conducted its Organic Design in Home Furnishing Competition, won by architects, and promoted “organic" design (no “vain ornamentation or superfluity"; read: no streamlining) as its aesthetic standard. In 1946, the new director of MoMA's industrial design department, Edgar Kaufmann, Jr., had invited members of the American Designers Institute (ADI) and the Society of Industrial Designers (SID) to attend a Conference on Industrial Design: A New Profession. The discussion was largely philosophical and resulted in no specific actions. If MoMA's objective was to gain a voice in the new profession, it failed to do so. So MoMA decided to go it alone and jump into the consumer market, acting as an arbiter of public taste. In January 1950, the first Good Design exhibition opened in the marketing center of the Chicago Merchandise Mart. It was conceived and launched by Kaufmann, in collaboration with MoMA's director, René D'Harnoncourt, and Wallace O. Ollman, general manager of the Mart, who issued this joint statement: It is the first time an art museum and [a] wholesale merchandising center have cooperated to present the best examples of modern design in home furnishings.... [We] believe and hope that in combining their resources they will stimulate the appreciation and creation of the best design among manufacturers, designers and retailers.1º There would be three Good Design exhibitions annually over the next five years: at the winter market in January and the summer market in July, both of these at the Merchandise Mart, and in the fall, selections were transported to New York for display at MoMA during the pre-Christmas period. Selection of home furnishings for the exhibition was by a threeperson committee consisting permanently of Kaufmann, and a designer and retailer appointed for each show. Public opinion was studiously avoided. Selections of 150 to 250 products were chosen from the showrooms of various manufacturers at the Mart and relocated to the exhibition on the 17th floor. At the winter market, the exhibition was open only to wholesale buyers, but after that it would be open to the public, with a guided tour offered for a fee. A well-known designer or architect designed each exhibition. A “Good Design” tag was attached to each selected design, all of which were available in the open market, and each manufacturer could purchase a kit of promotional materials, including “Good Design” tags, labels, suggested window displays, and literature to promote sales of their product as a MoMA-selected “Good Design." What exactly was "Good Design"? Kaufmann defined it like this: A frequent misconception is that the principle purpose of good modern design is to facilitate trade, and that big sales are a proof of excellence. Not so. Sales are episodes in the careers of designed objects. Use is its first consideration. Thus sales, the fundamental purpose of industrial design, was tossed aside. This seemed somewhat hypocritical from a man who conceived the entire “Good Design” program in a commercial setting, which promoted the sale of products branded with the MoMA “Good Design" labels. Further, the first consideration for selection seemed to not be use, but simply aesthetic, abstract, and artistic presentation of form. Elliot Noyes in 1941 had said "Good Design shows the taste and good sense of the designers. "Arbitrarily applied decoration," to MoMA, included any commercial message, such as an obvious brand name or decal, or any element that interrupted the purity of geometric form. This definition was “art” masquerading as “design," with the only criteria being the personal aesthetic choice of the individual designer. Genuine designers design not for themselves, but to meet the limitations and sales needs of manufacturers, to adapt to the physical requirements of users, and to appeal to consumers at the point of sale. Eva Zeisel (1906–2011), revered ceramic tableware designer who won countless “Good Design” awards, at age 90 said, “Good Design [as defined by MoMA] was never good business, it only appealed to a very narrow elite. 'Good Design' was nothing more than lack of applied decoration."12 Actually, it was more than that. The MoMA standard of "Good Design" were designs devoid of any emotional, representational, or playful characteristics, the subtle qualities that make designs more interesting, more personal, and more meaningful to average consumers. Faber Birren (1900–1988), a famous color consultant of the 1950s who monitored and published color trends, once remarked that any product recognized by MoMA as art was “the kiss of death" commercially. The bottom line was this: "Good Design" depended entirely on how it was defined and judged, and by whom. It is interesting to compare MoMA definitions of "Good Design” with the pragmatic business definitions of industrial design by Henry Dreyfuss. He was not impressed with the museum's idea of good design. When asked by visitors from an English museum in which museums he would like to have his designs exhibited, his reply was, "They are called Macy's, Marshall Field's, and the May Company—similar to your Eaton's, Simpson's, and Morgan's."13 A number of“Good Design” awards went to imported European products. Europe had largely recovered from World War II, found distribution for their products in the United States, and promoted them through trade exhibitions. Germany, Japan, and Italy had rebuilt their manufacturing capabilities, opening the gates for exported automobiles, furniture, and electronic products to the American market, and stiffening the competition for all U.S. manufacturers. Among these imports were Scandinavian products in 1948; the Volkswagen Beetle from Germany, which entered the U.S. market in 1949; and the French line of porcelainenameled cast-iron cookware, Le Creuset, in 1950. The Museum of Modern Art and other U.S. museums promoted European design aggressively through exhibitions of Italian designs and designers such as Marcello Nizzoli (1887– 1969) of Olivetti (typewriters), and Corradino D’Ascanio (1891–1981) of Lambretta (motor scooters) and Giovanni Ponti (1913–1993). It seemed as if art museums were clinging to their age-old assumption that European design was always superior to American design and were urging U.S. designers and industry to imitate. It never seemed to occur to museums that U.S. design had a unique personality of its own, or that it was U.S. design that was inspiring and leading the rest of the world to seek American markets. If other countries wanted to access the enormous U.S. market, they had to design for the U.S. consumer, and to accomplish this they often needed American industrial designers to advise them, or to hire American designers to work overseas.
