CHAPTER EIGHTEEN # Indigenous Arts of Africa, the Âmericas, Australia and Oceania Throughout the nineteenth century and for much of the twentieth, Western writing on the arts of many 'non-Western' cultures was heavily colored by the problematic attitudes of colonialism. Indian, Chinese and Japanese art won respect and admiration partly because these countries had literature (including sacred texts, history and laws), large settled populations and types of political organization that the Western colonial powers could not wholly brush aside. By contrast, and for a variety of reasons, in Africa, the Americas, Australia and Oceania, the Western drive for territorial expansion and domination of resources overcame resistance more speedily and went hand-in-hand with a wholesale devaluing of the indigenous cultures that stood in its way. During the later twentieth century this balance, at least in the field of art history, began to be redressed. The arts of these continents are now discussed in the context of the cultures that produced them, not – as before – routinely judged (and found lacking) by the academic standards of post-Renaissance Western art. In the absence of written histories, archeology and anthropology have in many cases provided evidence of deeply rooted cultural continuities that can be seen to inform present-day art practice. The earlier emphasis on the alien strangeness of indigenous arts when viewed through Western eyes has been replaced by an understanding of parallels and similarities, for example between the purpose and form of much African art and the arts of medieval Europe. European interest in the art of Africa and the Americas followed closely in the wake of the sixteenth-century voyages of discovery, trade and conquest. In the nineteenth century ethnographical museums were founded in Western cities including Copenhagen (1841), Berlin (1856), Leiden (1864), Cambridge, Mass. (1866), Dresden (1875) and Paris (1878) These can now be seen as monuments to that cult of progress enshrined in Charles Darwin's The Origin of Species (1859), subtitled The Preservation of Favoured Races in the Struggle for Life. Darwin's theories were taken to support a view of social evolution in which African, Native American, Aboriginal Australian and other peoples were believed to represent lower rungs of the human evolutionary ladder, with Western civilization as the pinnacle. A study entitled Primitive Culture (1871) by Edward Tylor, the first professor of anthropology at Oxford, mentioned the arts only in so far as they illuminated the problem of relating the mindset of so-called primitive' peoples to that ofcivilized' man. In addition to these negative overtones, the term primitive' had a positive meaning with reference to urban man's regression from an imagined primal state of harmony with nature – a recurrent theme in Western art and literature (pastoral poetry being among its finest expressions), and one of special relevance in the heavily urbanized and industrialized West of the late nineteenth century, as we have seen (p. 73o). In both senses, however, this now discredited term revealed far more about Western prejudices than about the art to which it was applied. The majority of exhibits in ethnographical museums were weapons and other utensils brought back by explorers, traders and missionaries from Africa, America and the islands of the South Pacific. More specialized collections were built up by the colonial powers, and colonial exhibitions were held in Amsterdam (1883), London (1887), and Tervuren, outside Brussels (1897). Their aim was clearly indicated in the entrance to the museum at Tervuren by contemporary European sculptures personifying Belgium – which in reality operated a brutally exploitative regime in Africa – bringing peace, prosperity and Christianity to the Congo! The objects in these collections were divorced from any meaningful context. Colonial attitudes further obscured the fact that they were products of widely dissimilar social groups which integrated the arts into complex structures of laws, customs, morals, knowledge and beliefs. Sustained attempts to relate these structures to the arts associated with them had to wait until the decades after World War II. Even the fascination of early twentiethcentury avant-garde artists such as Picasso for the highly developed abstract qualities of African or Polynesian wood-carvings must be seen more as a reaction to the Western academic tradition than a genuine attempt to understand the creative sources of these art forms. The original populations migrated from Asia. After about 26,ooo BC a succession of groups settled in Melanesia, which has remained multilingual. Polynesia was the last part of the world to be populated – Fiji by 1300 , Tonga by 1200 BC and Samoa by 1000 C. Not until shortly before the beginning of the Christian era did people occupy the westerly Fiji islands and then spread to Hawaii and Easter Island in about AD 300–6oo, and to New Zealand in about AD 750-1ooo. They were horticulturalists, fishermen and expert canoe-builders with an hereditary hierarchy and spoke a single Austronesian language. The dialects subsequently developed in the various sub-groups of islands thus had the same origin, and the arts similarly branched from a single root-stock. ## Polynesia After the initial settlement Polynesia remained isolated from the rest of the world until the mid-eighteenth century. Although Fiji had been visited by A. J. Tasman in 1643 and Easter Island by another Dutch sailor, Jacob Roggeveen, in 1722, it was only in the 176os that Europeans reached Tahiti in the centre of the Polynesian triangle – an English naval captain, Samuel Wallis, in 1767, the French scientist and explorer Louis de Bougainville next year and James Cook on his three voyages of discovery (1768–79). To these explorers, especially the observant Cook with his trained naturalists and artists, we owe a significant amount of our knowledge of Polynesian culture. For contact with the outside world was fatal for the Polynesians, who soon succumbed to diseases carried from Europe, especially syphilis. Their way of life was changed by the introduction of metal tools and fire-arms; their traditional beliefs and rituals were undermined by Christian missionaries. Easter Island's great monolithic heads and half-length figures, up to 6o feet (18.3m) high, are the most imposing Polynesian sculptures (,). Bodies are rendered in various fashions from schematized to relatively naturalistic and the heads are impassive and mask-like, all with the same forceful features, prominent noses, pursed lips, long ears, massive brows and deep sunken eye-sockets. Some, wearing cylindrical crowns of red rock, were set with their backs to the sea on stone platforms beneath which the dead were buried near the shore. Others are thought to have marked three long avenues across the interior. By the time of Cook's visit the islanders seemed to have lost interest in them; but Cook recognized their connection with the cult of ancestors of a ruling caste. They are now known to have been symbols of the power which, throughout Polynesia, ruling chiefs were believed to inherit from the gods and retain after death when they were themselves deified. In this way they are comparable with other images of sacred kingship – the faces inscrutably staring out from the towers of Angkor Thom (6,67) (contemporary with some Easter Island statues), for instance, or the much earlier ancient Egyptian royal statues and the Great Sphinx (2.33) – and are likewise the expressions of an hereditary politico-religious structure. Their peculiar jutting, block-like form is partly due to the nature of the only material available for large-scale work, a grainy, easily worked tufa. But only stone, shell and bone implements were available. Elsewhere in Polynesia stone sculptures were carved, though not on so gigantic a scale nor in such quantity. Greater use was made of wood, which was scarce on Easter Island and could be used only for small figures (,) and the plaques inscribed with symbolic designs. In the Hawaiian Islands some images of gods were composed of basketry covered with red, yellow and black feathers, dogs' teeth in their mouths and pearly shells for eyes (8,3). Basketry and featherwork quickly perish in the tropics, as does wood, although wood-carvings were generally valued and cared for. A high proportion of wood-carvings, whether made before the arrival of Europeans or in the following decades was destroyed as Christianity spread across Polynesia in the nineteenth century. Of those that survive, many were sent by missionaries to Europe for exhibition as trophies of their victory over heathenism. One of the most remarkable was collected in 1821 by an English missionary who recorded its significance (8,4). A'a, the deified ancestor of the ruling clan on the island of Rurutu (in the Austral group south of Tahiti), is shown with his progeny, tiny figures in relief, which take the place of his physical features, and a number of statuettes was enclosed in a cavity in the back, emphasizing the figure's procreative and genealogical symbolism. Similarly, other cult figures called staff-gods, from Rarotonga (in the Cook Islands west of Tahiti), apparently combine images of gods with their human descendants (8,s). They range in length between 28 inches (71cm) and 18 feet (5.5m) and were carried and displayed horizontally. At one end there is a highly schematized blade-shaped head and arms of the progenitive god with a succession of little figures rising from his body, alternately full-face and in profile – those in profile with penis erect. The staff itself terminated in a phallus. But this elaborately carved sexual imagery had less importance for the Rarotongans than the feathers and pieces of shell representing the soul of the god and enclosed in yards of bark-cloth wound round the centre of the staff. Missionaries denigrated them asidols' (man-made objects worshipped in place of the deities they represent), though the Polynesians regarded cult images as no more than temporary receptacles into which the spirit of a god might be induced by rituals. Simpler figures, always naked, usually male, sometimes intended to stand on the prows of canoes, were carved elsewhere in Polynesia in a remarkably wide variety of local styles.
