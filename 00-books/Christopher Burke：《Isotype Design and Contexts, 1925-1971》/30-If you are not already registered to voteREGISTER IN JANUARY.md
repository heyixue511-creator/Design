During her first stay in the Western Region, Marie Neurath made little progress on plans for training or a visual aids office, though shortly before she returned to London, in September 1954, she met briefly with Awolowo to discuss the matter."⁶ Awolowo reiterated his wish for a training scheme to be initiated and suggested offering a scholarship for training in London. The results were mixed.68 But Marie Neurath pressed on and by mid-April she had completed drafts of two documents setting out the aims and proposed arrangements for a visual aids office.

The first of these documents described how a team of local workers would be formed and trained.6 The team would consist of a teacher, an artist, and a technical assistant. Marie Neurath would initially take charge of training the team, with the greatest effort directed towards the teacher' since this person would act as the transformer and eventually lead the team. Training would be done by carrying out projects similar to those already completed by the Isotype Institute, and by practising at working as a team. Testing was seen as crucial to the training. Marie Neurath was uncertain about exactly how effective Isotype work done for the Western Region had been up to that time; after remarking that 'our visual material should be understandable to the literate, and easily explained to the illiterate', she admitted that 'how far it fulfils these claims has still to be tested.' To find out, she suggested setting up a permanent exhibition where work could be shown and the public's reactions noted, as a guide to refinements or redesign. She also recommended conducting tests in locations where the work would be deployed, including at schools in villages and towns, in hospital waiting rooms, among village farmers, and in adult education classes. It was estimated that the training would require a year in total, though even this was considered brief. Awolowo's offer of a scholarship for training in London was noted, as was an agreement by Marie Neurath to return to the Western Region to monitor the training, if necessary.

The second document set out in greater detail the twelve-month scheme for training, giving the time, cost, and logistics involved.7º The training would at first be in the hands of Marie Neurath, who would extend her stay in the Western Region for a further month; she would then be replaced by another Isotype Institute employee brought in from London."1 In her introductory remarks, Marie Neurath sounded a not entirely optimistic note about the immediate prospects of success, perhaps to justify the need for the scheme and its duration: 'In the course of training, a number of such pamphlets [i.e. like those already made by Isotype] should be produced by the Nigerian trainees under the supervision and guidance of members of the Isotype Institute. The rate of such production will obviously be handicapped; unsatisfactory work will have to be repeated; unsuitable trainees will have to be replaced.' She then made an account of the salary, equipment, and accommodation costs, and suggested again that additional training could take place in London as well as in connection with further work the Isotype Institute might carry out in the Western Region. The document ends with the suggestion that progress be reviewed after twelve months had passed.

Alongside these two documents, Marie Neurath devised a timeline of decision-making that, if followed, would enable the training scheme to go ahead as proposed. But as the latter days of her second visit passed, there were few signs that a decision to commence the scheme would be made; and indeed by the time of her departure, for reasons that are not entirely clear but which likely stem in part from the failure to identify suitable candidates, the scheme – at least as it might be conducted in the Western Region – had been given up." Despite the setback, efforts shifted to recruiting and training a Nigerian in London. A promising candidate was eventually identified who it seems had already begun a comparable programme of study at the University of London. The programme involved intensive exposure to cultural products including films, exhibitions, and books, and instruction in developing critical and artistic faculties. But for reasons that arc again unclear, efforts to draw this candidate into the activities of the Isotype Institute also faltered, bringing work on the scheme of training finally to an end.

## Gold Coast, Sierra Leone

Following Marie Neurath's return from the Western Region in May 1955, production continued on the second series of booklets, and on posters and several other items of work. During this period, she asked government officials and Awolowo himself for feedback on the use and effectiveness of Isotype material in the Western Region, but there is little evidence that much was supplied, despite her persistence.'

Projects in the Western Region were not, however, the only ones underway in West Africa. The Isotype Institute was also still active in the Gold Coast, as well as Sierra Leone, though in relative terms this work would prove limited. Following the Forward magazine initiative of 1952–4, no further projects in the Gold Coast had been immediately forthcoming. Although a variety of development topics had been identified as possibly in need of visual explanation, it was clear that the country's Information Services Department in Accra was already well organized and in little apparent need of servives of the kind provided by the Isotype Institute." In this context, the only project later completed for the Gold Coast was the booklet, The Volta River Project: what it means to you.7 The booklet presented what the Prime Minister, Kwame Nkrumah, described in his foreword as the 'most momentous development undertaking' in the country's history. The project would involve the coordinated development of hydroelectric power on the Volta River and bauxite extraction from surrounding deposits that together would generate cheap electricity and revenue from aluminium production and export. According to Nkrumah, the decision to go ahead with the project was in the hands of the people and so the booklet was to be both technically informative and persuasive, explaining why the project should be undertaken, how it would be organized and paid for, and what benefits it would bring.

To support the process of work, Marie Neurath travelled to the Gold Coast in late November 1955." While there, she worked on the booklet's text and illustrations, and agreed its overall design with Gold Coast officials. The booklet's size initially matched that of the Western Region booklets, though this was forcibly enlarged to accommodate what had become a lengthy text. The Isotype Institute produced maps, section views, depictions of processes, and pictorial statistics, which were then integrated with the text. ## Reaching the people

Having completed a sizeable number of projects in the Western Region of Nigeria, with further projects underway there and elsewhere, work was begun on a Buffalo Books capabilities booklet that offers perhaps the clearest statement of the partnership's aims, which had previously been only implicit in its activities. The booklet, entitled 'Reaching the people' and whose text was probably written by Frame Smith, had several intended functions.8⁶ It would describe the knowledge, expertise, and experience Buffalo Books could offer its clients, illustrate completed projects, and position its services in a larger context of spreading post-war democracy, social provision, and development, about which governments needed to inform their citizens and win support. The opening paragraphs of the text are rousing and optimistic, and worth quoting at length:

This has been called the Age of the Common Man. It is certainly the age in which, all over the world, the common man is becoming concerned as never before with the public responsibilities extending far beyond the traditional framework of family, village or tribe. New democracies are developing, in which men and women have votes, and their votes not only affect the destinies of their own peoples but in turn affect the history of the world, for the interdependence of all the countries and communities of the world becomes daily more apparent and inescapable.

Everywhere men and women see opening before them new opportunities and possibilities of better and fuller life for themselves and for their children. They demand the means to seize these opportunities and to realise these possibilities.

To meet the challenge, new political and social organisations are being evolved, new forms of relationship are being established between State and State, between government and people, between public authority and private enterprise, between management and labour.

## An end of work

With its impressive range of projects and hopeful declarations, 'Reaching the people' was part of ongoing efforts to generate new work in West Africa and (by intention at least) in other developing regions. Marie Neurath's visit to the Gold Coast in late 1955 was part of this effort: in addition to managing work on the Volta River Project booklet while there, she and Frame Smith planned for her to meet various Gold Coast officials, to encourage a programme of work like that underway in the Western Region.8⁸ After her stay in the Gold Coast, Marie Neurath's onward journey to the Western Region was apparently also seen as an opportunity to not only look after existing projects and strengthen relationships, but to encourage new projects as well.89

In the early months of 1956, after Marie Neurath's return from West Africa, the Isotype Institute concentrated its efforts on completing the second series of booklets and posters for the Western Region, and the Volta River Project booklet for the Gold Coast. Correspondence and working material from March and April list other projects apparently active in the Western Region.9º But frustration is occasionally registered that the status of some projects is unclear for lack of communication from the Western Regional Government, while other signs of strain in the relationship can also be detected, no doubt the result of working at a distance, with its accompanying delays, expense, and logistical challenges.9¹ Soon after, correspondence and datable working material for the Western Region ceases, implying no new projects getting started, only the marshalling of existing ones toward completion.

There seems to be no single reason why Isotype Institute work in West Africa ended. As mentioned, the acknowledged efficiency of the Information Services Department in the Gold Coast seemed to forestall projects there, while in Sierra Leone the booklet and posters for the 1957 elections were perhaps too limited in scope to provide impetus for other work. In the Western Region, where by far the greatest number of projects had been completed, their cessation may be explained by the simple fact that the programme of public relations outlined by Marie Neurath in her memorandum of 1954 had been largely achieved, leaving little else to do. But the end of work can also be linked to the abandoned plan to train locals and establish a visual aids office in the Western Region. Had the plan gone ahead, the relationship between the Isotype Institute and the Western Regional Government would surely have continued. But it seems that the government – and perhaps Awolowo, in particular – was unable to envision a future for visual aids and so demurred from any long-term commitment. Signs of disengagement may also be read into the government's failure to provide the Isotype Institute with substantive feedback on its work (Rotimi Williams's remarks notwithstanding), despite Marie Neurath's repeated requests for it to do so. Possibly, too, there was an inability to shift the application of visual aids away from the sphere of public relations where they were originally situated, into an educational context where their applications could have been expanded. Efforts to do this, it seems, had been pursued by Marie Neurath and Frame Smith in relation to Stephen Awokoya, the dynamic Minister of Education."² But in the first half 1956, Awokoya was relieved of his portfolio and with his departure any influence he might have brought to widening the use of Isotype would have been lost as well.93

In her recollections, Marie Neurath takes note of the failure to initiate a local training scheme in the Western Region, and to establish a visual aids office there, concluding that 'the whole situation was too uncertain in the long run for such a plan.' Her remark, though unspecific, clearly refers at least in part to the broader dynamics of government and politics in the Western Region at the time. ## International relations

Whatever reasons may be assigned to the end of work in the Western Region and elsewhere in West Africa, the Isotype Institute's engagement there was nevertheless significant and resulted in work of a high standard. The people Marie Neurath encountered in many different contexts were, it seems, enthusiastic about Isotype as a method for showing and explaining through intelligible and recognizable means those developments that would affect their future. In her memoir, written some 25 years after the West African experience, Marie Neurath recounts it in some detail. But she offers little by way of summary, only that (as quoted at the start of this chapter) 'it was good that we were able to have this experience. Otto had said that we were creating the method not, finally, for the Viennese, but rather for the Africans, and so it proved.' She continued, 'That we had to alter the symbols for man, woman, house and so on in the process, was within the scope of the rule that the symbols should "speak", that is, be immediately understandable. The international character was maintained in the basic approach.'97

These remarks, apparently the last about West Africa she committed to writing, summarize issues about pictograms (symbols), graphic configurations, and the work of tranformation. They round off similar statements made at greater length earlier, in three articles published in the fifteen or so years after projects in West Africa had ended. In each, Marie Neurath offers observations about the nature of working in international contexts including West Africa, and how these contexts compelled an expansion of Isotype's features, while at the same time confirming its 'basic approach'. Thus in ‘The origin and theory of Isotype' (196o), she wrote in regard to pictograms:

The international character of the symbols has occasionally been tested, but only in the Western world and in temperate climates. ... New symbols were needed for tropical Africa for such things as crops, tools, animals, man, woman, child, house, etc. The existing symbols had to be altered to make Isotype charts comprehensible to these Africans.

These, she went to remark, should be accompanied by alterations to the larger scheme of graphic depiction as well:

Every chart has to represent a familiar visual background – adherence to the method cannot go as far as imposing an alien background on those unable to share one's experience of it.

## And yet this only needed to go so far:

Any modifications of shapes and arrangement does not imply that the system had to be radically changed. The Isotype system is an elaboration of the child's approach which is more or less universal, and it appeals to a certain level of commonsense. Anthropologists who tend to concentrate on the differences between peoples predicted that Isotype would be misunderstood and misleading, but there has been no evidence of this at all. In adapting the method to the needs of the semi-literate, only a certain slowing down of the rate at which new ideas are represented is required. ... A whole booklet was designed to instruct the people of the Western Region of Nigeria how to become registered voters. The high percentage who responded showed how well the message was grasped.98

In 1971, Marie Neurath returned to the issue of the pictograms, in particular which ones and how many were required for an international' picture language, and, in this respect, what the West Africa work had led her to conclude:

The number of symbols cannot be foreseen: new tasks and subject matters often require new symbols. The method and the approach are, I think, more universal than the symbols are. I had to discover this when I worked for Africans for some time. I had to make things clear to them, and I could not force our international symbols' on them. Otto Neurath's view, however, was that this should be done in a restrained manner, stipulating in International picture language that pictograms should be contained in 'one general list of a limited number of signs needed for international use', carefully designed and accessioned under the control of one chief organization', by implication to insure their international effectiveness.1º1 Marie Neurath, in light of the West Africa experience, seemingly arrived at a more pragmatic position in accommodating international differences. This included the use of pictograms and graphic depictions whose pictorial content (or elements of it) would be meaningful locally but not always more widely, and also the transformation of narrative content into configurations wħose structure, pace, and density were adapted to culturally and linguistically specific audiences. One can only speculate on whether Otto Neurath's outlook might have evolved in a similar direction had he finally met the developing world of the colonies. Certainly the production of Isotype elsewhere, in the Soviet Union and the United States for example, signalled his enthusiasm to take Isotype into other cultures where work was done under – and indeed partly in response to – local conditions.

Looking back again at the sum of Isotype work, those projects completed for the Gold Coast, Sierra Leone, and the Western Region of Nigeria represent Isotype's farthest international extension. The need to respond directly to circumstances of culture, language, and governance in these territories brought productive challenges to the Isotype Institute, and salutary results. Marie Neurath in her recollections describes at length and in detail not only the work that was done, but also the many and varied people and places she encountered. For her, these experiences were crucial to getting the work right. And yet in this new and unfamiliar context, there was much that remained typical of Isotype, whether in the 'basic approach' of the work, in the wish to establish indigenous, self-standing visual aids production, or in the location of work in the broader currents of progressive, social

democratic development, welfare, and enfranchisement – in the Western Region of Nigeria above all. But the work done in the Western Region in particular prompts another observation, of the close and sometimes instrumental relationship that developed between the Isotype Institute and the Western Regional Government, its party in power and its policies. The relationship was an implicitly sympathetic one ideologically, though with it came all the social and political uncertainties of approaching independence. The price for the Isotype Institute, it seems, in Nigeria but also elsewhere in British colonial West Africa, was to operate in regions whose aspirations but unsettled circumstances meant that opportunities for work would only be short-lived.

## 12 Pictogram design Vienna and beyond

Christopher Burke

It is commonly assumed that the prevalence of pictograms in today's public signing is the principal legacy of Isotype. But it is difficult to establish a direct connection. Otto and Marie Neurath considered Isotype to be more than the sum of its pictograms – it was a method for making ‘visual arguments'. Otto Neurath explained this to a client (referring to pictograms as ‘symbols’):

I maintain the point: it is not the use of some symbols [that is] the essential element, but the procedures [for] HOw TO USE vIsUAL AIDs FOR AN ARGUMENT. That is the reason why we object to the application of our tools WITHOUT ISOTYPE TRANSFORMATION. You see, our international prestige is based on the COMPLETE ISOTYPE CHARTS, not on the symbols as such (of which we think that they are carefully made according to the general visual principles). We start with our dictionary of about 2ooo symbols (printing blocks etc.) only as BRıCks, and as an architecture is not characterized by the ELEMENTs only of which it is composed but by the PATTERN of the architecture, so our IsOTYPE method is characterized by the pattern and the procedures how the pattern is reached.

These charts all bear the signature 'Standard Mundaneum Vienna' and date from 1933. They were probably intended to serve as models for the Mundaneum branches. The experiences of adapting the Vienna Method to the ussR and of attempting to introduce it to the usA may also have spurred this standardizing approach. The use of Arntz's artwork for Mundaneum standards explains why only these pictogram drawings survive from the Vienna period: they must have been taken to the office of the Mundaneum and International Foundation for Visual Education in The Hague when that became the refuge for the core of the Vienna staff in 1934.

In general, pictograms from the mature period of Isotype are not excessively geometric, and much less so than some of their successors in public signing. Human figures in particular are marked by an attachment to a recognizable human silhouette. Again, this was deliberate: Arntz recalled that he and his fellow artists of the Progressives 'were no pure constructivists, we were too sensory for that. We could not forsake round, womanly forms in our pictures, in contrast to the Dutch and Russian constructivists.'2º Neurath stated: 'Viewers are already far more accustomed to a departure from naturalism in terms of colour than in form. A green person meets with less resistance than one that is squared-up [eckiger]’.21

Some early experiments were made at the Museum with more geometric human figures. The early chart on method (1926; p. 146 above) shows a variant intended for counting the heads of figures, in which the head is a circle floating free of the body. This proposal did not survive long in the Vienna Method; perhaps it was thought that internal breaks in the body compromised the integrity of pictograms when repeated in lines. (One later exception is the symbol/brandmark for Isotype itself, which is never repeated within a chart.) However, the severed, circular head became a common motif of public information pictograms designed from the 196os onwards. Masaru Katzumie's pictograms for the 1964 Tokyo Olympics included this feature, as did Otl Aicher's for the Munich Olympics. These examples led to the set of pictograms designed by Roger Cook and Don Shanosky in 1974 for the United States Department of Transportation (DOT), which include the human pictograms most commonly used in signs for public toilets today.

Isotype pictograms were never applied to public information signs in practice; the closest they came to being used in this way was a proposal made by Marie Neurath for signposts to the organizers of the Festival of Britain in 1951, but this was not accepted. Otto Neurath commented that things designed for one purpose were often adapted successfully to another, and he suggested this explicitly for Isotype pictograms in International picture language: 'Most of the signs may be used not only in lines but as separate designs.'²³ He gave as an example the automnobile pictogram, which was shown both in a statistical chart and in some proposals for traffic signs.²⁴ Neurath was no doubt aware of the efforts towards standardizing traffic signs which had accelerated during the 192os and early 193os,2⁵ but he did not live long enough to see the development of pictogram systems during the 195os and 196os. Standardized pictograms on signs in public places were not common in his lifetime, yet he foresaw their utility for such purposes. In 1933 he wrote:

Conventional signs are usual especially in cartography. Some of these signs would be classed as speaking signs in the sense of the Vienna Method. Also certain crate-labelling pictograms that indicate content can be counted here; the lightning sign to indicate strong current, and many other signs in transport, hotels, etc., the systematic organization of which is surely only a question of time.

A universal lexicon of all signs must be centrally worked out and distributed internationally. It is advisable that we use as far as possible the same signs for quantity-pictures, cartograms, and maps, inasmuch as it is not a requirement to distinguish between statistical units and plain object description. In this way a kind of international information script [Sachschrift] would develop, which would fundamentally ease international communication [Verkehr].2⁶

Neurath attempted to instigate production of signs for transport using Isotype pictograms towards the end of the Second World War. He proposed this idea to his cousin Gustav, who managed an electroplating factory in Birmingham (uk), which was involved in munitions production at that time. Neurath envisaged that Gustav's company could produce 'pressed metal plates in colour with symbols on', suggesting that Isotype pictograms could be used 'in ships, railroads, hotels, indicating everything of general interest: Boat, Lavatory, Gentlemen, Ladies, Water, Cloak room, Luggage'. He added that, in addition to pictograms for land and sea, 'I think one should as soon as possible try to get orders for such symbols dealing with airplanes'.² Neurath died within a year of making these proposals and they were never realized. After his death some consideration was given to copyright protection of Isotype pictograms, which would perhaps have been desirable in the context of commercial manufacture of such signs. Yet Marie Neurath was reluctant to pursue the matter, indicating perhaps her continued opinion that Isotype was something more than pictograms.28 Additionally, Arntz did not work with the Isotype Institute during or after the Second World War and it relied mainly on the stock of pictograms that he had designed before the war.

The boom in international travel during the 196os prompted the standardization of pictogram sets by organizations such as the Union Internationale de Chemins de Fer (uiC), Icograda, and eventually the International Organization for Standardization (iso). Yet, before this Marie Neurath was involved with a short project that may have helped to prepare the ground for these initiatives. In April 1956 Rudolf Modley asked her to work with him on a 'study of communications through symbols', which he had formulated at the request of the industrial designer Henry Dreyfuss. The use of standardized pictograms in airports and other public buildings (in particular the AIGA's DoT series) connects with the modernist aspiration to international communication in Isotype. A postmodern usage is also now well established: pictograms are employed as a branding accessory for everything from dating agencies to left-wing pressure groups. The ubiquity of pictograms in the public environment has led to a familiarity that begins to breed contempt, or at least irreverence. Satirical and ironic variations on the geometric human pictograms now abound.³7 Indeed, in Otto Neurath's home town of Vienna, an intriguing experiment was undertaken by the city administration to subvert the traditional forms of pictograms on public information

signs. This was referred to as an example of ‘gender mainstreaming', and took the form of introducing recognizably female figures into signs traditionally occupied by male (or non-gender-specific) figures, such as that for roadworks.³⁸ It is doubtful that Isotype's creators would have considered it the role of pictograms alone to challenge preconceptions. Otto Neurath in particular was conscious of the need to accord with expectations to a large extent in design, especially for the immediacy of communication he required of Isotype. In 1931 he wrote:

Actual life prevents us from confronting' all of our customs. The ridiculous rules of the western white man's clothes are obviously deeply rooted and will change only slowly. Whoever takes up the struggle in this single field excludes himself from the rest of life. ... Even large social upheavals which create sudden changes in a short time, have only a slow effect, for traditional habits are very tenacious. 39
