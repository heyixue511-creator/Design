Next to the industrial and tariff policies of the Government of India and the problem of the 'drain', it was its financial policy that drew the severest fire from the Indian national leadership during the period under study. The determining features of Indian finance in this period were: the large increase in military expenditure as a result of the accentuation of Anglo-Russian rivalry in Asia and the consequent fear of a Russian invasion of India, the fall in the gold value of silver, rapid expansion of railway expenditure, and increasing taxation necessitated by the growth of expenditure as a result of all these factors. Even though in some of the years Indian Finance Members were able to show surplus budgets, as a whole these were years of financial disequilibrium and embarrassment and of taxes chasing and trying to keep abreast of expenditure and often failing in the effort.1 Surprisingly enough, however, even though year after year individual Fnance Members bewailed their' hard lot, a very sanguine view of India's finances was taken by British and British Indian statesmen whenever they discusşed the topic in general. Thus, in 1893, in his book the Indian Polity, General Chesney went into raptures over 'the remarkable stability of Indian finance'.2 After reviewing the Indian finances between 1878-98 James Westland boldly asserted in the Financial Statement for 1898-9 that 'the record of twenty years' finance which I have displayed in the above figures is a better one than can be shown by almost any country in the world but the United Kingdom'.3 In 19o1, George Hamilton, Secretary of State, expressed his sense of wonder and pleasure at the 'stability and recuperative power in the financial system of India'.4

The national leadership, on the other hand, took just the contrary view. For example, the Amrita Bazar Patrika of 3o March 1882 declared the financial system of India to be ‘unsound from the top to the bottom'. At the National Congress cf 1894, D. E. Wacha contended that it was bad finance which was ‘at the root of our complaint', and that ‘if India is lost to the British it will be on account of its miserable finances'.s In his Presidential Address to the Congress of 1895, Surendranath Banerjea, after quoting John Bright to the effect that the condition of the people of any country could be adjudged by examining its financial condition, declared that judged by this criterion India's condition was ‘truly deplorable' since the financial position of India was ‘one of ever-recurring deficit, and of ever-increasing debt'.6 In his essay on ‘The Present Financial Position', published in January 1896, G. V. Joshi firmly refuted the official contention that Indian finances represented à brilliant feat of financial skill and maintained that the country stood 'on the brink of a financial crisis of unprecedented magnitude and severity'.7 The dangers of the situation were, moreover, aggravated by the fact that the existing state of affairs was not the result of the actions and mistakes of 'this or that individual administrator'; it was 'the system—the entire financial management of the countrywhich is responsible for the present difficulties'.8 Similarly, G.K. Gokhale in his very first speech in 19o2 on the Budget in the Imperial Legislative Council drew a harrowing picture of India's finances since 1885-6 and alleged that since that year ‘our finances have been so managed as to lend support to the view that other interests take precedence of Indian interests in the administration of Indian revenues'.9

Starting with this critical view of the nature and management of India's finances, the Indian leaders examined at length the official fiscal policy and suggested alternative policies. In particular, they took up for examination, condemnation, and popular agitation every important act of the government that they thought to be an instance of the imposition of new taxes on the masses, or of unnecessary, wasteful, or non-welfare expenditure, or of 'injustice in the financial relations between India and Britain. And from each such instance they sought to draw appropriate conclusions regarding the nature and motivations of British rule in India.

## I. TAXATION

The gross revenue of the Government of India increased from Rs. 74.3 crores in 188o to Rs. 90.6 crores in 1893 and Rs. 172.2 crores in 1904.1o But these figures contain an element of distortion in so far as they include, amóng other things, the gross railway receipts of state railways and irrigation projects and thé net railway receipts on account of other railways. Moreover, certain other factors like changes in the unit of account also undermined the comparability of financial statistics from year to year.11

Opposite opinions were held by the British authorities and the national leaders regarding the burden of this revenue on the people. A preliminary objection was taken by most of the British writers and officials to equating the whole of government revenue with taxation.12 Many of them, for example, tended to regard land revenue as state rent and not a tax.13 Similarly, they denied that opium revenue constituted a tax on the people of India.14 The Indian writers, on the other hand, classified nearly all the important items of government revenue as taxation. Dadabhai Naoroji, for instance, defined taxation as 'the amount which the government of the country takes for its administration, public debt, etc.' from the total annual income of the country.15 In view of the present accepted connotation of the term, the definition given by the Indian leaders may be taken as more correct.

The British administrators invariably asserted that India was very lightly taxed and that most probably it was the most lightly-taxed country in the world. For instance, in 1894 John Strachey, ex-Finance Member, confidently proclaimed, 'There is no country possessing a civilised administration where taxation is so light' as in India.i6 Curzon was no less sure of this position. Refuting G. K. Gokhale's assertion that taxation presses heavily upon the people of India, he sardonically remarked in 19o2: 'If the Hon'ble Member were to transfer his residence to any European country, I expect that he would very soon be back again here, with altered views about fiscal matters.'17

The British administrators stressed in this respect the low incidence in sheer physical terms of taxation per head in India as compared to other countries and particularly in Britain. As early as 186o, James Wilson, the then Finance Member, had contrasted'the tax burden of 5s. per head in India with that of £ 2. 3s. per head in England.18 Eleven years later, the Viceroy. Lord Mayo, tried to prove the light character of taxation in India by stating that it amounted to only is. iod. per head.19 He, of course, deducted the land revenue, opium revenue, tributes and such other receipts from the total gross revenue of the government for arriving at this figure. Working on the same assumption, Henry Fowler, Secretary of State for India, asserted in 1894 that 'the taxation per head in India is something like 2s. 6d., or 1/zoth the taxation of the United Kingdom and 1/13th of that of Ireland.'2o

Interestingly enough, the increase in the revenues of India during the period 188o to 1904—which, it was claimed, occurred without additional measures of taxation—was cited by the British Indian officials as a proof of the growing prosperity of the country.21 'The increase in the different branches of revenue indicates', asserted General Chesney, ‘a gradual and steady, but moderate, advance from year to year in the general prosperity of the country.'2z 'We have shown', proudly remarked Curzon in 1903, 'steadily improving revenues, large and increasing surpluses, advances in all the tests that indicate material prosperity.'3 Some of the British officials further maintained that the burden of taxation as such had actually been going down since 1860.24

The Indian national leaders completely rejected these official contentions.25 They emphatically asserted that taxation in India was a crushing and unbearable burden beyond the capacity and resources of the country and that it had reached its uttermost limits. The oppressiveness of the burden of taxation was, as a matter of fact, one of the regular iteins in the nationalist charge-sheet against British rule, and it moved many an Indian leader to express himself on the subject in strong, bitter and angry language.

As early as 1838, we find the Young Bengal complaining, among other things, against 'the enormous taxation to which the country is subjected'.26 In 1871, the Bombay Association submitted a petition to the House of Commons alleging that 'over-taxation has, for many years of British Rule, been the bane of India'.27 In 1879, Surendranath Banerjea contended that it must be ‘apparent even to the most cursory observer that the furthest limit of taxation has been reached in this country and that any further attempt to levy fresh burden might be the straw which would break the camel's back'.28 Similarly, Ranade remarked in 188o that 'further increase of taxation must be adjured as political insanity'.29 Opining that there was 'a limit beyond which it is impossible to push taxation' and that there were 'many evidences to show that India has reached that limit', the Amrita Bazar Patrika gave the following warning in its issue of 27 August 1885: 'When thoughtless rulers push beyond that limit they bring about a rebellion, or if the people are too weak for that, nature herself intervenes and gives unmistable evidence of her wrath. That the limit has been over-stepped in India is evidenced by the fact of successive famines.' Even at the first Indian National Congress taxation was the subject of many a bitter comment. Dadabhai Naoroji accused the rulers of 'screwing out more and more taxes, like squeezing a squeezed orange—inflicting suffering and distress'.3º Besides, Tilak's Kesari remarked in its issue of 31 January 1888 that 'No article in India has escaped taxation. Even the foliage of trees has been taxed'. Waxing sarcastic, the Kesari suggested: 'There are, however, some things which are yet to be taxed, so that the triumph of the English may be complete. Among such things may be mentioned the skin of the Indian people and their atmosphere.'31 The Satya Mitra of 9 February 1888. warned that the government must 'fully bear in mind that even the meek cow can, if enraged, tear the elephant to pieces or will kick the milkman if he attempts to milk after she has yielded the whole of the milk in her udder'. And in an almost seditious tone, it threatened: 'The queen of Jhansi, Nizam Ul-mulk, Chimnaji Appa, Bajirao, Tatya Topi and other heroic personages are, though in history, still brandishing their swords before the world and giving evidence of our heroism.'32 R. M. Sayani, the Congress President for the year 1896, was equally convinced that 'the ability of the country to bear any fresh taxation is exhausted'.33 Similar sentiments and opinions were expressed by many other nationalist leaders over the years.34

The Indian leaders further complained that not only was taxation in India high but it was constantly on the increase.35. For example G. V. Joshi calculated in 1896 that the burden of taxation proper, even after excluding Railway and other commercial receipts, had increased by 35 per cent during the period 1883-4 and 1895-6.36

To prove their. contention that the burden of taxation in India was very heavy, the Indian leaders, like their official adversaries, appealed 'to the testimony of· figures'. But this testimony they interpreted in their own way. They did accept that the absolute amount of taxation per head was very small But, they argued, the incidence of taxation was not to be judged from absolute figures of what was taken from a person irrespective of his income. Here not the amount ot taxation per head but the proportion it bore to per capita income was significant. If in India taxation was low, the national income was lower still. Seen in this light, the Indian leaders said, taxation in India turned out to be very heavy and oppressive indeed. Dadabhai Naoroji was the chief architect of this line of argument, which he evolved as early as 1871 in his statement to the Select Committee on East India Finance. He contended that the incidence of taxation was about 8 per cent in England and about 16 per cent in India.37 He, as well as other nationalist leaders, repeated this figurative representation of their case over the years, though they often varied the percentages.38

Some of the Indian leaders further felt that even this ratio of taxation to income failed to bring out properly the real heaviness of the burden. Taxation, they said, must be further correlated to the over-all capacity of the people to bear the taxes. It was one thing to take a certain percentage of income as tax from a poor person and quite another to take the same percentage from a rich one for the tax would definitely impinge with much greater harshness on the standard of living of the former. This line of reasoning found a clear and distinct enunciation in the writings of Dadabhai Naoroji, who used it to resolve the apparent paradox in the nationalist position, viz., the admittedly low per capita taxation crushing the Indian people. He elucidated this point in his statement to the Select Committee on East India Finance in 1871:

It must, moreover, be particularly borne in mind that, while a ton may not be any burden to an elephant, a few pounds may crush a child;...The capacity to bear a burden with ease, or to be crushed by it, is not to be measured by the percentage of taxation, but by the abundance, or otherwise, of the means or income to pay it from. From abundance you may give a large percentage with ease; from sufficiency, the same burden may be just bearable, or some diminution may make it so; but from insufficiency, any burden is so much privation.39

He repeated this formulation in his paper on the 'Poverty of

India' and later on expounded it in numerous speeches, etc.4o R. M. Sayani, President of the Indian National Congress in 1896, echoed Dadabhai's reasoning in very clear terms in his Presidential Address.41

This line of thought, when applied to India, led Dadabhai to declare that not only was the pressure of taxation in India double that of England but it was in fact far more oppressive, as it was exacted from poverty'.42 Similarly R. M. Sayani concluded that 'the ratio of 16½ per cent, of taxation to income, in the case of India, though nominally only two-and-a-half times higher than the ratio in the case of England, is, in incidence, considerably more heavy, and India is therefore, in reality, worse off than the ratio indicates'.43 K. T. Telang, Madan Mohan Malaviya, G. K. Gokhale, R. C. Dutt and several other leaders also correlated in a similar fashion the poverty of the country and the burden of taxation.44 In 1896, G. V. Joshi laid down the perfectly sound doctrine that no increase of taxation should take place unless the total national product was growing, and claimed that in India the former was increasing while the latter was declining.45 Dadabhai Naoroji and Madan Mohan Malaviya put the same proposition in slightly different words when they repeatedly asserted that the Indians would gladly pay higher taxes if only these followed increasing national income.46

The Indian leaders also cited as a pragmatic demonstration of their contentions an array of quotations from English :authorities to the effect that there was no margin of taxation left for the government to fall back upon in the hour of necessity and that India could not be taxed without bringing about an economic and political disaster.47

Later, when budgetary surpluses began to appear, they were advanced by the nationalists as yet another proof of the fact that 'ore taxes are wrung from the people than are right, necessary or advisable'.48 G. K. Gokhale, who made an analysis of the recurring surpluses the central theme of his budget speech in 19o2, started his onslaught on the financial policy of the Government of India by declaring that these surpluses illustrated ‘in a painfully clear manner the utter absence of a due correspondence between the condition of the people and the condition of the finances of the country'. It was obvious that 'the weight of public taxation has been fixed and maintained at an unjustifiably high level'; and thus 'the apparent paradox of a suffering country and an overflowing treasury stands easily explained'.49

Some of' the economists among the national leaders also looked into the question of the distribution of the burden of taxation among the various classes of people. A detailed inquiry into the incidence of taxation conducted by the Finance Department of the Government of India in early 188o's had shown 'most conclusively' that the major burden of taxation in India fell mainly upon the poorer classes.5o The nationalist economists concurred with this view and strongly criticised the system of taxation for its regressive character, that is, for its tendency to bear more heavily upon the poor than upon the rich. As early as 1871, Dadabhai Naoroji took the government to task for exacting more from the cultivators than from other classes of people and sarcastically enquired: 'Or is it that, because richer interests can resort to agitation, and make themselves heard, while the poor labourer and cultivator cannot, it is felt easier to squeeze them than the other classes?'si G. V. Joshi discussed the question at length in April 1888 in his essay, 'The Burmah Deficit and the Enhancement of the Salt Duties'. Condemning the Finance Member for following the policy of taxing the poor 'while letting off rich Zamindars and his rich friends, the tea-planters,'s2 Joshi bemoaned the inequity of a system of taxation under which 'the. richer few, who profited most by British admninistration, British justice and British peace, paid least, while the poorer millions, who profited least, paid most'. He also pointed out that the inequality of the burden of taxation existed not only as between one class and another but also as between 'the “"classes" in the cities and the "masses" in the country'.53 As a guide to future action, he advised the government to accept as its first duty the rectification and removal of the inequalities in the distribution of public taxation.54 Earlier in 1886, he had laid down the following principle of taxation:

And in all financial discussions, the one question for the practical financier is not, whether the total burden of taxation is heavy or light on the general population, —which is a point of practical politics,—but whether, taking the whole body of public burdens, it is equally and equitably distributed;... (and) the weight of it falls on all classes in just and equitable proportion to their ability to pay.55

Pleading for the enhancement of the income tax in place of an increase in the salt tax, the Swadesamitran, owned and edited by G. Subramaniya Iyer, in its issue dated 18 February 1888, echoed Joshi's thoughts as follows :

In every country administered by a juşt Government it will be considered proper to impose taxes on the rich to a greater extent than on the poor, for only the rich are benefited by the organisation of the police, the establishment of judicial courts, the railways, the telegraph, etc. But if the Ġovernment of a country is in the hands of the upper classes, will they be so foolish as to impose taxes upon themselves?s6

In its next issue, dated 25 Feb. 1888, the Swadesamitran laid down the doctrine that 'that only can be called a just Government which relieves the poorer classes from their distress and derives its revenue by taxing the upper and middle class'.s7 Similarly, the public meeting of the inhabitants of Poona held under the auspices of the Poona Sarvajanik Sabha on 18 March 1888 to protest against the enhancement of salt tax complained in its memorial that the poor in India were already heavily taxed while the higher and more wealthy enjoyed 'comparative exemption and relief'.58 Prithwis Chandra Ray was equally vehement in his criticism of the unequal incidence of taxation and called it 'the greater canker'. After analysing in detail the incidence of salt tax, income tax, and land revenue, he concluded that ‘all the burden of mismanaged finance is ultimately laid on the back of the poor'.59 Later, in his budget speech of 1903, Gokhale also expressed the opinion that any proposed relief in taxation should go to the poorer classes because they contributed to the exchequer 'much more than they should, relatively to their resources'.6o

Interestingly enough, this somewhat radical point of view got a strong backing from J. N. Tata, one of the leading Indian industrialists of the day. In a letter to the London Daily Chronicle in 1895, he wrote:

I have always held the opinion that all taxation in India bears most heavily on the poor and most lightly on the wellto-do classes. Those whose life and property require the greatest protection from Government have the least to pay for it, while those who have nearly nothing to lose, even by a change of Government, have often to forego their meals to pay the dues of Government.61

The wide support that all sections of nationalist opinion gave to .this line of thinking will be further brought out in the examination of the Indian attitude towards the salt tax and the income tax, wherein it is shown that most of the Indian leaders upheld the view that if people had to be taxed it was the rich and not the poor who should be taxed.62

## II. INJURY CAUSED BY TAXATION

In the Indian view over-taxation was one of the major and immediate causes of the extreme poverty of India and even of the oft-recurring famines. The tone of nationalist comment in this regard was set by Dadabhai Naoroji, who, in his 'Memorandum on a Few Statements in the Report of the Indian Famine Commission, 188o', contended that heavy taxation was responsible for the 'starvation and death of millions'.63

This view was reiterated over the years by innumerable nationalist publicists and pressmen of India,64 and was finally adopted by the Indian National Congress as one of its official creeds in 1896, when it declared that the recent famines were due to ‘the great poverty of the people' that had in turu been brought on, among other causes, by 'excessive taxation and over-assessment'.65

What is of even greater interest is that, in addition, some of the Indian leaders—and G. V. Joshi above all—very penetratingly condemned high taxation from the point of view of Indian industry and the Indian entrepreneur. They declared that high taxation hampered the process of capital formation and thus made economic development of the country difficult, if not well-nigh impossible.

It was in 1888 that G. V. Joshi first asserted that heavy taxation by the state ‘greatly interferes with the growth of the wage fund and rise of wages'.66 Later, in 189o, in his long article on the ‘Economic Situation in India', he observed that one of the hardships from which 'the industrial worker' in India suffered was the insufficiency of capital, which in turn arose, inter alia, from the fact that 'the low margin for savings out of our gross earnings is further reduced by heavy taxation'. He calculated that the net national savings (gross production minus necessary expenditure of the people for maintenance) amounted to Rs. 9o crores per year, out of which the government took away Rs. 5o crores as taxes. Thus more than one half of the national annual savings was 'swept into the public treasury to meet, what is for the most part, more or less nonproductive expenditure'. There could obviously be no 'greater economic evil than such a heavy drag upon our industrial progress'.67 These sentiments were echoed by G. K. Gokhale in his first budget speech (19oz). The object 'to be worked for in Indian finance, opined Gokhale, should be 'to keep the level of taxation as low as possible, so as to leave the springs of national industry free play and room for unhampered movement.'68

As a matter of fact, many of the Indian leaders raised their demand for low taxation to the level of a theoretical principle, linking it up with the peculiar economic and political conditions of India. This is very well brought out by the Amrita Bazar Patrika of 7 February 1871 which laid down the simple dictum :

Since the people do not tax themselves and they have no control over the finances of the empire, their cry should be for reduction and not for increase of taxation. A constant and firm demand for reduction is in our humble opinion the only wise policy which a people without a Parliament ought to adopt.

G.V. Joshi emphatically pronounced in 1886 that in India because of 'the peculiar conditions of British rule', it was 'essential to the material and moral progress of the nation that the weight of public burdens should be as light as possible'.69 When after 1898 surpluses began to appear regularly in the Indian budgets, most of the Indian leaders demanded remission of taxation.7o In 19o2 Surendranath Banerjea remarked that when the general taxpayer in a country was 'the starving ryot', the remission of taxation became ‘a matter of paramount obligation on the part of the rulers of the land'.7ı The case for low taxation was lucidly put forth by G.K. Gokhale in his speech on the budget for 1905-06:

In all countries, it is an accepted canon of finance that the weight ot pubiic burdens sncu.d be kept as lignt as possible. ...If this is so in rich European countries, it should be much more so in India, where the revenue is raised from a poor, helpless population, and the larger part is contributed by a broken and exhausted peasantry, and where, owing to the special circumstances of the case, the character of public expenditure is such that a great portion of it has to be spent on objects unconnected or but remotely connected with the moral and material advancement of the people.7²

## III. SOME FACTORS IN THE INDIAN ATTITUDE

Many factors were responsible for the growing popularity of the idea that the country was overtaxed. According to C.N. Vakil, during 1871-19o1 the incidence of taxation in India ranged between 8 to 9 per cent of the average national income.73 Secondly, over taxation or a high level of taxation does create much more hardship in an economically backward country than in a developing country, since it results in 'the forcible expropriation of peasants, artisans, and in a word, all elements of the lower middle-class'. This phenomenon was fully evident in India during the later half of the 19th century. Thirdly, there was no sentiment of patriotism or social consciousness backing the existing level of taxation. On the contrary, the Indian leaders apprehended, as will be brought out later, that because of the foreign nature of the Government of India any revenue in excess of the minimum requirements of administration would be diverted to Imperial purposes. Fourthly, the Indian leaders objected to the government's method of raising Indian revenues through taxes on land and salt and through an excise duty an liquor. Lastly, they correlated the level of taxation with the manner of its utilisation. After all the Government of India acted rigidly within the narrow orbit of the laissez faire theory of the functions of the state and did not undertake any large-scale development activity in those years..

## V. INCOME TAX 75

At the time of the commencement of the period of our study, i.e., in 188o, a modified form of income tax, known as licence tax, was being levied in India. A beginning in this direction had been made in 186o, when James Wilson, the first Finance Member, had imposed a tax on all annual incomes above Rs. 2oo arising out of trade, agriculture, professions and government or non-government service. Frequent changes were made in the tax, which was finally abandoned in 1865, with the announcement that it was to serve as 'the great financial reserve of the country'.76 After that year, taxation of personal incomes was. several times resorted to, abolished, and again taken up, though in varying forms.

In i878, the Governnent of India finally made up its mind to make 'due fiscal provision' to face the by-now regular famines by creating a long-terın Faiine Insurance Fund, ınoney for which was to be raised partly by the inposition of special cesses on land revenue and rent and partly by the levy of a licence tax on the commercial and trading classes and artisans. No central legislation was enacted for the purpose, recourse being had to a series of provincial mneasures. The so-called licence tax was ‘in fact a limited incone-tax assessed on a system of classification according to approximate income'.77 Agricultural, professional, and salaried incomes, as well as income from securities, were exempted from the purview of the tax. The extent and nature of the tax varied from province to province. In North-West Provinces, Oudh, and Madras the taxable minimum was Rs. 2oo, while in Bengal, Bombay, and the Punjab it was Rs. 1oo. Incidence of the tax varied according to the classes into which the assessees were divided, but in no case was it to exceed 2 per cent of the aunual profits of the assessee or Rs. 5oo. An abortive attempt was made by the government in 1879-8o to extend .tlie tax to professional and salaried persons. In 188o, the limit of exemption from the tax was raised to Rs. 5oo. At this tiine the tax yielded over half a million sterling and was paid by 228.447 persous.78

The licence tax was generally opposed by the Indian leaders. at the time of its imposition,79 and they continued to demand its abolition in the later years.8o It should, however, be kept in view that they mainly attacked, not the principle underlying the levy of the licence tax, but the manner of its imposition, which was declared by them to be iniquitous and unfair. First of all, they criticised the tax for 'descending very low in the scale of incomes' and thus falling on a large number of poor persons. In fact, some of them were of the opinion that the tax fell too heavily on the poor while leaving the rich relatively free. For example, the Indu Prakash of 14 January 1878 questioned the fairness of a tax which touched the pockets of the big and influential men, traders and men of business, who were mostly Europeans, so lightly while putting the main burden on the small traders and artisans, who were mostly Indians.81 Similarly, the Indian Spcctator of 23 October 1881 described the tax as a hybrid monster, half coward, half bully, passing by the richest and those most enjoying the benefits of good government and sucking the blood of small traders.82 The nationalist newspapers also pointed out that the poor were further hit by the tendency on the part of the petty officials in charge of the tax assessment and collection towards its maladministration.83 Sowe of the Indian newspapers therefore demauded that the exemption limit of the tax should be raised.84 Interestingly enough, the Bcngalee in its issue dated 17 January 188o vehemently objected to the fixation of a maximun limit of the tax as this would 'only have the effect of treating with exceptional favour those millionaire merchants who least require such favour' and who happened to be Europeans. It should be noted that the Indian newspapers were in this case giving expression to the strong sentiments of the urban poor, which were being ventilated in other forms too.85 In Calcutta, for instance, water carriers (bhistis), farriers (nalbands), and carters went on strike during 1S78-9 in protest against the tax..86 In Snrat, serious riots against high assessment of the tax broke out in April 1878.82

The other ground on which the nationalists attacked the licence tax was that high salaried European and Indian officials and thriving professional men were unjustly exempted from its operation.88 Consequently, they pressed for an extension of the tax to the professional and salaried classes.89 The nationalist criticism of the licence tax was fairly summed up in the Petition adopted at a public meeting of the inhabitants of Poona held on 16 May 188o under the auspices of the Poona Sarvajanik Sabha. The petitioners asserted that the tax 'violated al the principles of political economy taxing the very poor and the famine-stricken ut of all proportion to their means, and exempting the high salaried omcials and other rich classes who could best bear additional taxation'.9o

It should also be noted that two of the leading nationalist newspapers of the day, the Amrita Bazar Patrika and the Hindu, explicitly supported the principle of a direct personal tax and pressed not for the abolition of the licence tax but merely for its transformation into an income tax:9

Having surrendered the customs revenue in 188z, the Government of India was soon compelled by the falling rate of exchange and the rising military expenditure to look for new sources of income. Its gaze naturally fell on the licence tax, which yielded at the time the paltry sum of less than half a crore of rupees. In 1886 it imposed an income tax, which was really 'au enlargement, an extension, and equalisation' of the existing licence tax, at least as far as the lowest classes of income were concerned. The new tax was made applicable to the professional and salaried classes also. The rate of the tax was 4 pies in the rupee for incomes below Rs. 2,ooo a year and 5 pies for incomes above that amount. Incomes of Rs. 5oo a year or less were exempted from the tax. In the case of military officers the exemption limit was Rs. 6,ooo a year. Certain other incomes, like those derived from land—including teaplantations—, salaries, pensions and leave allowances paid in England, profits of shipping companies incorporated in England, interest on securities paid in England, and profits of the railways up to the amount of the guaranteed interest, were also put outside the pale of the tax. In the first year of its operation, i.e. in 1886-7, the income tax was paid by nearly 351,000 persons and it brought in nearly Rs. 1.37 crores. About 30 per cent of this amount was charged on salaries and pensions. In 1902-03, the number of assessees rose to nearly 531,0oo and the amount collected to nearly Rs. 2.1 crores.92

The national leadership was in general divided in its attitude towards the income tax. This division is reflected even more clearly if a long enough span of time is taken into account. In fact, some of the Indian leaders took different and even contradictory attitudes at different times.

The proposal for the levy of an income tax in the 188o's as well as its actual imposition in 1886 were opposed on a wide scale by the nationalist press and leaders.93 This opposition "was not, however, so much on account of the nature of the tax itself as for want of nationalist confidence in any proposals for additional taxation—which was a permanent anathema to the national leadership—and thc need for which, it was believed, was unproved. This was, for example, the manner in which the editor of the Voice of India understood the nationalist position when summarising the stand taken by the Indian newspapers.94 Moreover, prior to 1882 inany of the Indian nationalists were afraid that the imposition of an income tax might be designed to create a budget surplus, which might then be used as an cxcuse to abolish import duties.95 After 1882, when import duties were actually repcaled, many of the Indian leaders opposed the incomc tax on the ground that a better method of raising revenue would be to reimpose the import dutics.96 A few of the Indians also suspected that the revenue derived from the tax would be used by the government to meet the rapidly increasing military expenditure, that was gall and wormwood to the nationa--lists.97 What is of much greater importance, a large number from among the ranks of the opponents of the income tax supported one particular aspect of it, namely, the bringing in of the professional and salaried classes within its purview.98

Having said all this, we should also note that when some of the opponents of income tax declared that direct taxation was not at all suited to India99 or when some of them advocated the extension and enlargement of the salt tax in place of the imposition of the income tax,1oo they clearly revealed that their opposition was inspired not so much by their under. standing of popular weal as by narrow class and selfish interests. In any case, V. N. Mandlik, for one, was frank enough to admit publicly in his speech on the Income Tax Bill of 1886 that 'the syinmetry of the Act is marred by the higher classes being put under an income-tax instead of à licence tax'.ioı

But more significant than this opposition to the income tax is the fact—which has, moreover, been more or less ignored by nearly all the historians of the Indian income tax—that instead of opposing the move for the levy of income tax most of the more powerful organs of nationalist opinion extended some sort of support, often active and vigorous, but also in some cases grudging and lukewarm, to the tax before, at the time of, and after, its imposition.

The Amrita Bazar Patrika actively defended the income tax from the year 187o onwards.1oz Many of the nationalist newspapers lent support to John Strachey's effort in 188o to apply the license tax to salaried and professional incomes.1o3 And when Strachey's measure was withdrawn, as a result of opposition from the Englishmen in India, the Amrita Bazar Patrika was moved to bitter sarcasm. Its issue of 5 March 188o carried the following comment, taking the Indian critics of the measure to task:

Now that the tax upon salaries and professions has been abandoned, even people the most stupid will see how senseless was the outcry raised against the announcement of Sir John Strachey of extending the area of taxation. Asses there must be in everv society, or who would bear washermen's clothes on their backs?...but then they must be employed in carrying washermen's clothes and burdens, and not in leading political associations or editing public prints.

In the later years, a large number of prominent nationalist newspapers, like the Hindu, the Indu Prakash, and the Indian Spectator, asked for the levy of an income tax in place of the existing licence tax.104 Finally, at its first session in 1885, the Indian National Congress passed a resolution urging, among other things, 'the extension of the licence tax to those classes of the community, official and non-official, at present exempted from it... .'1o5 When the income tax was after all levied in 886, it was supported by the Amrita Bazar Patrika, the Hindu, and the Mahratta, as well as by many other nationalist newspapers.106

After 1886 the income tax gained even greater and wider popularity among the nationalist circles, particularly as it became evident that its abandonment would lead to the imposition of other taxes. This fact also substantiates the view that the earlier opposition of some of the Indian leaders was really inspired by their opposition to any new measure of taxation .and not to the income tax as such. Once it was apparent that new taxes had to be levied, the nationalist leaders as a whole opposed the demand for the repeal of the income tax and preferred it to any other form of taxation. This fact was brought out dramatically at the third session of the Indian National Congress in 1887 when a delegate, V. R. Chakravarti Aiyangar, moved an amendment to Resolution VI to the effect that the income tax should be abolished. Immediately there were cries of 'No, no, withdraw,’ 'We don't want to relieve ourselves of taxation,’ 'We won't have it', 'Sit down', and 'Shut up', and Chakravarti Aiyangar was constrained to withdraw his amendment.1o7 At the next session of the Congress in 1888, Madan Mohan Malaviya declared that the Congress ‘approved and desired the imposition, of the income tax on the rich, or those who could afford to pay it',1o8 In 19o2, while moving Resolution XIII, C. Y. Chintamani forcefully condemned the agitation for the abolition of the tax,o9 and the Congress President for that year, S. N. Banerjea, also expressed his lack of sympathy for the demand.o Among the other publicmen of India, Dadabhai Naoroji condemned, in a. letter dated 26 April 1889 to S. M. Malabari, the Indian members of the Viceroy's Council for voting for a salt duty and against an income tax.111 In his Economic History of India, R. C. Dutt praised the income tax (of 186o) for being ‘just and equitable'.112 In 1888 G. V. Joshi put in a vigorous plea for the extension and enhancement of the income tax in place of the enhancement of the salt tax.113 The Indian Association put forth a similar demand in 1894.114

Among the newspapers, the Amrita Bazar Patrika carried on with its crusade in favour of the income taxii5 and in its issue of 18 April 1893 demanded an increase in its incidence. The Hindu of 28 December 1887 opined that 'the income tax should be the foremost permanent source of Indian taxation'.116 The Sudharak of 15 December 189o declared that ‘a demand for the abolition of the tax would be unmitigatedly selfish'.117 The Sanjivani of 10 March 1894 pleaded for an increase in the rate of the income tax on the ground that the existing rate practically amounted to an exemption of the rich.118 The Bengalee of 4 November 1902 also objected to any repeal of the tax. The Mahratta of 29 May 1904 wanted the tax to be retained in spite of the budgetary surpluses; its proceeds, it suggested, should be taken on trust and spent on education, especially industrial education. Many other Indian newspapers took, over the years, a similar stand against the repeal of the tax and in some cases in favour of the enhancement of its incidence.119 In particular, at the time of the enhancement of the salt tax in i888, a large number of the newspapers advocated an increase in the income tax instead.12o

As against this overwhelming phalanx of nationalist opinion that arose in favour of the income tax after 1886, only a few Indian newspapers and leaders asked for its repeal after that year. The Som Prakash of 1o November 189o and the Sahachar of 11 February 1891 from Bengal and the Bharat Jiwan of 17 November 189o from the N-W Provinces and Oudh were the only newspapers to have done so, as far as we have been able to ascertain.12i Among the political leaders, G. R. Chitnavis, the Maharaja of Darbhanga, and Asutosh Mukerjea—none of them being a nationalist leader of the front rank—were the only ones to have opposed the tax.122

As regards the manner of the imposition of the income tax, some of the Indian leaders criticised from the very beginning the exemption from tax of zamindars and landlords. As early as 17 January 188o, the Bengalec put forth this view and refuted the assertion that the immunity granted to the zamindars was justified since they were already paying the Road Cess and the Public Works Cess. These cesses, it contended, were in reality being indirectly paid by the ryots. The exemption of zamindars from the purview of the Income Tax Act of 1885 was criticised by the Hindu of 7, 9 and 12 January 1886, the Indian Nation of 25 February, 1886, and the Gujarat Mitra of 10 January 1886.123 G. V. foshi argued at length in 1888 for the extension of the income tax to the zamindars, talukdars and planters.124 The Hindu of 9 January 189o, and the Kaiser-e-Hind of 18 February 1894125 also made a similar plea.

A few of the Indian leaders also commented on the absolute lack of progression in the Indian income tax. Objecting to all incomes above Rs. 2,ooo a year being grouped together for purposes of this tax, the Hindu asserted that 'the principle of graduated taxation holds good in India as well as in any other country'. It also opined that 2½ per cent of income was too low a rate of taxation for those drawing high salaries.126 In a memorandum dated 8 March 1894, the Indian Association suggested that ‘instead of the necessaries of life being taxed in an indirect manner, the income tax should be raised in a graduated scale, the higher salaries being assessed at a higher percentage'.127 Several other Indian leaders demanded a well graduated income tax.128

Perhaps it would not be out of place to briefly examine at this stage the grounds on which the large majority of the nationalist leaders came to support the income tax or at least its extension to the salaried and professional classes. The first, and perhaps the most important, of these was the understanding that the income tax was the only important tax through which the Europeans in India, whether bureaucrats, employees of private firms, professional men, or merchants, could bemade to contribute their share of the cost of the governance of India.129 The Amrita Bazar Patrika expressed this sentiment in the following words: 'An income tax in India touched the pockets of 1oo Europeans in a 1oo, while it touched the pocket of one native in a thousand'.13o Some of the Indian newspapers even asserted that to oppose the income tax was to play into the hands of the Europeans.131 The second reason for the nationalist support to the income tax was that of social justice and equity in taxation. This extremely democratic point of view was very distinctly articulated in some cases. For instance, the Bengalec of 17 January 188o asserted that ‘if Government is bent upon imposing new taxes rather than retrench expenditure, it is better—far better—that such new and direct taxes should fall on'those who can bear them than that they should fall on the poor'.132 The Amrita Bazar Patrika of 5 March 188o was even more emphatic: 'The real point at issue is not whether direct taxation is suited to India or not, but whether or not the wealthiest members of our society should be made to bear their fair share of burden. If they should, there is no other way of reaching them but by direct taxation.' Next year, the Patrika once again advocated the levy of income tax and, denying the validity of the' argument that it caused so much harassment and inconvenience that its advantages were negated, it wrote in its issue of 29 December 1881: 'First of all, who:cares for the petty inconveniences of the 2. lacs of rich mén in the country, certainly not. the 2oo.millions who pay the salt tax,.the land revenue, and the stamp revenue?'133 The Hindu of 19 December 1884 also pointed out that the poor in India had no reason to object to the rich being taxed; their complaint, on the other hand, would be against the salt tax, excise duty, stamp duty, etc.134- G. V. Joshi also articulated this view very cogently and clearly in 1888. Having defended the imposition of the income tax in 1886, he went on to point out that the burden imposed on the. 'upper and upper-middle classes' by the Income Tax Act of 1886 had not been ‘in any sense or degree, adequate and sufficient or proportionate to the burdens laid on the poorer classes. On the contrary, it was plain that the new measure of taxation...did not go far enough towards an equitable re-adjustment of public taxation....the fact remained that even after the taxation of 1886, the "classes" paid less, and the "masses" more than their due share.'13s

The supporters of the income tax did not, however, support it in each and every respect. While accepting it in principle, they criticised the manner in which it was being levied, According to them, as well as many of the critics of the tax who decided to submit to it with good grace, the worst feature of the income tax as imposed in 1886 was its low limit of exemption. They wanted this limit to be raised from Rs. 500 to at least Rs. 1,ooo a year. Broadly speaking, the nationalist case was that the income tax fell, because of its low exemption limit, with undue severity on people with low incomes, like the poorly paid servants of the government, petty traders, shop-keepers and artisans, who had little margin of income to spare for paying the tax, especially in view of the prevalence of the joint-family system; and that the unsatistactory administration of the tax led to undue harassment and oppression of people earning puny incomes, most of whom were incapable of resisting the extortions and tyrannies of the petty officials put in charge of administering the tax.

Some of the nationalist newspapers suggested, even before the income tax was levied, the fixing of a high exemption limit as a device to remove the sting out of an income tax.136. The Indian National Congress of 1885 also, while recommending the extension of the licence tax to the salaried and professional classes, suggested the maintenance ‘in the case of all classes of a sufficiently high taxable minimum'.137 At the. time of the imposition of the income tax in 1886, many of the Indian leaders pressed for raising its exemption limit.138 At its third session in 1887 the Indian National Congress passed a resolution urging that the taxable minimum be raised to Rs. 1,00o.139 In the following years this resolution, described by Madan Mohan Malaviya as 'the respectable poor man's resolution',14o became a hardy annual at the Congress sessions.141 The nationalist press kept up throughout the years a regular barrage of agitation on the issue.142 Indian publicmen raised the question again and again, on the Congress platform, iņ the legislative councils, and in print.143

The justice and validity of the nationalist criticism of the working of the income tax came in time to be fully recognised by the authorities, so much so that in 19o3 the government raised the taxable minimum to Rs. 1,0oo per. year.144 This. move was enthusiastically welcomed by the national leadership145 which, with rare exceptions, ceased demanding any further concessions in connection with the income tax.146

## V. IMPLICATIONS OF THE NATIONALIST APPROACH

It is clear from the above analysis of the nationalist attitude towards the income tax that in this instance the overwhelming majority of the nationalist leaders was able to rise above narrow group interests. Most of them were themselves lawyers and journalists, who had, moreover, close family connections. with the salaried groups and, yet, a large number of them approved of, and even advocated, the imposition of the income tax. Even of those leaders who opposed the tax as such, a large majority supported, and even demanded, its extension to the professional and salaried classes. Thus, there was the widest possible agreement among the nationalists on the desirability of bringing the 'educated middle-classes' within the purview of some sort of direct taxation. And once the income tax had come into existence, not a single important nationalist voice was raised in favour of its repeal in'the following years.

It should be carefully noted in this context that most of the top leaders of the Indiau National Congress as well as the owners and editors of the more important nationalist newspapers, like the Amrita Bazar Patrika, the Hindu, the Mahratta, the Bengalcc, the Hitavadi, the Sanjivani, the Indu Prakash, the Indian Spectator, and the Swadesamitran, were of certainty liable to be assessed to the income tax and were otherwise escaping the dragnet of direct taxes, or even of most of the indirect ones.147 But they were willing to offer themselves for being taxed and. thus, to suffer this self-injury, so that the foreign officials of the Government of India might also be taxed. This attitude of the Indian nationalist leaders is, on the one hand, a sign of the rising tide of sentiments of nationalism and hostility to the foreign rulers of the land at this time aud, on the other. a proof of the willingness of the nationalist leaders of the day to subordinate their owi personal interests to the general, national weal.148

The only section of the population whose interests in the matter were taken up for advocacy by the national leadership was that consisting of persons with annual incomes below Rs. 1,ooo, or, in other words, of poor shopkeepers and lowpaid government employees. It must be pointed out in this connection that the stand of Indian leadership in favour of raising the exemption limit of the income tax was not only quite correct on the grounds of social justice and equity but was on all counts a legitimate political device for attracting the small shopkeeper and the lower-level government servant to the nationalist fold. Even in this case, however, many ot the Indians fought harder for the reduction of the salt tax and land revenue than for raising the exemption level of the income tax. As a matter of fact, some of the Indian leaders recommended, very consciously and overtly, the above order of priorities in any scheme of grant of relief in taxation.149 It may also be noted that the Indian leadership did not, in fact, mechanically represent in the matter of the income ,tax the interests of the big inerchants and landlords, who were constantly pleading and agitating for the repeal of the tax.150

## VI. SALT TAX

The tax on salt was the second most important source of revenue to the Government of India during the years 188o to 1905. The mode of taxing salt varied from province to province. In Bombay, the tax took the form of an excise duty, in

Bengal it was levied chiefly as customs duty on imported salt,151 and in Madras, Northern India, and the Punjab it was included in the price fixed by the government on its own production of salt as a monopoly.

The rates at which the salt tax was levied differed widely from province to province till 1878-79,152 when near uniformity was introduced after the notorious inland customs line was abolished. The salt duties were equalised by revising them downwards in Northern India from Rs. 3 to Rs. 2-8 per maund and in Bengal from Rs. 3-4 to Rs. 2-14 and upwards in Madras and Bombay from Rs. 1-13 to Rs. 2-8. For the government the adjustment proved advantageous, as the salt revenue increased .by nearly £1 million during 1875-77 to 1879-81.153 But the burden of this increase was perhaps not felt by the people to the' full extent of the increase because at the same time the market price of salt was favourably influenced by the conteinporaneous extension of transportation facilities.

Even before 1858, the salt duties had come in for criticism at the hands of prominent Indians.154 Up to 1882, the Indian leaders generally pressed for a reduction, or even for the abolition,·of the salt tax; and since the process of equalisation of the tax begun in 1859 had led to a steep rise in it in the Bombay Presidency, naturally the criticism of the tax, as well as of the increase in it, was most vocal in that province.155 For instance, in· 188o Dadabhai Naoroji. called 'the salt tax a stigma' upoı the British name.156

We may also simultaneously take note of the fact that another section, though a minor.'one, of the Indian leadership tended to support the salt' tax.'Naturally enough, this section was to be found mainly in Bengal, where changes in the salt tax had been in the downward direction and where the zamindars had always shown a keen anxiety to defend their own interests at the .cost of the interests of the ryots. Two early representatives of the pro-salt tax lobby in Bengal were Raja Digambar Mitra and Kristodas Pal.157 But much more surprising is the fact that we find the Amrita Bazar Patrika also advocating an increase in the salt tax in place of other taxes like the road cess.158 The reason advanced by its editors for' taking such an unexpected stand was that the salt tax in Bengal was primarily an import tax on salt brought from Liverpool and Cheshire and an increase in the tax was likely to be borne entirely by foreigners.159

In 1882, the government reduced the salt tax, fixing it at Rs. 2 per maund throughout the country except in Burma or in the trans-Indus districts of the Punjab. The nationalist reaction to the step was a mixed one. The Mahratta, several other Bombay papers, and some of the Bengal papers, welcomed it and urged the government to reduce the tax further or even to abolish it.16o On the other hand, the Amrita Bazar Patrika and several other papers from Bengal and Northern India felt that the step was not really called for as the salt tax was never felt to be oppressive by the people and that relief should have, instead, been given to those paying the licence tax.161

Between 1882 and 1888, many nationalist voices were raised against the salt tax. For instance, G. V. Joshì, who can be credited with having pioneered the anti-salt tax agitation, started his campaign against the salt tax during these years. In his paper on 'Ways and Means of Meeting the Additional Armv Expenditure', published in April 1886, he made a detailed analysis of the salt tax and its impact on the life and labour of the people. He protested vehemently against the proposal for enhancing the salt tax, condemned the fatal disposition in some quarters' to look upon it as a financial reserve, and deprecated ‘its being maintained as a part of the permanent taxation of the country'.16z At the very first session of the Indian National Congress in 1885, S. A. Swaminath Iyer and V. S. Pantulu inveighed against any attempt to enhance the salt tax and urged the Congress and the people to raise their voice against any such eventuality.163 Some of the leading nationalist newspapers also pleaded for a reduction in, or :abolition of, the salt tax during these ycars.164 On the other side, several·other newspapers advocated in 1886 the 'enhancement of the salt tax in place of the imposition of the income tax.165

By 1888, the finances of the Government of India were once again in the doldrums as a result of the conquest and annexation of Upper Burma, military operations on the North-West Frontier, and the continued fall in exchange, and imposition of fresh taxation once again became imperative. This led James. Westland, the then Finance Member, to enhance the duty on salt by an executive order, dated 19 January 1888, from Rs. 2 per maund to Rs. 2-8.166 In 1902, the gross income from the tax amounted to Rs. 9.1 crores as against Rs. 7.6 crores in 1888.167

The enhancement of the salt tax aroused a powerful wave of dissatisfaction, anger, and protest in India. ## VII. REASONS FOR NATIONALIST ATTACK ON SALT TAX

What were the economic reasons advanced by the Indian leadership for attacking the salt tax? They started by raising a doctrinal objection. They declared that it was one of the basic canons of good finance and fair taxation that a prime necessity of life, which salt obviously was, should not be taxed, and that too to such an inordinate extent.19o They then questioned the very basic formulation of the administrators that even though the tax yielded'such rich revenue its burden was not felt by the people to be at all oppressive, since it was spread over a large part of the population.191 They said that its incidence should be gauged not in the abstract but in the context of, and with reference to, the stark poverty of the people of India. When the low level of income of the people was taken into consideration, even the tax burden of a few annas per head could be seen for what it was in reality—back-breaking. Explaining this line of argument, G. V. Joshi observed that the salt tax would not have been so mischievous if, among other things, there had been .a 'steady, advancing improvement in the condition of the poorer classes, so as to leave them a larger and larger margin of incoime for their necessary expenditure'.19² Graphically illustrating this assertion, N. V. Barve, mover of the resolution on the salt tax at the Allahabad Congress of 1888, pointed out: 'There are millions on millions to whom that eight annas extra means eight meals less in a year, and that to people who, even before this enhancement, never got more than one meal in the 24 hours.'193 Some of the nationalists sought to lend added șupport to their argument by making mathematical calculations of the incidence of the salt tax. For example, in 189o, Pringle Kennedy calculated that while the average incidence of the salt tax, at an income of Rs. 5 per month for a family of 5, was 6 pies in the rupee, the income tax was only 4 pies in the rupee in the lower slab.194 Similarly, D. E. Wacha calculated in the same year that salt duty constituted 1.1 per cent of per capita income even if the latter was taken to be £2.195

The chief ground for the nationalist attack on the salt tax was its ‘unjust and vicious' character arising out of the fact that it fell most heavily, oppressively, and 'cruelly' on the poorest of the poor' of the land, who could not afford to pay any taxes, and whose income barely sufficed—or in many cases failed to suffice—to maintain body and soul together.196 This was the main reason advanced by the Indian National Congress for repeatedly opposing the enhancement of the tax.197 In this context, a few of the Indian leaders also took note of the financial inequity and the regressive character of the salt tax. This was the main ground on which, as early as 1871, Dadabhai Naoroji objected to the salt tax. In his 'Statement to the Select Committee on East India Finance', he pointed out that the incidence of its burden on the poor was not similar 'to that on the other classes for the share they pay towards revenue'. After stating that the burden of the salt tax on a poor cooly, workman, or peasant was about 4 per cent of his paltry income of 2os a year, he contended that ‘4 per cent out of 2os is far more important to the poor man than 1o or 2o per cent out of the income of the richer classes'.198 Similarly, in 188o, the anonymous writer of the article, 'The Finances of India under Lord Lytton', in the Journal of the Poona Sarvajanik Sabha, wrote that 'the fact that this tax reaches in its incidence the poor and the rich alike and not proportionately to their means, is the most powerful reason why it should be religiously kept at such a low rate as not to fall oppressively upon the poor masses'.199 Similar sentiments were expressed by other leaders.2o0 It was, however, G.V. Joshi who articulated this view most clearly and unambiguously in 1886 and argued that 'the equality in the amount paid is a false equality, it does not imply true equality—namely, the "equality of sacrifice".'zoi Two years later, in 1888, he endeavoured to show that the salt duty enhancement 'tends to aggravate the inequalities in the distribution of public taxation'as between the poor and the rich.2oz

The next test applied by the Indian leadership was that of the effect of the tax on the consumption of salt. It should be noted in this connection that the British Indian administrators were in the past equally willing to put their salt tax policy to such a test.zo3 Even James Westland had, while enhancing the tax in 1888, expressed the hope 'that the burden of a duty of Rs. 2-8 will not now have any effect in restricting the rate at which the consumption is increasing'.zo4 The lndian leaders were, on the other hand, convinced that the practical effect of the high rate at which the salt tax was maintained, and, in particular, of its enhancement in 1888, was to restrict and diminish the consumption of salt. This in turn meant tremendous suffering and hardship for the people, since salt was a commodity that was essential for the very physical existence and well-being of man.205

Once again, it was G. V. Joshi who put forward the nationalist case in a reasoned and powerful manner. As usual, his main appeal was to 'facts'. He set out to make a detailed statistical analysis of the experience of the last 19 years.2o6 He showed that changes in the rates of the tax were invariably followed by changes in consuinption, increases following reductions of duty, and decreases following enhancement of duty'.zo7 He further pointed out that it was only the very poor who felt compelled to cut down their .consumption of salt when its price increased. While the well-off or nearly well-off members of the middle and upper middle classes certainly did not have to ever stint on their salt, on the consumption of the poorest strata of the population ‘the slightest variation in the salt duty tells in a perceptible degree'. Any fall in the consumption of salt would not, therefore, be shared in by the entire population; it would be borne entirely by the poorer sections.2o8 He resumed the argument in 1896 in his essay, 'The Salt Duty Question'. Here he pointed to the experience of the years since 1888, and asserted that his gloomy prophecies had proved to be too correct. Quoting statistics, he showed that the rate of growth of the consumption of salt had not remained the same after 1888 as it was before 1888. From 1882 to 1887 this rate had been on the average 3.8 per cent per year, from 1887-8 to 1894-5 it was 0.12 per cent. Wnat was even worse, the consumption of salt had 'not even kept pace with .the growth of population during the period'. Joshi calculated that the consumption of salt per head in India, which was 8.8 lbs. in 188o-81, had increased to 10.3 lbs. in 1886-7; but that by 1894-5 it had again fallen to 9.5 lbs. He estimated that the lower 8o millions of the population were compelled in 1894-5 to ‘eat from two to three lbs. less salt per capita than they used to do 8 years ago'. And so, the conclusions he had arrived at drove him to exclaim:

A fiscal system which renders it necessary to so bleed the starving poor—no matter how great the necessities of policy —is open to grave condemnation. No canon of finance, no. principle of political economy, could ever sanction such a merciless application of the fiscal lancet; and no necessity, however imperative—and no financial emergency, however pressing,—could be pleaded in justification of such cynical indifference to the sufferings of the poor.

Then came the final judgment, born of despair and anger: 'But our financial administration knows no such weakness of common humanity and shows little sympathy with struggling poverty.'2o9

Another leader who played a prominent role in the anti-salt tax agitation after 1888 was G.K. Gokhale. He followed, or rather repeated, in all essential respects, Joshi's reasoning as well as his statistics. His contribution in this respect was that he popularised Joshi's work.210

Another charge levelled against the salt tax by the nationalists was its tendency to injure agriculture by starving land and cattle of sufficient quantities of an essential commodity such as salt.2ı1 G. V. Joshi and G. K. Gokhale, in addition, condemned the salt tax for being a tax on an industry—the salt industry-and because it resulted in a system of monopoly which affected India's ‘economic growth in an essential and vital particular'.2i2 Moreover, they alleged that various factors emanating from the imposition of the salt tax, like the government monopoly of salt, equalisation of the salt tax in different provinces, and high rates of the tax had been responsible for restricting and destroying a flourishing local industry in large parts of the country, and particularly in Bengal, and for driving the indigenous producer out of the field.213 G. V. Joshi went to the extent of making the accusation that 'the total decline of the industry in Bengal marks a transfer of it, by :skilful fiscal manipulation, to foreign hands'.2i4 He also expressed the apprehension that in Bombay too 'the fatal Bengal policy' was being pursued 'though in a disguised form, without the intermediate stage of monopoly'.215

## VIII. ALTERNATIVE SOURCES OF TAXATION

Tacitly or otherwise, many of the Indian leaders recognised that the budgetary deficits,.which had forced the government to maintain the salt tax at such a high rate, had somehow or other to be covered and that they were, therefore, obliged to suggest other ways and means, less objectionable than the salt tax, of raising the necessary additional revenue. This task, they believed, was not really a difficult one. The first alternative they recommended was the re-imposition of import duties.2i6 Another alternative measure that some of them advocated was enhancement 'of the income tax²i7 and its extension to classes that had hitherto been exempted from its. purview.zi8 A few of them pressed for a general imposition of taxes on the rich and on the products consumed by them; they did not, however, specify the forms such taxes might take.2i9 Some of them also felt that the scope for the retrenchment of the existing civil and military expenditure should have been exhausted before the decision to raise the salt tax was taken in 1888.2zo Almost all the nationalist newspapers of Bombay Presidency, for example, suggested at the time that the financial deficit might have been better met by reducing the salaries of European government servants by about one-fourth.22i

Joshi and Gokhale also pointed to the built-in fiscal mechanism within the framework of the salt tax itself that should have been relied upon to increase its yield in the long run. This mechanism was based on the well-known principle of taxation that a source of revenue like the salt tax might be made most fruitful if a low rate of tax was accompanied by unrestricted consumption. In 1896, G. V. Joshi calculated that if the consumption of salt had continued to expand after 1888 at the same rate at which it had increased before, it would have risen by nearly 1o million maunds by 1895-6 and the state would have derived at the old rate of tax of Rs. 2 per maund an increase of revenue to the extent of 2 crores of rupees—almost equal to the amount of additional revenue that had actually been obtained under the enhanced tax with its concomitant low consumption of salt.2zz This principle of taxation was commended for application to salt by Gokhale in his budget speech of 19o2;223 and, finally, clearly enunciated in his budget speech of 1903: 'The soundest and best policy in the matter—even financially—would, therefore, seem to be to raise an expanding revenue on an expanding consumption under a diminishing scale of duties.'2z4

The Indian leaders were angry at the government's refusal to adopt any of the alternative measures of taxation suggested by them and many of them accused the government of cowardice and partiality for bowing before the pressure of the British manufacturers, the British officials, and the influential richer strata of the Indian population, while bullying and burdening the 'helpless' and 'speechless' millions of the land. For instance, the Kesari of 24 January 1888 made the following sarcastic comment:

If His Lordship had' resorted to import duties on cotton piece-goods from Manchester, it would have created consternation among the manufacturers of Manchester....Will the kind British Government prove faithless to those who render good assistance during the elections for Parliament? If the income tax be increased its burden will mostly fall upon the high European officers and traders, and the rates of exchange being already high, they will find it very crushing and will rise in rebellion.... Will a wise man like Lord Dufferin ever venture to have his fair reputation sullied by rousing such opposition? In short, Lord Dufferin was wise in rejecting both these alternatives and in having recourse to an increase in the salt duty, remembering that the disarmed and the loyal people of India will not, as long as they are alive, say 'no' to any demand and that they need not be very strong in body as Government has graciously undertaken to protect them.225

## Similarly, the Indian Spectator of 22 January 1888 wrote :

Or perhaps they (government) are afraid of 'public opinion'. For, it is certain that in the case of such an increase in the taxation of the rich classes, Government would have a bad time of it from 'representatives of the two hundred and fifty millions' whereas these two hundred millions and odd have no voice to protest against the enhancement of the duty on salt. Whatever may be said of their sense of justice, it cannot be denied that the Government of India are wise in their generation.226

The Mahratta of 22 January, the Hindu of 25 January, the Amrita Bazar Patrika of 26 January, the Bengalee of 28 January, and the Sanjivani of 4 February 1888zz7 commented in a like vein. G. V. Joshi too passed a similar judgment on the motives lying behind the government's action in the matter.z28 The Mahratta of 18 March 1888 even claimed that the real question involved in the issue of the salt tax was whether India existed for Indians or for others.

## IX. POLITICAL LESSONS

Many of the nationalist critics of the salt tax strongly censured the conduct of Peary Mohan Mookerjea and Dinshaw

Petit, the two nominated members of the Legislative Council, in extending their support to the enhancement of the tax in 1888;229 and cited it as a proof-positive of the nationalist contention that the existing Legislative Councils did not and could not reflect correctly Indian opinion or the interests of the masses of the country, and that these Councils had, therefore, to be reformed by introducing popular element in them.23o This conclusion was effectively driven home by G. V. 'Joshi who remarked that the debate on the enhancement of the tax ‘was discreditable to the Legislative Council;... that it 'was more discreditable to the native members..; and that, :above all, it was most discreditable to the system....'231 Similarly, Madan Mohan Malaviya said at the Congress of 189o: 'We would much rather that there were no non-official members at all on the Councils than that there should be members who are not in the least in touch with the people and who being ignorant of their true conditions and requirements, betray a cruel want of sympathy with them.' Describing P. M. Mookerjea and Dinshaw Petit as 'these big honourable gentlemen, enjoying private incomes and drawing huge salaries', he asked rhetorically: 'Do you think, gentlemen, such members would be appointed to the Council if the people were allowed any voice in their selection?' Amidst shouts of 'no, no, never' from the audience, he again enquired: 'And even if they were, by some mistake, once appointed, would they not be scornfully rejected at the next election?'32

## X. CONCLUSION

The above analysis of the nationalist attitude towards the salt tax clearly shows that the demand for reduction in, and abolition of, the tax was taken up by the Indian national leadership on a national scale and as a national policy. The demand was in fact utilised to question and assail the very foundations of the prevailing financial policy. Moreover, the interests of the poor of the land were, in this instance, correctly and' volubly expressed by the nationalist leaders, who, in this way, made an attempt to carry the poor with the growing national movement. This was, in fact, explicitly understood by the Indian leaders to be so. Introducing the resolution demanding a reduction in the salt tax at the Sixth National Congress in 189o, Pringle Kennedy addressed the following exhortation to the delegates :

Fellow delegates, will you show that they accuse you falsely who say, your case is all for yourself, that thìs movement is simply one for the substitution of Indian dark-skinned for European fair-skinned Brahmins, by saying: 'If in no other way can this yoke be made less grievous, tax us, tax the rich, but let the poor go!’233

Similarly, G. S. Khaparde, a delegate to the Congress of 1892.. described the resolution on the salt tax as a poor man's. ‘prayer to the Congress'.234

Conversely, the national leadership did not, in this instance. at all represent the interests of the rich of the land; otherwise, like the rich man's spokesmen, they too would have supported the salt tax and its enhancement in the belief that it would help ward off other taxes, for example, the income tax, on the rich.235 The divergence between the nationalist stand and the interests of the rich can be seen clearly if the former is contrasted with the pro-tax attitude adopted and publicly expressed by the contemporary spokesmen of the zamindars, big merchants, and unofficial Englishmen. In 1882, Durga Charan Laha, the spokesman of Calcutta traders in the Imperial Legislative Council, opposed the ‘questionable policy' of reducing the salt tax on ‘sentimental grounds'. It would have been better, he suggested, if the government had instead removed direct taxes which were unsuited ‘to the circumnstances of the country'.236 Maharaja Jotendra Mohan Tagore, who represented the Bengal zamindars in the Council, endorsed in full Laha's stand.237 The enhancement of the salt tax in 1888 was, simnilarly, fully supported by Peary Mohan Mookerjea, another representative of the Bengal zamindars, Dinshaw Petit, the spokesman of Bombay merchants, and various spokesmen of the British officials and businessmen in India.238 Supporting the enhancement of the tax, the Report of the Committee of the Bengal National Chamber of Commerce for 1887 claimed that it was 'far less objectionable than the doubling of the present rate of the Income Tax would have been'.239 And the Secretary of the Chamber and its President for 1889 opposed the demand for the reduction of the tax with no less vigour.24o

## XI. EXCISE REVENUE

Another indirect tax and an imnportant source of revenue was the excise imposed as duty upon the manufacture and as fees for licences for the sale of intoxicating spirits, hemp drugs, and opium. The gross revenue obtained from this source increásed from Rs. 1.18 crores in 186o-01 to Rs. 3.19 crores in 188o-01 and Rs. 6.64 crores in 1902-03.241 Only that aspect of the revenue which was derived from spirits—and indigenous or country-spirits at thatand which constituted the major portion of the total excise revenue has been dealt with here.242 The system of levying excise duty on country spirits varied from place to place and time to time. These systems can, however, be roughly grouped together under two broad categories: (i) the central distillery system under which a fixed duty was inposed on each gallon of spirit mnanufactured and issued for sale; and (ii) the outstill system under which duty was levied not on the quantity produced but in the gross by lump-sum payments on the basis of auctions. Obviously, there was much less governnental check over consumption of liquor under the outstill system than under the central distillery system.243 In the 189o's it was the declared policy of the Government of India to raise a maximum of.revenue from a minimum of consumption of spirits, that is, to increase the rates of duty and to restrict the number of places for the sale of liquor to such an extent that its consumption was minimised while, at the same time, illicit manufacture of liquor was kept within bounds.244 At least after 189o, it became the settled and proclaimed policy of the government to extend the central distillery system and to gradually abolish the outstill system.245

The Indian leadership' attached a great deal of importance to the question of consumption of intoxicating liquors and their taxation. It was as a whole anti-liquor and opposed to the spread of the drinking habit in the country. It looked upon drinking as a deadly evil—'a terrible scourge'—which was morally corrupting and 'vicious', economically impoverishing, and physically weakening.246 Some of the Indian leaders even believed that the spread of drinking would hamper the growth of industries by adversely affecting the efficiency of labour.247

But in condemning liquor-consumption, the lndian leaders once again 'struck the first blow at the government'. Putting the blame for the spread of the drinking habit squarely and primarily on the shoulders of the government and its excise policy, they censured the government for deliberately or unintentionally encouraging, or at least failing to actively discourage, drinking in order to maximise excise revenue.248 Criticism of the official excise policy waș particularly sharp and persistent in Bengal during the 188o's, because of the prevalence in the province of the outstill system, which was .accused of cheapening the price of liquor.249

The Indian leaders also alleged that even when the government publicly and professedly claimed to adhere to a policy of discouraging drinking, in practice the effect was just the reverse.25o The tendency on the part of the administration to lavish praise and promotion on officials under whose jurisdiction excise revenue increased and to pass censure on those who could produce no such results provided, in real life, an incentive to the officials to be zealous in increasing excise revenue, .even if it meant an increase in the consumption of liquor.251 It might be observed in this connection that the spokesmen of the government were no less vehement in rebutting the nationalist charge and in asserting that the increase in the excise revenue was in fact due mainly to higher rates of duty and stricter excise control.252 Interestingly enough, an increase in liquor consumption was indirectly acknowledged by government officials in certain other contexts, but then it was merely offered as an indication of the increasing prosperity of the people.253 Consistent with their entire approach to the question, the nationalists refuted this contention and maintained that, on the contrary, liquor brought ruin and destitution and misery to its imbibers and their families.254

The nationalist leaders were convinced that education would be too slow a way of fighting the evil and that only adininistrative measures could put an immediate halt to the spread of drinking.255 They therefore neglected popular agitation against drinking and concentrated their efforts on cajoling or browbeating the government to subordinate revenue considerations. to the moral well-being of the people and, hence, to follow a policy of active discouragement of drinking.256 Nearly all the administrative measures advocated by them were designed to make liquor more expensive or more difficult to obtain through a reduction in the number of distilleries and liquor shops. The measure which gained the widest favour with them was that of 'Local Option'. They demanded that in deciding the question of opening a new liquor shop or distillery at any place local public opinion expressed directly or through the local municipal bodies must be considered the decisive factor.257 Another popular demand, though confined mostly to Bengal, was for the abolition of the outstill system.258 When the outstills were abolished in the larger part of Bengal during 1889 and 189o, the Indian National Congress itself welcomed the step.259 The nationalists also favoured the enhancing of import and excise duties on liquor.26o Some of them wanted the government to take purely administrative steps like the closing of liquor shops and the adoption of 'stringent measures... in granting licences to retail liquor shops'.26ı Surprisingly enough, prohibition was not one of the remedies that found advocacy on a significant scale in the nationalist ranks. Perhaps, it was thought to be outside the realm of the possible.262

The above analysis shows that the nationalist attitude towards excise revenue derived from liquor was purely nationalistic in motivation and content. Even though liquor excise was a fruitful source of revenue, which was, moreover, paid by the actual consumers of liquor only, thus obviating the need for imposing additional taxes on the general mass of population, the nationalist leaders looked upon it solely from the point of view·of the general good as they saw it and did not hesitate to condemn it. This aspect of the nationalist policy can be viewed from another angle also. On the one hand, those among the Indian leaders who consumed liquor would be clear losers if the nationalist plea for costlier liquor was adopted. On the other hand, the nationalist demand for reducing the supply of liquor, which would result in a fall in the excise revenue, was indirectly opposed to the interests of the non-consumers of liquor as tax-payers. It is thus obvious that in this, instance we have another example of nationalistic altruism in matters of finance.

Two other aspects of the question under discussion may also be noted. Firstly, while the tendency towards utilisation of anti-liquor agitation as a powerful political weapon that the nationalist movement in India was to reveal later was not yet present, the consciousness was dawning in the ranks of the Indian leadership that anti-liquor agitation might be useful in training the people for political work. Speaking at the meeting of the Anglo-Indian Temperance Association, held on 17 April 1905, Dadabhai Naoroji observed :

They had 3oo branches of the Association in India, and this meant that people of all classes, creeds, and of every position from the top to the bottom, were learning the great lesson of associating together, becoming like brothers to each other, working for one great cause.... This association was starting not only the cause of breaking down drinking in India, but the equally good cause of raising the people to a higher position of thought and of union which would be of the greatest use to them.263 (Emphasis added)

Secondly, the very tendency to blame the foreign government for the spread of the drinking-habit lent to the nationalist excise policy a nationalist undertone.

## XII. OPIUM REVENUE

Opium was another important source of revenue for the

Government of India. Till 1884, it was the second, and after 1884 the third, most productive item in the budget. Its yield, however, fluctuated violently from year to year, thus lending a certain amount of instability to Indian finance. For example, the net opium revenue amounted to Rs. 8.45 crores in 188o-81, Rs. 5.70 in 1890-91, and Rs. 4.97 in 1900-01, while in 1897-8 it even went down to Rs. 2.70 crores.264 The opium revenue was derived from export of Bengal opium which was produced under a system of state monopoly in Bihar and N.W. Provinces and Oudh under the jurisdiction of the Bengal government and from the levy of a heavy export duty in Bombay on Malwa opium. Most of the export opium was sent to China. A part of the Bengal opium was sold in India under the excise system and revenue from its sale was credited under excise. The excise revenue from opium increased steadily from Rs: 36 lakhs in 1870-71 to Rs. 103 lakhs in 1900-01.265

The opium trade with China was criticised throughout the 19th century by various sections of the press and public opinion of Britain on moral and humanitarian grounds. In 1888, an organised movement against the opium trade and its patronage and promotion' by the government came into existence in Britain with the founding of the Society for the Suppression of the Opium Trade.266 As a result of this agitation, the House of Commons passed a resolution on 10 April 1891, affirming that the system of opium revenue in India was immoral and urging the Government of India to 'cease to grant licences for the cultivation of the poppy and sale of opium in British India, except to supply the legitimate demand for medicinal purposes'; and in 1893 a Royal Commission was appointed to investigate the question in its entirety. The Commission reported in 1895 and advised against any disturbance of the status quo.267 All these years, the Government of India strongly objected to the proposals for prohibiting or restricting the opium trade, mainly on the ground of financial and political inexpediency of such steps.268

The attitude of the Indian leaders towards the opium trade and the revenue arising from it makes an interesting study. Here was a situation in which they had to chocse between national self-interest-which was their guiding factor in most other respects—and humanitarianism and philanthropy-on the basis of which they usually appealed to the British people and government to grant them economic and political concessions.

As regards the moral aspects of the opium revenue, most of the national leaders assumed a position that was at great variance with the official position, and nearer the position of the English critics of the revenue, since these leaders openly conceded that the opium trade and revenue were grossly immoral and deserving of severe condemnation. As early as 187o, Keshab Chandra Sen had asked for the abolition of 'that iniquitous opium traffic which kills thousands of the poor Chinese people'.269 In 188o, Dadabhai Naoroji indignantly proclaimed : 'This opium trade is a sin on England's head, and a curse on India for her share in being the instrument.'27o Writing in the same vein, the Mahratta of 7 August 1881 protested against the Indian people being made to carry on their head 'the curse of being the poisoners of 4oo millions of human beings'.271

Dadabhai Naoroji and R. C. Dutt disagreed with the officials on the nature of the opium revenue also. While the official view was that the opium revenue was paid not by the Indian people but by the Chinese consumers,272 Naoroji and Dutt averred that it was in fact a disguised tax on the people of India since, in the absence of state monopoly of opium, the governiment profits, known as opium revenue, would have accrued to the people of India.273

But on the main aspect of the opiumn question, viz., abandoninent of the opium trade, differences of opinion arose within the ranks of the Indian leaders. While a large majority adopted an approach that very nearly coincided with that of the Government of India, a significant number favoured giving up of the opium trade on humanitarian grounds. Opposing the sacrifice of the opium revenue for mere sentimental reasons, the former argued that the opium revenue constituted such an important part of Indian finances that its cessation was bound to lead to the imposition of new taxes. They therefore strongly disapproved of the anti-opium agitation being carried on in England at the time.274 Thus the Amrita Bazar Patrika observed in its issue dated 9 July 188o : ‘To abandon such a source of revenue and then make good the gap by ruinous taxes wrung from the life-blood of an already impoverished people would, according to our humble ideas of morality, be in the highest degree immoral.'275 The hyper-moralistic organ of the militant Sadharana Brahmo Samaj, the Brahmo Public Opinion, warned in its issue of 15 July 188o against the fiscal hazards of taking too moralistic an attitude towards the opium revenue.276 Even the Indian Spectator, the most consistent and vehement advocate of social reforms in India, branded, on 12 March 1882, the anti-opium English agitators as ‘a powerful class of well-meaning but ignorant fanatics'.277 Another reformist newspaper, the Hindu, declared in its issue dated 3 July 1893 that it would agree to the abolition of the opium revenue only if the British tax-payers promised to make good the resulting loss to the Government of India.278

Many of the Indian leaders waxed sarcastic at what they described as the attempt of the anti-opium Englishmen to practise philanthropy at the cost of other people. They suggested that if the British people were genuine in their regard for the welfare of the Chinese people they should persuade their own government to make good the loss of the opium revenue to the Indian governmnent.279 Interestingly enough, the ranks of the anti-opium agitators in Britain, whose integrity was questioned in this fashion and who were branded as fanatics and fools, included such pro-Indian National Congress stalwarts às the Members of Parliament W.S. Caine and Samuel Smith.

This group of Indian leaders also advanced a few other interesting arguments in favour of their stand against the prohibition of the opium trade. Firstly, they contended that before starting to undertake a reform of the vices in China the authorities and reformers in England and India owed a duty to condemn and fight against the drink evil in India, which was no less pernicious than opium smoking.28o Secondly, some of them averred that any action by India resulting in the giving up of its opium exports would prove to be infructuous since the gap would be readily filled by countries like Persia, Turkey and the United States or by the Chinese themselves by extending the cultivation and manufacture of opiumn in their country. Thus, the ends of philanthropy would still remain unsatisfied, and only the Indian people would have lost a source of income.281 Curiously enough, the newspapers did not offer the argument that prohibition of the opium trade would adversely affect the Indian merchants engaged in it. On the contrary, in its issue dated 23 December 1893, the Sanjivani. an anti-opium paper, accused the merchants of supporting the opium trade because it yielded them large profits.28z The absence of trade motive from, and the predominance of the revenue motive in, the armoury of arguments advanced by the nationalist opponents of the anti-opium agitation show that the interests of opiumn merchants did not perhaps play an important role in the formation of the opium policy of this group of the national leadership

The second group of Indian leaders, which was in favour of prohibiting opium production and banning of the opium trade out of humanitarian considerations, proposed the bridging of the consequent revenue gap by retrenchment of expenditure. In this respect the position of many newspapers like the Mahratta, the Hindu, the Som Prakash, and the Ananda Bazar Patrika was ambivalent, depending on the personal proclivities of whosoever happened to be writing the editorials at a particular moment of time. The anti-opiun nationalist sentiments were cogently and vigorously sunimed up by the Ananda Bazar Patrika of 20 July 188o as follows:

What code of ethics has taught the rulers the argument that, because China must, to satisfy her wants, necessarily import the drug from elsewhere, there is no harm in India supplying her with it? As well might a robber justifv his acts by pleading necessity, and a murderer by remarking that the person whom he had murdered would have certainly died some day, though not by his hands. The British Government can reduce the public expenditure in India by half if only it wishes to do so; it can thereby easily discontinue the inequitable trade in opium.283

Dadabhai Naoroji, who was a consistent opponent of opium trade, even though he recognised that most of the other national leaders were not one with him on this question,284 made an attempt to utilise the opium debate then going on in England to propagate the cause of Indian nationalism, thus proving once again that there was no public issue which he could not press into the service of the cause to which he had dedicated all his life and passion. Denying that opium trade benefited Inia in any manner, he claimed in 188o that the profits of this trade in reality went to meet the drain to England. India received only 'the curse of the Chinese race'. As a matter of fact, the opium revenuc merely disguised the serious economic malady and fiscal bankruptcy from which the country was suffering. If this 'cursed' opium trade had not existed, he argued, India would not have been able to meet England's claims and 'India's iniseries would have much sooner come to the surface' and been redressed. The opium trade had,

therefore, only prolonged the agonies of India'.285 Repeating this argument word by word in an address to a conference called by the Society for the Suppression of the Opium Trade in London in 1886, Dadabhai urged the Society to see the problem of opium in the over-all context of the main problem of India, viz., that of poverty. If India was allowed to keep what it produced and to freely develop its material resources, he averred, it could yield a large enough revenue to easily enable the government 'to dispense with the curse of the opium revenue'.286

Thus, the attitude of the national leaders towards the opium revenue was the result of the interplay of two, and in this instance conflicting, tendencies-humanitarianism and national self-interest. While some of these leaders tried to reconcile these two opposing considerations and, when forced to make a choice, embraced humnanitarianisim, many others, including M. G. Ranade. B. G. Tilak. S. N. Banerjea, and the brothers Motilal and Sisir Kumar Ghose, let the fear of fresh taxation and considerations of national self-interest triumph over their moral hesitations. At the same time, it may be noted that the Indian leadership did not offer the same type of intense and bitter and widespread and united opposition to the British agitation for the surrender of the opiuin revenue as it did from 1877 to 1882 to the agitation for the surrender of the customs revenue, even though the dimensions of the financial loss involved would have been much larger in the case of the former.287 This can perhaps only be explained by the fact that in the case of opium revenue the loss would have been only financial, not affecting any industrial interest, while in the case of the cotton import duties the loss was financial as well as industrial. Obviously the Indian leadership was of the view that, while the mere financial loss might be borne under protest, if not with equanimity and even satisfaction of having stood true to principles, the industrial loss had to be fought tooth and nail since it involved the long term and 'real interests of the nation.
