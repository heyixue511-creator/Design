# EDITED DESIGN ![](images/aa904c6f86cebad1401114fba58073ab942b763989992e07a864f1cff6eaf8e0.jpg) Postmodern product design took two directions. The first was in expensive, limited-edition pieces by major architects and designers, and more improvisational one-off objects by artisans, who could explore new possibilities without committing to a particular style. The second was in machine-made production pieces that used the classical ornament associated with postmodern architecture as well as the ice-cream colors that elicited both enthusiasm and consternation regarding the style. Though the architecture and design communities had followed Postmodernism from its earliest incarnations, it was only with its translation into functional objects that the style was assimilated into popular culture. At first, the most successful of these objects were designed by architects, who already understood the principles behind the style. The idea was not entirely new—architects such as C. F. A. Voysey and Josef Hoffmann had designed products—but now architects were not designing to express their aesthetic impulses but working at the behest of producers, who were using the architects' celebrity as a marketing tool. Most of these collaborations were successful; some extraordinarily so, making Postmodernism part of current fashion. The earliest and costliest production pieces were, as could be expected, the most admired (and most admirable). As it moved into more commercial categories, the quality of both production and design suffered, with commensurate damage to Postmodernism's image. Objects like toasters, vacuum cleaners, mugs, and desk accessories, gussied up with postmodern colors and classical details, commercialized the style and made little design sense. The only asset of this miscellany was its disposability. Producers, however, were not necessarily interested in promoting the style; they were simply taking advantage of a marketing opportunity. Market-savvy manufacturers realized that attaching "starchitect" names to their products would add fashion-forward cachet to otherwise conventional goods. For the architects, designing on a much smaller scale was a challenging change of pace, though admittedly with less at stake. Some produced what were in effect miniature buildings, or merely decorated existing forms, but others explored options not possible in their usual projects, with often felicitous results. The Italian firm ALESSI was a pioneer in this area. In i983 its Tea & Coffee Piazza project, with silver services designed by eleven prominent international architects, followed by other elegant serving accessories, established Alessi as a prestigious and forward-thinking firm, despite occasional complaints about elitism. Of MICHAEL GRAVES's (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design” and “Living with Postmodern Design") iconic tea kettle, one critic mocked its elevation from utensil to “a symbol, a lightning rod for the economic and class aspirations of its owner. That's yuppie pornography—the objectification of objects." The same year, FORMICA's competition to promote their ColorCore material produced FRANK GEHRY's (see under “Architecture") equally iconic fish sculptures, transforming Formica into a credible source for designers and architects.
