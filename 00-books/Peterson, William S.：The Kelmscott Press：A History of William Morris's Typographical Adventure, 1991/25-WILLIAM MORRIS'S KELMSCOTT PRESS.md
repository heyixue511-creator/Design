Figure 8o The discounts offered by Bernard Quaritch were so generous that Morris feared some customers would be tempted to buy the Chaucer from Quaritch rather than from the Kelmscott Press itself. Even by the leisurely standards of the Kelmscott Press, the Chaucer was a long time in the making, and the abundant references to the book in contemporary letters and diaries make it possible for us to follow its progress with some precision. Trial pages were pulled in July 1 892, and during the same month Morris began to discuss the illustrations with Burne-Jones. Morris designed the first border in February 1893. On 8 August 1894 the first sheet was printed (on vellum). Cobden-Sanderson discussed the binding with Morris in October 1894. On 21 December 1894 another Albion press arrived and was installed in Sunderland Cottage exclusively for printing the Chaucer. By April 1895 all the designs for The Canterbury Tales had been redrawn by Catterson-Smith, and in the following month the printing of the Tales was completed. Between May and August, however, there was a hiatus while Morris tried to solve the mystery of the yellow stains on the sheets already printed. Burne-Jones drew the last illustration in December 1895; at about the same time Morris began to design the pigskin binding,47 and in February 1896 he started the engraved titlepage, finishing it on 27 March. At the end of May the printing of the Chaucer was done, and Morris celebrated by giving presents to his pressmen. Aside from the magnitude and complexity of the book, a major source of delay (at least during the final year of its production) was Morris's failing health. His friends wondered whether he would live to finish it. T. M. Rooke has left a moving account in his diary of a visit Morris paid to Burne-Jones's home on 2 March 1896: Morris appeared at the door ‘very ghostlike, feeble and old looking', and Burne-Jones declared that the Chaucer was the finest book ever printed — if W.M. had done nothing else it would be enough'. Morris responded, 'I must own I'n complacent about it — must try though not to be too conceited. And there's one thing that's not to be forgotten, that you backed me up well in it, old chap. If you'd been at all slack over it and hadn't been as much excited about it as I was, we should never have got through with it. The first two bound copies of the Chaucer were delivered to Morris and Burne-Jones on 2 June, the latter passing his copy on to his daughter the following day as a birthday gift. On 24 June Douglas Cockerell took a train to Folkestone, where Morris had gone on the advice of his doctor, and handed him a copy in the pigskin binding. 'Douglas came with first bound Chaucer about 1. very satisfactory,' Morris wrote in his diary. When he gave May her copy, she afterwards recalled, the look of profound melancholy that (perhaps unconsciously) he turned on me in smiling tenderly seemed like a glimpse into a very far country. What it quite meant I don't think I realized at the time, though it moved me deeply: all was made clear not long afterwards . . . it was the look of an intensely lonely man — never to be forgotten while memory serves.'48 The Chaucer was Morris's last great achievement: he died the following October. ![](images/7569d0ab084ab1a51145e36584d65f4688539c5472f33ee4a4c5db3b90b930a6.jpg) Figure 81 In this characteristically light-hearted caricature by Burne-Jones, Chaucer blesses Morris and Burne-Jones (who look like awkward schoolboys) for their work on the Kelmscott Chaucer. The Works of Geoffrey Chaucer represents the culmination of Morris's lifelong search for the Ideal Book; it is the kind of book that he and Burne-Jones dreamed of creating when they were undergraduates and that they attempted unsuccessfully to produce during the 186os and 187os: it should have come out when Morris and I were at Oxford in our first term,'Burne-Jones wrote, and we should have lost our senses with bliss.'49 Instead it came at the end of Morris's life, when he was able to pour into this single volume all his bibliophilic passion and his unmatched skill as a designer of ornaments; it is significant that even those readers who find Morris's pages too heavy or congested for their tastes feel compelled, in the end, to pay tribute to the Chaucer as one of the great books of the world. ‘To me it is the most beautiful of all printed books,'Yeats declared;5º it is a sentiment that many, sometimes grudgingly, have echoed through the years.
