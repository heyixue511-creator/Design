Most buildings have resilience, in that they are suffi ciently robust to allow a remodelling to occur. An uncomplicated building may have a number of distinct lives; it may undergo many quite simple transformations. An obvious instance of this is the 18th- and 19th century warehouse, of which there are innumerable examples all over the western world. Many which have outgrown their initial use have been converted into galleries, apartments, shops or offi ces. However, a building that is designed so specifi cally to accommodate the needs of the users, that is so exactly measured to the rhythm of their requirements, can be incredibly diffi cult to adapt. Some buildings are unambiguous

and precisely designed around the functions and the activities that take place within them, meaning that the character of the space is extremely distinct. To make the building anywhere near approaching habitable, almost impossible concessions would have to be made by the new occupiers of the building.

One such building is St Peter’s Seminary. It was designed by the Glasgow practice Gillespie, Kidd & Coia, and was consecrated in 1966 as a training college for Roman Catholic priests. It is a powerfully modern cast concrete or béton brut building set within the wild countryside of southern Scotland. It contains the

traditional elements of a seminary: chapel, refectory and cells, all of which are organised around an open court. In the time since construction, the manner in which priests are educated has changed – rather being cloistered away in isolated communities, they are encouraged to gain experience of real life and enter into small parishes. In 1980, the Catholic Church closed St Peter’s down and the building was used as a drug rehabilitation centre for a few years. However, since 1987 the building has lain empty and has been allowed to become derelict. The building is very exact: it responded so completely to the needs of the users, so acutely that it has proven impossible to convert it into anything else. There have been a number of proposals to convert the building, including plans for a hotel and for domestic accommodation, but the inconvenience of the site, the cost of the proposals, and the very specifi city of the building itself defeated these.

The neglected building has started to be used as a canvas by artists: graffi ti adorns the walls and pop videos have been fi lmed in the dirt and detritus of the ruin. This Brutalist masterpiece is also a place of pilgrimage for architects, who stare in wonder and understanding at the exposed structure. The ruin is a place of romance.
