Fourth, Tukey's counsel. Most diagnostic plots involve either a more or less definite dependence that bobbles around a lot, or a point spatter. Such plots are rather more often better made wider than tall. Widerthan-tall shapes usually make it easier for the eye to follow from left to right. 7John W. Tukey, Exploratory Data Analysis (Reading, Mass., 1977), p. 129. Perhaps the most general guidance we can offer is that smoothly-changing curves can stand being taller than wide, but a wiggly curve needs to be wider than tall. . . .7 And, finally, Playfair's example. Of the 89 graphics in six different books by William Playfair, most (92 percent) are wider than tall. Several of the exceptions are his skyrocketing government debt displays.
