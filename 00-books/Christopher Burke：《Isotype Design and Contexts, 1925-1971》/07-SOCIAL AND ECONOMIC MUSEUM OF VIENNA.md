## Technique

For the most part, scissor cutting is used; this ensures the unity of the area of colour that is essential in achieving the desired effect; the letter-like abstract character of the pictograms becomes clearly apparent. Scissor cutting is easily learned and has been taken up by the teaching programme of the modern school. Geographical and anatomical charts are also produced by this technique.

## Lettering

The pictorial charts are so designed that the pictures speak for themselves as far as possible; lettering appears only as an accompaniment. Capitals, which are simple and thus easily readable, are used throughout. For longer explanatory texts, upper and lower case is used.

The chief interest of the chart on p. 146 above is in its references to conventions and practices that were to be superseded later. Notable among these are:

objects (cars) are stacked in columns; the principle of natural arrangement had evidently not yet been formulated;

— a human pictogram with a perfectly circular head is to be employed in charts 'where heads are to be stressed as items to be counted';

— men are to be dark in colour and women light, children to be green and old people grey;

— background colours are to be used for signification;

— a method of producing pictograms that is easily learnt (scissor-cutting) is to be employed; this consideration of a method that could be easily learnt by the layperson was passed over when linocutting was adopted;

— capital letters are preferred over upper and lower case in titles.

One may note too that the idea of using pictograms 'to explain the content of statistical tables' was proposed again in International picture language in 1936 (p. 18).

These features of the chart suggest that it was made in the second half of 1925 or early in 1926. It seems certain to have been made after Neurath's article about 'Graphic methods of presentation for statistics' had been written (published in August 1925), for it is clearly an advance on that inevitably limited discussion.³⁵ It cannot have been made after the time of the 'Gesolei' exhibition (summer 1926), for by then background colour was consciously avoided as a means of signification (see the section 'Colour and form', pp. 117-18 above). Another strong reason for assigning an early date to this chart is its assumption that objects are to be stacked in columns rather than arranged in rows.

If the chart was made in the period suggested then it is perhaps surprising that it should have been published only in September 1926, and again early in 1927. And the chart may well have been shown at the 'Städtebauausstellung' held in Vienna in the autumn of 1926; in an article in the special number of the Österreichische Gemeinde-Zeitung published for this exhibition, Neurath referred to 'a special explanatory chart' that 'shows the basic rules of method that are used by the Museum'.3⁶

This would, then, seem to be a case of a chart being reused some significant time after the occasion for which it had been made (see the section 'Reuse of charts', p. 111 above). It was inevitable that the quite detailed and explicit explanation of method of this chart, made at a time of rapid development, should have gone out of date very quickly – although much of the content of the chart was still valid in the autumn of 1926. One would suppose, nevertheless, that soon afterwards, in 1927, the outdated features of the chart would have become so anachronistic as to prevent its further use.

The idea that discs or circles could be used as heads in human pictograms seems not to have been much put into practice. One chart that shows it (T5o) is reproduced on p. 167. Another is shown below; an exhibition version may be seen in a photograph of the Museum, N499. The circles of this chart were repeated on an accompanying map of Vienna, which thus showed density of population (see p. 54 above). Both map and chart were reproduced in the same issue of Der Aufbau in which the chart on method was first published.

## A Births and deaths in Vienna 1925

Österreichische Gemeinde-Zeitung, Jg. 2, Nr.16, 15 August 1925, p.19.

Chart A shows in one configuration material that occupies two separated configurations in T3c; this may be considered as evidence for the chronological precedence of T3c – and Marie Neurath confirmed that this was indeed the order in which these charts were made. Chart A can be considered as an advance on T3c in using what was to become the normal way of making visually apparent a surplus or deficit in two sets of data. On the other hand, chart A incorporates the essentially non-pictorial element of the bar graph to state the surplus or deficit, thereby repeating the statement that the pictograms would make if left unencumbered by these added elements. Later versions of charts on this subject show that the bar element was judged to be superfluous.

Both charts are primitive', in terms of the developments of the work of the next few years. They are strongly influenced by the conventions of non-pictorial graphic statistics: the inclusion of exact numerical data; the labelling of columns; the use of a key to the columns in chart A.

## T3f Births and deaths in Vienna 1926

Columns are supported by light coloured (according to Neurath's discussion) year bands – to encourage a vertical reading. And these bands also serve to relate the appropriate births and deaths columns. The bar elements showing surplus and deficit are now incorporated into the column of pictograms. The exact numerical data are no longer given.

Though the subject of the chart is Austria, not just Vienna, one can legitimately compare it with the births and deaths charts of 1925 and 1926, as if it was another treatment of their material. The major change is that material for five years is shown, where the earlier charts had treated two years. This move to a more selected and visually clearer statement of the material is consolidated by all subsequent births and deaths charts.

The problem of showing the pictograms as constituting (vertical) columns, rather than (horizontal) rows is now met by a more subtle pattern of space between columns. And the background strips are now incorporated into the pictograms – in that the pictograms are reversed out of implied rectangular elements. This device seems to have been a feature of charts made at this time; it was soon to be dropped, being made redundant by the use of more essentially modular pictograms. The reversing-out of pictograms also enforces a visual equality between births symbols and deaths symbols; this was an issue that Neurath had raised in his discussion of T3c and T3f, but which had not perhaps been satisfactorily resolved in T3f. The gravestone is now drawn in projection.

According to the statement on the chart, where the pictograms constitute a surplus or deficit they are bounded by a red line; this is not clear in the black-and-white reproduction, though a similar line may be seen in T3f.

The visual key to the pictograms is now omitted, and these are explained verbally; this became conventional practice.

The material of this chart differs significantly from that of preceding births and deaths charts; here the comparison is between figures for different places at a fixed time. The ordering principle that is evident here also differs from that of the preceding charts; this would seem to reflect the difference in the material, and the particular fact that no country shows a deficit. This allows the right half of the chart to be headed Excess of births over deaths'. In the absence of a strictly comparable chart among earlier charts of this series one cannot make any general deductions about development of method from this apparently new ordering principle; though one may conclude that force of precedent was overcome and that the previously applied principle was here replaced by one which would make a clearer visual statement of the material. One comparison with earlier charts is possible. The figures shown here are relative, not absolute. One may note that the list of countries is ordered geographically, from north and west to south and east.

## Charts on unemployment

The charts on unemployment form one of the largest subject groups within the whole body of work. Not only do they allow one to compare treatment of graphic detail over a quite extensive period, but their subject matter allows a more precise dating than is usually possible, particularly when these charts show data for periods within a year. It is from these charts that one can often draw the most precise conclusions about the chronology of the graphic conventions in the work as a whole.

The chart is based on percentages, not absolute figures, and this differentiates it from most of the other unemployment charts. The need to show a constant ten pictograms divided into two parts leads to the use of a face-on pictogram. But the need to signify unemployment through form has not allowed the symmetry that a pictogram should have in order to achieve a completely natural divisibility; only a form that is symmetrical about its axis of division shows an unequivocal half unit. The styles of pictogram and lettering suggest a date for the chart earlier than 1929, the date that the content might suggest. Both these elements seem to predate the presence of Arntz in the team (from September 1928), though the chart could well have been made not long before this time. The pictogram is

more developed than the obviously scissor-cut forms of charts made earlier in 1928. If the chart was made in 1928, then the figures for 192g would have had to be estimated, and one would have expected an explanation of this on the chart.

T5t shows the dangers of dating these charts on the evidence of their content: this chart is merely a revised edition of T5s, adding data for 1931. A revised or second edition seems to have been planned, for T5s shows the space for an extra row. Charts normally show no such generous space between last row and signature.

The existence of T5t allows one to put forward another theory concerning the disparity of style and apparent date of T5s: could T5s have been made in 1928 when data up to 1927 were known, leaving space for two rows (for 1929 and 1931) to be added?

## T298 Unemployed

Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft, 1930, no.87.   
Great Britain; France; Germany.   
Each figure = 250,000 unemployed.

The data indicate that the chart was made before the figures for 1929 were known: earlier rather than later during the period in which the charts for Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft were made. The principal change shown by this chart is that the pictograms now face left to right and thus follow the conventional reading direction; though Marie Neurath suggested that the left-facing symbols have the advantage that the rows read better as queues, being lengthened by additions at the end.

The heading and signature are now in upper and lower case. There is no marking-off of pictograms; the rows may not have been considered long enough to demand it.

With this chart there is a significant change in the language of the title: what had been termed 'Arbeitslosigkeit' (unemployment) now becomes 'Arbeitslose' (the unemployed). The shift is from a concept to the human beings: from the abstract to the concrete. This movement of thought was very typical of Otto Neurath's drive for material reality.

The sequence of five charts illustrates the progression from signifying meaning primarily through colour to signification primarily through form; T5o and T116b were exhibition charts and thus potentially less constrained in their use of colour than the other three chars, which were designed for reproduction. In T5o the subgroups are shown by the colour of pictograms and by the lines that bound them. In T5a, the main burden of signification is transferred to the background, and the various devices used there; the need to design for black-and-white reproduction seems to have been the primary factor in this change. T116a, as reproduced in Die bunte Welt, uses colour to distinguish between the four main groups, but the chart is still intelligible in black and white, through the qualifying symbols that are placed over the pictograms. These are not quite incorporated, for groups of two or three pictograms share the same qualifying symbol: this was clearly a convention that could only be used in the rare charts with only very small groups of pictograms to be qualified. T116a also employs the large background symbols (printed in light grey) that feature in some charts of late 1928 or early 1929; these symbols (in this case loosely connected forms suggesting 'machinery' and industrial work') have no strictly qualifying or distinguishing function, but serve only to reinforce the general subject matter of the chart.

The qualifying symbols are properly incorporated in T116b, which also now makes distinctions through the form of the main pictograms. T282 consolidates the principles of incorporation evident in T116b.

The pictograms of τ5o are an attempt at a radically simplified form, such as features in the early chart on method. The heads are circular and aim (according to the chart on method) to function as tokens to be counted. The uniform colour of bodies and the varied colour of heads, implied in this reproduction, suggest that bodies were a constant colour in the chart, while the various colours of the heads did the work of signification. T5a uses pictograms that manifest their scissor-cut origin. T116b shows early pictograms in the Arntz mode, though their style seems not quite to be that of Arntz himself; they could well have been drawn by Augustin Tschinkel (on the evidence of resemblance to work known to be by Tschinkel). T282 shows what one may call a 'mature' human pictogram – though modifications to this form were to continue for some years to come. Comparison of this pictogram with that of T5o shows the contribution of Arntz in achieving Neurath's desire for a 'human' human pictogram – and yet a pictogram with the qualities of form that would allow it to act as a (modular) unit of the 'picture script'.

One might repeat the observation made in concluding the commentary on T97a and τ97b: the later version is visually more articulate.

Finally, one may note these changes common to the three later charts. There is a greater control in handling the material: pictograms are ordered more coherently; the size and position of all the elements – pictograms, titles, headings and captions, empty space – are now related to each other more effectively than in the earlier versions. The pictograms now ‘read' better, as modular forms.

## Summary

## 1925

This is the period of 'first steps'. Principles for ordering or configuring material are already quite developed; though material is not yet always ordered in the most visually effective form – rows are presented for simple comparison, where later a 'shift' configuration would be used.

In this summary the period described is broken down into stages. These do not, of course, correspond to exact calendar years: the year designations are no more than approxımate markers in the day-to-day continuum.

Graphic aspects of the work are quite undeveloped and primitive. Pictograms are comparatively realistic; the materials of pen and ink provide little encouragement for the drawing of abstracted forms. Colours are used without much sense of order or constraint.

Charts of this period are noticeably influenced by the conventions of non-pictorial graphic statistics; for example, in their inclusion of exact numerical information and in employing elements of the bar graph.

## 1926

This is a time of rapid development, stimulated notably by the task of making charts for the 'Gesolei' exhibition in the summer of this year. Most exhibition charts now use elements cut from paper; this imposes considerable constraints on form. Simplification of form is taken to an extreme in a few special charts in which human pictograms have circular counters for heads. After the realistic extreme of the first year and the abstract extreme of the second, subsequent forms will attempt to find a satisfactory intermediate position.

The 'Gesolei' charts use colour, in pictograms and in backgrounds to pictograms, as principle means of signification; but after this point formal devices are employed instead. This change of method is caused by a growing awareness of the need to find ways of showing that can be understood in black-and-white reproduction,without special adjustment.

## 1927

Charts of this period consolidate the achievements of 1926. The guide-pictures become more evident and develop their function of making distinctions within the body of the chart. The use of background colour or form comes to be replaced by the use of secondary symbols that qualify the main pictograms.

## 1928

There are signs of an increased pace of development and experinent in the earlier part of the year; this would be stimulated by the preparation of charts for publications, culminating in the Museum's first book, Die bunte Welt, published early in 1929.

Some charts show pictograms reversed out of rectangular elements. This is a device that achieves modular forms by a short cut: the signifying forms themselves are not modular and are relatively undeveloped. The aim of finding modular forms is certainly apparent, but the means of achieving them has not yet been found. Another experiment in charts made in the second half of the year is the use of large background symbols with some of the functions of the guide-pictures. The anomalous vertical arrangement of pictograms in the births and deaths charts changes in this year. This is perhaps a sign of a greater consciousness of the work as being informed by a set of principles, some of which can be made explicit. The use of the monogram of the initials of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum on charts and publications may be regarded as another sign of self-consciousness.

## 1928-9

This is the period of the breakthrough, in which the means (principally in the person of Gerd Arntz) were found to realize Otto Neurath's intentions. Through Arntz's talent for drawing abstracted but subtle forms, the pictograms could now become genuinely modular. His introduction of the practice of cutting pictograms in linoleum enables these forms to be retained in reproduction more easily than scissor-cutting would have allowed – and linocutting makes possible easy multiplication of pictograms. Ease of reproduction and of multiplication allows pictograms to be treated much more as units, and, as a consequence, fractions of pictograms appear more frequently, and so does the practice of marking pictograms off into small groups. Qualifying symbols become incorporated into main pictograms: another practice made possible by the new modular forms. Another aspect of the breakthrough of this period is the introduction of printing with metal type for the verbal matter of charts, and, coincidentally, a policy of ranging rather than centring any verbal matter.

## 1929-30

In these years the work begins to achieve maturity, when the practices associated with the breakthrough have been assimilated. It is the period of work on Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft; this commission allows and necessitates an expansion of the working team which, in turn, brings a new awareness of the need for coordination and consistency. Thus the guideline notes come to be written. This is also the point at which the T and N files start to be compiled: another sign of greater organization within the Museum.

## From 1930

A way of working graphically is now formed in its essentials; it will be refined and it will expand, but the approach to ordering material in graphic form has been established.
