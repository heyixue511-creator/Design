It was from them, rather than from the adherents of Rational Architecture, that the Modernist architects absorbed these ideas. During the very earliest years of Soviet power,the new creative trend, which was being formulated as part of the fight against stylization, eclecticism and the Moderne style, took shape through close cooperation between architects and artists involved in the Leftist trends which had arisen before the First World War in opposition to official academic art. A new style was crystallizing in European art as a result of a complex process which found its most intense expression at the frontier between figurative art and architecture. In the past, architecture had always been the determinańt of stylistic change, but it lost this position during the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. This leading role was then for many years taken over by painting, which established tastes in art and aesthetic standards. When the objective circumstances for the development of a new style materialized in the 1910s and 1920s, a discrepancy came to light between the state of fine art, on the one hand, and of architecture together with applied art on the other. Architecture, industrial design and applied art were clearly lagging behind. Eclecticism and stylization prevailed, while innovation hardly ventured into the open. Yet, on a world scale, architecture was on the verge of an inevitable and radical break with accepted aesthetic values. The attempt of the Moderne style to replace tectonic ornament bv overtly decorative elements failed to satisfy architects. The Moderne approach helped many architects to grasp the futility of eclecticism and stylization. It promoted the integration of many technological achievements and methods ofinterior spatial organization, but failed to provide an aesthetic foundation for a new architecture and was never more than a transitional style. A more radical revision of aesthetic principles was necessary. The stereotypes which had become established over the centuries needed to be swept away, so that the objective requirements of the new style', already integrated in many ways in the architecture of the early twentieth century, might receive artistic recognition from the architects themselves. Leftist painting supplied the requisite catalyst by accelerating the formulation of fresh aesthetic concepts in contemporary architecture. New trends, such as Cubism, Suprematism, Purism, Neo-Plasticism and Cubo-Futurism, appeared from the turn of the century onwards, connected not so much with technical innovations involving only the figurative arts, but with the elaboration of new methods applicable to all spatial art. This trend in formal aesthetic experimentation continued to diverge more and more from the main line of Leftist painting. It also dispensed with certain basic features of figurative art and, as it were, prepared a new deal' for architecture and design, the artistic development of which had been retarded. In the history of art as a whole, the last five years or so in Russian painting before the outbreak of the First World War stood out by the intensity, depth and diversity of the innovation involved. Soviet architecture could hardly have achieved such brilliant artistic results so quickly had it not been for this leap forward in' Russian painting. ## Mayakovsky: the cultural focus of the new art Vladimir Mayakovsky was certainly the most significant figure of the new art in the early years of Sovietpower. He left his mark on virtually all aspects of contemporary artistic activity, including architecture. He first joined the intellectually quite accessible Russian Futurist movement. When this split into a number of different groupings, such as Cubo-Futurism in Moscow andEgo-Futurism in Petersburg, he became one of the leaders of Cubo-Futurism, the supporters of which gathered about the Pervy zhurnal russkikh futuristov (First Journal of Russian Futurists) in 1914, among them Khlebnikov, Kamensky,the Burlyuk brothers, Sergei Tretyakov, Kruchenykh and others. Futurism was widely regarded as being largely for show, a matter of shocking the public, a destructive and negative trend. The Futurists themselves, on the other hand, believed that the rejection of the old order was no more than a stage in their artistic progress. As early as 1915, Mayakovsky wrote: We regard the first part of our programme – destruction – as completed. And so do not be surprised if you next see in our hands a builder's sketch rather than a jester's rattle." Poets such as Mayakovsky, Khlebnikov, Kruchenykh, and others, indirectly influenced the solution offormal problems in the figurative arts and architecture by their approach to linguistic potential and an unprecedented freedom in the treatment of language. The Futurists did not merely regard language as an established system of communication, but intruded upon its ostensibly invulnerable foundations – syntax, for instance, and even the word itself. When they set out to reshape all the elements of poetic form, they considered it equally essential to reform the language itself, and rejected every suggestion that the latter was not susceptible to structural changes. By their freedom in the handling of language, and their experiments in the generation of new words in particular, the Futurists may be said to have removed all inhibitions restraining the creation of new forms in general. If poetry, working in a language that served the purposes of both literature and everyday communication, could successfully survive such change, then other forms of art with idioms of their own should all the more easily achieve such a transformation. This does much to explain the great influence of Cubo-Futurism on the new trends in the figurative arts in the pre-Revolutionary period, and on the Symbolist Romanticist experiments during the early Soviet era. Mayakovsky's influence on Soviet art, including architecture, was not confined to purely aesthetic problems of form. Even before the onset of the Revolution, he was already linking a reform of the means of expression with the need to relate these to the new content of art. Thereafter, he shifted his emphasis to the subject matter and equated innovations in form with the changed role of art in the new social order. The Futurists were probably first among Leftist artists in attempting an approach to art based on its new social function. On 15 March 1918, the Gazeta futuristov (Futurists’Gazette) published Decree No. l concerning the Democratization of Art (Writings on Hoardings and Painting in Public Places)’signed by Mayakovsky, Kamensky and Burlyuk. They proclaimed the sloganAll art for all the people!’ and called on artists and writers to seize their paintpots and use the brushes of their trade to illuminate and bedeck all the flanks, foreheads and breasts of cities and stations, and the endlessly running flock of railroad trucks. From now on, the citizen will at every moment savour the profound thinking of his great contemporaries, contemplate the colourful brilliance of today's joyful beauty and listen everywhere to the music – melodious, thunderous or noisy – of superb composers.’ Two main lines of developmentmay be identified in Leftist painting at the start of the century, one proceeding from Matisse, the other from Cézanne and Cubism. The divergences between the aesthetic principles underlying each of these appeared most clearly when figurative composition came to be rejected. ## Kandinsky: the concept of Monumental Art Vasily Kandinsky, a distinguished representative of the first of these trends in Russia, sought to elucidate particular states of emotion through art and investigate the role of the subconscious in the artistic process.He set out to discover definite objective rules for deriving pictorial compositions from inner personal reality and subjective experience. Kandinsky studied the psychological perception of the means employed in various forms of art, in an attempt to synthesize a new and Monumental’or total art by bringing such effects into play, so as to produce kindred reactions among a widely varied public. Particular attention was paid to the link between painting and forms of expression primarily intended to convey individual emotional states, such as music, dancing and lyric poetry. Kandinsky was moved to combine painting with these other art forms in order to create mutually interchangeable means of expression capable of conveying a variety of moods within a single individual. He referred, for instance, to the movement of colour through space, the musical quality of colour, the interaction between non-representational form and colour. As early as 1913, he wrote: ‘The underlying identity of the means used by different forms of art ultimately becomes obvious and provides a basis for different attempts to stress and amplify the resonance of the special sound generated in a given art form by means of the identical sound available in another branch of art, thereby securing an extraordinarily powerfuleffect. . . . Every art tends to plumb its own depths and may be said to become specialized. Nevertheless, it also opens a door into a new world, the world which combines individual art forms within a single work, the world of Monumental Art."2 Kandinsky continued to develop his concept of Monumental Art throughout the period immediately following the Revolution. His interest in the special features and rules affecting the impact of works of art and in the application of new scientific and technological methods to the exploration of psychological and physiological perception of various art forms indirectly influenced the development of formal architectural innovations, especially those involving objective visual criteria. The followers of the other main new trend sought their means of expression in the world about them, rather than within man's inner reality. Two approaches, fundamentally and explicitly opposed to each other, emerged from this, but both exerted a deep influence on twentieth-century architecture and design. One of these was linkedwith Kazimir Malevich's Suprematism, the other with the explorations of Vladimir Tatlin. ## Malevich: the search for a path into architecture; Lissitzky's Prouns In his Suprematist compositions Malevich had tried ever since 1913 to free painting from anything that interfered with the direct effect of colour on the eye. He considered that colour and shape were closely related and set about enclosing patches of colour in simple and well-defined geometric figures on a white background. He believed not only that colour emphasized clear geometric shapes, but that form itselfinfluenced the effect of colour, so that, for instance, certain geometric shapes subjected a particular colour to maximum tension. By the use of white backgrounds Malevich visually extended representation into depth, while integrating it into surrounding space. The geometric shapes on the picture surface seem to float in infinite space; dynamic tension is achieved by interrelating plane andcolour. Suprematist painting with its characteristic relationships between colour and geometry promoted thedevelopment of a distinctive style in applied, decorative and monumental art, including textiles, posters and murals. The further development of Malevich's Suprematism after the Revolution made geometric planes even more important as elements of pictorial composition, and colour was relegated to a secondary role. Colour-free dynamic Suprematist compositions began to appear in which the organization of space was based on geometric figures. By this stage Suprematism had virtually lost all connection with painting, as a result of its rejection of colour and its reduction to black and white planimetric figures. In the next stage, volumes and stereometric compositions pointed the way to Suprematist architecture. Volumetric Suprematism introduced novel architectonic relationships into formal aesthetic experiments, a field to which Lissitzky made an important contribution. Lissitzky was a trained architect, and was among the first to grasp the significance of Malevich's experiments for the development of modern architecture. His output straddled architecture and the fine arts, and he was therefore able to apply to the new architecture those formal and aesthetic discoveries of the Leftist painters which could contribute to the formulation of a contemporary style. In 1919–21, Lissitzky created his Prouns (Proekty utverzdeniya novogo [Projects for the Affirmation of the New]), which were axonometric representations of variously shaped geometrical solids in a state of equilibrium, some standing out from a firm background, others seeming to soar into space. Lissitzky's Prouns were original models for a new architecture, experiments in the generation of architectonic forms, attempts at new three-dimensional representations, a kind of compositional stock-piling for later use. Lissitzky deliberately stressed the architectural aspect of his projects. He saw Prouns as a staging-post on the journey from painting to architecture' and the names he gave them such asthe city’ or the bridge' were not fortuitous. Later on, Lissitzky applied some of his Prouns to specific architectural tasks such as a pumping station, a horizontal skyscraper’, a dwelling, and to exhibition displays. The Arkitectons created by Malevich early in the 1920s heralded a new step in Suprematism's advance into architecture. These were by now real three-dimensional compositions, rough models of original architectural compositions in which horizontal and vertical parallelepipeds of different shapes and sizes coalesced or interpenetrated at right angles. Through three-dimensional Suprematism and the Arkitectons, Malevich actually came to behave as an architect in putting forward his Planits – the dwellings of the future. Malevich’s formal Suprematist experiments with geometrical figures and volumes, including colour as a means of achieving rhythmic plastic organization, strongly influenced the development of stylistic methods and forms in contemporary architecture and design. By the geometric purity of their abstraction Part I/Aesthetic problems of design Malevich’s Suprematist compositions, his Planits and Arkitectons, acted much as a crvstal does when immersed in a supersaturated solution. They precipitated the latent abstract tendencies in the Dutch De Stijl group, the German Bauhaus, and many Soviet architects, Ladovsky, Leonidov, Melnikov, Lissitzky, Ginzburg, Nikolsky, Ilya Golosov, Rudnev, Khidekel and Ivan Fomin among them. As a painter, Malevich helped architects to see plain geometric shapes afresh and revealedthe virtually inexhaustible opportunities for their combination in effective and complex spatial compositions. The juxtaposition of volumes in Malevich’s architectonic compositions and Lissitzky's Prouns generated relationships and devices seldom or never before used for architectural purposes: the horizontal andvertical displacement of volumes relative to each other; overhangs of one volume over another; the siting of a large and bulky inchoate shape over smaller scattered ones; the soaring into space of a large volume supported only on asmall area of its lower surface, and so on. A negation of symmetry, a fresh approach to gravity – with the visuallyweighty' above the apparently light' – the rich opportunities offered by light and shade, contrasting differences of scale, the constantly changing general spatial composition as an object is viewed in the round from every angle – all this gave an architect new means of achieving effects substantially different from those of traditional architecture with its symmetry, its clearly defined façades, its upward lightening of the composition, itstectonic' decoration. Malevich regarded his experiments as part of the search for a new language of art and saw his Arkitectons, for instance, as the Suprematist art of spatial construction’; as a species ofSuprematist order'. Hebelieved that afterit had created new forms and new relations between them, Suprematism would break forth from the canvas and provide a new architecture. He wrote in 1928 that the whole path retraced by the new art in all aspects of culture has emerged into the one truly contemporary art, whichis architecture'.4 Malevich stressed that the processes of form-generation were connected with the aesthetic rules of art, and that even a functional form could not be created without an aesthetic effect'. Vladimir Tatlin, like Malevich, carried out formal aesthetic experiments on the borderline between painting and architecture. Malevich-was primarily interested in the opportunities provided by simple geometric forms and complex three-dimensional compositions, while Tatlin looked for aesthetic effect in the contrasting juxtaposition of different materials and new constructional shapes.In other words, both were concerned with aesthetic problems of form, but while Malevich aimed at the stylistic transformation of man's actual environment, Tatlin attempted to express an object’s inner essence, implicit – as he thought – in the substance of its elementary form. Tatlin and Early Constructivism – Gabo, Rodchenko, the Stenberg brothers, ## Medunetsky, Klutsis and others. Tatlin was concerned with the properties of materials, such as their grain and their texture. He stressed the opportunities provided by a variety of materials in order to achieve a particular aesthetic effect. His work moved beyond the bounds of painting. The saw, hammer, chisel andaxe became his working implements alongside the brush. Applied colour increasingly gave way to the natural colours of metals and woods, and the picture' dealt with three-dimensional constructional problems rather than pictorial tasks, Tatlin's quest for the aesthetic potential of actual materials led him to search out new paths whereby Leftist painting might move towards architecture. Other artists were pursuing such a path simultaneously, though not always consciously. Miturich produced Spatial Painting as part of his experiments with Cubist compositions, Popova and Klyun produced pictorial compositions comprising three-dimensional elements, Bruny engaged in the pictorial treatment of materials’. Tatlin's experiments not only taught artists to work in real space with materials quite unknown to painting, but also made them aware of the artistic potential of engineering structures. They became attracted to technology by the accuracy and efficiency of these structures, the unfamiliar spatial relationships involved and their rational utilization of materials. Tatlin was among the first to make a deliberate transition from painting to the creation of a human environment. A tendency to switch from depiction of the world to construction of the environment had been evident in his work since post-Revolutionary years. In 1918-19, he taught in Moscow at the Svomas, where his experiments influenced young artists, such as Vladimir and GeorgyStenberg, Medunetsky and others. From 1919, he directed the workshop in the reconstructed Petrograd Academy of Arts under the slogan Construction, Volume and Material’. A bench, vice, anvil and assorted tools replaced palette, brushes and paints, and compositions assembled from metal, wood, mica and other materials were produced under Tatlin's guidance. From Tatlin's point of view, these experiments with new materials and constructions were no mere formal investigations. To him, Constructivism was an organizational and formative principle, a fresh way oflooking at the world and its perception in three dimensions. Tatlin saw the creation of a new objective environment – a new perceptual world – as the ultimate object of his experiments. The method le called artistic production’ would be extended to the design of all objects. In 1919, Tatlin designed his Monument to the Third International, known as Tatlin's Tower, and in the autumn of 1920 he produced a model for it. Two young painters, Vinogradov and Meerzon, and a sculptor, Shapiro, helped with the detailed work involved. Both the latter eventually became architects. Tatlin's Tower was planned as a vast, 400-metre-high framed structure in spiral form, housing three units of accommodation one above the other, which revolved at different speeds. The lowest of these, cube-shapedand turning at the rate of one revolution a year, was to house the International's conferences, congresses and legislative meetings. The middle unit, shaped as a pyramid, would revolve once a month and contained administrative and executive offices. The uppermost unit, a cylinder, would complete one revolution every twentyfour hours and act as an information centre. The significance of this project in terms of innovation in Soviet architecture becomes obvious when it is compared with competition entries of the same year for the design of crematorium buildings in Moscow and Petrograd. These reflected that transitional stagein the emergence of new architecture in which the majority of architects still clung to their Classical heritage. Tatlin's project, on the other hand, enabled many architects to overcome the psychological barriers in determining therole which new constructional methods should play in shaping the stylistic characteristics of a modern building. In this respect, Tatlin’s Tower was as significant for its period as the Eiffel Tower had been for the end of the nineteenth century. A large number of young painters set about experimenting in this fieldunder Tatlin's influence. Naum Gabo set out to produce volumetric, and then spatial constructions, and he and his brother, Anton Pevsner, announced in 1920, in their Realistic Manifesto, that they proposed to liberate volume from mass and that depth was the only pictorial and plastic form of space. The Stenbergs and Medunetsky began their laboratory experiments at about the same time. They first produced colour constructions, derived from Tatlin's Counter-Reliefs, and then during 1919–20, volumetric constructions. These were openwork spatial compositions using metal angle brackets, T-, double-T- and U-section bars that included elements of glass, sheet iron, enamelled metal, brass pipes, drawn wire components and wood, the latter usually serving as a pedestal. The artists regarded this work as laboratory research in different forms of construction: as the process of inventing technically literate’ and appropriate elements for constructive design. This notion of laboratory work’ was characteristic of those who supported the formal aesthetic experiments connected with Tatlin and the early Constructivists. All of them maintained that it is the engineer who produces new form', whereas to Spatial Suprematists, form was created through art. Constructivists contrasted their own spatial constructions with the hermetic Prouns and Arkitectons, and saw them as the quintessential expression of modern technological civilization. Early Constructivist theory was reflected in a number of statements by the Stenberg brothers. It involved a form that was technically efficient in engineering terms, rather than an artist's invention. It meant space organized by means of an open structure, be it the linear sequence of rods in a girder or a lattice, rather than enclosed volumes. It meant a combination of frame and glazing rather than solid walls, all these devices being aimed at preserving the visual impression of undivided space. Ioganson and Rodchenko experimented with spatial constructions at the same time as the Stenbergs and Medunetsky. Ioganson imparted a symbolic significance to his spatial compositions by basing all hìs constructional designs on a threedimensional cross. Rodchenko came to three-dimensional compositions by way of the Non-Objectivism – closely related to Suprematism – which he had proclaimed before the Revolution. In 1918, he produced a series of spatial constructions Part I/Aesthetic problems of design Chapter 2/Interaction between architecture and fine art made of paper, wood and metal representing basically symmetrical compositions set on pedestals or a flat surface; these could be collapsed andreassembled and were described by Rodchenko as non-objective' sculpture. In 1920–21, Rodchenko produced a second series of original spatial compositions, without a specific upper or lower side, which might be seen as floating in space in a state of suspense. In these compositions, which Rodchenko described as conforming to the principle of identical forms’, it was no longer a matter of a painter enthralled by the interaction ofopen spatial compositions, but of a constructor in search of new methods of design and the opportunities made available by the structuring of space. This second series contained the embryo of that new conception of transformable’ furniture which was later applied to domestic equipment and exhibition stands under Rodchenko's direction at the Metalworking Faculty of Vkhutemas. The theoretical importance of this second series of Rodchenko's spatial constructions lay in the method he used to escape from the picture surface into space. Malevich first depicted volumes in a plane and then transferred them into space. Tatlin may be said to have translated the illusion of plasticity in a Cubist picture into a three-dimensional object. The Stenbergs and Medunetsky first sketched out their constructions in a plane and then carried them out in space. Rodchenko, however, literally converted the picture surface into a three-dimensional composition without adding anything to it. The easewith which the volumes of this second series can be turned back into a plane stresses this. He took a plane of plywood or sheet metal with a specific geometrical shape such as a square, circle, hexagon, ellipse or triangle, and drew on it a series of regularly diminishing concentric figures, in accordance withthe principle of identical forms'. He then cut or sawed the surface along these lines and rotated the resulting figures in space to create three-dimensional compositions. These were shown, together with many spatial constructions by the Stenbergs, Medunetsky and Ioganson, in aseparate room at a Moscow art exhibition in 1921. During virtually the same period, in 1920–21, Rodchenko produced yet another, third series of spatial compositions using wooden rods as standard components. Gustav Klutsis produced a number of constructive compositions at the start of the 1920s, the most interesting being his dynamic spatial constructions intended for agitational purposes and his designs for various street decorations in 1922 for the fifth anniversary of the October Revolution and for the Fourth Congress of the Comintern, among them amplifier stands, cinema screen frames, rostrums with screens, and kiosks. Klutsis was among the first to use extendable, unfolding, sectional and movable structural elements in these designs, and he devised a number of novel methods for varying the arrangement anddynamic impact of such spatial compositions. Openwork metal constructions became increasingly popular among painters and sculptors for their artistic, as well as their technological, potential. Two designs by the sculptor Babichev early in the 1920s should be noted in this context. The first, amobile sectional theatre, exploited the technical possibilities of a latticed structure, while the second incorporated two arched girders to impart a contemporary flavour to a city monument in a competition design for a memorial to Sverdlov in Moscow. ## From Leftist art to the new architecture Thus the influence of experiments by Malevich, Tatlin and their followers reached out well beyond the realm of painting. The features identifying a new style common to painting, architecture and design probably first took clear shape in Malevich's and Tatlin's work, and they were the channels through which the formal achievements of Leftist art reached the architects. The explorations of these innovators decisively crossed the borderline between painting and architecture. Suprematist compositions, Prouns, Arkitectons, Counter-Reliefs and spatialconstructions – together with Mondrian's Neo-Plasticism and Le Corbusier's Purism – formed part of the mechanism whereby the baton of innovation passed from painting to architecture. They played a significant role in that interaction between art forms which was a characteristic feature of the early post-Revolutionary period. Within this interaction new forms of art, such as product design, came into being; formal methods were developed in previously unexplored arts such as the cinema and photography; existing art forms were enriched. Attempts were made to create fresh synthetic arts by a fusion of either purely spatial considerations (such as those underlying architecture, painting and sculpture), or by a fusion of time-regulated media such as music, dance and theatre with painting; or simply by dissolving all existing forms of art into one synthetic art of the future. For purposes of architecture, however, what mattered after the First World War and the October Revolution was the process whereby it took over the lead in formulating contemporary aesthetic ideas. This change took place in circumstances of close co-operation between painters and those architects who were able to assimilate the vast fund of artistic experience accumulated by Leftist painting during earlier decades. The interaction that occurred between different art forms at thattime affected not only experimentation, but the actual character of artistic associations. Collaborative groups of architects and Leftist painters arose in France (Esprit Nouveau), in Holland (De Stijl) and in Germany (Bauhaus). Innovative architects co-operated just as actively with Leftist painters in the Soviet Union and this led to the creation of such unusual and multi-disciplinary organizations and institutions as Unovis, Zhivskulptarkh, Inkhuk and Vkhutemas. ## Unovis –1919–22 Unovis, meaning Affirmers of the New Art, was an association of artists based on the Vitebsk Art School directed by Malevich, and it operated during the years 1919–22. Among its members were Lissitzky, Ermolaeva, Chashnik, Suetin, Khidekel, and Yudin. Suprematism was the Unovis creed, and the movement of Suprematism towards architecture was in its most intensive stage during this Vitebsk period, thanks chiefly to collaboration between Malevich as a painter and Lissitzky who, as anarchitect, taught building design there and was simultaneously producing his Prouns. The students produced architectural compositions under Lissitzky's supervision, a notable one being the speaker's platform which Chashnik designed in 1920, on which Lissitzky based his Lenin Tribune of 1924. The interaction between Leftist painting and architecture, and the emergence of Suprematism into building and design, are vividly illustrated by the output of Unovis members. As an architect, Lissitzky radically altered his artistic approach under the influence of Malevich’s Suprematism but, reciprocally, he also influenced the formal experiments of Malevich and Unovis as a whole. The short period – 1919–20 – spent by Lissitzky at Vitebsk undoubtedly gave Malevich a better understanding of the architectonic implications of his Suprematist works, as well as stimulating his interest and that of the Unovis students, such as Suetin, Khidekel, Chashnik and others, in architecture and design. Suprematism's shift towards architecture and design was already obvious in work of Unovis members before they left Vitebsk. It became even more marked when Malevich's group moved to Petrograd in the autumn of 1922 and went to work in the laboratory he directed at the local Ginkhuk, where he and his pupils produced the Planit and Arkitecton projects in the mid-1920s. Chashnik and Suetin immersed themselves in decorative and applied art, such as designing textiles and ceramics, and did exhibition design, while Khidekel continued his architectural training and made great use of Suprematist methods in his projects. ## Sinskulptarkh and Zhivskulptarkh – 1919–20 The Commission for the Resolution of Questions Bearing on the Synthesis of Sculpture and Architecture, known as Sinskulptarkh, was created in May 1919 and attached, initially, to the Subsection for Sculpture, then to that for Artistic Work, of the Fine Arts Department (Izo) of the People's Commissariat for Education. The Commission consisted of a sculptor, Korolev, who chaired it, and seven architects: Dombrovsky, Istselenov, Krinsky, Ladovsky, Raikh, Rukhlyadev and Fidman. This was to be the first new association of avant-garde architects. Significantly, it did not arise under the aegis of the Architectural Art Department of the People's Commissariat, because that Department was headed by a dedicated Classicist, Zholtovsky. The young architects who disliked the general line then enforced by Zholtovsky set out to look for new ways of generating form and rejected Classicism. It was shared dislikes in the field of art, rather than common aims, which united the members of Sinskulptarkh fromthe start. They still did not see their way to a new architecture at all clearly, yet were convinced that the revival of Classicism could not provide astarting point for new departures, which they sought, instead, in the study of the formal achievements of Leftist art. The Commission set itself the ultimate aim of synthesizing all spatial arts– painting, sculpture and architecture– but confined itself at the start tothe resolution of problems arising from a fusion of sculpture with architecture. The actual experiments of the Commission were first directed towards the design and production of plans for a Temple of the New Cult – a building for communication among nations' – which was to be a new type of edifice intended for the performance of mass events. At this stage the sculptor Korolev greatly influenced the experiments of thearchitects who made up the Commission. Korolev's own works were Cubist – the best known being the Bakunin monument in 1918 and the design for a monument to Marx in 1919 – and his experimental work was notable for its mixture of materials, such as metal with plaster of Paris. Soon after the Revolution, Korolev also shifted his interests towards the boundaries of sculpture with architecture, and with the new field of design. He produced projects such as poster pillars and platforms for a variety of agitational purposes as well as domestic items like table lamps and ornamental combs. In his sculpture Korolev clearly displayed architectonie inclinations and a strong sense of the interaction of sculpture with its architectural setting. He invariably designed the pedestals for his monuments himself in order to establish an organic link with their environment, as for instance in his Monument to the Fighters of the Revolution in Saratov, of 1924–25, and the Lenin Monument in Tashkent, of 1933. All this prompted a convergence of views between Korolev and the architects at that early stage and made him a central figure in passing the torch' to them from the arts. His influence on these architects bore first and foremost on such men as Ladovsky, the leader of Rationalism, and Ilya Golosov, the leading exponent of Symbolist Romanticism. Leftist art exerted its influence on these two men's work mainly through the intermediary of Cubist sculpture, as their artistic credos of the early 1920s clearly showed. This applies particularly to IlyaGolosov. He had not been a member of Sinskulptarkh, but was closely connected with the architects belonging to the Commission, shared their inclinationsand enthusiastically identified himself, as they did, with the experiences and methods of Cubist sculpture. In December 1919, a group of sculptors, architects and students belonging to the Second Free State Art Studios founded a sculptural co-operative, which Ilya Golosov joined, together with the active members of Zhivskulptarkh (see below), Korolev, Ladovsky and Istselenov. Korolev led this cooperative, and under his direction the architects and students learned to apply the methods of plastic composition then current among Cubists. Korolev's own work within Sinskulptarkh may also be regarded as an attempt to introduce sculptural methods into architecture, and even to impart to a building some distant association with sculpture of a generalized, usually Cubist kind, together with dynamic composition. There was a marked touch of anthropomorphism about these architectural designs, but a series of projects evidently dating from 1920 already point to a more clear-cut break by Korolev with a sculptural approach. The influence of Cubist sculpture is obvious in the designs of the Sinskulptarkh architects during the Commission's early, plastic' period in 1919. In their search for new means of expression they drew upon the past; on the ways in which various geometric solids combine, and on visual associations with natural objects, such as rocks and plants. It was assumed that one of the distinguishing features of the new architecture should be pronounced dynamism in the composition. Two painters, Rodchenko and Shevchenko, and then an architect, Mapu, were co-opted at the end of 1919, and the organization changed its name to Zhivskulptarkh (Commission for the Synthesis of Painting, Sculpture and Architecture). The main emphasis during this second stage of the Commission’s work, occupying the first half of the 1920s, was upon experimental designs for buildings with a new social function, such as the Housing Commune (Kommunaly dom) or the House of Soviets (Sovdep). The strongest influence from painting at this stage was that of Rodchenko. When Rodchenko worked on the decoration of the Café Pitoresk in 1917, he was already attempting to apply Leftist painting methods to the design of a lamp. This would produce a transitional series of abstract compositions, which became the basis of sketches for the Café’s lamps. In Zhivskulptarkh he was effectively repeating this process of escaping from Leftist painting by way of intermediary compositions, but now shifting into architecture rather than product design. Rodchenko first produced a whole series of abstract architectonic compositions, then based actual architectural projects upon them, to create a kiosk, a Sovdep, and so on. His output and that of Korolev vividly illustrate the way in which artists belonging to the Leftist trend sought their way out of Cubist and non-objective compositions into object design, and later into architecture – this latter stage involving a process of cross-fertilizationwith the work of the architects in the group. The members of Zhivskulptarkh showed their work at an exhibition in Moscow during the summer of 1920; the Commission then dissolved and many of its members joined Inkhuk in the same year. These interactions between fine art and architecture contributed to the emergence of new aesthetic concepts. But another and no less important aspect of this interaction involved the theory and practice of so-called Production Art. Industrial design activity was now emerging at the junction of architecture and fine art in order to create new consumer products. Apart from their combined work on decorations for towns and public holidays, innovative architects and Production artists were also united by their Rationalist approach to the provision of an appropriate environment for the labour, leisure and everyday life of the working population. This activity was already reflected in Zhivskulptarkh and Unovis, but its two main and complementary centres were Inkhuk and Vkhutemas, both of which combined innovative architects and Constructivist painters. From these circles emerged the first designers, such as Tatlin, Rodchenko, Gan, Stepanova, the Stenbergs and Popova. It was there that the theory and principles of the two most influential movements inSoviet architecture of the 1920s– Rationalism and Constructivism – were elaborated amid arguments, experiments and debates. ## Inkhuk (1920–24) The acronym Inkhuk stood for Moscow Institute of Artistic Culture, This was a unique association of creative individuals in Soviet art, bringing together painters, sculptors, architects, art historians and theoreticians of Production Art. It provided an important centre in which the concepts underlying Leftist tendencies in art were formulated. Inkhuk had several dozen active members and was headed by an elected Presidium successively chaired by Kandinsky, Rodchenko,Brik, Arvatov and then Brik again, while Babichev, a member of the Presidium virtually throughout its existence, provided continuity. Lectures ongeneral topics were read and discussed at plenary meetings of the Institute. Particular points of theory and creative work were discussed in greater depth within individual sectionsand working parties. Inkhuk went through several stages of development duringits short existence. Embittered arguments took place; some opinions gained the upper hand, while the supporters of others left the Institute; the general orientation of Inkhuk's work and its tasks changed; so did its membership; its structure and methods altered; and new people came to the top. Once past the organizational stage, the first period of Inkhuk's activity – from May 1920 until the autumn of that year – was concerned with the work ofits then unique Section of Monumental Art, devoted to the pursuit of Kandinsky's concept of a synthetic Monumental Art. Attention focused on perceptual psychology and the mutual relation between means of expression proper to painting, poetry, dance and music. Kandinsky's approach to problems of form-generation and questions concerning relations between the arts provoked protests from a number of Inkhuk members, most of whom had come from Zhivskulptarkh. Opposition to Kandinsky was headed by Rodchenko, whose supporters created the Working Group for Objective Analysis, consisting of Rodchenko himself, Babichev, Stepanova, Popova, Ladovsky and others, in November 1920, and tabled a proposal for a change in the orientation of the Institute's work. Disregarding the more intricate contradictions involved and the finer shades of difference between a variety of opinions, it would be fair to say that the split in Inkhuk towards the end of 1920 occurred between two groups of Leftist painters with different views about the further evolution of non-representational art and the part it should play, both in the development of art as a whole and within ordinary life.Both sides believed that non-representational art should provide the basis for a new synthetic art. Kandinsky, however, saw the future of painting as involving its transformation through interaction with timerelated arts (music, dance and poetry) into a specifically dynamic form of expression in which colour, music, movement and speech would combine. Rodchenko, on the other hand, believed that painting would contribute to the creationof a new objective environment by interacting with the space-related arts (sculpture and architecture). When Kandinsky and a group of his followers left Inkhuk in January 1921, Rodchenko became its chairman. The work of the Group for Objective Analysis followed a programme drawn up by Babichev. The Group's most important event was a discussion held in January-April 1921 onThe analysis of the concepts of construction and composition and the dividing line between them’. Each participant was expected to contribute a thesis supporting his or her point of view, and two graphic works illustrating the differences between composition and construction as he or she saw them. This debate revealed fundamental disagreements between Rationalist architects and Constructivist painters concerning the approach to the generation of form. The first stressed the role of composition, the latter based form-generation on the manifesting of constructive rationality. These differing approaches resulted, among other things, in the creation at the very start of 1921 of two new working groups. The first consisted of architects – Ladovsky, Dokuchaev, Krinsky, Efimov, Petrov, and others – the second of Constructivists – Gan, Ioganson, Medunetsky, Rodchenko, the Stenbergs and Stepanova. Alexander Vesnin, the future leader of architectural Constructivism, at that time himself a member of Inkhuk, joined neither group, but in May 1921, entered the Working Group of Objectivists formed in April and comprising Drevin, Popova and Udaltsova. Until the summer of 1921, these four working groups broadly covered the work done at Inkhuk. That autumn, further developments once more changed the Institute's orientation. Militant members of Lef and Production Art theorists headed by Brik joined Inkhuk, and sharply criticized the direction the Institute's work was taking. They branded it as Veshchizm (Thing-ism, or the Culture ofThings), and regarded Constructivism as an intermediary stage between Veshchizm and Production Art. The proponents of Production Art summoned the painters to work in real production, effectively abolished Inkhuk's division into separate working groups, and concentrated all its activity on the plenary meetings. The main emphasis of Inkhuk's work on professional problems concerning form-generation shifted to sociological questions propounded in lectures by Arvatov, Brik, Kushner, Tarabukin and otherProduction Art theorists. From then on, working groups were set up mainly in order to promote Production Art and Constructivism beyond the confines of Inkhuk. It was precisely for this purpose that a student group for the architectural faculty of Vkhutemas was created by Inkhuk in February 1924, with Brik, Alexander Vesnin and Lavinsky representing Inkhuk while Barshch, Simbirtsev, Krasilnikov, Lavinskaya, Komarova and Semenova were among the students. Throughout its activity Inkhuk was closely connected with a large number of artistic, educational and scientific organizations, such as Zhivskulptarkh, Obmokhu, Unovis,Lef, the Museum of Painterly Culture, the Workers' Preparatory Faculty (Rabfak) of the Arts, and others, some of whose members and contributors also belonged to Inkhuk. The latter thus became during the early 1920s a sort of doctrinal centre and debating club for like-minded creative artists and promoters of new trends in the figurative arts, architecture and the newly emerged field of product design. The aesthetic outlook of both leaders of the two most influential trends in Soviet architecture during the 1920s – Ladovsky for Rationalism and Alexander Vesnin for Constructivism – was in fact shaped in Inkhuk. It was also there that the first organizations representing these trends took shape – the Working Group of Architects in 1921 and the Vkhutemas Architectural Students’ Group in 1924 – on the basis of which the main associations of creative architects, Asnova and Osa, were established in 1923 and 1925 respectively. Inkhuk acted first and foremost as a centre for the formulation of new theoretical concepts, many of which were subsequently embodied in experimental work at Vkhutemas. ## Vkhutemas and Vkhutein – 1920–30 In the first years after the Revolution all major architects with any claim to originality devoted a good deal of attention to higher educational establishments, where virtually all the new trends and schools were born in the 1920s. This was not a matter of chance. When the professional associations in Moscow and Petrograd renewed their activity after the Revolution, the prevalence within them of traditionally minded architects forced the innovators to turn to the young. Their leaders were all aware that the outcome of the struggle between them and the traditionalists would mainly depend upon the way in which the rising generation of architects had been trained. The battle for control over higher architectural education became an issue of principle on which architecture's orientation in the immediate future would largely depend. The student body, still open-minded and lacking any set models, became a target for anyone wishing to launch a new trend or found a school. That is why Zholtovsky, Ladovsky, Ivan Fomin, Ilya Golosov, Alexander Vesnin and other architects with a clear system of aesthetic views devoted so much time and trouble during this first period of development to teaching and regarded it as crucial in determining the future direction of Soviet architecture. Art education throughout the country was totally reorganized in the autumn of 1918. The variety of schools and colleges was replaced by Free State Art Studios, offering either a broad general curriculum or a specialized one in which the students were allowed to pick their tutors in the various subjects. Painters, architects and other types of artists all had the right to claim a studio of their own. The Stroganov Industrial Art College and the College of Painting, Sculpture and Architecture in Moscow were accordingly reorganized as the First and Second Free State Art Studios (SGKhM). Individual studios in the Second SGKhM were supervised by Zholtovsky, Shchusev and Rylsky. Methods of teaching were similar in all three studios and were laid down for each faculty by a single Curriculum Commission. Training began with study of the architectural orders and exercises on Classical subjects. The first academic year in the Architectural Faculty of the Second SGKhM, 1918–19, passed reasonably peacefully, coinciding as it did with the Classicist period among Soviet architects. By the end of the second year, however, student ferment broke out in earnest. The Classical bias of the teaching now failed to satisfy many students, and an exhibition of designs by members of Zhivskulptarkh greatly contributed to this. Ladovsky's and Krinsky's projects attracted the young as exceptionally dynamiccompositions,and as forms completely breaking with tradition and pointing the way to an architecture of the future. Balikhin, Mochalov, Krasilnikov, and others, organized an action group within the Architectural Faculty and called upon fellow students to reject Classicism. Leftist studios were set up in the autumn of 1920 as a combined venture by students both within Vkhutemas and outside it, in Zhivskulptarkh and elsewhere, in which two separate streams of protest against the imposition of Classicism were united. The development coincided with the fusion of the First and Second SGKhM into a single educational establishment – the Higher State Artistic Technical Studios(Vkhutemas), renamed Higher State Artistic Technical Institute (Vkhutein) in 1927–30. Vkhutemas was a novel type of educational establishment in which faculties of architecture, production (wood processing, metalwork, textiles, ceramics, polygraphy) and art – painting and sculpture – were combined. The composition of the teaching staff in the Architectural Faculty of Vkhutemas reflected the intricacies of the struggle between the traditionalists and the innovators. Three centres gradually formed within it between 1920 and 1923, each with its own set of artistic and educational principles: the academic studios, with Zholtovsky as chairman of the Curriculum Commission, and Shchusev, Norvert, Kokorin, Rylsky, Leonid Vesnin among the teaching staff; the United Leftist Studios (Obmas), with Ladovsky, Krinsky and Dokuchaev; and the independent Studio of Experimental Architecture, with Ilya Golosov and Melnikov. Many students switched from the old studios to the newly established Leftist ones. The two new centres established in the Architectural Faculty during the academic year 1920–21, with teaching methods of their own, signalled substantial changes in the creative mood within Vkhutemas. These new, forward-looking workshops each required a Curriculum Commission of its own to lay down teaching methods and, consequently, the orientation of the work as a whole. In Obmas, Ladovsky's psychoanalytical method provided the basis for the course, with Krinsky and Dokuchaev acting as lecturers. The Studio of Experimental Architecture relied on Ilya Golosov's theory of the construction of architectural organisms, since Melnikov had only just completed his studies and started out as an independent architect. Obmas achieved independence first, and the Vkhutemas Architectural Faculty was thus split into two sections, each with its own Curriculum Commission. Then, inNovember 1922, the Golosov and Melnikov workshop gained recognition (and a Curriculum Commission) as a third independent group within the Architectural Faculty. Both these workshops rejected traditional architectural forms, and the Classical orders in particular, but Ladovsky's approach differed sharply from that of Golosov in terms of the development of form. Ladovsky regarded space as the basic material of architecture and subordinated questions of volume to the organization of space. Golosov, on the other hand, gave priority in form-generation to elements of volume. These differences in approach between Ladovsky and Golosov also extended into their views on architectural composition, and therefore into their teaching methods. Despite all the differences between them, however, Ladovsky and Golosov were united in their attitude to the basic rules governing design in architecture. Both of them gave precedence to aesthetic issues, so that abstract formal tasks predominated in the initial stages of tuition in order to induce a deeper study of thie possible rules and methods of architectural composition. New architecture gradually consolidated its position in Vkhutemas from the start of the 1920s onwards. The new trends even began to affect the academic studios. An independent Foundation Department was created in Vkhutemas for introductory courses in which architects taught space, painters taught colour and sculptors taught volume. Ladovsky's psychoanalytical method became the basis of theSpace' discipline, while in Obmas students supervised studies under the direction of Krinsky, even though most of them (including Balikhin, Korzhev, Turkus and Lamtsov) had still not graduated. New student ferment broke out in Vkhutemas during 1923–24, reflecting developments throughout the modernist architectural movement. The Rationalists had strengthened their position and confirmed it by establishing Asnova – the Association of NewArchitects – but in the meantime another innovative trend was emerging in the form of Constructivism. The common front against traditionalism was breaking down, and divisions began to appear within the new architecture itself. Constructivism and Production Art secured much support among Vkhutemas architectural students. In Vkhutemas, as well as in the Rabfak of the Arts, where the Izo section under Babichev was preparing students for entry into Vkhutemas, the fight was on, with the support of Inkhuk and Lef, for the inclusion of Production Art concepts into the curriculum, in the Industrial and Architectural Faculties in particular. This struggle for new ideas was at its peak in the innovative studios of the Architectural Faculty, where it turned into an attack on abstract formal classroom tasks. The adherents of Constructivism and Production Art demanded that the study of formal problems should be replaced by work on practical ones, with a real purpose in view. The Lef group organized byInkhuk in the Vkhutemas Architectural Faculty was joined by some of Ladovsky's and Ilya Golosov's students. As a result of these sharpening demarcations within the new architecture, and the intensive growth of the new Constructivist trend, Alexander Vesnin's studio emerged as a new focus of ideas in the Vkhutemas Architectural Faculty during the school year 1924–25. Groups of students transferred to him from the academic studios as well as from those of Ladovsky and Ilya Golosov. From the mid-1920s onwards, when Golosov went over to Constructivism, two main centres with programmes clearly supporting the new architecture held their ground in Vkhutemas-Vkhutein: Ladovsky's and Alexander Vesnin's studios. There was a sort of silent competition between the course and diploma projects of these two studios run by the leaders of the main current trends in Soviet architecture – respectively, of Rationalism and Constructivism. Vkhutemas was not just an educational establishment. It provided one of the most important centres for the development of a new orientation in Soviet architecture. The Vkhutemas students' course and diploma projects received much publicity in the contemporary Soviet and foreign press, with the most interesting projects coming from the students of the Vesnins and Ladovsky. They contributed significantly to shaping the new architectural ideas, and it can safely be said that the role of Vkhutemas in shaping twentieth-century architecture equalled that of theBauhaus in Germany. By contrast with the organizations in existence before the Revolution and revived early in the 1920s, such as Mao, Poa, Oakh, etc, the new groups and organizations that came into being in higher educational establishments and other institutions were not narrowly vocational in character. The senior architects who joined them were linked by a common creative aspiration and a single set of aesthetic principles, rather than by narrowly professional interests. This was as true of the first essentially artistic associations formedin 1919–20, as it was of the purely architectural groupings which grew from the former during the first half of the decade. The direction then taken by art as a whole was the main reason why these mixed organizations and groupings emerged at all, and it also influenced the entire character of the work done by the leaders of the period. Regardless of their original training, Malevich, Tatlin, Lissitzky, Alexander Vesnin, Rodchenko, Lavinsky, the Stenbergs, Exter, Klutsis, Gan, Krinsky and many others practised painting, architecture, graphics, design and theatre art simultaneously. The multi-media character of their output was not simply a reflection of their multiple talents. It was the consequence of an era in which the features of a contemporary style were emerging through the interaction between various types ofart. Arkitectons, Counter-Reliefs, Prouns and experimental three-dimensional constructions helped architects to assimilate and make creative use of the formal and aesthetic discoveries of Leftist painting. It could be said that in these experimental creations painting was moving from the representation of objects to the shaping of them. ![](images/1d4eb4812a15a733188a7e07cdcdbd33a16579fa5182d78836879281b9dbb703.jpg) Symbolist Romanticism The lack of any actual building work during the first years of Soviet power led architects to concentrate on laboratory experiments. An evolution took place within their thinking that produced few outward signs, but several fundamentally new approaches were fast taking shape at this stage. Symbolist Romanticism was not a homogeneous movement with a body of theory and a stylistically specific set of compositional methods and artistic forms. Instead, it should be seen as an early stage in the search for a new image and fresh lines of development for Soviet architecture in oppoșition to the traditionalism of the Classicists. The revolutionary uplift of those early years, War Communism and the Civil War, created unusual conditions for artistic experimentation. The dynamism of public life increased intensely. The people became the masters of streets and squares. Demonstrations, meetings, assemblies and solemn Red Funerals required a new kind of presentation. The intensity of the class war also affected attitudes to objects, to fashion and to forms in art. When the workers rejected the previous way of life they often also did the same for its material manifestations, which they perceived as the symbolic embodiments of the past. The outlook of the masses at the time favoured the promotion ofideas concerned with art which rejected traditional aesthetic principles. The surge offeeling in favour of disowning the old order, its socio-economic relations, its ethics and the mercantile spirit, also embraced the arts. Buildings and objects representedmore than mere material assets which had to be handed over to the working people. They were seen as the embodiments of aesthetic ideals inherent in the dethroned classes of exploiters, and were therefore put aside, rejected and sometimes physically destroyed. Later on, when such associations had lost their immediacy, another psychological factor came to the fore, and the aesthetic images offered by these buildings became the subject of reinterpretation. Certain basic features were common to almost all shades and schools of Symbolist Romanticism: the rejection of traditional aesthetic principles prevailing before the Revolution; the determination to express the revolutionary surge of the masses and the dynamism of public life in the function of buildings, in their organization in terms of volume and space, and in their architectural form; the recourse to severe or ascetic forms with a view to creating a new system of aesthetics which would contrast with theeffeminate’ andpompous’ architectural forms of the past; the inclusion in the aesthetic vocabulary of features suggesting a symbolic meaning or even a story, in other words, the incorporation of architecture into Agitational Art; a generous use of industrial and mechanical motifs as symbols of the industrial proletariat's labour; the deliberate introduction of quantitative factors into the aesthetic canon so that squares, esplanades, halls, monuments and structures, and so on, became physically vast; a clear bias in favour of the collective, non-individualized client. In its stylistic dimension, Symbolist Romanticism ranged widely, drawing equally upon archaic forms and on the compositions of Suprematism and Cubo-Futurism. ## Archaicizing trends ## within the new architectural concept Although Classicism continued to be seen by architects as the embodiment of certain standards of professional proficiency, it was already widely regarded immediately after the Revolution as a stylistic system too obviously associated with pre-Revolutionary ideals. However, two trends attempted to draw on the architectural heritage for forms and compositional methods to create a new heroic' image. One of these used Classicism as a starting point, the other rejected it. The two archaicizing trendsultimately clashed in the large competitions held in 1919–20 for designs of a Workers' Palace and district public baths in Petrograd, and crematoria there and in Moscow. Interesting contributions were submitted from many stylistic directions, with Classicists suchas Ivan Fomin among them. Those architects who preferred not to use Classical orders resorted in their search of the past to building types like fortresses and medieval castles, which had romantic and heroic associations. The new Workers’ Palaces’, be they Palaces of Labour or of Culture, copied traditional palace forms in order to stress an inherently majestic quality, while allusions to fortresses reflected the power and irreversibility of the Revolution. Typical of these projects were Belogrud's reinterpretations of Roman architecture in compositions for the 1919 Petrograd Workers’ Palace competition, and for the Moscow Palace of Labour competition in 1922–23. The former, entitled 'Workers’ Fortress’, was particularly characteristic: a structure where the power of its external features was intended to represent the Revolutionary masses' firm resolve. With its rusticated castle walls, crenellated towers and the general effect of an impregnable fortress, this original and well-proportioned spatial composition incorporates elements that evoke such diverse medieval buildings as the Castel Sant’Angelo in Rome and the Palazzo Vecchio in Florence. Several other architects experimented with the same kind of archaic romanticism, Buryshkin and Langbard among them, as well as some members of Zhivskulptarkh. Istselenov's sketches display a determination to produce a new type of building not dictated by tradition, but it is equally obvious that none could be found and that he was forced to turn to images from the past in order to escape from present stereotypes. He experimented extensively with Gothic shapes which attracted him by their dynamic upward motion, their compositional intricacy and the angularity and spikiness of their lancet forms. The resulting compositions were Gothic in plan and elevation, but to varying degrees stripped of ornament. Yet despite all Istselenov's efforts to transfigure and denude traditional forms, their stylistic imprint still shows through in his sketches. He was clearly aware of this and tried his hand at complex hybrid structures, which fused Gothic and Classical features, or both styles -jointly or separately – either with modern metal lattice structures, or with elements inspired by even more ancient models, vaguely reminiscent of Egyptian pyramids. A Gothic origin is also perceptible in many of Vladimir Fidman's sketches at Zhivskulptarkh, even though such stylistic ingredients are simplified to the limit and transformed into Cubist forms, or eveninto vegetative ones that disturbingly resemble fantastic flowers. Fidman experimented in particular with vaulted demi-ogival arcades incorporated into horizontally flattened and multiple-storeyed compositions. He also sketched out new, intricately composed towers made up ofjuxtaposed or intersecting cylinders and half-cylinders, or of cylinders cut back along an inscribed spiral, etc, with overlying tiers pierced by demi-ogives. Sigismund Dombrovsky's sketches also display a search for inspiration in various medieval prototypes, such as castles and fortresses, Usually he drastically simplified such forms, however, and related them more closely to natural shapes, such as mountains and crags. Dynamic compositions: ## the influence of Cubo-Futurism The extreme Left wing of Symbolist Romanticism consisted of painters and architects whose output was clearly influenced by Cubism, and by Cubo-Futurism in particular. Their works conveyed the visually volatile and unstable effect of restless dynamism. They mostly worked on the decorative treatment of urban spaces and saw it as their main task to achieve a maximum transformation of the bourgeois’ urban scene through monumental art and small, quasi-architectural structures. The Cubo-Futurist commitment to a chaotic assemblage of random, dismembered and dynamic forms proved very much to the point, when these artists were expected to break up the existing façades of bourgeois buildings solely by decorative means. When they decorated streets and squares for public holidays they did their best not to stress the architectonic features of existing structures and complexes, and to destroy them visually by superimposing a totally fresh artistic conception. Sharp lines and bright spots of colour produced new and consciously destructive' compositions. In his design for the decoration of a viaduct in Moscow, for example, Vladimir Krinsky overlaid its rectangularspans with a collapsing' arch. The minor, quasi-architectural structureswere designed to contrast sharply with the former pattern of city building, so as to disrupt the regularities, to inject Revolutionary dynamism and passion into a tidy bourgeois conception. In 1921, for instance, a project by Krinsky inserted a speaker's platform between houses so as todisturb the existing lay-out and bring movement into the aspect of the street. In those early days, when existing architecture was still linked with the past in many people's minds, painters and architects did their best with arts at their disposal to explode, wreck and destroy earlier architectural forms and make the old buildings unrecognizable. Natan Altman used 20,000 arshin (more than 15,000 yards) of canvas to alter the Palace Square for the celebration of the first anniversary of the October Revolution, In his project, the Alexander Column was flanked by a speaker's platform whose restless forms were calculated to destroy' this imperial monument, while other emperors'monuments were entirely hidden by decorations. Cubist and Cubo-Futurist influences are obvious in the projects of Zhivskulptarkh members, even in those based on older styles. The assertion of one of the fundamental features of the new architecture – visible dynamism combined with the rejection of symmetry and right angles – led to the creation of complex and novel plans and spatial compositions. Dynamic designs incorporating shapes with destructively acute angles, diagonals, shearedsurfaces,cantilevers, curved and fractured forms, the contrasting utilization of light, all formed part of the varied means of expression employed in projects such as the competition designs for a kiosk by Rodchenko and Krinsky, the experimental projects for the Temple of Communion Between Nations by Ladovsky, Krinsky and Rukhlyadev among others, for a communal house by Ladovsky and Krinsky, for a Sovdep by Rodchenko and Shevchenko. Unlike Istselenov, Fidmanand Dombrovsky, certain Zhivskulptarkh members such as Ladovsky and Krinsky consistently rejected traditional forms and methods. Every one of them, however, independently contributed to the common cause of creating dynamic compositions of unusual form. Nikolai Ladovsky concentrated on experiments with a variety of geometric volumes – parallelepipeds, spheres, cylinders, cones, pyramids, and so forth – which he linked in complex and most unusual combinations. He was particularly attracted by a dynamic, upward-surging movement, and experimented widely in this area: with solids overlapping by a cantilever effect, gradually tapering towards the top, surmounted by a cone, a pyramid or a spire; or with intricate many-tiered compositions which seem to spiral into the sky. Vladimir Krinsky made no attempt to impart any particular direction of movement to his dynamic compositions dating from this period. Some of his building designs seem to be blown apart by an inner force, as though seen just as an internal explosion was in progress: the foundations appear to be firmly set on the ground, while the upper parts look like debris projected upwards and outwards, or like a chaotic conglomeration of items. Even when using simple geometrical solids, Krinsky invariably tried to impart apparent instability to the composition as a whole: as, for example, with a cube-shaped building stood on edge. Ladovsky and Krinsky might have been expected to interact mainly with Leftist sculpture through Korolev. Instead, the influence revealed in their projects and sketches of this period comes from painting. This is particularly true of Krinsky, whose graphic compositions, sketches and projects of 1920–21 clearly show the strength of Rodchenko'sinfluence at this stage. In his early non-objective compositions Rodchenko had made extensive use of the combination of straight lines with planes rotated through different angles. In his architectural designs of 1919–20 for a kiosk and a Sovdep, this vocabulary was extended into dynamic compositions involving planes and volumes together, with a variety of lines acting as supports and tension members. ## The symbolism of artistic forms and of the architectural image In its various manifestations and techniques art usuallycarries some symbolic meaning. This may be consciously grasped, or alternatively not perceived, so that it merely forms part of a complex of associations and representational stereotypes. Every compositional system may be treated as a language, each letter’ and word' of which has a symbolic as well as an aesthetic meaning. With the passage of time, however, the balance between symbolic content and artistic expression gradually alters, both within architectural forms themselves, and in the perception of the beholder. The associations that caused a specific meaning to be linked to a given form become blurred and fade, so that symbolic meaning increasingly yields in importance to the style's purely aesthetic properties. But if the forms and methods of art built up during the course of centuries should come to be swept away by a new era, attention once again focuses sharply on the symbolic function of art. It is as thoughall development reverts to fundamentals, so that fresh images emerge which hark back to a primordial meaning of form and its attendant system of symbols, as part of the struggle against outdated stereotypes. Attempts were made in the early years of Soviet architecture to give back to symbolism its commanding role in shaping the concept of art. Architects tried to make the symbolic content of their concepts more readily accessible by resorting to thematic and quasi-representational elements, to purely external associations and visual clichés. Great efforts were made in the early post-Revolutionary years to evolve a universally intelligible symbolic language in monumental art and architecture, by means of simple geometric shapes and colour. Painters and sculptors contributed a great deal to these endeavours. Such symbolic forms also represented one of the intermediary stages or channels used by Leftist painting to make the transition from representational expression, via symbolic forms, into the language of architecture. The work of many leading artists, Malevich and Tatlin among them, passed through a symbolist’ stage. At Unovis, Malevich worked out a system ofsigns' aimed at closing the gap between representational and non-representational media. He allocated explicit symbolic meanings to abstract geometrical figures and colour. The square stood for the world as a whole, the circle for the earth's movement, the triangle for a higher force. Extended rectangles which he called Supremy symbolized theinteraction of forces, with black, white and red representing, respectively, evil, good and revolution. A red square thus symbolized world revolution, and so on. Such a symbolism of geometrical forms and colour was used by the members of Unovis in their Monument to the Victory of the Radiant New, in Vitebsk, a parable for the significance and meaning of the October Revolution. A square platform, symbolizing the world, was surmounted by a half-sphere, representing the globe, into which three white and red wedges were driven to symbolize the victory of the Radiant New'. This symbolism of geometry and colour was also used to decorate the town as a whole and to change its inherited image, just as the Cubo-Futurist compositions of painters and sculptors had done earlier in Petrogradand Moscow.Whereas the latter, however, remained minor visual events amongst the mass of city buildings, the Vitebsk scheme was consistent in style throughout, and did genuinely alter the visual character of this much smaller city.A strange provincial town,’ wrote Sergei Eisenstein in 1920. Red brick – like so many towns of the western provinces. Sooty and dismal. But this town is uncommonly strange. The main streets here are covered in white paint over the red bricks. Green circles straggle over the white background, orange squares, blue triangles. . . .’ It was like a sort of Suprematist confetti scattered along the streets of this bedazzled town'. A symbolism of simple geometrical forms and colour was widely used in Agit Art during the years immediately following the Revolution, It affected the dlecoration of cities at public holidays, because of the opportunities these occasions offered for the use of Revolutionary symbols and emblems, as well as the design of posters and memorials. Lissitzky's famous poster Cleave the Whites with the Red Wedge, of 1919, was one of these. This form of symbolism became so ingrained that it was incorporated in monuments even by such architects as Ivan Fomin, Rudnev and Gegello, who for all other purposes used traditional forms in their designs. Ivan Fomin, for example, designed a series of monuments clearly influenced by the symbolism of simple geometrical shapes and the aesthetics of Cubism and Cubo-Futurism. The titles of his competition designs speak for themselves: Cube, for example, was one project for a monument to the victims of the Revolution at Lesnoy in Petrograd, consisting of a massive cube supported by short round pillars, carrying a brief inscription. Displacement (Sdvig) was a monument to Sverdlov in Moscow, with simple geometric shapes, dynamically displaced, resting on a base consisting of broken classical fragments – an allegory of the old world's destruction. The widespread use of symbolism in architecture was due not only to the function of the structures concerned – monuments, memorials and palaces – but equally to the fact that the investment of symbolic meanings in the new forms hastenedthe rejection of the old, while helping to familiarize the masses with the new simple geometric shapes and opening their eyes to the aesthetic values involved. Simple, static, geometric solids, such as the cube, the sphere, and the pyramid had already been much used in the past. Cubo-Futurism added the dynamism of displacements, diagonals and spirals, the instability of crumbling'compositions, the stress of cantilever effects and suchlike. The story told by the symbolic interpretation of an architectural form was regarded as so important in Agit Art terms that a unified formal symbolism was applied across the boundaries of competing stylistic concepts and was given priority over all other aesthetic considerations. What first struck many spectators in Tatlin's Monument to the Third International was less the unaccustomed architectural devices, such as its armature or the use of glass, than the symbolism of the spiral, an allegorical representation of the Revolution’s upsurge and drive. This design was often imitated: a spiralling composition was combined with traditional forms, as in Yakulov’s project for a Monument to the 26 Commissars of Baku, or with understated and subdued shapes, as in Lyudvig's project in 1922–23 for the Palace of Labour in Moscow, or with openwork metal structures like Shchuko's project of 1924 for a Lenin Monument. The applicatiðn to architecture of the narrative symbolism used at that early stage in Agitational Art also clearly reflected the tastes of the masses. During the Revolutionary years, they had become accustomed to bright, poster-like images with a distinctly symbolic use of shape and colour, and to the widespread use of emblematic forms. The numerous projects and proposals for the perpetuation of Lenin's memory are indicative in this sense, especially the amateur contributions submitted to the open competition for the design of the Lenin Mausoleum in 1925, almost all of which incorporate a symbolic meaning in the architectural conception An All-Union competition was also held that year in Odessa for the design of a combined Lenin monument and mausoleum to local victims of the Revolution. Here too, many of the submissions relied upon symbolic devices common at that time, such as displacements, dynamism, and spirals, while a number of contributions resorted to directly thematic symbolism. In the project entitled Mayak (Lighthouse), for instance, a tower seems to be spiralling upwards through a domed building of emphatically classical design, like the Pantheon in Rome. The symbolism of simple geometric shapes, combined with displacement and dynamism derived from Cubo-Futurism, turned out to be no more than a passing attraction in so far as most architects were concerned. Many such forms and compositional methods gradually shed their symbolic content and merely became yet another means of expression used in the new architecture. Symbolist Romanticism, however, acquired such importance in the output of certain architects that they founded a new school (1920-24) based upon an architectonic reinterpretation of the symbolism of simple geometric forms and colour. Ilya Golosov's influence was decisive in the formation of this school. He progressed from a simplified form of Classicism in 1918–19, as in his designs for a hospital, a crematorium and a school, to the application of Symbolist Romanticism in 1920–23. In Golosov's mind the strong dynamism of these works expressed the heroic nature of the Revolution in architectural form. Tension, upsurge and struggle were conveyed here by the interaction of volumes; the architectural masses display lines ofstress, and displaced volumes, acute angles and slanting lines are prominent. In the early 1920s, IlyaGolosov propounded a new design approach in histheory of the structure of architectural organisms’, devoted to the general rules governing the structuring oflarge forms. He supplemented this with a theory of visual dynamics (literally of movement’) in architecture, which dealt with the practical methods of generating a dynamic composition. Golosov's theories served as a foundation for the tenets of an independent Symbolist Romanticist architectural school. It had its own theoretical principles, elaborated in Ilya Golosov's works; its compositional techniques; its specific teaching methods devised by Golosov and implemented by him in the Architectural Faculties of Vkhutemas and the Polytechnical Institute; and its distinct set of followers. A group of Golosov's pupils from both establishments joined him because they believed that his pioneering work would lead to the creation of a revolutionary architectural concept. From their enthusiastic experiments with new forms and compositional methods, we have a range of student projects by Shibaev, Fedor Andreev, Vegman, Khiger and others. ## llya Golosov: theories of the structure of architectural organisms and of visual dynamics Although Golosov was dedicated to the search for new architectural forms, by far the most important aspect of his creed was its emphasis on the expressive combination of large forms or volumes, rather than on detailed stylistic innovation, as the basis of design. He was a master of massive form, and practically all his works, regardless of their individual stylistic character, aim to manifest the geometrical specificities of a form of this type which dominates the spatial composition in question and subordinates to itself all other forms and details. In his theory of the structure of architectural organisms, Golosov gave great importance to a clear distinction between mass’ and form’. He defined mass’ as the primary threedimensional shape devoid of all content. Form’, on the other hand, was dependent upon the particular content and manifested in that content. Architectural form was thus mass with specific meaning. It was the totality of mass plus idea – the idea being the architectural intention which had brought it into being. Any piece of architecture could therefore be regarded both as a mass expressing its own relative position in space, and a form bearing the imprint of a specific inner content. Golosov differentiated between objective and subjective mass. He defined the latter as independent of the relative position of other masses connected with it. As he saw it, the subjective mass was a solid that not only supplied the nucleus of an architectural organism's general structure, but represented the compositional centre in which the entire spatial concept was focused. In architectural terms, its function was to subordinate to itself all other masses within the composition, while retaining its own independence in relation to them. Since the subjective mass provided the very centre of a composition and was the form carrying greatest expressive responsibility, its design required the closest attention. The presence or absence of harmony in the composition as a whole depended on how well it was conceived. An objective mass always remained subordinate to the subjective mass, and dependent upon it. Golosov also attached great importance to identifying the motion inherent in masses' – which he saw as a function of the geometrical properties, and as one of the starting points of the compositional process. He was particularly concerned with asymmetrical volumes, which in his view interacted with each other more intensively than symmetrical ones. The shape of a volume grafted on to one of these, and the position of the graft, were far less constrained than in the case of symmetrical, fully balanced volumes; and this fact further stimulated Golosov's interest in the ‘motion inherent in masses’. He introduced, moreover, the concept of the line of attraction’, according to which every organism carried a built-in force consisting of the balanced tension between all the forces within an organism', and causing that organism to gravitate in a given direction. It was these lines of attraction which held individual architectural elements together as an integrated composition. Golosov distinguished between lines of attraction that wereactive’ or vertical, and those that werepassive’ or horizontal. The development of an architectural composition was based on such lines, which constituted the intangible compositional framework perceived by the designer, and which bonded together and unified the organism visually. The mark of a true artist was the ability to sense the lines of attraction and use them to establish a harmonious composition. Golosov and his pupils used these principles extensively during the early 1920s, in dynamic compositions which vividly demonstrated their intensive pursuit of ways to express the movement of masses’ and their attempts to manifest the close interaction of mass and space, both horizontally and vertically. According to Golosov's theory, lines of attraction did not necessarily imply dynamism in a composition. They were merely a tool for revealing the possible roles of a given three-dimensional element within the general composition of an architectural organism. As such they could provide the architect with material for a variety of compositions: both static, with the lines of gravitation of individual elements balancing each other; and dynamic, in which the resultant of the lines of gravitation is clearly – even though notionally – located outside the architectural organism itself. The principle of the motion of architectural masses' became one of the most important features in Golosov's design theory during the early 1920s. He therefore supplemented his general theory of the construction of architectural masses by a special theory concerningmovement in architecture. He connected the elucidation of movement in architectural compositions with the task of reflecting the dynamic character of an era, and thus naturally attributed particular importance to expressing this notion of movement in the design of large public buildings. In 1920, he produced a project for a city bakery in which the horizontal movement of the arches in the roof of the transport shed sweeps over into the spiral of the store and through it into a vertical movement along the main axis of the cylindrical building. In his project for a radio station dating from 1921, the building seems to grow stepwise out of the ground as its moves towards the aerial mast, where the horizontal movement is arrested well beyond the confines of the building and switched to the vertical axis of the free-standing mast During the same year, Golosov also produced two versions of a project for an observatory, the main element in both being a vertical cylinder topped by a dome. In one variant, the horizontal movement developed within a complex stepped composition of prisms is halted at the tower and redirected vertically through two semicircular annexes, which oppose the horizon- These early projects by Golosov, carried out in a spirit of Symbolist Romanticism, incorporate bulging volumes, slanting lines, clashes between horizontal and vertical movements, the dynamism of spirals and many other ways of generating a dynamic composition. The displacement of volumes in the plan features rarely. This is equally true of some later designs, in which a spatial composition conceived on a straightforward or even a strictly symmetrical plan is locked into position by a structural element which in itself is complex and restless – as in the project for a forge and pump house of 1921 – or, alternatively, in which a rhythmic series of equally pitched roofs with an angular, saw-toothed outline sets up an accelerating horizontal movement within the composition as a whole, as in the project for the Ostankino stud in 1922. Golosov went on to make increasingly pronounced use of rectangular volumes displaced in plan, and the rhythm of parallel blocks in echelon. The most interesting of these projects is his entry for the Moscow Palace of Labour competition, of late 1922 and early 1923, which represents the culmination of his SymbolistRomanticist experiments. He kept to a rectangular plan for the majority of the component elements of the Palace, but rotated these through an angle of 45° in relation to the long sides of the building. The result was a highly original composition in both plan and spatial organization. The façades carried three triangular projections, the edges of each of these being treated differently. The movement of theresulting complex composition was directed towards an open amphitheatre containing a speaker's platform, which projected outwardson a cantilevered metal beam. The movement in the masses inscribed into the facades of this building was focused on this platform and directed along a diagonal towards the square. In 1923, Golosov produced a design for the Afghan pavilion at the All-Russian Agricultural and Handicraft Exhibition in Moscow, where he moved from the earlier echelon device to link the square-planned blocks on the diagonal. This project influenced Lyudvig's design of 1925 for the Afghan embassy in Turkey. Thereafter, Golosov gradually lost interest in angular displacements in the plan; they became less přominent and were eventually eliminated from his projects. Colour took their place as the basic device of expressive composition. During 1923–24, experimentation in colour became characteristic of many Golosov projects, notably the Far Eastern pavilion at the 1923 Agricultural Exhibition, the competition projects for the Soviet pavilion at the 1925 Paris Exhibition and the Lenin House of the People at Ivanovo-Voznesensk in 1924. He did use triangular projections in 1924 in two competition designs – for the LeningradskayaPravda newspaper's Moscow office and the Arkos joint-stock company building – but these were no more than bay fronts, rather than volumes set at an angle on a rectangular plan. Certain of Golosov's experimental projects of 1923–24 show him trying to combine simple geometrical compositions, contrasting tonal effects and latticed structural elements. This work brought him close to early Constructivist experiments and induced him to align himself with that position in the mid-1920s. The evolution of Golosov's work was reflected in that of his pupils. Their main aim in 1921–22 was to produce dynamic compositions and touse for this purpose all the various means and methodes developed by Symbolist Romanticism – stepped increases of volume, displacements, sloping lines and planes, and so on. The projects by Fedor Andreev for a Palace of Water Sports and a café on the Tverskoy Boulevard, and by Georgy Vegman for the Central Archive of Old Legal Proceedings and a port lighthouse, all carried out under Golosov's supervision, are typical in this respect. By 1924, however, Vegman had produced a project for the Museum of Red Moscow which clearly marked an intermediate stage on the way from the dynamism of Symbolist Romanticism to the strictness of Constructivist spatial designs. The architects whose work was inspired by Symbolist Romanticism in the early 1920s made a definite contribution to the development of a new direction in architecture, not least by extending the range of new formal research. In their attempts to create forms and compositions that would visually embody certain states of consciousness, such as repose, dynamismand aspiration, they applied the structural opportunities offered by newtechnologywithgreat artistic freedom. Their approach to the part played by new constructional techniques in the development of a new architectural conception allied them with the Rationalists. There was also an affinity between some of the external formal devices used to produce spatial compositions by the followers of both schools. The Symbolist Romanticists, however, developed theirown concept through the symbolism and dynamism of form; attributed a meaning to form as such; and introduced certain narrative and depictive devices. Their formal and aesthetic experiments were conducted on the borderline dividing architecture from sculpture, and they treated every structure as being akin to a monument. The Rationalists, on the other hand, sought to display ![](images/25b29df5920d2491481edfd8a6a21d237335b4e921f9c30fc97495eebe6ac422.jpg) The problem of new form During periods of transition, two requirements become paramount in art, and that includes architecture. They are the requirements for new means of expression and for a fresh aesthetic image or concept. It is on these problems that the innovative architects have always concentrated their energies. Those of them who have achieved a significant measure of success in this field have also been those who exerted the greatest influence on the development of new schools and trends. The whole tone of Soviet architecture was set by the intensive formal experimentation which followed immediately upon the Revolution. The innovators were well aware that specific problems of function and structure, and questions concerning the new aesthetic imagery, could not be answered without mastery of the new formal possibilities. The rejection of eclecticism and stylization clearly led to the abandonment of traditional architectural forms, but new formal and compositional methods to replace them were still only in the making. As a result, projects and buildings remained primitively unprofessional, since no amount of progressive social attitude and new artistic conception could overcome these architects' lack of formal and compositional resources. Of all the aspects of the design process – the structural and functional aspects, the compositional aspects and the problems of ultimate aesthetic image – it was the issue of architectural form which proved to be the weakest link in the chain. Its retarded development held back the whole evolution of architecture as an art. Two problems involving architectural form gave rise to special difficulties: the relationship of such form to the objective rules governing individual perception, on the one hand, and its relationship to the new functional and constructional bases of a building on theother. These were the problems which engrossed followers of the two main innovative trends in Soviet architecture in the 1920s: the Rationalists and the Constructivists. In practice, despite the doctrinal and artistic disputes that divided them, these two trends were engaged in a common task. But while the Rationalists approached form in terms of the objective laws of perception, the Constructivists approachedit through thestructural and functional aspects of building design: they were, so to speak, digging the same tunnel from opposite ends. The evolution of Rationalism was protracted and full of contradictions. At the time when the doctrinal and artistic principles of this trend gradually took shape in 1918–22, its adherents were much influenced by Zholtovsky's teachings, by Cubo-Futurism, Symbolist Romanticism, experimental Suprematism and the ventures of Constructivist painters, as well as by Kandinsky's psychological theories. Nikolai Ladovsky became the acknowledged aesthetic leader, the theoretician and organizer of this Rationalist trend. ## Ladovsky, the leader of Rationalism Apart from his role in the Rationalist movement, Ladovsky's name is also connected with the reform of artistic aspects of architectural education and with the development of an original town-planning concept. He attended a course of lectures by Zholtovsky immediately after the Revolution, at the same time as closely following current experiments in the figurative arts and establishing artistic contacts with Leftist painters and sculptors. A working group of like-minded artists gradually formed around him,first in Zhivskulptarkh in 1919–20, then from the end of 1920 in Inkhuk's Working Group for Objective Analysis, and thereafter, from 1921, in its Working Group of Architects, as well as in Obmas from 1920 to 1923. These were the people who in 1923 would form the Association of New Architects – Asnova. While still working in Zhivskulptarkh, Ladovsky was not only producing experimental projects but was already drawing up a set of theoretical tenets for Rationalism. He accorded great importance tomodern technology, but regarded its new materials and structural methods as more significant for what they could contribute to spatial organization than for their aesthetic potential. The Zhivskulptarkh architects saw the creation of a new, synthetic art based on architecture, painting and sculpture in terms of the struggle for an architecture that would employ to the full the latest achievements of technology, sculpture and colour, while rejecting stylization and eclecticism. In the course of the debates at Zhivskulptarkh during 1919, on ways of formulating the new image of a building, Ladovsky always stressed the role played in architecture by space. He pointed out that though form may be abstract and independent of all materials, its impact on the spectator was to some extent a function of the material employed. When Ladovsky underlined the significance of abstract form inaesthetic experiments, he also emphasized that it was engineering which could endow such new form with material substance. 'We must look,' he said, at the latest technological forms; they can be borrowed and reworked because they have not so far been developed in decorative respects.’ He believed that the look of a building should not be solely determined by its function,and that the task of design involved more than a mere external reflection of its internal purpose; it also involved the creation of an image. The new architecture, as he saw it, would be marked by the use of simple solid forms without superimposed decoration, and he therefore stressed the need to evolve methods whereby the geometrical characteristics of each solid could be highlighted by architectural means. Yet, in this context, Ladovsky did not merely call for the exclusive use of simple geometric compositions, but maintained that the architect should be able to stimulate the spectator's imagination through the conjunction of clearly intelligible forms. In his arguments against the early Constructivists, who stressed the primacy of structure and materials, Ladovsky claimed that the most important factor in the generation of form was space. In April 1921, the Working Group of Architects in Inkhuk, which was headed by Ladovsky and included Krinsky, Dokuchaev, Alexander Petrov and Alexander Efimov, put forward a programme which covered such basic principles of Rationalism as greater attention to problems of spaceand the psychology of perception. The basic factors governing the generation of form were listed in this programme in order, as Space – Form – Construction, a reflection of Ladovsky's belief that construction plays a subordinate part in this process. He stressed the priority of spatial considerations for thę architect over questions of artistic potential. The quest for a new artistic concept greatly concerned him. He used the latest building materials extensively in this connection, and accorded great importance to the pursuit of objective laws for composing architectural form. It was essential that there should be a scientific study of the psychological and physiological principles governing individual perception of architectural form, space and colour. Rational aesthetics and rational architecture – ratio-architecture', as he put it – were primarily the application of objective criteria, derived from scientific research, to questions of art in architectural design. In Ladovsky's view, Constructivists paid too much attention to the technological and functional efficiency of architectural form, while he and the Rationalists argued additionally for the need to elicit objective perceptual, psychological and physiological criteria. The task, as Ladovsky defined it, was to discover not merely emotional but also rational principles for the artistic aspects of architecture, so as to enable an architect to apply objective perceptual criteria when he designed abuilding. He called for research into new architectural principles and solutions, as well as a more direct individual involvement in this task. Creative imagination had a tremendous part to play in generating genuinely innovative architectural ideas, and Ladovsky therefore regarded it as a main responsibility of architectural schools to elicit and develop an understanding of space and volume among future architects. ## The psychoanalytical teaching method By 1920, when he was engaged in the reform of artistic aspects of the curriculum in the Architectural Faculty of Vkhutemas, Ladovsky had already worked outa new, so-called psychoanalytical method of teaching. He put forward a new system of teaching architectural composition, devised compositional tasks for the construction oflarge spatial forms and introduced the practice of making students prepare models for their compositional exercises. The object of the psychoanalytical method was to develop in students from their very earliest days at a higher educational establishment the spatial outlook required for architectural work. Ladovsky suggested that particular attention should be paid at the early stages of the students' education to the study of artistic compositional criteria based on actual psychological and physiological perception. They became familiar with the basic varieties of spatial forms, surface, volume, confined space (i.e. interiors), as well as with the means of architectural composition, such as proportion, rhythm, colour and dynamics. Part I/Aesthetic problems of design The students were set problems involving elucidation of the geometrical features of shapes; their physical and mechanical properties, such as mass and weight, mass and balance, etc; the identification of dynamism, rhythm, relationships and proportions, the description of volume and space, and so forth. The work set on each theme consisted of a twofold sequence,a first, abstract’ part, in the form of a compositional task, the second productional’ – the solution of the same compositional problem, but as part of the design of an actual object. In the tasks set by the course, the complexity of the spatial forms involved gradually increased, as did that of their environment and of their perception by an outside observer. In addition, more and more intricate compositional and functional demands were stated in the production’ element of each task. The main difference between the psychoanalytical method based on Ladovsky's set of principles, and traditional teaching lay in a different approach to the role of space and to problems of perception. In accordance with his views, Ladovsky taught his students that space was the basic material of architecture, rather than the structural elements. He also shifted the emphasis from the standard study of proportions and the hierarchy of architectural forms to criteria derived from individual perception. One of the features peculiar to this system was its insistence on problem-solving. Ladovsky did not cram his pupils, or needlessly intervene in the details of their design work, but invariably added some extremely complicated problem – or even a conundrum – to each set of abstract and production tasks. Regardless of actual content, the tasks he set his students radically changed their attitude to project work and kept them in a state of creative tension throughout the process of design. He knew how to draw out the very best in his students by setting them abstract and production problems which demanded for their solution the use of all the potential and talent at their disposal. His students have vivid memories of the brain-teasers he set, which nagged at them as, again and again, they went over the countless possible solutions. When outlining an abstract problem bearing on the determination of mass and weight, for instance, Ladovsky suggested that the solution must take account of the complex relationships between mass, weight and volume. In terms of architectural perception, weight was usually linked to size and also to mass, but only if thelatter was obvious, since mass was not always linked to such factors as size. Even such a purely preliminary analysis disclosed the full complexity of what had appeared straight forward at first sight. Many of Ladovsky’s abstract problems lay on the borderline between the compositional and the technological. Students tended to shun compositional solutions and preferred the easier, technological path. In problems concerning mass and weight, for example, they would seek to demonstrate the destruction of form by mass and weight. Ladovsky would sharply criticize such evasions and would demand instead the properly architectural spatial solution of a spatial task, rather than a mere illustration of the effect of physical forces. The solutions produced in Obmas often went to the limit of existing technology. But even though Ladovsky encouraged the quest for original, or even extreme spatial solutions, he was very strict in ensuring that these should always be in every way technically sound. The staff of Obmas included a consultant engineer, V. V. Kuzmin, who at Ladovsky's request carefully checked all designs and analysed them with the students from the technical and constructional point of view. Yet Ladovsky never insisted on simplification of form until all possible constructional solutions had been exhausted, and he himself often put forward solutions of great technical virtuosity. Ladovsky tested the full psychoanalytical method on several groups of selected Obmas students. However, when the Vkhutemas Basic Course achieved independent status as the Basic Section, headed by Krinsky with Balikhin, Turkus, Korzhev, Lamtsov, Glagolev and Spassky, the first graduates and students of Obmas, as his assistants, the psychoanalytic method became the basis of teaching in the Space’ discipline, which was common to all faculties. Although ‘Space' was taught on the basis of Ladovsky’s method,members of the Basic Section's staff, especially Viktor Balikhin, graduallyintroduced into it additions and corrections of their own. ## Krinsky's evolution As a leader of Rationalism,Vladimir Krinsky made a considerable contribution to its theory; he too was active in the theoretical discussions held in Zhivskulptarkh and Inkhuk. In 1919–20, Krinsky was still producing distinctive designs displaying great originality in spatial composition and full of restless dynamism. By the early 1920s, he gradually shed Cubo-Futurist influence in his formal aesthetic experiments. His compositions were now increasingly aimed at expressive spatial solutions achieved bythe greatest possible use of new building technology. Duringthis period he contributed to the formulation of a doctrine for Rationalism. In a lecture read in May 1921 to the Inkhuk Working Group of Architects on ‘The Path of Architecture’, he stressed the impact of rationality on the evolution of modern architecture, but also called for the independent study of architectural form. In December of that year he drew up a statement of his personal beliefs in which he developed these ideas. In1922–23,Krinsky designed a skyscraper to test this theoreticalconcept,and it is significant that successive sketches display increasing restraint in composition and shape. The dynamic emphasis in the silhouette of the building gradually faded, and diagonal members of thearmature were replaced by right-angles. This progressive rationalization of artistic means and methods is illustrated in the projects carried out by Krinsky during the 1920s and at the start of the 1930s: the Palace of Labour in Moscow, Arkos, the Soviet pavilion at the Paris Exhibition, the Lenin House of the People at Ivanovo-Voznesensk, the House of the Soviets at Bryansk, the Columbus Monument in Santo Domingo (the last three in collaboration with Alexei Rukhlyadev), the Moscow sports stadium and the Palace of Soviets in Moscow. These projects show that by the first half of the 1920s Krinsky had already rejected many of the methods characteristic ofhis earlier work. In his search for an effective architectural image, he boldlydisplayed ferro-concrete frameworks, contrasting juxtapositions of glazed and blind surfaces, colour and the symbolism of new emblems. At the end of the decade he tended to make increasing use of the centred and symmetrical compositional modes of Classicism, combined with a widespread application of modern types of construction and new architectural forms. ## Asnova The Rationalists set up their own organization – the Association of New Architęcts (Asnova) – in July 1923. The founders of this first innovative union of Soviet architects, derived from Zhivskulptarkh and the Working Group of Architects in Inkhuk and Obmas, were Ladovsky, Dokuchaev, Krinsky, Rukhlyadev, Efimov, Fidman, Balikhin and Mochalov, together with two engineers, Loleit and Kuzmin. Accordingto its statutes, Asnova's activities were confined to Moscow. Ladovsky was Asnova’s ideologist, its guide in matters of art, its organizer and leader, while Konstantin Melnikov became one of the most influential architects affiliated toit. It was the revival of the Moscow Architectural Society – Mao–founded before the Revolution and now gradually taking over the control of architectural competitions, which impelled the Rationalists to form an association of their own. As one might have expected, competition juries mostly consisted of Mao members who were often highly traditionalist. In 1922–23, Mao had been put in charge of the competition for a Moscow Palace of Labour, and the Rationalists, then mainly located in the Inkhuk Working Group of Architects and Obmas, regarded the jury as incapable of assessing innovative projects objectively and refused to enter the competition. Although a number of Rationalists, Krinsky and Lamtsov among them, had already done some sketches, the majority decided that no one should take part and everybody complied, except for Melnikov, who was not formally a Rationalist at that time. As a result, Rationalism, the strongest innovative trend of that period, remained wholly unrepresented in this competition, which was so important for the development of new approaches in Soviet architecture. Traditionalism was countered only by projects emanating from the various schools of Symbolist Romanticism, submitted by Ilya Golosov, Lyudvig, Belogrud and others, and the recently emerging Constructivists, represented by the Vesnin brothers. The task set out by Ladovsky for Asnova was to make a place in the community for new architecture and to establish a centre for creative experimentation in opposition to Mao. It was proposed gradually to attract all innovative architects to Asnova, to branch out into various fields of specialized architecture, to organize publications and to take over the competitions. Asnova held general meetings and organized sections for the design of housing and of transport buildings, for architectural theory, town planning and so on, which all went into action. Copy was also prepared for an Association periodical. Asnova, however, failed to become a single centre for the new architecture because of the way things developed in the Soviet Union. Rationalism proved to be only one of the more important innovative trends of that period. It largely took over the minor Symbolist Romanticist schools, but Constructivism, which was taking shape at about the same time, was in direct opposition to Rationalism in its approach to form-generation. The union set up in 1925 by the Constructivists – Osa – was joined by a number of Asnova members, and some others had left for the newly founded Vopra organization by the end of the 1920s. Difficulties arose over publicity for Rationalist concepts and for Asnova itself in the press. The Rationalists failed to publish their own periodical. When the Constructivists’ journal Sovremennaya arkhitektura (Contemporary Architectura) – SA – appeared in 1926, in which Rationalist work was not mentioned, Osa gained a clear advantage over Asnova in terms of publicity. Asnova material mainly appeared in the officially published periodicals Stroitelstvo Moskvy (Moscow Building) and Stroitelnaya promyshlennost (The Building Industry). Many Rationalist student projects produced in Vkhutemas were published in the school's own bulletin of 1927 entitled Arkhitektura Vkhutemasa (Vkhutemas Architecture). Lissitzky did much to disseminate the ideas of Asnova, both in the USSR and abroad, where he had been working when the Association was founded. He acted as its appointedexternal representative, kept in close touch with Ladovsky and published a number of Rationalist projects in ABC and other journals. When Lissitzky returned to Moscow, he and Ladovsky tried to arrange for the publication of a regular Asnova bulletin to be called Izvestiya Asnova (Asnova News), but succeeded only in bringing out a single issue, dated 1926. This, however, contains the most detailed statement available of Asnova's theoretical positions, above all in two articles by Ladovsky. The Rationalists pointed out the importance of objective psychological and physiological criteria in dealing with questions of formal aesthetics.They gave priority to the quest for a new architectural aesthetic, and for new kinds of dwellings and public buildings to celebrate the great and splendid changes taking place in Soviet society. The professional problems – of both theory and practice – discussed in Asnova documents and declarations were usually linked to newly arising social requirements. Asnova was a fairly small artistic organization numbering only thirty-five members, centred on Vkhutemas for purposes of collaboration among students and teachers, as well as for scientific debate. The Rationalists were the first to compete with the traditionalists for the support of the rising generation of architects, and were therefore more closely involved in teaching work than members of other innovative trends. Virtually the entire strength of Asnova was engaged in academic work at Vkhutemas. The Rationalists gradually extended their activities within it and involved recent Vkhutemas graduates as well. As a result, Rationalists set a general direction for Vkhutemas training in various ways, especially in the Basic Section, and gained the upper hand there. Despite its considerable influence on the development of the new architectural movement in the Soviet Union, the special circumstances prevailing in the debate about art during the years immediately following the Revolution led to Rationalism being underrepresented in practical work during the first half of the 1920s. The activities of Asnova members also contributed to this situation. They were mainly concerned at that time with problems of methodology and form-generation, with devising new types of buildings and a new concept for architecture. They could best formulate and explorethese questions in Vkhutemas, where Obmas' Curriculum Commission provided full scope for a variety of experiments. That is whysuch theoretical investigations were primarily built into the course, and the methods of generating forms were treatedas part of the solution of abstract’ and production’ tasks. Experiments connected with new types of buildings, new spatial composition and the development of a new architectural aesthetic were conducted with great gusto by Ladovsky in his Vkhutemas studio and involved several students working together on the same set subject. Themes thoroughly explored under his supervision included an administrative skyscraper, a new kind of workers' dwelling and new types of industrial buildings such as an aircraft factory. Such work continued in Ladovsky's studio throughout the second half of the 1920s. The resulting projects were therefore not merely training tasks, but also the experimental formulation of theoretical problems, and thus no less important for the development of Soviet architecture thanprojects intended for competition purposes or direct commissions. Indeed, major projects were often more broadly stated, more subtly tackled and provided with more interesting theoretical solutionsin such training tasks. When we later come to examine the various problems that faced Soviet architecture we shall therefore frequently refer back to projects carried out in Vkhutemas under Ladovsky’s guidance. ## The two centres of Rationalism around Ladovsky and Balikhin After the establishment of the Space’ discipline in the Basic Section, two theoretical, methodological and organizational groupings developed within Asnova, one of which included the teaching staff of the Basic Section, while the other comprised Ladovsky's students. Gradually the outlook of these two groups came to diverge. Viktor Balikhin acted as ideologist for the new Rationalist grouping. He regarded Ladovsky's attitude to problems of art as excessively rationalistic and, although he accepted his general principles, believed that his theory needed to be supplemented by resorting to art. Whereas Ladovsky put the objective geometric properties of space and form first in his teaching, Balikhin stressed their artistic features. Ladovsky proceeded from the principle of rationality in design and therefore concentrated on the organization of space and volume and on the form that manifested them, rather than on a composition’s purely artistic aspect. His purpose was to teach future architects to convey to an onlooker the message inherent in their designs. An architect must be capable of stressing or elucidating by architectural means whatever form he or she had in mind, or whatever features characterized the space he or she had organized. In other words, as Ladovsky saw it, the perceptual criteria of form andspace needed to be established and distinct means and methods found in order to enable an architect to assess such criteria and put them to good use. Balikhin, however, felt that Ladovsky ignored purely artistic considerations in his rationalistic approach. He therefore elaborated the Basic Section’s Space’course so as to introduce à degree of rationality into artistic methods of composition. A comparison of training tasks set in Obmas and the Basic Section on the subject of, say, Construction’ or Balance’, reveals the difference between the methods of Ladovsky and Balikhin. The abstract tasks carried out under Ladovsky's supervision invariably reflect the work and tension involved in each particular construction, whatever its outward expression, while those carried out in the Basic Section concentrate on outward balance, regardless of the actual characteristics of the construction concerned. At first sight, it might appear that Balikhin genuinely developed and imparted greater depth to Ladovsky's theory by enriching it with artistic refinements while preserving its rational approach. In practice, however, the situation was more complex. Ladovsky was a confirmed Rationalist both by intellectual inclination and in his approach to form. His Rationalism was concerned with perception rather than function and structure, as in the case of the Constructivists. Yet, very much like the latter, he did not confine artistic creativity merely to its rational aspect. The Constructivist leaders claimed that the functional structure of a building only provided a basis for its artistic composition, and Ladovsky similarly saw in perceptual criteria the objective foundation for artistic composition. Taking everything into account, Ladovsky recognized the existence of a clear dividing line in matters of art which could not be crossed by rational methods alone. In his ratio-architectural’ theory, he did his best not to impinge upon purely aestetic problems, while Balikhin, though he remained an equally dedicated Rationalist throughout, often crossed every such borderline. It is clear from Ladovsky's output and teaching that he was gifted with fine artistic intuition and was no mere Rationalist in matters of form. He consistently and strictly instilled in his pupils rational methods of formal development, but always knew when to pull up in time, before criteria that could not be formally stated came into play. In their personal contacts with him, his pupils sensed both the power of his unyielding logic in terms of form and the unlimited freedom of his artistic concepts. The secret of Ladovsky's teaching resided in his ability to induce this creative state of mind in a pupil. That is why almost all the Vkhutemas projects produced under his supervision stand out by the astonishing freedom of their spatial composition, combined with an unmistakably logical approach. And so two centres for theory and organization developed within Asnova in the mid-1920s. Ladovsky increasingly allowed the alternative centre in the Basic Section more scope, while Balikhin and the rest of the teaching staff continued to improve upon the psychoanalytical method. In 1927, Ladovsky created a psycho-technical scientific research laboratory in Vkhutein, the famousBlack Room' with walls, floor and ceiling painted black. Various experiments involving objective psycho-physiological criteria of perception and the impact of architectural forms were conducted there. Instruments for the measurement of visual perception, spatial representation, and so on, were produced to Ladovsky's specifications. In the course of this work, almost as though he was still arguing with Balikhin, Ladovsky stressed the scientific justification for those aspects of form-generation which he considered capable of rationalization, but stopped short when it came to artistic methods. The split in the Rationalist movement was formally recognized when Ladovsky and his followers left Asnova to set up a new organization, the Union of Architect-Planners – ARU – which will be dealt with in greater detail in connection with town-planning experiments. Asnova continued as a group of young teachers in the Basic Section led by Balikhin. Asnova’s history thus falls into three periods: the first, 1923–25, when it represented the only grouping of innovative architects andincluded the majority of young progressive architects; the second, 1926–28, when a number of members left it for Osa, while Asnova split into two centres headed by Ladovsky and Balikhin; the third, 1929–32, when one of these centres became an independent organization – ARU-in 1928, while some members joined Vopra in 1929. Apart from those enumerated earlier, the members of Asnova included, at various stages, Borisovsky, Budo, Bunin, Bykova, Varentsov, Volodko, Gelfeld, Zalesskaya, Karra, Korzhev, Kruglova, Krutikov, Vitaly Lavrov, Lamtsov, Melnikov, Myslin, Viktor Petrov, Valentin Popov, Prokhorova, Spassky, Silchenkov, Travin, Turkus and others. Asnova maintained contacts with innovative architects abroad such as Adolf Behne in Germany, Le Corbusier in France, Mart Stam in Holland, Lundberg Holm in America, Emil Roth in Switzerland, Karel Teige in Czechoslovakia, Ljubomir Micic in Yugoslavia and Muroyama in Japan Judging by the foreign press, European architects took a genuine interest in Asnova’s activities during the 1920s and, in particular, in projects carried out at Vkhutemas under Ladovsky's direction. ## Asnova teams The rejection of the individualism characteristic of academic studios in architectural faculties was mentioned in the earliest Rationalist Vkhutemas publications, under the slogan: Long live individuality, away with individualism!’ In this context, individualism’ primarily meant the subordination of students' opinions to the views of whoever was in charge of a studio and the resulting suppression of personal creativity. This was set against the ideal of a collective studio in which individualities combined and everyone was given a. generous opportunity to develop and display an artistic identity, while the leader merely acted as the senior member of the team. Asnova opted for collective work in modern architecture from the start, and the very first practical project undertaken by its members, the design for an international Red Stadium in Moscow in 1924, was a collective effort. Work on the stadium project (and then in more detail, within the Sport Structures Division) marked an important stage in Asnova's activity. It was used in competition entries by Obmas students, as well as in practical designs and schoolroom tasks, including diploma drawings. Moreover, the extensive assortment of building types involved made it possible to derive from it a number of problems bearing on experiments in formal aesthetics and new types of buildings, such as stadiums, dwellings, theatres etc. This first practical project undertaken by Asnova helped to establish many aspects of the collective method promoted by the Rationalists. The stadium project as a whole was directed by Ladovsky, but each participant had a task of his or her own in the overall sports complex. The second major Asnova project was to designand build the Shabolovka residential area in Moscow. When the Moscow City Soviet commissioned this work in 1927, a competition was held within Asnova, which picked Nikolai Travin's entry as the best. This became the basis for the actual development work, but other Asnova members contributed to the final design. Asnova was most active in the last phase of its history when the earliest Obmas students had graduated. At the turn of the decade, it became closely involved in the contract competitions which were then much in use. As soon as Asnova had secured a contract, it would first carry out an internal competition to elicit ideas. A design team would then be formed to represent Asnova in the open competition as the Asnova team’. But the project would be discussed, at various stages of drafting and before its final submission as a complete entry for the open competition, at Asnova’s general meetings. At the end of the 1920s and start of the 1930s, as professional groups – Mao, Asnova, Osa, ARU, Vopra – proliferated, projects were often entered in the name of such organizations. ARU used teams extensively to draft competition entries and other organizations followed suit. In Asnova, however, teamwork became an artistic principle and was regarded as a method of collective creativity appropriate to the new social conditions. The team method was also used to implement the concept of synthetic art put forward by Balikhin, in the combination of architectural work with the output of painters and sculptors at the early planning stage. Combined teams of sculptors and painters as well as architects were formed and treated as creative co-operatives of artists concerned with space, volume and colour. Such collaboration was typical of Vkhutein's Basic Section, to which the Association's centre of creativity shifted when Ladovsky left Asnova. Practically all important projects undertaken in the Association during its closing phase were carried out by teams. These projects, which had gone through a process of internal competition, followed by the approval of the Association as a whole, vividly illustrate the Rationalists’ orientation at the end of the 1920s. Much in them derives from early projects carried out in Vkhutemas by young Asnova members under Ladovsky's direction, but there are novel features, such as the use of certain favoured methods of spatial composition, the occasional absence of a clearly indicated centre of composition for the general design within the space allotted to it and an extensive synthesis with figurative art. The most interesting projects carried out by the Asnova teams, some of which are dealt with in later chapters, included in Moscow the House of Industry (1929–30), the All-Union Palace of the Arts (1930), the Proletarsky District Palace ofCulture (1930), the Palace of Soviets in the first-round competition of 1931, the School for the All-Union Central Executive Committee (Vtsik) in the Kremlin (1930), the Kharkov Massed Musical Performance Theatre (1930–31) and the Sverdlovsk Synthetic Theatre (1932). ![](images/1d597688772235da581e3e6fbeb86cff6b340c6d013dcd1295b59ff45cd1d49f.jpg) Constructivism, the second main trend in Soviet architecture during the 1920s, achieved maturity some years later than Rationalism. It reflected the requirements of the new architecture as real building work got under way again after the end of the Civil War. As an architectural movement, it was greatly influenced in its early stages by Constructivism in the wider sense, where it was a broad trend affecting early Soviet art as a whole, in book and exhibition design, theatre art, applied and decorative art, in posters, and so on. It emerged from the struggle to establish new forms of art and bring them into line to reflect the changes then taking place in society and the new production processes, and the development of fresh aesthetic ideals. As a phenomenon in Soviet art, Constructivism was the outcome of a unique synthesis of, on the one hand, Leftist experiments in painting, especially by artists searching for a way into architecture by imparting aesthetic significance to materials, textures and structural methods, and, on the other, the theories promoted in Production Art by Brik, Arvatov, Kushner and Gan, in particular. ## Constructivism and the theory of Production Art Although the theories of Constructivism and Production Art very often coincided, they were by no means identical. In 1921–24, when the development of Production Art theory was at its peak, its supporters regarded the early Constructivism of 1920–21 as no more than an early stage on the way from Veshchizm or the Culture of Things’. The evolution of Constructivism itself, however, was affected by many extraneous influences and in its fully developed form, during the second half of the 1920s, it drew on Production Art for a significant contribution to its own theory. Production Art ideas had been formulated after the establishment of Soviet power as part of an attempt by Leftist artists to acknowledge the role now devolving to art in society. One of the first such attempts, already mentioned earlier, was made in March 1918 in the Futurists’Gazette, when it published Decree No.1concerning the Democratization of Art'. In the same – and only – issue, Mayakovsky wrote in his Open Letter to Workers’: The twin fires of war and revolution have taken hold of our souls and our cities. The palaces of yesterday's pomp stand as burnt-out skeletons. The ruined cities await new builders. . . . To you who have accepted Russia’s inheritance, you (I have faith in it) who will tomorrow become masters of the world at large, I address one question: what fantastic structures will you use to cover the sites of yesterday's conflagrations? . . . Only the explosion of the Revolution of the Spirit will cleanse us of the tatters of bygone art. . . . The revolution of the content . . . is unthinkable without a revolution of form. . . ."1 In rejecting the old art that had served the ruling classes, Mayakovsky called upon painters to bring the new art within reach of the broad working masses. He adjured the workers not to be seduced by the old art's apparent beauty and to make the new art their own. Mayakovsky's poems and articles of this period not only embrace the struggle for the new art, but also display traces of Veshchizm and refer to the tasks of Agit Art and aspects of Production Art. As a great artist, Mayakovsky was at the centre of the web of complex and conflicting processes whereby the new art came into being, and he could not be identified with any single trend. He was, for instance, an equally authoritative figure both to Rationalists and to Constructivists, even though they were in bitter dispute with each other. Mayakovsky's Veshchizm, with its appeal for an art of everyday usefulness, represents a stage in the development of the theory of Production Art. He wrote in 1921, in his poem Order No.2 to the Army of the Arts’: We shuffle and we argue In search of mystic meaning While things put up a clamour: Endow us with new shapes!’² The next attempt to define a social role for the new art was made in the periodical Iskusstvo kommuny (Art of the Commune) between December 1918 and April 1919. Its editorials and articles by Brik, Kushner and Punin supplied the first identifiable tenets of Production Art. They suggested that the purpose of art was not to decorate life, but to shape it. The job of artists was to create objective reality from the things composing the environment, rather than to depict it. At this stage, however, Production Art was still in its first, Veshchist phase. In fact, the term veshch (thing) was introduced by Art of the Commune. The call to put art at the service of the broad working masses – to decorate labour’ – was thus followed, for social purposes, by the concept of the art of things’. The reality of art as such was not denied, but its role was to be confined to providing useful objects. Artists were to make’ things, and find their fulfilment in this activity. Very soon, between 1918 and 1921, the Veshchist view turned into a labour’ conception of art. Artists were to switch their creative endeavours to producing objects of use, rather than mere art. It was next proposed to alter the very nature of artistic activity:making'was to be replacedby producing'. What had originally been a demand for the conversion of an art object into a useful item – the Veshchist stage of Production Art – now developed into a labour' concept in which art equalled labour. It was not long before Art of the Commune required free creativity' to be merged with mechanical work' in a unified form of creative work'. The most articulate statement of Production Art's ‘labour'concept can be found in a collection of articles, Iskusstvo v proizvodstve (Art in the Production Process), No.1 (Moscow, 1921), in which Brik and others stated some fundamental Production Art principles, such as the denial of any substantive difference between art and any other form of labour, and the assertion that the generation of form was no more than the creation of a useful object. This view of things triumphed at Inkhuk in the autumn of 1921. An artist was now required to abandon his or hercalling and enter into production. Innovative architects, for their part, approved of the appeal for an art of the masses and thedecoration of labour'. They did not object to Veshchizm because – in any event – architects had always created useful things'. They merely saw this as an opportunity to inject greater democracy into architecture and to devote their attention equally to single, one-off’ projects and mass dwellings, communal buildings and industrial works. At the Veshchist stage, Rationalist architects, Ladovsky and Krinsky among them, successfully collaborated with future Productivists in Inkhuk, but as the theory of Production Art developed, their paths diverged. The Rationalists were not prepared to give up art in its own right and refused to accept the utilitarian making of a thing', such as a building, as the only valid reason for the generation of form. Significantly enough, the strictest promoters of Production Art – such as Brik, Arvatov and Kushner – were pure ideologists, not practitioners. They urged artists to go into production,but saw them primarily as organizers who would raise its cultural level, rather than active representatives of art. The term‘Constructivism’ was current before Production Art theory developed its strict orthodoxy, and was often thought to include theconcept of Veshchizm. This must be clearly borne in mind in order to understand how Lissitzky, a Constructivist when he left the Soviet Union in 1921, could represent the Rationalist Asnova organization abroad, but collaborate with the Constructivists in architecture and design when he returned to Moscow while nevertheless remaining a member of Asnova. Lissitzky took Constructivism abroad when it was still at the Veshchist stage for theoretical purposes. He could not, in fact, have adopted the strict Production Art doctrine, since he was himself a practising artist in the relatively traditional sense. The very title – Veshch – of the periodical which he founded in Berlin, together with Ilya Ehrenburg, makes this point clear. The leading article of the first issue in 1922 promoted the Veshchist concept or, more accurately, the concept of Production Art at the stage when Veshchizm was turning into early Constructivism. Early Constructivism developed as an independent trend, though not as yet in architecture, during 1921, and the bitterest controversy over it unfolded in Inkhuk. The Constructivist Working Group mentioned earlier was set up there in March 1921 with Alexei Gan, Konstantin Medunetsky, Alexander Rodchenko, Karl Ioganson, Vladimir and Georgy Stenberg, and Varvara Stepanova. Gan formulated the theoretical basis for the trend and later put forward his views in Konstruktivizm, a bookpublished in 1922. The programme of the Working Group was drafted by Gan and approved at its meeting on 1 April 1921, with the fundamental aim of promoting the Communist expression of material values. The declared intention was to pass from laboratory work to real activity and practical experimentation. The basic means of expression provided by the materials available were said to be tectonic methods, construction and facture that would bring the material elements of industrial culture into play'. In this context, tectonic method was regarded as dependent upon ‘the purposeful use ofindustrial materials', while construction was to be understood as the chosen function taken to its limit'. The Group proclaimed the incompatibility of artistic activity with the functional nature of intellectual labour',declared unrelenting war on art as a whole', and assertedthe unacceptability of artistic tradition in so far as the Communist form of Constructivist buildings is concerned’. The promoters of Constructivism and Production Art called upon artists who had rejected easel painting to undertake genuinely practical production work, They believed that the new art should, by means of production, foster the creation of a new material environment. They dreamed of a well-adjusted new human being surrounded by convenient things in a well-appointed city. They also contrasted objects in common use with artistic creations and equated the latter with the way of life of the former ruling classes. They thought that the proletariat would come to reject all useless’ things, including objects of art, and would only require whatever was useful. Boris Arvatov and other orthodox exponents of Production Art regarded Constructivism as no more than a transitional stage on the way to a new art which would, in accordance with the conceptions expressed in the journal Lef, become the art of living. Arvatov was probably the strictest among these theoreticians, and the ideologist of Production Art's final stage. Brik had been at the origin of this trend, and Gan was influential in formulating the theory of Constructivism, but – by developing the labour’ concept – Arvatov in fact took Production Art beyond artistic creativity and replaced it by the structuring of life’. Assessments of the part played in the formulation of architectural Constructivism by the theory of Production Art often fail to allow for the artistic aspect of architecture. In so far as architects were concerned, the appeals by the exponents of Production Art not to represent reality but to shape or construct a human environment instead, a world of relevant objects, did not in any way call upon them to abandon the essentials of their work. Architects have always devoted themselves to the creation of a human material environment, and they therefore regarded the Production Art demands as mainly a matter of social priority and orientation towards the mass consumer. As to the utilitarian view of form-generation, Constructivist architects saw in it little more than an additional argument to be used in the fight against the Classicists. If form is generated by function and structure,any elements of Classical order must certainly be discarded as antiquated – a very clear and useful point to score against the traditionalists, and therefore most popular with Constructivist architects. The analysis and assessment of any trend in art must obviously take account of the theories put forward by its supporters, since a movement's creative principles contain two inseparable ingredients: theoretical statements and the ideas embodied in the works which mark its successive stages of development. In such a context, statements of theory should be tested not only against the practice of their advocates, but equally in the light of counter-arguments put forward by their opponents. It is impossible to assess trends in Soviet architecture of the 1920s fully and accurately if one confines oneself to the logical and literal meaning of contemporary statements about principles, while disregarding their practical implications and the debates that surrounded these trends. One must grasp not only what was postulated in any particular set of theories, but what this actually meant in practice in each case. Thus, the Classicist line of argument should be borne in mind when one is confronted with the obdurate denial of art throughout the 1920s by a number of architectural Constructivist thinkers. Much that the Classicists claimed was correct, and it is difficult to understand nowadays why Rationalists and Constructivists should have reacted so sharply against the concepts they put forward. The Classicists were stressing the needto take account of tradition, the importance of continuity, of national traditions, the inappropriateness of transplanting architectural forms from the past direct into the contemporary scene, the need to adapt one's heritage creatively, instead of copying it. All this, so it would seem, should have been equally acceptable to the supporters of the new architecture. But the true meaning of such Classicist statements can only be understood by examiningthe underlying practice. When Zholtovsky's disciples, for instance, talked about a creative assimilation of the heritage, they apparently maintained that no direct copying of Classical models should be allowed and that all stylistic features must be modified, however slightly. Such a startling conclusion drawn from an apparently quite acceptable proposition led the Classicists’ opponents to distrust all their suggestions about the assimilation of tradition. As a safeguard against such suggestions, these opponents rejected all claims in favour of tradition, and some even denied all art from the past. This was undoubtedly a – mostly verbal – over-reaction, but it enabled the innovators to eschew hollow talk about questions of tradition. Only a knowledge of the realities of this debate about art explains why, for example, Ladovsky entirely excluded Classicism from his teaching, while sensitive artists such as Alexander Vesninand IvanLeonidov explicitly rejected the concept of art itself. Bearing this in mind, it also becomes easier to understand the link between Production Art theory and architectural Constructivism. The artistic nihilism prevalent among orthodox supporters of Production Art met with no objection in principle from thearchitectural Constuctivists, who, in practice, harnessed it to a rejection of inherited forms and an emphasis on the structurally functional aspect of a building in so far as its form was concerned. ## On the term ‘Constructivism' The term Constructivism’ has gained wide currency among art historians. It is, however, not altogether clear when and where it arose, what it originally meant and what art forms, including architecture, are covered by it. An attempt to answer each of these questions in turn follows, but without any claim to total accuracy. Mayakovsky wrote in his Paris’ essay: For the first time, an art term has winged its way from Russia, and not from France– Constructivism . . . , which regards the formal activityof the artist as merely a matter of engineering required for the design of our entire existence. It is from us that the French artists will have to learn. Here no clever stuff will prevail. The building of a new culture demands a cleared area. It requires an October broom.'3 Although Mayakovsky touches upon all three of our questions in this passage, it is worth considering his answer to the first of them – did the term Constructivism’ in fact reach Europe from Soviet Russia? In the periodical G, No.3 (1924), its editor, Hans Richter, wrote in an article entitled Concering Constructivism': 'The concept ofConstructivism arose in Russia. It is applied to an art that utilizes modern structural components instead of the usual materials, and pursues constructional aims.The concept of Constructivism was used in an extended sense by Doesburg, Lissitzky and myself at the Düsseldorf Congress in1920.’ When he used this quotation, Ivan Matsa correctly gave the date of the Congress as 29–31 May 1922.4 Alexei Gan wrote on several occasions that the first Constructivist group was formed in 1920.⁵ An information article in the architects' journal SA (No.2, 1926) also links this event, as does Gan, with the foundation of a Constructivist group in Inkhuk⁶ and adds that the Constructivists' programme, together with photographs of their works, had been passed for information to Lissitzky who was on the point of leaving for Germany and France. This date, however, also requires correction, since Gan had clearly forgotten that the first Constructivist group was set up in Inkhuk in March 1921, not 1920. Works by members of the group were shown in a separate room at the Obmokhu exhibition in Moscow that year,' and photographs of these works were published by Lissitzky in the Berlin periodical Veshch, Nos. 1–2 (1922), p.19. It can therefore safely be said that the term Constructivism' appeared in Europe only in 1922, brought there from Soviet Russia by Lissitzky. As to its first appearance at home, documents in the archive of the Inkhuk's Working Group of Constructivists indicate that it was in use at least by the beginning of 1921, although evidence still undiscovered may push this date back to 1920, or even 1919. The initial meaning of the term is important because art historians nowadays, consciously or unconsciously, overlook a social content which was inherent in the term from the start, and chiefly stress its purely formal aspect. The literal meaning of Constructivism carries a number of overtones – associations with technological constructions, with the structuring of a work of art (described in Inkhuk atthat time as a construction’), with engineering work in the construction process, and with the task of organizing – or constructing – a new environment. All these expressions were current in Soviet art early in the 1920s. The promoters of Production Art, for example, regarded the construction of a material environment as the most important activity of all, while those who saw Constructivism as a fashionable new style were primarily interested in its visual connection with that external bareness of technically oriented construction work which became, in some sort, the hallmark of Constructivism. Soviet architects never saw Constructivism as implying the primacy of construction, but regarded it first and foremost as a new method of design. As to the activities that the termConstructivism’ covered, apart from its usage abroad, it would be wrong, especially in so far as architecture is concerned, to impart a purely stylistic meaning to it in a Soviet context, or to link it literally with the denudation of a structure.Yet this is precisely what often happens, and foreign writers attribute all innovative Soviet works in the 1920s to Constructivism, or treat as Constructivist all new structural innovations. In the Soviet architecture of the 1920s, Constructivism represents an influential but by no means unique trend, with identifiable creative processes which are peculiar to it, including its own approach to the generation of form. ## Constructivist artists Production Art theory, which dominated Inkhuk from 1921 onwards, greatly influenced Constructivist principles in many branches of art: in cinema, graphics, the stage, literature and thenew-born art of design. A factual approach in literature, documentary shots in film, abandonment of painted stage sets, insistence on functional requirements in objects of everyday use, all these were part of the new system of aesthetics promoted by Constructivist artists. Their fundamental principles included the rejection of artistic conceits and decorations, and of past forms of art, indeed even of art as such. As noted earlier, the rejection of old-world art after the Revolution was connected with the prestige it had conferred on individuals in a class-ridden society, in which human values had been displaced by the value of things. Moreover, in the social climate of the times, the Constructivists contrasted things of everyday use with objects of art, which they regarded as luxury products involving wasted labour. Constructivist aesthetics took shape amid the complexities and contradictions prevalent as War Communism changed over to NEP, the New Economic Policy. An unrestrained burst of petit-bourgeois self-interest and the money-making tone set by traders further sharpened the conflict between the aesthetic ideals proper to the different layers in society. The workers set about showing up the profiteers by a deliberate restraint in dress, manner of living and arrangement of public establishments. Asceticism became the proletariat's ethical standard. Art soon followed suit, and artists conscientiously set about creating a material environment appropriate to these principles. Mayakovsky was writing poster captions, instead of lyrical poetry. Varvara Stepanova and Lyubov Popova were producing textile designs. Dziga Vertov deliberately filmed only documentaries. Grigory Miller, Stepanova, Rodchenko and Tatlin designed working, sports and everyday clothes. Meyerhold dressed his actors in overalls and replaced his sets by the machine'. Gan introduced new methods of book design and new typefaces so as to use the lettering itself to express the content of a text. Klutsis and the Stenbergs applied photomontage techniques to poster work. In architecture, as in other fields, simplicity of form was associated with democratic sentiment and represented the new human relationships. The denial of art and a marked utilitarian attitude towards form led to a generally ascetic approach to design. In addition it was entirely predictable, although Constructivist artists had not foreseen such an outcome, that the simple and severe forms which seemed to them strictly utilitarian and wholly devoid of ornament would soon be widely taken up as the outcome of a new stylistic trend. Restraint derived fromsocial and moral considerations thus came to bolster artistic experiments directed at the search for a new form. The Constructivist output most closely relevant to architectural interests was connected with the construction’ of all aspects of the material environment – in everyday life, at work, in public buildings, for transport purposes and in town planning – and with Agit or Agitational Art. The supporters of Production Art contrasted the creation of works of art with the construction of a material environment and, in doing so, often equated the production of objects with a structuring of everyday life. In its fully developed form, however, Production Art went beyond Veshchizm to herald the dissolution of object and form within the changing social environment. Thus Kushner linked the withering away of the object' with the progress of technology: the multi-purpose object was in the making and would adapt itself to actual needs, thus doing away with fixed shapes for particular objects. He believed that this evolution of the object would generate new ways of meeting requirements. Amaterial setting' would come into existence and provide a unified system of equipping accommodation so as to satisfy generalized social requirements and remain adaptable as these requirements varied in time and space.The material setting' represented an ideal material environment cleared of individual objects and their intractable shapes. This ideal inevitably influenced the planning of a material environment in the 1920s. It also prompted interest among Constructivist and Production artists in adaptable multi-purpose equipment. They designed – or constructed’ – products such as mobile market booths, sectional exhibition equipment, combined table-cum-divans, adaptable seat-beds for use in aircraft, coaches etc; standard collapsible furniture for use in cinemas and clubs, cupboard-beds, folding wall-beds, sectional bus shelters, folding wall brackets, sectional cupboards, mobile cinemas, and multi-purpose equipment for clubs, shops and other establishments. Gan, Lavinsky and others took a hand in designing such equipment, but the largest contribution toconstruction’ in this field was made by Rodchenko, who incorporated the techniques concerned into the training schedule of the Metalwork Faculty which he directed in Vkhutemas. Teaching at the Stroganov College and other pre-Revolutionary establishments had been based on the development of handicraft skills. As against this, the Constructivist Production artists working in the Vkhutemas Production Faculties introduced a fundamentally different method that relied on a rational approach and aimed to create, by the widest possible use of modern technology, things that were both useful and aesthetically valid. They fought for rationalization in the production of objects of everyday use and based their construction work' in this field on each object's function, component materials and make-up. The first specialists in what they termed the artistic construction’ of equipment graduated from Vkhutein towards the end of the 1920s. Rodchenko, Tatlin and Lissitzky were among their most important teachers. In addition to their involvement in the construction’ of everyday objects, the Constructivist artists were also active in Agit Art. They and the theoreticians of Production Art discerned a certain ‘utilitarian’ aspect in the need to stress ideological content and subordinate artistic formulation to agitational and propaganda purposes. They therefore included this in the list of functionally justified forms of creativity. Alexander Vesnin and his followers assimilated a great deal of Constructivist Production Art theory and practice and then applied this to architecture. They were determined in their opposition to the Rationalist Asnova supporters and at one with Constructivist artists in their fight for modern functional architecture, new objects of everyday use and the rationalization of artistic labour. They therefore also adopted their allies' chosen description of themselves as Constructivists'. Neither the artistic principles, nor the theoretical apparatus of architectural Constructivism could, however, be transplanted wholesale from another discipline and they evolved as part of a complex interaction between architecture and various other art forms and theories. The process whereby the fundamental beliefs of architectural Constructivism came to be formulated can be followed most clearly in the work of Alexander Vesnin, the leader of this movement. ## Alexander Vesnin, ## leader of the Constructivists Until the Revolution, Alexander Vesnin worked with his brothers Leonid and Viktor in a variety of architectural styles. He was also active as a painter and worked for a time in Tatlin's studio. He had looked to the past, both as a painter and an architect, although even before the Revolution his output displayed a tendency to enrich Classical traditions with the achievements of the new art and technology.Thus the ceiling which he painted in 1915 in the Sirotkin House, built in Nizhny-Novgorod by the three Vesnin brothers, hints at Cé- zanne's influence among its Renaissance subjects and shapes while designs such as that for the Konovalov factory produced in 1917 in collaboration with his brother Viktor show traces of Rationalist influence. In the early Soviet years Alexander Vesnin was mainly engaged in painting. He was interested in the formal aesthetic experiments of Leftist painting in which he sensed a great potential for the development of a new style. He turned out nonrepresentational works and showed these at a number of exhibitions. These paintings became, much as the Prouns had done for Lissitzky, not merely a matter of stylistic experiment, but a bridge of sorts linking the achievements of Leftist painting with the architecture of Constructivism. As a painter, Alexander Vesnin was influenced by a number of masters and trends during this period. He was drawn to Leftist art by the formal experiments in which the painters passed from colour to the organization of space. The experiments which interested Alexander Vesnin were not, however, those which sought a way into architecture through Counter-reliefs or spatial constructions, but rather those which linked painting and architecture by implanting the former into architectural space by means of architectonic compositions. This approach, adopted by Popova soon after the Revolution in a cycle of works which she described aspainterly architectonics’, attracted Alexander Vesnin to her output, in which he found a more organic interaction between the methods of Leftist painting and architecture. As a result, when working at Inkhuk, Vesnin joined neither the Working Group of Architects, nor the Constructivists, but became a member in May 1921 of the Working Group of Objectivists founded a month earlier by Alexander Drevin, Popova and Nadezhda Udaltsova. The programme drawn up by its founders stated that their aim was to create ‘substantial and concrete constructions, both in space and in a plane’, and to worknot on the representation of components, but on the creation of a concrete organism, both in space and ina plane’. Discussions about theory within the group bore on problems such as the definition and analysis of colour, the materialization of colour, the analysis of the elements of colour, substantiality, and the quality of space. Special attention was devoted to subjects connected with the notions of prodúction' in art and of facture'. Experiments in spatial construction were undertaken. The Objectivist programme reflected the approach to Leftist formal experiments underlying the teaching in two Vkhutemas studios, one headed by Drevin and Udaltsova, the other by Popova and Alexander Vesnin, who had taught painting and drawing there since 1921. Popova’s influence is clearly perceptible in Vesnin’s early non-representational work immediately after the Revolution. But in his paintings and graphic compositions he was also intent on taking in the widest possible range of those discoveries made by Leftist painting which pointed the way to architecture. He had produced a number of compositions in1917 containing square planes of different sizes set at various angles to the picture surface and foreshortened, sometimes in a very cursory way. These planes seemed to be floating in neutrally grey space. They partly overlapped each other and displayed a variety of colours, such as red, white and black. Solids appeared in later compositons, together with some – barely identifiable – pieces of real objects and fragments of writing. The elements of the composition shrank insize,while their number grew, so that they gradually more or less squeezedout the empty areas from the picture surface. These elements were no longer discrete geometric figures floating in space, but jagged fragments incrusted in one another and solidly carpeting the entire picture. These compositions date from 1921, but by the following years there are already indications of the complex process whereby Vesnin assimilated for architectonic purposes the achievements of Leftist painting. He had closely explored the latter and made many attempts to apply them in the actual architectural work available to him at this time, such as the simple geometric volumes of the Red Square speaker's platform in 1918 and the Kamerny Theatre stage decorations in 1920–22. He was now, at long last, clearly finding his way from Leftist painting into architecture. A growing tendency to organize the composition makes itself felt in works dating from 1922 as compared with those of the previous year. The component-elements have not increased in size, but a unifying, specifically graphic principle becomes apparent, even though the works as such remain wholly pictorial. The composition tends to be more closely structured, a square grid emerges from the medley of individual fragments and a large proportion of the flat elements combine to generate a kind of cellular framework. Depth is introduced and emphasized by the superimposition of another graphic grid forming a second framework closer to the spectator, but this time consisting of rods rather than planes. In Alexander Vesnin's graphic works dating from 1922 a tendency to introduce a rectangular framework is even more pronounced than in his paintings. These works represent a store of architectural compositions for use, specifically as façades, in future Constructivist buildings. This is significant: unlike Lissitzky's Prouns and Malevich’s Arkitectons, Constructivism came to architecture from non-representational painting by using a framework marked by a graphic rectangular grid, and not through simple geometric solids. These non-representational compositions by Vesnin, which vividly illustrate the aesthetic approach of early Constructivism, were contemporary with the formulation of early architectural Constructivist doctrine and the movement's first projects. Alexander Vesnin stated his personal creed at Inkhuk in April 1922 and further clarified a number of the points concerned in June of that year, as part of a course about the problems that face an artist. The early Constructivist theory was set out in these documents and the principles linking architectural Constructivism and Veshchizm, as well as Constructivist and Production Art, were clearly explained.Vesnin was at that time working in virtually all the fields where these ideas found an echo – the stage, painting, book production, public holiday decorations and architecture – and he set out his personal understanding of the concepts on which early architectural Constructivism was later based. He wrote that the artist should neither represent nor interpret existing objects, whether produced by nature or human hands, but should create things that were new; that every object should structure perception; that the order of an object was determined by the rapid tempo of modern life and its mathematically accurate rhythm, by its component materials andby its effectiveness; and that whatever an artist made should be constructive without anyvestige of representation. Work for the stage did much to shape Alexander Vesnin's outlook. The theatre acted as a sort of testing ground for architecture and design during the early years of Soviet power. The tremendous accumulated artistic potential could find no outlet in architecture at that time. Instead, it switched to the stage, where architectural compositions and objects of everyday use could be produced life-sized, rather than on paper or as scaleddown models. The brilliant successes of the Soviet theatre after the Revolution were not only due to the pioneering work of directors such as Meyerhold, Tairov andVakhtangov, but equally to the output of artists attracted to stage design at that time, among them Yakulov, Exter, Popova, Alexander Vesnin, Rodchenko, Stepanova, the Stenberg brothers and Viktor Shestakov.When Vesnin designed the setting for Claudel's L’Annonce faite à Marie at the Kamerny Theatre in 1920, he simplified medieval architecture to the limit and created a sort of Cubist Gothic. In 1922, the same approach led to a form of Neo-Classical Cubism in Phèdre. Constructivist designs and sets appeared in Moscow theatres during 1922–23, the most original and radically novel of these being Popova's Magnanimous Cuckold, directed by Meyerhold at the Actor's Theatre in 1922, and The World Upside Down, directed by him at the Theatre of the Revolution in 1923; Alexander Vesnin’s The Man who was Thursday, directed by Tairov at the Kamerny Theatre in 1923; Viktor Shestakov's Lyul Lake, directed by Meverhold at the Theatre of the Revolutionin 1923; and Exter's sketches for a production by Goleizovsky in 1922. There had been various Constructivist holiday decorations, speakers’ platforms and experimental structuresbefore this move into theatre work. They were, however, all produced by painters and, as a rule, lacked true architectonic quality and any awareness of the impact of such shapes on the appearances of a modern city. Alexander Vesnin was probably the first to sense the urban quality these structures demanded. In 1921, he and Popova designed an intricate set of open-air decorations on the Khodynskoe Field in honour of the Third Congress of the Comintern. These consisted of two symbolic cities, each of which stood for a social system. One of these – the citadel of capitalism’ – consisted of blind Cubist solids, while the other –the city of the future' – was composed of blind and openwork dynamic elements: wheels, transmission belts, pylons, booms, struts and cantilevers. In form, both compositions were close to the Symbolist Romanticist experiments of that time, and the openwork elements involved were primarily used for symbolic rather than architectonic purposes. The restless dynamism of this composition by Vesnin and Popova matched the actual movement of various parts such as the wheels. After completing this joint project, the artists, who jointly directed one of the painting studios in Vkhutemas, worked separately on two stage productions; Popova for Meyerhold with The Magnanimous Cuckold, and Vesnin for Tairov with The Man who was Thursday. Popova undoubtedly set to work on The Magnanimous Cuckold only after the Khodynskoe Field project had been completed, by the first half of 1921, and Vesnin might be thought to have started work on The Man who was Thursday after her, since the first performance of The Magnanimous Cuckold took place on 25 April 1922. But the gap in time was not as great as is sometimes thought: the first performance of The Man who was Thursday was scheduled for 1922, though the departure of the company on a tour abroad delayed the performance by a year. In other words, the designs for The Man who was Thursday embodyAlexander Vesnin's artistic experiments during 1922. They represent a link between the dynamic compositions of the two cities in the Khodynskoe Field project, which hark back to Symbolist Romanticism and Cubo-Futurism, and the competition projects, submitted together with his brothers in 1922–23, for a Palace of Labour in Moscow. The stage designs, of which many have survived, show that Vesnin was relying on the Khodynskoe Field project – rather than on The Magnanimous Cuckold, a rural mill setting for Constructivist purposes – as a starting point. He had created on stage the generalized outline of a modern town – an artistic embodiment of the town-planning concept – by using frame structures, girders, cantilevers, lifts, moving pavements, various technological elements, such as revolving wheels, etc. Dynamism dominated both his designs for the Khodynskoe Field decorationsand his early sketches for The Manwho was Thursday, both literally, as mobile components, and in compositional terms. As time went on, the frame structure gradually asserted itself and finally came to govern the conception of the setting as a whole. These designs are crucial in the formulation of aesthetic principles for architectural Constructivism. They show that early Constructivist ideas developed from openwork structures and mobile parts, thus combining the sense of space with that of movement. The elimination from stage designs of thematic and representational elements was then regarded as the principal Constructivist aim in the theatre. The construction was merely required to provide a machine tool’ – or setting, as the saying then went – for the actors. Despite this, the Constructivist designs immediately came to be regarded as a new style of theatre art.The sets were seen to represent the industrial mood of the new era, while the structures and mechanical devices on stage acquired an aesthetic significance. Constructivism had reached the theatre starting from laboratory work and unadorned buildings. Now, for thefirst time, it was embodied in large structures deliberately endowed with aesthetic content. Architects and artists built into the Constructivist stage designs of the early 1920s the images of the city of the future and of a new way of life. They also initiated the aesthetics of town planning and mechanization. The latest structures and everyday things acquired a new dimension and an aesthetic quality when they appeared on stage. By the time they returned to real life for building or production purposes they had become elements of a new artistic environment, because strictly utilitarian items of this kind were perceivedas part of the era’s aesthetic image, which was often far from what the artists themselves had intended. This increment of aesthetic quality through Constructivist work for the stage was particularly well illustrated by Alexander Vesnin's output. He was an architect and therefore created for the actors’ use not just a machine tool', but areal and complex architectural composition, a fact which establishes his designs for The Man who was Thursday as marking an important stage in the development of architectural Constructivism's aesthetic theory. Bare structural frameworks had been used earlier by some architects and in some small architectural items, but the aesthetic content with which they were now endowed here raised this method of construction to the level of a new system of composition. It was architectonically perfected in competition projects carried out in 1923–25 by Alexander Vesnin, jointly with his two brothers, Leonid and Viktor. The Vesnin team finally took shape in 1923 and in the course of the next decade the brothers became the acknowledged leaders of the innovative direction in Soviet architecture. The team's strength lay in the organic collaboration of talented, uniquely creative, but also thoroughly diverse architects. The brothers successfully complemented each other, not only by their various individual talents, but also by their different professional experiences. Alexander's quest for a new artistic form, Viktor's experience of industrial construction and Leonid's design work on housing all merged in the Vesnin team’s approach and during the years 1923–32 imparted a fresh quality to their output that was very unlike the earlier work carried out by each ofthe brothers separately. From 1923 onwards, the Vesnins generated a large number of projects, many of which became landmarks in Soviet architecture and influenced the outputof other architects. When they were engaged on combined work, they dealt jointly with all artistic problems. But each had his own special area in which his influence proved decisive because he had takenthe greatest trouble over it: Alexander was the authority on composition, Viktor on structural technology and Leonid on functional planning. Architectural Constructivism first made itselffelt as an independent creative movement in 1923 in the competition for the design of a Palace of Labour in Moscow. This competition – the largest since the October Revolution – in many ways determined the path that Soviet architecture was to follow. The majority of the entries – of which there were nearly fifty – offered a Palace of Labour on traditional monumental lines. (As we have already said, the Rationalists, headed by Ladovsky,refused to take part in the competition.) The Vesnins’ new-style project stood out among the rest by its truly modern appearance, its clear and rational plan and the daring use of the latest structural components and materials. The true road to creative experiment was opened for many architects by this Vesnin competition entry. The project proved outstandingly important for Soviet architecture, organically combining as it did a new building's social significance with a modern, technically functional approach and a different architectural image. The Vesnin Palace of Labour project represents a landmark in Soviet architecture as a whole, and the evolution of Constructivism in particular. It is therefore more than usually important to provide an objective assessment of each brother's individual contributions,not so much in terms of elaborating particular structures or providing functional solutions for particular building requirements, as with regard to the creation of a radically new architectural image. This approach suggests that, in the last resort, credit forthe design must go first to Alexander Vesnin, though it was executed with the assistance of his brothers. Alexander's experiments in 1921–22 led him directly to a new architectural image, and it is therefore not surprising that the surviving sketches for the Palace of Labour are from his hand. In fact, the spatialcomposition and architectural character of these Palace of Labour designs are closely related to Alexander Vesnin's previous output by the structural methods and stylistic features employed (as in the projects for the Khodynskoe Field decorations and The Man who was Thursday sets) and are quite unlike earlier works by Leonid and Viktor Vesnin. Leonid was a declared supporter of traditionalist tendencies at the start of the 1920s and taught in the academic studios of Vkhutemas. Viktor's work in the early post-Revolutionary years was closely related to Rationalist architectural concepts. The position achieved by the Vesnin team during the 1920s was undoubtedly the result of Alexander's influence on the general direction adopted by it. This influence was decisive early on, in 1923–25, and the Vesnin projects of this period can therefore legitimately be regarded as expressing, first and foremost, Alexander Vesnin's principles. This needs to be stressed because the three Vesnins are frequently treated as joint leaders of Constructivism, while no mention is made of Alexander’s special role as the actual figurehead. In fact, it is sometimes assumed that, since Alexander Vesnin was a painter rather than an architect immediately after the Revolution, the decisive role in the creation of architectural Constructivism should be attributed to his brothers rather than to him. In fact, given the circumstances prevailing during the early years of Soviet power when the torch was passing from Leftist painting to architecture, Alexander Vesnin's activity as an artist – painter, stage and graphic designer – proved a strength in relation to his brothers, rather than a weakness. In his case, the interaction between Leftist painting and architecture unfolded within one individual's output. Alexander Vesnin reached new architecture by way of stage design, a marginal area, rather than simply through Cubism and non-representational art. This enabled him to adapt the formal aesthetic methods of Leftist art for architectonic purposes in the course of his transition to architecture. The surviving sketches indicate that the best and artistically most brilliant Vesnin work – the Palace of Labour and the Leningradskaya Pravda building in particular – is in fact by Alexander Vesnin assisted by his brothers. Furthermore, a number of Vesnin projects which do not carry his signature were executed by his brothers in the light of his advice, which clinched the definitive visual solution. After the Palace of Labour competition, while Alexander Vesnin became the acknowledged leader of Constructivism, Moisei Ginzburg made a considerable contribution to the theory of this movement. ## Ginzburg's evolution towards Constructivism Moisei Ginzburg taught architectural composition at Vkhutemas and the Moscow Institute of Civil Engineers (formerly the Polytechnical Institute), and played an active part at the beginning of the 1920s in the artistic and social life of the architectural community. In his onslaughts on eclecticists and Rationalists about problems of form he stressed and expounded the technical arguments for the new architectural designs in leading articles published by the periodical Arkhitektura in 1923 and in his book Stil i epokha (Style and Epoch) in 1924. Alexander Vesnin established the basic principles of architectural Constructivism and Ginzburg's theoretical works dating from 1923–24 may be regarded as the next step in formulating a body of doctrine. They contained many statements of principles which became part of the basic theory of Soviet architectural Constructivism in its mature stage. In his article Estetika sovremennosti’(‘The aesthetics of the present time’) written in 1923, Ginzburg stressed the effect on architecture of the growth in production resources and progress in industry and technology. He chiefly looked for new architectural ideas in industrial and engineering structures because it was in these that architects had first abandoned traditional ornamentation and discovered an aesthetic value in its own right. Ginzburg, moreover, believed that the application of science and technology involved in the mechanization of life' was not merely a matter of new components, but implied the introduction of new and rational methods of design derived from construction engineering practice into architectural work. Ginzburg did his best to sum up the experiments carried out by Soviet architects during the early 1920s in Style and Epoch, and the theories which he advanced concerning the influence on stylistic development of the needs felt in the various layers of society are of outstanding importance. The book also illustrates Ginzburg's own progress towards Constructivism, a term the meaning of which he analyses as he goes along. As a historian of architecture, he was vividly aware of the important part that constructional considerations had always played inthe evolution of form. But he did not regard structure and decoration as mutually incompatible, despite the different roles they performed at various stages of the formulation and development of style. Ginzburg suggested in Style and Epoch that new architectural forms throughout history had usually been the product of changing structural or functional requirements and had been bare of decorative detail at the start. Such detail only appeared later, without at first disrupting the organic unity of form, but came to take over in time. ‘The youth of a new style is mainly a matter of construction, its maturity is organic and its decay decorative.’ According to Ginzburg, the keen interest in structure displayed by modern architects should be seen in this light: if emphasis on construction characterizes the early stage of development of every new style, this must apply all the more strongly to modern architecture. Ginzbúrg therefore saw Constructivism at the start of the 1920s as the inevitable first phase in the development of a new architecture. It was only in his later writings that the term Constructivism’ came to stand for a particular school. Ginzburg himself was as yet only feeling his way into a new architecture, as his entry for the Moscow Palace of Labour competition in 1922-23 clearly shows, with elements of streamlined Classicism, Symbolist Romanticist influences and recollections of American grain silos, a form which attracted him at that time. ## Constructivism embodied in architecture: the foundation of Osa, the Union of Contemporary Architects The innovative movements entered a new phase towards the mid-1920s when building work began in earnest throughout the country. Many questions of formal aesthetics came to be greatly clarified and the focus of experimentation in Soviet architecture gradually shifted to the design of buildings that were new in social terms. It had become clear by1923 that the theory and practice developed by the Rationalists and Constructivists had created a movement that grew and gained a foothold in Soviet architecture in its fight against eclecticism and traditionalism. It only gained the upper hand, however, in the mid-1920s, greatly helped in this by the competitions held in 1924–25 for building designs, such as the Moscow office of Leningradskaya Pravda (where entries by the Vesnins, Melnikov, Ilya Golosov, were particularly notable); the Arkos Joint-Stock Company (notably projects by the Vesnins, Melnikov, Ilya Golosov, Krinsky); the Soviet pavilion at the Paris exhibition (notably Melnikov, Ladovsky, Ginzburg and Ilya Golosov); the House of Textiles (Ilya Golosov and Ginzburg); the Moscow Central Telegraph Office (Shchusev, the Vesnins and Vegman); the Gospromyshlennost House in Kharkov (Serafimov, Felger and Kravets, Dmitriev), and a number of others. Two Vesnin projects of 1924 must be singled out for the influence they exerted on the evolution of Constructivism. The first was the Leningradskaya Pravda design, extensively discussed in the architectural press worldwide and generally acknowledged as one of the artistically most accomplished pieces of twentiethcentury architecture. This product of Alexander Vesnin's great talent brilliantly illustrated the vast aesthetic potential of modern architecture. Its well-balanced composition, the finely developed proportions of the elevations, the organic combination of ferro-concrete framework, glass surfaces and metal armatures, convey a sense of aesthetic perfection. #

The impact of these designs at a time of experiment in architecture was exceptionally powerful and immediately attracted attention on all sides. Eye-witnesses recall its effect even now, so powerful that by comparison with it all the other entries seemed to-shrivel and fade. The success of the innovative trend in Soviet architecture at these competitions, and of Constructivism above all, caused it to be followed by other architects who merely regarded it as a new fashion. A Constructivist manner might well have spread and turned into a purely external Constructive Stóle. The Constructivist leaders were aware of this and fought stylization by concentrating on the formulation of a set of artistic beliefs and the foundation of an organization uniting the new movement's supporters. The Unionof Contemporary Architects – Osa – was set up towards the end of 1925 with Alexander Vesnin as chairman, Moisei Ginzburg and Viktor Vesnin as deputychairmen, and Georgy Orlov as secretary.In their request for the registration of the new Union the representatives defined its principles as follows: Osa brings together individuals closely linked by a common ideology and carries on collective theoretical, scientific, techní- cal, and practical work in a struggle against inertia and the vestiges of the past. . . . Osa collectively determines and gives practical substance to the new architectural form functionally derived from the purpose of a particular building, the materials used in it, its construction and other production requirements in accordance with the concrete demands arising from the socialist construction of the country."8 As noted earlier, architectural Constructivism had initially been organizationally connected with Inkhuk. A group of likeminded individuals gradually gathered round Alexander Vesnin in 1923–24, mainly recruited from his students, such as Barshch, Andrei Burov, Sobolev, Komarova, and Nikolai Krasilnikov, in Vkhutemas. A number of prominent members of Lef (the Left Front of the Arts) directed by Mayakovsky, also took part in the group, notably Brik and Lavinsky. This architectural group was formally embodied within Inkhuk at the beginning of 1924, in fact replacing Ladovsky's WorkingGroup of Architects, the members of which had founded an independent creative association – Asnova – in 1923. In its controversies with the Rationalists, the new group had consistently drawn attention by 1924 to the role of a building's functional purpose as the basis for generating its form (e.g. Ladovsky's lecture in February 1924), thus providing a link with the promoters of Production Art. The Lef architectural group at Inkhuk thereby served as the nucleus for the Constructivist organization, Osa. Osa was joined by some of Ginzburg's followers from the Moscow Institute of Civil Engineers, e. g. Vegman and Vladimirov, a group of Viktor Vesnin's pupils from the Moscow Higher Technical Institute – MVTU – (among them Orlov, Nikolaev and Fisenko) and some Production Art Constructivists from Leningrad headed by Nikolsky. The membership of Osa included Alexander andViktor Vesnin, Barshch, Andrei Burov, Vegman, Vladimirov, Gan, Ginzburg, Ilya Golosov, Leonidov, Nikolaev, Nikolsky, Nikolai Krasilnikov, Orlov, Pasternak, Nikolai Sokolov, Khiger, Yalovkin and two engineers, Loleit and Karlsen. From 1926 onwards, Osa published its own periodical called Sovremennaya arkhitektura (Contemporary Architecture) or SA, with Alexander Vesnin and Ginzburg, the founders and leaders of the group, as its chief editors. ## The Functional Method The first issue of Contemporary Architecture (SA) carried the slogan Contemporary architecture must crystallize the new socialist way of life.’ The drive to participate in producing types of buildings intended for new social purposes – the social condensers of the era' – runs through all Constructivist theory and practice. The movement's basic theories and methods were set out in its periodical. In the early days of Constructivism at the beginning of the 1920s, its followers mainly stressed the constructional effectiveness of the new architectural form. At this later stage, on the other hand, they concentrated on its functional quality, one of the main bulwarks against the threat of a stereotyped Constructive Style. Their doctrinal statements constantly emphasized that Constructivism was an artistic method, not a style, and that it demanded a specific attitude with regard to questions of construction, function and form. SA proclaimed: Architect, do not counterfeit technological shapes, but master the constructor's methods instead.' In a series of articles about Constructivist theory, Ginzburg set out the principles of a so-called Functional Method which was then further elaborated and given wide currency. Function, in so far as the Constructivists were concerned, must be given priority in the preparation of a spatial composition for a building. The Functional Method proceeded from the functional requirements of a given structure, such as the siting ofindividual units of accommodation in relation to each other, the flow chart of the activity in question, and so on. Constructivists made great use of the so-called pavilion method’ of composition, whereby a building or complex was divided into blocks or units of space in accordance with their purpose. These were then linked by corridors or bridges in accordance with the requirements of the overall functional process involved. The Functional Method was in fact a fully developed artistic programme. It confronted the architect with a host of challenges, such as taking part in the invention of new types of buildings; combating eclecticism; fighting for the industrialization of the building process, for standardization, the industrial manufacture of components and the conversion of building work into a process of assembling standard components. The Constructivists promoted the utmost use of scientific methods in the process of architectural composition and, as a result, sometimes oversimplified the complex interaction between the function of a building and its spatial organization. They also tended to exaggerate the importance of purely rational considerations in the creative architectural process, probably inevitably at a time when the latest technological achievements were being applied to building work. The Constructivists made great use in planning and spatial compositions of new inventions from applied science in fields such as thermal insulation and acoustics, many of which were introduced into design and building work at that time. Attempts were even made to quantify project work itself scientifically, so that the selection of a particular plan and spatial composition would largely be governed by the application of mathematical formulae, tables and graphs as proposed by Nikolai Krasilnikov. Generally speaking, however, when dealing with form in functional terms, the Constructivists were most concerned with a structure's social function rather than its utilitarian aspect,though the very simplicity of the new architectural forms justified them in a sense for functionally structural purposes. Utility was no longer burdened by aesthetic considerations and the visual concept was derived from the structure's social context. For the Constructivists, the architect's basic task was to organize a new way of life. In so far as they were concerned, technology was the means of generating a new and functional form. Functionalist leaders, such as Vesnin and Ginzburg, believed that there was a qualitative difference between a merely utilitarian form and a functional form that had achieved aesthetic perfection and become an object of art. The application of the Functional Method did not, in so far as they were concerned, imply that only one single form could serve a given function and construction. They set great store by the artist's imaginative power and bythe special effects of perception. Osa’s first exhibition was held in 1927, its first conferencę in April 1928 and its first congress in May 1929. ## Engineers and the new architecture The creative principles of innovative trends such as Rationalism and Constructivism were well understood by engineers and constructors bent on applying the latest technological achievements. Great engineers such as Shukhov, Loleit and Krasin, were happy to work with the innovative architects. They jointly explored new ways of developing architecture, and engineers became active members of Asnova and Osa. The switch at the start of the 1930s to an intensive application of methods derived from the past is often primarily explained by the inability of the country's building industry to meet the technical requirements set by the standards of architecture prevailing during the 1920s. It would,of course, be wrong to deny that the Soviet authorities had inherited a low level of building technique from Tsarist Russia. It was no lower, however, than that obtaining in many other areas of the national economy which equalled performance elsewhere in the world during the era of the First Five Year Plans. Moreover, as pointed out earlier, Russia at the turn of the century did not lag far, if at all, behind the developed countries of Europe and America in the number, standard and originality ofits building and engineering works. There were therefore no good reasons for Soviet building work to develop more slowly than other branches of socialist industry. And if building technology was seriously retarded in the Soviet Union during the thirties, forties and fifties, this was due in large measure to the orientation of architecture during that period, which did not always encourage the introduction into building work of the latest improvements in technology. In the twenties, on the other hand, Soviet architecture set tasks for the building industry which prompted the employment of new materials and structural elements, and raised building standards. Engineering technology in the building industry achieved great successes during this period, such as the metal structures by Shukhov and Krasin, Loleit's work in the field of ferro-concrete, the elaboration of modern timber work by Karlsen and the production of new hyperbolic paraboloid roofing by Makarova.The very latest structures and building materials, as well as modern production methods, were applied in the construction of engineering and industrial buildings, such as Shukhov's radio tower in Moscow in 1922 and Krasin's viaduct at the Shatura power station in 1925. Engineers not only helped the architects to elaborate and implement their ideas in technical terms, but influenced their formal experiments, both directly and indirectly. Thus, the pronounced skeletal look of early Constructivist compositions is closely related to the latticed metal structures which gained widespread currency even before the Revolution. It is also worth stressing the influence of the quantitative theory taught in higher architectural and constructional educational establishments on the forms developed in the new architecture. This led to a maximum application of modern technology in many student projects dating from the 1920s and lent additional point to the formal experiments which they embodied. ![](images/1bafa68a4a4430bd519a290feb9198fc03617d1a232da2739a571d4d0470d256.jpg) Creative innovation in the second half of the 1920s: teachers, schools, groupings Canonization: a threat to the formal tenets of the new trend By the mid-1920s, the innovative trends came to exert a decisive influence on creative experimentation in Soviet architęcture. The next stage involved the reconciliation of uncompromising attitudes in the quarrels betweenRationalists and Constructivists. In the second half of the decade the main task was to achieve an organic synthesis of all that had been secured so far by theConstructivist Functional Method and Rationalist experiments in formal aesthetics. The Functional Method undoubtedly consolidated what had been done in the field of spatial composition,but its strictly rational approach to functional construction did not in itself point the way to successful composition. Innovative architects who were struggling against traditionalism during the 1920s relied on the achievements of science and technology, and constantly stressed the importance of a rational approach to architectural work. This led them to tip the scales in favour of rational factors in architectural design, as opposed to emotional ones. It also increasingly directed attention to the functional justification of a structure, often at the expense of artistic considerations, particularly in Constructivist designs. The technological factors introduced into a design by the architect were essentially rational, and tended very much to displace the emotional or intuitive aspects involved in the work. Inevitable as this would seem to have been under the circumstances, it involved a danger that this transient phenomenon might become a permanent feature of the new architecture. Conservatism, as many innovators saw it, was chiefly a matter of drawing on the past for architectural forms and methods. These architects were not always clearly aware as they fought against eclecticism and Classicist stylization that an equally serious threat to every new movement fromwithin was a conservative tendency to erect its own means and methods into canons. Such tendencies increasingly made themselves felt in the new architecture, not least because long years of eclecticism and stylization had conditioned architects in favour of canons that fetter and limit creative imagination. Orthodox Constructivists were also sometimes hampered in this respect by a simplistic view of how shape and the basic functional structure of a building were interrelated. Complex asymmetrical compositions designed to satisfy the functional connection between various structural components proved to lack artistic unity. Moreover, stylization in the Constructive manner could mean that individual elements of the new architecture would be canonized and become merely external and formal features of buildings, imparting to them a stylistic stamp of approval. An outdated architectural concept and a lack of compositional ideas were often cloaked by a fashionable covering of glass and concrete. Stylistic clichés apart, progress for modern architecture involved the solution of a host of problems by organic means, in particular by imparting expression to buildings. ## Ilya Golosov's Constructive Style Golosov, as we have already pointed out, never based his work on any single style and worked with more or less equal success in a variety of idioms while remaining faithful to his own personal methods of spatial composition. The Vesnins' brilliant entries for the Arkos and Leningradskaya Pravda competitions impressed him deeply and converted him to the methods developed by the Constructivists in search of an artistic image. A clearly accented framework and a prevalence of glazed surfaces became characteristic features of Golosov's output after 1925. He turned out a series of artistically first-rate projects after joining the Constructivists and did much to spread an understanding of this trend. Golosov was the most successful interpreter of Constructivist methods in the mid-1920s. He was certainly uniquely popular as a Constructivist architect in the years that separated theVesnins' triumphal success from the flowering of Ivan Leonidov's work at theend of the decade. And yet, although he did brilliant work in the Constructivist manner, Golosov was never a true Constructivist when all is said and done. A close scrutiny of his output reveals that he treated Constructivism more as an outward trend than as a functional method. He nevertheless rose as a star in the Constructivist firmament and for a time outshone even such leaders of the movement as Alexander Vesnin and Ginzburg. Golosov's rise as a Constructivist started with the award of a first prize in the competition for a House of Textiles in Moscow in 1925. Thereafter, he took part in virtually all the competitions whichwere proliferating at that time andcarried away what was probably a record number of prizes. He seemed insatiable in the quest for diversity in his work. He easily and freely combined ultra-modern Constructivist forms, boldly introduced a conjunction of contrasting volumes, such as a cylinder and a parallelepiped, with planes composed of glass and blind walls, and confidently modelled is façades. He displayed great mastery in demonstrating the artistic potential of Constructivism and made exquisite use of the means and methods at its disposal, still fairly limited at that time. In 1925–27, when many architects were switching to Constructivism, Golosov even came to be regarded briefly as something of an artistic leader. Constructivism was becoming fashionable and many new converts began to work in a sort of Constructive' style. Golosov probably contributed a good deal to this development. Careful inspectionof Golosov's Constructivist projects, however, especially if one traces his evolution during that period, clearly shows that he was not primarily concerned with the externalsof style, then as at all other times. In practice, the Constructive Style does not play a leading part in any of his projects dating from these years in so far as the appearance of the buildings is concerned. It is true that all projects by Golosov dating from the second half of the 1920s are outwardly typical of Constructivism. But even here the spatial composition is invariably based on a large complex form which harks back to the methods of spatial organization elaborated by Golosov in his theory of the structure of architectural organisms. His principle that thebasic architectural image must be a large spatial form which retains its power of expression even when some of its details are replaced by others, continued to dominate his output evenduring his Constructivist period. Golosov was attracted to the outward stylistic opportunities offered byConstructivism, such as the generously glazed frameworks. This is particularly characteristic of 1925–26 when Golosov designed a series of exquisitely proportioned office building projects, with beautifully traced frameworks and windows. Most outstanding in this respect were the competition entries for the House of Textiles in 1925, jointly with Boris Ulinich, the Rusgertorg in 1926 and the Elektrobank in 1926. Golosov took special trouble in drawing these façades and designing frameworks and openings, but his idiosyncrasies showed nevertheless: he could not simply leave the façades plain, or put up with the wall as a theme’, even though it was, to all appearances, an ultra-modern piece of glazed framework. Eachof these projects also exhibited a touch of plasticity – a combination of right-angled prisms in the House of Textiles, a cylinder of glass at the angle of the Elektrobank and a halfcylinder in the Rusgertorg. Golosov soon exhausted the possibilities offered by glazed frameworks and increasingly reverted, towards the end of the 1920s, to the large spatial elements which he always saw as the basis for outwardly expressive buildings. ## Symbolist and Expressionist responses to engineering structures – Lyudvig and Chernikhov The Constructive Style and its ever more canonical aesthetic modes earnedpopularity and wide-ranging application, but also stimulated a search for ways of dislodging the aesthetic stereotypes connected with it. During the second half of the 1920s, the quest for variety in the approach to architectural composition developed not only within the innovative trend itself, but also at the interface between the innovative trends – Constructivism and Rationalism – and Symbolist Romanticism, as well as Classicism. As to the Symbolists, one should bear in mind those architects who set out to integrate engineering techniques into Symbolist and Expressionist compositions in order to resolve functional, structural and technological problems. One of the most original of these was Genrikh Lyudvig. He had been trained as an engineer as well as an architect, and seemed to combine a strictly scientific, engineering designer’s approach with that of an artist inspired by the dynamic forms born of revolutionary symbolism. Classicism marked his student work in 1920–21 and he came to new architecture by way of both formal symbolism and the extensive practice of building technology and applied science, a sort of Symbolist Functionalism’ grounded in the achievements of science and technology. This can already be seen in his first major work, an entry for the Moscow Palace of Labour competition in 1922–23, in which Symbolist shapes combine with others, derived from the technological purpose of the buildings: a spiralling tower of traditional Symbolist design, together with a ferro-concrete cantilevered pad answering contemporary engineering requirements on to which aircraft could be lowered in front of their hangars by means of a special crane-lift. The acoustics of the main hall have been very closely studied, with the speaker's platform as their natural focus, and with facilities for acoustically tuning the accommodation as a whole. In 1924, Lyudvig submitted a design for the Soviet embassy in Ankara built up from simplified Neo-Classicist forms. He also submitted a design (which was not carried out) for an Afghan embassy in Turkey, based on a saw-toothed plan. In 1926, he provided Kemal Pasha with a project for the Turkish President's summer palace at Čeflik, consisting of a combination of offset rectangular apartments diagonally intersected by corridors and galleries, with a large number of terraces under a vertical dominant consisting of a mooring tower for dirigibles. The originality of Lyudvig's talent is best exemplified by designs in which the symbolic shapes which he favoured, such as the spiral and the five-pointed star, are combined with a novel functional and technological solution embodying some characteristic engineering concept. The designs for a Columbus Monument in Santo Domingo in 1929 and for the Palace of Soviets in Moscow in 193l–32 are typical of his competition entries. True to his convictions, Lyudvig covered the engineering aspect of the Columbus Monument, as well as the symbolism of this memorial complex. He lined the shore with a green vegetative acoustic barrier, its width calculated according to a formula, together with rows of trees, the height of which was governed by a graph. For the first round of the Palace of Sovietscompetition, Lyudvig designed a pentagonal building surmounted by a stepped pyramid. For the second round, he proposed a gently tapering cylinder on a pentagonal foundation and, wound around it, a spiral car ramp for tourist access to the viewing platform on the roof and ease of maintenance of the outer walls while the building was in use. Lyudvig's brilliant command of engineering considerations in construction and architecture is demonstrated by two projects for platform shelters in leisure parks. The first is a temporary guyed structure with wooden uprights, wire struts and a tarpaulin cover, which was erected in the Moscow Central Park for Culture and Leisure in 1929. The second was intended to cover a stadium holding an audience of 6,000 for stage and musical performances. The structure was based on a ferro-concrete ring, one side of which was supported at the bottom by the foundation of the building and at the top by the segmented cone of metal girders and glass forming the side of the amphitheatre. A circular screen enclosed in the ferro-concrete ring reflected the sound towards the amphitheatre. The work of Yakov Chernikhov exemplifies another approach to the use of technology in order to impart outward expressiveness to buildings. He was well versed in matters of engineering, had perfect command of both perspective drawing and architectural draftsmanship, and created a large number ofarchitectural fantasies' during the second half of the 1920s. These fantasies' display an original amalgam of Symbolist Romanticism, Rationalism and Constructivism. Many of them reveal an inventive talent, but, despite superficial differences between them, the vast majority are no more than variations on familiar themes. There are few original discoveries here or any great attempt at the higher reaches ofform-generation, such as characterize the output of Melnikov and Leonidov, for example. ## Zholtovsky's Harmonized Constructivism Overtly traditionalist concepts could no longer hold their own against innovations when the new movement came to dominate Soviet architecture in the second half of the 1920s. As a result, many traditionalists rallied to the new architecture. Even such stalwart Classicists as Zholtovsky and Fomin came to experiment on the frontier between innovation and Classicism. As we have noted earlier, although they both harked back to the Classical tradition, Zholtovsky regarded proportionand harmony as the most important elements in Classical composition, whileFomin laid stress on the individual idiom of the Classical order involved. This determined the difference between the means and methods they employed when they attempted to find a place for their work in the new architecture, starting from a Classicist position, In the second half of the1920s, when Zholtovsky returned from Italy, where he had lived during 1924–25, he again tried to found a Neo-Renaissance school by gathering about him a group of talented young architects. Its mainstay was provided by Georgy Golts, Mikhail Parusnikov, Ivan Sobolev and Sergei Kozhin, described in architectural circles of that period as Zholtovsky's Quadriga’. The first two had completed their training under Zholtovsky at the start of the 1920s, while the others had graduated in Vkhutemas from Alexander Vesnin's studio and had therefore been trained in the spirit ofConstructivism. Given the complex circumstances prevailing at that time, Zholtovsky fought on two fronts againsts innovative trends. He produced basic models incorporating many compositional methods and forms derived from the Italian Renaissance and, at the same time, took up the new architecture with the intention of artistically harmonizing its utilitarian forms. He used the Renaissance compositions in the design of public buildings – the Makhachkala House of Soviets, the Gosbank (State Bank) building in Moscow – and the new forms for industrial purposes – the Ivanteevka factory, the power station boiler houses for the Moges, Kievges and the Dneproges competition projects. Zholtovsky came to set great store by industrial architecture. He could see that changing aesthetic criteria had made his Renaissance designs uncompetitive and that his skill would be best and most convincingly displayed within the latest manifestations of new architecture. Asa result, a peculiar trend, a sort of Harmonized Constructivism’, emerged on the borderline between Neo-Renaissance and Constructivist formal experiments during the 1920s. The industrial structures designed by Zholtovsky's group were characterized by an aesthetically refined composition of façades. The Renaissance wall became a model for a carefully proportioned and strictly symmetrical façade. In his polemics against new architectural concepts, Zholtovsky defended the leading part plaved by the façade in the creation ofan architectural image against innovators who increasingly rejected it as a central element for architectural purposes, not only in industrial design, but in other types of construction as well. He reserved the central place in any composition to the front elevation. Glazing was widely used to provide a neutral background, while the structural framework was only picked out when this was required in order to articulate the composition, or as a stylistic component. The Neo-Renaissance school certainly injected a potent cultural element intoindustrial architecture.The buildings conceived by Zholtovsky and his pupils stand out to advantage among the many only too drearily utilitarian industrial structures of those times. This fact in itself did much to establish the authority of Zholtovsky's school among young architects. The rising generation's close interest in Zholtovsky's ventures into industrial architecture was also due in part to the threat of canonization which hung over the innovative forms and methods described earlier, and the resulting disillusionment with the Constructive Style. ## Fomin's Proletarian Classicism Ivan Fomin's determination to combine Classicist and new architectural concepts led him to undertake yet another attempt in this direction. We have already seen how he had tried to adapt the Classical order to early Soviet circumstances by picking out the most heroic’ and elementary forms of what he described as Red Doric. By the mid-1920s, he was switching from the selection of Classical forms to their simplification, a process which he described as the reconstruction of the Classical order. A system of composition gradually emerged in his projects and became the basis of his new conception, of Proletarian Classicism’. He superimposed a Classical order on the armature of a façade which was largely glazed and without concealing the framework imparted a Classical look to it. By 1928, Fomin's Proletarian Classicism had become a full-blown artistic system backed by an elaborate theoretical justification. Fomin believed that a modern style should be at once international and democratic. He suggested that such a style should be based on a severely simplified version of a Classical order, standardized and stripped of all ornament. In other words, he proposed that only the framework of a Classical order should be preserved. He regarded it as essential, for instance, that the window area should be extended so that the whole surface between the supports – or columns – of the building's framework was glazed. All versions of an order should be simplified to the limit. Complex cornices should be replaced by simple lintels, all fascia, moulding, capitals and plinths should be suppressed and tapering columns replaced by simple cylinders. Fomin used this system in his design for the Dynamo building in Moscow, in which he linked six of the storeys by paired columns, while the seventh acted as a sort of entablature pierced by rounded windows. Fomin regarded paired columns, a basic component of Proletarian Classicism, as among his personal artistic creations. These allowed the design of a single multistorey building to be unified by means of a single order, thereby creating the effect of one monumental pile. The new Mossoviet (Moscow Soviet) building, erected in 1929–30, was regarded by Fomin himself as the most successful embodiment of his Proletarian Classicist concept. The attractively proportioned paired columns enclosed square windows and were topped by a plinth surmounted by an attic storey pierced by low rectangular windows. Fomin used Proletarian Classicism in a series of projects dating from the early 1930s, such as the Moscow Workers' Faculty, the Ivanovo-Voznesensk Polytechnical Institute and theNKPS (People's Commissariat for Transport) in Moscow. In justifying his choice of Classicism as the basis for a new style, he pointed not only to the universal acceptance it commanded, but equally to the regularity and discipline inherent in the compositions derived from it. He sought in thereconstructed’ order a means of providing both variety and continuity in a way that would not conflict with new structural technology. Fomin strove to impart a renewed sense of architectural relevance to the tectonic approach of Classicism by combining an order based on it with the use of ferro-concrete, the proportions involved having been adjusted to the efficient use of this new material. As he saw it, far from conflicting with the principles of a Classical order, a ferro-concrete framework would open up new potentialities for this age-old architectural system. Proletarian Classicism was akin to the Neo-Classical experiments then being carried out by, among others, Auguste Perret in France, Gunnar Asplund in Sweden and Marcello Piacentini in Italy. Two main movements concerned with form developed in Europe early in the twentieth century as a reaction against eclecticism and the Moderne: a Classical revival and a new functionally constructive approach to architectural design. The Neo-Classical variant of this trend – to which Fomin's school, among others, belonged –strove to achieve both the rationalizing aesthetics of Classicism and a rational use of new materials. This was no mere Classical revival, but a determination to make a ferro-concrete framework serve the working principles implicit in a Classical order. ## A New stage in design It was, however, notGolosov's Constructive Style, Chernikhov's Symbolism and Expressionism, Zholtovsky's Harmonized Constructivism or Fomin's Proletarian Classicismthat impelled the new architecture on to a higher level in the second half of the 1920s. An influx of fundamentally new ideas was now required, not least in matters of form. Yet all the experimentation described so far during the late 1920s, and much else besides, was either a matter of developing and perfecting what had been devised so far or of injecting means of expression derived from symbolism, or the art of the past, into the new methods and forms. The new architecture had been launched by artists, such as Malevich and Tatlin, and architects, such as Ladovsky and Alexander Vesnin, whose creative concepts and artistic experiments were marked by originality of mind and true innovation. Their successors merely developed their ideas within the scope of such talents as they possessed, a respectable and necessary activity. There is nevertheless a fundamental difference between original talent and a mind, however brilliant, which develops and interprets what another has discovered. As the signs that the formal methods of the new architecture were being erected into canons became ever more threatening, masters capable of discovering fundamentally new ideas and creative methods, fresh artistic thought and innovation were needed, capable of imparting to architectural concepts uniquely personal and pristine qualities. Two outstanding personalities, KonstantinMelnikov and Ivan Leonidov, made major contributions to the innovative forays of Soviet architects in the second half of the 1920s.Their output displays broadly original artistic concepts. These two architects were both capable of deep emotional response, but they bowed to the requirement for rationalization, in terms both of the functionally structural conception of a building and of its architecturally aesthetic image. Their experiments in formal aesthetics implied a wish to discard the established stereotype of what a building should look like. ## Melnikov, a master of expressive composition Konstantin Melnikov was an outstanding master of expressive architectural form and one of the most inventive architects of 521–22 Ilya Golosov. He designed the Makhorka pavilion at the 1923 Moscow Agricultural and Handicraft Exhibition as an original spatial composition, with a blind, overhanging upper structure, an open winding staircase, a vertical glazed shaft and a roof with a sharp diagonal slope. He developed this new approach to the design of exhibition buildings even further in the Soviet pavilion at the Paris International Exhibition in 1925, the first and immediately triumphantlysuccessful appearance on the world stage of the new-born Soviet architecture.This pavilion stood out among the rest by its modern appearance – simple geometric forms, bold use of colour, glazed timber framework and an unusual external staircase cutting diagonally across a two-storey building laid out on a rectangular plan. During the late 1920s and early 1930s, Melnikov produced a whole series of original designs which helped to extend the scope of experimental work and intensified polemics about form. Projects by Melnikov such as the Rusakov Club, his own residence, the Columbus Monument and the Palace of Nations demonstrated the possibilities of creating expressive spatial compositions by such means as overhanging elements, unusual forms of symmetry – such as symmetry about a horizontal axis, a generous use of diagonals etc. Melnikov's merit during this complex period in the development of Soviet architecture was that he got rid of many conventions through his innovativeprojects and helped to remove psychological barriers hindering the acceptance of new forms. He attached great importance to the functional approach and to achieving the most rational organization possible in any composition. But his main concern in connection with every building was to devise an appropriately expressive appearance for it, an artistic image that would incontrovertibly link its spatialorganization with a novel approach to its interior design. All Melnikov's designs are marked by virtuosity in the treatment of their interiors. What is more, the means of spatial organization are never repeated and remain entirely original in each individual case. Melnikov boldly extended the range of means and methods of expression in modern architecture and set great store by novelty in an architect's output. Ashe put it,Creativity begins at the point where one can say: “This is mine."" This attitude to the production of original forms and images is evident throughout his work. His spatial compositions were striking by their novelty. But Melnikov never distorted a geometric form. Cylinders, cones, parallelepipeds, rhomboids and other elementary volumes freely combine, intersect, interpenetrate and are articulated with each other in a variety of ways while retaining their clearly defined geometrical identity. The hallmark of Melnikov's architectural forms is their inner tension, rather than any showy dynamism, emphatic structure or excessively obvious balance. There are no idle elements in his compositions: everything is thoroughly resilient, but no display is made of the great inner strength held in reserve. In so far as he wasconcerned, architectural form should be like a tensed muscle, and this accounts for the overhanging components which he favoured, because they supplied the most loadbearing shape in visual terms. ## Leonidov, a poet of pure form During the second half of the 1920s, Soviet architects not only successfully fought off the threatened canonization of stylistic features, but made a major contribution to the solution ofa series of problems affecting the treatment of form and a new approach to spatial organization in the architecture of their period. One of the most outstanding creative personalities involved in these developments was Ivan Leonidov, an exceptionally gifted artist, inventor and original thinker. Le Corbusier called him a poet, and the hope of Russian Architectural Constructivism’. Leonidov's graduation project for a Lenin Institute, drawn up in Alexander Vesnin's Vkhutemas studio, was shown at the first Moscow Exhibition of Contemporary Architecture in 1927. Alexander Vesnin saw in it the birth of a new architecture, and it was also highly commended by Ladovsky and Ginzburg. The project provided for a large building complex on the Lenin hills comprising a library, a scientific research institute, an auditorium and other supplementary accommodation. The treatment of the large auditorium was unusual and was regarded at that time as breaking new ground. It consisted of a vast sphere raised above ground level on an openwork metal armature; the top half of the sphere was glazed over, while the lower part housed the auditorium. The whole structure was flanked by a vertical parallelepiped, anelement in the same spatial composition, intended for a book store. In 1928–30, Leonidov became the effective leader of Constructivism. He single-handedly represented this movement in a series of outstandingly important competitions and matched the entries submitted by entire teams working on behalfof various other organizations. During this period he produced a whole series of competition entries which stood out by the depth of their conception, their technical mastery and their graphic presentation, among them the Tsentrosoyuz (Central Union of Consumer Co-operatives) building in 1928, the House of Industry in1929–30 and the Palace of Culture in 1930, all for Moscow, and the Socialist Settlement attached to the Magnitogorsk Industrial Combine in 1930. These projects tackled in an original way problems such as the siting of a cultural and social centre in the construction of a new town, the lay-out of a modern city complex and the relationship between areas of housing and their environment. The principles of spatial organization in modern buildings worked out by Leonidov were to exert great influence on the further output of Soviet architects. He was among the first to sense, as early as the 1920s, the basic tendency of modern architectural design towards simplicity in the treatment of volume, which only secured widespread recognition during the 1940s and 1950s. He demonstrated in his projects that an extremely laconic use of geometrical volumes devoid of decoration was no less expressive in its effect than more elaborate compositions. Such utter restraint in the organization of space was truly innovative in the 1920s, at a time when many architects were still exploring the expressive potentialities of modern architecture by bringing greater complexity into compositions. ## Suprematist Constructivism – Nikolsky and Khidekel Other means of overcoming the stylistic stereotypes which had established themselves were adopted by the leader of the Constructivists in Leningrad, Alexander Nikolsky, and Lazar Khidekel, a pupil of Malevich. Nikolsky discarded Classicism soon after the Revolution, then came under the influence of the Cubo-Futurist methods employed by the followers of Symbolist Romanticism. Early in the 1920s, he produced a number of projects inspired by Cubo-Futurism, often oddly combined with themes derived from ancient Russian architecture. By the mid-1920s, however, his experiments took a different stylistic turn. The designs then produced in Nikolsky's studio sought to create expressive compositions by means of large rectangular horizontal and vertical volumes contrastingly juxtaposed, with a generous use of colour, blind surfaces, either strictly sómmetrical or sharply asymmetrical compositions, and violent contrasts of light and shade, at the expense of intersecting forms, overhanging elements and any extensive use of ledges. The originality and polemical nature of Nikolsky's formal aesthetic experiments is largely accounted for by the situation that had arisen in the architectural world of Leningrad. In altering the city's appearance during the early post-Revolutionary years, the destructive’ Cubo-Futurist experiments had shaken Classicism to its roots, but failed to destroy its compositional basis. By the mid-1920s, the new architecture was consolidating its position with every passing day, but had to cut through the canons imposed by the Classical order. Leningrad architects gradually succeeded in sloughing off traditional forms and ornaments, but they retained many ingrained habits – symmetry, for instance – and preferred the use of straightforward geometric volumes in the external design of buildings to Constructivist means of expression, such as exposed frameworks and large areas of glazing. The experimental designs of Malevich's group – the Arkitectons and the Planits – contributed to this approach after the move to Petrograd from Vitebsk in 1922. Malevich’s experiments with form, in which his students – Suetin, Khidekel and Chashnik – shared, were well known in Petrograd, where they had greatly influenced decorative art and architecture. After the Unovis move, Lazar Khidekel began to study architecture, and was thus able to inject Suprematist ideas into it from within, rather than indirectly, as in Nikolsky's case. Khidekel's first projects go back to 1922–24, although he did not begin to concentrate on architecture until the middle of the decade. His Suprematist forms gained in realism as his interest in architecture increased and his compositions, such as his Aero-Club designs in 1922, are the first originating from this school to include door and window openings in what had previously been blind geometric volumes, therebyimparting a measure of architectural actuality to Suprematist projects. Khidekel studied in Nikolsky's studio from the mid-1920s onwards and the increasingly Suprematist Constructivist flavour of its output may well have been partly due to his influence. It was in fact Khidekel who produced the first fully developed architectural project – a Workers' Club – in a Suprematist spirit in 1926, a design which already reflected all the basic features of Suprematist Constructivism. He went on to produce a whole series of designs in the later 1920s and early 1930s based on the formal aesthetic discoveries of the Suprematists. Suprematist Constructivism was an original approach to form and style in composition which left some mark on many innovative ventures by Leningrad architects in the 1920s. At the same time, however, Classicist influences were also more significant in the output of Leningrad architects than in that of their Moscow colleagues. This was most obvious in the competition entries submitted from either side in the mid-1920s. The asymmetrical composition, glazing and bare frameworks of the Central Telegraph Office and House ofTextiles in entries originating from Moscow contrasted with the symmetry of compositions incorporating simple geometrical shapes in designs submitted from Leningrad, as in those for a House of Culture in the city's Moscow-Narva district, and for the Gosprom (State Industry) building in Kharkov. Such differences between the creative concepts prevalent in these two cities led to the application of conflicting criteria in the assessment of entries for competitions organized by the two local architectural associations. A split developed among these architects, so that many of those in Leningrad refused to enter competitions launched in Moscow, and vice versa. As a result, the work ofNikolsky's group became fundamentally important because it helped to consolidate the process of creative experimentation by Soviet architects wherever they might be. It provided a synthesis of the artistic experimentation carried out in the three main Soviet artistic laboratories – in Moscow, Petrograd and Vitebsk – though Nikolsky rejected theincreasingly stereotyped features identifying the various schools. This enabled him and the members of his studio to design truly original projects for clubs, schools, baths and stadiums. Nevertheless, even though it had assimilated the ideas of Moscow-based Constructivism, the Suprematist Constructivism which Nikolsky’s group adopted was closer in terms of design to Malevich’s experiments and Khidekel's architectural projects. ## The graduates of MVTU Pre-Revolutionary Rational Architecture, as we have already said, did not provide a basis for an innovative trend immediately after the Revolution. It would, however, be wrong to deny it any role in the evolution of Soviet architecture. In the 1920s, interaction between the tradition of Rational Architecture and Constructivism led to the formation of a school of industrial architecture, the representatives of which made a significant contribution to resolving problems not only in the lay-out of production and engineering structures, but also in their artistic formulation. An architectural section was set up in the Moscow Higher Technical Institute – MVTU – in 1918, which had by the mid-1920s trained a group of talented architects under the guidance of such distinguished representatives of Rational Architecture as Alexander Kuznetsov, as well as Viktor and Leonid Vesnin. The teachers of architecture at MVTU differed from their opposite numbers in Vkhutemas by a more practical approach to the problems in hand and this largely determined their graduates’ subsequent choice of work in the field of industrial and housing construction, as well as structural design. Nevertheless, the training of the MVTU architectural students was, Rational Architecture apart, influenced by the contemporary mood in Soviet art as a whole. A Creative Group gradually formed among these students. They arranged ‘Wednesday' fixtures at which reports were read and information provided concerning architecture, the visual arts and poetry or new stage productions by Meyerhold and Tairov. These Wednesdays’ began in 1922–23 and carried on until 1928, even after the members of the Creative Group had graduated. Among those who took part in them were Vladimir and Gennady Movchan, Nikolaev, Kalish, Fisenko, Meilman, Evgeny Popov and Sergei Turgenev. These fixtures helped to shape the outlook onart of the young architects and to found a school, mainly in the field of industrial architecture. The clearest examples of this school's orientation can be seen in projects carried out in Moscow by MVTU graduates, such as the complexes of buildings for the Central Institute of Aero- and Hydrodynamics, Tsagi, and the All-Union Electro-Technical Institute, Vei; the laboratories of the Moscow Textile Institute and the headquarters building of the All-Union Electro-Technical Association. These buildings combine functional and structural efficiency with the application of new architectural methods, such as the geometrically accurate articulation of volumes, contrasts between glazed and blind walls, and the juxtaposition of rectangular and semicircular elements. ## Shchusev and the adoption of new architectural principles The innovative trend achieved a dominant position in Soviet architecture during the second half of the 1920s and the early 1930s. One after another, architects belonging to an earlier generation, who immediately after the Revolution had seen the way forward in the greatest possible reliance on traditions, began to change sides. The evolution of Alexei Shchusev's output is a case in point. He was not a strict Classicist and found the picturesqueness and rich plasticity ofancient Russian architecture more attractive. He freely combined its forms with the new types of structures and the Moderne, which he found more tolerable than Classicism. Shchusev was an uncommonly gifted artist, whose enthusiasm was readily captured by new styles. If some architectural conception attracted him even though it conflicted with his own at that moment, or if he considered that it answered present requirements better, he was apt to switch to it. This degree of adaptability took him to the verge of eclecticism, but also enabled him to appreciate innovation more broadly and objectively than many other architects of the older generation. It was due to his support, for instance, that Melnikov's Makhorka pavilion was included in the 1923 exhibition and, unlike Zholtovsky, he did not consider the Vesnins' Palace of Labour project unacceptable. The evolution of Shchusev's approach in the 1920s is clearly illustrated by the process of designing and building the Lenin Mausoleum in Moscow's Red Square. Shchusev's first design for a temporary wooden mausoleum provided a cubic base on which it was proposed to erect a monument consisting of four columns carrying an entablature. Tight construction schedules and building difficulties prevented the project from being fully carried out and the first mausoleum remained unfinished, with only the base completed. In the course of designing the second wooden mausoleum, Shchusev attempted to return to his initial idea by crowning a stepped base withan extended colonnade, either circular or rectangular in plan. But the spare forms of the unfinished first mausoleum fitted well into the surroundings of Red Square and the very fact of their presence influenced all further design. As sketch followed sketch, the colonnade gradually shrank until it ultimately became the uppermost tier of the stepped design. Stylistically, the second wooden mausoleum was treated in the Classical manner for compositional and formal purposes, with columns, pilasters, consoles, and so on. Between 1924, when one wooden mausoleum followed the other, and 1929, when the stone one was designed, Shchusev's artistic outlook completely altered. This change followed the success of the Vesnin Arkos project. Shchusev allied himself with the innovative movement and produced a competition entry for the Moscow Central Telegraph Office in its spirit. This new approach equally affected the design and construction of the stone mausoleum in 1929–30. He was set the task of perpetuating the composition of the wooden one, which had become a familiar feature of Red Square, and of translating it into stone. Nonetheless he completely discarded the traditional architectural elements incorporated in the previous monument and concentrated on spatial composition, so as to stress the simplicity of the geometric forms employed, the proportions of the composition and the relationship between the colours involved. Many other older architects, such as Vladimir Shchuko and VladimirGelfreikh, who had previously worked with traditional forms, similarly went over to the innovative movement during the second half of the 1920s. ## A unified new trend The existence of a variety of trends in the first stage of Soviet architectural development, each with its own set of clearly formulated tenets, helped to impart shape and cohesion to the innovative movement. By the end of the 1920s, however, these individual groups gradually lost their significance as rallying points for the innovators, though not before they had played an important part in the struggle against eclecticism, the shaping of a new artistic orientation and the establishment of an identifiably Soviet architecture. By the beginning of the First Five Year Plan, in 1928–32, objective circumstances favoured joint action by all the innovators at a time when the construction of new towns and settlements, as well as industrial premises, dwellings and public buildings,was being carried outon a grandiose scale. The existence of different associations was beginning to hamper united action by Soviet architects. One sign of the times, among others, was the abandonment of the fundamental ideological struggle between Rationalists and Constructivists, who all turned to the solution of the most important practical problems thrown up by industrialization. As we have already noted, Ladovsky left Asnova in 1928 with some of his followers and founded the Union of Architect-Planners, ARU, whose members set out to solve the theoretical and practical problems involved in socialist town planning. In the same year, Ginzburg also left his theoretical researches in Osa to head a group of architects in the Typology Section of the Committee for Construction of the RSFSR (Russian Republic), investigating in depth the design of new types of inexpensive dwellings. Early in 1929, the Presidium of Osa, which comprised Alexander Vesnin, Ginzburg, Leonidov, Khiger and Orlov, suggested that individual architectural associations should be merged in a single Federation of Revolutionary Architects. Not only did this prove impossible, but a further independent grouping was founded in the same year –the All-Union Proletarian Architects'Association,Vopra, which numbered among its members Alabyan, Baburov,Vlasov, Zaslavsky, Ivanov, Kochar, Alexander Krestin, Kryukov, Mazmanyan, Matsa, Mikhailov, Mordvinov, Nikolai Polyakov and Simbirtsev. The part taken in competitions at the turn of the decade by representatives of the various groups brought an element of creative rivalry into their debates. Each organization followed the example of Asnova and ARU in setting up a special team for each major competition, althoughtheir various entries hardly diverged from the general stylistic orientation of the innovative movement. There was also a good deal of cross-fertilization between the groups concerned. The closeness of the views held by these groups became apparent whenever they combined to resist traditionalist trends. Thus a joint protest by Osa, Asnova, ARU and Vopra was organized against the decision to build the Lenin Library on Classical lines. A joint meeting of architectural associations to discuss participation in the design for a Palace of Soviets resolved on a united front in opposition to conservative tendencies. All this contributed to the consolidation of artistic and organizational attitudes among innovative architects. ## Growing controversy within the new trend Despite the gathering momentum of the movement for creative unity among innovative trends, dissensions within the new Soviet architecture actually sharpened, largely owing to the activity of Vopra members. The creation of this new group, at a moment when objective circumstances already pointed the way to a common programme uniting all progressive Soviet architects, greatly complicated the situation and converted polemics about art into internecine warfare.In the initial statement of their organization's aims, and a number of articles by Alexei Mikhailov and Arkady Mordvinov among others, the supporters of Vopra claimed the sole right of their group to speak for Proletarian Architecture and classified all other creative trends as openly bourgeois', fellow-travelling’, and so on. They promoted a vulgarized version of Marxist-Leninist teaching about the role of art in social life, adopted cliquishness as a guiding principle and set about expelling from the world of socialist art all who were not of a like mind with them. They used rabblerousing methods of criticism under the pretence of fighting for Proletarian Architecture. Many influential members of Vopra conducted a group warfare of sorts: they replaced intellectual leadership by crude administrative procedures, put forward inflated assessments of the – professionallyinadequate – output of Vopramembers and conducted destructive criticism of work by membersof other organizations. Architects such as Leonidov, Melnikov, Ladovsky and Krutikov, whose work best displayed signs of experimental activity, took the full brunt of attacks from the Vopra side. Leonidov was one of the earliest targets of what became a genuine persecution. The expression Leonidovshchina –
