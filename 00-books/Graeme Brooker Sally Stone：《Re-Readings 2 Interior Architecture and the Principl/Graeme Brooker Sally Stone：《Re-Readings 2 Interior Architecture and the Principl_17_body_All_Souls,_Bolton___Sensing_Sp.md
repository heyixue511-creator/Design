The design of the elements of an insertion always generates the tricky problem of the language that the designer should adopt. The decision about whether to deliberately contrast the new with the old, that is to create distinction and difference, or to purposefully generate form and detail that has a similarity or a direct connection and continuity with the host building has to be considered. This second option does not necessarily mean resorting to pastiche, but can entail using the clues about form, rhythm, materials or scale that have been

generated by the existing building to inform the design of the new elements.

This is the problem that the architects OMI were faced with when they were asked to convert a disused church into a community centre. The adaptation of a religious building can pose delicate questions. The architect or designer has to signal the new use, while also paying respect to the original building. A church is often a very dominant building in an area, and frequently has a dramatic or symbolic

interior. The architect or designer has to fi nd a way for the conversion to tacitly connect with the new occupiers without overpowering the original intentions of the structure. This is especially true if the structure is in a very ethnically mixed area, and overt Christian imagery may be inappropriate.

The approach that OMI took to the conversion of the Grade II listed Gothic Revival church by Paley and Austin was very defi nitely one of generating variation and difference. They chose to not only contrast the language of the new elements of the community centre, but also to subtly break the ordering geometry of the interior.

Bolton, a large town in the northwest of England, was once a highly affl uent area in the heart of the cotton-weaving district. It is close to Manchester and was part of the huge industrial swath that crossed this part of the country. As with most post-industrial towns and cities, the population has changed, and thus the church has lost its congregation.

All Souls is an immense church capable of holding a congregation of 800, and, at 16 metres wide, has the largest single span nave of any parish church in England. It was built at the junction of two terraced streets, and although the local street confi guration has changed over the church’s 130-year existence, it is still a dominant landmark in the area. OMI continued this streetscape into the building by constructing a series of pods or small structures that actively celebrate the continuation of the road through the front door and into the nave. This was achieved by placing the asymmetrical objects at either side of the great hall, thus leaving a path or visual route through the centre of the building. This echoes the organisation of the original church, but the irregular shape of

the pods and the vertical circulation breaks this order. Only the bridges interrupt the length of the church and the views of the immense timbered ceiling.

Major historic repair works were carried out to the church, including the replacement of the Westmoreland slate roofs, brickwork and stone repair and replacement of most of the leaded glass windows to the nave. The insertion has been pulled back from the carefully conserved fabric of the building. This has established a new relationship between the building and the visitor. Where once the building had to be observed only from ground level, the occupiers can now appreciate the intricate beauty from a much more elevated position from within the new pods. The architects were presented with a very diffi cult problem with the actual construction of the new structures. All of the materials and the machinery of construction had to be brought in and out through the church’s two metre wide west doorway. # Sensing Spaces

Location   
Royal Academy,   
London

Date 2014

Designer Grafton Architects

![](images/77c05a3e193889a9e4156c769b3914d4b87ece65266b86c149cd9b59a348aac7.jpg)

Figure 2.17 Natural daylight is fi ltered through visually heavy boxes hung from the ceiling.

Sensing Spaces: Architecture Reimagined invited seven architectural practices to create site-specifi c installations that were inserted into the main galleries and front courtyard of the Royal Academy in London.

The exhibition was designed to explore the most fundamental elements of architectural space. The curator expected the architects to radically transform the apparently dominant character of the classically

planned and detailed interiors.

The Irish architectural practice Grafton Architects was given two adjoining but contrasting rooms and here they created two dramatically different, but contrasting insertions. Yvonne Farrell and Shelley McNamara, the directors of the practice, worked very much with the conditions that existed within those spaces. It was not a question of simply installing some sort of artefact, object or structure into the interior; they wanted to change or heighten the nature or character of the room itself. In order that the gallery and the insertion became the same thing, it wasn’t the case that the insertions could be moved, transported, or transferred to another gallery. The insertions could only exist in this particular position. They were completely specifi c to that site.

McNamara and Farrell identifi ed the sense of pleasure of moving from the darkness to the light. One of the rooms gave the notion of weight and moodiness, while the other was fi lled with light. Light is something that has long fascinated the architects. They deliberately emphasise the quality of light within all of their buildings, they consider it as another material with the palette.

With this in mind, the roofl ight was the site; this was the place that provided the difference in atmosphere. Large wooden framed structures were hung from this ceiling which controlled the space and, by extension, the nature and character of the light. The fl oor of both galleries was left completely clear, so the visitor could move unencumbered through the two rooms as the exhibition was created by the ceilinghung installation.

The fi rst room was bright and in here huge white fi ns were hung to refl ect the brightness onto the white walls. The length of the space was accentuated with a long bench with different degrees of hardness to help the visitor pause and contemplate the ways in which light and substance change each other.

The second room was considerably more gloomy and foreboding. Huge textured orthogonal box-like structures were hung in the space. These appeared to defy gravity; they contained great weight and felt not only melancholy, but also heavy. This created the conundrum of the appearance of heaviness in what were obviously lightweight structures hanging from the ceiling of the gallery. The huge suspended box-like frames were organised on the diagonal, in opposition to the classical and ordered organisation of the Royal Academy buildings. This build-up of the elements broke the linear axial views and movement with the space. The oblique angle refl ected the manner in which the visitor negotiated the space, and encouraged the eye to also cross the gallery in a particular manner. The architects also created a connection between the spaces through a new doorframe. Concealed lighting moved gently across the ceilings to subtly change the conditions of the rooms at different times and encourage the visitor to stop for a while and contemplate these strange calm spaces.

Grafton used the language of architecture to create insertions that discussed surface, structure, weight, lightness and light. One gallery explored the revelatory character of light and lightness. The other discussed the ominous nature of dark and heaviness: thus the two rooms complemented each other in their opposition.

A sense of continuity within any environment can provide a link between the past and the future. A building may exist within the collective memory of a local population, and its reuse emphasises resilience and permanence in times of great change. This connection between the building, the urban pattern and the local residents can contribute towards a viable and sustainable future for an area, which has the opportunity to be both creative and cultured.

Adam Khan Architects has designed a new timber staircase and mezzanine platform

for an arts space and recording studio in Tower Hamlets, East London. The building is a former church, which dates from the beginning of the 20th century, although it is some time since it was used as such; indeed, after the Second World War it was used as a shoe factory. The studio is situated in the enormous open fi rst fl oor space, which runs through the complete length of the building.

The architects claim a number of points of inspiration, the fi rst is a direct link to a detail of the building itself: the Empire’s insignia of a rampant lion. The leonine shape

informs the balcony, which is almost animalistic in shape. Four legs support the structure, with a head wrapping around an existing column, and the dog-leg staircase forming a tail.

The second inspiration for the project was a most poetic precedent: the studio of Gustave Moreau in Paris. Khan describes this as containing the fabulous winding stair: a carefully choreographed promenade for showing off and schmoozing. The circular staircase, which links the open second fl oor with the studio above, revolves twice, thus allowing those below to appreciate the fi gures of those descending, while also ensuring that those using the staircase are fully aware of the occupants of the room. This, claims Khan, reminded him of the complaint made by Oscar Wilde about gallery openings: so much art you couldn’t see the people properly!

The balcony is carefully constructed to fi t exactly into the space at the far end of the studio space, with the four legs at the front boxed for strength as well as utility. While the rear of the lion is securely attached to the wall, avoiding the important acoustic barrier. The whole thing is constructed from 19mm larch triboard, which is cheap, easy to work and available in huge sizes. The board was imported from Austria and machined in Sheffi eld in the north of England before being transported to the site. This made it inexpensive and simple to construct; indeed the total construction budget was just £15,000.

![](images/1d3df567087c4c0ae1844e0f6a80488eb6c86bd22eb0d486b05edaf0ef7072fc.jpg)

The architects have created an economical yet dynamic insertion, which promotes interaction between the different areas of the building without compromising the necessary mass of the space. The balcony houses the technical equipment, but also creates a romantic promenade within the space.

# Roma Tre University

Location Rome, Italy

Former function Abattoir

Original date 1891   
Remodelled 2016

Original Designer Gioacchino Ersoch

Designer Stefano Cordeschi

![](images/19256d0182f221084a95318a929a98d3bba20606f83582bec008d6a69d97e299.jpg)

Figure 2.20 The lecture theatre from the stage towards the audience.

When it opened in Rome in 1891, the Testaccio Slaughterhouse by Gioacchino Ersoch was regarded as the most advanced in Europe. The collection of orthogonal brick and cast iron buildings were organised in a then innovative and rational manner that paid great heed to cleanliness, sanitation and hygiene. Just in case there was some doubt about the purpose of the complex, above the pediment of the main building (mattatoio), an extraordinary sculpture of a winged hero slaughtering an ox was placed. The enormous abattoir was designed to

accommodate an eightfold increase in the population of the city and also to provide meat for much of central Italy. The workers at the stockyard were provided with free meat, but of course only the bits that couldn’t be sold, so they became creative with the rejected heads, tails, hearts, lungs, and intestines. This tradition lives on and the restaurants in the area have become known for the presence of offal on the menu. The original buildings were constructed at a time of transition, and although an important example of industrial archaeology, they also demonstrate the

move from classical to rational or more modern architecture. The slaughterhouse was moved to an eastern suburb of the city in 1975, when the delivery trucks could no longer navigate the inadequate, constricted and busy roads of central Rome.

After many years of discussion about the best use for the vast complex of buildings, structures, courtyards and other structures, an arts-based function was agreed upon. The area now hosts the large multifunctional collective: the City of Alternative Economy, an outpost of the Museum of Contemporary Art, a centre for fairtrade sales, spaces for multimedia projects and exhibitions, and the university’s architecture department. The vast spaces have been renovated with sensitivity to the past function, and care has been taken to incorporate many of the original features, such as the meat hooks hanging from the elevated track that was used to transport the cattle, and the animal pens outside.

# Porta Nuova Tower

Location Arsenale, Venice

Former function Naval building

New function Exhibition and conference centre

Date 2011

Designer MAP studio

![](images/a88579fb4c331054da5d0173facba3f941d348a85ee8f11998000dfcce7aca4e.jpg)

The concept of an echo or ghost of an original element can be the generator for the adaptation of a building, especially if the original object is invested with particular meaning. The architect can choose to engage with the concept of replication, reproduction or imitation. The copying of simple objects can reveal ambiguity, it can shift their identity and introduce an element of uncertainty. The act of duplication is invested with shared meanings, in that the memory of an object is often a distortion of the original.

Figure 2.22 The tower from the edge of the canal.

This approach of creating a copy, a simulacrum or impression of an existing object is the methodology that MAP studio took to the remodelling of the Porta Nuova Tower in the Arsenale area of Venice.

The Arsenale was the heart of the Venetian naval industry from the 12th century and is a great symbol of the ancient maritime power. It was once the supreme shipyard of the world and held the most formidable navy. Jan Morris, in her book Venice, describes the war-readiness of the Venetian Navy. “As soon as the alarm was sounded the first warships were towed out of the Arsenale and an almost Fordian succession of activities were rapidly completed to make the boat ready for sea. First supplies, then cordage, small weapons, oars, and fi nally at the entrance to the Arsenale, the crew leapt aboard, and thus the galley sailed away ready for action. The Porta Nuova Tower, which stands at the lagoon of the north side of the dock, was built at the beginning of the 19th century, and was constructed as a building to fi t the masts onto the ship.”<sup>11</sup>

The Arsenale area is no longer used by the military, and this project is part of a wholesale remodelling of the workshops and warehouses. The incredibly solid and necessarily strong stone building was divided into three, both horizontally and vertically. The interior is tripartite and contains three full height spaces, while the exterior is classically a tower, with a base, shaft and top. The project recognises and interprets this; the new elements are as visually strong and robust as the original building, although notably of the 21st century. The architects have inserted an exhibition area, conference space, laboratories and library into the building. The materials used are exposed concrete, fi bre-cement fl oor panels, waxed COR-TEN steel. These contain the same magnitude of expression, but contrast quite simply with the existing stone and timber.

The waxed COR-TEN staircase is a most magnifi cent single element within the space. It sculpturally rises through the whole open interior space and is a dramatic insertion in the heart of the building. It doesn’t touch the edges of the walls, but is specifi cally designed to fi t tightly within the existing alcove. It is an extraordinary echo or impression of the still existing but structurally unsafe original timber staircase, which sits immediately next to it. The new imitates the old.

The solidity of the staircase, the sense of its completeness, and the repetition of the structure echoes the language of the Minimalist sculptures or works of the

mid-20th century. A constant play between the experience of perception and the understanding of the insertion has been created. The staircase contains the qualities of wholeness produced by the simple geometric forms, thus there is a comprehension of the complete object. The staircase is not perceived as a collection of risers, treads, and landings but as a simple geometrical undivided object. MAP studio have created a timeless insertion, something that is both sculptural and useful, dramatic and solid, and yet speaks of the future for the building and the area.
