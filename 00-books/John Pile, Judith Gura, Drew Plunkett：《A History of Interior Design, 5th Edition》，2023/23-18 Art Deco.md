The circular entrance hall to the new part of the house—added to the rebuilt sixteenth-century shell of Eltham Palace— with its marquetry wall decoration and circular glazed dome. The luxurious decorative work exhibited at the International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts of 1925 in Paris was described as Style Moderne (Modern Style). Designers whose work would be classified as Moderne, worked without any sense of obligation to traduce tradition but they understood the time they lived in and designed as they felt appropriate for it— there were few references to the past. American art historian Jeanne Willette said that the arrival of Style Moderne represented the point at which signifiers of modernity became fashionable, when objects in the style made either by hand or machine from natural or artificial materials were prized as symbols of modernity. Modernists liked to insist that their work was driven by philosophy and not style, which was, they implied, ephemeral and superficial. Le Corbusier, and his followers used “art déco” (decorated art) as a, perhaps playfully derogatory, description of any modern work that seemed concerned with fashion or traditional aesthetics. In the 1960s, when modernist architecture was at its least popular, Style Moderne found its name and its admirers. What began as a term of abuse was legitimized in 1968 when the British historian Bevis Hillier used “Art Deco”—ironically—as the title of his book on the period and the style. Art Deco then became the retrospectively acceptable label for a distinct and democratic style, which was considered less pure because it was less austere. Historians, critics, and academics are generally uncomfortable with what may seem frivolous and practitioners of Art Deco issued no manifestoes to suggest serious intent—it was a laissez-faire strand of design. Its practitioners broadly shared a visual language but did not demand creative conformity. They admired the machine-made but did not prioritize function or technology. It was less doctrinaire and its output too diverse to be easily classifiable, too uncontentious—and charming—to draw polemical attention to itself but it was undoubtedly modern. It did not recycle past aesthetics but it understood that reductive hardline modernism would thwart the potential of interiors. ## FRANCE A room at the Salon des Artistes Décorateurs in Paris (1928) designed by Michel Roux-Spitz (1888–1957) demonstrated the forms, colors, and patterns that would typify Art Deco. The carpet and the screen had the angular, abstract motifs and deep earthy hues of the Cubist paintings of the first two decades before the outbreak of World War I. The folding screen carries a pattern influenced by African tribal art, which two decades earlier had influenced developments in Cubism. The lack of traditional moldings at baseboard and cornice levels, the smooth edges of desk and chair, and the “chandelier” do not suggest anything from the past—but neither are they related to modernistic functionalism. They were fashion-oriented and decorative (18.2). ![](images/88adac28504b52ea1ff515a0ef486386c64a85d7ab7b57646488afa02ac85d62.jpg) Michel Roux-Spitz, Salon des Artistes Décorateurs, Paris, 1928. Established designers adjusted to the new style. Emile-Jacques Ruhlmann (1879–1933) inherited his father’s decorating company and began working in an Arts and Crafts style without much enthusiasm. He designed furniture that veered, by way of Art Nouveau, unmistakably toward Moderne with smooth, simple forms in exotic materials—rare hardwoods, sharkskin, and ivory—made by highly skilled craft workers (18.4). He was not interested in experimenting with modish tubular metal and chrome. Typical of his work is a corner cabinet in rosewood and ivory of 1922 (18.3). He had his own pavilion at the International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts in 1925 and acquired more wealthy clients as a result: bankers, automobile and aircraft manufacturers, the fashion designer Jeanne Paquin, and the novelist Colette. ![](images/cc4312e6269fed3491a8fd42a813bc8d858dc964645df42c845602bf70c338f9.jpg) The most celebrated French furniture designer of the Moderne era, Ruhlmann used exotic woods and often ornamented his designs with ivory inlays on edges and legs. This piece, one of his most-publicized designs, was made in severa variations with different base woods and either black-on-white or white and black floral marquetry. The carpet pattern, the chairs, and the diamond inlays on the cabinet fronts all confirm Ruhlmann’s move from Art Nouveau to Moderne. Maurice Dufrêne (1876–1955) made a similar transition from Art Nouveau to Art Deco. He was well known and widely influential as a result of his writings and teaching and his work as a designer of furniture, textiles, glass, metalwork, and interiors (18.5). Jacques Dunard (1877– 1942) had learnt lacquering and used it in large decorative panels and screens. He exploited its colors to break up the mass of familiar pieces of furniture (18.6). Established designers, such as Ruhlmann, Dufrêne, and Dunard, were not theorists but shared with their clients an understanding and appreciation of the post-World War I zeitgeist. They were wary of modernist demands for a tabula rasa and understood that design styles evolve; they were not inclined to wipe their creative slates clean. ![](images/9724558a74b38ed572b160e6ebe5e5652cfdab3514bbcedaf736ecd3c00fd21a.jpg) Maurice Dufrêne, Hall, La Maîtrise Pavilion, International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts, Paris, 1925. Dufrne’s impetus to find a new decorative language is apparent in his work for the pavilion at the show. He added decorative painting on walls and ceilings, slim metal rails, hanging lights, and decorative objects. ![](images/e00d9a5a9be8fe33d3db66d2063bf29e7115d9a50ff268f69d03bd5cf891f05b.jpg) Jean Dunard, a lacquered cabinet, 1921. Traditionally lacquered furniture was black with deep red used as a respectful finishing touch. Dunand’s mastery of the process allowed him to experiment with breaking up functional forms to create a more abstract object. ## Mallet-Stevens Robert Mallet-Stevens (1886–1945) was born in Paris a year before Le Corbusier and, by the end of the 1920s, they were the leading architects in the city. In 1907 Mallet-Stevens enrolled at the Ecole Spéciale d’Architecture. It was a private school founded in opposition to the Ecole des Beaux-Arts by Emile Trélat (1821–1907), an engineer and early enthusiast for the elimination of architectural ornament. Mallet-Stevens went on to teach with Trélat and his own work made no reference to traditional ornament but displayed the lightness of touch that set Moderne designers apart from the work of doctrinaire modernists. Willette called him “the architectural counterpoint to Le Corbusier . . . less purist less radical yet still modern.” She described him as a bricoleur, a designer who borrowed from the plethora of ideas assailing designers in the early 1920s and collaged them and fine-tuned them into coherent wholes. He did not accept the ribbon window and commentators have said that his early experience designing for cinema was formative. His precisely positioned windows allowed natural light to dramatize his interiors. He put desirable flesh on bare modernist bones, he was about style but it was progressive style. While Le Corbusier concentrated on evolving ideas for social housing. Mallet-Stevens designed stores, fire stations, theaters, a casino, exhibitions—and houses for wealthy clients. His store for Fanchon in London in 1937 (18.9) mixed Moderne furniture and theatrical set dressing. He designed several structures for the International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts in Paris that were distinctly Moderne. The most spectacular was the Pavillon des Renseignements et du Tourisme (Pavilion of Information and Tourism; 18.7) with a tall concrete tower it stood at the entrance to the exhibition beside—and in futuristic contrast to—the monumental Beaux-Arts splendor of the Grand Palais exhibition hall built only twenty-five years earlier. Mallet-Stevens’s works would not share the lasting fame of Le Corbusier’s Pavillon de l’Esprit Nouveau but the work of both men caused scandals, particularly Mallet-Stevens’s Garden of Modern Housing for which he designed concrete trees (1925; 18.8) that were constructed by the twin sculptors Jan and Joël Martel (both 1896–1966). It may have been a joke about the urbanplanning proposals that Le Corbusier was beginning to promote—or curiosity about what the material could do. ![](images/1c2614c9991522f62ffb2722a00af08be1077ba2c315808a90245fa8276017e1.jpg) Robert Mallet-Stevens, Pavilion of Information and Tourism, International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts, Paris, 1925. His interiors were undoubtedly modernistic as demonstrated in his studio for the Martel twins in Paris, which is one of six houses he designed in the short street that, since 1975, has been called Rue Mallet-Stevens (18.10). He designed their house and studio. The ingredients are the same as those for Le Corbusier’s Pavillon de l’Esprit Nouveau but on a grander scale. It is composed with drama and glamor in mind. Mallet-Stevens’s aunt had married Baron Stoclet, for whom Josef Hoffmann created the eponymous Palais and Mallet-Stevens designed two large private houses himself: Villa Noailles (1931–2) in Hyères in southeastern France, and Villa Cavrois in Croix, northern France (1931–2; 18.11) that were undoubtedly modernistic. They are long, low buildings with flat roofs. The walls are load-bearing and pierced with orderly lines of regularly spaced, square windows separated by thin masonry piers. They lack the apparent weightlessness of Le Corbusier’s Villa Savoye balanced on its pilotis (see here) but have a solidity appropriate to their size and status. The interiors are free of moldings but are enriched with flourishes that are the result of instinct rather than rules. ![](images/6189cb45caf2f41de8c5ebb0aa8bbc6126a203cfa927080c2be97aece35f0192.jpg) Robert Mallet-Stevens, interior, Villa Cavrois, Croix, France, 1931–2. The villa is undoubtedly modern but, ultimately, is shaped by Mallet-Stevens’s instinct to add rather than subtract visual detail. Mallet-Stevens also designed furniture, and his little black-metal stacking chair No. 222 (18.12) recalls the simplicity of the Thonets’ No.14 chair (see here) and, like it, is still in production. His armchair that lies back to facilitate the languid sprawling appropriate to an artistic lifestyle is visible in photographs of the Parisian studio he designed for the Polish painter Tamara de Lempicka in 1929 (18.13). ![](images/545a3874cb7eeca81ecf69bb5f0a1b5a9fe31e8fb9b62015b72fcd5771dddb39.jpg) ## 18.12 Robert Mallet-Stevens, chair, No. 222, France, 1928. Mallet-Stevens’s experience as a film-set designer is at work to create a working stage for Tamara de Lempicka, who was a popular society painter. In 1929 he founded the Union des Artistes Modernes (Union of Modern Artists), a group of twenty decorative artists and architects of varied opinions, among them Eileen Gray, Charlotte Perriand, Francis Jourdain, Jean Prouvé, Le Corbusier, and Pierre Jeanneret. The catholic mix of members might suggest that the philosophical differences between them were not as great, or as definitive, as has been suggested. All were dissatisfied with the existing Societé des Artistes Décorateurs (Society of Decorative Artists), which had itself been formed in 1901 as a reaction against the Paris Salon to break away from the then prevailing principles and practices. They talked about overcoming the “habit of the eyes”—the tendency of architects and designers to develop a personal aesthetic that is repeated regardless of brief or context. Each year they exhibited as a group at the Salon d’Automne annual art exhibition in Paris, declaring that they were putting function before decoration. Francis Jourdain (1876–1958) exhibited an interior for a worker’s home, Perriand showed folding chairs with a steel tub structure, and Prouvé presented a Plexiglass chair. After the surrender of France to Germany in 1940 both Mallet-Stevens and Le Corbusier retreated to Vichy France, the unoccupied zone expediently tolerated by Germany. Le Corbusier hoped to get architectural commissions from the puppet government. Mallet-Stevens hoped to protect his Jewish wife, Andrée, from deportation. His Paris practice collapsed and he died in 1945 having ordered that his architectural archive be destroyed. As the creative world emerged from the war, he and his example were forgotten. Without his demonstrating alternative values, the Corbusian vision of towers in featureless parkland became the solution to post-war housing needs. ## Gray The reputation of designer Eileen Gray (1878–1976) is built around a modernist villa, E1027, at Roquebrune-Cap-Martin, France, which she designed for herself. Initially, her work was classified as Moderne and later she would become critical of some aspects of modernist thinking. She was born in Ireland but had a long career in France. Her mother was a baroness and her father an artist. Given her aristocratic background, she might have become a society decorator but something in her psyche, perhaps inherited from her father, diverted her from that obvious destination. She spent her time between Ireland and London, attended art schools in London and Paris, and studied the Japanese craft of lacquering under the Japanese master craftsman Seizo Sugawara (1884–1937), and rug weaving and wool dyeing with Arab women in the North African desert. She served as an ambulance driver during World War I and, when it ended, designed an apartment for a society hostess and fashion-store owner, Juliette Mathieu-Lévy (18.14). In 1920 Harper’s Bazaar described it as “thoroughly modern although there is much feeling for the antique.” Photographs published in 1933 show zebra skins lying on the floor and draped over a sofa, and Gray’s Pirogue (canoe) daybed, said to be inspired by a Polynesian dugout canoe or perhaps an Irish currach, and finished with a patinated bronze lacquer (18.15). She designed a second daybed in 1925, with black leather upholstery and a minimal chromed frame. The extraordinary contrast between the two may demonstrate her growing interest in a modernistic aesthetic. The following year, she designed the Bibendum chair with its three layers of leather tubes on a chrome base. It was inspired by Bibendum, the fat mascot of the Michelin tire company. It was as modern as any piece produced by a Bauhaus graduate and had wit and charm but it appeared to have no obvious reforming agenda and pre-dated the modern classics of Le Corbusier and Mies. ![](images/dea4742ed86e88d330aaab92afbe01e3faec5ce13f6316b2588449d215ccc3cb.jpg) 18.14 Eileen Gray, interior, apartment of Juliette Mathieu-Lévy, Rue de Lota, Paris, 1920. The mix of ancient and modern admired by Harper’s Bazaar, including the Bibendum chair, so called because it was inspired by the mascot of the Michelin tire company, known as Bibendum, with its three layers of fat leather tubes and chrome base. It was as disconcertingly modern as any piece by any Bauhaus graduate. ![](images/794a2e0043bceae127833d690381c72f6b1b8415a20299e845234c42a1581dfa.jpg) ## 18.15 Eileen Gray, Pirogue daybed, Paris, 1920. It is easy to see that Gray’s daybed was inspired by a simple boat of some kind but the supporting legs also suggest an animal or insect. Whatever the inspiration, the piece has something of the so-called “primitive” art that was then fashionable. In 1922 she opened the Galerie Jean Désert showroom on Paris’s most fashionable street, Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré. It was so called because she thought a man’s forename would cause the gallery to be taken seriously and she liked the desert of North Africa. She designed rugs with abstract geometric patterns that were woven in the workshop of British designer Evelyn Wyld (1882–1972), with whom she had traveled in North Africa. After that journey, Wyld had returned to London and, with Eyre de Lanux (1894–1996), an American designer of lacquered furniture and geometric abstract rugs, opened a workshop in which Gray’s rugs were woven. Galerie Jean Désert was a success, and customers included the Irish writer James Joyce, the American poet Ezra Pound, and the Italian fashion designer Elsa Schiaparelli, but in 1929, after the Wall Street Crash, Gray closed it. Gray’s lover, the Romanian architect and writer Jean Badovici (1893–1956), fostered her interest in architecture and the theories of Le Corbusier. In 1926 she began to design a holiday home to share with Badovici on the coast of the Côte d’Azur in southeastern France. It would be called E1027; “E” stood for “Eileen,” the “10” and the “2” for the tenth and second letters of the alphabet, “J(ean): and “B(adovici),” while the “7” is for the seventh letter “G(ray)” (18.16). ![](images/57ad4747f09cbdb66c8eeec70dc713c01a80588de3ea318e4182806ba113ea5e.jpg) 18.16 Eileen Gray, interior, E1027, Roquebrune-Cap-Martin, France, 1926–9. Gray designed the E1027 house, its rugs, and some of the furniture. Other pieces such as the Bibendum chair (left; 1926) and the Transit chair (right; 1924) are examples of her earlier work. E1027 took three years to complete and Gray was continually on site, fine-tuning the design. She taught herself architecture by reading technical books, learning to make architectural drawings and accompanying the Polish architect, Adrienne Górska (see here), on site visits. Badovici was an occasional visitor. The house, supported on pilotis above the steep rocky shoreline, was long and low, and incorporated Le Corbusier’s five rules of architecture (see here) and, after it was completed, he was impressed. However, as an interior designer, Gray was critical of what she saw as modernist architects’ obsession with the exterior, which she believed should be organized in response to interior activities. She thought Le Corbusier and his more doctrinaire admirers were fixated on building diagrams and looking for the universal solution. She recognized the importance of the particular: the way the building would be used by its occupants, and the visual and physical connection of the interior to its site. She also saw the interior as a context for her furniture designs, and she wanted to create “camping style” furniture that was light, practical, and efficient (18.17). ![](images/802d564101c350e6ab4030cd4dd0bfa06e9d46f0aff9cda1c7944f432eb0c419.jpg) ## 18.17 Eileen Gray, Rivoli table, 1928. Gray designed an adjustable serving table for the E1027 house to serve her guests tea. She wanted to do this “elegantly while standing; serving tea sitting at a low table is rather awkward.” At the same time she wanted to display cakes, pastries or fruits in an “interesting way.” The table has a foldable top and two swiveling trays. When E1027 was completed, Badovici devoted an edition of the magazine he edited, L’Architecture Vivante (Living Architecture), to it and said he was its joint architect. Jennifer Goff, curator of the Gray Collection at the National Museum of Ireland, has revealed that all existing drawings for the house are by Gray. Whether Badovici’s appropriation of credit for E1027 caused friction between the couple is unknown but their relationship ended in 1931 when Gray moved out. She had supplied the money to buy the site and build the house but was not a French citizen and could not own property. The house and land were registered in Badovici’s name and he retained possession. She moved along the coast to Menton to build Tempe à Pailla a twobedroom house with a big terrace. She had moved on from the idea of a single, open space toward greater compartmentalization. Much of the furniture for the house was multifunctional: a dining banquette provided storage and could be transformed into a table. The kitchen was influenced by the Frankfurt Kitchen (1926), designed by Austrian architect Margarete Schütte-Lihotzky (1897– 2000) for a social-housing project, which was organized ergonomically and is the forerunner of the modern fitted kitchen. ## INSIGHTS ## Eileen Gray, Le Corbusier, and E1027 Jean Badovici invited Le Corbusier to stay at E1027 after Gray moved out. The praise heaped on E1027 may have irritated Le Corbusier because, from 1938 to 1939 he chose to paint eight garish Cubist (not Purist) murals of nude women, one of which alluded to Gray’s bisexuality. Whether this suggestion upset her is unknown but she certainly denounced the daubs as vandalism. Photographs show Le Corbusier painting in the nude, prompted perhaps by the Mediterranean heat, a desire not to soil his clothing—or sexual frustration. Knowingly or not, maliciously or not, he was blatantly disregarding Gray’s intention that the building’s interior should be white, as a neutral background for her furniture. He was either indifferent to Gray’s clear intention or trying to subvert it. She had annoyed him by disagreeing with his pronouncement that a house should be a machine for living in, suggesting instead that it should be a living organism, a reflection of the evolving lives of those living in it. At Badovici’s suggestion Le Corbusier left and built himself a little hut, which looks more like a cabin in an American forest than a holiday home on the Côte d’Azur. Despite a propensity to make rules, he was inclined to changes of style throughout his career and it may be that he simply wanted to try something new. The hut was organic and may have been the first step on the road to Notre-Dame du Haut, a chapel he designed in the Vosges mountains in France, built in 1955 with thick load-bearing walls that taper in toward the roof and are punched, seemingly at random, with small windows, thus breaking all his five rules (see here). E1027 has been restored, but not entirely because the murals have been retained, although they are increasingly seen as impediments to appreciating the greater creativity that made the house. Anthony Flint, author of Modern Man: The Life of Le Corbusier (2014) summed up the situation saying, “Brilliant but unassuming female designer from Paris builds an elegant minimalist villa on the shores of the Mediterranean and boorish male architect takes it over. New York: New Harvest, 2014 ## Górska Adrienne Górska (1899–1969) was born in Moscow to Polish parents and then, in 1919, her family migrated to Paris, where she studied under Mallet-Stevens at the Ecole Spéciale d’Architecture. She graduated in 1924, and was one of the few women at the time to qualify as an architect. She designed a Moderne apartment in Paris for her sister, the painter Tamara de Lempicka, and collaborated with another Pole and established decorator, Sarah Lipska (1882–1973), to renovate a French farmhouse for an American heiress, Barbara Harrison. A barn became a dining room and the bathroom was finished with orange, yellow, and gold mosaics. In 1930 a review in the British journal The Architect and Building News said, “One might suggest that modernism was ruthless, even brutal, and that these attributes are masculine. But we have evidence in modern interiors that women are equally responding to the urge for modern expression.” Górska went on to design modern movie theaters for the Cinéac chain, in partnership with her husband, French architect Pierre de Montaut (1892–1947). ## Frank When interior decorators wanted something modern, they often favored furniture by Jean-Michel Frank (1895–1941). He was born in Paris, inherited considerable wealth, and integrated easily with artistic innovators. From 1920 to 1925 he traveled and made contacts among the European avant-garde. By the standards of the day, the interiors he designed were uncluttered and his color palette neutral but he did not reject decorative detail. After the discovery of the tomb of the Pharaoh Tutankhamen in 1922, he, like many of his contemporaries, took inspiration from Ancient Egypt, declaring that “the noble frames that came to us from the past can receive today’s creations.” A table covered in shagreen, or stingray skin, made his reputation and in 1924 he was commissioned to design furniture for the Hermès company. The most celebrated of those pieces was an armchair stripped to its basic form and upholstered in sheep leather (18.18). He took cues from the capacious armchairs of London’s gentlemen’s clubs but eliminated the bulging armrests and curlicues. Le Corbusier and Perriand had taken the same inspiration for their Grand Confort (see here) but, in pursuit of cubic precision sacrificed upper-body support. ![](images/c1f64c40a65f00cc952b512d5a3942cd3c8ee92c402cf3f3e023310a1817a413.jpg) An interpretation of the traditional leather club chair but covered in softer sheep leather. In 1927 he encountered Eugenia Errázuriz (1860–1951), a Chilean heiress and champion of modernity. A style leader in Paris, she introduced him to, among others, Coco Chanel, Jean Cocteau, Pablo Picasso, and Igor Stravinsky. She is credited with inventing, in 1914, Luxe Pauvre (poor luxury) having whitewashed the walls of her country house as French peasants did, using unlined linen curtains, and leaving the red-tiled floor uncovered. She introduced Frank to the idea that elegance was achieved by elimination of the superfluous. Frank’s commitment to simple forms and exotic materials was clear in his lighting design, which combined materials such as plaster, mica, obsidian, vellum, and terracotta with utilitarian metals and woods. His X table lamp (c. 1928; 18.20) was assembled from metal, wood, and unglazed terracotta. ![](images/e906e51dd8b8470bba7c121316f46a31829a2d417dff73f38d0e00fb2ef02a63.jpg) Jean-Michel Frank, X light, c. 1928. In the late 1920s, Frank employed Adolphe Chanaux (1887–1965), a decorator and cabinetmaker, to upgrade his apartment and in 1931 they joined forces to decorate the homes of leading figures in the arts and society. In 1932 they opened a workshop staffed by artisans capable of dealing with the esoteric finishes that had become the distinguishing marks of Frank’s furniture. He drew on the talent of various artists: Salvador Dalí painted screens for him, Christian Bérard designed floral carpets, Emilio Terry made console tables and frames for mirrors, and Alberto and Diego Giacometti produced bronze-and-plaster light fittings. In Elsa Schiaparelli’s Parisian apartment he introduced an orange leather sofa, black rubber curtains and dining banquettes upholstered in quilted blue chintz, a purple sofa, bright red chairs and white and gilded shelving for her salon. She approved and had him design her first perfume bottle in 1929. Frank’s work was not confined to Europe. In 1929 he designed a penthouse in San Francisco for American philanthropist Charles Templeton Crocker, which Vogue magazine hailed as the first truly modern apartment in the United States (18.19). He was Jewish, and after the outbreak of World War II, left France for Argentina to work on private and commercial projects. He combined unlikely materials: rosewood desks with heavy black iron frames, floor lamps in mahogany, alabaster and metal. His designs had wit. Such combinations of fragile, decorative, and robust industrial materials were key in the making of Maison de Verre (House of Glass) in Paris, which he designed and built between 1928 and 1932. It is an undeniably modern concept but veers away from the accepted palette of modernist materials in an interior project inside the three lower floors of a townhouse that sits within a small courtyard (18.23). A tenant on the top floor of the building refused to sell to the new owners of the site, Dr. Jean Dalsace and his wife Annie, who had commissioned Chareau. Hence, an ambitious strategy was agreed to retain the upper floor by supporting it on bulky steel columns that would rise through the new interior. Chareau, without experience of coping with such a structural conundrum, collaborated with Bernard Bijvoet (1989–1979) a technically knowledgeable Dutch architect with whom he had worked previously. ![](images/8d0d03cd66c9a9402c2d7fd1fc083347651a8e12e6018475df9733a2d508d681.jpg) Pierre Chareau, salon, Maison de Verre, Paris, 1928–32. Furniture that might be found in any traditional Paris apartment sit on a dimpled rubber floor behind a glass block wall and between raw steel columns. The ground floor accommodated the client’s gynecological practice and an entrance lobby, around which curved perforated metal screens rotated to give access to the stairs to the apartment above. The ground floor has floor-to-ceiling clear glazing but the elevations above were clad with translucent glass blocks set in steel frames with strategic strips of clear glass to give narrow views into the courtyard. The natural light that filters through the glass blocks gives the salon a greenish hue as if it might be underwater. At night, external lights give it an amber glow. The double-height salon is dominated by the black steel columns, with their orange connecting plates. They rise from gray dimpled rubber floor tiles on which stand pieces of furniture in exotic woods, Japanese screens, and a grand piano, all of which might belong in a chic apartment in a conventional Parisian block of the time. The two floors of private accommodation that wrap around the salon can be subdivided by a battery of sliding soundproofed doors and adjustable metal screens. Most of the complex details for moving furniture were evolved on site in consultation with French metalworker Louis Dalbet (1885–1950). A rolling ladder that runs along the towering bookcase is fabricated from a single piece of steel tube and inlaid with wood. The brass window casements in the rear elevation were recycled from a passenger train. Bespoke pieces on the surgery floor include a desk with a retractable tabletop, so that space between the doctor and his patient could be reduced during sensitive conversations. Maison de Verre is eclectic modernity; it was modern but did not deny the pleasures of complexities and contradictions, incongruities of style. Chareau never received such an eccentric commission again but continued to design interiors and furniture. He and his wife Dollie were both Jewish, so when Germany threatened to invade France in 1939, they sold their collection of modern paintings cheaply and escaped to New York. There, he tried to rebuild his career but had few contacts in the United States and the fame of the Maison de Verre had not preceded him. He received one other extraordinary commission from the American painter Robert Motherwell, who asked him to design a house and studio in East Hampton, Long Island, in 1947. Motherwell had visited Paris in 1935 and may have known of Maison de Verre. Chareau assembled the building from half-cylindrical, corrugated-iron-clad, army-surplus Quonset huts, and cheap greenhouse windows. He painted structural members orange, as in the Maison de Verre. The result was ad hoc and extraordinary but was demolished in 1985 (18.24). ![](images/0099a5c5d1a57c20bb34e02c76276f504f7d4715927213da92dd95a17f4fdd0d.jpg) In 1950, the year he died, Chareau suggested to the Museum of Modern Art in New York that it might exhibit his work. The museum’s director, Philip Johnson, was then besotted with the work of Mies and turned down the idea. Notwithstanding this rejection, Chareau’s reputation has grown and the Maison de Verre has been very influential, particularly among High-tech architects and designers. British architect Richard Rogers (1933–2021) cited it as an inspiration when remaking his own nineteenth-century house in London in 1983. He removed internal walls and floors and employed Chareau’s strategy of a towering salon wrapped around by floors of smaller rooms accessed by a delicate metal stair. ## Ocean Liners Le Corbusier had cited ocean liners as an inspiration, but their interiors became showpieces of Moderne. For the French government, the liner SS Normandie offered an opportunity to promote French design. Richard Bouwens van der Boijen (1863–1939) and Roger-Henri Expert (1882–1955), both exponents of Moderne, were responsible for the interiors and drew on French decorators and decorative artists including Raymond Subes (1893–1970) a metalsmith who was also involved with the SS Ile de France in 1927 and SS France in 1962. At the beginning of his career, Subes worked principally in wrought iron and occasionally in bronze and copper but, by the 1930s, he had added aluminum and oxidized or lacquered steel to his repertoire. Jean Dupas (1882–1964) was a decorative painter who began in Art Nouveau and progressed to Art Deco. He decorated the grand salon of the SS Normandie with 4,305 square feet (400 sq m) of painted and frosted glass (18.25). ![](images/cddf16c17bda250ebea2af0c71a44b32f6fe29bb9a726f3a04055ae97530b3d9.jpg) Roger-Henri Expert and Richard Bouwens van der Boijen, Grand Salon, SS Normandie, 1935. In the double-height, first-class main lounge of the great French ocean liner SS Normandie, tall murals of etched and painted glass designed by Jean Dupas took the history of navigation as their theme. The tower-like glass lighting fixtures were designed by Auguste Labouret (1871–1964). Great urns visible on the right emerge from round seating clusters and contain lights directed upward to the ceiling. The room, like most others on the ship, was a showcase of Art Deco concepts. ## UNITED STATES ## Designers from Europe Many designers working in the United States in what became the Art Deco style were immigrants from Europe and brought European ideas with them when they migrated. Many were from, or had trained in, Vienna and were familiar with Secessionist work. Paul T. Frankl (1886–1958) was born and trained in Vienna but went on to have an extensive career in the United States. It was he who observed the step forms of American tall buildings, a profile that resulted from zoning laws rather than aesthetics, and created his “skyscraper” furniture in which he exploited the strength of plywood—then a newish material—to cantilever elements (18.26). ![](images/f94afc918a4f6d4c4c4f1c50a27e79f67a53751ad49c127746f1a8d4c0e246a4.jpg) 18.26 Paul T. Frankl, “skyscraper” furniture, 1930. Skyscrapers in New York and their stepped-back profiles led Frankl to design stepped furniture, which became a staple of Art Deco. Joseph Urban (1872–1933) also trained in Vienna and went to the United States as a stage designer. He then turned to interior and furniture design and worked with an Art Deco vocabulary. His spectacular Ziegfeld Theater (1928) with its elliptical auditorium, and the richly colored walls and the exotic roof-top restaurant at the St. Regis Hotel in New York (1929) have been demolished. Frederick John Kiesler (1892–1965) was originally a stage designer in Vienna and associated briefly with the De Stijl movement before emigrating to the United States in 1926. His first American project was the Film Guild Cinema, a small theater in New York, one of the first Art Deco interiors open to the general public. Radio, the new means of communication and entertainment, was seen as one of the key social innovations of the post-World War I era and it seemed natural that Art Deco forms should be applied to the cabinets of table and console radio receivers. The material was wood, but the curved elements of the cabinets were no longer historically based and so the medium carried Art Deco into almost every home. The NBC studios in the New York Rockefeller Center were often referred to as Radio City in the 1930s and were Art Deco in form and vigorous use of color. Blue was thought to suggest electricity, and black and chrome represented new technology. The public were invited to watch programs being broadcast in an auditorium by the American designer Donald Deskey (1894–1989) and the lobbies and lounges were as spectacular as the huge auditorium itself (18.27). 18.27 ![](images/fa0f30a247e424d1b1a122f5a029a00c2b1d1ce188eef3f74979d6490f95107b.jpg) Donald Deskey, Radio City Music Hall, New York, 1932. The huge theater was designed to be a major feature of the Rockefeller Center development and to be used as a setting for film and stage productions. Deskey used Bakelite and aluminum to combine Moderne luxury with functionality. Deskey also designed residential interiors and, in the dining room of a New York apartment, lined the walls with thin sheets of cork normally used to insulate refrigerated storage rooms. Furniture was veneered in maple, bleached nearly white, and walls, doors, and furniture were free of moldings. Deskey created Art Deco interiors within the modernistic shell of the Richard H. Mandel House at Mount Kisco, New York, designed in 1933 by Edward Durell Stone (1902–78). He also designed Art Deco textiles for several American manufacturers and, as he moved toward industrial design, his work included clocks and lamps in Art Deco style. ## Architecture New York’s Chrysler Building (1930) by William van Alen (1883–1954) with its stepped setbacks and stainless-steel spiked top was ornamented with details suggesting the headlights and radiator caps of Chrysler’s automobiles. The building’s lobbies, stairs, and elevators were extravagantly Art Deco. Many other offices and apartment buildings had similar but less spectacular characteristics (18.28). ![](images/f04a5c4edc39ff5886440ba7d78edefcab1fd674e6aa3dfb94df07dc37696ee0.jpg) ## 18.28 William van Alen, lobby, Chrysler Building, New York, 1930. The Art Deco lobby to the most impressive Art Deco building in New York mixes various colors of marble with exotically grained woods. The fourteen buildings, on a 22-acre (9 ha) site, that made up the Rockefeller Center were designed by a team, led by Raymond Hood, and built between 1930 and 1939 in gray Indiana limestone in a restrained Art Deco style. The lobbies to the individual buildings are much more flamboyant—that for the International Building, completed in 1935, is typical (18.29). ![](images/508695fb1a75f366b3ecb9aa4add553471159ed9feba8feb571890c4b62c1d1d.jpg) Raymond Hood, International Building, Rockefeller Center, New York, 1935. The monumental lobby contains escalators to upper and lower concourse levels. The green marble columns and goldleaf ceiling established an appropriate grandeur for this formal entrance to one of the Rockefeller Center’s skyscrapers. A series of World’s Fairs were held in the deep economic depression of the 1930s. The Chicago Fair of 1933–4, called A Century of Progress, was a cluster of Art Deco buildings, many with brightly colored exteriors, interiors, and objects. While public acceptance was often hesitant, some manufacturers launched furniture and other products that were clearly Art Deco. The “waterfall modern” design, in which veneers curved over the edge of the top and front, and similar modest pieces of inexpensive factory-made furniture, brought the essence of Art Deco into the homes of the less wealthy. ## BRITAIN In Britain, examples of Art Deco were usually first encountered in the interiors of theaters, hotels, and restaurants. In the Strand Palace Hotel, in London, public interiors were filled with angled and zigzag forms in glass and metal. The London building for the Daily Express newspaper (1931) by Sir Owen Williams (1890–1969) and the firm of Ellis & Clarke. Externally, a spectacular and pristine composition of black Vitrolite and clear glass in chromed framing, with curved corners, sat confidently among London’s traditional stone office buildings. Public interiors, like the main entrance hall, were full-blown Art Deco (18.30). The subway stations, trains and buses of London Transport, under the direction of Frank Pick (1878–1941) carried Art Deco to an even wider audience. ![](images/d161153d71ca97be32386fa382fe99b0b83827d1058284ed41b1d00d3fceaf8b.jpg) ## 18.30 Ellis & Clarke with Owen Williams, Daily Express Building, London, 1931. The entrance lobby, designed by Robert Atkinson (1883–1952), is extravagantly embellished with Art Deco murals and a spectacular ceiling light fixture. Betty Joel (see here) designed the furniture. Architectural heritage and Art Deco interacted eccentrically at Eltham Palace in southeast London (18.1). It was a former royal palace dating from the fourteenth century but abandoned in the seventeenth. By 1930 only the Great Hall with its hammer-beam roof and a few other remnants survived. The new owners, Stephen and Virginia Courtauld, decided to restore the Great Hall and attach a modern house. Their architects, Seeley & Paget, took inspiration from Hampton Court Palace, Surrey, begun in the early sixteenth century in the Tudor style, but the resulting building suggests no particular period. In blatant contrast, the interiors are distinctly Art Deco. In the entrance hall, by Swedish designer Rolf Engströmer (1892–1970), a glazed dome lights dark timber veneers inlaid with figurative marquetry. The Italian interior designer Piero Malacrida de Saint-August (1889–1983) designed the dining room, the drawing room and Virginia Courtauld’s circular bedroom and adjoining bathroom, all of which lean less emphatically toward Art Deco. ## SCANDINAVIA While Scandinavian countries are not generally thought to have had significant involvement with Art Deco, work there in the 1920s and early 1930s parallels developments in other countries to a degree. Scandinavian designers sought new directions appropriate to the twentieth century, but for some time did not participate in the development of modernism, preferring a more cautious approach that retained links to the strong roots in traditional crafts and intelligent use of materials. The resulting work avoided “modern” materials, the chrome frames and black leather upholstery preferring wood and woven fabrics and simple reworkings of vernacular precedents. ## Sweden In Sweden, Stockholm City Hall (18.31; 1911–23) by Ragnar Östberg (1866–1945) was an example of a gentler kind of modernism that hinted at tradition. National Romanticism is the label often used for such work. It did not attempt imitation of past achievements but sought to recall the past in more contemporary terms. Whatever the aim, it had an immediate appeal to a large audience. The term Swedish Modern came to suggest furniture and interiors that were not reproductions but had a warmth and appeal that was easy to accept. ![](images/e0fa33e524cb3e6593b427c3e92c8a3c1c10839eee9c688dc7f64deea6ae4193.jpg) Ragnar Östberg, City Hall, Stockholm, 1911–23. Swedish design in the early part of the twentieth century had popular appeal. It offered a design vocabulary that was modern but rooted in tradition. This monumental hall was known as the Golden Chamber. Rooms by Gunnar Asplund (1885–1940) defined and publicized the Swedish approach to interior design. His Senna chair of 1925, with its smoothly curved seat, high back, and stubby arms suggests a simplified version of some classic prototype. He was the architect of Stockholm Public Library in 1928, an example of a restrained and classically based modernism, while his role in the design of the Stockholm Exhibition in 1930 asserted Sweden’s modernistic direction. ## Denmark Danish design of the 1920s and 1930s became known for furniture that was simple, practical and well made with a history that stems from the early years of the twentieth century. Kaare Klint (1888–1954) was a leader in the development of furniture based on human proportions and human needs. His updated version of the traditional safari chair and deck lounge chair have become classics. Another Danish classic was the hanging-light-unit, PH, developed in 1925 by Poul Henningsen (1845–1967). Mogens Koch (1898–1969) is known for his variety of foldingfurniture designs based on traditional vernacular forms as well as some simple upholstered chairs. Finn Juhl (1912–89) was responsible for the Trusteeship Council Chamber of the United Nations headquarters building in New York (1952–3), which has a wall of undulating wood strips in the ceiling that reveal the ducts and equipment overhead, an idea that was to become a commonplace expediency by the end of the century. ## AN ALTERNATIVE MODERNITY ## United States At a time when the decorated interior was being attacked by modernists, a number of female decorators in the United States and Britain had important roles to play in preserving—and developing—ideas about what a twentieth-century interior might look like if it did not reject decoration. While modernists and proto-modernists were pushing unpalatable ideas on to a puzzled laity, these women responded to the new patterns of twentieth-century life with a less barren aesthetic. They began as dilettantes and became professionals. Increasingly, they were invited to work in more commercial environments—offices and hotels—because they could meet a demand for a kind of interior that their male counterparts, particularly in architecture, were not equipped, or prepared, to carry out. Few women rose to the top in architecture but interior decoration was their territory and clients, captains of industry, businessmen, speculators—the, men who commissioned the modernist towers—called them in to customize their own private offices. ## De Wolfe Elsie de Wolfe (1859–1950) was an actress and set designer who progressed to interior design. (As a child she threw a tantrum when her parents redecorated their drawing room with William Morris wallpaper, thus demonstrating aptitude for both acting and interior design.) She advocated bright colors, and rejected heavy drapes and Victorian clutter, substituting painted furniture and mirrors that reflected natural light. She fused inside and out with trompe-l’oeil wallpaper and flowery chintzes. In 1913 she published The House in Good Taste. New Yorker magazine suggested that she had invented the interior-decoration profession and she did dominate it until the late 1930s (18.32). ![](images/1c562bc67d221863963dc0806fdeace0e45697d2ef038808b7dfe3631cfa990a.jpg) Elsie de Wolfe, the designer’s home, New York, 1898. De Wolfe declared herself to be a decorator after receiving praise for the transformation of what was her overcrowded Victorian home, which had been influenced by The Decoration of Houses by Edith Wharton and Ogden Codman Jr (see here). She eliminated dark backgrounds and clutter in favor of wallpaper, white painted moldings, and clean-lined furniture in an eclectic style drawn from a eighteenth-century sources but not reproducing any particular era. ## Wood Ruby Ross Wood (1881–1950) began her career as a newspaper reporter and may have ghostwritten most of The House in Good Taste for de Wolfe, before becoming her assistant. She set up her own business and published a book The Honest House, in 1914, in which, she advocated simplicity and common sense independently of but in sympathy with modernist architects. Her work was characterized by eclectic historicism, featuring English period furniture with floral wallpapers and strong colors. ## Draper Dorothy Draper (1889–1969) began working for acquaintances but in 1925 set up Architectural Clearing House, which the magazine Architectural Digest has suggested was the first interiordesign business. She found domestic projects tiresome and moved into the more public sphere of apartment lobby renovations. In 1929 she changed the name of the business to Dorothy Draper and Company and, in the early 1930s, redecorated the Carlyle Hotel on Madison Avenue, New York, which led to further designs for hotels in New York, Chicago, San Francisco, and Rio de Janeiro. In 1954 she designed a restaurant for the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York in which bronze nymphs danced on water-lily pads across a pool that took up half the floor area. Chairs and tables occupied the perimeter beneath birdcage chandeliers. It was dubbed the “Dorotheum,” was startlingly camp, and survived until the late 197s when it was replaced by something utilitarian (18.33). She had a newspaper column, “Ask Dorothy Draper,” syndicated in seventy newspapers. Her admirers bought a million rolls of her Cabbage Rose fabric designed for Princess Grace of Monaco’s visit to The Greenbrier luxury resort in West Virginia in 1963. ![](images/5cb977a90a24976d45b02a4465c58a8bbe57ff95814ad13119385820700cd304.jpg) 18.33 Dorothy Draper, the Dorotheum, Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York, 1948. Draper was retained to convert a former exhibition area to a restaurant. Chairs and tables were set out around an enormous pool with its dancing nymphs. ## Sister Parish Born Dorothy May Kinnicutt (1910–94), “Sister” Parish was Draper’s cousin and her grandfather was Edith Wharton’s doctor. Her younger brother nicknamed her “Sister.” In the small farmhouse that was her first marital home she created interiors that were picturesque and economical. She used white striped wallpaper and white mattress ticking for curtains and painted the bedroom floor deep red, dotting it with white diamond motifs. When family finances suffered during the Great Depression (1929–39) she opened her first small studio, in 1933, in New Jersey. Her palette of white, wicker furniture, brightly colored floors, cotton ticking, chintz, quilts, hooked and rag rugs, botanical prints, and painted lamp-shades produced light, relaxed interiors. She had created the American Country style. ## Elkins Frances Adler Elkins (1888–1953) accompanied her older brother, David Adler (1882–1949), when he went to Europe to continue his architecture studies. He was excited by classical architecture, while she was impressed by what she saw in the Paris studios of radical artists and designers, an experience that encouraged her to add contemporary pieces to her repertoire. When they returned to the United States, the siblings collaborated: he designed conventional Neoclassical houses, she was enthusiastic about modern furniture while placating conservative clients with French provincial pieces. For a colonial-style house in Monterey, California she counterpointed light brown and black walls, Chippendale furniture, Queen Anne mirrors, and Ming screens with sofas and armchairs covered in peach corduroy and lights by Jean-Michel Frank (see here), whom she had met while traveling in Europe. She and her brother were the first American importers of French country antiques and she became the sole distributor of Frank’s furniture in the United States. She established her own workshop to reproduce period pieces and her own designs. She covered walls with panels of beige goatskin and books in parchment with cut-outs to reveal the titles. She mixed an antique English games table and Queen Anne chairs with a Frank sofa and club chair. In 1930s San Francisco she worked on houses and public interiors, including the Yerba Buena Club for Women in which she covered the walls of the waiting room and lounge in yellow velvet, and the existing Victorian seating in deep reds and blues. She simplified a spiral stair with a solid balustrade and punctuated the room with white uplighters on tall cylindrical bases wrapped around with plaster leaves (18.34). ![](images/8ce4252099e49432764a0484f43ecfe19cefa2ef1bdd6ccefaea490d4632853b.jpg) 18.34 Frances Adler Elkins, Yerba Buena Club for Women, San Francisco, 1938. A transformation of a Victorian interior with walls of yellow velvet, tall columnar uplighters, and a sweeping solid balustrade to an existing spiral stair. The decorator’s work became known through magazines, like House and Garden and House Beautiful, dedicated to chronicling the homes of the rich and famous. Ladies’ Home Journal and Good Housekeeping carried news of decorating trends to less-elevated social levels. ## Britain A similar kind of confident and well-connected woman was changing interior decoration in Britain. ## Joel Betty Joel (1894–1985) was born in Hong Kong and grew up in the British colony of Singapore surrounded by Chinese art and artifacts. In 1918 she and her husband David (1890–1973) moved to England and set up a furniture-making business. When it became obvious that she was the more talented designer, he concentrated on business matters and they built a reputation in commercial sectors. A typical project was the design of interiors and furniture for the Art Deco St. Olaf House office building completed in 1932 (18.35). Initially influenced by Georgian and Arts and Crafts pieces, Joel began exploring Moderne ideas but, after using luxury veneers and fabrics, she turned to plywood and plastic laminates. She favored restrained color with geometric and abstract patterns for textiles and carpets but distanced herself from doctrinaire modernism, saying in 1935 “A great deal too much is talked about functionalism.” She also questioned decorators’ capacity to cater for the mass market. ![](images/3d47377dfd269c5e2105bcd27b94933d0074576e2a2a45226a4b95de04e6874f.jpg) Betty Joel, boardroom, St. Olaf House, London, 1932. St. Olaf House was an early example of British Art Deco. Joel designed furniture for the board room and the directors common room. In both she found a bridge between the radical interiors and the dignity the directors felt to be their due. ## Maugham Syrie Maugham (1879–1955) was fashionable London’s preferred decorator throughout the interwar years and she became a success on both sides of the Atlantic with outposts in New York and Chicago. She drew on Regency precedents and added frissons of modernity. In 1927 she caused a sensation by painting the music room of her house in Chelsea white. The society magazine Harper’s Bazaar reported “white walls, white satin curtains, white flowered chairs, white lilies and the light softened by a white velvet lamp-shade, all give that modern feeling which, in conjunction with old furniture, is so attractive.” (18.36). This appreciation of mixing old and new elements was similar to Harper’s Bazaar’s approval of Eileen Gray’s flat for Juliette Mathieu-Lévy (see here). Maugham’s coup de théâtre changed fashionable London’s taste. At the beginning of the decade their rooms were dark green, with detail picked out in gold; sofas were piled high with brocade cushions which, like their curtains, were fringed and tasseled. As was happening in the United States, dark tones were being replaced by pastels until Maugham made white de rigueur. Old furniture was retrieved from attics and painted white. Maugham invented her own technique for craquelure—the artificial inducement of thin cracks in paint to suggest ageing. Brand new is desirable, but she and other decorators knew that the patina of ageing, natural or induced, is romantic. ![](images/52281ea5d0050ce482446953a29a09a19131b43f382d3712b5afd59ed4b9e2a9.jpg) ## 18.36 Syrie Maugham, Music Room, London, 1927. Maugham’s room was predominantly white but faceted mirrors and indirect lighting were equally innovative and influential. She took the modernists’ white and showed them what could be done with it. In 1931 she converted a stable at the back of her Chelsea house into a room for parties and painted it in shades of white, described as parchment, pearl, ivory, oyster, lily, and calcium (18.37). Slim vertical strips of beveled mirror offered kaleidoscopic visions of guests. Furniture came from Frank’s boutique in Paris and included a white plaster uplighter that masqueraded as a palm tree. The room was published in The Studio, which praised its combination of the “severe simplicity of Cubism and the elegant gaiety of the eighteenth-century Baroque with rare taste and skill.” The room was undeniably modern, right for a society responding to social change but with an appetite for visual richness. ![](images/038e90b423a76aaaa98fd279161002a9171dc3156581c49de33f3d4645e16912.jpg) ## 18.37 Syrie Maugham, Stable Room, London, 1931. The rug, by Marion Dorn, features Art Deco’s popular zigzag motif. ## Dorn For her white room, Maugham commissioned a rug from the London-based American textile designer Marion Dorn (1896–1964) in white wool with a zigzag pattern created by different lengths of pile (see 18.37). Until then, Dorn was known for strongly contrasting colors. However, after the Maugham commission she concentrated on single-color, sculpted rugs that appeared in important modern British interiors, among them the Savoy Hotel and Claridge’s in London, and the RMS Queen Mary ocean liner. She favored monochromatic color schemes and what she called “butter yellow” in particular (18.38).
