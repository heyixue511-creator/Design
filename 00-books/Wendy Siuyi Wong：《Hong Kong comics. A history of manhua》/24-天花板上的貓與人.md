Sam's Manhua Series, 1995–2000

Art/Script: Choa Yat

The artist first began this manhua series in Ming Pao in 1993. Its light-hearted approach to the everyday life of ayoung couple was well received by young readers. The main character, Sam, is a typical family man, and his wife, Yan Yan is a typical wife who loves shopping. Their fat and cute housecat, Hamburger, is the main character of the series. The plot is about the fun things that happen in Sam's household and to Hamburger. Other characters——Fatty, Cream, Ah Lay— are cats, and Angela and Ronald are Sam's parentsin-law. The series has been published in six collections, including The Cat and the People on the Ceiling (1995), None of Your Cat Business (1996), Three Households and One Cat (1998), Supreme Cat (1999), Cat of the House (1999), and Cat Comes, Cat Goes (2000). Apart from the books, other merchandise, such as figurines of Sam’s family, a bag, a piggy bank, and a mug are also for sale.

Leung's Saga Series, 1998–2001

Art/Script: Choa Yat

As the title suggests, the main characters of the cartoon strip are Leung's family: Mrs. Leung, Mr. Leung, and their three children, son, Song Song, and his wife, Yo Yo, first daughter, Mimi, and her husband, Kim Kim, and second daughter, Dor Dor, who is still in high school. The series is targeted at female readers with a plot that revolves around family values and love. It also reflects the everyday life of Hong Kong people. A collection of the series was first published in 1998 under the title The Leung Saga 1, and was followed by The Leung Sagas 2, 3, and 4.

spirits of kung fu: action manhua

To most non-Chinese speakers, kung fu is simply a martial art made popular by Bruce Lee. Yet kung fu refers not only to martial arts, or wu-shu in Chinese, but also to the common spirit shared by various styles of wu-shu. The kung fu genre of Hong Kong manhua thus includes not only stories of hand-to-hand martial arts, but also sword-fighting epics, traditional Chinese myths, gangster stories, science fiction, and tales of historic fighting in full period costume.

Until the 1950s, Hong Kong depended on Shanghai for its supply of lianhuantu, the traditional illustrated storybook. The format of lianhuantu, sequential line drawings with descriptive text at the top of the page, remained almost unchanged until the end of the Second World War. By the 1920s and 1930s, Hong Kong readers were already familiar with the martial arts lianhuantu published in Shanghai

After the Communist revolution, when the supply of lianhuantu from Shanghai dried up, the British territory began to develop its own comics. Hui Guan-man's 1958 lianhuantu, Uncle Choi, not only broke new ground in format and style but also marked a new direction in Hong Kong comics toward a more accurate depiction of real life. Other works, such as the adventure story Boy Scout (1960), by Ng Gei-ping, followed Uncle Choi’s lead.

Still, during most of the 1960s, the Hong Kong comics market was dominated by penny papers and humorous comic books. The action and adventure titles that were available were mainly copies of American superhero comics and Japanese science-fiction manga. That began to change in 1968, when the work of Wong Yuk-long appeared. Wong's Little Vagabond, published in that year, was immediately popular. Two years later, Wong established a new genre of fighting comics with Little Rascals (Siu Lau-man), a realistic story of young people living in the Hong Kong housing projects and fighting gangsters and criminals. The best-selling Little Rascals spawned many imitators; the main competition was Seung-gun Siu-bo's 1971 Lee Siu-lung (Bruce Lee's Chinese name). When the colonial government passed the Indecent Publication Law in 1975, banning explicit violence in comic books, Wong established the daily newspaper Sang Po to continue his work because only comic books, not daily papers, were covered by the law. He also changed the name of Little Rascals to Oriental Heroes (Lung Fu Mun).

By the end of the 1970s, Wong and Seung-gun were the two main artists producing fighting comics. Although several competing manhua newspapers provided venues for younger artists, theywere too inexperienced to be threats to Wong and Seung-gun. In the early 1980s, Seung-gun joined Wong's company, Jademan Comics, consolidating their positions in the marketplace. Their King Manhua Weekly, introduced in 1982, showcased Hong Kong's top manhua artists. Wong also added two martial arts titles to Jademan's catalog, Drunken Master (1982) and Buddha's Palm (1983). Included in the first issues of Drunken Master was another new series, Ma Wing-shing's The Chinese Hero (Chungwah Ying-hung). Ma's realistic style and superb drawings garnered the series immediate recognition; when it appeared separately in 1983, it marked the beginning of a new era in martial arts comics.

The success of Wong's Jademan Comics helped establish Hong Kong's manhua business as a major industry. In 1986, the company was listed on the stock market, making it the first publicly traded comics firm. However, when the market crashed in October 1987, Jademan suffered greatly. In the following years, some of the company's most promising artists, including Fung Chi-ming, Lau Ding-gin, Ma Wing-shing, and Seung-gun, left to establish their own firms.

In the late 1980s and early 1990s, fighting manhua became more diverse. Some of the outstanding works of the era, such as Tin Ha Pictorial, by Ma Wing-shing, and Dagger, Sword, Laugh, by Fung Chi-ming, upheld the traditions of the genre. However, several new types of fighting comics appeared, including the video game-derived Street Fighter stories, science fiction such as Li Chi-tat's Black Mask, and a gambling series called The Legend of the God of Gamblers. Wong Yuk-long also created a period-costume martial arts series, The Legend of Emperors, in 1993.

Gangster manhua remained popular, although some titles, like Teddy Bøy, introduced in 1992, came close to glorifying violence and vice. As competition between manhua publishers intensified, some works overstepped the acceptable boundaries of violent and sexual content. In response, the Hong Kong government amended the Indecent Publication Law, forcing publishers to seal explicit comics in plastic bags, attach warning notices on the covers, and limit their sale to adult readers. These regulations remain in effect.
