Japan, cockroach traps have a wily ability to capture the unpopular insects. The cockroach, to enter the trap, wipes oil off its feet on the entrance mat, soon becomes attached to the adhesive, and eventually starves to death. These products are designed to look like the home of some happy cockroach family, which sounds absurd, but gives us some relief by dismissing from our minds the real, brutal nature of the product's function: killing bugs. This conventional cockroach trap is quite popular and actually sells very well. ![](images/e2a29ebc9cf85797a514dc0291aabb321c2f468224b0bcb3826b28be52175ca7.jpg) Kuma's re-design proposal is a roll of adhesive tape. To use it, you cut off the desired length and fold it into a square tube. Because the inner part is adhesive, it becomes a sticky translucent tunnel. The outsides of the section joints are coated with an adhesive mixture as well so that the tube-shaped roach trap can be placed vertically on walls, too. Long and thin, it can be easily set into chinks along kitchen cabinets and such, from which roaches are tempted to emerge. Kuma's product goes well with modern interiors; from now on, even roaches will be captured within functional beauty. Though very small, this product is definitely architecture and gives us a clue to understanding this architect's way of thinking. It's just like Kengo Kuma to negate a monumental house, choosinginstead a changeable tube.
