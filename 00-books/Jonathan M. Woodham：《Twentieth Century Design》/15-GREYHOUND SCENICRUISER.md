There was a similar institutionalizationof the notion of Good Design in Europe, often with support from the State, as with the Council of Industrial Design (COID), established in Britain in 1944 or the Rat für Formgebung (Design Council) in Germany, which, following a 1951 mandate in the German Parliament, was inaugurated in 1953. The COID was charged from the outset with ‘improving' standards of design in the consumer and manufacturing sectors and initiated a Good Design Award Scheme for selected products in 1957, the year after opening its own Design Centre in the Haymarket, London. The awardsgenerally recognized products which conformed to the modernist canon—-genteel and restrained, and devoid of excessive ornamentation or ephemeral styling. Similar awards reflecting parallel aesthetic priorities were offered in other European countries, including the Compasso d'Oro scheme, initiated by La Rinascente department store in Italy in 1954, and the Beauté France award, made through the French Ministry of Commerce. That there was an established international canon of taste' in the 1950s can be argued further through the evidence collected by Jay Doblin, Director of the Institute of Design at the Illinois Institute of Technology, in 1958.25 He wrote to 10o of the leading' designers, architects, and design teachers around the world, asking them to identify the ten 'best designed mass-produced products of modern times'. About 8o ballots were returned and, after the issue of further questionnaires, a final order was drawn up. Although the overall ranking was significantly more accommodating with regard to ephemeral styling than would have been the case with MOMA, theCOID, and comparable organizations with a commitment to the concept of Good Design, the top three products were the Olivetti Lettera 22 portable typewriter of 195o, designed by Marcello Nizzoli, the Eames side-chair of 1947, produced by Herman Miller, and Mies Van Der Rohe's Barcelona Chair, originally designed in 1929, produced by Knoll Associates. Raymond Loewy Associates-designed products were featured six times (including the Coldspot refrigerator for Sears Roebuck, a 1948 vacuum cleaner for Singer, and the 1954Scenicruiser greyhound bus [110]), but overall the favoured products generally looked to modernism, with a particular leaning towards designs from Europe and Scandinavia, including designs by Aalto, Barnack, Breuer, D'Ascanio, Ghia, Gretsch, Nizzoli, Pinin Farina, Ponti, Porsche, Thonet Brothers, and Wegner. ## The corporation and cultural heritage As has been seen in the case of Herman Miller and Knoll International in the UnitedStates, a significant number of companies were active in the promotion of the international style through the marketing of high-quality furniture with a heavy premium on aesthetic distinction. Cassina, the Milan-based furniture company, played a similar role, movinginto the field of architect-designed furniture from the late 194os onwards, with a particular emphasis on Italian designers, such as Franco Albini, Gio Ponti, Vico Magistretti, and Mario Bellini. From 1965 it also initiated a policy of producing a range of past classics from the late nineteenth and earlier twentieth centuries. Entitled the Masters'series, it commenced with manufacture, to the original design, of the 1929 chaise longue by Le Corbusier, Charlotte Perriand, and Pierre Jeanneret. Later, particularly in the heritage-aware 198os when thepervasive fashionabilityof Post-Modernism cast many aspects of modernist design into a historical perspective, the Masters' range included the resurrection of furniture by Gunnar Asplund, Breuer, Frank Lloyd Wright, Gerrit Rietveld, and CharlesRennie Mackintosh. Also important in the wider projection of companies has been their involvement in cultural activities, a sphere in which Olivetti has been particularly prominent. Exhibitions specifically centred upon the company's own products and designers were widely disseminated, typified by such shows as Olivetti: Form and Concept put on at the Musée des Arts Decoratifs in Paris (as well as at venues in Barcelona, Madrid, Edinburgh, London, and Tokyo between 1969 and 1971) and Os Artistas e a Olivetti (The Artists and Olivetti) shown in Sao Paolo, Brazil in 1976. However, wider aspects of Italian artistic heritage were celebrated in a series of art-historical films by Carlo Ludovico Ragghianti in the 195os, as well as in exhibitions such as the Italian Modern Art Exhibition 191o-35, drawn from the Mattioli Collection, which was shown in New York and Europe in the late 196os and early 197os. Film andphotography have also been a potentvehicle for the projection of company ethos, environment, design practice, and social policy, with commissions from, and about, other countries, cultures, ecology, and the environment. Even gifts, souvenirs, desk diaries, and company calendars, first introduced in 1951, were a part of the process of establishing the company's personality as cultural. Corporate gifts and souvenirs have included melamine paper knives by Marcello Nizzoli (196o), a melamine desk tidy by Bruno Munari (196o), a canvas brief case by Giorgio Soavi (1965), and an enamelled-iron pencil tray (1966) and electric table clock (1968) by EttoreSottsass Jr., many of which became extremely sought after as status symbols by business executives and managers. ## 'The New International Style' Globalization of the designed object accelerated significantly in the 198os with the emergence of design groups such as Memphis. It originated in 1981 in Milan and, centred around Ettore Sottsass Jr., effectively set on a commercial footingmany ideas which hadoriginated in the studio-based experimentation of 197os Italy. Memphis furniture, lighting, ceramic, glass, and jewellery designs drew upon the symbols, forms, materials, and decorative motifs of mass culture; it utilized seemingly mundane materials such as plastic laminates, and emphasized surface decoration, colour, and layers of meaning at the expense of apparent function. Such products radically undermined both the architectonic principles of the modernist aesthetic inherent in international style furniture and the linear gracefulness that had generally characterized design-conscious Italian products since the Second World War. In addition to Sottsass, the group had a strong international range of contributors to its output, including Andrea Branzi, Michele De Lucchi, Matheo Thun, and Marco Zanini from Italy, Michael Graves and Peter Shire from the United States, Hans Hollein from Austria, Xavier Mariscal from Spain, and Shiro Kuramata and Arata Isozaki from Japan. Endowed with the immediate cultural status conferred through featuring widely in exhibitions and the more image-oriented media across the world, Memphis products were the preserve of an affluent élite who saw them essentially as works of art. This notion of'design as art', where design was generally collected for its visual identity and implicit cultural values rather than any premium on function or practicality [111], was also reinforced by the outlook of companies such as Officina Alessi²7 from Italy or the New York-based Swid Powell [112]. Specializing in what has been termed 'the· tabletop industry'’or micro-architecture',28both companies focused on the commissioning of small-scale pieces of decorative art by well-known architects anddesigners, whose buildings, interiors, tastes, personalities, and exhibitions were increasingly widely covered in the media. Officina Alessi launched a series of Tea and Coffee Piazzas29 which were commissioned from eleven internationally recognizedarchitects [113], including Robert Venturi, Michael Graves, and Charles Jencks from the United States, Alessandro Mendini and Paolo Portoghesi from Italy, Oscar Tusquets from Spain, and Hans Hollein from Austria. The eleven sets were each produced in limited editions of 99, priced individually at about £12,ooo, and galleries in Milan and New York simultaneously hosted their launch in 1983, reinforcing the cultural status and economic exclusivity of the objects. Swid Powell was founded in 1982 by Nan Swid and Addie Powell, significantly both previous employees of Knoll International, the former as design director in product design development, the latter a sales vice-president. Swid Powell's first range of architect-designed pieces, including porcelain dinnerware, glassware, and silverplate, was launched in 1984 in six American cities, proving an instant commercial success in the upper echelons of the market-place. The company continued to maintain a fashionable aura throughout the decade, sustained by such high-profile events as the striking window displays in the Bergdorf Goodman department store which were planned to coincide with the American Institute of Architects'1988 convention in New York.
