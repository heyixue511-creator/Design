![](images/644737171dc00e2e7ebbea52d07139bc86b1bce5e86f87bc249d35c3d6aeb4e8.jpg) ▶Enamelled plate for David-Andersen, 1950s >Thorbjørn Lie-Jørgensen silver and ebony jug for David-Andersen, 1934 # david-andersen fonde 7 o oway) This renowned Norwegian goldsmith workshop bears the name of its founder, David Andersen, who established the business in Christiania(now Oslo) in 1876. By the turn of the century the David-Andersen workshop had begun to forge an international reputation for its exquisite ArtNouveau enamelled wares designed by, among others, GustavGaudernack and Thorolf Holmboe (1866-1935). The founder's son, Arthur David-Andersen (1875–1970), also designed silverware with swirling floral motifs in the Art Nouveau style for the family firm. Between 1901 and 1952, he wasthe artistic director of the workshop, and during this tenure he employed a number of leading designers including Harry Sørby, Johan Lund (1861–1939), Ludwig Wittmann (1877–1961) and JohanSirnes(1883-1966). In thelate 1920s Arthur's son, Ivar David-Andersen (b. 1903), became the thirdgeneration of thefamily to produce work for the firm. During the late 1920s and early 1930s, the workshop's output was increasingly influenced by the Art Deco style. Thorbjørn Lie-Jørgensen's(1900-1961)silver and ebony jug of 1934 reflects this stylistic shift with its bold undecorated form, although its softened geometry also anticipated thegentle organic forms that became so characteristic of Scandinavian design in the following decade. After the Second World War, Ivar David-Andersen took over as artistic director, while his father resumed his career as a designer, winning a gold medal at the 1954 (X) Milan Triennale. Since then, the workshop has continued to produce modern silverware designs alongside objects with more traditional flatware patterns, such as the Rococo-style Telemark. The company is also noted for the design and manufacture of contemporary metalware incorporating its famed and brilliantly coloured jeweller's emalje (enamels). Using a time-consuming technique that involves the building up of layers of enamel, the workshop has producedvases, dishes, bowls and spoons made of various metals – silver, aluminium, copper and stainless steel – that perpetuate the century-old Norwegian tradition of enamelling. Today, David-Andersen proudly claims to be Scandinavia's largest jewellery store, retailing not only its own designs (including delicately enamelled jewellery)but also those produced by other Scandinavian workshops. ![](images/f06676ea0408b637c5e6f973f926998672dc762e9e59e1bf1fceada8d2704a04.jpg)
