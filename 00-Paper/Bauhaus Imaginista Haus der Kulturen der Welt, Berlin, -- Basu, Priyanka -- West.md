# Exhibition Reviews

## Bauhaus Imaginista

Haus der Kulturen der Welt, Berlin, Germany MARCH 15–JUNE 10, 2019

Also shown at Zentrum Paul Klee, September 20, 2019–January 12, 2020, Bern, Switzerland; and as Still Undead: Popular Culture in Britain beyond the Bauhaus at Nottingham Contemporary, September 21, 2019–January 12, 2020, Nottingham, United Kingdom

Catalogue   
Bauhaus Imaginista:   
A School in the World   
Edited by Marion von Osten and Grant Watson

London: Thames & Hudson, 2019. 312 pp.; 206 color and b/w ills. Cloth £39.95

ISBN 9780500021934

Bauhaus Imaginista at the Haus der Kulturen der Welt (HKW) in Berlin was one of many 2019 exhibitions in Germany and elsewhere marking the centenary of the founding of the Staatliches Bauhaus, located first in Weimar, and then in Dessau and Berlin. In addition, spin-off exhibitions and programming for Bauhaus Imaginista took place in India, Brazil, Nigeria, and elsewhere, in relation to the exhibition’s core theme of rethinking the college’s transcultural nature and afterlives. Both of its curators are known for earlier work on postcolonial modern topics. Marion von Osten curated the exhibition In the Desert of Modernity: Colonial Planning and After, at the HKW in 2009, on how colonial areas of North Africa were deployed as laboratories of architectural modernism. Grant Watson has carried out a series of research and curatorial projects at the Institute of International Visual Arts (Iniva) in London and elsewhere about

the experimental schools founded by the Indian polymath Rabindranath Tagore in Santiniketan, West Bengal, India. In the HKW exhibition, in addition to historical works and documents, the curators commissioned a number of contemporary artists to make works related to its subthemes.

In many ways, Bauhaus Imaginista picks up from the 2013 exhibition The Bauhaus in Calcutta: An Encounter of the Cosmopolitan Avant-Garde, a project of the Bauhaus Dessau. That exhibition was curated by Regina Bittner and Kathrin Rhomberg, with publication contributions from many scholars of South Asian modernism discussing the Bauhaus exhibition that took place in Kolkata in 1922 and the relationships between the Bauhaus and the art school, Kala Bhavan, and the university, Viswa-Bharati, started by Tagore in Santiniketan in 1919 and 1921, respectively. Like it, Bauhaus Imaginista is less an exhibition about the school featuring exemplary objects than one tracing relationships between the Bauhaus and global figures and locations, as well as a proposal for how to think about modernism differently.

Von Osten has articulated a number of curatorial tenets in previous projects that are relevant to this exhibition. In the essay “In the Desert of Modernity,” she writes that her underlying methodology for that exhibition was the notion of the museum as “contact zone,” a concept derived from James Clifford and Mary Louise Pratt.<sup>1</sup> For Pratt, the contact zone is “the space of colonial encounters, the space in which peoples geographically and historically separated come into contact with each other and establish ongoing relations, usually involving conditions of coercion, radical inequality, and intractable conflict.”<sup>2</sup> Clifford writes that “[w]hen museums are seen as contact zones, their organizing structure as collection becomes an ongoing historical, political, moral relationship—a power-charged set of exchanges, of push and pull.”<sup>3</sup> In a strict sense, colonial encounters are not the primary framework of Bauhaus Imaginista, and the sorts of friction involved in the contact zone are present only in parts of the

show, which is far more positive overall about the global relationships that it posits and produces. The curators consider the Bauhaus as globally constituted and spinning out into a set of further transcultural linkages and experimental practices that generate their own energies and interest.

The introduction to the Bauhaus Imaginista catalogue sets out some of the key ideas about the exhibition’s goals. What makes the school so resonant across the world to this day is that it provides an example of how to “reimagine the relationship between the arts and society” by those seeking to “reject a nationalistic, militaristic and authoritarian past” (12). The members of the school tried to do so, first of all, by breaking down hierarchies between fine art and craft, and through material experimentation. Even more important, the exhibition is based on the premise that “[f]rom its inception, the Bauhaus was internationally oriented.” This was due not only to the international profile of its teachers and students, but also to the fact that the work produced in the college had a “transcultural element, gleaned through exposure to non-western cultures, from books and museum collections and research travel, and through interaction with foreign students and visiting lecturers” (12). The exhibition and catalogue, therefore, become an “opportunity to explore a history of transcultural exchange in the twentieth century through the specific lens of the Bauhaus and its reception in different geographies and time periods” (13). Thus, the show argues that the global significance of the Weimar-era Bauhaus lies not in how the German school influenced subsequent western and nonwestern practitioners and theorists, but rather that it was transcultural in its very fabric, and in retrospect when understood through a global modern lens.<sup>4</sup> The relationships shown and connections made in the exhibition are not always developed in depth, and instead open up laterally, especially in its second section, where it moves between disparate mediums, locations, objects, and figures.

Bauhaus Imaginista is made up of four sections: “Corresponding With,” “Learning From,” “Moving Away,” and “Still Undead,” each beginning with a “focal” Bauhaus object that is quickly taken over by a plethora of related material. This review will briefly discuss each of these in turn, including the interaction of historical, archival, and contemporary aspects in each section, and how

they conceptualize art historical temporalities, but will focus especially on the first two, which seem most fleshed out in the exhibition. “Corresponding With” starts with the so-called Bauhaus Manifesto as “comparative document,” shown with examples of student exercises from the school. This is the only section of the show dedicated to the historical period of the Bauhaus itself. In their introduction to this section, von Osten and Watson write that the Bauhaus “corresponded with” contemporaneous experiments in art education in other parts of the world through parallel investigations, as well as sometimes through contact with artworks and people (16). Accordingly, this section juxtaposes the Bauhaus with Kala Bhavan in Santiniketan and with the Research Institute of Life Construction (Seikatsu Ko¯sei Kenkyu¯sho) in Tokyo. All three early twentiethcentury institutions, it is claimed, “navigated the tensions between nationalism and internationalism” and tried to “reshape society through educational aesthetics” (13, 16).

Kala Bhavan tried to put into practice a simultaneously rural, anticolonial, national, and cosmopolitan art education, one not linked to the western timeline of modernity. Established in colonial India, it was, like the Bauhaus, both past and future oriented, looking to various ancient and still-existing art, craft, and educational forms on the subcontinent, as well as to international ones, such as those from Japan. The 1922 exhibition in Kolkata of 250 Bauhaus works that resulted from contacts between Austrian art historian and Kala Bhavan professor Stella Kramrisch and Bauhaus master Johannes Itten is, as Magdalena Droste writes, only one of the instances in which “[i]magined India and real India encountered each other repeatedly” at the Bauhaus (28).

This first section and the exhibition concept overall are deeply inspired by the decades-long writings on Indian art and the notion of “cosmopolitan modernism” proposed by Partha Mitter, who contributes a catalogue essay on Santiniketan. Mitter argues that the radical transformations in art education associated with the Bauhaus were not limited to western Europe. Both the Bauhaus and the art school at Santiniketan responded to modern forms of “alienation” from long-established communities. Artists who came to teach there, such as Nandalal Bose, who headed the art school, combined Tagore’s environmental ideals with Gandhian anticapitalist critiques and the “nationalist valorization of village India” (36, 38). Like the Bauhaus, Santiniketan tried to revive forms of apprenticeship-based learning and to create a community among students and teachers through collaborative artworks and festivals.<sup>5</sup> The exhibition includes copies of pedagogical literature from Santiniketan; sketches and notes by two of Bose’s students; a selection of rustic craft objects made at Santiniketan, some originally designed by Tagore; and photographs of students in workshops carrying out traditional craft methods, such as Bengali alpana, or ritual floor designs.

The story of the Research Institute of Life Construction begins with a 1931 exhibition organized by architect Renshichiro¯ Kawakita together with Takehiko Mizutani, a Bauhaus graduate recently returned to Japan. This featured elements of the Bauhaus’s preliminary course and ideas for the new institution that was launched shortly thereafter. Kawakita never left Japan but was a human node of international learning. He translated numerous art theoretical writings during his career and circulated them throughout the country via journals and lectures, propagating a philosophy that combined international modernist design ideals with “spiritual and oriental art practices melded together into an appealing progressive methodology,” in Helena Cˇapková’s words (64). Most significant was the journal Architecture Design I See All (Kenchiku Ko¯ gei I See All), published from 1931 to 1936, examples of which are displayed in the exhibition.

One of two contemporary artworks in this section of the show is the Otolith Group’s 2018 film O Horizon, commissioned for this exhibition with the Rubin Museum, and including footage of contemporary Santiniketan shot over the course of five years. Although in some ways a documentary about the school as it continues today, with imagery of students and teachers taking part in song, dance, theoretical discussions, and craftwork, its poetic and allegorical qualities, staged and fictional elements, and extraordinary use of sound and evocative natural imagery take it far beyond. Santiniketan’s implications become environmental and cosmic, as we see it in its natural settings at various scales.

“Learning From” is the largest and most complex, even overwhelming, portion of the show. The “focal object” is Paul Klee’s drawing Teppich (Carpet) of 1927, an example of a Bauhaus approach “in which masters and students actively studied premodern forms and techniques to innovate modern designs

for use in a new democratic society” (80). After this process of global contact, another phase was initiated by the closing of the school by the Nazis and the exile and dispersal of its affiliates throughout the world, leading, in many cases, to direct experience of what had previously been studied in books and exhibitions, as well as to deploying this knowledge at new experimental institutions. The fundamental question of this section is related to a classic problem of so-called primitivism: “[W]hat does it mean to extract cultural materials from their original context and inscribe them within new regimes of meaning, whether this is done by the nineteenthcentury ethnographic museum, the avant-garde artist, the mid-century teaching collection or in contemporary art?” The curators’ response is that the results can be contradictory. On the one hand, they can produce a “critique of high-art traditions and introduce perspectives beyond the western episteme,” and, on the other, “dislocation” and “destruction” can occur (80).

Klee’s drawing, its inspiration from North African textiles, and his involvement with the Bauhaus weaving workshop are used to lead into a number of other figures and types of work in this part of the show. Like Klee, the female students in the weaving workshop studied “oriental” textiles. Klee was also influenced by ethnographic texts in his own book collection and in the Bauhaus library, which owned many texts on “world art” published in the 1920s. Susanne Leeb writes that although such texts served to both inspire and critique the western canon, “the world represented in these series was that of the traditional ethnological museum, whose collections were for the most part compiled in the wake of colonialism” (106). Such hierarchical encounters were not transformed until later, through migration, collaborations, and travel, and, not least, by processes of decolonization.

Many of these alternative encounters took place in the realm of textiles and in the works of women artists. Students in the Bauhaus weaving workshop had been particularly inspired by the abstract Andean textiles collected by German ethnologists in the early twentieth century, just as ancient and indigenous textiles would interest later artists of the fiber art movement, such as Sheila Hicks. These later artists traveled more extensively than the Bauhäusler and had more complex relationships with indigenous people through encounters that also involved contemporary “countercultural desires.” Auther concludes that the admiration for indigenous textiles of the Americas was accompanied by “more problematic forms of psychic identification with the ancient and/or indigenous weaver, [which] highlight the appropriation of the ancient past as instrumental to modernism’s allegiance to the present” (137). Equivalent appropriations by women ceramicists are revealed in the exhibition, notably in the work of Marguerite Wildenhain, and the show associatively moves to other cross-cultural encounters in the Americas: autoethnographic films by Navajo people recording methods of textile production, countercultural fiber projects, Hannes Meyer and Lena Bergner’s study of Mexican popular printmaking, Sibylle Moholy-Nagy’s study of vernacular architecture, and others.

Two equally large subsections of “Learning From” are devoted to practices of modernism and art education in Morocco and Brazil that involved the rediscovery and study of vernacular culture. Once again, in these cases, the exploration of craft was mobilized in order to challenge the social order and create new educational methods that sometimes included a reactivation of Bauhaus ideas. The exhibition includes works from Brazilian artistic groups and schools with direct or conceptual connections with the Bauhaus curriculum, but the majority of this section is devoted to the circle around designer and architect Lina Bo Bardi. She and her partner, the museum director Pietro Maria Bardi, brought European-influenced design ideas to Brazil in the 1950s, but they also helped establish institutions in Brazil that connected contemporary design practice with native crafts. Tavares’s contribution to the show examines the design journal Des-Habitat: Revista das artes no Brasil that Bo Bardi founded in 1950, re-creating a journal issue for the exhibition that mimics its original design but mixes select historical images and articles with new texts and images challenging the Brazilian government’s program of pacification and colonization of indigenous lands and peoples.

In the Morocco-centered section, the focus is on the decolonial curriculum of the School of Fine Arts in Casablanca. In 1966, some ten years after the country’s independence, faculty members began to emphasize the country’s “own ethnic iconographic heritage,” drawing from vernacular items such as rugs, jewelry, calligraphy, and wall painting in their work. This experimental pedagogy also involved a relationship with the Bauhaus, not in order to study it, but instead, as Mohammed Melehi, one of the original faculty members of the School of Fine Arts, writes, to “explore its contribution to the clarification of our perspective and to our thinking on the reciprocal relationship between our indigenous Moroccan artistic legacy and the broader scope of art history” (118, 122). The exhibition shows graphic work by students and pages from the contemporary journal Maghreb Art featuring vernacular art forms, as well as documentation of the school.

The importance of North African silver jewelry for this decolonizing tradition is also explored in the films made by the contemporary artist Kader Attia. In Colonial Melancholia, Attia interviews female Berber family members from Algeria who discuss memories of wearing silver jewelry made with colonial-era coins. For Attia, such objects demonstrate that traditions in colonized countries are not “erased.” Instead, he identifies a process of cultural reappropriation and forms of mimesis on the part of colonized peoples (112). Attia’s second film is made up of interviews with radical anticolonial thinkers, historians, and curators and is one of the primary works in the show in which the violence of cultural appropriation is directly addressed.

The show’s third section, “Moving Away,” has for its “focal object” Marcel Breuer’s famous filmstrip published in the Bauhaus magazine in 1926 showing the development of the Bauhaus chair from the expressionist “African Chair” to more functionalist examples, and on finally to an as-yet-unknown form. The closing of the school under financial and political pressure led to the migration of faculty and students across the globe. Bauhaus émigrés played roles in the development of urban design across Europe, the Soviet Union, the United States, China, and Taiwan, in postcolonial and nonaligned India, and elsewhere. The catalogue and exhibition include various documents and texts from these locations. The section on the National Institute of Design (NID) in India is most extensive and deserves closer attention.

Postcolonial India launched policies of national development in which symbolic expressions of confidence in the future and ties to the past were both important. British and American cultural institutions played a role in shaping these developments, resulting in projects such as the 1955 Textiles and Ornamental Arts of India exhibition at the Museum of Modern Art in New York. Bauhaus Imaginista explores the contacts between the US designers Charles and Ray Eames, the Indian government, and Indian design authorities, above all, for the 1958 India Report. Also on view are examples of the design journals Marg and Design, launched in the wake of Indian independence in 1948, and striking a balance between contemporary international design trends and Indian vernacular traditions. The long-term result of the India Report was the establishment of a national school of industrial design, the NID in Ahmedabad in 1961, and later also the Industrial Design Centre (IDC) in Mumbai in 1969. The exhibition features examples of NID-designed products from the 1960s and 1970s, from graphic design for industry to women’s fashions. Both of these institutions adapted the program of the Ulm School of Design, which had been established in 1953 as a reincarnation of the Bauhaus. As Suchitra Balasubrahmanyan writes, however, both schools were aware of the limits of the application of Bauhaus and Ulm pedagogies to Indian contexts, and were focused on design challenges facing modern India and its craft heritage.

In his film The Most Beautiful Campus in Africa, the historian and architect Zvi Efrat deals with

interconnected histories of architecture at the Bauhaus and in postwar Israel and Nigeria. Key to the story recounted in this film is the shift in architect Arieh Sharon’s work from an early superficial notion of “tropical” architecture to a richer consideration of local culture and history, as seen in his design of the University of Ife in Osun, Nigeria, between 1960 and 1985. Sharon was a student of Hannes Meyer, the second and most politically oriented director of the Bauhaus. Sharon and his team studied the local environment and aesthetics at Osun, resulting in a responsive structure that harmonizes with the climate and indigenous culture. Other works in this section also deal with Meyer’s global legacies, touching on the work of the “brigade” of Meyer’s former students in the Soviet Union in the 1930s (in Daniel Talesnik’s essay; 188), the dissemination of Bauhaus ideas in China in the 1930s, and a postwar Gropius commission, involving former student I. M. Pei, in China.

The final section of the show, “Still Undead,” opens with Kurt Schwerdtfeger’s projection device of 1922, Reflecting Colour-Light-Play. This apparatus was designed to project light effects for one of the famous Bauhaus parties, with accompanying music and performance, creating the kind of immersive environment that would be developed later by László Moholy-Nagy and György Kepes. Such works combining art, science, and technology were “seen in the US to mirror the diversity of an open society” and “heralded a subsequent generation of artistic practice that utilized new technologies as a medium” (244). Examples in the show of these two lines of thought—that of playfulness or “surplus” production and the combination of art and technology—include party costume designs by students of Josef Albers at Black Mountain College, Moholy-Nagy’s experimental films, and Muriel Cooper’s work in design and data visualization at MIT’s Media Lab. However, the emphasis here is on Bauhaus-inspired works that interface with popular cultural currents in some way, like Stan VanDerBeek’s films, hairstyles by Vidal Sassoon, the work of the German band Kraftwerk, and punk subcultures in British art schools. An iteration of Bauhaus Imaginista shown at Nottingham Contemporary expands upon this influence in British music and fashion, punk, and New Wave cultures in the 1970s and 1980s (curated by Sam Thorne in addition). The goal of this part of the exhibition

is to testify to a “genealogy of both academic and extra-institutional settings” that demonstrates how “creativity exceeds and transgresses institutional structures.” It is also to emphasize the global dissemination of Bauhaus ideas in the postindustrial world that “signifies not only resistance but also innovation within commodity culture, and the blurring of the lines between experimentation, curriculum and commercialization at the Bauhaus, which has become the norm today” (244).

One’s final judgment on this rich exhibition depends on one’s openness to the expansive approach to modern and contemporary art and design history taken by its curators that emphasizes research and documentation over objects, global networks and relationships over focused description and analysis, and reconfiguring art historical temporalities. The overall implication of the show is that these experimental and theoretical connections, this global “surplus” generated by the Bauhaus and its interlocutors, only become visible when thinking about the school transculturally.<sup>6</sup> Kobena Mercer took this idea up in 2010, writing, “[M]odernity defines a state of being or condition of life in which disparate material elements and social actors are constantly uprooted from their origins and brought into contact by proliferating networks of trade, travel, and market exchange.”<sup>7</sup> While this is not a new idea to scholars of modernism, such thinking has been limited to an astonishing degree to the concept and lens of “primitivism” and to the study of global encounters from the western artists’ or scholars’ points of view. The exhibition does an excellent job of showing the limitations of this approach and providing examples of alternative itineraries.

# Cars: Accelerating the Modern World

Victoria and Albert Museum, London NOVEMBER 23, 2019–APRIL 19, 2020

Catalogue Cars: Accelerating the Modern World Edited by Brendan Cormier and Elizabeth Bisley

London: V & A Publishing, 2019. 224 pp.; color and b/w ills. Paper £30.00

ISBN: 9781851779673

Approaching the Cars: Accelerating the Modern World exhibition in the new Sainsbury Gallery at the Victoria and Albert Museum, the visitor first encounters a silver-blue E-Type Jaguar. This opening display is telling in a number of ways. To begin, Prince Harry and Meghan Markle drove this car away from their wedding reception in 2018. Significantly, it had been fitted with an electric-powered engine, combining the celebration of iconic British car design with a concern for ecological matters that the Royals support. The week I visited the museum, Meghan and Harry had just announced their withdrawal from state duties, casting the Royal family into their biggest crisis since the death of Princess Diana, Harry’s mother, some twenty-three years earlier. It is not a stretch to interpret that event as symbolic of the problems facing the auto industry at large, because the same week Boris Johnson announced that Britain would phase out gasoline and diesel motor vehicles by 2030. The Royal crisis may attract more column space in the newspapers, but the gasoline/diesel ban will occasion perhaps the greatest change in automobile culture since the introduction of the internal combustion engine in the 1880s. Depending on your point of view, this moment could be either the best or the worst time to consider “the role of the car in shaping the world we live in today,” as the V&A literature touts. The unintended paradox at the entrance helps to introduce several of the themes that recur throughout the exhibition.
