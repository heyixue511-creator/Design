# TWOWILLIAMMORRIS&THEARTS OFTHEBOOK ![](images/897e43a6335387ffbf33102fd0b4924ffc22457d9d6ef76d8dba1069f751f9c4.jpg) HOUGH William Morris displayed a lively interest in the arts of the book at various times throughout his life, the first volume printed at the Kelmscott Press was completed only five and a half years before his death. Seen in isolation, the Press appears to be an inexplicable phenomenon of Morris's final decade, an undertaking brought to fruition only by the desperate energy of a dying man. Yet Morris was able to achieve so much as a printer in so little time not only because of the obsessive intensity he brought to every task but also because of the profound understanding of the physical form of books that he had acquired over a lifetime. In fact, he made several noteworthy (but mostly abortive) efforts to produce lavishly ornamented and illustrated books before 1891. The Kelmscott Press, which to the casual observer perhaps seems to have sprung fully matured from the brow of Morris, had actually undergone a long period of gestation. The Press alone would have been enough to make the reputation of any other man or woman, but it was merely the last phase of a long, extraordinary career in which Morris achieved fame as a poet, revived a whole series of traditional crafts, established himself as the foremost English designer of the nineteenth century, and founded and managed a firm (Morris, Marshall, Faulkner, and Company, later reorganized as Morris and Company) which was to have a marked effect upon the furnishings of the middle-class Victorian home. In his later years he became, moreover, a pioneer in the architectural preservation movement and one ofthe most influential leaders ofEnglish socialism. Merely listing Morris's activities, however, cannot convey adequately the vitality of either the man or his ideas: born in an age of growing specialization, he was a brilliant generalist who did superlatively well whatever he turned his hand to. In the latter half of the twentieth century we have increasingly come to recognize Morris as one of the seminal figures in the history of modern architecture, art, and design. Though not disposed to introspection, Morris has left us in his work itself — the tapestries, stained-glass windows, wallpapers, poems, books — an unmistakable record of the growth of his inner life. 'I can't enter into politico-social subjects with any interest,'he wrote to his friend Cormell Price in July 1856, for on the whole I see that things are in a muddle, and I have no power or vocation to set them right in ever so little a degree. My work is the embodiment of dreams in one form or another." In the 188os he did after all try to translate these dreans into political realities through socialist agitation, but he remained convinced, like Dickens's Stephen Blackpool, that nineteenth-century life was ‘aw a muddle', perhaps a hopeless muddle, and ultimately only his compelling personal vision of a simpler, purer world — located especially in the Middle Ages and, in moments of optimism, in the future — sustained him. Near the end of his life, reflecting on the comparative failure of the Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings (of which he had been primary founder), he lamented, Now that I am grown old and see that nothing is to be done, I half wish that I had not been born with a sense of romance and beauty in this accursed age." The echo ofHamlet seems to be deliberate in this letter: finding himself living in a time that was out of joint, Morris, silently cursing, bent all his prodigious energies to setting it right. The Kelmscott Press was his last great attempt to restore beauty and sanity to an ugly, mad world. It is scarcely necessary to trace intellectual ‘sources’ of Morris's powerful, visionary attachment to the medieval past, for apparently from earliest childhood he was drawn to Gothic churches and cathedrals. The most intense experience of these years seems to have been a rapturous glimpse of Canterbury Cathedral at the age of eight. At Marlborough he devoured books in the school library on archaeology and ecclesiastical architecture, and invented stories about knights and fairies'; at Oxford he was intoxicated by ‘a vision of grey-roofed houses and a long winding street, and the sound of many bells'.⁴ Burne-Jones, whose moods often mirrored Morris's, has left a revealing account of a walk across Oxford's Port Meadow fron the ruins at Godstowe on a winter day when he (Burne-Jones, that is) forgot altogether the modern world and lost himself in a rapturous dream of the old days, the abbey, and long processions of the faithful, banners of the cross, copes and crosiers, gay knights and ladies by the river bank, hawking-parties and all the pageantry of the golden age — it made me feel so wild and mad I had to throw stones into the water to break the dream. I never remember having such an unutterable ecstasy, it was quite painful with intensity, as if my forehead would burst.' It is tempting to dismiss this sort of experience as adolescent fantasizing, but in Morris's case his passion for the Gothic world was connected with the growth of a comprehensive set of ideas which attempted to explain the moral, artistic, and social decline of Victorian England. They were Ruskinian' ideas, of course, yet one suspects that Morris was drawn so irresistibly to Ruskin's writings because he was already, by temperament and expericnce, predisposed to find the distant glamour of the Middle Ages more absorbing than the industrialized, urbanized world of the nineteenth century. In 1851-3 the three volumes of Ruskin's The Stones of Venice appeared, and they struck Morris and Burne-Jones at Oxford with the force of revelation. Morris was to reprint its central chapter, ‘The Nature of Gothic', as the fourth book of the Kelmscott Press in 1892: one of the very few necessary and inevitable utterances of the century,' he called it in his preface. ‘To some of us when we first read it,'Morris wrote, now many years ago, it seemed to point out a new road on which the world should travel. And in spite of all the disappointments of forty years, and although some of us, John Ruskin amongst others, have since learned what the equipment for that journey must be, and how many things must be changed before we are equipped, yet we can still see no other way out of the folly and degradation of Civilization." What exactly did Morris learn from Ruskin? Above all, that art is the expression of man's pleasure in labour'(as Morris put it in the Nature of Gothic preface); and that the only hope for the future lay in the handicrafts, for the longing of simple people will take up the chain where it fell from the hands of the craft-guilds of the fifteenth century, and . . . the academical art which has developed from that misreading of history which we call the Renaissance, will prove a barren stem'.? The modern artist (or craftsman, as Morris and Ruskin might say, since they did not recognize any distinction between the two categories) was compelled to look back to the Middle Ages for models and inspiration, though mere imitation of medieval art was to be discouraged. Writing to the illustrator C. M. Gere in 1893, Morris observed: One piece of advice I will now give you, which you must deal with according to your own mind, as it is I am aware a little dangerous. This it is; that you should now try to steep yourself, so to say, in mediaeval design; look [at] illuminations in 13th & 14th century books[,] at wood cuts and so on, and make sketches from them. . . . But (there is always a but, you know) all this will be of no use to you unless you feel yourself drawn in that direction and are really enthusiastic about the old work.
