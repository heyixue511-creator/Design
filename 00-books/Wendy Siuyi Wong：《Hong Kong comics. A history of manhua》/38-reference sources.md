# reference sources

![](images/45b004036509e2316c58d3f1893dcce42eb8feb3ed439fcbd8542fd94d6edbdd.jpg)

How to Draw Cartoons, by Ngai Nam-shan, Nam-shan Publishing, 1960s. This is one of the earliest how-to books on drawing cartoons. Though primitive, it introduced the Hong Kong audience to basic cartoon drawing techniques.

Violence and Sex in Children's Comic Books, Hong Kong 1973–74, published by nongovernment social groups, 1974. This publication interviewed 918 elementary and middle school students on their reading habits and their opinions of comic books. It collected evidence of violence and sex from comic books and invited specialists to evaluate the influence of them on children.

The Cartoons Exhibition of Hong Kong, Hong Kong Arts Center, 1981. This is a catalog of an exhibition held at the Hong Kong Arts Center, jointly presented by Hong Kong Arts Center, the Cartoons Exhibition Organizing Committee, and the Consulate General of France. It includes an article by Cheng Ka-chun on "Forty Years of Manhua in Hong Kong and Guangzhou," Ah-wu's "The Common Language of Manhua People,"“Forty Years of Hong Kong Manhua,” “The Production of Animation Film in Hong Kong,” and “Meetings with Hong Kong Cartoon Artists, and Advertising Designers."

Cartoon Works Exhibition of Hong Kong, Cartoons Exhibition Organizing Committee, 1981. This booklet includes forty-three brief biographies of selected cartoon artists in Hong Kong from 1978 to 1980.

![](images/300f98fd87389ad10d5418990d11e56ca8e33060d167f4abb8092138dc5b6af9.jpg)

WOW! Wong Yuk-long, Hong Kong Cartoon Institute, 1984. This book is a detailed account of the life of Wong Yuk-long, beginning with his childhood, and includes a psychological and social analysis by specialists of the effects on readers of violence in comics.

![](images/788701cdb6c4d3093b88c76d38f41a497ba4e9f9b5f76d2576c41d939d4cf931.jpg)

![](images/9b0e27ae5a54dd845fcd2006c7dddd00e88a1c58a2bf9941fde9d216138f034c.jpg)

Selected Cartoons of Liao Bing-xiong, by Liao Bingxiong, edited by Yeung Wai-pong, Hong Kong Cartoon Institute, 1984. This collection covers thirty-nine important works of the artist from 1934 to 1984. They include Standard Slave (1930s), The Empire of Cart (1940s), Poet and Cartoon (1950s), Nightmare (late 1970s), Not Yet Sober (1984), and an artist biography.

The Selected Cartoons of Zheng Xin-yao, by Zheng Xinyao, Hong Kong Cartoon Institute, 1988. Zheng was the rising manhua artist in China in 1988. He was the art editor of New People Evening Post beginning in 1984, and his signature style is black humor.

![](images/8e6e6c96741fc46be96a763cb6e1202265d872f9d051d09cdbc22504d4196341.jpg)

Seung-gun Siu-bo Autobiography, by Seung-gun Siubo, 1988. This autobiography records how the artist began working in the manhua industry, tells of the hardship of being a comic artist, and examines the history of the Hong Kong manhua industry.

![](images/c68d81865de10cf3639422d6dbc89ff4f3c8060f7acd5d552c70c0da60870a50.jpg)

Manhua News: The Retrospect of 50 Years of Cheng Kachun's Works, Hong Kong Cartoon Institute, 1988. This resource includes a brief history of Hong Kong comics by Cheng Ka-chun, as well as other articles by major manhua artists in the 1960s, such as Lam Cham, On Ping, Mak Ching, Au Ching, and Heung-shan Ah-wong.

![](images/6f528d6ad84d7d34c35ef9e52349136883fa24334eb9751f85206ed64873f84f.jpg)

The Works of Ma Wing-shing, by Ma Wing-shing, 1989. This publication contains a brief biography of Ma, the reprint of the first issue of Tin-ha Pictorial, the making of Tin-ha Pictorial, and his new work as of 1989.

![](images/0013fac6abd66288fac9b310065a5f685308d0587ab2e1dbbc41e95347864ee9.jpg)

![](images/1758307666c480e5b74ed34aef51d6bc6cae34ddbf54a266a85fc55e63d2f3c7.jpg)

![](images/047405ed5f13ce2fa73ea0f946208a84fdb93728a63b6842e677f3b87c68bec7.jpg)

Contemporary Hong Kong Columnist Cartoon, Hong Kong Arts Center and Hong Kong Cartoon Institute, 1989. This exhibition catalog includes works from thirty-one participating cartoonists.

The Storms of Lianhuantu, by Lau Ting-kin, 1989. This book explores assorted topics about the lianhuantu industry. The contents lack organization and are a bit random.

Painting the Rainbow: The Biography ofMa Wing-shing, by Ma Wing-shing, Yau-woo Production Company, 1990. The book is about Ma's road to success and examines the author's life from childhood to adulthood,

The Chinese Comics Collection, by Pak-muk, Yau-woo Production Company, 1990. The book includes short articles on the development of comics in China, Hong Kong, ànd Taiwan, as well as a brief biography of Chinese comics artists.

![](images/97d9548adedfa3f60c3159c9f57b6f444941e52a0d7b89c6c215faa6eb921430.jpg)

Manhua on Capitalism, by Lai Ba-lun, 1990. Lai Balun is a script writer for comics and writes of the Hong Kong manhua industry from an insider's perspective.

The Trends of Hong Kong Manhua, by Lai-ba-lun, 1990. The author introduces and analyzes the manhua industry in Hong Kong. The format of this book is uncommon.

Hong Kong Comic Artists 1990, Tin-ha Publishing, 1990. This book is one of the best biographical references on Hong Kong comics artists published in the 1990s. The detailed information makes it one of the most important sources on the history of Hong Kong comics.

![](images/258e8a1695a963a07bf2d2d444b014cd93fe6d44a95fade1b774a9b8bd68fcdb.jpg)

Manhua Classroom, by Ma Long, 1990. This book is a how-to about cartoons, but also includes a personal account of the author.

![](images/12213a803b6299cc1ad60ca2fbfef193f8b1e766f02c5b5c3ebf4bf9e8bb34b9.jpg)

The Scandals of the Lianhuantau Industry, by Lau Dinggin, 1991. The book tells the untold story of the lianhuantau industry in Hong Kong in the 1970s and 1980s.

![](images/e82065c42220bb7bec2616a1b2f0a6a4378daf37d190dc8365db1dad5eaf2606.jpg)

The Comics Are Bad, by To Mei-tse, 1991. The book is divided into four chapters: “Contemporary Lianhuantu," “Features on Selected Artists,”“Critics of Contemporary Lianhuantu," and “Talks on Lianhuantu." One section includes examples of selected classic lianhuantu.

![](images/a170333cd3f34dff536038abce878b641c47ed0a703d4a9b516a613eaba958a4.jpg)

![](images/981b2d41964a231c683fa0c243bd6359e1636a67e9ab1f5c3263ae816ea45eb3.jpg)

The Language of Lianhuantu: The Birth of a Manhua, by Yuan Kin-toa, 1992. This book examines the visual language of comics and includes topics such as the dialogue balloon, frame, montage, camera angles, and the underlying meanings of various symbols.

The Records ofHong Kong Manhua, by Cheng Ka-chun, 1992. Cheng was one of the oldest manhua artist at the time this book was written. This is the first book on Hong Kong comics history, in an abbreviated format, with black and white illustration.

![](images/f6db6c02f5db4dec2541cb58290120f1c427e4a3e8bb74b1986661c997467370.jpg)

The Critics of Wind and Cloud, by Ng Ka-keung, 1993. This book works as a critical analysis of the work of Wind and Cloud by Ma Wing-shing.

![](images/09ed4041d46ded17d73990bd82947c03a65dfa31542cfa3057a09041b632c4a9.jpg)

Dream of Ten Billion, by Wong Yuk-long, 1994. Wong told the story of the development of his company from 1985 to 1991, including the legendary story of how his company became listed on the stock market, how he became a billionaire, his experience in high society, and how he lost his company.

![](images/5faca4802d94628acfb243c3b986ce1cb65646a77dea3aff7d54f5ecbd628275.jpg)

Tiger in the Cage, by Wong Yuk-long, 1994. This book was written about the author's life, including his time in jail and what he learned from that experience.

![](images/b6a24bbc0c5d9f16de4574a7d350229462e8689e856258255a5939a76371095b.jpg)

The Good or the Bad: Textbook and Comic Book of Hong Kong (30s to 70s) Exhibition, Hong Kong Arts Center, 1994. This exhibition catalog records the classic examples of textbooks and comic books in Hong Kong from 1930 to the 1970s.

![](images/76d789df4dd8825f5c4a8d759e055184e9afc3eec4ca3e33a80bc52dac5b1cfb.jpg)

Comic in Revolt: The Shanghai Comics in the 30s and 40s, Hong Kong Arts Center, 1995. This exhibition catalog shows selected works with antiwar messages from comics magazines published in Shanghai during the 1930s and 1940s.

![](images/4d29b973064d058da2a2926de9ecd2c00f358b97a815abb2efebdd4b2fbf50ed.jpg)

Farewell Kiddy Cheung: A Tribute to the Hong Kong Cartoonist Yuan Bou-wan (1922–1995), Hong Kong Arts Center, 1996. Yuan belonged to the first generation of manhua artists born and raised in Hong Kong. He has been a household name since 1939, and this exhibition catalog records his life as a comic artist.
