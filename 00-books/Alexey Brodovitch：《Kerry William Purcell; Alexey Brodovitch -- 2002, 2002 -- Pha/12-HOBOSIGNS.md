It was a combination of the no-expense-spared ethos of Portfolio and a rejection of all forms of advertising that, unsurprisingly, resulted in the magazine folding after just three issues. However, even faced with imminent closure, Zachary was determined to keep the magazine afloat. He visited Henry Luce, the publisher of major magazines such as Time and Life, to discuss the possibility of his buying Portfolio. Luce was not interested. Trying anything to keep Portfolio alive, Rosenthal starting dipping into the funds from Zebra Press's more profitable titles, such as Murder Incorporated and Life and Death in Hollywood. When George W. Rosenthal, Sr., credited as the business manager for the magazine and the main financial backer, saw the losses Portfolio was making he promptly closed the publication down. As Zachary implied, the source of the escalating running costs for the magazine probably lay at the feet of Brodovitch and his profligacy with the number of photostats he ordered. However, while such stories contribute to the legend of Brodovitch, if anything could be singled out as a solitary cause of the magazine's demise, it was the decision not to include advertising.

The far-reaching influence of Portfolio on the graphic arts was immediate. Such was Brodovitch's spirit of experimentation and invention that when Portfolio was first published it instantaneously gave rise to a discussion group on its design at the American Institute of Graphic Arts. More than fifty years after the magazine's demise, this legacy of Portfolio still endures. A luminous example of content, production, and design in perfect synthesis, Portfolio continues to be held up among designers and artists alike as the standard by which all other graphic and industrial arts magazines are judged. In 1999, when he visited an exhibition on the work of one of Portfolio's photographers, Hans Namuth, Zachary himself was made aware of the high esteem in which the magazine is still held. Wishing to use Namuth's images of a Jackson Pollock painting in Portfolio #3, Zachary was asked by

the curator if he still had a copy of this now rare magazine. Zachary recalls that “I said I [didn't], but I tried.... I was quite surprised, they did find a mint copy of that issue with Hans's pictures of Pollock. You know, they had it under glass on a pedestal, in the middle of this whole exhibit. Jesus, that's how relevant the magazine is.... People are always amazed, I mean, to see this thing, and it's still as timeless and fresh as it was when it came out."14

Calder is the third generation of a family of sculptors. His grandfather, Alexander Milne Calder, created the giant statue of William Penn that stands or top of the Philadelphia city hall. His father, A. Stirling Calder, designed the arch which stands in New York's Washington Square, He was responsible for getting Sandy into the Metro politan Museum of Art when he used his four year old son, clad in bearskins, as the model of the statue, "Man Cub," which the museum bought.

Sandy resisted the family tradition as long as he could. He took an engineering degree from Stevens Institute in Hoboken, then worked for an electric utility, a department store, a window sash company and a lumber trade journal. He even disguised himself as a lumberjack and fled to the forests of Washington state. Inevitably, however, he returned to the Art Students League where he studied under John Sloan and drew illustrations for the Police Gazette. In 1926 he surrendered completely and went to live on the Left Bank, from which, aceording to Elizabeth Hawes who knew him then, "he loomed over Paris in a lovely orange suit" for the next few years.
