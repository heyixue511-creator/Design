## Introduction

Our main intention in putting together this anthology of essays has been to present the subject of design history from a feminist perspective. Designed objects surround us and form part of our culture in a much more intimate way than art – we interact with artefacts like clothes, cars, buildings, streets and food packaging directly in our everyday lives. But because we encounter these things in a more mundane way – we wear shoes, walk on carpets and drink coffee from mugs – design tends to become invisible and absorbed into the less thinking part of our daily existence. Art, on the other hand, has to be sought in special places such as galleries and concert halls, where it is set apart for contemplation and appreciation. While the fashion and style aspect of design is so self-conscious and visible, depending as it does for its very existence on ephemerality, the fascinating depths of meaning it embodies are disguised and hidden beneath its apparent triviality. What does design tell us about the society we live in? Does it form people's attitudes and reproduce patterns of dominance? Or is it formed by the shape of people's dreams, wishes, needs and desires?

In a century which has seen such rapid industrial and technological development as well as the harmful effects of production pursued purely for wealth creation, design can no longer be regarded as either politically neutral or merely a matter of aesthetics. One of the characteristics of design is its ability to act as a mediator or vehicle through which people interact with each other and make sense of an increasingly complex world. We have not restricted its definition to the confines of a professional activity, nor have we tried to arrive at a unified view of what we think a feminist critique of design history should be, except to distinguish it from awomendesigners' approach. Some of us have discussed the kind of issues a feminist approach to design and its history could take up,¹ but it still remains a largely unformulated area offering fascinating opportunities for enquiry. We hope that this collection of writings will spark off ideas and lead to further research.

A feminist analysis reveals the socially constructed nature of history and alerts us to difference generally, so we need not confine it to gender but can look at class, race, and other ‘differences'. In

Part One we see how this sensitivity can throw light on topics such as fashion, planning, and ephemeral articles like labels. The fascinating history of the stiletto heel discusses how a seemingly impossible design came to embody an image of femininity in the 1960s. We see how fashion for men has changed in the context of gender and class relations. An intriguing study of textile wrappings shows how imperialist and sexist attitudes are expressed in images of race and gender. Architecture can also build representations of class and gender into buildings, embodying ideas ·of cultural dominance in a concrete form.

One of the positive reasons for naming women designers is to provide role models, particularly in areas normally considered male preserves. But hidden from history’² has an inner, secret quality which cannot be entirely explained by a simplistic conspiracy of silence. Although we know that women have been left unnamed in many areas of endeavour it hasn't always been against their will. Some have consciously chosen anonymity. However where women do succeed it is good to be able to celebrate their work and point the way for others to follow. Part Two looks at two areas where women have managed to gain a foothold – in textiles and pottery design. They are still decidedly in the minority in architecture. It is noticeable that women predominate in the area of domestic design, to which they are considered more ‘naturally' suited and where many have felt moreat home'. More research is needed into the part that general and specialised design education has played in excluding women from entering certain areas such as industrial design. Some design historians consider mass-produced articles the only legitimate objects of study for design history, which of course excludes women in more ways than one.

Wher women have entered male-dominated areas of design many have excelled. In order to validate the work of women in the field of production it is essential to assert the importance of the hand-made object produced in the small workshop or in the home itself. Without this, and a consideration of crafts and anonymous design, much of women's contribution to design immediately becomes invisible. This is clearly illustrated in Part Three: we see how the Arts and Crafts Movement both confined women to the conventional division of labour and at the same time gave them an opening to practise as professional designers. 'If You Have No Sons’shows just how contingent on accidents of birth or marriage entry into the furniture trade was for women. The furniture industry is also an example of how tradition keeps certain conventions alive which restrict women's entry to this day. The history of women's contributions to design through their participation in the electricity industry illustrates how powerful women were in shaping it both from within the industry and as active consumers.

This book grew out of our frustration with books on women and design that do little more than add a few women's names into an orthodox and problematic design history, rather than applying a feminist perspective which transforms familiar terrain into an unexplored world. In Part Four the context is the home but the three case studies show how a shift of position can illuminate a different facet of women's relationship to design. They deal with practicality and rationality; the pleasures of consumption and popular taste; and the use women make of design to form their individual identity and their group relationship to the outside world. We can take a step further and postulate the home as a metaphor for our inner or interior state and our relationship with the outside as the development and construction of our social and gendered self. It is clear that we do not propose one simple methodology – or even a single complex methodology.

Feminist analysis is new to design history but its existence in other fields has given a base and support for our studies. We owe a debt to the work done by feminist social historians, anthropologists, art historians, and theoreticians working in media and cultural studies. The articles offered here are a contribution to work in progress within all branches of cultural history. We see ourselves as part of a wider movement which seeks to dislodge the notion of a single, unquestioned dominant view, whether it be on history, women's taste or place.

We have enjoyed working on this project and have gained enormously from each other and the various contributors who have worked 'with us. Their commitment to the book has been evident throughout and is reflected in the positive way they have responded to our comments, demands and deadlines. It is their enthusiasm and scholarship that has made the book what it is and we thank them wholeheartedly. We have a separate list of acknowledgments for the many people who have helped us all in a variety of ways. We wish to give a special mention to two people in particular who have made our work a pleasure - Ros de Lanerolle, the original commissioning editor, whose enthusiasm for our project helped us make it a reality, and Edda Ivan-Smith, the most supportive, thoughtful and efficient of editors, who was never too busy to give us help when we needed it. Finally, since the notion of women's pleasure is raised within these pages, we hope that our readers will enjoy the book and share in the pleasure that we had in writing it.

Judy Attfield Pat Kirkham December 1988
