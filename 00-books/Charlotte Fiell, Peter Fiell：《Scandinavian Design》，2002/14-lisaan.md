Between 1917 and 1921, she also worked as a ceramics painter in Herman Kahlers' workshop in Næstved and later from 1922 to 1929 she apprenticed as a weaver at Gerda Henning's (1891-1951) workshop in Copenhagen. She subsequently worked as a freelance designer producing woven textiles for furniture by Kaare Klint, Børge Mogensenand Mogens Koch. Duringthemid-1930s, Ahlmann established her own weaving workshop in Copenhagen, and later in 1948her work was included in the exhibition “Danish Art Treasures through the Ages” held in London. She also participated at the Milan Triennale (from 1957), and her work was included in the American touring exhibition “Design in Scandinavia”(1954–1957). During the1950s, Ahlmann moved away from hand-woven textiles in favour ofindustrially-produced fabrics that were distinguished by their simple checked and striped patterning and muted earth tones. Like many Scandinavian designers, Ahlmann used traditional craft processes, such as weaving on a handloom, as an aid in the conception and planning of designs intended for industrial manufacture. From 1953, she worked with Børge Mogensen as a design consultant for the Copenhagen-based textile manufacturer C. Olesen, and also designed fabrics with him for the Cotil company. During this period, Mogensen persuaded Ahlmann to adopt a bolder use of colour and to design textiles that could be woven on more sophisticated power looms. For her design work she received the prestigious EckersbergMedal in 1964 and later the C.F. Hansen Medal in 1978. As a leading modern designer who made the transition from craft-based production to industrial manufacture, Ahlmann was one of the key individuals responsible for the post-war renewal of Scandinavian textile design. Initially it was intended to produce wares for the Russian market anduntil 1900 the factory mainly manufactured designs originating from Rörstrand, including tableware (earthenware and porcelain), sanitary products, and tiled stoves. During the 1890s, the factory under the directorship of Gustav Herlitz appointed the architect Jacob Ahrenberg(1847–1914) as its artistic adviser. In 1896, Arabia also hired its first in-house designer, Thure Öberg, who designed and decorated a number of distinctive vases. Four years later the factory exhibited at the 1900 Paris “Exposition Universelle et Internationale”, where it was awarded a gold medal for the excellence of its wares. From 1900 onwards, Arabia manufactured products of its own design, such as the distinctive Capella earthenware range with printed ornamentation ofinterlocking forms, and theFennia ware range(1912)that was intended specifically for the American market. This latter product range, which wasdistinguished by its stylized decoration, was designed by the Finn Eric Ehrström, who was better known for his Arts & Crafts style architectural metalwork thatadorned buildings by Gesellius, Lindgren & Saarinen. In 1916, Carl Gustav Herlitz succeeded his father as managing director of the factory and the same year Arabia separated from its parent company, Rörstrand, marking a new chapter in its history. Between 1916and 1929, the factory underwent an extensive programme of modernization, which included the installation of the world's longest funnel kiln (in 1929)that measured an impressive 112metres. During this period, Arabia began producingnotable designs such as Thure Öberg's vases, which were decorated with Aubrey Beardsley-like female nudes. During the early 1930s Greta-Lisa Jäderholm-Snellman (1894-1973) also designed a number of pieces for the factory, includingan elegant black porcelain covered jar (1930) with a softly undulating yet classical form, and her earthenware Koti dinner service. The latter was simplydecorated with hand-painted motifs of flowers and countryside scenes, which revealed the influence of the Art Deco style. The studio was responsible for the design of utility wares, such as the revolutionary Kilta range(1948) made from heat-resistant earthenware. Available in a number of “mix-andmatch” plain colours – white, black, green, dark blue and yellow – this inexpensive standard range could be purchased as “open stock” rather than as a traditional service, and was multi-functional, being suitable not only as tableware but also for cooking and serving. The Kilta pieces were also interchangeable, for example, the small dish could also be used as a saucer. Its elemental undecorated forms ensuredits functional practicality aswell as its suitability for mass-production. A similar heatproof multi-functional cooking/serving range known as Liekki (Flame) was later designed by UllaProcopéin 1957. Procopé also designed the highly influential Ruska service (1960), as well as her much-loved blue and white Valencia tableware range (c. 1960), which has remained in continuous production since its introduction. Around1960, Birger Kaipiainen also produceda landmark tableware design for Arabia, the Paratiisi (Paradise) service with its colourful explosion of pansies, blackcurrants, plums, grapes and apples. Originally produced in earthenware, the range was later reissued usinghigher-fired stoneware in 1987. Alongside its mass-manufactured products, Arabia has always produced “art ceramics”, such as Kaipiainen's bead birds of the 1960s and the Pro Arte collectionsissued from 1987 onwards. In1990, the Hackman Groupacquired Arabia (as well as littala and Rörstrand), and the following year theproduction of bone chinawas moved from GustavsbergtoArabia.
