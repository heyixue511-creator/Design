# War Drums and the New York Fair According to sculptor/designer Alfonso Iannelli (mentioned earlier), a group of Chicago designers, including himself, Dave Chapman, "Chick" Waltman, H. Creston Doner, and Jean Reinecke, formed the Chicago Society of Industrial Designers (CSID), which limited its members only to the design of products for mass production, a much more attractive and permissive option to many industrial designers, who were in the process of trying to define their scope and marketability to industry in the broadest manner possible. Prior to and during this period, noted sculptor Iannelli had established Iannelli Studios in Chicago, and in addition to his sculpture projects, he designed consumer products, such as mixers, hair dryers, knife sharpeners, and hair clippers, for the John Oster Manufacturing Company, Sunbeam Electric Company, Wahl Eversharp Company, Mueller Products, and Fairbanks Morse Stoker.1 During 1938, the international situation was growing worse, almost by the day. In March, Germany annexed Austria to Hitler's Third Reich by means of the Anschluss (Connection), with little international resistance. In May, Hitler awarded “cultural prizes" to Fritz Todt for the Autobahn (Auto Way), the first "super highway," of which 3,000 kilometers had already been completed; to Ernest Heinkel for his He 1ll bomber design; to Wilhelm Messerschmitt for his Me 109 fighter plane design; and to Dr. Ferdinand Porsche for his design of the new Type 38 Kraft durch Freude (Strength through Joy) or KdF Volkswagen (People's Car). Porsche had initiated the design in 1932 as the Kleinauto (Small Car), and Hitler subsidized its development when he came to power in 1933. Thirty sample cars were built in 1937 for promotional purposes, orders were taken, and Hitler introduced a sávings scheme where the public could collect stamps that would eventually pay for the car. ![](images/25691095526cb7668cbdd31342e28b500924bc6436907a63bb361236bd859628.jpg) Volkswagen Type 38, 1938. So on May 26, 1938, witnessed by 70,000 people and 150 reporters, Hitler laid the cornerstone of the Volkswagen factory in a small village in Lower Saxony, named the car as described as above, and renamed the village KdF Stadt (KdF City). The first production was to start in September 1939, the same month that World War II would officially start. Then on July 29, 1938, Japan invaded Russia, and on September 29, neutral European nations agreed to German occupation of part of Czechoslovakia, called the Munich Agreement, which British Foreign Secretary Neville Chamberlain called “Peace in Our Time." Only a few Volkswagens were produced before World War II, and those were exclusively for Nazi dignitaries. These dramatic events in Germany demonstrated to many that Hitler threatened world peace and that war was inevitable. To others, however, they demonstrated the incredible technological, organizational, and production capabilities of Germany. This initiated the reputation, still acclaimed today, of“German engineering." Perhaps Germans were “the Master Race," as Hitler claimed, and would lead the world out of the Depression with public works. Most people just wanted America to stay out of yet another European war, because the memories of World War I were still vivid. The U.S. economy had suffered a brief recession in early 1938, but with war already building in Europe, the economy began to recover, and by autumn, it was booming from orders from European countries for arms and war equipment. Into this politically charged international atmosphere stepped the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) with a highly publicized retrospective exhibition, Bauhaus 1919–1928, which ran from December 7, 1938, to January 30, 1939. As we know, numerous faculty and students of the Bauhaus had already immigrated to the United States after Hitler closed the school in 1933. The latest to arrive, in 1938, was Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, the last director of the Bauhaus, to teach design and to design new buildings in the new, MoMA-approved International Style for the Armour (later, Illinois) Institute of Technology in Chicago. Herbert Bayer (1900–1985), a former Bauhaus print shop master who had resigned with Gropius and Moholy-Nagy in 1928, created Nazi publicity campaigns in Germany until he came to the United States in 1938 to work with Walter and Ise Gropius in designing the MoMA exhibition. He had managed to spirit many Bauhaus artworks out of Germany. Bayer would stay here after the exhibition as a consultant to major U.S. corporations, including the Great Ideas of Western Man advertising series for the Container Corporation of America starting in 1946. The Bauhaus exhibit featured the sculptures, housewares, furniture, and architectural renderings of such Bauhaus luminaries as Gropius, Mies, and Marcel Breuer. The show and catalog, edited by Bayer with Ise and Walter Gropius, downplayed the school's Expressionist beginnings and excluded the political turmoil periods under Hannes Meyer and Mies. Mies reportedly was asked to take part but declined. The names of Bauhaus artists still living in Germany were suppressed for fear of reprisals against them, and Alfred Barr, MoMA director, stopped well short of condemning Hitler in his preface to the catalog; equally concerned that the show would be viewed as anti-German and that the Bauhaus would be seen as a “Jewish-Communist cabal," he proposed to include a statement specifying the small number of faculty who were Jewish. Gropius apparently dissuaded him from doing this, but had to be prevailed on in return to omit an assertion that the school under the directorships of himself and Mies "had always been non-political in character." MoMA desired to demonstrate that the Bauhaus had already been reestablished in the United States. The "New Bauhaus: American School of Design," established in Chicago in 1937 by Bauhaus master Làszló Moholy-Nagy, would have been a perfect example to demonstrate this, and perhaps had even been planned by MoMA the year before. But the school had closed in the spring of 1938 and would not reopen until the fall of 1939, although this was not known at the time. It was no longer a good example for MoMA. Students from the Design Laboratory School, based on Bauhaus principles, now urged MoMA to exhibit their work. By the summer of 1938, the left-leaning Federation of Architects, Engineers, Chemists, and Technicians (FAECT) had terminated its sponsorship of the school due to Depression cutbacks, and Gilbert Rohde had resigned as director. By changing its name to the Laboratory School of Industrial Design, it obtained a charter from the New York Board of Regents that permitted it finally to confer degrees, but most of its students and faculty continued their participation in Popular Front activities. The school had struggled along on revenue from tuitions and by December was rewarded by its token inclusion of student work, tucked away in a corner of MoMA's Bauhaus exhibition, as MoMA's example of how the innovations of the Bauhaus were “gradually being introduced in the U.S."² MoMA's Bauhaus exhibit elicited many negative reviews from critics, who found it manipulative, chaotic and gadgety. Lewis Mumford's positive column in the New Yorker was a rare exception.³ The exhibition fit perfectly with MoMA's political agenda, initiated six years earlier at its International Exhibition of 1932: establishing the primacy of European architecture in the modern movement. But it also provided additional ammunition for MoMA's campaign to establish the Bauhaus as the original and "proper" source of the new profession of industrial design. If this was established, American industrial design could be described as a poor or misguided imitation of European innovation and could be “corrected" by MoMA to achieve maturity. This political objective stemmed from MoMA's basic assumptions (some would say biases) that guided its policies. First, like virtually all museums in the United States for decades before, MoMA at that time believed that Europe was, and should be, the center and global leader of all artistic endeavors, particularly in modern art, architecture, and design. MoMA had touted the Bauhaus exhibition as modern design based on the “best of Europe," implying that America should imitate Europe. In fact, in the previous March, MoMA had exhibited the architecture and furniture designs of Finnish architect Alvar Aalto (1898–1976) to reinforce European superiority in these fields. As a result of this Eurocentric template, European architects were regarded by MoMA as more significant than American architect Frank Lloyd Wright, even though Wright had inspired many of these same European architects nearly 30 years before and recently had designed a simple, modern Usonian ranch house community that would become a model for most U.S. postwar suburban developments. With similar logic, European Bauhaus designers were regarded by MoMA as more significant than American industrial designers, despite the fact that Americans were the first to actually design mass-produced products for mass consumption in a highly industrialized environment. Second, MoMA, at that time, believed that architects had been, and should be, the leaders not only in architecture, but in all artifacts within a home, including furniture and accessories. Historically, this had been true for many famous architects, both in Europe and in the United States. This was certainly true at the Bauhaus as well, and since MoMA was under the control of architects, there was an assumption that because of this history, architects should rightfully be leading the new profession ofindustrial design. Many architects were particularly upset not only because industrial design in the United States over the previous ten years had been led, not by architects, but by advertising artists, stage designers, display designers, commercial artists, writers, and art critics, to name a few, but even worse, the leaders, such as Loewy, Dreyfuss, and Teague, had become national celebrities and commanded incredible fees far higher than architects could hope for. Lastly, at that time, MoMA, like many art museums, defined fine art as pure, aesthetic, individual expression, based on philosophy, theory, or rationality, and without any intent of commercial gain, mass production, or popular appeal. In fact, it was generally believed that art was good only if it defied popular tastes, traditions, and commercial interests. The Bauhaus fit this idealistic template combining artistic, counterculture, avant-garde, academic experimentation, historical context, and intellectual theory. American designers did not fit this template. Their crassly commercial designs used popular “gimmicks” such as streamlining or skyscrapers, were acclaimed by the public through mass purchasing, and made lots of money for rich industrialists. What was there for a sophisticated, elite art museum to like? MoMA's curator of architecture and industrial art, John McAndrew, praised the Bauhaus industrial designs as timeless, and “free of both modernistic and streamlined aberrations," but lambasted the designs of designers like Loewy, Dreyfuss or Bel Geddes as “not properly modern," for “imitating superficially the forms of modern art, and for “restyling almost everything so that what we owned might look old fashioned as soon as possible" (the accusation of “artificial obsolescence" surfacing) These assumptions dominated MoMA policies for years, and put them at loggerheads with many practicing industrial designers and leaders of the profession, who believed that it was American industrial design practitioners of the Machine Age in America who defined the practical implementation, process definition, education, practice, and commercial success of industrial design in mass production and marketing. Industrial design in America was much more than aesthetic purity or fine art, as MoMA narrowly defined Bauhaus contributions. It was the complex and iterative process of design: the advance planning of a product to include a reasonable balance of functionality, engineering and manufacturing feasibility, user emotional wants and ergonomic needs, manufacturing costs, appropriate retail prices, suitable materials, and also an artistic and attractive visual presentation to persuade consumers to buy. Fine artists did not do this. MoMA's claim that the Bauhaus “invented” modern industrial design was a specious one. From Christopher Dresser (1834–1904) in the 1870s to the Bauhaus in the 1920s, there were dozens of designers and Arts and Crafts workshops that produced individual designs that could be called modern' in the sense of modern simplicity and style, but they were all produced by hand or in limited quantities. The Bauhaus itself, as mentioned earlier, was patterned after medieval craft guild workshops. Its so-called “invention"” was based on an academic theory of Purism described by Reyner Banham, a respected international authority on architecture and design: [Corbusier stated in Esprit Nouveau in the early 1920s that] "Purism is evidence of the Law of Mechanical Selection” ... [which was] clearly intended to share the status of Darwin's Law of Natural Selection ... [This law of Purism] ... “establishes that objects tend toward a type that is determined by .. the ideal of maximum utility and the demands of economical production, which conforms to the laws of nature." Therefore they concluded that "objects of maximum utility and lowest price have simple geometric shapes” .. such as cylinders, spheres and cones. To most architects this would appear watertight, but to most production engineers it would appear too abstract to be useful, and demonstrably false in its outcome.4 In other words, Purism was seen as an absolute and irrefutable law of nature. Bauhaus designs were of bottles, jugs, pots and pans, glasses and pipes, handmade forms which approximate these simple geometric shapes. In fact, students were criticized when they strayed from such forms. There were no designs of complex mechanical devices having multiple parts or functions. The American machine designers of the 1930s were the first to create truly industrial designs, in collaboration with engineers, for industrial mass production and consumption, including electromechanical products at low prices for middle-class consumers and mechanical monsters such as locomotives. No other group of designers had ever accomplished this before. The terms “industrial” and “machine” were appropriate and unique adjectives for this group. What did they think of Bauhaus design? Harold Van Doren, in his book Industrial Design in 1940, would write: The Bauhaus approach is a philosophy of life, as well as a method of design. It lacks, however, the realistic qualities that we Americans, rightly or wrongly, demand ... but it will be difficult, I believe, to acclimatize the esoteric ideas of the Bauhaus in the factual atmosphere of American industry.⁵ The American styles criticized by MoMA in the 1930s were popularly referred to as "streamlining,” “Art Deco," “futuristic," “Art Moderne, “modernistic,""machine design," or "contemporary." Most used modern imagery that appealed to popular taste and public expectations of the times. What they were called was much less important than what they accomplished. They were new, exciting, and different from traditional designs, and together represented a uniquely American style that changed the look of the country's industrial products. Because of this, for the first time, world industrial design leadership resided in the United States, not in Europe. This had been achieved, not by following any formal design standards, academic theories, or museum promotions, but by individual industrial designers getting down and dirty into industrial environments and determining what consumers actually wanted and needed. This was pure capitalism at work, restoring the American economy. Everyone was happy, except perhaps for MoMA and engineers. Engineers were not all that happy with industrial designers intruding on their traditional turf in manufacturing firms. Because for decades they were the highest authority in most companies on how to design and produce practical mechanical products at the lowest possible costs, they were not pleased with the demands of industrial designers to change traditional production processes and materials or to add cosmetic costs for purely nonfunctional purposes. Many designers, including major ones like Dreyfuss and Loewy, had to fiercely battle in-house engineering cultures for their proposed design changes. The day after the Bauhaus exhibition closed at the MoMA, on January 31, 1939, the Pennsylvania Railroad (PRR) completed the streamlined shell designed by Raymond Loewy for its S-1 class steam locomotive, the longest and heaviest rigid-frame reciprocating steam locomotive ever built, and the only one with a 6–4–4–6 wheel arrangement. The engine and tender was 140 feet long, almost half the length of a football field, and too big for many curves on PRR lines. It was intended to replace PRR's aging K-4s locomotives, designed in 1914. Loewy had designed a streamlined shroud for the K-4s in 1936, and his design of the S-1 shroud was a refinement of that design, with its bullet-nosed cylindrical boiler with headlight at the tip, resting on a half-bullet "cowcatcher" base. The overall design was very similar to Otto Kuhler's 1937 streamlined, bullet-nosed, steam locomotive for the B&O Royal Blue line. The photos ofLoewy standing by, or on, the S-1's base are among his most famous publicity poses. In a more human-scale result, Loewy's group in South Bend, Indiana, had just completed the design of the 1939 Studebaker Champion. The car was actually designed by Clare Hodgman, on Loewy's staff, who would also work on the 1940 line of Studebakers for Loewy. As the Bauhaus exhibition ended in January 1939, America's designers were as busy as ever. Raymond Loewy designed the Farmall tractor for the International Harvester Company, one of his famous “before-andafter" transformations of an ironwheeled contraption, looking a century old, into a clean and modern piece of farm machinery. This led to the design of a distinctive new sales center building for International Harvester, of which 1,125 would be built, and also to the design of the Tractractor caterpillar tractor. By this time, the Barnes & Reinecke design office in Chicago had a staff that included David Painter (1913–2003), vice president in charge of design; James Teague; Fred Priess; and George Mendenhall. Reinecke had also served in 1937 as a part-time instructor at the New Bauhaus under Moholy-Nagy. Barnes & Reinecke's new client was the McGraw-Edison Electric Company, which had introduced the first “pop-up" toaster in 1926. There was an infinite variety of clients; for example, Robert Davol Budlong was designing accordions for the Scandalli Dallape Cellini Company. Russel and Mary Wright were making headlines in 1939 with the introduction of their highly successful line of ceramic dinnerware called American Modern, made by Steubenville Pottery in East Liverpool, Ohio. Wright designed the line in 1937, but production had been delayed due to the 1938 recession, during which Wright attended New York University School of Architecture. The name American Modern came from Wright's successful line of furniture of the same name still being produced, which was designed in 1935 under its original name of Modern Living. In 1938 Wright had established a “casual living" concept line of accessories. The new ceramic dinnerware forms were soft and organic, and glazes were in rich, earthy colors. American Modern dinnerware became the perfect gift for young brides. With Wright's name on the bottom of each piece, he quickly became a design celebrity, as 80 million pieces were sold. In 1941, American Modern would receive the American Designers' Institute award for the best ceramic design of the year. Gilbert Rohde, who had resigned as director of the Design Laboratory in 1940, while continuing as director of design for the Herman Miller Furniture Company, now taught an industrial design program with Donald Deskey at the New York University School of Architecture, which they would continue until 1943. In 1941 Rohde designed a dramatic, transparent, circular coffee table for Herman Miller using plate glass for the top, resting on acrylic legs with brass tips. He would soon convince the company to discontinue production of all its period reproductions because of his belief that imitation of traditional design was insincere aesthetically. This was the first furniture company to do so. In Toledo, Van Doren & Associates (VDA) designed a Speedway scale for Toledo Scale and a Maytag Model E Master Washer, the first to use white rather than the speckled gray and green finish common at the time. VDA had worked with engineer Thomas R. Smith to design the sleek, unified form that concealed the motor and hoses, increased capacity by 50 percent, and featured an insulated tub that kept water hot longer. After the war, demand would increase, and Model E's appearance went essentially unchanged for 44 years, becoming one of the most successful household appliances of the century. Since 1936, in addition to their normal projects, many designers were deep in preparation for the New York World's Fair, scheduled to open on April 30, 1939. As the approaching fair dominated the news, Vogue magazine asked the leading industrial designers (Teague, Loewy, and Dreyfuss) to design fashions to be worn in the future. Vogue published the resulting designs on February 1 in an article entitled “Dress of the Future." All the top designers would be represented at the fair. Planning had begun four years earlier. In 1935, at the height of the Depression, a group of New York City retired policemen decided to create an international exposition to lift the city and the country out of the Depression. They formed the New York World's Fair Corporation (NYWFC), elected former chief of police Grover Whalen as president, and invited a number of political and business leaders to plan, build, and organize the fair and its exhibits, with countries around the world taking part in the biggest international event since World War I. Walter Dorwin Teague was one of eight directors on the fair's Board of Design, and was not only designing four exhibits, one building, a pavilion, and a diorama for the fair, but had a full plate of consulting work. One of Teague's projects in 1939 was designing glassware for Libbey Glass in Toledo, the design specifically to be used in the State Dining Room at the New York World's Fair. Teague designed a series of proposals, including a footed tumbler with a fluted, rectangular-shaped stem, inspired by the Ionic Greek columns on the front of the fair's Federal Building, and stemware with a rounded, fluted stem and cylindrical bowl. The first became the State Dining Room design, which had the etched crest of a spread-winged American eagle surrounded with thirteen stars. One hundred and twenty place settings were produced, with ten different styles of glasses and finger bowls. This same pattern without the etching would become a stock Embassy pattern in the Modern American line of 1940, developed with Owens-Illinois' design department head since 1935, Edwin W. Fuerst. The second design, with the rounded fluted stem, would become the Monticello pattern in the same line. That same year, Teague designed an internal grinder for the Heald Machine Tool Company, a tank vacuum cleaner for Montgomery Ward, a 620 Special for Kodak, an executive desk lamp for the Polaroid Company (designed by Frank Del Guidice), ![](images/9b199d9afa318a98c1591bd84cf17bdb84f1b15900eff330a4886f7ffd35090e.jpg) 1939 World's Fair brochure (courtesy Victoria Matranga). and a grand piano for Steinway & Sons. On April 30, 1939, the World's Fair in New York, optimistically titled The World of Tomorrow, held its grand opening attended by 206,000 people, with President Franklin Roosevelt giving the opening address. His speech was not only broadcast on radio networks but was also televised. NBC used the event to initiate regularly scheduled television broadcasts in New York City. An estimated 1,000 people viewed the Roosevelt telecast from about 200 television sets scattered throughout the New York area. In the Radio Corporation of America (RCA) building, its theme and concept designed by John Vassos, RCA president David Sarnoff (1891–1971) in April also introduced the first consumer television sets, which were designed by Vassos and wrapped in a clear Plexiglas enclosure also designed by Vassos. Sarnoff's speech was titled “Birth of an Industry.” He predicted that television would one day become an important entertainment and information medium. His crystal ball was 100 percent correct. Picture tubes in 1939 ranged in size from 5 inches to 12 inches, and the largest tube faced upward on top of a wooden cabinet with a raised, tilted mirror in back of the tube to view the picture horizontally. The fair's official guidebook praised the captains of the Machine Age: The true poets of the twentieth century are the designers, architects, and the engineers who glimpse some inner vision, create some beautiful figment of the imagination, and then translate it into valid actuality for the world to enjoy.⁶ The theme of the fair was "Building the World of Tomorrow." "Builders of the World of Tomorrow” was emblazoned on all fair workers' uniforms. Technology was the magic ingredient that made it all possible, and fairgoers were confident that technology would take them into the future. To people in 1939, technology meant ordinary stuff, like refrigerators, automobiles, trains, airplanes and radios; it meant building hydroelectric dams to produce electricity for rural areas; it meant Pyrex and Lucite, rayon and nylon; it meant new ways to build bridges and skyscrapers; it was about elevators and escalators; it was about speed, travel, labor saving and comfort. Within the lifetime of people in 1939, technology like this had transformed everyday life for the better. Within a decade, technology had changed the shape of everything in the American home, including the home itself. These things are now so common and traditional that we tend to take them for granted as if they always existed, but they did not. They were created in the 1930s by technology, controlled by the “true poets” of the 20th century: the engineers, the scientists, the architects, and the designers. Compare 1939's technology to today's impersonal technology of space stations, supercolliders, and genomes, which are often quite remote to our personal, everyday lives. The trademark symbols of the fair were a 610-foot-tall Trylon spire, which was connected by what was then the world's longest escalator to a 180-foot-diameter sphere called the Perisphere, both gleaming white and dominating the site at Flushing Meadows. The Perisphere housed a diorama called Democracity, which depicted a model future utopian city and suburbs of 2039 on the banks of a river. The city radiated outward in widening concentric circles around a single towering skyscraper at its center. A great hydroelectric plant on the riverbank powered the city, and the surrounding, rolling countryside was full of parks and towns.7 Designed by Henry Dreyfuss and Julian Everett, the diorama was viewed from above on a moving sidewalk, as if seen from an airplane at 7,000 feet, while a multi-image slide presentation was projected on the interior surface of the sphere, serving as a movie screen as large as an acre. In the diorama, miniature cars entered and left the model city via high-speed roads that connected to beautiful green towns clustered around factories where they worked. As night fell, the domed sky grew dark and stars came out, like a real night sky, and lights came on in the model city. Then a thousand-voice chorus was heard, and images of a dozen different groups of people (farmers, miners, engineers, etc.) were projected on the dome. The images repeatedly dissolved and were replaced by larger images, as if people were converging on Democracity. When they had arrived, shimmering light rose from the horizon to engulf the entire dome, then faded and the show was over. After the presentation, visitors exited the Perisphere on a 950-foot-long Helicline, an elevated spiral ramp that partially encircled the sphere threequarters of the way around on the outside.⁸ The message for the future was clear. People would no longer have to live in a city. They would live in the countryside or garden apartments, around the city. Factory workers would live in green towns just like executives and white-collar workers. Democracity was a vision of modern suburbs and planned communities. Henry Dreyfuss Associates (HDA) also designed the fair's administration building and the AT&T exhibit, which featured "Pedro the Vodar," an early voice synthesizer robot. The fair was divided into differently themed “zones," such as the Transportation Zone, the Communication and Business Systems Zone, the Food Zone, the Government Zone, the Community Interests Zone, the Production and Distribution Zone, etc. Different wall colors and colored lighting distinguished zones from each other. The Transportation Zone included the General Motors (GM) pavilion that featured a 36,000-square-foot Futurama exhibit designed by Norman Bel Geddes in the Highways and Horizons building. It was a diorama of a section of the United States, comprised of miniature highways, towns, waterways, 500,000 individually designed homes, 50,000 miniature vehicles, and a million miniature trees. Incredible details, such as “working” waterfalls, low clouds fashioned with chemical vapors clinging to mountainsides, clotheslines, and cow patties that many visitors never noticed, created a spectacular overall effect. Bel Geddes had perfected the diorama from his 1937 City of Tomorrow exhibit for Shell Oil, and from his earlier models of horse racing tracks and war games in the 1920s and early 1930s, described earlier. ![](images/1b7cec9b62312f7b48ded23cbc7316ffda15a2eb45a58a27bc52c2b3000e414f.jpg) World's Fair map. Futurama was by far the most popular exhibit of the fair, with long, winding waiting lines in front of the enormous, high, narrow cleft flanked by a huge silver “G” and "M" on the modernistic, silver, GM pavilion shaped like a long, low triangle. Upon entering the bright red cleft, visitors descended a ramp to a darkened room with a large map of the United States on the far wall, and a narrator gave a talk about the country's overburdened road system, then announced: “General Motors invites you on a tour of future America. The moving chairs below the map will transport you into 1960." Each chair had its own piped-in sound track with music and narration. The train of chairs was more than 1,500 feet long. The chairs moved smoothly as they floated on a rising and falling track that was 23 feet off the ground at its highest point, and each chair's sound system delivered the narration in perfect sync with the passing scenery. How was this done without computers or audiotapes? At the time, there were only two ways to record and play sound: vinyl phonograph records played on a turntable, or sound recorded on movie film. At Futurama, this feat was accomplished by something called a Polyrhetor, developed by Westinghouse: a 20-ton, 12-foot-high cylinder holding 22 bands of movie film that recorded and played only sound, each individually triggered by the moving cars on a specific segment of chair track. At the end of the ride, the last you saw of the diorama was a typical city street intersection of the future, up close. Then the chairs curved around and you got off. The Futurama ride was over, and there you were, at that very intersection, in the heart of the GM complex. The intersection was life sized and in complete detail and exactly as it was in the diorama. The visitor experience was an enthralling, simulated arrival into the future. The sense of arriving by airplane was especially powerful for the two-thirds or more of the public who had not yet traveled by air.? Bel Geddes also designed other fair attractions, including the exhibit and building of the National Dairy Company, the exhibit and building of the Wilson Packing Company, and the Crystal Lassies Theater (see below). During 1939, Bel Geddes also designed a full line of radios for the Majestic Radio and Television Company; radios for the Emerson Radio and Phonograph Corporation; and prepared, at President Franklin Roosevelt's request, a program for establishing a National Motorway Planning Authority.10 Next to the GM building was the Ford pavilion, designed by Walter Dorwin Teague. By this time, Teague's office had grown to 55 employees, including architects, engineers, artists, and industrial designers. The Ford pavilion featured a spiral roadway, like a coil of rope with wider coils at the bottom, with cars zooming up and down on part of the “Roadway of Tomorrow." Moving into the entrance hall, a large, round room, there was a vast mural in oranges, yellows, and blues jumping with activity: silver pistons that moved up and down, valves hopping in and out, a three-dimensional spark plug, factory production lines, crisscrossing conveyor belts, and smokestacks of the Ford River Rouge factory. Henry Ford's first engine and first car were displayed reverently. The first Ford Mercury was introduced, designed by chief designer Bob Gregorie and staff. During the fair, as fairgoers watched, actual full-size pastel renderings were drawn of Mercury, Ford, and Lincoln by Tucker Madawick (1917–2006), a newly hired Ford apprentice designer who had just graduated in Pratt Institute's first class of industrial designers. In the next room was an immense wedding cake turntable displaying the “Ford Cycle of Production," a round white turntable, 30 feet high and 100 feet in diameter, with stepped sides, topped by a platform on which three blue Fords were mounted in a triangle, nose to tail, canted outward toward viewers. The slopes of the turntable were covered with small, colorful, moving models of stylized men performing the jobs that contributed to the finished product on top. They mined and harvested materials, refined and finished them, made parts and assembled cars. The turntable floated on pontoons on a concealed lake and was rotated with motors. On the “Road of Tomorrow," also designed by Teague, visitors rode in late-model Fords on the tight loops of the coiled, spiral ramp under a succession of flying buttresses on a wide half circle on the outer edge of the hall that housed the turntable, tunneled straight through the entrance hall, and looped back down the spiral again. The spiral ramps were intended to show how traffic could be lifted to the express level and descend to street level in the heart of a city without wasting space. The private lounges Teague designed for the Ford building were often described as the most elegant interiors at the fair." Teague designed a number of additional exhibits for the fair, including the interior for the DuPont exhibit, which had a large sign: "From Miscellaneous Ores & Inorganic Raw Materials, Chemistry Makes ..." The multiple answers included the introduction of nylon, primarily to make women's stockings, which excited at least half of the population in the country. Teague also designed exhibits for the Kodak Company, as well as buildings for the U.S. government and U.S. Steel, the latter appearing to some as a big blue helmet or a steel hemisphere lifted off the ground and supported by curving steel ribs of some sort running along the outside. Teague claimed that the steel was too interesting to hide beneath the stucco, so the intended concealment wall was eliminated. Teague designed National Cash Register Company's (NCR) new Model 100 and created a seven-story replica of the design on top of the NCR building, which was also displayed at the 1939 Golden Gate International Exhibition in San Francisco, which ran concurrently with the New York Fair.12 ![](images/f49bbed4a9676a191f24b512287d5741e4ae85f47f200afe33730957e53d1079.jpg) National Cash Register Model 100, 1939, by Walter Dorwin Teague Associates (Patent No. D113,900, U.S. Patent Office). Dorwin, who worked for his father's firm since 1934, claimed he designed the Model 100 in 1935, but explained that the rule in all the early design offices was that every design was attributed to the head of the office, regardless of who actually created it. The Teague office was no exception. The explanation given for this practice by Teague was, "clients are paying for my services so my name must be on it, otherwise the client will think he is being cheated." Both father and son's names are on the patent. Teague designed another masterpiece diorama, the largest in the world, which thrilled thousands and was located in the Con Edison building. Called the “City of Light," it was located along the concave wall of a gigantic room where people stood in a central viewing well to watch the show. Unlike other dioramas, it was viewed not in the round, but from the front, showing all of New York City, centering on the towers of Manhattan. Buildings to the rear seemed to be glued to the wall. As they stepped outward, in perhaps 15 ranks, they grew larger, and the ones in front were freestanding. The Empire State Building was 22 feet high. At the start of the show it was dawn, with buildings in pinks, roses, and grays against a pale blue background. The effect was breathtaking because of the flattened perspective, with the whole city compressed into perhaps a dozen feet. There was much activity in the model city, with trains moving, cars crossing bridges, and elevators moving up and down in the towers. During the show, there was a thunderstorm with realistic light and sound effects, and as it darkened, all the countless tiny lights came on and the buildings turned deep blue. When the storm ended, a peaceful evening fell and still more lights came on. It was a magically beautiful sight.³ Gone with the Wind won the Academy Award for best picture that year, but fairgoers who saw The Wizard of Oz found Emerald City to be a familiar city of the future, just a bit more modern than the many model cities they saw at the fair. Behind the GM and Ford buildings was the Chrysler Motors Exhibit building, designed by Raymond Loewy. Chrysler's building housed the Transportation Zone's focal exhibit, which featured Loewy's Rocketport exhibit. A futuristic rocket, shot out of a giant gun, would take passengers to London. Every twenty minutes, visitors were admitted to a semicircular area, in total darkness except for the spotlighted rocket on its launchpad. There were blinking lights, white and colored, along with a deep rhythmic sound, the whirling of motors, compressors, and high-frequency sound waves, conveying a sense of activity and suspense. Then the sound of sirens: Liftoff! A blinding flash of hundreds of strobe lights and the roar of compressed air suddenly released conveyed the optical illusion of the rocket disappearing overhead into the blackness. Then total silence while a gradually diminishing point of light faded away into the stars.1⁴ Public statements by the fair's science director acknowledged that "rocket flight is at present impossible, but the problem is almost wholly limited to the invention of a proper fuel."s Within five years, rockets would in fact be raining on London, not from New York, but from Hitler's rocket bases in Germany. In the building, Loewy also presented models and drawings of“the Automobile of the Future," "the Taxicab of the Future" (an electricpowered, three-wheeled vehicle), and "the Motor Coach of the future." Loewy also designed the “House of Jewels” interior at another part of the fair. The railroad exhibits in the Transportation Zone included Loewy's huge Pennsylvania Railroad S-1 locomotive and tender, absolutely stationary, but screaming at full 60 mph speed on special rollers beneath its wheels during the entire fair. At the fair's “Railroads on Parade" extravaganza, the S-1 met face to face with Henry Dreyfuss's 20th Century Limited, pitting against each other the two most dramatic, streamlined, steam locomotives in the world, and their two most famous industrial designers. In the Production and Distribution Zone was a focal exhibit designed by Egmont Arens that had a large, concave movie screen showing simple, everyday activities, such as ordering food in a shop, and then all around it were scenes of the far-flung businesses upon which this simple act depended. More interesting was a display showing boxes devoted to each of the main categories of consumer spending; food, clothing, housing, health, etc. The government decided on four income levels: "Subsistence," a family income of \$800 per year; "Maintenance, “\$2,000: “the Good Life," \$2,500; and “Luxury," \$5,000. The average of these categories was \$1.266, far below the “Good Life." In 2013 dollars, these arbitrary income levels would be \$13,400, \$33,500, \$41,900, \$83,900, and \$21,200, respectively.16 The claim in 1939 was that a third of the country makes less than “Subsistence," and nine-tenths, less than the “Good Life." In 2013, the median income, about \$51,413, would be considered well above the “Good Life” using 1939 standards. The focal exhibit for the Community Interests Zone, designed by Gilbert Rohde, made the point that people depend much more on each other than in the past. In a huge room with a semicircular wall, a series of scenes were arranged before it. One by one, each scene was lit up chronologically, and a narrator intoned, Scene 1, 1789: "No leisure, no time to play. Eight hours to sleep and sixteen to work." The last scene was 1939: “Eight hours' work, eight hours' rest, eight hours' leisure." The lights fade and a spotlight picks out the words, “For What?" An electric phonograph, according to Rohde, controlled the entire timing of the presentation.7 Rohde also designed the exhibit for the Home Furnishings pavilion. That same year, the Herman Miller Company, for which Rohde headed design, pioneered in the use of showrooms for controlled furniture selling in New York and Chicago. Russel Wright designed the focal exhibit for the Food Zone, which was by general agreement the most entertaining of the focal exhibits. The climax of the exhibit was a sixty-foot egg, inside of which was an avocado with five glowing jewels in its skin: a flock of lobsters flying inland from the sea, an aqueduct spilling roses into the desert, an eye blinking within a cave, and a can holding a clock racing backward. The jewels in the avocado symbolized carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins and minerals. The lobsters signified that inlanders now eat fresh seafood. The blinking eye reminded all of “man's victory over night blindness through vitamin A foods." The aqueduct meant that irrigation made wastelands flourish, and the backward clock illustrated that “canning had perpetuated harvest time."18 The amusement area of the fair had a carnival-like feel that included pornographic exhibits, which were ignored by many attendees in favor of the showmanship in the main areas, but still were quite popular. One of these was the Crystal Lassies show, claimed at the ticket booth to be “The Lovliest Dancing Girls at the Fair." None other than Norman Bel Geddes designed the show. A nude manikin stood on a pedestal outside, and behind her the words, "Inside she's real. 15¢." A single dancer gyrated within an octagonal structure whose inner surfaces were completely mirrored. Two spectators could stand at each facet of the octagon and peep through one-way mirrors at the many dancers evidently cavorting within. The dancer's costume varied at different shows, but at times she was completely naked. Bel Geddes was probably demonstrating that he still had his original skill as a dramatic set designer.19 Germany and China were the only major countries not represented in the Government Zone at the fair. Germany was probably not invited, and China was being invaded by Japan. However, Japan was among the 60 countries that participated, all providing an array of cultural offerings to fairgoers, many of whom had never traveled overseas. Due to its fall in 1938 to the Nazis, Czechoslovakia's pavilion was left unfinished. The international situation became much worse during the fair on September 1, when the Nazis invaded Poland, officially starting World War II. By September 3, Great Britain and France would declare war on Germany. The Soviet Union would sign a nonaggression agreement with Germany in August and on September would invade Poland from the East. The Polish pavilion would be closed when the fair reopened in 1940. On September 27, 1939, general admission to the fair was reduced to 50 cents, initially just for October, but that price would be continued for all of 1940. On October 31, the fair closed for the season under the dark clouds of war. Twenty-six million attended that first year, but the fair was \$23 million in the red. The fair had closed, but its influence would last for ten years, when many of its predictions of the future would become the reality. Meanwhile, the Depression had become a thing of the past, and business, including the design business, continued to grow. Its leaders, the original pioneers, were becoming household words, and they were writing books about design.
