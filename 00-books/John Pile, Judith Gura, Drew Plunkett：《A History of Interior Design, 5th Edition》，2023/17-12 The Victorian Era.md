The Victorian Era CHAPTER TWELVE ![](images/266eda31c451b270520f389d069615a5756ff616a1a398f253921e7d89ce39d1.jpg) A. W. N. Pugin, St. Giles Church, Cheadle, Staffordshire, England, 1846. For pious Victorians—and most were—Gothic Revival church interiors became the inspiration for the decoration of their homes. In 1815, Britain defeated Napoleonic France to end the Napoleonic Wars (1803–15) and became the dominant world power. Naval supremacy secured trading links to its empire and to the raw materials that fed the factories that drew country dwellers to its booming cities and towns. Factory work, however brutish, offered a fragile financial security that the uncertainties of country life could not. Industrialized Britain gave the rest of the world a model for post-agrarian society and political power and social influence began to shift from land-owning aristocrats to self-made captains of industry. With the rise of the middle class, the history of interior design, which had been about the aggrandizement of church, state and status quo, was shaped by individuals with aesthetic and social aspirations. In growing industrial cities and towns, the new economic migrants needed housing. Speculators seized the opportunity to build terraces or row houses and tenements appropriate to their occupants’ status and income. Exteriors were simple in the Georgian manner but, apart from rudimentary moldings, the new dwellings were empty shells—it was left to their occupants to decorate and furnish them. New wealth, however modest, meant that women who had worked long hours on family farms and in cottage industries became full-time housewives and responsible for the decoration of their homes. Decorating options offered to country dwellers by local artisans and passing peddlers were few but in the new conurbations, shops specializing in household furnishings appeared. Householders made their money in factories and, in a virtuous circle, spent it on factory products. The philosophizing economist Adam Smith declared at the beginning of the industrializing of production methods that “consumption is the sole end and purpose of all production” and a symbiotic relationship between consumers and manufacturers began to change the nature of what was produced and what was bought. Popular taste became prevailing taste. ## VICTORIAN STYLE In 1837, the eighteen-year-old Princess Victoria of Kent became queen. Her youth, gender, overt piety and enthusiasm for domesticity encouraged a reaction against Georgian decadence along with an enthusiasm for religious debate and practice. It was presumed that the success of the country and its empire resulted from Christian values. Indeed, The Secret of England’s Greatness (c. 1862–63), a painting by Thomas Jones Barker, depicts Queen Victoria presenting a kneeling African potentate with a Bible, which was deemed to be a handbook for civilized behavior and prosperity. ## Gothic Revival in Britain As political self-confidence grew, Britain became doubtful about the pagan Neoclassical architectural traditions borrowed from continental Europe. It wanted something indigenous to mark its supremacy. Gothic architecture had begun in twelfth-century France but Britain had its own variations. Extant examples were overwhelmingly of religious buildings and that further validated the change of style. Creative ingenuity adapted Gothic principles for the new building types such as railway stations, and monumental municipal and commercial buildings. The beginnings of Gothic Revival architecture and design had coincided with the beginnings of the Industrial Revolution. Horace Walpole (1717–97), a writer and art historian, designed Strawberry Hill House in Twickenham, London. Building began in 1749 and the exterior showed evidence of Gothic influences but they were most evident in the interior, particularly in the fan vaulting of the Long Gallery. Walpole, increasingly fascinated by medieval history, published the first Gothic novel, The Castle of Otranto, in 1764. It prompted a genre, including Mary Shelley’s Frankenstein in 1818 and Bram Stoker’s Dracula in 1897. Edgar Allan Poe and Henry James wrote Gothic horror novels in the United States. In 1796, William Beckford (1760–1844), another writer and the author of the Gothic novel Vathek (1786), commissioned James Wyatt (1746–1813) to design Fonthill Abbey, an enormous country house on Salisbury Plain in Wiltshire (12.2). It was a fanciful assemblage of battlements, pinnacles and towers with vast Gothic halls and a 276-feet (84-m) high tower above an octagonal vaulted chamber, a stage set in which the romance and dramas of medieval life could be relished. Built largely in wood and stucco, it collapsed in a windstorm in 1825 and appropriately became a romantic ruin. ![](images/2241946582a680b2d16d8a5cc7acaa8d803cf55add65e1adcc1dca38ff755d74.jpg) 12.2 James Wyatt, south end of St. Michael’s Gallery, Fonthill Abbey, Wiltshire England, from 1795. This engraving, of 1823, shows the richness of detail achieved in the wood and stucco construction, the plaster simulation of fan vaulting, the stained glass and the tracery. However, the proportions, the windows with clear glass and transom, and the shallow arch suggest that there was no serious intention to replicate a Gothic interior. A young architect and author, Augustus Welby Northmore Pugin (1812–52), took Gothic architecture more seriously and wrote Contrasts (1836), The True Principles of Pointed or Christian Architecture (1841), and further polemics in which illustrations made comparisons between classical and Gothic styles of architecture, always to the disadvantage of the former. The intensity of Pugin’s attack and his moralistic tone set off the Battle of the Styles, in which Greek and Gothic revivalists vehemently aired their conflicting opinions. Most architects and designers were happy to work in whatever style clients might request. However, Pugin, the purist, argued against the trivial interpretations of Walpole and Beckford, for whom Gothic had been about romantic escapism, and made his case on moral and philosophical grounds. Pugin and his camp prevailed. Gothic Revival was confirmed as the new national style when a fire destroyed the Houses of Parliament in 1834 and Charles Barry (1795–1860) won a competition to design a replacement. He was a Neoclassicist but was encouraged, it is said by Queen Victoria, to elevate in a Neo-Gothic style. Pugin, who had made the drawings for Barry’s entry, was given responsibility for designing the interiors and comprehensively decorated every surface with color, pattern and carving, and topped every opening with a pointed arch (12.3 and 12.4). ![](images/52c02f184cd7a976e01ffad7ea0a841016ad6dd6867f1a4310d8aafffa398c01.jpg) ## 12.3 A. W. N. Pugin, New Palace of Westminster (Houses of Parliament), House of Lords, London, 1836–60. A richness of color and carving surpassed Gothic precedents and banished the comparative restraint of the Regency period. It was an exuberant expression of Britain’s emergence as the dominant world power of the nineteenth century. ![](images/1e8e2b7d8591c1fee6465456f0b02a1838304ecf7fea1631138173b4c9f569d3.jpg) Charles Barry and A. The great chamber with its traceried stained glass, the arched windows and paneled ceiling are convincing replications of Gothic originals. Pugin established his own practice and was prolific, as only a high-minded Victorian could be. He brought an intensity of Gothic decoration to the forty churches (12.1) and six cathedrals he designed before he died at the age of forty. He poured out Gothic Revival designs for furniture, metalwork, ceramics, stained glass, embroideries and wallpapers for his buildings and for manufacturers who recognized the growing popularity of all things Gothic. He made time to address the social problems that were byproducts of industrialization and, in 1841, published Contrasted Residences of the Poor. In a pair of drawings, he contrasted bleak, nineteenth-century workhouses, their meager food, harsh discipline, and paupers’ graves, with the glories of a medieval abbey. He proposed that the latter provided accommodation and spiritual guidance, nourishing food, and the dignity of Christian burial. Gothic values and Gothic style could, he asserted, solve the problems of industrialization. When Gothic Revival churches were built precisely to architects’ instructions, they often lacked the vitality that came from slow building by generations of craftsworkers, each making their idiosyncratic contribution. The work of William Butterfield (1814–1900) was not as stylistically correct as Pugin’s but its divergences make it interesting. His church, All Saints (1849–59) in London, has patterned bands of differently hued red bricks, glazed brick tiles, and various marbles laid in geometric patterns. It has something of the vigor of Victorian buildings in which Gothic devices are reinterpreted for secular purposes (12.5). ![](images/df05704b837dc6597e5978bd93f9ecd039d748c4b4e74e80802119e1d2a9b90e.jpg) William Butterfield, All Saints, Margaret Street, London, 1849–59. Butterfield’s work has an energy and originality that goes beyond mere conscientious replication. The great arches and buttressing half arches have a raw strength that is counterpointed by the colorful wall and floor tiles typical of many Victorian interiors. The British government was worried that revolution would incubate in factories and slums. So, to strengthen the restraining hand of religion, it financed the building and refurbishment of 2,859 churches between 1841 and 1870. Not attending church risked social exclusion at higher social levels and unemployment at the lowest. Once food and shelter have been secured, human instinct has been to accumulate and decorate—to paint their caves. If the restraint of the Regency interior had contrasted with the period’s social decadence then Victorians’ domestic overindulgences contrasted with their social restraints. Gothic Revival church interiors sanctioned congregants’ natural impulse to decorate and the Victorian interior became synonymous with excess. Inspired by the spectacles of church architecture and fired by an instinct to match and better the homes of their neighbors, Victorian householders went shopping. ## Shopping Shopping took its first steps to becoming a leisure activity when retailers recognized the value of treating customers to spectacle. It began in 1838 in Paris with the towering central hall of Le Bon Marché, which provided entertainments for children and a reading room for men content to leave their wives to enjoy the rigors of buying (12.6). The Bon Marché name was subsequently borrowed by imitators offering varying degrees of splendor but the more ambitious borrowed the idea of spectacle. In 1855, Robert Wylie and William Lochhead offered the citizens of Glasgow a hall, 200 feet (61 m) long and 70 feet (21 m) high, which was so popular that they issued free tickets to control the numbers of Saturday crowds. Whiteleys in London had a central hall 300 feet (91.5 m) long. In 1875, Liberty & Co., designed in Tudor style, grouped its sales areas around a five-story void (12.7). The specialist London furniture store, Gillows carried a collection of historical furniture that, it claimed, offered an educational experience better than that of any museum. Its workshop reproduced designs from the pattern books of the distinguished eighteenth-century designers, Chippendale, Hepplewhite and Sheraton. It became a byword for good taste, mentioned in novels by Jane Austen and William Makepeace Thackeray, and an operetta by Gilbert and Sullivan. Shopping could indeed be educational. In the German writer Thomas Mann’s novel Confessions of Felix Krull (1954), the young Krull studied the windows of luxury stores to learn the details of taste that would ease his access to the layers of society on which he aimed to prey. ![](images/d98eac2235b5fb709f5a551f40ea54c9c45695f7ba14c4c41b9a4578727fd54f.jpg) 12.6 Louis-Charles Boileau and Gustave Eiffel, Le Bon Marché, Paris, 1876. Grand stairways lead to the store’s upper level. The slim iron structure allows views of the spectacul ar central aspace and supports the glass roof. Customers found the store a source of entertainment. ![](images/a59f3297494041937d2ab3ac9c86f25a38ebc9c33b68c277f4c31000be14811c.jpg) Edwin T. Hall and Edwin S. Hall, Liberty & Co., London, 1875. Liberty & Co. began by selling Japanese and Indian fabrics and objets d’art. The timber structure of the central atrium suggested the medieval values of the Arts and Crafts movement. Irish writer Oscar Wilde called it “the chosen resort of the artistic shopper.” As stores became more specialist, so the customers who were increasingly eager to take advice became “clients” but commercial prudence ensured that advice did not stray far from popular taste. In 1873, the House Furnisher magazine suggested that the “true upholsterer” was “as trusted an agent in many homes as are the family lawyer and doctor.” Stores learned how to address their markets; even shoppers with a Victorian’s thirst for ornament seemed to sense that a sparse window display, with no indication of prices, denoted quality and good taste. Stores at the cheap end of the market packed their windows and flaunted low prices. In London, a secondhand furniture market had emerged in the eighteenth century and grown throughout the nineteenth. Tudor pieces were popular; more readily available than genuine Gothic, they represented another part of England’s heritage. Some stores opened antique sections. Harrods had a gallery for antique, secondhand, and contemporary furniture. Heal and Son, which would become a specialist in modern twentieth-century furniture, began by combining antique furniture and contemporary fabrics. Since women were responsible for the decoration of their homes, it might be argued that the Victorian domestic interior is a feminine creation but The Gentleman’s House, a guide to the strategies and tactics of home decoration published in 1865, advised: the dining room should have an air of “masculine importance” and the drawing room should be “entirely ladylike.” The paterfamilias entertained his peers in the dining room and the materfamilias entertained hers in the drawing room (12.8). ![](images/80cf7a42a6e6786020f9f8b4e8fba2f0edc8daab5739398fe4a794fc16382d6f.jpg) Richard Norman Shaw, drawing room, Swan House, Chelsea Embankment, London, 1876. A Victorian middle-class interior with antiquarian elements—Queen Anne chairs and a Georgian spinning wheel (left). New pieces show early evidence of the emerging reaction against the decorative excesses of factory-made objects in favor of the handmade. ## A Great Exhibition In 1851, Britain had chosen to demonstrate the superiority of its industries and the breadth and wealth of its empire with The Great Exhibition of the Works of Industry of All Nations, or Great Exhibition. It did so confident that its exhibits would surpass those of any other country rash enough to take part. The exhibition building became known as the “Crystal Palace” (12.9) and was an enormous prefabricated cast-iron and glass structure designed by Joseph Paxton (1803– 65), a pioneering designer of greenhouses for aristocrats and municipalities. With its barrelvaulted roof, it was evidently not Gothic Revival and, to Victorians, it seemed starkly utilitarian. To modern eyes, drawings of its molded iron constructional details illustrate how far decoration was considered an integral component in any building, even a work of innovative engineering. ![](images/f18d91ee2c0b9f4fde41328e6d0365c7fbd811e48ca02cb880dabfda3c31de54.jpg) Joseph Paxton, Crystal Palace, London, 1851. This prefabricated cast-iron framed, glazed building, seen in a contemporary lithograph, housed the Great Exhibition, the showcase of Victorian prosperity and taste. The simplicity of the iron structure, glass walls and roof contrast with the elaborately decorated goods and classical statuary on display. The large tree predated the building and remained after it was dismantled. Pugin could not approve of the non-Gothic structure but consented to design the Medieval Court (12.10) exhibit and furnished it with objects in Gothic Revival style to his own design. Although a philosophical medievalist, Pugin happily took advantage of manufacturing innovations. Artifacts for his ecclesiastical interiors were assembled from generic components, produced to his design by his preferred metalworkers—Victorians were pragmatic. A third of the British population visited the exhibition and were further convinced of their country’s supremacy by the audacity of Paxton’s building and the machinery it contained. ![](images/a2dfe322c3338746a96d40b7fd3cbbc002f275dc9eb23bafce9a968d1123524f.jpg) A. W. N. Pugin, the Medieval Court, the Great Exhibition, Crystal Palace, London, 1851. Whatever doubts he had about the Crystal Palace’s structure, Pugin overcame them to make his case for Gothic virtues at the Great Exhibition The importance for the economy of the burgeoning interiors market was recognized by government. Design schools were established in industrial centers to support local manufacturers. In 1856, the architect, designer, and theorist Owen Jones (1809–74) produced his The Grammar of Ornament (12.12), a compendium of decorative motifs from across the world adapted for the convenience of any manufacturers who cared to use them. Critics and theorists, inevitably, considered that the glory of Gothic was being compromised as manufacturers competed to flood the market. ![](images/e0fcbccca7044ce776321280b9a8067dba77b3fc44f1ed3c67dcc9e76fddcb26.jpg) Owen Jones, The Grammar of Ornament, 1856. One of the pages dealing with Egyptian ornament—original motifs are simplified and colored, made suitable for decorating industrial products, particularly fabrics and ceramics. The acceptance that the practical should be overlaid by decoration was demonstrated in Queen Victoria’s private railroad car, which was opulently upholstered and trimmed with Neo- Gothic woodwork. Fringed and tasseled curtains shut out evidence that Her Majesty was speeding along behind a locomotive that was designed for maximum operating efficiency and decorated by no more than a few painted stripes (12.11). The dressing up of the utilitarian is often derided as a denial of reality but in 1979 Lance Wright, editor of the British magazine Designers Journal, wondered if it “might be possible ‘to accept the advantages of industrial production without feeling any need to express the thing as itself’ as the Victorians had done.” 12.11 ![](images/27ac0c05609caa187706c379291fe754b7f97d23af319a7fac13f25b9a707091.jpg) Queen Victoria’s Royal Saloon (railroad car), 1869. The major Victorian technological advance of the railroads came face to face with Victorian taste in interior design. In the queen’s private railroad car, surfaces are padded and quilted, draperies are fringed, furniture is elaborately carved and sumptuously upholstered, and lamp shades are fringed. ## Aristocrats and Plutocrats Aristocrats were not immune to Gothic Revival. Despite the rise of the industrial barons, they still dominated politics, had, generally, lost none of their wealth, and embraced the style when building new. The most enthusiastic was John Patrick Crichton-Stuart, 3rd Marquess of Bute, who was rich from owning the port of Cardiff that exported coal from the mines of South Wales, which he also owned. He appointed the architect William Burgess (1827–81) to create Cardiff Castle and Castell Coch, a summer residence in a wooded landscape, a few miles outside the city. Work began on the former in 1868 and the latter in 1875. Both were built on and incorporated the remains of small Norman forts but were examples of Gothic Revival although details were filtered through nineteenth-century processes and perceptions. When the Marquess’s principal home, Mount Stuart House, on the Isle of Bute, Scotland, burnt down in 1877, he employed Robert Rowand Anderson (1834–1921) to design a replacement (12.14). Burgess designed the oratory. Mount Stuart was on a grander scale than the Welsh buildings and incorporated the world’s first domestic heated swimming pool. While detail remains Gothic-inspired, the massing, with its strong horizontal emphasis, was not. In Ireland, Edward William Godwin (1833–86) made extensive examinations of Irish castles before designing Dromore Castle in County Limerick for the Earl of Limerick (12.15). However, when completed in 1874, the finished building with its turrets, tall chimneys and gables bore more resemblance to castles on the Rhine than Ireland’s modest precedents. Not all aristocrats agreed on the merits of Gothic. When building Castle Ward in Northern Ireland, between 1761 and 1767, Bernard Ward, Lord Bangor, had indulged the enthusiasm of his wife, Lady Anne Magill, by splitting the house along its long axis. Half, on the pedimented entrance side, remained Palladian and the other was adapted to satisfy her yearning for the Gothic. ## Other British Revivalist Styles Other revivalist styles found favor. Wealthy industrials, merchants and bankers aspired to have houses that matched those of the nobility but they took the great, non-aristocratic Tudor, Jacobean, and Carolean houses as models. Half-timbered gables, battlements, and clock towers were popular. Ruins were sometimes incorporated into new structures to suggest pedigree. Architects and decorators were knowledgeable about historical styles although, as with the murals and carvings in Cardiff Castle and Castel Coch, prevailing stylistic predilections asserted themselves and there were novel activities and social mechanisms to be accommodated. The communal life of the medieval great halls was superseded by drawing rooms, dining rooms, libraries, batteries of guest bedrooms, and servants’ wings. The most imposing example of the genre was Cragside near Rothbury, Northumberland, built for the industrialist William Armstrong, an armaments manufacturer and amateur scientist whose interests included hydraulics and electricity. He had been seduced by a potential building site on the side of a moorland crag and had a house with eight bedrooms, a modest “shooting box,” designed by an unknown hand and completed in the mid-1860s. It was well built, but Armstrong wanted something grander, a place to entertain the potentates and military men who were his customers, so he employed Richard Norman Shaw (1831–1912) to extend the box and begin its slow but spectacular evolution. A narrow ledge was dynamited to accommodate successive additions that were stacked on the cliffside like an Italian hillside village but the architectural language is undeniably English and Tudor, a collage of half-timbered gables and towers. Armstrong required that the shooting box be retained as the core of the extended house so the interior meandered as it became grander. Most reception rooms and their accompanying service rooms were on the ground floor apart from the extension built for a visit by the Prince of Wales (later Edward VII) in 1884. Royalty traditionally announced its intention to visit well in advance and hosts usually felt the obligation to impress. The Cragside library of 1872, with oak half-height panels and packed with heavy dark wooden furniture, is generally considered to be Shaw’s finest interior (12.16). Cragside was designed and built between 1869 and 1884. Both client and architect, and the designers they engaged, were aware of the growing opposition in aesthetic circles to the decorative excesses of the Victorian interior. The scale of the building militated against delicate domesticity. As Armstrong’s art collection grew, so his museum became his gallery, remodeled as a processional route to the drawing room and its huge marble inglenook fireplace (12.17) designed by William Richard Lethaby (1857–1931), Shaw’s assistant, who would go on to pioneer the preservation of ancient buildings (12.18). Armstrong introduced water-generated electricity to power lighting and early examples of household appliances such as a vacuum cleaner, a washing machine, and dishwasher. John Norton (1823–1904) remodeled Elveden in Suffolk around 1870 in a potpourri of styles. It was owned by the Maharajah Duleep Singh, who had Norton replace the Georgian classical exterior with something more ornately Italianate and recreate the interiors in the Indian Mughal style. The room was remodeled in what was considered to be an Indian style, in tribute to its previous owner, an Indian maharajah. ## UNITED STATES: VICTORIAN VARIATIONS Just as Britain moved on from Regency Neoclassicism, so there was reluctance in the United States to continue with the Greek Revivalism that had symbolized the country’s republican independence. As in England, taste was swayed by romantic Gothic novels and reproductions of work by the English Pre-Raphaelite painters, who were themselves inspired by medieval values and techniques. It is a reflection of Britain’s nineteenth-century pre-eminence and its totemic queen that architecture and design of the period in the United States is described as “Victorian.” The emancipated thirteen states continued to share British tastes in matters of interior decoration and items imported from Britain and Europe were particularly valued. Where these were unobtainable or too expensive, Americans improvised. Visiting Britons, perhaps still annoyed by their defeat in the American Revolutionary War (1775–83), were, like Fanny Trollope in her Domestic Manners of the Americas (1832), not always appreciative of their efforts. Yet American energy and ambitions accelerated the development of homegrown industries. In addition, a drift from country to town and city saw rural families become urban, farmers and farmers’ sons become business owners, managers and professionals. Prosperous urbanites, like those in Britain, were able and willing to improve their homes and, as the market grew, the flow of imports from Europe increased. In return, the New World provided fast-sailing Yankee Clipper ships to speed up ocean trade and McCormick reapers to replace farmhands who had gone to work in factories. ## Gothic Revival Richard Upjohn (1802–78) was born and trained as a cabinetmaker in England and his Trinity Church (1839–46) on Wall Street in New York is a convincing version of an English Gothic parish church (12.20). Its vaulted nave, stained glass, and decorative detail gave Americans their first serious experience of Gothic. Other specialists in Gothic design emerged. In 1843, James Renwick (1818–95) won a competition with a Gothic proposal to design Grace Church on Broadway in New York, a rival to Trinity Church, with another accurate recreation of an English Gothic church. Renwick’s most important Gothic Revival work, St. Patrick’s Cathedral in New York, was completed in 1878. This was modeled on French Gothic examples, complete with cruciform-plan aisles and ambulatory, clerestory and stained glass. The vaulting appears to be stone but is papiermâché and the flying buttresses needed to resist the thrust of stone vaulting were omitted. ![](images/e8f6914f26cf57c28eb66c7ebd46285b5df7b1e74f23d8bdd07fd039ec9f2cf5.jpg) Richard Upjohn, Trinity Church, New York, 1846. A carefully detailed impression of an English medieval parish church. Upjohn had wanted to design a church with a simple timber roof but his clients wanted vaulting and papier-mâché was used to imitate stone. The window tracery and stained glass adds conviction to the illusion. As in Britain, Gothic Revival quickly spread across architecture and interior design. Renwick designed the main building of the Smithsonian Institute (1844–46) in Washington, D.C., which also takes inspiration from medieval examples, with a Romanesque and Norman exterior and a Gothic interior. The firm of Town and Davis, alert to the shift in popular taste, abandoned Greek Revival and Alexander Jackson Davis became particularly enthusiastic about Gothic Revival. In Lyndhurst, a large house in Tarrytown, New York overlooking the Hudson River (1838–65) he applied Gothic elements, including an impressive tower (12.21). The plan was originally symmetrical but when he enlarged it in 1864 it became picturesquely asymmetric. Most rooms are filled with Gothic details: plaster ceiling ribs that suggested vaulting, pointed windows with stone tracery and stained glass, and much carved ornamental detail. The combined billiard room and art gallery had a wooden roof structure that suggested a baronial hall and Davis designed Gothicstyle furniture including wheelback chairs with carved backs that suggested the tracery of rose windows, a dining table with Gothic carving, and beds with massive, pointed arch heads and footboards. ![](images/0d72eb63537a37fcbc4c4337ebdb554753b6688e8143b64081d7d9319fb2dede.jpg) Town and Davis, Lyndhurst, Tarrytown, New York, 1838–65. The interiors add conviction to the Gothic Revival exterior. Pointed arches, paneling, tracery and crockets, and the furniture, all executed in wood, complement the stained glass. Ralph Adams Cram (1863–1942) was, as Pugin had been, a protagonist for the virtues of Gothic design and a skilled practitioner in the style. In his book Church Building (1901), he argued for the virtues of medieval English Gothic, illustrated examples of excellence, and criticized what he considered to be poor work. His design for All Saints (1891) in Dorchester, Massachusetts, is an accurate recreation of a typical English parish church. Cram and his firm, Cram, Goodhue and Ferguson, became successful designers of Gothic churches and Tudor Gothic groupings for college campuses. The term “Collegiate Gothic” came into use to describe such work. They designed a number of buildings for Princeton University, including interiors for the refectory and chapel. The firm’s large St. Thomas Church (1906–13) in New York has an impressive interior, with stone vaulting, stained glass, and a tall sculptured reredos that combines details from many Gothic precedents. The landscape gardener, Andrew Jackson Downing (1815–52) wrote Cottage Residences (1842) and The Architecture of Country Houses (1850) with engravings showing plans and perspectives of houses in a range of sizes and variety of styles, including a simplified version of Gothic suitable for wooden construction. Carpenter Gothic was produced by local builders, who used a scroll saw to cut pointed arch forms in wood. Exterior walls were given vertical emphasis with board and batten sidings, pointed arch windows often with leaded glass inserts, elaborately carved eaves, and pointed window heads dignified buildings of local importance such as churches and railway stations. They became as evocative of small town, rural America as the dungarees and pitchfork in Grant Wood’s painting American Gothic (1930). There were other indigenous variations. Italianate drew on Northern Italian sources and was distinguished by shallow-hipped roofs, porches, loggias, and sometimes a small tower, often of wooden construction with wooden columns and carvings. Mansardic, sometimes called “General Grant style,” was inspired by the almost vertical French Mansard roof, generally visible from the street, with cast iron or wooden decorative trim on the lower floors. It was popular for public buildings in bigger towns. Queen Anne or Queen Anne Revival was influenced by English Arts and Crafts developments and involved asymmetrical elements, such as bay windows. Decoration was provided by different brick types, stained glass, and terracotta moldings. ## American Homes The newly rich, manufacturers, entrepreneurs, and speculators, built big houses and packed them with objects, often in a random confection of styles. Hard-earned wealth was considered worth flaunting. Samuel Colt, inventor of the revolver, built a large house in a vaguely Italian manner and embellished it with Moorish domes. The landscape painter Frederick E. Church, assisted by the English architect and landscape designer Calvert Vaux (1824–95), designed his own house in what he considered to be a Persian style. In 1857, Vaux published the Villas and Cottages pattern book, which offered illustrations of external and internal ornamental detail for thirty-nine houses, from modest cottages to enormous mansions. The American farmhouse moved from symmetry and classical detail toward picturesque irregular plans and the plain homes of early settlers began to fill with the decorative indulgences of the affluent East Coast. In industrial centers, houses for workers were arranged in rows: twinned or semidetached houses were for the better paid, while larger, single-family houses on their own plots in the manner of modern suburbia were for managers. Such houses, whether for sale or rent, were built by speculators who understood the importance of decoration. Books and manuals offered factory-made ornamental details to prettify interiors. In comparatively modest houses, elaborately carved and upholstered furniture stood on boldly patterned carpets. Factory-made objects—cast-iron stoves and harmoniums (12.22)—came ready-encrusted with ornament. In expensive dwellings, elaborate paneling, mantelpieces, stair rails, and moldings were enriched further by strongly patterned wallpapers and fabrics. Oil lamps, the primary source of artificial light, had intricate brass bases and colored glass shades. For Victorians, empty spaces cried out to be filled and the “what-not,” an open-shelved display unit, provided extra space for ornaments. Pictures and prints in intricately carved and gilded frames offered sentimental depictions of children and landscapes. Small plaster figures by specialist modelers such as John Rogers (1829– 1904) depicted scenes of love and loss. ![](images/ddb56b282bee1bf361b3a70921f90e282a1bbcd2d9b6dfe356ea280901283239.jpg) Mason and Hamlin, harmonium, 1876. Even the convention of the rectangular room fell victim to gratuitous complication, which prompted a short-lived fad for octagonal houses. The eight sides of the Armour-Stiner House (1860) in Irvington, New York, were skirted by a porch with florid cast-iron columns and railings and a dome topped by a cupola, topped by a spire. Octagonal plans led inevitably to strange little triangular rooms, expediently designated as libraries and music rooms. In the larger cities, even for the better off, row houses (12.23) replaced detached houses on their own plots. The uniformity and monumentality of New York and Boston’s brownstones and Philadelphia and Baltimore’s brick rows established a civic structure that, when planted with trees, dignified urban living. Houses began to benefit from technical innovations: central heating (usually by ducted warm air), gas lighting, bathrooms and kitchens with piped hot running water, coal, and later, gas cooking ranges, and iceboxes. Storage cupboards and dressing spaces next to bedrooms, often with wash basins and running water, became staples. Better houses had built-in marble surfaces and ornamental stairs climbed through three or four floors. Servants moved unseen through narrow backstairs. Doors and windows were tall and narrow to emphasize ceiling height but daylight was restricted by heavy drapes and permanently drawn net curtains. Exhibits included a working example of a steam engine big enough to power a city’s pumping station and delicate decorative objects for the home. A small organ, suitable for a Victorian parlor, was encrusted with inlays and carvings but described by a contemporary critic, Walter Smith, as “free from all the abortions in the shape of ornament with which many pretentious instruments are disfigured”. Excess is in the eye of the beholder and determined at the time of the beholding. The unpretentious organ and much of the furniture exhibited would have been classified as Eastlake, after Charles Locke Eastlake (1836–1906), an English architect influenced by the growing reaction in English aesthetes against what was seen as over-decorated factory-made products. Exterior ornament was usually simple but building forms were often complex, with gables, projecting wings, porches, dormer windows, rounded bays, turrets, and towers. Internally, wooden paneling, grand fireplaces, and nooks with built-in sofas replicated the cozy complexities of English Queen Anne style. A few hotels, casinos and clubhouses used Shingle techniques, and acquired informal charm. ## Adirondack As rail travel improved, mountainous landscape and cooler weather offered alternatives to summers in the city. The Adirondacks were accessible from East Coast cities and a romantic pastiche of frontier living evolved with cabins and lodges as summer homes for hunters and fishers. Toward the end of the century, the rusticity of the first simple dwellings evolved to become the Adirondack style. Oversized, rough stone fireplaces dominated rooms, timber-clad walls were left to find their natural color, rugs and cloth wall hangings, oil lamps and hunting trophies added to the illusion (12.25). Furniture often incorporated tree branches with bark intact for added rusticity. ![](images/e4cd2a37b25566c87210d4c83c40bd3f9d84eabe3e5f3ac685b363f15d232f8f.jpg) 12.25 Camp Cedars, Forked Lake, Adirondacks, New York, 1886. The “camps” built for summer-vacation houses in the mountains were often luxurious but flaunted faux rusticity with rough stone fireplaces and furniture of rough-hewn timbers. The lanterns, fans, and curios were more typical of Victorian taste. ## Shaker Shakers emigrated from England in 1774 to escape religious persecution. They founded agricultural communities and members shared property and work in a simple form of communism. Large dwellings provided separate accommodation for males and females. The communities chose to isolate themselves from neighboring settlements, constructed their own buildings and, as far as they could produced what they needed from agriculture and in their workshops. Their religious beliefs forbade “worldly” ostentation and favored efficient application of human effort, which led them to produce objects of great simplicity and functional excellence. By 1830, they had evolved their essentials and thereafter their dwellings and objects conformed to those meticulous standards. Their interiors had white walls and dark wooden floors, benches, chairs, tables, cupboards and cabinets, were enriched by subtle proportions and detail (12.26). Banks of built-in drawers provided storage and pegs on high wooden rails carried cloaks, hats and chairs, which made floor cleaning more efficient. The regular and repetitious organization of drawers provided pattern that satisfied, at a fundamental level, the human need for decoration. ![](images/02969022d51f45a77f5ebcdc61c96dbba81b1a3a3ab10e289f7a9233f4487aeb.jpg) Shaker Village, Hancock, New York, c. 1820. The bedroom of an elder or eldress in the communal residence contains a rope bed and wooden washstand. The wood burning stove incorporates a Shaker invention, an upper smoke box that extracts extra heat from chimney gases. The wooden trim is painted in a tone the Shakers called “Heavenly Blue.” Shakers advanced no aesthetic theory but achieved collective excellence, perhaps by subordinating the egos that drive designers who are less spiritual. They offered some products for sale, ladderback chairs, rocking chairs and side chairs, all assembled from simple wooden components and these achieved surprising popularity given their divergence from the norms of Victorian taste, a popularity that contrasts with the failure of the later Arts and Crafts movement in England to find a substantial market. Arts and Crafts artifacts were perhaps too much of an intellectual construct, too much the product of individual activists with individual perceptions, and Arts and Crafts designers handed over their designs to the craftsworkers who would make them. It has been suggested that designers who did not make could not properly understand the potential of the materials they specified. Shakers made and understood. The contradictions and problems of the Victorian era did not go unnoticed, and eventually movements dedicated to reform emerged.
