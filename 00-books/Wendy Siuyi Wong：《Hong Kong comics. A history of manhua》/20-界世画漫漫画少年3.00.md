Manhua by Lee Dak-hong, 1984   
Art/Script: Lee Dak-hong   
This collection of more than 156 cartoons by Lee Dakhong was the artist's first manhua publication. His next collection was called Little Funny Booklet and was very similar to this one. 203-204.   
Onion Head Manhua Series, 1984   
Art/Script: Lam Chun-keung   
This series was first published in Ming Pao Weekly and was a kind of combination of comics and poetry. The main character had an onion-shaped head and liked to talk to himself. 205.   
The Four Treasures of Dragon Gate, 1986   
Art: Cai Zhi-zhong   
The title of T’he Four Treasures of Dragon Gate, a collection of the artist's early works, refers to four different characters. 206.   
Manhua Zhuang-zi: Natural Sound of a Common Artemisia, 1986   
Art: Cai Zhi-zhong

In this illustrated version of the ancient Chinese work of philosophy Zhuang-zi, appealingly drawn characters accompany the concise original text. Because it was so easy to read and provided a new approach to classic Chinese philosophy, this comic received a great deal of attention and eventually broke sales records in both Hong Kong and Taiwan. A series of manhua based on ancient Chinese philosophy and literary works followed, along with English versions published in Singapore.

Comic World Bimonthly, 1987   
Editor: Seung-gun Siu-wai   
This imitation of Yuk-long Manhua lacked the original’s sophistication. Its main strip was The Legend of Siu-wai, drawn by the editor. 208.   
Comic Pig, 1988   
Editor: Wong Kwok-hing   
Wong Kwok-hing, who already had a reputation for humorous cartoons, provided most of the comic strips and columns for this publication. In addition to comics, Comic Pig included entertainment news. 209–210.   
Comics for City Boy, 1988   
Editor: Hui King-sam   
This serial was another magazine started by younger manhua artists. It primarily covered Japanese popular culture and was aimed at young readers.
