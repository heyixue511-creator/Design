During the years 1905-14, a great deal of attention was given by supply engineeers to the potential of domestic demand and its likely effects upon the load factor, and to the development of appliances, without which there could be no domestic consumption of electricity. Cooking and space heating, both heavy consumers of current, were thought to provide the most promising means of building up domestic load. Engineers who believed in the future of domestic demand (and by no means all of them did) recognised that to attract consumers to electricity, cookers and heaters needed to be reasonably efficient and, above all, to demonstrate a superiority over gas.\* Anticipating a rapid growth in domestic consumption, a number of entrepreneurs began to make electrical appliances in the years before 1914. Most of the appliances were produced by handcraftsmanship, and as there were many manufacturers and few domestic consumers, it is likely that only minimal quantities were made of any design.
