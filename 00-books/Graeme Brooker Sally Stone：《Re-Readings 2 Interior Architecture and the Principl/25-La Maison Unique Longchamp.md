Floor planes can be used to separate as well as link interior spaces. In order to do this they may sometimes have to confound expectation and become shifting landscapes of material and movement, working hard to create tangible and visual connections inside diffi cult or unusually conceived existing interior contexts.

After the success of the Zip Bag, a bestselling luxury bag for the fashion brand Longchamp – which was made from a long run of Zip that allowed it to double or half its size by unfastening it – Thomas Heatherwick was commissioned to design the new fl agship store for the same company in New York. The challenging site offered very little street impact, with virtually no shop window presence – considered a vital and valuable component in successful retail design. Instead, all of the available retail zones were to be housed in spaces on the upper two fl oors of the building. Therefore, a radical solution was needed to ensure shoppers were intrigued enough to enter the store and journey to the upper levels of the space – a notoriously diffi cult proposition in store design. Heatherwick decided to entice customers to the upper fl oors of the building in two ways: using daylight and by creating a fl owing, fl uid fl oor that would blur distinctions between what was fl oor and wall plane in the three levels of shopping space.

The fi rst element to consider was the street presence in the form of a new shop window. But in order to fl ood the interior space with daylight, Heatherwick inverted the idea of a shop window as a street front façade. He opened up the roof and created a new rooftop ‘shop window’. The glazed opening still acted as a viewing device but, instead of contemplating the goods in a street level window, the new aperture bathed the fl oors in natural light, animating the interior and drawing the customers’ view through the space, up into the depths of the store.

The second element designed to counteract any reluctance to move upstairs and through the store was the insertion of a new horizontal and vertical fl oor stair. Drawing inspiration from the Zip Bag and its complex structural realisation, a fl uid landscape of ribbon-like sheets collapsed distinctions between the different levels. It was conceived to entice the customer inside and invite them to ascend through the three levels, culminating at the new

Each ribbon, weighing over 60 tonnes, was made from a 2.5cm thick steel plate, welded into position on site and inlaid with strips of coloured rubber, ensuring a non-slip surface on each tread. In order to ensure complete compliance with building regulations, balustrades guaranteed customer safety. In order to complement the sculptural qualities of the element, specially treated glazed panels were attached to a series of upright poles on the ribbons. These were heated in a specially made ‘toaster’, making them pliant, drape like a fabric, and form unique gravitygenerated forms derived from their own weight. These were left to harden before being attached to the uprights of the rails. The effect was of a set of billowing transparent sheets, set against the fl owing waterfall of the curved steel streamers, as they poured through the space. To complete the sinuous qualities of the fl oor, a set of display stands, with thin steel legs supporting a platform, were attached to the steel edges of the ribbons with magnetic feet. The bags displayed look as if they are enjoying a rollercoaster ride down through the shop and cascading through the different fl oor levels of the interior space.
