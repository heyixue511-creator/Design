This special quality ofTuer's publications can be seen most vividly in the famous booklets.in chapbook style published during the 188os, illustrated with peculiar (but effective) woodcuts by Joseph Crawhall and set in type that appears to have been deliberately battered.²4 For many English readers in the final decades of the last century, Tuer's name was synonymous with old-style printing, which had by then become extremely fashionable. Even in Tuer's own journal, writers occasionally expressed misgivings about the way in which‘old style' seemed to lead so often to an unhappy display of meretricious virtuosity in typography and spelling. Commenting on Ye Printers’ Blotter: A Votarie of ye Wm. Caxton & ye Bn. Franklin, privately printed atween times' by Edwin C. Bell of Titusville, Pennsylvania, a contributor to the Paper and Printing Trades Annual (June 188o) observed that it has evidently been projected, and its leading ideas copied, from Mr. S. L. Nussey's Ye Quayut, published in this country, and it is only to be regretted that the imitation is not typographically more complete - for Mr. Bell, although evidently smitten with the idea of following the old-style, lacks the material, or more probably the necessary knowledge to carry it out, as his production is full of the grossest inconsistencies, literals, and bad imitations of early English spelling. . . . He is fond of the long f,' but in every case makes an f’ do duty, and worse than that he uses it for finals, as for instance in the word ‘news' in his opening address, which he spells‘newf’ — two mistakes in a word of four letters.' It was no doubt easier to criticize the amateurish blunders of a printer across the ocean, but in fact the English landscape was also littered with long ‘s's (frequently misplaced) and ubiquitous ye's. Quaintness had become a typographical disease. In The Art of Illustration (1 894), Henry Blackburn complained bitterly about the fraudulence of imitative modern books in which process engravings masqueraded as woodcuts, machine-made papers with deckle edges pretended to be the product of hand-labour, and artificial binding materials were disguised as vellun. Such books, he said, cracked in half with a loud bang when they were opened the first time. Old-style printing, then, was a mixed blessing. It originated with a commendable attempt to resist the tyranny of modern-face types by reviving the use of Caslon, a superior eighteenth-century face, but it became very quickly associated with deception and cheap materials. When Ruskin subjected one of the most famous nineteenth-century English paintings, Holman Hunt's The Awakening Conscience (exhibited at the Royal Academy in 1854), to a characteristically close analysis, he seized upon one detail - the modern book in a papier-mâché binding which lies upon a table — as a particularly telling symptom of Victorian moral failure.2 Ruskin was much interested in bookproduction: he tried his hand at designing bindings, and for the 1889 edition of Modern Painters he insisted upon a newly cast fount of type and painstakingly oversaw the printing of the plates.2 Morris must have been aware of Ruskin's prolonged effort to control the printing of his own books. Ruskin also played a minor role in launching what the Victorians called artistic' printing, the only serious rival toold-style' printing in the 188os.27 Thomas Hailing, a Cheltenham printer who had achieved some celebrity by establishing a small trade journal called Hailing's Circular in 1877, proposed another periodical in which leaves printed by various printers would be bound together and circulated for their mutual edification.28 The first number (188o) of the Printers’ International Specimen Exchange, under the editorship of Andrew Tuer (who had a foot in both the artistic and old-style camps), contained a letter from Ruskin to Tuer: 'It seems to me . . . that a lovely field of design is open in the treatment of decorative type — not in the mere big initials in which one cannot find the letters — but in a delicate and variably fantastic ornamentation of capitals, and filling of blank spaces or musically-divided periods of sentences and breadths of margin. Paper that won't break or won't mildew would be literally a ‘godsend" to me. I scarcely care to design an engraving to go on to modern paper. I have the most entire sympathy with your objects, but believe that people will have bad paper nowadays, bad printing nowadays, and bad painting nowadays – and nothing else.' It is a curious letter of endorsenent — its bleak pessimism is certainly at odds with the upbeat tone of Tuer's introduction — and if Ruskin thought that Victorian printing was about to be regenerated by the Printers' International Specimen Exchange, he was seriously mistaken. The artistic' style which dominated the Specimen Exchange from the beginning had been imported froı the United States and was based upon a hideous (or so it now seems to us) combination of bizarre ornamental types and bent rules. The Printers'Register (7 July 1884), reviewing the fifth volunme of the Specimen Exchange, argued that it represented a higher level of taste and workmanship than was to be found among any of the great printers of the past. But this was pure bluster (of the sort very comnon in trade journals of that decade), and the most sensitive printers recognized that the artistic style was a temporary aberration which could only be deplored. Jacobi remarked that ‘the excessive rule-work, twisted and twirled in all manner of fantastic shapes, the adoption of grotesque types, together with the employment of diverse colours (not always harmonious) is work really overstepping the bounds of proper typography, and it appears to me but an emulation of lithography or chromolithography'.29 De Vinne, in a lecture delivered in Philadelphia, offered this sarcastic observation: ‘What advances have we made in rule-twisting! What unknown possibilities in typography have been developed by our new race of compositors! . . . How it does delight us to employ a typographical gymnast who tortures brass rules and spends hours and days in experiments with borders, fancy job types, tint grounds, and flourishes!’3o ![](images/97dbe8017df7eccda147311b6044eb959c0b2cf2fa841fb28f307adbac1b5697.jpg) Figure 11 An example of ‘artistic' printing in the Printers' International Specimen Exchange (188o), edited by Andrew Tuer. More sophisticated Victorian printers like De Vinne and Jacobi decried the obsession with bent rules and weird decorative types. Fortunately the artistic style had its strongest impact on job-printing; it crept into books only occasionally, and then mainly on bindings and title-pages. Many book-printing houses such as Richard Clay and William Clowes were affected remarkably little by either the old-style or the artistic modes yet managed to produce typography that was at least neat, decent, and unobtrusive. Scottish printers in particular had a reputation for making books with these old-fashioned virtues. When Morris began to proclaim his typographical views in the 189os, it is understandable that some of these firms must have felt they were being overshadowed by a showy upstart. In an interview published in the Printers' Register – Supplement (6 February 1894), Walter Blaikie, head of T. and A. Constable, Edinburgh, made a number of barbed comments that were clearly directed at Morris. Many machine papers are quite as durable as hand-made,' he said; deckle edges had become an end in themselves (here Morris would have agreed with him), ‘just as in some of Morris's books, he strives to perpetuate the embossing of papers by the type, which was one of the defects of the hand-press'. Caslon type, he added, was used chiefly for what I call quasi-facsimile reprints'. And then the final veiled allusion to Morris: you must always remember that the first object of a book is to be read, not to become an article of bric-a-brac.' When a journalist from the periodical Bookselling interviewed Morris the following year, he found equal distrust on the other side. ‘The Constables are good printers,’ Morris observed, but those square capitals with the thick faces, which they use in the headlines of their books, are simply hideous. . . . In my opinion the best printers in Scotland are the Clarks. They have done some really beautiful work and have done it cheaply." Among nineteenth-century English private printers, the only one whose work anticipated Morris's in any sense was the Revd C. H. O. Daniel, Fellow of Worcester College, Oxford. The term private', incidentally, is extremely troublesome: Emery Walker was even prepared to argue that the Kelmscott Press was not, strictly speaking, a private press, since Morris was willing to print books for others.³² When John Martin, the librarian of Woburn Abbey, compiled a Bibliographical Catalogue of Privately Printed Books (second edition, 1854), he explained that ‘by privately-printed books, the Editor means to designate such only as were not intended by the writers for sale, and the circulation of which has been confined entirely to their friends and connections, or to those who took an interest in the matter contained in them' (p. vii). The Daniel Press would fit such a narrow definition, but certainly not the Kelmscott Press – or most other modern private presses, for that matter. Between 1845 and 1863 Daniel produced a large number of ephemeral pieces of printing, at first on a toy press and later on a small Albion press. In 1875, after an interval of neglect, he moved the press into his college rooms at Oxford and began printing again in earnest, and during this second phase he created a series of pamphlets and books which are admired and collected today. Falconer Madan, who has written the fullest account of Daniel's work as a printer, made a large claim for his press: ‘Dates appear to show that the Daniel Press was the first attempt to raise the standard of English Victorian printing — a work which was carried on with higher artistic and professional ideals and better opportunities on the establishment of the Kelmscott Press in 1891. The use of the Fell type in 1877 and the production of the Garland of Rachel in 1881 may fairly be regarded as the first genuine signs of the Revival of Printing in this country.'3 But is this true? Stanley Morison has shown that Daniel was not after all the first printer to revive the use of the splendid Fell types (owned by the Oxford University Press) in the nineteenth century, and Jacobi, among others, pointed out that Daniel's presswork was not as good as his typography.³⁴ A close examination of The Garland of Rachel, which Madan describes as ‘the most celebrated and valuable product of the Daniel Press' (p. 86), reinforces such misgivings. The little collection of eighteen poems, written by Daniel's friends to celebrate the first birthday of his daughter Rachel, is attractively set in the Fell types with ornaments by Alfred Parson, RA, and Mrs Daniel; yet the initials are weak, the inking is uneven, the register is erratic, and the paper has deteriorated badly. It is a charming production — indeed, intensely private' — but scarcely will stand up to a comparison with even the smallest of the Kelmscott books. This is not to disparage Daniel's printing: it is likely to appeal to precisely those collectors who are offended by Morris's penchant for the monumental book. But to see the Kelmscott Press as the direct descendant of the Daniel Press (as Madan suggests) is misleading. Was Morris familiar with any of the Daniel books? He could easily have known about them, but as usual he seems never to have mentioned Daniel or his press. The only tantalizing clue we have is an entry in the diary of Sydney Cockerell, Morris's secretary, on 28 September 1898 (after Morris's death): ‘Went to Bodleian & then to see Revd C. H. Daniel & his printing press (which has been idle for nearly a year) at Worcester House. Had a nice talk about printing &c. Just two years after Daniel began printing in Oxford, a major exhibition was held which was to have a more direct effect on the history of the Kelmscott Press. To observe the four-hundredth anniversary of printing in England, a Caxton Exhibition opened in South Kensington on 3o June 1877 with unprecedented attention from both the press and the public. Squeezed in between the large array of early printed books (mainly but not exclusively Caxton's) and modern printing equipment was a small exhibit which proved to be the hit of the show: while curious visitors watched him, a white-haired pressman in knee-breeches operated a wooden press approxinately two hundred years old. (At a Health Exhibit in 1884 Tuer's ‘Ye Leadenhalle Presse' managed to put their hands on the same press for a reconstruction of a medieval printing office.) Morison asserts that the 1877 exhibition ‘had a decisive effect upon bibliographical and typographical studies'.³ Whether this was the case or not, it certainly had the effect of elevating Caxton to the status of a national hero. William Blades's revised biography appeared in the sane year; in 1882 a Caxton Memorial Window was unveiled in St Margaret's Church, Westminster. In his last typographical experiment before founding the Kelmscott Press, Morris used a modern imitation of one of Caxton's types, and five of the Kelmscott titles were Caxton reprints. Though Caxton's books could not have appealed to him aesthetically, Morris obviously shared his countrymen's enthusiasm for England's first printer.36 A growing interest in old books, facsimiles, and improved typography was evident on every side during the last two decades of the century. A writer in the Paper and Printing Trades Annual (December 1879) declared that ‘fifty years ago a facsimile or an imitation of Caxton type could only be seen in the private library of the lover of books, but now the taste for Caxtonian and old-style works is so general that pamphlets and tradesmen's circulars are printed in type so nearly resembling the original that only the initiated can discern the difference'. One well-known London publisher, Elliot Stock, exploited this popular antiquarian taste by issuing a series of typefacsimiles of older English books, including Walton's Compleat Angler, Pilgrim's Progress, Paradise Lost, and Herbert's The Temple. They were attractively printed at the Chiswick Press and were well received by readers and reviewers. During the 188os Kegan Paul published a similar series, known as the Parchment Library'. The cult of Caxton led also to a minor revival of Gothic types, at least for display purposes. Calbe Stower's The Printer's Grammar (18o8) had been able to declare emphatically that blackletter ‘is now abolished in England, except in a very few instances'(p. 41), and throughout most of the nineteenth century its use was restricted to legal and ecclesiastical books, usually on title-pages. In the 188os it began to appear again (though still for display only) in books and journals. The extent to which the Caxton exhibition had filtered down to the popular level is amusingly illustrated by this advertisement in the Antiquary magazine, 188o—I: Cash's Woven Initial Letters and Monograms for sewing on household linen, socks, and underclothing. The letters are manufactured in three sizes of Old English Type, in Turkey Red, and orders can also be executed in Black, both of which are warranted perfectly fast. . . . Sold by Drapers and Hosiers everywhere.' By the time Morris turned his attention to designing the Troy type, the Victorian Gothic Revival had already touched the Gothic typeface. Another indication of the new interest in typographical experimentation was A. H. Mackmurdo's periodical the Century Guild Hobby Horse, which first appeared in 1884 but did not resume publication until two years later.³7 Though its style was more art nouveau than Morrisian, the Hobby Horse was a distinguished journal that reminds us once again (if we need reminding) that many of the ideas usually attributed to Morris were in fact simply in the air at the end of the last century. With an engraved cover designed by Selwyn Image (whose Greek type Morris was to use in the following decade) and ornaments by Image, Herbert Horne, and others, the Hobby Horse - printed, not surprisingly, at the Chiswick Press — anticipates many of Morris's notions about the well-designed page. And, since Morris knew Mackmurdo and others associated with it, there can be no doubt that he was aware of the existence of the Hobby Horse. It is most unlikely that he was impressed by the Hobby Horse, though Mackmurdo in later years was to boast that his periodical was the true precursor of the Kelmscott Press. One can perhaps find a hint of Morris's probable attitude in a letter (undated) which he wrote to Charles Ricketts and Charles Shannon about their Dial, another journal, founded in 1889, with similar typography and ornaments: ‘Thank you for the Dial which duly arrived. I confess that I looked at the art portion of it with somewhat mixed feelings, as the talent and the aberration of the talent seemed to me to be in about equal proportions.'8 The world of typographical scholarship — with which Morris had numerous connections — was also showing signs of renewed life in the final years of the nineteenth century. William Blades and Talbot Baines Reed were laying the foundations for the history of English printing; at the major British libraries figures like Robert Proctor and Henry Bradshaw were pioneering in the study of incunabula. During the 189os Kegan Paul published a bibliographical series under the general editorship of A. W. Pollard: the titles included The Great Book Collectors by Charles and Mary Elton, Book Plates by W. J. Hardy, Books in Manuscript by Falconer Madan, Early Printed Books by E. Gordon Duff, Early Illustrated Books by Pollard, and Book Bindings by Herbert P. Horne. Along similar lines, Elliot Stock inaugurated a series called ‘The Book-Lover's Library' under the editorship of H. B. Wheatley as well as several bibliographical journals (the Antiquary, 1880-1915; the Bibliographer, 1881-4; Book-Lore, 1885-7; the Bookworm, 1888–92). In the 189os the Bibliographical Society was formed in London, with the primary purpose of recording all ’early English books. Morris himself read a paper entitled ‘The Ideal Book' in June 1893 to the Bibliographical Society, and on the same evening Jacobi of the Chiswick Press also spoke on ‘The Printing of Modern Books'. Another organization with which Morris was actively associated, the Art-Workers’Guild, devoted a substantial number of its meetings from 1886 onwards to the book arts; the topics discussed or demonstrated at the Guild's meetings included Bookbinding', Calligraphy, Typography, and the Book Beautiful', ‘The Manufacture of Paper' (this by Joseph Batchelor, Morris's papermaker, who brought along a washtub for his demonstration), Book Illustration' and ‘Reformation of Printing Type'. Shortly before his foundry cast Morris's Golden type in 189o, Talbot Baines Reed delivered an erudite lecture on Old and New Fashions in Typography' to the. Society of Arts (the group to which Morris was later to speak about ‘The Woodcuts of Gothic Books’). Reed's talk offers an excellent illustration of the enlightened climate of typographical opinion in which Morris's own theories were formed. Surveying the development ofletterforms through the centuries, Reed praised the superiority of Venetian and old-face types — especially those of Nicholas Jenson and Caslon — and argued that the modern fashion [i.e. modern-face type] lacks all the graciousness and dignity of the old. Its straight hard serifs, its stiff interiors, its harsh contrasts of thick and thin, more than nullify the passing advantage of exact lining and delicate finish.'9 This assertion has a familiar ring, because it is precisely the doctrine that Morris was to promulgate within a year or two after Reed's lecture. Morris may not have been directly indebted to Reed, because there were others from whom he could have absorbed the same ideas (most notably Emery Walker, who was present that evening and participated in the discussion); but the lecture demonstrates that Morris's typographical theories did not evolve in isolation. A slightly earlier lecture which eerily anticipates many of Morris's complaints about modern books was delivered by Henry Stevens, an American rare-book dealer long resident in London, to the Library Association at Cambridge in October 1884.4° Published under the title Who Spoils our New English Books Asked and Answered by Henry Stevens of Vermont (1884) and dedicated ‘to the memory of two old friends Charles Whittingham and William Pickering', Stevens's lecture was a powerful attack upon declining standards in Victorian printing. ‘The manufacture of a beautiful and durable book costs little if anything more, it is believed, than it does to manufacture a clunisy and unsightly one,' he declared in words that Morris was to echo (almost verbatin) in the following decade.4 Like Morris, Stevens insisted that margins of classic proportions, which he specified, were essential to a beautiful printed page, and that good design was meaningless without good materials, especially ink and paper. He even inade a stirring assault on the indiscrininate use of the binder's knife, which Morris later described as one of the chief enemies of civilization. As always, we find ourselves asking the obvious question: was Morris familiar with this lecture? And, as in other instances considered earlier, there is no firm answer, though he undoubtedly had an opportunity to read it, for the booklet was on display at the 1888 Arts and Crafts Exhibition where Morris must have seen it. The issue, however, is not whether Morris borrowed particular ideas or attitudes from some of his contemporaries; what we need to keep in mind while examining the history of the Kelmscott Press is that Morris's reaction against the enfeebled Victorian book reflects a surprisingly widespread response to the typography of the day. Morris was not alone in protesting against the modern debaseiment of printing, nor was he the only one to suggest sensible remedies. What sets Morris apart from Jacobi, Reed, Stevens, and other lesser voices is that he was able, during the 1 89os, to fuse a brilliant synthesis of their individual criticisms, to bring the craft of printing back in touch with literature and art, and to move these typographical debates out of the realm of theory by producing books of unsurpassed beauty.
