# The Rise and Growth of Economic Nationalism in India

ECONOMIC POLICIES OF INDIAN NATIONAL LEADERSHIP, 1880—1905

The process of freedom from British rule in India began early in the last century. The movement passed through many vicissitudes and had many strands but its essential character was that of a peaceful revolution. When the first tide of resistance to the advance of alien domination had nearly exhausted itself and the opposition offered by the Indian States, the first victims of aggressive imperialism, had dissolved, the emerging educated urban middle class adopted the method of constitutional agitation for seeking a voice in the administration of the country. Their main instrument of action was the public press and the political or social associations whose forum was utilised for pressing the needs of the people and removing their grievances. This early expression of public opinion, confined largely to the Presidency towns, had only one object, that of submitting representations to the authorities. It was pre-eminently an appeal to the conscience of England, to the sense of justice and spirit of liberalism' which then prevailed in that countryMany Indian administrators were inspired by utilitarian ideas and had firm faith in paternalism as the only principle of state action in India. Yet their policies were fashioned not by considerations of the welfare of the Indian people but by the motives of profit of the East India Company and development of industry and progress and prosperity of the British people. This had its inevitable repercussion on the economic condition of the mass of population of India.An immense change was rapidly taking place in Indian economy. It was fast losing its self-sufficiency and that healthy balance, the co-ordination between agriculture and industry, which had made India the sink of the bullion of the West. Indian economy became subordinate to the British economy and the main purpose of its agriculture was to furnish raw materials to feed the growing industries of England. Indian exports had shifted in emphasis from industrial products to cotton and foodgrains. The imports no longer consisted of gold and silver and a few luxury goods but consisted of textiles and other manufactured articles of daily use. This alteration in the nature of foreign trade reflected the decline in Indian industries and increasing dependence on agriculture and consequently rapid march towards poverty. 'The consciousness of this phenomenon led to a violent drift in the expression of public opinion which steadily forsook the aspect of meek and mild requests for iminor administrative reforms and grew defiantly critical of the measures and policies of the rulers.

The Revolt of 1857 and its inhuman suppression by the military might and bureaucratic repression deflected the course of political expression for some time, but the fire of revolt continued to smoulder beneath the surface and the temper of the people found vent in the growth of political associations and the spurt of journalistic enterprise. Its volume continued to grow and bureaucratic frown or measures of repression like the Vernacular Press Act failed to hinder its progress. There was an unprecedented rise in the number of journals and newspapers most of which had opened their columns for ventilating the feelings of the people, particularly of the urban middle class. A number of political associations also were established at the various provincial centres and many among these had extended their vision to comprehend the problems of India as a whole. The endeavours of the seventies in this direction culminated in the establishment of the Indian National Congress in mid-eighties of the last century, and this institution provided a steadily strengthening forum for ventilating the grievances of the Indian people and putting forth demands of a political and economic nature for râdically altering the character of the government. One main purpose of these associations was to press for constitutional development leading to the increasing participation of Indians in framing state policies and implementing then. In the nature of things then it was difficult for the common people, the mass of the population, to be identified with such organisations of public will. But without the wider participation of these elements, enough pressure failed to be generated and the government remained relatively unaffected by the rhetoric of the upper class politicians. The masses were also not intimately touched by a mere programme of constitutional advance, which had no meaning for them in terms of representative institutions and Indianisation of services.The people were groaning under the weight of heavy taxation and suffered from unemployment and scarcity which were recurrent features of Indian economy at the time. Economic appeal was the only appeal which could stir thei from their stupor. And without their active and visible support the Indian political movement would remain a feeble stream. Hence to rouse them from inertia and to beat the government with a heavy club, it became essential for the political leadership to direct its gaze on the plight of the people, diagnose the malady and analyse the factors which were responsible for their economic sufferings.It was in this context that economic ideas of Indian leadership had their origin.

Dadabhai Naoroji gave the lead in this direction and he was soon followed by a galaxy of brilliant exponents of economic ideas in the last few decades of the nineteenth century. Ranade, Joshi, Gokhale, G. S. Iyer, Wacha and Dutt are some of the prominent names whose contributiou in this field was enormous and who moulded the trend of economic thinking at the time. The theme was taken up with alacrity by the Indian journals and repeated from various platforms all over the country. The resolutions of the Indian National Congress and the speeches on them embodied their ideas. Thus the economic ideas of these leaders nourished the nascent national movement and provided it with a new base and made it strong. The fundamental question at the moment was that of the impact of British rule on India and the results which it had brought about. In the latter half of the nineteenth century, the British statesmen and the bureaucracy in India did not tire of stressing the>White Man's Burden and proclaiming that under the British 'sway India had made all round progress, thereby justifying imperial domination. All European imperialist powers were gloating over the civilising mission which they emphasised was the main object of territorial grab and world expansion. Colonialism was assuming a new shape and being presented in a new garb, without the least alteration in its real nature, that of economic exploitation In India also, the apologists of British rule harped on the values of peace, new education and above all the growing prosperity of the country, represented by the extension of railways, roads and other public works. Many of them contrasted the economic conditions of the nineteenth century with those under the Mughal rule and, misrepresenting the facts, depicted the existing position in golden hues. Moderate leadership was impressed by the cultural contacts and had developed a sense of adoration for parliamentary institutions of the British, and there were many in the country who had been influenced by the new preaching that affluence had resulted from British rule. This theory had to be exploded if the national movement for freedom from foreign rule was to make any headway. The principal contribution of Dadabhai Naoroji and others was to prick the bubble and expose the reality of British rule. By doing this they gave a new and solid plank for the national movement to stand on and helped the growth of extremism which ultimately paved the way for Swaraj.

The main point of their economic ideas was the theory of the drain, which in simple words implied the constant flow of wealth from India to Great Britain in the form of excess of exports over imports, the balance being the tribute India had to pay to maintain the most expensive machinery, civil and military, of foreign ruleThey revealed that Indian taxpayer had paid richly for his own servitude in so far as all the wars of territorial expansion were financed out of the Indian revenues. The growing public debt was a heavy burden. So also the railway expansion and the costly public works were imposing an increasing burden on the revenues of India in the form of interest payments. The railways were also instruments of imperialism for its own security. While Indian agriculturist demanded irrigation'to feed the people, the British financier and steel industry multiplied railway mileage to relieve famines. But the most important sources of the drain, according to their thinking, were the Home Charges, the savings of British officers and British merchants remitted to England and the army charges. This point was ably stressed by Gokhale before the Royal Commission on Indian Expenditure, when he pointed out that Indian army was used mainly for imperial purposes, both on the borders of India and elsewhere. This drain was causing poverty, lack of capital growth and the crippling of industry. Another target of attack by Indian leadership was the tariff policy of the British government, which was not only inequitable but positively directed at the suppression of the infant industry of India and the killing of the old cottage industry. The unjust imposition of the countervailing cotton excise duties and the removal of the cotton import duties were glaring examples of the unjust treatment meted out to India in the interest of British textile industry. R. C. Dutt took up the challenge to expose the tyrannous revenue policy of the governient which had bled the agriculturist and caused extreme penury leading to recurrent famines. Curzon's government was hard put to combat his arguments, which had an uncommon appeal to the Indian public.

Thus these early Indian economists laid bare the colonial character of British rule and demolished the facade of a welfare state which British statesmen and economists had laboured hard to put up to hide the·reality of the situation.<This resulted in a flood of anti-British writings charging the government of callous neglect of the interests of the people. The subsequent stages of political movement derived their sustenance considerably from this source, and the stream of opposition to foreign rule gathered volume.

Dr Bipan Chandra has utilised contemporary writings and made profuse use of the newspapers to analyse the economic ideas of these early leaders of Indian national movement and thereby made a valuable contribution to the understanding of the basis of national movement and its development in the formative stage. I have great pleasure in introducing it to the wider public and have full confidence that this study will encourage others to analyse the economic, political or social ideas which conditioned the emergençe of Indian people from colonial subjection to freedom.

This book is a revised version of the thesis titled 'Economic Policies of Indian National Leadership, 188o to 1905' accepted by the University of Delhi for the award of the Ph.D. degree in 1963. It seeks to examine the economic policies of the Indian national movement before the swadeshi agitation carried it forward to a higher and different plane.

I have taken as leaders all those persons who exercised the function of forming and guiding the growing nationalist public opinion ou economic questions. As such I have relied in this study not only on the proceedings of the Indian National Congress, the provincial conferences, other public associations, the legislative councils, the speeches and writings of the prominent nationalist public men, but also on the nationalist newspapers and journals, which were, during these years, a major forum of nationalist agitation and very influential creators of public opinion. In fact, in the absence of a recognised nationalist political party with a well-knit organisation and functioning all the year round-and the Indian National Congress did not yet amount to such an organisation-and the tradition of the huge public, maidan meetings not having been yet born, the nationalist press played the role of the opposition in matters of day-to-day administration. Even the resolutions and proceedings of the Indian National Congress and other nationalist associations were in a way effective in moulding public opinion only to the extent to which they filtered down to the people through the coluinns. of the nationalist press. It may also be pointed out that many of the newspapers and journals of the time were run by their proprietors and editors as nationalist organs at some cost to themselves rather than as business propositions. Moreover, many of these editors had intimate connection with the life of the urban lower-middle classes.

I have studied in original several of the leading English language or Anglo-Vernacular nationalist newspapers and magazines of the period, including the Mahratta, the Hindu, the Amrita Bazar Patrika, and the Bengalee. Moreover, I have utilised for a study of the Indian-language newspapers the unpublished Reports on the Native Press for different provinces. The Reports on the Native Press were a weekly official summary or reproduction of the editorial comments and articles to appear in Indian-owned newspapers and magazines. The Bombay Report included in its coverage both the English and Indian-language newspapers while the Reports of other provinces covered only Indian-language press till the end of the 19th century when they started covering all Indian-owned newspapers. These Reports were strictly confidential and were made available only to officials of the rank of Divisional Commissioner or above. They met a high standard of fair and accurate reproduction, even though some of them-particularly that of Madras—were rather inadequate in their coverage.

As national have been taken all those persons, associations, newspapers, and journals who looked upon India as a growing nation whose people had common interests and destiny in the broadest sense of the term, who visualised the ultimate aimn of self-government for its people, and who tried to look at public questions from the point of view of the whole people of the country, or 'at least of the masses of the people, rather than that of a smaller, narrower body within it. The criterion in this respect has been what they professed and not what they wanted in practice. The latter aspect has been examined at length in the main body of the thesis. In other words>I have excluded from consideration as national leaders only those who'either explicitly denied the nationhood of India or the common political destiny of its people or openly came out as the representatives of a small segment of the population only. Thus I have excluded the Hindu, Muslim and Parsi communalists, and several newspapers, including the Rast Goftar after 1888 and the Hindustan after 19o1. The British India Association and the Hindu Patriot, which had lost their original national character by 1881 and openly become the champions of the Zamindars' interest alone, have also been excluded.

In this work I have been interested not so much in the question as to who were the originators of an economic idea or policy as in its propagators and the extent of its propagation and the amount and type of political campaigning behind it. The large number of public men and uewspapers cited in the footnotes is explained by this effort to bring out the wide prevalence of certain policies. Similarly, I have been concerned not with the correctness or otherwise of the nationalist attitudes and policies as judged according to the canons of the science of economics, but with what the Indian leaders said and their manner of saying it and what can be learnt therefrom about their basic economic and political understanding and approach. To attempt the former task would involve nothing less than the writing of an exhaustive economic history of India of the period and perhaps much more than that. I have mostly used secondary sources for tracing contemporary economic developments.

This is of course a study not of the institutional basis of Indian nationalism--that is of the class and other human relations which lay at its base—but of its ideological-programmatic aspects. There can be no full understanding of Indian national moveinent without the former. Yet, I hope that this study of the reflection of the economic reality in the minds of the early national leaders would be found useful, for though social relations exist independently of the ideas men form of them, men's understanding of these relations is crucial to their social and political action. Thus the study of the political and intellectual tools that a movement uses to grasp the reality, to formulate its aims, and to induce changes in the desired direction are as important as the study of the social and economic forces which give birth to the movement. This study does not, therefore, trace the growth of colonial economic relations or the rise of modern economic forces and classes in India. Our problem has been to study the gradual development during the years 188o to 1905 of the nationalist understanding of the economic basis and policies of British imperialism in India and the evolution of an alternative nationalist programme for the development of an independent national economy. I also hope that I have been able to bring out, as a by-product, the quality of intellect and of political and intellectual integrity and courage of the pioneers of Indian national movement. The desire to bring out accurately the type and quality of nationalist economic agitation is responsible for the large number of quotations in this study. Often, the manner in which the national leaders put forward a point of view is as important as the point of view itself. Moreover, the quality of their economic understanding is brought out better when given in the original. In any case, as Winston Churchill has put it, ‘a phrase struck out at the time is worth many coined afterwards'.
