# Context and Environment

## Introduction

The relationship between the interior and its wider location has often been seen as somewhat ambiguous, and yet, it is possible to describe some situations – that is rooms, spaces or buildings – as encapsulating, in miniature, the characteristic qualities or features of a much wider situation. The interior space has an obvious and direct relationship with the building that it occupies and with the people who use it, but it can also have a connection with the area in which it is located. Much has been written and discussed about the manner in which we inhabit space and about the interior’s relationship with its immediate context. But little attention has been paid to the association between interior space and its more extensive context or environment, that is, the room as a microcosm of the city or region.

The designer can work with the identity of a place to create interior space that is appropriate to its location. There are a great many examples of such spaces that are so particular to a specific location, that not only could they not have been constructed anywhere else, but also it would be impossible to transfer them elsewhere. Carlo Scarpa’s interventions could not have existed anywhere other than Veneto, Giancarlo de Carlo’s work is determinedly fixed in Urbino and Tadao Ando’s buildings are firmly rooted in Osaka. Other contemporary designers have been able to interpret the meaning within different places: Jørn Utzon, David Chipperfield, Steven Holl; even Rem Koolhaas is now claiming affiliation (Venice Biennale Exhibition 2011). Examples of interior design projects that have a strong affiliation with their context include the Viennese shop interiors of Hans Hollein, the bar interiors of Ben Kelly and the retail units of Future Systems.

This method of working closely with the given environment can be referred to as Contextualism. It is an approach that advocates a careful reading of the situation before making any alterations to it. It is almost 30 years since Kenneth Frampton wrote about Critical Regionalism, Colin Rowe and Fred Koetter composed Collage City and Aldo Rossi recorded The Architecture of the City. Although the movement, which emerged as a reaction to Modernism, is a generation old, it is now more relevant than ever. The architect or designer will propose changes that do not destroy the nature or character of an area, but actually contribute to that built environment. The designer must also create interior space that is totally relevant to the time in which it is constructed, design that uses contemporary technology and is suitable for the needs of today. This means not resorting to pastiche, but designing interiors that are visually and operationally applicable to the present day.

The architects Lone and Søren Asmusson have converted the barn which was next to their farmhouse in Farum, about 15 miles to the north of Copenhagen, into a music studio. At first sight it looks pretty much like the other buildings in the complex: single storey with whitewashed walls and what appears to be a thatched roof. However, although the walls of the studio are standard, the slightly elevated roof is made from black aluminium. The approach that the architects took was to design a perfect replica of the building that could slip very carefully into the void of the original barn.

This highly insulated box has openings that align exactly with the original openings, the reveals of which are lined with laminated timber. The interior walls are painted white to reflect the sunlight and this is reinforced with an enormous mirror that covers the

entire slanting ceiling of the north half of the open space.

The building is referred to as the Posehuset, which means “bag house”. This is a direct reference to the somewhat eccentric previous owner of the property who had a penchant for collecting plastic bags. The studio has the quality of looking as if it has always been there, while at the same time being completely new. It contains the character of the region, but also belongs to the 21st century.

The intimate relationship between an interior and its immediate context can be observed in Florian Beigel’s design for the

Half Moon Theatre. The auditorium was a collection of enclosed spaces that were formed in an unused and neglected gap on the busy Mile End Road in London. The distance between the actors and the audience was consciously reduced to encourage deliberate interaction. Thus, the open and connected interior spaces were conceived of as a continuum of the street, as if the journey from the outside world was almost accidental and the audience members had unintentionally wandered into the drama.

The context that a building, interior or situation occupies can inspire the nature of the adaptation or design. The natural and artifi cial elements that constitute the site can provide the impetus for the remodelling of the environment.

Figure 1.27 The swimming pools are squeezed between the massive infrastructure of the road system and the energy of the Atlantic Ocean.

Through careful study, combined with intuitive reaction, the designer can create a new environment that appears to be more than an extension of the context: it actually completes it. The situation is so inspiring that any interventions seem to be born of that environment, to have evolved within

that place, rather than having been designed and placed there.

This is exactly how the swimming pools by Álvaro Siza on the Portuguese coast just to the north of Porto appear. They are situated on the Leça de Palmeira beaches, on the northern coastline of Matosinhos, crushed next to the rocks between a duel carriageway and the Atlantic Ocean. The industrial port can be observed across the sands.

Siza embraced this situation by integrating the pools with the rocky coastline. The

Leça Swimming Pool complex consists of changing rooms, a café and two swimming pools, one for adults and one for children. The building is sunk below the level of the road; this immediately creates a disconnection between the pools and the infrastructure of the city. The visitor enters the complex via a smooth concrete ramp which is parallel to the road, and as the path slopes downwards, so the disengagement with the city begins. A concrete barrier, just a shade lighter than the natural stone, reinforces this detachment. The visitor passes through the quite brutal changing rooms made of the same coloured concrete, and is then encouraged to turn towards the sea.

This is the fi rst real engagement with the magnifi cence of the water, and the experience is intensifi ed by the delayed gratifi cation. The two pools merge with the rocks and they appear to be of a similar magnitude. The children’s pool, which is situated towards the land, is bound on one edge by a curving concrete wall and the other by the rocks, whereas the adult pool is positioned further out towards the sea. Three edges are made from concrete, while the other is the natural formation of the rocky coastline. The water is refreshed daily as the tide fl ows in and out of this natural location. The orthogonal concrete interventions do not seem out of place in this tranquil and organic setting. There is a clear view towards the Port of Leixoes, one of the most important cargo ports in Portugal, which handles around 14 million tonnes of goods and 65,000 passengers each year. The shared industrial language creates a strong relationship between the pools and the quays and harbours. Thus the Leça Swimming Pools appear to have the quality of emerging from the nature of the area and the culture and industry that has developed there.

The Half Moon Theatre Company was a radical and innovative group that developed creative and alternative ideas to the established order of theatrical productions. They challenged the conventional nature of the auditorium, and questioned the space between the theatre and the street, the cast and the audience. The theatre company developed progressive productions that drew on the most direct forms of theatre of the past, such as the Italian commedia dell’arte, and the Elizabethan theatres with galleried courtyards such as Shakespeare’s Globe. The guiding principle was to break down the barriers between the actors and the audience and encourage both to be seen by each other.

Beigel and Christou proposed a design for the new theatre that exploited this concept; they developed an auditorium space that resembled a typical street scene. Thus the Half Moon Theatre was basically a spatial progression starting from the pavement of the Mile End Road in the East End of London, through a gateway into an open-air scenic space or public courtyard. The journey continued through a steel curtain and into the main performance area, which again both fi guratively and metaphorically resembled a scenic street. From here, large doors led into a small garden and the Young People’s Theatre pavilion. This was a dense and layered series of spaces, some inside and some outside; each was distinct but held

together by their proximity and the manner in which people moved between and occupied the journey.

The street scene was so particular and strong because it was actually a series of pure spaces within the awkward shape of a once derelict area. This was achieved with the careful placement of new three storey block walls containing rectangular openings.These acted as linings, defi ning the exact dimensions of the rooms and also importantly providing acoustic control. This complex surface geometry to the sides of the auditorium effectively scattered the sound to ensure good acoustic diffusion. The new high ceiling also created a problem of late-refl ected sound, and an absorbent sky, that is fairfaced woodwool slabs above what appeared to be a fl oating perforated metal ceiling, provided the acoustic control. The pattern on the fl oor, which was a slightly different angle to the geometry of the space, reinforced the dynamism of the journey, and encouraged movement within the platonic solidness of the auditorium. Beigel and Christou created not a building, but the space between buildings. The theatre responded to the context by extending the street into the heart of the space, encouraging a dynamic relationship between the urban elements of the immediate surroundings and the intimate nature of the theatre.
