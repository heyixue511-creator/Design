The signs on this clay tablet are considered an early form of cuneiform writing. Made with many small strokes, the signs seem to be drawings of particular things. The pictorial origins of cuneiform can be traced in these and other artifacts. But these signs are considered writing because they can be related to the sounds in the Sumerian language. The visual structure of cuneiform tablets is well ordered. The surface is usually divided into columns and/or rows, either by visible or implied lines. The size and spacing of the marks are regular, and their rhythmic calligraphy has a momentum that carries the eye forward. The marks are grouped according to a system which is as elaborate visually as a code for language is verbally. The unwritten rules of this system are fundamental to visual communication, and, although scripts and letterforms have changed in the five thousand years since these first written records were produced, the graphic principles of size, sequence, grouping, and ordering still apply. of Mesopotamian cuneiform scripts. Writing in the Indus Valley (presentday Pakistan) may also have been borrowed or adapted from cuneiform. In China, writing emerged later and apparently independently, although some debate exists about cultural influences from Europe to Asia. Glyphic writing originated in Mesoamerican cultures in advance of contact with Europeans and has its own graphic rules (Figs. 1.6a-c). Writing and sign making are directly related to administrative tasks and the social structures needed to enable civil societies. For instance, by 3000 BCE, the state bureaucracy of Uruk in Mesopotamia had ten numerical systems in use, each with a specialized sphere of activity. The management of tasks associated with irrigation systems led to the invention of maps in Sumer by the end of the third millennium BCE. Writing codified law, and its enforcement came to supplement (and sometimes replace) violence as an instrument of power (Fig. 1.7). Cuneiform was first used to represent ancient Sumerian, one of a handful of ancient languages that have never been deciphered. The number of cuneiform signs suggests that they represented words in a logographic script. cuneiform. The need for efficiency in writing systems propelled simplification from pictorial to schematic signs. A stylus in wet clay made some marks more easily than others, and the orientation as well as the shape and combination of these marks changed as writing became stabilized. Sumerian linear and cuneiform marks from about 2400 BcE were adopted by successive peoples in the same geographic regions: Akkadians, Babylonians, and Assyrians each used cuneiform for their own distinct language. Not only was cuneiform graphically efficient, but also it represented an enormous conceptual advancement: it was a fully developed, adaptable written code for language. indicate pictographic origins, but some debate exists about whether these signs derived from the more schematic imagery of token notation. In any case, these Sumerian pictograms themselves soon became schematized so that they could be drawn in a few quick strokes of the stylus on a soft tablet. The cuneiform script was adopted by the Akkadians, a Semiticspeaking people who invaded the Mesopotamian region about 2400 BCE. The adoption of a writing system developed for one language by another is a pattern that is repeated many times in the history of scripts. The Akkadians distilled a set of cuneiform signs that represented syllables. These voweland-consonant combinations were numerous but functioned more efficiently than signs for every word (Fig. 1.8). In a subsequent wave of migration into the same region around 1600 BCE, Hittites also adopted cuneiform, this time to represent their Indo-European language. The cuneiform script spread throughout the ancient Near East and was used extensively across various cultures and languages for diplomatic purposes in the third and second millennia BCE. From the example of cuneiform another important principle emerges: scripts and languages do not have a one-to-one relation to each other. Although more than two dozen language families are known throughout the globe, only two major scripts are used: alphabetic writing and scripts derived from Chinese characters. The fiexibility and efficiency of the alphabet have made it particularly adaptable for use in representing the basic sounds of many, often unrelated, spoken languages. The archaeological evidence for the development of language in humans is linked to the evolution of the shape of the skull and organs of speech. The developed palate, tongue, and mouth organs found in Homo sapiens remains suggest that language could have emerged by about 60,000 sce. The question of the relation of world languages to each other is much debated. Although a common origin may be possible, the spontaneous appearance of language as a specieswide adaptation is also credible. Speculation about the historical relation of languages is based on the structure of sounds, morphemes, syntax, and other features of grammar. technologies (clay, wax, stone, etc.). With the advent of stable writing systems, the record of human existence becomes linked to texts. These offer evidence of cultural developments alongside artifacts such as pottery, tools, jewelry, textiles, dwellings, burial sites, and other remains (Fig. 1.10). From a graphic perspective, one of the fascinating features of hieroglyphic writing is that it is organized according to underlying grid structures rather than in a fixed word order. Hieroglyphics can be arranged in right-to-left, left-to-right, or up-and-down rows. They were usually composed to achieve maximum spatial efficiency inside invisible but implied quadrants on a stone or papyrus surface. This rationalization of a surface area, combined with attention to the direction and arrangement of signs within a picture plane, demonstrates considerable graphic sophistication. The flexibility of this system is shown by the variable orientation of hieroglyphic animal and human profiles. The Narmer Palette is considered one of the earliest artifacts of Egyptian writing. Demotic script represented an even more schematic, informal writing form used for daily occasions. The more elaborate pictorial forms of hieroglyphics were used for formal, official purposes.
