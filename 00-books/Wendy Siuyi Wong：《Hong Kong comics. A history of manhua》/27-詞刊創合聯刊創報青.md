## 137–138.

## Ching Bo, 1977

Publisher: Seung-gun Siu-bo

The name of this newspaper, which means “green," conveyed its youthful and energetic spirit, as most of its manhua artists—including Wong Kwok-hing, Fung Chi-ming, Ma Wing-shing, Tse Chi-wing, and Chiu Lui-tak—were less than twenty years old. Ma Wing-shing's work Experiment of the Devil showed particularly great potential.

## 139–144.

Golden Bo Daily, 1977

## Publisher: Wong Chun-lung

The main attraction of this manhua newspaper was Wong Yuk-long's Oriental Heroes. Other featured series were Sau Sing-chai, by Sheng-gun Siu-keung, Ghost Story, by Hon Man, and Ma Wing-shing's The Blood Sword, which first appeared in Golden Bo’s pages. These outstanding artists made Golden Bo the best-selling daily manhua newspaper, beating out its sister publicatión, Sang Po. Both papers were published until the late 1980s.

## 145.

Charming, 1978

Art/Script: Ma Wing-shing

This early work by Ma Wing-shing shows signs of his trademark shadows, smoothly drawn figures, and realistic backgrounds. The story line, which combines traditionally costumed characters with modern ones, promotes justice.

## 146-148.

Lianhuan Daily, 1981

Art: Various artists

This full-color newspaper's main attractions were Little Bar Girl, by Sheng-gwan Siu-wai, Lee Siu-lung, by Seung-gun Siu-bo, and Iron Dragon Man, by Fung Chi-ming.

## 149.

Tin Lung Comics, 1982

Art: Various artists

This daily newspaper featured sword-fighting epic Tiger Panther Heroes on the front and Shui Mukleung's The Dragon God's Battle on the back. As the manhua newspaper industry boomed in the 1980s, many newcomers entered the field, but some older manhua artists also wanted to try out the popular format, to test their commercial viability. Tin Lung Comics never became very popular; the stories and drawing style of the older generation did not suit the contemporary readers’ tastes. Tin Lung Comics marked the beginning of a downward trend for daily manhua papers.

## 150–151.

## King Manhua Weekly, 1982

As the comics industry boomed in the 1980s, it was led by the manhua empire of Wong Yuk-long. King Manhua Weekly began publishing shortly after Wong hired some of his key competitors, including Seunggun Siu-bo and Seung-gun Siu-keung. Together with the outstanding artists already on Wong's staff these new contributors created top-quality comics; the tabloid-size, sixteen-page magazine served as a showcase for the company. Redrawn versions of some of Wong's early works—Little Vagabond and Solar Lord—also appeared in the weekly. Although the comic feature on the magazine's cover was usually a space adventure, a variety of action, adventure, and humorous comics appeared inside.

## 152–156.

Drunken Master, 1982

Art/Script: Wong Yuk-long

Although the title of this comic comes from Jackie Chan's 1978 movie of the same name, the story did not follow the film. Instead, it tells the story of a young man who fights off unsavory characters as he learns martial arts. Drunken Master was also produced as a television series in 1984.

157–158.

Buddha's Palm, 1982

Art/Script: Wong Yuk-long

The first seventy issues of this costumed comic were based on Jin Yong's sword-fighting epic, Story of the Sword and the Sabre. The main character is Lung Kimfei, a young man who is given the chance to become the leader of the Mo-lum martial arts society.
