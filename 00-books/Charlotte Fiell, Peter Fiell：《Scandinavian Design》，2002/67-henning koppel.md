![](images/cb92b206cef7a02903efb83046c3b56780ebd50c243b8170ad64ae1f368836f1.jpg) Henning Koppel, Model no. 323 silver and enamel brooch for Georg Jensen, 1956 # henning koppel ## (b. 1918 Copenhagen – d. 1981 Copenhagen, Denmark) ![](images/1e3c84cd66b2639353d0a185d9b3a8142de78d67859bfbffe59a6cc5b13f7c67.jpg) The Danish designer Henning Koppel studied drawing under Bizzie Høyer in Copenhagen from 1935 to 1936 and then sculpture under Anker Hoffmann at the Kongelige Danske Kunstakademi, (Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts), Copenhagen, from 1936 to 1937. Following this, he trained at the Académie Ranson, Paris, from 1938 to 1939, where he became aware of currents in avant-garde sculpture. During the Second World War, Koppel was exiled in Stockholm and while there produced designs for the Orrefors glassworks and Svenskt Tenn. Around this time, he also began designing gold and silver jewellery. In 1945, he returned to Denmark and initiated his long association with the Georg Jensen silversmithy, which lasted until his death in 1981. Although he worked in other areas of design and for other companies, it was his beautiful sculptural silverware designed for Georg Jensen that attracted the greatest international attention. Inspired by the contemporary abstract sculptures of Hans Arp(1887-1966)and Constantin Brancusi(1876– 1957), Koppel designed biomorphic silver jewellery, such as his Model no. 89 bracelet (1946), which was exceptionally forward-looking and functioned as wearable sculpture. Despite the fact that Koppel worked within the Danish craft tradition, his jewellery and silverware products such as the Model no. 978wine pitcher (1948) and his fish dish (1954) were fundamentally modern because they articulated a design rhetoric that rejected historicism in favour of a universal language of organic form. His concepts for product designs were initially worked out in sketches and then modelled in clay, which enabled him to perfect their forms before they were translated into silver. In 1957, Koppel introduced a more expressive mode of design to Georg Jensen's flatware collection with his Carave/ cutlery, but during the 1970s his work became increasingly geometric and less sculptural. One ofhis last designs for Georg Jensen, which reflected this shift towards a more rational and pared-down aesthetic, was the Vejrstation (weather station) series(1980), which included a barometer, thermometer, clock and hydrometer. Between 1961 and 1981 Koppel also designed a number of ceramics for Bing & Grøndahl, including his undecorated porcelain Form 24 service (1962), which is sometimes referred to as the Koppe/ White service and ![](images/b251f46d4034444f5f7989a3fe6b2a78dae2a46acc5836db6020538732197f48.jpg) featured a tea pot with an unusual teak handle jutting from its side. From 1971, Koppel resumed work as a freelance designer for Orrefors. He also designed clocks and lighting for Louis Poulsen and postage stamps for the Danish Post Office. Duringhis illustrious career,hewas awarded three gold medals at the 1951 (IX), 1954 (X)and 1957 (XI)MilanTriennale, the Lunning Prize(1953), and the International Design Award of the American Institute of Designers(1963). Koppel's masterful understanding of the intrinsic nature of materials enabled him to produce exquisitemetalware that was remarkable for its sculptural plasticity and sensuous organic form.
