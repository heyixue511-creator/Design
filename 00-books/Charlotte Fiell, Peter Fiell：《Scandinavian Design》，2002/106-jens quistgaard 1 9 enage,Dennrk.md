Quistgaard was the son of the sculptor Harald Quistgaard, who was a professor at the Kongelige Danske Kunstakademi (Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts), Copenhagen. At a young age he learnt the art of woodcarving and later recalled, "I could not keep away from it [wood]; it fascinated me." He later trained and worked as a sculptor, carpen ter, ceramicist and draughtsman, utilizing diverse other materials – clay, sílver, bronze, iron and steel – but wood remained his material of choice. Quistgaard also served an apprenticeship with the Georg Jensen silversmithy. Following the Second World War, he became an independent designer and designed a variety of products, including a stoneware teapot with a brass and cane handle (1949) that was inspired by Oriental antecedents. In 1954, Quistgaard was awarded the Lunning Prize and the same year co-founded with the American entrepreneur, Ted Nierenberg, a manufacturing company, Dansk International Designs. He ran the firm with Nierenberg until 1984, and during their thirty-year partnership was responsible for the majority of the designs produced by this company. Quistgaard's products were distinguished by the use of subtle organic forms and by a super lative handling of materials. His work, such as the enamelled steel Kobenstyle cookware (1954) or the teak and steel Fjord cutlery (1954), allowed the materials used to express their inherent qualities. The sculptural form of Quistgaard's famous teak ice bucket (1960) was inspired by his study of the wooden hulls of Viking ships, while its bridge-like handle recalls those found on early Japanese ceramic vessels. Since 1984, he has operated a studio in Copenhagen and has designed buildings, furniture and jewellery for both European and American companies, Quistgaard's well-designed and well-executed products typify the Danish Modern style and reflect his interest in both the traditional crafts and earlier historical precedents. The soft and fluid outlines of his designs – often copied but rarely equalled – also attest to his belief that “the craftsman who designs for mass production must first of all know and appreciate his material. And man must manage the result, not the machine. ...
