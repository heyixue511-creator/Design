## RUSSIAN CONSTRUCTIVISM

CHRISTINA LODDER

One of the most exciting movements in twentieth-century art, Russian Constructivism radically reassessed the role of the artist and of his work in society and has had reverberations in Western creative activity ever since the 1920s. This book provides the first detailed account of its development, illuminating a chronology that has been lost of a movement which has been misunderstood and often confused with its Western counterpart.

Christina Lodder traces the way in which the relatively isolated explorations of new formal vocabularies and new attitudes towards materials conducted at the level of small abstract constructions were harnessed after the 1917 Revolution by artists like Tatlin and Rodchenko to develop a full and multi-dimensional philosophy for the design of functional objects for the new Socialist society.

The Constructivists' attempt to evolve a training for the newly fomulated concepts of the 'artistconstructor' who represented a fusion of the new ideological content and the emergent formal vocabulary were implemented in the teaching programmes of the Higher Artistic and Technical Workshops (VKhUTEMAS). This is the first detailed study in English of this school, which was as innovative in formulating concepts and methodologies of design as the Bauhaus in Germany. The concrete achievements of such Constructivists as Popova and Klutsis in textile, furniture, clothing. kiosk and theatre design were not inconsiderable when set against the industrial limitations and material and cultural constraints of the period.

Extensively based upon original sources in Soviet archives, this work brings to light a wealth of new factual and visual material. It painstakingly reconstructs the period to reveal a totally new picture of a movement that has never before been fully understood in the West.

An inspiring piece of scholarship . . . Lodder successfully achieves that delicate balance between detail and overview which lends sensitivity and depth to an already authoritative treatment of the subject.'—Roxane Permar, Anglo-Soviet Journal
