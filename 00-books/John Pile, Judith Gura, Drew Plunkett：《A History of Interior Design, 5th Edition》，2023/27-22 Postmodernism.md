Intimate small spaces, with bespoke furniture by Starck, made the Royalton Hotel lobby the preferred haunt of New York’s fashionistas. For all its dominance in world popular culture, and its apparent progressiveness, Britain in the 1960s had looked back, embracing Art Nouveau and Art Deco. Modernism in interior design and architecture seemed to have lost conviction; there were tentative changes of direction but both were in suspended animation. It needed something outrageous to set it in a new direction and that—postmodernism—would come from the United States. ## UNITED STATES ## Venturi and Others In 1966 the American architect Robert Venturi (1925–2018) published Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture in which he argued for architecture’s “difficult whole.” He included decoration and what he called “honky-tonk” elements—those that broke accepted aesthetic rules. He advocated “messy vitality” and counterpointed Ludwig Mies van der Rohe’s “less is more” with “less is a bore.” He designed a small house for his mother completed in 1964 in which the front elevation was treated as a broken classical pediment but built in the timber-framed construction techniques of North American suburbia. Its painted exterior, even its pediment, had similarities to its neighbors’ gables but it was challengingly unfamiliar. The interior was comparatively conventional, dependent on a few angled walls around the stair (22.2, 22.3, and 22.4). ![](images/ded2ba0d41cffe65e1a91e12832514894291545c26ae329d27746ff369ece256.jpg) ## 22.2 Robert Venturi, Vanna Venturi House, Philadelphia, 1964. The front elevation could be seen as a timber-boarded gable of a timber-framed suburban house but the symmetry, the central slot above the entrance that suggests a broken pediment, and the square openings all suggest, perhaps subliminally, something Neoclassical. ![](images/8eb5e896082268fd67a0270bc7e2fe9f29f356d3aa32c73352078319ca05abe9.jpg) Venturi added complexities and contradictions to his plan. Walls are angled, corners cut off rooms, the central entrance requires a sharp turn to reach the front door, and the stairway widens and is then suddenly awkwardly narrowed. ## 22.3 Robert Venturi, ground-floor plan, Vanna Venturi House, 1964. ![](images/6dde000b07dd6d83d13d7c7a234ff69a815efd08b3674e978d658cf946424784.jpg) 22.4 Robert Venturi, interior, Vanna Venturi House, 1964. In 1977 Venturi, with Denise Scott Brown (b. 1931) and Steven Izenour (1940–2001), published Learning from Las Vegas in which they denounced the sterility of corporate architecture and praised the populist vigor of Las Vegas. They argued that symbolism and decoration were crucial in making popular architecture and suggested that inspiration for both might be found in pre-modernist styles, singling out classicism for attention, which particularly annoyed modernists for whom it represented social and political repression. Postmodern classicism was itself honky-tonk enough to earn the affectionate abbreviation of “PoMo.” Its essence was effectively demonstrated in a series of chairs, in which Venturi and Scott Brown made molded plywood “cartoons” of work by the great eighteenth-century English designers—and painted them with bright random dots (22.5). The inspiration came as much from Disney as Chippendale. Modernism was no longer modern. ![](images/4d6936a6a94d55c196b840dc9153db62e1996200c170c444a7d5dd2f279342c2.jpg) Robert Venturi, Chippendale chair, 1978. The molded plywood back has cutouts that allude to eighteenth-century Chippendale furniture with cartoonish simplification. In 1977 the critic and architectural historian Charles Jencks (1939–2019) published The Language of Post-Modern Architecture suggesting that variations on the theme of modernism could counter its aridity. He identified five postmodernist sub-sects: the Historic, the Neo-Vernacular, the Adhoc, the Metaphorical, and the Metaphysical. In 1980 he complained that postmodernism had become synonymous with PoMo, which had eclipsed the other categories. He was more of an advocate for Metaphorical and Metaphysical. When in 1977 the magazine Architectural Design asked the New York Times’ architectural critic Paul Goldberger to explain postmodernism it really meant PoMo. Goldberger defined modernism as “puritan austerity” and postmodernism as “indulgent complexity.” Architects took advantage of the sheltering shells of the buildings they were converting to design what were, in effect, buildings within buildings. In 1979–81 George Ranalli (b. 1946) converted a listed schoolhouse in Newport, Rhode Island, to six self-contained condominiums on three levels, grouped around communal spaces that were the equivalent of squares and streets (22.6). Between 1979 and 1984, in Frankfurt in Germany, Oswald Mathias Ungers (1926–2007) converted a large house into the Deutsches Architekturmuseum (German Architecture Museum) and installed a structurally independent diagrammatic house at its core which rose through the four stories of the existing building. Both projects were seen as opportunities to design buildings that were not compromised by the need for weatherproofing and security (22.7). ![](images/ddbf18851e5e4585ecf9b30f4f504308a9fcc5de1850a5cfaf5bc1f33e7c92b6.jpg) George Ranalli, Callender School renovation, Newport, Rhode Island, 1979–81. The conversion of a small two-story former school building into six self-contained apartments. The whole is conceived as a small town with dwellings accessed from a central shared public square flanked by diagrammatic house façades. ![](images/fa28c275dace8ba6af42728b8469f3fcd5e9b32e2af00d1be848e6ff0dce432a.jpg) Oswald Mathias Ungers, German Architecture Museum, Frankfurt, 1979–84. The conversion of a large house to an architectural museum with a structurally independent house at its core that rose through four floors of the existing building. In 1978 Jencks, in collaboration with Britain’s leading PoMo architect Sir Terry Farrell (b.1938), began the five-year transformation of his Victorian villa in London to what he called The Cosmic House (22.8). The new interior had fragmented forms, architectural quotations, changes of level, and views across areas into others. The foundation that administers the house describes it as “a synthesis of ideas about science and the arts . . . a true PoMo Gesamtkunstwerk.” Jencks invited leading postmodernists to contribute elements to the whole. Michael Graves designed fireplaces, Piers Gough, who had designed Knitwit in 1968 Campbell, Zogolovitch, Wilkinson and Gough (see here), created a sauna. A first design for the Spring Room was rejected as insufficiently symbolic. ![](images/58783481b88f7c014105086e5038d8371fc07ebd9547ae9844f7905177d74cc3.jpg) Charles Jencks and Michael Graves, Spring Room, The Cosmic House, London, 1978–83. Jencks, the author of The Language of Post-Modern Architecture, worked with, among others, the United States foremost postmodern architect, Michael Graves, to convert his Victorian villa using his cosmic theories, which he then used in landscaping projects. In 1979 Lance Wright, an architectural and design journalist, complained in Architectural Review that twentieth-century buildings were designed to process people efficiently and that, when interiors were treated as extensions of exteriors, they charmed few and offended most. He suggested that function should not eliminate fantasy and wondered if it might be possible “to accept the practical advantages of modernity without rejecting aesthetic traditions as the Victorians had done.” In 1981 critic Ada Louise Huxtable wrote in The New York Review of Books, “whatever comes next will be the product or inheritor of modernism . . . It will have at its heart the twentiethcentury revolution that we call modern architecture.” The following year, Robert Jensen, an academic, and Patricia Conway, an architect, interior designer and academic, published Ornamentalism: The New Decorativeness in Architecture and Design. Jensen and Conway wrote of the impulse to decorate as fundamental to the human condition, a way of identifying buildings and the cultures that create them, and that ornamental detail gave a building scale. In the foreword, Goldberger described ornamenting as a radical act in defiance of prevailing articles of faith imposed on architects and designers. ## Graves The great American exponent of PoMo was Michael Graves (1934–2015). With the Hanselmann House (1967–71) and the Snyderman House (1972; destroyed 2002) in Fort Wayne, Indiana, he had displayed his frustration with the limitations of the modernist formula. He took enthusiastically to PoMo. His work is distinguished by distortion of the proportions of classical elements and strong color. In 1979 he designed a series of showrooms for the Sunar Furniture Company, across the United States and in London, which experimented with the column and capital but avoided the more literal interpretations that Venturi had used to make his polemical points (22.9). ![](images/5ecc095a9a56b9507ee40d12b011d683b14622318f1c2bff99b78725152ad94e.jpg) Michael Graves, Sunar Furniture Company showroom, Houston, Texas, 1979. Paired columns sharing a diagrammatic capital that contains uplighters and the use of strong secondary colors makes a quintessential postmodern classical interior. As awareness of PoMo grew, Graves was commissioned to design large new buildings in which he evolved an architectural language that did not imitate any particular style but succeeded in matching the richness of form, color, and materials of pre-modernist architecture. His buildings had a subliminal familiarity and for each project he provided a complementary interior with appropriate adjustments of scale and visual complexity. He evolved a language that was uniquely his. It was humane and could be compared with Art Deco, for its visual richness and popular appeal. Architects and designers still committed to modernist principles were disturbed that he was getting such commissions from major corporations, of which, the headquarters of the Humana Corporation in Louisville, Kentucky, in 1982 was one of the most spectacular (22.10). 22.10 ![](images/45081cab4067c4f3c04a0519847e3a8d401e9acf24c3158a70f6eb2d106fda17.jpg) Michael Graves, Humana Building, Louisville, Kentucky, 1982–5. This interior doorway makes no direct allusions but clearly suggests that it belongs philosophically—if ambiguously—to a long architectural tradition. Such was the excitement around postmodernism that in the early 1980s Graves and other prominent postmodernists were commissioned by the Alessi company in Italy and departmentstore chain J. C. Penney in the United States to design household objects. He contributed kettles, coffee pots, and pepper mills. He also designed furniture that could be mistaken for Art Deco. Postmodernism found a following, among designers and non-designers in Europe and beyond, much more quickly than modernism had done. It was due to the excitement caused by the first extraordinary examples—which were conceived to give pleasure to those who used them. ## AUSTRIA ## Hollein In Austria, Hans Hollein (1934–2014) was another pioneer in the search for a more palatable modern architecture. He had studied engineering and architecture in Vienna and in 1963 began his career by rejecting the principle of “form follows function.” He became the most accomplished practitioner of European postmodernism. Jencks described him as a product of finde-siècle Vienna and his work as “much funnier and with more consummate details than the American postmodernists were producing. . . .” In 1985 he won the prestigious Pritzker Architecture Prize and pronounced that architecture was an art “not primarily the solution of a problem, but the making of a statement.” In 1965–6, as Venturi was publishing his first polemic, Hollein designed the little Retti candle store in Vienna. Its aluminum façade, in which the thin entrance slot may be seen as a pictogram of a candle, an Ionic column—or a phallus—generated controversy among architects and the seemingly gratuitous imagery led to its being described by architects—dismissively—as the work of a designer. He designed two jewelry stores for the Schullin company in Vienna, both small but with dramatically ambiguous frontages. The first (1972–6) offered a grid of terrazzo panels split by a seam that metaphorically dripped gold to make an irregularly shaped door. In the second store (1981–2) a door flanked by thin columns supporting an elliptical pediment seemed to allude to classicism (22.11). ![](images/172870e244f0d28569e7516cf5ca28af76016ffdc9a910c35589607ff1538b78.jpg) Hans Hollein, Schullin II jewelry store, Vienna, 1981–2. Hollein accepts the components of a classical façade and evolves them until they belong to a different, unfamiliar language. In his Österreichisches Verkehrsbüro (Austrian Travel Agency) in Vienna (1976–9), Hollein scattered a golden domed kiosk, chromed and gilded palm trees, and a chromed cylinder that rose from the core of the fluted bass of a classical column (22.12). Two stuffed eagles hovered beneath a glazed barrel vault inspired by that in the city’s Austrian Postal Savings Bank created by Otto Wagner in the early twentieth century (see here). ![](images/5cbadf3dffdb99c4acb2026a062f272bb214dc27686de5dd6155bd56b8a53cb4.jpg) Hans Hollein, Austrian Travel Agency, Vienna, 1976–9. A collection of references to foreign places and different times, all under a ceiling inspired by Otto Wagner’s work on the Austrian Postal Savings Bank (see here). At the Venice Biennale in 1980, Hollein was one of twenty leading postmodernist designers chosen to contribute a façade to the Strada Novissima (Newest Street) and his contribution was provocative and not the most beautiful (22.13). Under a pediment that had a passing resemblance to that for his second Schullin store, he created six columns, the symbol of classical architecture, which represented the new and the worn, the relationship between natural and humanmade materials, the natural origin of the column and the strange imagination of his Austrian predecessor, Adolf Loos (see here). ![](images/b19c9457b9ddde983657bf07e48d38cb0529eef2ad039ad29d72a01544e22792.jpg) Hans Hollein, Strada Novissima, Venice Biennale, 1980. Leading postmodernists were invited to contribute a façade to the Strada Novissima. Hollein presented variations on the theme of the column. ## BRITAIN In Britain established practices followed the lead of architects and designers in the United States. Fitch & Co used PoMo in stores, DEGW used it in offices. A few young architects and designers also sensed that the rules of modernism had been dismantled. They were uninterested in pastiches of classicisms but set out in other directions that would soon influence the way commercial interior design would evolve. All had individual styles and needed clients who wanted extraordinary interiors. They found them among their contemporaries: the fledgling entrepreneurs in fashion and pop music. These game-changers were in their mid-teens during the 1960s, when norms were usurped and they were encouraged to break free from the modernist past. Three of them, David Connor, Julian Powell-Tuck and Ben Kelly, trained in provincial art schools and were more influenced by art movements than architectural dogma. Their ideas matured on the interior course at the Royal College of Art in London, which gave them a portal to a metropolitan world. They had three years to develop their creativity in a challenging environment, time in which to develop the confidence to challenge orthodoxies. ## Connor David Connor (b. 1950) was excited by German Expressionist cinema and the work of Max Clendinning (see here) but thought his generation’s responsibility was to move on from modernist orthodoxies. He cooperated with musician and clothes designer Malcolm McLaren and fashion designer Vivienne Westwood, the doyens of British punk, to create the store Seditionaries on the King’s Road in Chelsea, London, to sell punk clothes in the former premises of Granny Takes A Trip (see here). The interior was dominated by photomontages of the German city of Dresden after its destruction by Allied bombers during World War II and an inverted image of London’s tourist hub, Piccadilly Circus. In 2013 its long-vanished interior was recreated in the Museum of Modern Art in New York. In 1980 the New Romantic pop singer Adam Ant, who was then at the zenith of his fame and managed by McLaren, commissioned Connor to design his apartment in London. Connor gave him a stage set. Marbling, a paint technique that replicates the appearance of the stone was applied to all hard surfaces by specialist painter, David Campion. Armchairs were overstuffed and draped in bespoke fabric, as if to protect them in the unexplained absence of the owner. Drapes were too long and spilled across the floor. An empty picture frame leant against a wall. The apartment was moodily melancholic, which was very New Romantic and postmodern (22.14). ![](images/4fae14aae9cfe09e36b5151e961c48a04a5b4631c4011ddc70ce3fab39957318.jpg) David Connor, interior, Adam Ant Apartment, London, 1981. The walls, ceilings, and all hard surfaces were transfigured with a faux-marble paint finish. Fabrics were designed and printed to harmonize. Connor’s next project, in 1981, was the large entrance area of another London apartment for another musician, Marco Pirroni, but instead of faux-marble precision, walls were daubed with abstract slashes of paint made with unequivocal decorative intent by Madeleine Palme, a Swedish painter. It was German Expressionist punk and contradicted perspective: doors were twisted out of shape, tops of base boards sloped sharply, and radiators were hung askew (22.15). 22.15 ![](images/d668bbc672adca7a4eb3ec420d7e6a72afe4716489cbbf9a6f944d212b797e44.jpg) David Connor, Marco Pirroni Apartment, London, 1982. Rather than craft finish, there are abstract paint daubs by Madeleine Palme on all the surfaces, including the recycled chairs that Connor claimed were upholstered with dog fur. His admiration for German Expressionist cinema inspired the distorted doors, the sloping base boards that contradicted perspective, and the radiator hung at an angle. Young designers and students were shocked and delighted by these two projects which seemed to be the antithesis of the sensible, problem-solving modernism they had been taught. Imaginations and creative ambitions were unlocked. A few years after they had graduated from the Royal College of Art Connor drifted into a professional partnership in the late 1970s with Julian Powell-Tuck who described it as a “mutual arrangement . . . we collaborated when the work was there . . . there was no business plan . . . it was basically just dealing with stuff that presented itself.” With that attitude and concentration on creative output, they were the most influential practice in London in the first half of the 1980s. ## Powell-Tuck Julian Powell-Tuck (b. 1950), in collaboration with the architect Pierre d’Avoine (b. 1951), maintained his practice’s commitment to applied decoration. In an office for Research Recordings, a video-editing company, they invited the textile designer, Sally Greaves-Lord (b. 1957), another Royal College graduate, to decorate the reception area. She painted walls with intricate abstractions and designed the patterns cut into the linoleum floor (22.17). The outcome, with its precise changes of scale and strategic inserts of sharp color, was as extraordinary as the faux marble in the Adam Ant apartment or the expressionistic gestures in the Marco Pirroni apartment. It was made to be beautiful. 22.17 ![](images/6e6664137d0e7619d03e368682ccb920e8d7d4fc3bbe5ace81784f528aa580b4.jpg) Powell-Tuck, Connor Orefelt & D’Avoine (design), Sally Greaves Lord (wall painting and chair fabric in the main double height reception), Research Recordings, London, 1984. In the reception area of this modestly sized project, Greaves-Lord demonstrated that foursquare functional solids could be transformed by precisely detailed decoration in an inspired rebuttal of white walls that gave the project its identity and success. Powell-Tuck created his own masterwork at the end of the 1980s, with the conversion of a lofty bus depot in London to a recording studio. He split the volume on its long axis to make a fullheight atrium that was dominated by a twisting steel stair. Two floors of studios and recreational rooms presented a canted plywood-clad façade to the atrium. Concrete walls, cast in situ, provided sound-deadening mass. Studios were splayed on plan and section in accordance with acoustic principles and surface detailing dealt with reverberation (22.16). ![](images/fc6e25314dfb0b113458063588b0d0ddee256031877737d8f79e7ccc2a5b9f4a.jpg) Powell-Tuck, Connor & Orefelt, interior, Metropolis Recording Studios, London, 1989. The mezzanine café for breaks between recording sessions overlooking the atrium. The plywood panels at the upper level clads the studios. ## Kelly Ben Kelly (b. 1949) cited artists Marcel Duchamp and Andy Warhol as influences, both of whom elevated the mundane to the extraordinary. He brought the ideas of creative recycling and repurposing to interior design and demonstrated how to apply them in the design of a clothes store in central London for the retailer Paul Howie in 1977 (22.18) and to The Haçienda, a nightclub in the northern industrial city of Manchester, in 1982 (22.19). The club inhabited an abandoned warehouse, of which Manchester had many. The building’s brick exterior enclosed a huge volume under a glazed roof, and Kelly hyped a few strategic elements. He painted cast-iron columns with diagonal stripes and the edges of the wooden dance floor were marked by black and white bollards. Balcony supports were clad in sheets of plywood, sometimes painted, sometimes not. Newsweek magazine called it the most famous club in the world in the 1990s. Kelly continues to retune existing elements and make the mundane glamorous. He describes his projects as installations, maintaining that, in interior design, art is more important than architecture. ![](images/e3f6df25b0da75137c863b4087c58f796a27c6bb1a9f7114df1f7123c096ba06.jpg) ## 22.18 Ben Kelly, Howie store, London, 1977. In the spirit of French artist Marcel Duchamp, Kelly’s found materials and gadgets make a rough and robust proto High-tech interior. ![](images/eadc14e0c5937472f8a7cfb4c2c0f1336df43a14a4cdcbe5d073edf1f3163168.jpg) 22.19 Ben Kelly, The Haçienda, Manchester, England, 1982. Paint and pattern pick out and elevate existing elements in the nightclub. The black and red painted cladding to the mezzanine structure was one of a series of totems that were variations on the color theme. ## Jiricna Eva Jiricna (b. 1939) trained as an architect and engineer in her native Czechoslovakia but relocated to London in 1968 as a political refugee and built her reputation as a High-tech designer. She worked with the architect Lord Richard Rogers on the interiors of his Lloyd’s Building in London (completed in 1986) while establishing her own practice—in partnership with Australian interior designer Kathy Kerr until 1987—and beginning a long series of store interiors for the Moroccan-born fashion retailer Joseph Ettedgui giving him white walls, smooth-tiled floors and black and chrome furniture, which established his brand and complemented his fashion collections. These established her national and international reputations and her meticulous detailing attracted commissions for jewelry stores and museums. Her early training in engineering has driven the series of increasingly delicate stairs that have become her signature pieces. They are the spectacular center pieces that elevate the Joseph stores above the minimalism that prevails in high-end fashion stores and, in ancient buildings, their alien presence complements and is complemented by the old walls that contain them (22.20 and 22.21). ![](images/f954c1f8d85d870068c4ace7bca9ccc993202cd4d4dc5b102dd5ca74e5a1527b.jpg) Eva Jiricna, Somerset House, London, 2015. As her work has developed, she has progressively eroded elements. ## Pawson John Pawson (b. 1949) is the quintessential exponent of minimalism, which he describes as ‘‘the perfection an artifact achieves when it is no longer possible to improve it by subtraction.” He suggests that simplicity has a moral dimension that is shared across cultures. His walls and ceilings are white and devoid of moldings, floors are wide wooden planks or square tile grids, natural light may be filtered through translucent glass, artificial light sources wash across perfectly flat white surfaces, and his stair treads and risers meet in right angles and are squeezed into narrow, high-walled slots. Objects are stored in cupboards that lack obvious means of opening (22.22). 22.22 ![](images/cad8a05a424de705dbcd8bd5c5ef30f00e8c702dfd2fa7c8b91bdfc4e57e76ae.jpg) John Pawson, interior, own home, London, 1996–9. The ground-floor living area exemplifies Pawson’s aesthetic. The floor is honey-colored limestone, which is also used for stairs, wall benches, worktops, baths, and basins. A narrow shadow-gap recess separates hypersmooth white walls and floor. Utilitarian objects are stored in a wall of floor-to-ceiling cupboards that also conceals the narrow stair connecting the four levels of the house. The oak-framed, white-upholstered bench conforms to prevailing orthogona principles. Pawson made his name with austere dwellings but his stripped-back aesthetic works well for fashion stores. In 1993–5 he designed the Calvin Klein Collections Store in New York, filling the interior with lofty white towers (22.23). For the Jigsaw chain of fashion stores in London he subdivided floors with sheets of translucent glass that filter daylight and blur the untidiness of clothing racks. His work is assumed to have a spirituality that has brought commissions from religious bodies. He has designed an installation in St. Paul’s Cathedral in London, in 2011, and an abbey in Bohemia (1999–2004), but his status as the begetter of fashionable minimalism has also led to his designing kitchenware. ![](images/1afe4bce59af1fd6982668de6f4272103361dcab430b96803b4252887822ccac.jpg) John Pawson, interior, Calvin Klein Collections Store, New York, 1993–5. The tall windows on two sides of the store made the control of natural light difficult but the enormous piers that sit in orderly fashion throughout the space give new surfaces to provide light and shadow—and give order. ## Coates Nigel Coates (b. 1949) admired the Festival of Britain’s “clunky but kind of dynamic fusion of art, science and narrative” and thought postmodernism a call to arms. His London apartment, with high ceilings, generous moldings, and exotic artifacts, among unpainted pink plaster walls and doors partially stripped to reveal successive layers of ancient paints, was published in a Japanese lifestyle magazine and he was asked to design a Tokyo restaurant, Metropole, in 1985. He responded with an assemblage of European artifacts: columns with capitals of twisted-steel reinforcing bars, black armchairs under a chandelier in front of a marble fireplace. It was entered through a proscenium arch and its walls were hung with antique casts and mirrors that stretched it to infinity. Café Bongo opened in 1986 on Tokyo’s busiest pedestrian corner. Its collage of hanging draperies, painted ceilings, and trashy objects sealed in acrylic floors evoked European decadence (22.24). A stretched facsimile of an airplane wing curved around the room providing a plinth for classical statues before bursting through the façade to become an entrance canopy. This quasicatastrophic collision of tradition and technology astonished other designers, who could only wonder both at Coates’s nerve and that there was a client who would build his design. ![](images/fdbe2fdf08e7388f5de66fb61dc908f0626a4445d4af6ac081d814209e5eb049.jpg) 22.24 Nigel Coates, Café Bongo, Tokyo, 1986. The antidote to Japanese minimalism, Café Bongo is an ironic collage of European culture. An airplane wing wraps around the room before it crashes through the façade to become a canopy above the entrance. It is supported on raked columns that suggest harps and acts as a plinth for facsimiles of classical sculptures. The ceiling painting would not be out of place in an Italian palazzo. Coates’s tsunami of Japanese projects brought him British projects. He challenged fashion retailing’s commitment to sparse white rooms. In 1986 Lord Norman Foster (b. 1935) gave the fashion designer Katharine Hamnett a top-lit store in London with white walls, towering mirrors, and racks of clothes distributed casually across the concrete floor. A few years later Coates’s interior for Hamnett, in a conventional store unit, was a conundrum of draped fabrics, etched glass, abstract floor patterns and fish tanks, which was much more to Hamnett’s taste (22.25). Foster gave her a magnificently definitive Foster, a hard-edged empty room; Coates gave her something ephemeral, of its moment, like fashion itself—and complex enough to take seasonal tuning without losing its character. At first glance, Coates’s interiors seemed to be overloaded, with elements and incidents, an aesthetic tension that made them compelling. ![](images/d5d11671ebca41d9c3221ce807fac28751294391fce9b7be11165326c0a59387.jpg) 22.25 Nigel Coates, Katharine Hamnett store, London, 1988. The starting point here is Art Deco, or perhaps 1950s haute-couture salon, yet there is no doubt that it belongs to the late 1980s because the mix is too rich, the motifs too flamboyant, and the lighting and the fish tanks are too incongruous. ## Collins David Collins (1955–2013) was another architectural scion for whom modernism was simply another style that he added to his mix when appropriate. A Dubliner, he was not part of London’s inner architectural circle but in the mid 1980s his pronounced social skills brought him rich and adventurous clients for whom he created sumptuous interiors that were ignored by professional publications but welcomed in fashion magazines. When his house for an art collector, in an expensive and fashionable area in London, was published a stream of commissions for grand private houses followed (22.26). At first glance, the various elements are conventional but then the fine-tuning becomes clear. The marble that frames the fireplace stretches beyond the edges of the chimney breast, the flat, plain cornice is acutely angled, everything has been tweaked. If it had to be labeled, it is closest to Art Deco. ![](images/792940ee54fac16c0efab42d8161056ceae5f3e122bb5fdf807627f04d4549ff.jpg) David Collins, interior, apartment, London, 2008. His other forte was designing smart restaurants but he was equally successful creating sandwich bars. He designed restaurant chains that aimed to satisfy the aspirations of the modestly affluent to have better food in better places, where atmosphere was more important than cuisine. He knew how to plan and decorate to make every restaurant table desirable and to create the appropriate atmosphere. His strategic planning and superficially retrospective detailing perfectly supported twenty-first-century informalities and taste. His highly tuned aesthetic worked for haute-couture fashion designers, giving them a lush elegance that complemented their creative bravado. His design vocabulary was broad and could evoke a period that one could never be sure what that period was, as in the ambiguous nostalgia of The Wolseley brasserie in London (2003). His studio continues after his death, creating spaces such as Alexander McQueen’s Aoyama flagship store Tokyo (2014; 22.27). They also excited a lay public who found their extraordinary images in glamorous magazines. In 1982 the journalist Min Hogg (1938–2019) founded World of Interiors, the first of many such magazines and newspaper supplements that followed. The first edition featured an apartment created in London by interior designer Nicky Haslam (b. 1939) for the pop singer Bryan Ferry, an art-school graduate, whose studied elegance intrigued his fans. They, Hogg rightly anticipated, would boost sales of the magazine. While working with Italian office-equipment manufacturer Olivetti, he began to develop complex, highly colored objects that anticipated the work of the Memphis Group that he founded in 1980. Memphis was a multinational collective of young designers drawn to Milan by its reputation as a creative hub. They produced brightly colored, boldly patterned, wantonly nonfunctional objects that could only find a place in museums or as trophies for rich collectors. If American postmodernists looked at European classicism for inspiration, then Memphis looked at more recent Italian precedents, particularly the plastic laminates and terrazzo floors in the cafés that were the egalitarian heart of Italy’s daily life. In 1992, Madrid was voted European Cultural Capital, Seville staged the Expo World’s Fair, and the Olympic Games were held in Barcelona, the capital of Catalonia, the most affluent province and the center of Spanish design activity. In the 1990s Barcelona’s stores and bars attracted world attention. Post-Franco, the city decreed that its existing urban fabric should be preserved and new interiors were slotted into historical structures. The Spanish custom of the evening promenade, to socialize and display one’s style, provided impetus for fashion stores and bars. The fashion business grew faster than the wider economy and architectural graduates, without commissions for buildings, were keen to demonstrate their abilities. Alicia Núñez (b. 1955), with her Zig-Zag and Tokyo, followed the British tradition of the boutique owner–designer. The 1980s changed perceptions of what interior design might be and financial evidence confirmed its economic importance but, at the end of the decade, its apparently effortless expansion was derailed by a global recession, driven by rising oil prices and a loss of confidence by businesses and consumers. Recovery was speeded up by the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991 and the subsequent disintegration of the communist bloc, which changed the political and financial balance in Europe. New markets were opened up. A well-educated and ambitious workforce headed west. By 1993 the recession was over. However, the Japanese economy was hard hit and would not recover for twenty years. Although work in Japan for European interior designers disappeared, new opportunities appeared as China removed trade tariffs. China’s economy began an extraordinary growth spurt leading to it becoming a leading economic force and opportunities opened up for Western designers.
