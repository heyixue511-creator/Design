A glance at his flexible working habits may help us understand why such a self-assured individual as Alexey Brodovitch felt it necessary to repeatedly accede to Snow's dictates. On particular days of the week Brodovitch was permitted to spend only halfdays in the office. On these days he would undertake freelance work for other companies. However, the work he performed for these companies would sometimes eat into his duties at Bazaar - the outcome being that many of his assistants would find themselves continually making excuses for his absence. As his work outside Bazaar grew throughout the forties, Brodovitch's guilt over his frequent absenteeism probably persuaded him to silently concede to the majority of Snow's requests. Another factor that probably compounded this deferential relationship was that when Brodovitch was actually at the Bazaar offices he would frequently disappear for long liquid lunches. Ted Croner remembered one such occasion when he went with Brodovitch to “a little restaurant called La Berge [and drank] Martinis.... Brodovitch would have as many as four or more, and I would get to three. He would go back to the office and work. I went back to the studio and went out on the couch. I couldn't take it."74

His permanent rejection of the unexceptional and commonplace in design during his twenty-four-year spell at Harper's Bazaar brought him widespread attention for freelance work. Working as the art director for Saks Fifth Avenue (1939-41) and I. Miller (1941-43) gave Brodovitch a new creative freedom away from Carmel Snow and the repetitious world of fashion. At Saks (where Irving Penn was his assistant), Brodovitch understood the need to treat the store's design as an organic whole. Everything from sale advertisements to wrapping paper to the store's letterhead was produced in a visually attractive typé and style. In an ad for the Saks in-house gym, Brodovitch recycles a silhouette design used frequently on the pages of Bazaar. Fashioning the main body of text into the shape of a woman, the ad promotes the gym as a way of losing weight. With this in mind, Brodovitch emphasizes the waistline of the “woman" through the use of bold type. Finally, this dense central section is brought into strong relief by the illustration of five butterflies fluttering around the text-image. HRouGH the medium of the fashion magazine, Brodovitch had introduced into everyday American life some of the most revolutionary ideas, artists, and innovators of twentieth-century art, design, and photography. He changed forever our expectation of what could be done on the magazine page. Pursuing his objective to “irritate and intrigue" those around him, he charged a generation of photographers, designers, and art directors to be permanently inventive, to never stand still. It was this talent to inspire an idea, to spark something new in an individual's psyche, that obliged people to return again and again to Brodovitch's side. However, while he generously encouraged those artists unafraid to experiment, he was cruelly dismissive of those unable or unwillingly to commit to his philosophy of permanent change in the graphic arts. The apotheosis of this theater of invention was his famed Design Laboratory.

In 1933, three years after his arrival in Philadelphia, Brodovitch added the Design Laboratory to his classes offered at the Pennsylvania Museum School of Industrial Art. A prospectus of one of these early workshops stated that the class was “an organization for studying new materials, new ideas, devices of the past in order to establish new devices for the future, Rationalism, Logic and practicability - and their artistic interpretation to the needs of the industry." When Brodovitch began work at Harper's Bazaar in 1934, he was no longer able to maintain regular

teaching duties at PMSIA. As he had formed such a close working relationship with his students, he decided to turn over the running of the design class to Mary Fullerton and Raymond Ballinger, and the photography class to Nelson Gruppo and Ben Rose. While he had officially relinquished his teaching duties at PMSIA in 1934, he continued to return once a week to oversee his workshops. To aid his ex-students, Brodovitch drafted “Laboratory of Design Conception and Technique" to instruct them on the objectives of the class. The paper stated that the aim of the course was to study “psychology, fashion, and the market to prepare students (‘members of the Design Laboratory') not only as professional freelance artists, but also stylists and art directors."2 As this paper indicates, these early classes were open to all professions. At any one time they may have contained a designer, theater director, or even a professional model. It was an openness to vocation that was mirrored in Brodovitch's expectation of novel solutions to the various class assignments he set: “I don't care if the student produces a photograph, a design, collage or sculpture," he once said. “If he has avoided a cliché and captured the essence of the assignment, this is the important thing 993 As we observed in the previous chapter, Brodovitch's teaching encouraged students to draw on all aspects of modern life when manufacturing their graphic illustrations. The Design Laboratory was a class which served to intensify this wide-ranging approach to design education.

Brodovitch's teaching style had obviously been influenced by the pedagogical ideas of the Bauhaus movement in Europe. Central to the Bauhaus ethos was the desire to educate the whole individual by directing his or her attention/to a variety of modern solutions in their graphic projects. The Bauhaus artist Laszlo Moholy-Nagy crystallized this aim in an 1946 article entitled“New Education-Organic Approach." Published by Art and Industry in 1946, nine years after he had set up the New Bauhaus in Chicago, his article stated that with an arts education:

The main intention is to produce an adequate rhythm between the biological capacities of the student and the contemporary scene.... The exercises are generally of such nature that fthe student] cannot look for solutions in books or in museums. Because these exercises have no direct counterpart in tradition but are built around the potentialities of tools and materials, they direct his vision to new and unexplored channels. The student must use his imagination and wit, he must debate and contemplate, he must make independent findings. Since he is not allowed to imitate past solutions, he soon finds the power to face new situations fearlessly, to develop new habits of the imagination.4

A distinction can be made, however, between Brodovitch's approach to design education and the highly prescriptive teachings of many of the other European émigrés. In a conventional understanding of the word, Brodovitch did not actually “teach." Unlike Moholy-Nagy, Brodovitch in his classes and writings never set out a rigid theoretical approach to the graphic arts. In fact, paradoxically, Brodovitch perceived arts education as, on the whole, something to be avoided at all costs:

I personally am very much against, in principle, any art or visual education. It seems to me that education of this kind is very dangerous and is likely to develop certain clichés. Students have come to me from different schools. Everyone who came to me would bring a certain type of picture. From the pictures I could

tell immediately which school the student came from. This is a tragedy. The instructor or teacher should not be a pedant. What is required is someone to tease and irritate the student and help him discover himself.⁶

As an arts teacher who berated arts education, Brodovitch actively cultivated a propensity for contradicting himself. In class he would say one thing one week and dismiss it out of hand the next. Such an approach infuriated many students, but it also obliged them to think for themselves. As the Design Lab student and photographer Art Kane observed, "Brodovitch teaches you to rebel constantly and not accept any generally accepted standards. I even admired the way he contradicted himself. This irritated and annoyed me. It kept me always re-evaluating and forced me to find things out for myself. I found out from Brodovitch that you can't learn photography by the books but from yourself and from experience."7 Curious about his desire to constantly “tease and irritate" his students, Kane once asked Brodovitch what it was that inspired such an approach to teaching: “He told me to read a book by Krishnamurti called Education and the Significance of Life. It clued me in on his teaching, his thinking, and opened many doors for me. I read it over and over again."8

In Education and the Significance of Life the spiritual teacher Jiddu Krishnamurti argues that it is not until an individual learns to liberate himself from his social conditioning in terms of nationality, religion, and tradition that he will be truly free. Instead of awakening the integrated intelligence of the individual, most education, argues Krishnamurti, encourages the student to conform to a pattern and as a result hinders any comprehension of himself as a total process. One aspect of the book that must have struck a chord with Brodovitch is Krishnamurti's constant reference to the need to break with the past and establish a dialogue with the new. In a sentence that resonates with his desire to provoke those around him by repeatedly contradicting himself, Krishnamurti claimed: “A consistent thinker is a thoughtless person, because he conforms to a pattern; he repeats phrases and thinks in a groove."9 Brodovitch would have no respect for any student who simply echoed what he said in the Design Lab sessions. Ted Croner remembers that “Brodovitch could be very sharp and biting" with such individuals. He illustrated this with a brief example involving one of the few female photographers to attend his class, Karen Radkai:

Brodovitch is sitting at the end of the table, Karen is sitting right over here to the right and Brodovitch is [criticizing her photograph] and he says: “What do [you] think Mrs. Radkai?" “Oh, I agree with you completely Mr Brodovitch.” “Too bad." I mean you could feel the saber cutting through flesh. It was unnecessary, I think. But look, there was a side to him.10

Such an approach was not welcomed by all students. Many left his class after being on the receiving end of scathing remarks about their work. He was not, however, being personal. By denying the traditional master-student relationship, his aim was simply to delegate the responsibilities of both teaching and learning to the pupil. Like a Zen master, Brodovitch invested the student with the responsibility for his or her own creative development. Photographer and Design Lab member Stephen D. Colhoun realized that had Brodovitch's criticism “been a little less severe, I might have become discouraged, but it enraged me and in some peculiar way I was forced to explore many paths which I otherwise would have neglected. Brodovitch's great gift as a teacher is that somehow or other he causes people to think." " As Brodovitch once said: “I am not a teacher - I can only point the way. It is you who will do the work." 12

Throughout the mid-thirties, Brodovitch continued to commute to Philadelphia to oversee the work produced in the Design Lab sessions. However, because of his increased workload in New York he eventually ceased making these weekly trips in 1936. By this time the Design Laboratory was acknowledged as one of the most prestigious design classes in America. Because of this widespread recognition, Brodovitch was offered guest lecturing positions at Cooper Union and the Pratt Institute in 194o. Returning to education after a two-year absence obviously precipitated a desire in Brodovitch to reestablish the Design Lab class in New York. Thus, beginning in spring 1941, the class was held in the Thomas Hart Benton room under the auspices of the New School for Social Research. Brodovitch continued to split the lab into two sections per week, one speeializing in design, the other photography, with each section running for about ten weeks. The aim of these courses was, according to the catalogue, to "help the student to discover his individuality, crystallize his taste, and develop his feeling for the contemporary trend by stimulating his sense of invention and perfecting his technical ability. The course is conducted as an experimental laboratory, inspired by the ever changing tempo of life, discovery of new techniques, new fields of operation."13

The photography classes met on a Tuesday or Wednesday night and ran from 7 to 10 pm. With Brodovitch's reputation preceding him, more than sixty people would sometimes arrive at his class on the first night. However, Brodovitch always embraced the potential of photographic accidents to produced fresh and original works.

Brodovitch approved of Croner's accidental image. He recognized that with a rather straightforward subject matter Croner "brought a certain fantasy to the problem, created a strong picture, stronger than realistic attempts, which were too dry." 19He encouraged Croner to do more, especially photographs that were produced using available light. Brodovitch constantly espoused the values of this form of photography. Obviously thinking of Munkacsi's groundbreaking images for Harper's Bazaar, Brodovitch had no time for images that seemed staged or created or gimmicky. He once said that “things should be used which could happen, not things which are obviously posed, obviously artificial."20 Nevertheless, after promoting the unaffected virtues of availablelight photography, he would then confound some students by going on to advocate the value of blurring, chemical reducers, or airbrushing – anything that created “surprise value" in their images. Like Croner's New York at Night, with subjects such as Central Park or traffic, photographers were urged to use all the techniques available to produée images that conveyed an almost corporeal interpretation of the assignment. For instance, in William Helburn's take on the assignment “fantasy," he uses both softfocus and high-key tonality to produce a dreamlike image of a New York street. He wittily heightens the reverie of the image by adding two characters from Alice in Wonderland. In Ben Rose's interpretation of the project "rollercoaster" he utilizes a 36o-degree circuit camera to reproduce photographically the experience and sensation of this ride.

In our age of computer manipulation, the radical nature of Brodovitch's proposals seem commonplace. It has to be remembered, however, that around the time Brodovitch began his Design Laboratory the two dominant strands of photography were the fine art work of the f.64 group and the documentary photographs of the Farm Security Administration (FSA) – both precious about the individual image, whether as a print or document. Through the work of photographers such as Ansel Adams, Imogen Cunningham, and Edward Weston, the f.64 group promoted precisionism in the photograph. Advocating the use of large-format cameras, they shot images through a very small aperture (f.64) and printed by contact rather than enlargement.

The outcome of such a process was that the significance of the work lay in the purity of the final print. Subject matter being secondary, objects were chosen that would offer the greatest opportunity to express the values and tonal ranges of the print. While print quality was important with FSA photographers such as Dorothea Lange and Margaret Bourke-White, it was the subject matter that was paramount. Charting the ravages of the 193os depression, the worth and authenticity of the image resided in its content. While Brodovitch respected the values of the print as object or document, he was, unlike many of the above, never precious about its manipulation. As we know from his work at Harper's Bazaar, cropping a photographic image (following the photographer's approval) was just another way of achieving the right look and feel to a layout. In fact, in the Design Lab sessions he repeatedly stressed the importance of cropping as a tool:

One of the photographer’s most important tools is cropping. I know Cartier-Bresson does not approve of anyone cropping his pictures. For most photographers, however, I believe it is a mistake to refuse to crop. Photographers should make three of four prints from a negative and crop them differently. The cropping can very often create your new vision and help discover and explain your new language. I read a profile in the New Yorker of Yehudi Menuhin’s interest in Yoga, and before each concert he stands on his head. I don’t care if he does this if he plays beautiful music. It is the end result which counts.²'

Art Kane's response to the assignment “personality" illustrates what could be achieved through cropping. A darkened image of a cab driver looking right into the lens is given added contrast by the cab's white roof. The rectangular cropping of the photograph sets up a duality between the dark and light aspects of the frame and lends an almost sinister aspect to the cab driver's gaze.

For Brodovitch, deciding which image would benefit from being eropped came with an examination of the contacts of a shoot. He always urged his students to look through their contacts and choose those images that expressed a personal point of view. He said that the picture chosen should “be a completely individual expression which intrigues the viewer and forces him to think."22
