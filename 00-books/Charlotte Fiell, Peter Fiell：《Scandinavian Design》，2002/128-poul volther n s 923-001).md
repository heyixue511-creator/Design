Upon graduating in 1949 he established his own architecture and design practice, which he operated until 1985. Over his career Volther designed numerous pieces of furniture, including an elegant teak dining chair (1951)for FDB(Association of Danish co-operative stores), which can be seen as a radical reworking of the traditional ladder-back chair. A decade later he used similar floating elements for his wellknown Corona chair (1961) which were reminiscent of time-lapse photographs of solar eclipses. Constructed of moulded plywood upholstered with cold-cured polyurethane foam, these elliptical seat pads could also be used as armrests. The frame of the chair to which they were attached was first produced in solid oak, but in 1962 this was changed to chromed spring steel. Similar to the upholstered furniture designed by Arne Jacobsen and VernerPanton, the Corona chair, which was and still is manufactured by Erik Jørgensen, exemplified the trend among the Danish avant-garde away from the re-working of existing furniture types towards a more sculptural and “synthetic" language of design. But while forward-looking designs such as these embodied the optimistic spirit of the period, they did not embrace the Pop culture ethos of ephemerality and disposability – they were highquality products intended to stand the test of time. Beyond his architecture and design work, Volther also taught at the Kunsthåndværkerskolen (until 1953) and later at the School of Furnishing and the Polytechnic in Copenhagen between 1955 and 1980. As a leading Danish architect, he was also a member of the Academic Architects' Society from 1955. The origins of this remarkable company can be traced to the 1920s, when Sweden was still heavily reliant on car imports – at the time between 12,000 to 14,500 automobiles were being imported annually. To remedy this situation, Gustaf Larson (1887-1968)and Assar Gabrielsson (1891-1962) decided to join forces and produce their own automobiles in the summer of 1924. Gabrielsson had previously studied economics and was a successful businessman, while Larson was an engineer and a designer who had worked on the development of Morris engines at White & Poppe in Coventry, England. In 1926, they came up with the idea of designing high quality car components that would be manufactured to their exacting specifications by selected suppliers, and then assembling them with the assistance of experienced car builders. Having secured the necessary financing from the company for which they both worked, Larson and Gabrielsson acquired factory premises and established their own firm, which they named Volvo (Volvo meaning “I roll” in Latin). Their first series-manufactured car, the ÖV4, left their factory in Gothenburg in April 1927, thus marking an important turning point in Swedish industrial design history. Nicknamed Jakob, this first Volvo production car was based on contemporaneous American models. With its strong chassis and four-cylinder engine, the Jakob had a top speed of 90 kph, but sales were slow during the first year partly because the car's high build quality meant that it was relatively expensive – the opentopped version cost 4,800 Swedish krona, while the sedan cost 5,800 Swedish krona. In 1929 Volvo introduced its first six-cylinder model, the PV651 – “PV” meaning PersonVagn (People Wagon) – which had a more robust chassis than the Jakob and was especially favoured by taxi fleets. In 1935 the company launched the streamlined PV36 designed by Ivan Örnberg (1881– 1936), who had spent the whole of his career working in the US car industry. With its integrated headlamps and sleek design the PV36, which was also known as the Carioca, was undoubtedly inspired by Carl Breer's famous Airflow of 1934. During the Second World War Volvo produced several military vehicles, including a jeep, and also developed a number of civilian prototypes. 1944 saw the launch of the PV444, which combined American styling with an unconventional, unitized all-steel body. Developed by Helmer Petterson (1901-1980), the PV444 was the first Volvo that “could be afforded by the average-man-in-thestreet”. In 1956, the classic 120(known as the Amazon in Scandinavia) was introduced and featured numerous innovative safety features, including a padded dashboard. This stylish design was exhibited at the New York Motor Show in the spring of 1956, and within three months Volvo had gained more than one hundred dealers in America. With its emphasis on safety, in 1959 Volvo became the first manufacturer in the world to equip all its cars with three-point safety belts which were invented by Nils Bohlin and lessened the risk of internal injuries by having the buckle placed on the side rather than over the abdomen. During the 1960s and 1970s, models such as the 144 sedan (1966), 145 estate (1967), and 240/260 series(1974) became best-sellers while pioneering other innovative safety features such as disc brakes all round, split steering columns and outer rear-seat three-point safety belts. More recently, Volvo's truck division has introduced its VN series of vehicles, which has taken the US market by storm: Way ahead of its competitors in terms of design, Volvo has developed a truck that is renowned as much for its safety as for its comfort. With its roomyand ingeniously planned integrated cab and sleeper compartment, within a yearof its launch the VN defiedall expectationsand became the best-sellingtruckin the United States. As a leading Scandinavian company, Volvo has always been guided by a humanocentric approach to design that has dismissed superfluous styling in favour of product integrity and high build quality. This approach has also ensured that Volvo cars have become renowned as durable workhorses that just keep on going. Indeed, Volvo has historically added much value to its brand and enjoyed increasing success and brand loyalty by raising the overall durability of its products. Thus annual sales of used Volvo cars actually surpass the number of new vehicles it produces each year (around 400,000). The huge and ever-increasing secondary market for its vehicles and branded parts is massively profitable for the company. But while durability equals profitability at Volvo, the company's success can also be attributed to its outstanding reputation for safety. From the seat belt tensioner and the integrated child booster seat to the self-adjusting belt reel and the integrated side impact protection system, Volvo's innovative design programme has afforded its drivers and passengers superior protection and in so doing has set the standard in the automotive industry. Pia Wallén Form, in 1979 before studying fashion design from 1981 to 1983 at the Beckman School of Design in Stockholm. Following this, she designed exhibitions for the Museum of Architecture in Stockholm and was commissioned to participate in the nationwide project “Knitting with Tradition and Rejuvenation” (1985–1986). Since 1983 she has explored in depth the textural and sculptural properties of various textiles – especially felt, which she describes as “the oldest form of textile material" and as a "material of survival" —with the objective of reviving traditional skills through the development of innovative contemporary products. In 1989 Wallén received the Designer of the 90s Award from the Swedish magazine Sköna Hem, and in 1992 she was commissioned to design the Felt Programme for Oggetti Cappellini. Since 1998 Asplund has retailed a number of her product designs, including her ubiquitous Felt slippers with distinctive contrasting stitching and her Krux blankets and rug, which use a cross motif as a “powerful symbol” that transcends global cultures. In 1998 she also designed a range of sleek silver jewellery with felt linings. She has participated in numerous exhibitions, including “New Scandinavia" in Cologne (1999), “Swedish Style in Tokyo” (1999) and “Design World 2000” in Helsinki Her more recent work includes the Dot Collection (2000), which focuses on tennis ball construction and comprises clothes, bags, bowls, and jewellery in different coloured felts.
