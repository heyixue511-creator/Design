Additionally, all three artists utilized these publications to explain and promote the work of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum, in some cases drawing parallels between the Museum's work and their own artistic projects.48 Between 1929 and 193o all three artists produced essays for avant-garde journals in their respective languages, elucidating the principles of the Vienna Method, reproducing examples of charts, and considering the relationship between the free and applied work.

The journal A bis Z was particularly significant in this regard. During the course of its three-and-a-half year run (from October 1929 to February 1933), it featured the work of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum on five separate occasions (along with passing references in other issues).4º Images of the Museum's work first appeared in the journal's May 193o issue, accompanying an essay titled Bewegung in Kunst und Statistik' (Movement in art and statistics), wherein Arntz reflected upon the changing social roles that painters and statisticians would play as contemporary society underwent political transformation.

As was typical of all Wendingen issues, the story was largely told through the illustrations themselves. Thus, the large-scale reproduction on the issue's first page of Arntz's wall painting for the Museum immediately linked Vienna and the work of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum with the international artistic current here designated by the term ‘sociological graphics'. The image of Arntz's painting is followed by twelve reproductions from Gesellschaft and Wirtschaft, and more than twenty additional examples of sociological graphics, including works by Alma, Seiwert, Tschinkel, El Lissiztky, and Helios Gómez. The atlas charts illustrating the first section of Alma's brief, two-part introductory essay, Pictorial statistics and sociological graphics', appear to have been selected both for the diversity of conceptual and stylistic approaches they represent, as well as for their thematic variety. Similarly, the survey of sociological graphics provided by the selected prints and drawings reveals great stylistic diversity within the movement, demonstrating that the search for universal and collective forms did not, in fact, preclude individual differences in artistic temperament, despite the rhetoric of anti-individualism that prevails throughout the artists' writings.

Produced during Arntz's initial period of long-distance collaboration with Neurath, Zwölf Häuser der Zeit features twelve architectural cross-sections, each representing a different type of social institution. The institutions chosen by Arntz vary widely and represent the prevailing social order in a number of different ways. Generally speaking, though, the depicted settings seem to fall into two broad categories: the first relates directly to fundamental relations of production, power, and authority; this category includes a factory, prison, army barracks, bank, hospital, and apartment house.s⁵ The second category, to which the remaining prints in the series belong, relates more obliquely to relations of production and power; this category revolves around culture, consumption, and entertainment, and includes a department store, stadium, theater, hotel, bar, and bordello.⁵⁶

The typological approach, which figures so prominently in the Zwölf Häuser der Zeit series, is evident not only in the categorization of institutions, but also in the types of figures that inhabit those institutions. Rather than particular individuals, these faceless, identical figures represent social categories, distinguished from one another only by their respective uniforms and compositional placement. In Arntz's 'Stadion' (Stadium), for example, figures are distributed within the arena's seating according to type: the upper echelons of society – military officials, politicians, business elites and their wives – occupy the front tiers; behind them, in greater numbers but with fewer distinguishing characteristics, sit the masses, indicated only by their workers' caps and fedoras. This typological approach is even more pronounced in Alma's 1929 woodcut series Acht portretten, reproduced in its entirety in the Wendingen issue. There is some irony in the title, given the contrast between the connotations of individuality associated with portraiture and the series' reduction of individual figures to symbols for their respective professions. In this case the categories are limited to professions associated with power, wealth, and authority: military official, government minister, diplomat, banker, priest, lawyer, judge, and jailer.⁵7

The depiction of figures as social types was a central feature, not only in figurative-constructivist artworks, but within pictorial statistics as well. Among the pages from Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft reproduced in Wendingen, the chart depicting Social subdivision in Vienna’ is exemplary in this respect (see image overleaf). It presents a cross-section of society organized according to profession, charting developments between the eighteenth and twentieth centuries. By classifying the population of Vienna along professional lines, the social impact of industrialization becomes immediately evident: certain professional categories, such as nobility, clergy, and itinerant workers appear to have dropped out (or at least constitute such a small proportion of the population that they no longer register), replaced by new categories such as industrial workers and government administrators. Seen through this typological lens, figures in both sociological graphics and pictorial statistics become collective protagonists, representing class dynamics in ways that individually recognizable actors could not – or would be less effective in so doing.

Beyond its illustrations, the Wendingen issue is especially noteworthy for Alma's introductory essay, particularly the second section, which self-consciously articulates sociological graphics' unifying features and common objectives. Above all, these artists employed what Alma described as a ‘sociological perspective' in their work, taking subjects (here Alma used the term 'data') similar to those handled by pictorial statistics: ‘movements of the masses', relations of ‘wealth-poverty, labor-capital, rural and urban life', and the conflicts arising from the contradiction of class interests'. Such a perspective, according to Alma, required an emotional distance from the subject matter – an attitude of 'objectivity' (zakelijkheid). For Alma (as well as for Tschinkel), objectivity in art meant the elimination of individualizing features that

might express emotional or subjective content, and their replacement with generalized characteristics.'We are interested in worker X', Alma explained, not for his private life but as a social driving force in a factory with 15,ooo workers who are jointly producers and class warriors'. Likewise, 'the personal interests of a factory owner do not interest us, but the fact that the goods he produces have an important place in the world order, thereby becoming a source of power and making him a "socially” influential man'. From this 'objective' vantage point, Alma could see little room for the accommodation of subjective expression or, it must be added, purely aesthetic' considerations. He was careful, moreover, to distinguish his notion of objectivity (which, like pictorial statistics, aimed to document general trends) from the kind associated with the Neue Sachlichkeit (New objectivity) in painting. The latter tendency, Alma argued, foregrounded the particularities of surface appearance at the expense of social content. Yet this approach retained its appeal among mass audiences, who, Alma lamented, were still too under the spell of the traditions of naturalism and individualism'.s8

Tschinkel was similarly critical of the Neue Sachlichkeit painters' reliance on naturalism – a tendency that he, like Alma, associated with an anachronistic individualism. Tschinkel articulated this position in his article ‘Quantity-pictures and collective form', published in Výtvarné Snahy, where it was illustrated with ‘a selection of sociological graphics' from the collection of the Gesellschafts-und Wirtschaftsmuseum.⁵ In place of the emphasis on appearances that characterized the painting of the Neue Sachlichkeit, Tschinkel advocated a new collective' mode of figuration, free of naturalistic detail and private-subjective contents'. In developing this collective form', he found a model in modernist typography: ‘The letter d is all the more a letter d’, Tschinkel noted, the closer it approaches its standard form, and becomes less so when it is individually adorned'.6º Typography, in this case, offered more than a mere analogy; indeed, modernist typography provided an assortment of forms that could serve as the basis for the new figuration. The New Typography movement, for which Jan Tschichold's 1928 book Die neue Typographie became the seminal text, advocated a position that embraced standardization in production and demanded that function (rather than purely aesthetic considerations) dictate form. Such ideas had great appeal for the Progressives and there exist many points of intersection and collaboration between them and the designers who championed New Typography: Výtvarné Snahy, the journal in which Tschinkel first published his essay on 'collective form', carried the first Czech translations of Tschichold's text in 1929, one year after the book's publication in Germany. Ladislav Sutnar, the journal's editor and designer as well as Tschinkel's long-time colleague and collaborator, was one the major proponents of the new typography beginning in October 1930, adopted Paul Renner's Futura typeface for captions, subheadings, page numbers, and footer. Again, the points of overlap and intersection are numerous: Seiwert had been featured in Tschichold's 1928 book and later participated alongside Tschichold in the 1932 'Modern typography' exhibition in Warsaw and Łódź. Futura, moreover, had earlier been adopted as the official typeface for publications of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in Vienna, having been recommended to Neurath by Tschichold and put into use under Arntz's direction.
