The first ambitious book undertaken by Updike during the 189os was the Altar Book for the Episcopal Church, and from the beginning it was conceived as a homage to the Kelmscott Press. In August 1893 Updike wrote to Morris, I am engaged in the printing of a devotional book which I sincerely desire to make the most perfect piece of printing that is possible. In Germany there was rapid recognition of the importance of the Kelmscott Press, which inspired a flourishing private-press movement in the first decade of the new century. A few years earlier Walker, Johnston, and Eric Gill also contributed their skills to a series of German classics issued by the Insel Verlag of Leipzig, a commercial publisher. The Morrisian influence, as mediated through his associates and disciples, was strong in Germany until 1914, and after the war, in a backward flow of ideas, some English printers received their first exposure to Morris's typographical principles through German books. Other European countries also reacted enthusiastically to the Kelmscott Press books. Switzerland, at this time under German cultural sway, tended to reflect the typographical fashions of its larger neighbour. In order for private presses to move beyond the Victorian parlour, it had to be demonstrated that there was a substantial number of book-lovers who were prepared to purchase handsomely printed volumes. The success of the Kelmscott Press proved this was so. At the same time Morris.supplied an ideology, rooted in Ruskinian thought and the Arts and Crafts movement, which justified the hand-labour required by such undertakings. The nature of Morris's contribution to modern book-design may still inspire debate, but it is indisputable that he is the progenitor of nearly all twentieth-century private presses that have emphasized fine printing. Naturally these presses have evolved in various ways, and few of them today are similar in every respect to the Kelmscott or Doves model. After the economic collapse of 1929 the bibliophilic market shrank dramatically, making it unthinkable to produce works on the scale of the Kelmscott Chaucer, the Doves Bible, or the Ashendene Dante. Many of the private presses in Britain and the United States in the period following the Second World War embodied, in fact, a temporary reversion to the Daniel ideal of the hobbyist printer distributing slender little volumes to his family and friends. But that pattern has changed yet again in recent decades, and today the private presses seem to be prospering once more, flourishing with amazing vitality in such centres as Leicester, Iowa City, and San Francisco. One major difference between the earlier Kelmscott-type presses and contemporary private presses, however, is that the latter are less likely to have any direct influence on the world of commercial printing. The reason for this appears to be that their respective printing technologies have diverged so abruptly in the last two or three decades that they no longer have much in comnon except certain problems of designing. The hand-press methods still used by the majority of private printers and the automated realm of offset presses and computerized typesetting simply represent two separate worlds, and, despite occasional attempts to bridge the gap - such as the University of California's admirable lithographic reprints of private-press books like the Arion Press Moby Dick and the Pennyroyal Alice volumes — it seems unlikely that any private press in the future will ever have the impact on printing and publishing at large that the Kelmscott and Doves Presses and their ilk once did. This is an unhappy development, since there is unmistakable evidence of deteriorating typographical standards at the moment as a result of the new technologies. Except for Blake, who rejected letterpress printing altogether, Morris was the first English artist of major stature who succeeded in controlling every aspect of the printing of a book from types to bindings. He did so not only because he had a passion for thoroughness but also because it seemed to him the only way to achieve that unity of effect which he and Walker regarded as the primary characteristic of fine printing. Two centuries earlier Joseph Moxon, in his Mechanick Exercises on the Whole Art of Printing, had offered a definition – often since quoted - of the typographer (or, as we might say today, the book-designer) as ‘such a one, who by his own Judgement, from solid reasoning with hinself, can either perform, or direct others to perform from the beginning to the end, all the Handy-works and Physical Operations relating to Typographie'.s7 With increasing automation and specialization in modern printing, there has been an ever greater need for one man or wonan to orchestrate all the multitudinous activities now required to produce a book: Morris, it might be argued, invented (or reinvented, if we take Moxon into account) the role of book-designer. Yet such a claim on his behalf has troubling implications, because it suggests an acceptance of the division of labour which Morris, as a disciple of Ruskin, found abhorrent. Peter Floud, in a talk on Morris delivered on BBC radio in 1954 and later printed in the Listener, made the point that though Morris's designs [for fabrics and wallpapers] were certainly executed by hand, they are not examples of individual craftwork, but rather what one can call serial hand-production on a factory basis, which makes full use of the division oflabour and differs very little in its moral and social implications from straightforward machine-production'.s8 Floud may have put his case in strong language, but there can be no doubt that he has discovered the essential discrepancy between Morris's theory and practice. A similar analysis of the Kelmscott Press would show that Morris's printers were compelled to follow the typographical patterns and designs he devised in his study at Kelmscott House rather than to express the roughness and majesty of their own souls (as Ruskin phrased it in The Nature of Gothic). On the rare occasions when they were allowed complete freedom of choice, the Kelmscott printers produced something conspicuously hideous like the Wayzegoose menus. But it is almost too easy to point out the inconsistencies of Morris's system ofideas. If we find Morris the thinker irritating, we can do what Pevsner, Morison, and others have done: ignore the intellectual foundations of his medievalism and concentrate instead on Morris the precursor of twentieth-century functionalism. But to do so is surely to ignore the richness and complexity of the man and to isolate only a small part ofhis mind that happens to suit our modern tastes. In the end the Kelmscott Press is important to us for reasons that go beyond the technical and aesthetic innovations described in earlier chapters of this book. The Press still fascinates us today because of the profound questions that Morris posed about the triumph of the machine. Morris saw that more was at stake than merely the revival of attractive printing, and it is the reverberation of his ideas, quite apart from whether one is personally drawn to the Kelmscott Press books, that makes the books so extraordinarily compelling in their appeal. Although he was unable to foresee the specific technology of the next century — films, radio, television, video cassettes, computers — Morris once expressed to Burne-Jones, in a characteristically jocular way, a dark vision of the future of the printed book in modern civilization: he railed fiercely against the invention of printing and gave it a hundred years at most to come to an end. Already said he the magazines are driving books out of the field — presently newspapers will have killed magazines — then the telephone will come — bottles of talk — & the newspapers will be ended, & thats a comfort — as a centipede eats a cockroach & a cockroach eats a bug — a hundred years will do it said he. . . .'s9 The Kelmscott books are imperfect creations, produced by occasionally uneasy compromises with modern technology and often flawed by Morris's hatred of all post-medieval thought and art. But from our point of view today, a century later, they represent a logical and, on the whole, satisfying culmination of the art offine printing that began with Gutenberg. Of course Morris was wrong in a literal sense: books that are in themselves works ofart are still being printed and will continue to be printed in the future. But Morris's astonishingly accurate prediction of the death of the book as a central cultural artefact in the twentieth century, with moral and social consequences we cannot yet gauge, makes the Kelmscott Press seem, more and more, like the end rather than the beginning of something good.
