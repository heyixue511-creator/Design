Upon completing her studies she joined the design team at the Riihimäki glassworks. Her work there included both blown “art” glass and moulded production glassware that was distinguished by unusual forms, textured surface decoration and functional utility. As Still McKinney noted, “Practicality has always been extremely important to me. I cannot make impractical objects. You must be able to wash them, and a beer glass must sit comfortably in the hand." At Riihimäki she also felt “a special urge to create colour”, so in collaboration with the chemical laboratory there she developed a number of memorable colours, including a brilliant turquoise that had been inspired by a shard of glass she found on a beach while holidaying in Capri. Her subsequent product designs, such as the cobalt blueHarlekiini range(1958), which included covered jars, jugs and glasses, and the warm amber coloured Ambra candlesticks (1961), which were included in the“Finlandia” exhibition that toured Europe (1961–1962), signalled a new and brilliantly coloured phase in Finnish glass-making.Some of Still McKinney's later blow-moulded glass designs, such as her Fantasma vases(1968–1971)and her daisypatterned decanter and shot glasses (c. 1970), were remarkable for their highly textured surfaces and bold relief decoration that reflected the informality of the period. She continued designing glassware for Riihimäki until 1976, when the glassworks discontinued its production of blow-moulded glass to concentrate solely on the mass-manufacture of commercial glass, such as bottles and jars. Although Still McKinney adopted glass as her material of choice, stating “It has many more aspects than anything else – stone, marble or bronze”, she revealed her extraordinary versatility as a designer by working in a number of other materials, such as metal, enamel, plastics and wood, and other disciplines, including ceramics, graphics, jewellery andlighting. Her polished teak and cane salad servers of 1954, for example, testify to her remarkable sense of line and handling of materials, while her Good Morning breakfast set (c. 1964) for La Porcelaine de Baudour – with its “tongued” lids to enable better control while pouring – reveal an innovative approach to ![](images/0174d5436902b5ce00c341d9fa9b48e9f5a65d9dbe51fb2e9527febaf67057db.jpg) functional problem solving. Still McKinney also designed ceramics for Cerabel-Porcelain, Heinrich Porzellan, and Rosenthal, glassware for Val Saint-Lambert, plastic products for Sarvis, and cookware for Hackman. Its “humble production” of chairs and sofas was significantly upgraded in 1936 when a new factory was established that enabled much higher rates of production. In 1955, Georg Stokke acquired sole ownership of the company and it subsequently became known as Stokke Fabrikker AS. A sister company, Westnofa, was founded in Åndalsnes in 1968 to specifically cater to the domestic seating market, while Stokke concentrated on the development of other types of furniture. In 1972, Stokke launched the Tripp Trapp high-chair/chair designed by Peter Opsvik, which could be height-adjusted so as to in effect “grow with the child” from the age of six months to eight years old. Since its introduction, over three million of these revolutionary chairs have been sold worldwide. Later, Opsvik began research with Hans Christian Mengshoel (b. 1946)into dynamic seating solutions based on ergonomic principles, which resulted in the introduction of the remarkable Balans Variable sitting tool in 1979. Based on the company's philosophy of “movement and variation”, thisinnovative rocking seat evolved from the concept that furniture should stimulate a good sitting position and freedomof movement, while providing enough flexibility for a variety of body sizes. The Balans Variable and other seating designs from the Balans range are renowned for promoting excellent posture, and as such are particularly recommended for backache sufferers. During the 1980s, Stokke produced Opsvik's unusual Garden Seat and the playful Ekstrem chair designed by Terje Ekstrøm (b. 1944), which function as much as interactive sitting sculptures as ergonomic seating products. In 1999, Stokke launched a new children's furniture concept designed by Grønlund & Knudsen known as Sleepi, which transmutes from a crib to a bed to a sofa or two chairs. Like the hugely successful Tripp Trapp chair, this innovative design can be adapted to the children's needs as they grow and underscores the Stokke Group's belief that “good design is good business”. Stokke's specialization in ergonomic seating and innovative children's furniture typifies the Scandinavian attitude towards not only health issues, but also children's development.
