In the essay ‘Curtain Wars: Architects, Decorators, and the 20th century Domestic Interior’, the author Joel Sanders suggested that the curtain embodies many of the tensions and prejudices that have divided the roles of the decorator, architect and designers of interior space.<sup>19</sup> He suggested that it is here that the hard surfaces of the architecture meet the soft fabric of the decorator in a juxtaposition that reinforces the common perception that architects work conceptually, with tough and durable materials, while the decorator works intuitively with moveable and often ephemeral objects and surface coverings. Sanders concluded the essay with a call fo the invention of a new design vocabulary that overrides conventions that would collapse distinctions between ‘masculine’ or ‘feminine’, ‘decoration’ and ‘architecture’, ‘solid’ and ‘ephemeral’ and instead embody a new design language for domestic space and beyond.

## He stated:

The time is ripe for a new generation of designers to move beyond Curtain Wars and invent a hybrid design vocabulary that will allow a range of human activities to transpire in domestic spaces. <sup>20</sup>

In 1998 OMA/Rem Koolhaas designed a house for a client who, during the project’s undertaking, was involved in a serious car crash that left him paralysed from the waist down. Rather than create a one storey, fully fl at and easily accessible bungalow style building, ensuring the wheelchair-bound client encountered no change of level, the resulting project was arranged with three fl oors stacking vertically into a hillside house.

The ground fl oor basement was carved into the hill and contained private spaces such as bedrooms. The ground fl oor contained a garden room and the upper fl oor housed parents’ and children’s rooms. The fl oors of the vertical building were connected via a 3 by 3.5 metre central platform ‘room’. This level plane, with no walls, was operated by a hydraulic lift that would rise through the building allowing the wheelchair user to access all spaces of the house as well as his library of books, artwork and a cellar of wine.

Fifteen years after the original design and construction of the house, Inside Outside were commissioned to revisit the spaces and reassess their qualities. They were asked to do this in order to propose some new interventions that would revitalise the domestic space for the new occupants of the home.

Inside Outside devised a series of curtains that would be utilised to accentuate or edit various aspects of the existing qualities of the house. The bottom fl oor was sunk into the landscape, with an interior courtyard facing the caretaker’s residence and guesthouse. Here Inside Outside introduced a brown and silver, double layered, silk curtain, enhancing the light quality when closed and drawing light into the lower spaces.

The middle fl oor was the most transparent space, boasting a huge glass façade. The client wanted to be able to shut out the light coming in from changing angles of the sun during the day. The designers created and installed a white cotton curtain, a surface that contained a horizontal slit, and a panel that refl ected the bright light of southern France outwards, while subtly diffusing it inwards. An exterior curtain track on the southern façade was reused to house a grey net curtain. In its early years, this part of the house was accompanied by a jute curtain. The new and large net screen fi ltered the view and the harsh sunlight, creating an extra in-between space on the terrace.

It seems surprising that so little is done with the fl oors. They are, after all, the stages upon which our lives are acted out.

– Edwin Heathcote, The Meaning of Home. <sup>21</sup>

When carefully considered, the fl oor plane can provide much more than just the pragmatic requirements of covering a surface. The fl oor is a surface that can be utilised by a designer to create a variety of effects. It can be employed in an overtly functional manner, providing a covering that supplies direction, warmth and colour, as well as a distinct surface or texture, inside of a building. It can also be used to link, as well as to separate, spaces. Whichever way it is deployed, as in all tactical moves, the selection and use of a particular fl oor plane will reveal the designer’s full intentions for the realisation and ultimately the identity of the space.

The Carrer Avinyo apartment was designed for two Spanish brothers to share, and in

which to spend their family holidays in the city. The apartment occupies the fi rst fl oor of a building that is located at the intersection of two important roads in the city: Carrer Escudellers, and the street that gave the apartment its name, Carrer Avinyo. Through the demolition of a number of internal partition walls, a large, angular, double-height space with many small rooms, was converted into one large, well-proportioned single space. The demolition of all of the interior walls ensured that the single space was now ordered and articulated with a series of full-height windows, each with a balcony.

The preponderance of windows and balconies united and also animated the room. This enlivenment of the interior was through natural light, now illuminating the interior, and by overlooking the streets below – allowing the hustle and bustle of the busy city to become part of the interior. A ‘stack’ of bedrooms was placed at one end of the large room in the form of a large, over-scaled piece of two storey furniture. At the other end of the room, and above the kitchen, the designer placed the other bedroom. A mezzanine level, incorporating a library, was designed to afford the occupants access to the bedrooms and also the upper level bathrooms.

While the interior of any apartment is usually a resolutely private space, the Carrer Avinyo is treated as a semi-public space, drawing in the atmosphere of the immediate urban context and distilling its essence into the main family room of the interior. The formation of the large room opens up the interior to the city through the light and views of the numerous windows of the space. Akin to the adjacent George Orwell square, the room was designed to be like a public plaza, a place where the brothers and their families could congregate and enjoy each other’s company around dining and socialising. In order to accentuate this, Kohn installed a large, irregular, lozenge-shaped table at the pointed end of the triangular shaped apartment.

Figure 3.24 Triangular fl oor tiles and the lozenge-shaped table reinforce the dynamic shape of the apartment.

Unifying the interior, yet subtly shifting the emphasis from the private to the public, is an intricate and highly articulated mosaictiled fl oor. It was designed with a triangular pattern profi le, one that emphasised the geometry of the plan of the apartment. The tile pattern was graded in colour from green at one end of the apartment to red at the other. This was done in order to subtly differentiate the brothers’ private spaces. Ensuring continuity with other great designers of the Catalan capital, the encaustic tiles were manufactured by Mosaics Martí, suppliers of tiles to the projects of Antoni Gaudí. As well as symbolising the geometry of the apartment, the triangular profi le of the tile was set up to mirror the adjacent George Orwell square: an even deeper connection made with the urban environment inside the interior space.

In the smallest detail, in this case a fl oor tile and its colouring, the story of the interior, its occupants, and their relationship to each other, and the city they inhabit can be told.
