ETTORE SOTTSASS (also see under "Masters of Postmodern Design") designed showrooms for the clothing retailer Esprit\* in a user-friendly interpretation of his lively, MEMPHIS-inflected style (see under “Edited Design"). And PHILIPPE STARCK (see under "Design") designed the Royalton and Paramount hotels in New York, in iterations of the postmodern style he had begun with Paris's Café Costes. TERRY FARRELL's (see under “Architecture") TV-am headquarters made a design statement in keeping with the news-making introduction of morning television to the United Kingdom, with a bold interior that complemented the equally attention-getting building. And the jubilantly colored interiors of JOHN OUTRAM's (see under “Architecture") Judge Business School at Cambridge University benefted from the fact that the building was privately funded, allowing a complete departure from the staid university norm. Despite such striking and widely published interiors, permanent spaces of this kind were relatively rare, and many of looked dated just a few years after they węre built. Postmodernism made relatively few inroads into residential interiors; it was often considered something to look at and talk about, rather than to actually live with. Consumers, even those taken with the style, found it too “trendy" to commit to on a large scale, and most so-called postmodern homes were limited to an infusion of colors or the occasional piece of furniture, rather than an entire series of rooms. Arcane touches of classical architecture only intermittently migrated into interiors, as ambiance took precedence over authentic ity. Even within the congenial environments of assertively postmodern houses, the vocabulary was somewhat subdued, or even watered down, for everyday consumption. Architects, however, ![](images/d40c0d504f8a5242e99848a5db604bc36c9a2a48236899ecdaa79411ca39b78d.jpg) ![](images/3d7a93d7fcd97b713548aa81f90f2ee9118c9c4f241ec68fd79d2beef93902ca.jpg) often carried out the theme when designing for themselves: the homes of CHARLES JENCKS (also see under “Masters of Postmodern Design") and CHARLES MOORE (also see under “Architecture") in particular were outfitted in postmodern style at its most provocative; and high-ceilinged lofts, like those designed by Alan Buchsbaum, took well to the postmodern vocabulary. The mostly Memphis, overdone interiors of a few Hollywood films brought greater visibility but probably few new admirers for the style, since they were usually designed tó highlight ostentatiousness or bad taste—neither of which helped it win fans. At its most fully developed, Postmodernism was perhaps too extreme a break with its predecessors, too "high style" to endure. Nonetheless, with repeated exposure in the media it became more familiar and its influence began to take hold among even conservative designers and consumers, particularly in America, where keeping up with fashion is considered a sign of sophistication. Although relatively few postmodern interiors survived the impulse to redecorate, they often sported accessories and accent pieces in the new style. The introduction of a varied color palette and a permissive attitude toward mixing styles and periods—a new freedom for individuality and self-expression—are postmodernism's most important, and much-appreciated, legacies. ![](images/10439df3aad7b5f7576af61ce30b9fb5891b740011cc5229e3143ad5c5db866f.jpg)
