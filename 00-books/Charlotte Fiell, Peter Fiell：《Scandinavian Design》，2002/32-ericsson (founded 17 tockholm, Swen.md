Initially the business concentrated on the repair of telegraph equipment, butsoon its founder recognized that there was a real need for improvements to be made to existing telephone equipment. By 1878, the company was developing telephones that were basedon an earlier design by Alexander Graham Bell (1847– 1922). In the early 1890s Ericsson began manufacturing telephones of its own design, and in 1892 launched its first major product, a desk instrument with a hand-held microphone. Soon after this the company began exporting telephones throughout Europe, and its success led to the building of several overseas factories, including one in Mexico. In 1909 Ericsson launched the first cradle telephone, the Model88, which was enormously influential throughout Europe. Seeking to update this landmark design, the company commissioned the artist Jean Heiberg(1884-1976) and the Norwegian engineer Johan ChristianBjerknes in 1930 to design a telephone with a Bakelite casing to replace existing metal types. Developed using plaster prototypes, the resulting DHB 1001 telephone was less angular than earlier models, and its coherent relationship between the handset and the main instrument body established the standard for telephone design until the late 1950s. Although it was not the first to use Bakelite in its construction, the DHB1001 telephonewas highly influential and inspired Henry Dreyfuss'(1904- 1972)later plastic model 300 (1930–1933) for Bell. Between 1940and1954, HugoBlomberg (1897–1994), Ralph Lysell(1907–1987), and Gösta Thames (b. 1916) designed and developed another groundbreaking model for Ericsson, the Ericofon (launched in 1956). This unusual andforward-looking telephone had a completely unified form that integrated the earpiece, mouthpiece and dial. The design of the Ericofon, which incorporated new lightweight materials such as plastic, rubber and nylon,was made possible bythe increasing miniaturization of technology. Fun yet stylish, it was the most popular one-piece telephone for over three decades. In the early 1970s an up-datedpush-button version of the Ericofon was introduced known as the Model 700. During the 1980s and 1990s Ericsson became one of the leading manufacturers of mobile telephones, producing well-designed yet at times rather conservative products. Throughout this period the company was the world leaderin mobile telephone systems, connecting nearly 40 percent of the world's mobile callers and was at the forefront of the WAP (Wireless Application Protocol) revolution – the bridge between mobile communication and the Internet. As one of the leading players in the telecommunications industry, however, the company was severely hit by the stock market turmoil in the electronics sector during the early2000s. He began working as a freelance architect in 1988 and between 1992 and 1993exhibited a number of furniture designs as part of Cappellini's Progetto Oggetto collection in Milan, New York and Cologne. These pieces were notable for their often symbolic, simple elemental forms, such as his E-shaped Progetto 9304 occasional table, his ladder-like Progetto9209 wall-mountedcoat-rack, and hiscross-shaped enamelled steel Progetto9208 medicine cabinet. Eriksson's later furniture for IKEA, most notably the PS television table (1995)and PSclock/cupboard (1995), had similarly pared-down forms. Apart from his furniture designs, Eriksson has also designed various domestic products and architectural fittings. In 1995, he was awarded the Swedish Design Award and three years later was commissioned bySAs to design its Euroclass Lounges in Milan and London. He has also designedSAS airport lounges inStockholm, Copenhagen and Newark (New York) in a contemporary yet understatedNordic style utilizing blond woods and simple, unadorned, functional forms. Eriksson'sinterior design clientele also includes Absolut, H&M and Virgin, while Asplundand CBI have also manufactured some of his furniture and product designs. In 1998, Eriksson founded the design company Stockholm Design Lab with Björn Kusoffsky (b. 1945) and Göran Lagerström (b. 1965) with the concept and goal of it becoming an “important platform for the creation of quality design that matters and makes a difference." The philosophy of Stockholm Design Lab is“rooted in Scandinavian tradition and based on the fundamental ideas of simplicity, clarity, openness and innovation”. Throughout the early2000s Eriksson has continued to pioneer a fresh interpretationof Swedish Modernism.
