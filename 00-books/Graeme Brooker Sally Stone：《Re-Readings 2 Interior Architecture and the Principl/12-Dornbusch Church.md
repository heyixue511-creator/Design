# Dornbusch Church

Location Frankfurt, Germany

Original date 1962

Remodelled 2005

Designer Meixner Schlüter Wendt

It is the responsibility of the architect or designer to acquire a highly specifi c knowledge of the needs of the client and the users of the remodelled building. This knowledge allows the designer to tailor the design of the adaptation to their exact requirements. The designer can propose a strategy that the client may not have considered; they can suggest a method of organisation that the users of the building had not previously thought of. The designer has the ability to garner a strategic view of the problem, which the client or the user may not be able to see.

The Dornbusch Church is situated in a residential area in the north of Frankfurt. The original building was constructed in the 1960s, at a time when fi re stations and churches seemed to look very similar: a great hall of suffi cient size to hold several engines or a large congregation, fl anked by a tower that was either used to dry the hoses or support the bells.

This particular tall white bell tower became symbolic of the progressive attitude of the area, while the congregation of over 12,000 were housed in an enormous glazed block.

However, by the beginning of the 21st century, the building was in poor condition and the congregation had dwindled to less than a third of its original size. A proposal was made for the complete demolition of the building with the construction of a new much smaller prayer room to replace it.

The architects Meixner Schlüter Wendt felt that this was not the right approach and after intensive consultation with the community, undertook a series of planning studies from which they were able to develop an overview of the project. They proposed a solution that was far more advantageous to the congregation, the people of the town and to the immediate urban area. This alternative scheme involved just the partial demolition of the building, and the remodelling of the remaining structure to create a considerably more clever and complex solution than just a simple prayer room. Thus the immediate settlement contains what could be regarded as an archetypical church with a landmark tower, a churchyard or public square, and a community hub.

The nave of the church was substantially reduced; half of the building was simply taken away. This act of subtraction concentrated the internal organisation of the building. A highly coloured stained glass window, which had once been on the periphery of the space, has become the focus of the reordered church. An elaborate wall that appears to be carved in relief now fi lls the raw edge of the building – this provides both stability and also articulates the narrative of the change. The sculptural surface seems to almost echo the elements that have been removed from the building, thus acknowledging the passing of time and the changing of attitudes.

Other changes to the building were fairly minimal, basic conservation and decoration. However, on the outside of the building the remodelling is very obvious. The demolition created a great separation between the church and the tower. This exterior space is marked with the memory of the original nave, the layout of which is painted onto the grey asphalt, thus creating a surface that is obviously reminiscent of the lost building, but strangely also looks somewhat like a playing fi eld. The public space is approachable, it encourages people to use it and yet is under the protection of the church – an evocative memento, perhaps, of the place that the church once held within western society.
