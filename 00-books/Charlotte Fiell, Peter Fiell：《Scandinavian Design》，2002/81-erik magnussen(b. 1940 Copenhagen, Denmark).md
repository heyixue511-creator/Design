![](images/2cfed23ebf39fa58ecf7cdfd53eeed77b2e1afe87a7673bba10aa18886384bc8.jpg) ▶ Erik Magnussen, salad bowls and salad servers for Stelton, 1986 ▶ Erik Magnussen, Thermal Carafe (Colour 950 – Orange) for Stelton, 1976 # erik magnussen (b. 1940 Copenhagen, Denmark) ![](images/621b47e79b9780faa74bcffc35ed14a11bcaf07185de46dd647209e91fac0b1f.jpg) The Danish designer Erik Magnussen trained as a ceramicist at the Kunsthåndværkerskolen (School of Arts & Crafts) in Copenhagen until 1960. From 1962, he worked as a designer for the porcelain manufacturer Bing & Grøndahl designing simple geometric tableware such as his stoneware teapot with integrated strainer (1963) and his stackable Form 25 Termo service (1965). In 1967, Magnussen was awarded the Lunning Prize for his ceramic designs, which clearly demonstrated his talent for combining artistic sensibilities with an understanding of industrial production technigues. The following year, he designed the highly functional tubular metal and canvas folding Z-chair for Torben Ørskov & Co., which revealed his ability to work within different product categories. In 1969 he designed an innovative teapot for Bing & Grøndahl with a handle that was incorporated into the main body of the vessel. Around 1970, he co-designed with Per Kragh-Müller a range of school furniture, including a stacking chair with a frame constructed of a single piece of bent tubular metal onto which elliptical moulded plywood seat and back elements were attached – an extraordinarily rational solution that was reminiscent of Poul Henningsen's Snake chair (1932), Magnussen also worked for Stelton from 1976 and designed a number of well-known products, including the Thermal Carafe (1976), an extremely elegant and practical insulated jug that was made available in both stainless steel and brightly coloured ABS plastic. This hugely successful thermos, which was intended to complement Arne Jacobsen's Cylinda Líne metalware collection (1967), exemplified Magnussen's design principles: functional and aesthetic durability, formal simplicity, ease of use, cost-effective manufacture. From 1978, Magnussen designed hollowware for Georg Jensen and the silversmithy also produced a sterling silver version of his Therma/ Carafe. During the 1980s and 1990s, his product designs included the Mode/ no. 301 kerosene lamp for Harlang & Dreyer (1988), a clock and barometer for Georg Christensen, and a table and chair for Paustian (1989), the frame of which comprised a continuous piece of bent tubular metal. As a designer who seeks perfection even in the smallest of details, Magnussen's work epitomizes the high object integrity of Danish design – especially in terms of consumer products intended for industrial mass production. ![](images/a392aab4bff4d52598961ef5b279e5a99dfef600b41f11c470777e4d7979c146.jpg) ![](images/dea5e1e585563255f3e17e1042658fde9ba9b5038bea4c247bd2a6654a8e362b.jpg) ![](images/6511b43f1a34faada83258dfa356d55f04220cea7e1b42c812958df31e5d190f.jpg)
