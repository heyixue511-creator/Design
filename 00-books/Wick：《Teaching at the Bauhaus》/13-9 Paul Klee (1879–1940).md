# 9 Paul Klee (1879–1940)

## Appointment to the Bauhaus

“Dear Paul Klee. We have voted unanimously to invite you to join us in Weimar as a master at the Bauhaus."1 This telegram—signed by Gropius, Feininger, Marcks, Muche, Itten, and the two future "Secession professors" Engelmann and Klemm —reached Klee in the fall of 1920. In the spring of 1921 Klee took up his teaching position at the Bauhaus. Ten years later he left the institute to take over the leadership of a painting class at the art academy in Düsseldorf.

The background of Klee's appointment to the Bauhaus has yet to be fully expiained, although it can now be considered certain that Itten was one of the driving forces.² Klee was, of course, not unknown. In 1920 the first monographs on the artist were published; that year he contributed a highly informative text on his understanding of life and art to a collection edited by Kasimir Edschmid, Schöpferische Konfession (Creative credo); that same year Klee exhibited 362 works in an exhibition at theGalerie Goltz in Munich; also in 1920 two books were published that contained his illustrations. This all points to the artistic success that the forty-one-year-old artist was just beginning to enjoy. It does not at all explain, however, whyan artist whosework seemed to be entirely untouched by the discussions of the day about an artistic and social synthesis—rather, who showed tendencies to escape the world and isolate himself from reality—would have received an invitation to join the Bauhaus, an art school that, for all its utopian perspectives for the future, advocated pragmatic goals.

Oskar Schlemmer, who as student representative at the academy in Stuttgart had worked unsuccessfully in 1919 to have Klee appointed as

Hölzel's successor,³ and who took up work at the Bauhaus in early 1921 at the same time as Klee, described the confusion that resulted from this apparent discrepancy between Klee's conception of art and the Bauhaus program as follows: "By the way, Klee's appointment seems to provoke the greatest head-shaking, for he is considered a l’art pour l’art type with no conceivablepractical contribution to make; I personally find such a judgment short-sighted."4 During the founding phase of the Bauhaus it

was clearly part of Gropius's strategy for appointments—with strong influence from Itten—to attract to the institute artists of high reputation who were considered progressive and to count on their innovative potential even in the applied disciplines, without fear of falling back on the old positions of traditional architectural academies, art schools, and arts and crafts schools (and this surely explains, at least in part, Klee's invitation to join the Bauhaus). But leaving that aside for a moment, initially there seems to have been agreement between Gropius andKlee, even in programmatic matters, that for a long time was hardly remarked upon at all. In 1976 Jürgen Glaesemer called attention to a text in which Klee professed a firm belief in the political and social function of art. He had a vision for an art school of a new type that he confided in a letter to a friend, the draftsman Alfred Kubin, that shows remarkable affinities to the program of the Arbeitsrat für Kunst (Work council for the arts) in Berlin and to Gropius's Bauhaus manifesto:

Even though from the beginning it never seemed that this Communist republic would last, it nevertheless gave us an opportunity to test the subjective possibilities of existence in such a community. It was not without positive result. A pointed, individualistic art is not suited to the pleasure of the whole population; it is a capitalist luxury. But we are certainly more than just curiosities for rich snobs. And that which in us is somehow striving beyond that for eternal values would sooner find support in a communist community. The productive effect of our example would play out on a wider basis if channeled differently. As the ideal experimental station we wouldnot be training new inventors that really were no such thing but only masks of inventors (iust as the academicians are to their teachers like children to their fathers); rather, we would be able to transfer the results of our inventiveness to the body of the people. This new art could then permeate into the applied arts and produce an enormous flowering. For then there would be no more academies but only art schools for artisans.

The soviet republic taught us many things. Realizing it in practice is unthinkable especially now. So soon after this ideal intensification we are not yet mature enough for it.

My dear Kubin, perhaps you are not so interested, but it engaged me very deeply for a time. I have deviated in nothing essential as a result, as my Secession collection will prove to you.⁵

Although he could not establish the intellectual background behind this text without reservations, Franciscono does not rule out the possibility that Klee had known of the Bauhaus manifesto, which was printed as a flyer the month before.⁶ However that may be, at the time Klee—like numerous other artists and intellectuals—seems to have been drawn into the intellectual undercurrent of the November Revolution and the republic of soviets, so this attitude on Klee's part, even if it was only an ephemeral one, could have played a role in his appointment to the Bauhaus.

On the other hand, we must approach with skepticism Günter Dünkel's hasty attempt—based on this very isolated and by no means generalizable statement—to reclaim Klee as a political, antifascist artist and nimbly dismiss the general perception of Klee as an “apolitical, lonely, esoteric artist” as “fable."7 Apart from Klee's famous lecture in Jena in 1924, in which he complained that art had lost the people as its raison d’être and in which with an eye to the Bauhaus he pledged to search for the people,8 there are no indications .that could plausibly support Dünkel's thesis. On the contrary, the analysis of written sources and Klee's artistic work gives a very different image, one that confirms the artist's detachment from quotidian events at the Bauhaus, with its perpetual internal squabbles and continuing defensive wars against attacks from outside,9 as well as from the great social and political events of the time.

In order to clarify this point—also in terms of our understanding of Klee as a teacher at the Bauhaus—it seems useful to examine first Klee's ideas on the function and role of art and artists.

## The Role of the Artist—The Function of Art

Paul Klee made an ample number of statements that deviate fundamentally in content from the letter to Kubin just cited. In the art is described as an instrument to make visible a reality other than the one we experience daily and—analogously— the position of the artist is said to be outside the real circumstances of life.

Writing in his diary in 1916, in the middle of the war, Klee characterized his own position in contrast to that of his friend Franz Marc, who had died in action shortly before:

When I tell what kind of man Franz Marc is, I must at once confess that I shall also be telling what kind of person I am. . . . He is more human, he loves more warmly, is more demonstrative. He responds to animals as if they were human. . . . In Marc, the bond with the earth takes precedence over the bond with the universe. . .. I only try to relate myself to God. . . . [Marc] was still a member of the human race, not a neutral creature. . . . Art is like Creation: it holds good on the last day as on the first. What my art probably lacks, is a kind of passionate humanity. 1 don't love animals and every sort of creature with an earthly warmth. I don't descend to them or raise them to myself. I tend rather to dissolve into the whole of creation and am then on a footing of brotherliness to my neighbor, to all things earthly. I possess. The earth-idea gives way to the world-idea. My love is distant and religious. Everything Faustian is alien to me. I place myself at a remote starting point of creation. . . And since not too many people reach that state, few will be touched by me. . . . In my work I do not belong to the species, but am a cosmic point of reference. . . . Art imitates creation [my emphasis—R.W.].10

Klee thus proceeds from the old idea, here rather pointedly articulated, that artists resemble God, a view Itten also affirmed in his diary. The resemblance to God is derived not only from the artists' creative powers but is also expressed in their withdrawal from the cosmos, in their distance to earthly things, from history and society. And because hardly anyone is in a position to reach this place, which Klee sees as neutral, outside of historical and social reality, and transcendental, the art, too, which is made there and which he sees as analogous to Creation, should not be universally intelligible either. Very much in contrast to the view expressed in his letter to Kubin, this diary entry not only does not relate art to the broad masses but considers it the preserve of a numerically tiny elite.

A similar understanding of the function of art and the role of the artist can be found in an essay written in 1918 but published only in 1920, in the compendium Schöpferische Konfession. In trying to determine the new tasks of art, the essay begins with a sentence that has become famous: "Art does not reproduce the visible but makes visible."11 This was in some sense the motto for Klee's entire artistic activity. Klee continued: “Formerly artists depicted things that were to be seen on the earth, things people liked to see or would like to have seen. Now the relativity of visible things is made clear, the belief expressed that the visible is only an isolated case taken from the universe and that there are more truths unseen than seen."12 The task of art, then, is to use its own means to juxtapose earthly, visible reality with another, invisible reality and in that way to point to the relativity of the visible, the limitations of the earthly. According to Klee, the function of art is not mimesis of earthly reality but the creation of a new cosmically transcendental order. Thus the artist sees art as an instrument whose help makes it possible to go beyond the here and now, to turn away from daily reality, from the "work-a-day gray,"13 and to provide consolation to people, to elevate them: "And that is not all: Let it help you to shed your shell: try, for a moment, to think of yourself as God. To look forever forward to new holidays when the soul will sit down to feed its hungry nerves, to fill its tired veins with new sap."14

This was also Klee's position during the Bauhaus period, as if the ideas expressed in the letter to Kubin had never existed, as can be shown by a passage in the above-mentioned Jena lecture of 1924, “On Modern Art," in which he laid out in depth his vision of the function of art and the role of the artist. Here artists are seen as the tools of a force outside of themselves, as people to whom— like the trunk of a tree—juices “flow” from the roots and who “[b]attered and stirredby the strength of the flow"15 create the work of art as the crown of the tree, as it were. And it is these "realities of art," then, that “help to lift life out of its mediocrity. For not only do they reproduce, with a greater or lesser degree of spirit, what is seen, they also make secret visions visible."16 Seen from this perspective, the artwork is seen both in opposition to a given reality, and a possibility to extend and transcend it. Or, as Klee put it in "Creative Credo," it is a means to “console the mind, by showing it that there is something more than the earthly and its possible intensifications."17

In his dissertation, Das Problem der Bewegung bei Paul Klee (The problem of movement in Paul Klee's work), Andeheinz Mösser summarized the escapist tendencies that are clearly visible in Klee's writings as well as his artistic oeuvre:

What is remarkable is . . . the view of historically determined reality, . . . which is an unmistakably negative one. A certain fatalism is suited to the view, for Klee clearly understands this reality as a predetermined one that can never be changed directly. . .. Against the more or less negatively perceived human world, art erects (the artist builds) a complementary independent work of the artwork. . . . The function of art is conditionedby this view of reality mentioned . .., as well as by the particular origin of the artwork, which is unreal, otherworldly, and ultimately "divine." Art is supposed to "distract" us from our reality, to raise us above the condition of earthly existence, andprovide, at least for a moment, insight into the divine (perfect) plan of creation.18

Klee was by no means alone in this flight from historical reality and the assertion of an autonomous special status for art beyond the bounds of society. At the Bauhaus it was above all Kandinsky, Itten, and Schlemmer who shared a similar basic attitude and hoped for a "healing through the spirit,"19 and KonradFarner put it. The extent to which this attitude was reflected in Klee's teaching at the Bauhaus will be discussed later. First, however, we will comment on three aspects that are equally central to an understanding of Klee's art and teachings: (1) the relation of art to nature; (2) the problem of motion; and (3) the roles of intuition and intellect in the artistic process.

## Art and Nature

In the previous section we noted that Klee understood art as analogous to Creation, a view that will now be supported using several of the artist's written statements. There is an interesting entry in a diary from 1902 (Klee was in Rome at the time): "I drew a few queerly shaped tree trunks in the park of the Villa Borghese. The linear principles here are similar to those of the human body, only more tightly related. What I have thus learned l at once put to use in my compositions."20 The remarkable thing here is not only Klee's insight into the structural correspondences between the human being and nature but also the scrupulousness with which he applied the regularities learned from nature to his art. In doing so, the relationship between nature and art is not fixed in the naturalistic sense as a model-imitation correlation. Rather, Klee proceeded from the idea of a homology of nature and art (and in this he was close to Goethe's conception of art), that is to say, nature and art possess a shared basis and correspond to one another with respect to their genesis. They are, Klee believed, merely various manifestations of one and the same law, and the study of nature and its laws was for Klee the basic prerequisite for knowledge about the regularities of art.21 If one has “perceived" the functional laws and structural principles of nature, then one will at the same time also possess the regularities of art. In his essay "Ways of Nature Study" (1923) Klee was more specific about the character of the relationship between nature and the visual arts, as , well as the artist's relationship to nature: "For the artist, dialogue with nature remains a sine qua non. The artist is a man, himself nature, and a part of nature within natural space." The study of nature had previously consisted of a "meticulously detailed investigation into the appearance of things," which led to the neglect of the task that is the goal of the "artist of today," namely, making "nonoptical impressions"—the “inside"—visible. Both the artist and the object confronting him are part of the same natural, earthly creation; both share their "earthbound connection" and "cosmic bond."22 On the basis of these shared features, the visual artist can develop a "synthesis of outward sight and inner vision"23 that make it possible to go beyond the simple perception of surfaces “in order not just to appropriate the object by representing it but to create it . . . anew in a way analogous to Creation."24 In relation to students, Klee added: "Through the experience he has gained in these various ways and which he has turned into visible accomplishment, the student manifests the degree to which he has achieved a dialogue with the natural object. The growing maturity in his attitude toward, and observation of, nature, enables him, as he arrives at forming his own philosophical outlook on the world, to achieve a completely free representation of abstract shapes, which beyond the consciously schematic approach reaches a new naturalness, the naturalness of the work. He then creates a work or participates in the creation of works that are the image of God's work."25 The interesting point here—in addition to the renewed assertion that art is analogous to divine Creation—is the notion of a “new naturalness" of freely created “abstract shapes." Although this "new naturalness" is derived from natural laws according to the premise of homology, the artwork is, according to Klee, "more than nature": "Reduction! One wants to say more than nature and one makes the impossi ble mistake of wanting to say it with more means than she, instead of fewer."26 Or: "If I wished to represent man 'as he is,'I should have required so bewildering a tangle of lines that .. . there would only have been an unrecognizable blur."27Reduction of pictorial means was thus for Klee the condition for the possibility of speaking to people more directly and clearly than nature, being more complex, can do. Thus the connection between nature and art doés not consist of a correspondence in form between the two but rather, more generally, entails that art is subject to the same regularities that nature is. Although art can produce shapes that; even though they are not representations of nature, permit figural associations with natural phenomena,2⁸ the relationship of nature and art in Klee's notion is of another, more metaphysical quality: "From prototype to archetype. The chosen artists are those who dig down close to the secret source where the primal law feeds the forces of development. What artist would not like to live where the central organ of all space-time motion, call it brain or heart of creation as you will, activates all functions? In the womb of nature, in the primal ground of creation, where the secret key to all these things lies hidden?"29

## Movement

From the definition of the artistic we have just sketched, one of Klee's central pictorial principles can be derived, namely, that of movement. Its special significance is expressed in many of the artist's statements, such as "think not of form but of the act of forming″30 or “[the artist is] more concerned with formative powers than the finished form."31 The development of the concept of movement began for Klee in 1905 and passed through several stages that will be sketched here briefly. In 1905 his diary reads: “More and more parallels between music and graphic art force themselves upon my consciousness.... Certainly both arts are temporal; this could be proved easily. At Knirr's [Klee's academic teacher in Munich— R.W.] they rightly spoke about the presentation of a picture, by which they meant something thoroughly temporal: the expressive motions of the brush, the genesis of effect."32 Here one can see already an understanding of art as a temporal process, an understanding that was by no means common at the time. Klee recognized not only that the act of painting implies a physical movement on the part of the artist but also that in the finished painting the traces of the act of painting bear witness to the process of creating the artwork, to its genesis. This idea of the genesis of the painting, defined here initially in terms of the act of painting, connects in Klee's thinking with the idea of art as analogous to Creation, the idea that the ways in which nature and art originate are analogous. Thus in a diary entry from 1908 we find the following revealing detail about the genesis of an oil painting:

I shall paint human figures from nature in a deeper sense, dividing them into two time periods and treating each separately [my emphasis—R.W.].

1st state: (imperfect): the space, the environment; this stage must be allowed to dry too.

2. Action (perfect, passé défini, aorist):33 the figure itself. For: in nature too the environment was already complete when motion joined in, occurred. .. . Furthermore, there remains:

3. To test the mutual effect of 1 and 2. The picture is finished.34

If the statement that art originates in a way analogous to nature remained relatively abstract during the discussion in the previous section, this quotation should help make it more concrete. Thus, "from nature in a deeper sense" does not mean to capture a given impression of nature on the picture plane in a naturalistic manner but to make use in some way of developmental processes from nature as a rule for proceeding with the genesis of a painting. Movement clearly means more than just the “expressive motions of the brush"; rather, more broadly, it is that form of artistic activity that has as its prerequisite the passivity of the surroundings. "Whatever is thematic to the painting, from an artistic perspective it is always this confrontation between the surroundings and the intervening action.... And, to emphasize this point again, this artistic principle is for Klee legitimated by parallel occurrences in Nature."35 In 1914, shortly after his famous journey to Tunisia (together with August Macke and Louis Moilliet), Klee reinforced his view of the artistic work as a genesis: “In my productive activity, every time a type grows beyond the stage of its genesis, and I have about reached the goal, the intensity gets lost very quickly, and I have to look for new ways. It is precisely the way which is productive—this is the essential thing; becoming more important than being"³6(my emphasis—R.W.). And again:

Genesis as formal motion is the essential thing in a work.

In the beginning the motif, insertion of energy, sperm.

Works as form-determining sperm: the primitive male component.³7

Klee thus saw the genesis of the artwork as a dualistic process, as a harmony and conflict of mind and matter, of the male and the female principles: on the one side, the mind of the creator as a factor that initiates and determines the form; on the other, matter as a factor that creates form independently of mind. It is easy to see, and requires no further comment, that the metaphor of sperm and the interpretation of the creation of form as a primitive female phenomenon fit seamlessly into Klee's basic concept of art as analogous to Creation.

In his essay "Creative Credo," in which he picked up the duality of "primitive male" vs. "primitive female" components, Klee dealt at length with the problem of movement. Here he defined movement not only as the constituent factor of form but as an overarching, universally valid principle of existence (much as Johannes Itten did): "All being is based on movement. . .. And likewise in the universe, movement is the basis of everything. Peace on earth is an accidental congestion of matter. To take this congestion as basic is mistaken. . . . The work of art, too, is first of all genesis; it is never experienced purely as a result."38 Formation implies time, and so in “Creative Credo" Klee derived, much as Kandinsky did, from the timeless "in itself dead point," qua movement as principle, the elementary pictorial means of line, plane, and space. This is a procedure to which we will return in the context of Klee's theory of form.

It did not escape Klee that the principle of movement is valid not only for the process of creating a painting but also for the process of its reception (or, more precisely, the process of perception):"What the beholder does is temporal too. The eye is so organized that it conveys the parts successively into the crucible of vision, and in order to adjust itself to a new fragment has to leave ·behind the old one. . . . In the work of art, paths are laid out for the beholder's eye, which gropes like a grazing beast. .. . The pictorial work springs from movement, it is itself fixed movement, and it is grasped in movement (eye muscles)."39 In a draft for this essayin which the viewer is referred to as the "postcreator," a characterization that captures very well the viewer's active role in the process of creatively assimilating an artwork Klee argued that the viewer of a painting, in contrast to the listener of a piece of music, is in the unfortunate position of being "placed before an ending at first" and, in relation to the genesis, of "seeming to go the wrong way." From this he concluded that the visual artist has the task of making the process of perception easier by establishing paths in the painting and by assuring "some discernible simplicity" in the structure of the work, which should not be considered a deficiency.40

The concrete pictorial modalities of giving shape to movement—either by employing curved or jagged lines, by using scales of light to dark and shadings of color, or by introducing particular symbols—will not be discussed here but rather in later sections on Klee's work as a painter and his teaching during the Bauhaus period.

## Intuition and Intellect

For Klee, as we have seen, the regularities of nature to some extent play the role of methodological rules for the process of artistic creativity, so that art as Klee understood it is analogous to Creation. The question that arises in this context is whether this is an application of natural laws to art in order to transfer principles from one area to another according to a rational process (we should recall the homology between two areas that Klee postulated), or whether there are other components that act as regulators in this process.

There is no doubt that Klee always attributed the highest importance to rational knowledge of laws and their deliberate employment in painting. His search for the laws on which art is based can be traced in his diaries back to the period just after 1900. During his Italian journey in particular the impression of classical buildings gave him in this respect “an immediate illumination": “Although these are utilitarian structures, the art of building has remained more consistently pure than other arts. Its spatial organism has been the most salutary school for me. . . . Because all the interrelations between their individual design elements are obviously calculable, works of architecture provide faster training for the stupid novice than pictures or 'nature.' And once one has grasped the idea of measurability in connection with design, the study of nature will progress with greater ease and accuracy"41 (my emphasis— R.W.). This quotation not only confirms Klee's basic thesis of the parallelism of natural and artistic laws, but it also sheds light on the artist's early interest in the rational, measurable permeation of the regular in nature and art (see also Kandinsky, for example in “Theory of Design" and“Theory of Color and Form" in chapter 8).

Still, as early as 1905 Klee admonished himself not to build on the rational alone in the creative process: "Oh keep the infinite spark from being stifled by the measure of law."42 This means that the "objective—the regular, which is intellectually testable" has “as a truly essential and decisive component the subjectively inspired—the spark—that for Klee is what really makes it artistic."43 This “infinite spark," whose provenance cannot be more precisely specified and whose source is generally assumed to be in a transcendental sphere (which also legitimizes the artist's idea of a "cosmic bond"), this spark, which can also be described with terms like “inspiration" and "intuition," is ultimately the "measuring stick and regulator for the applicability of the law and above all for any deviation from it. Art does not mean, according to this quotation, simple regularity. Empowered by the subjective inner spark, it transcends the law."44

This position, which most resembles remarks made by Kandinsky and Schlemmer of the other artists teaching at the Bauhaus, was articulated by Klee several times, most succinctly in "Creative Credo," where he attributed great value to rational calculation whenever one was dealing with questions of form, but diagnosed the failure of intellectual operations as soon as it was a question of art “in the highest sphere": "The liberation of the elements, their arrangement in subsidiary groups, simultaneous destruction and construction towards the whole, pictorial polyphony, the creation of rest through the equipoise of motion: all these are lofty aspects of the question of form, crucial to formal wisdom; but they are not yet in the highest sphere. A final secret stands behind all our shifting views, and the light of intellect gutters and goes out."45

In 1928, during a phase in which Klee himself was intensely engaged in making drawings of geometric constructions with the aid of compass and rule (perhaps for a book he was working on that remained unfinished, “Bildnerische Gestaltungslehre" [Pictorial theory of design]),46 and during which time the tendencies at the Bauhaus to emphasize system, rationality, and mathematics were clearly on the rise, the artist once again expressed his reservations about art that excluded the intuitive, the irrational, and the metaphysical in an essay “Exact Experiments in the Realm of Art": "We construct and keep on constructing, yet intuition is still a good thing. You can do a good deal without it, but not everything. . . . In art, too, there is room enough for exact research, and the gates have been open now for quite some time. . . . All this is fine but it has its limits: intuition is indispensable. We document, explain, justify, construct, organize: these are good things, but we do not succeed in coming to the whole... . But we may as well calm down: construction is not absolute. Our virtue is this: by cultivating the exact we have laid the foundations for a science of art, including the unknown X."47 This defines a position that is not only characteristic of Klee's artistic oeuvre as a whole but also for his teaching, in which he always emphasized the limited range of a purely cognitive procedure andmerely constructive approach to the design process.

## The Paintings of the Bauhaus Period

As was true of most of the other artists teaching at the Bauhaus, for Klee, too, teaching and artistic creation represented an inseparable set of conditions. It is not always easy to decide whether it was the external pressure to didactic reflection in the context of the preparation for a course that inspired the artist to new pictorial inventions or whether the production of images was primary and influenced the teaching only secondarily. However it may be in a specific case, during the time he was active at the Bauhaus in Weimar (less so in Dessau) Klee seems to have understood the pedagogical and the artistic as a unity: "Here in the studio I paint a half dozen paintings and draw and think about my course, all at once. It has to go together; otherwise it wouldn't work at all."48

Apresentationof Klee'sextensiveoeuvre during the Bauhaus period that would be even approximately exhaustive would be impossible within the framework of this book. To that end, from the ever-expanding secondary literature on Kiee, we mention only Christian Geelhaar's work Paul Klee and the Bauhaus,49 in which the author has attempted to organize the material into thematic groups. With an eye to the interrelationships between his artistic oeuvre and the didactic reflections that interest us, we will choose the following three groups of works to examine as examples: (1) the color gradations, (2) the paintings of squares, and (3) the arrow paintings.

The color gradations should be seen in direct connection to Klee's systematic occupation with color theory (on this see the section "Theory of Color"). They are small-format sheets on which the artist “tried out the partial colour actions . . . one after another"50 that he later discussed in class. Klee used a glazing technique for them that he had already used in the earlier Black Watercolors51 (1908), which he explained to his students as follows:

We divide a long strip of white paper into seven fields. Except for Field 7 we coat it with a thin, pure red water-color. After the red coat has dried, we coat the strip (except for Field 1) with a thin, pure green water-color. When the two coats are dry, we perceive a reddish Field 1 and a greenish Field 7. In between there are five colorless Fields 2–6. We now enhance the rather feeble effect of these first two steps by continuing the operation. Alternately we add red from above and green from below. . . . In the language of painting this way of mixing colors is called glazing. Separate coats are laid on at intervals. At each step something is added. . . . By the gradation of our coats we obtain a precisely graduated movement from red to green-red to green, or the other way round.52

Klee used this "time-measuring technique,"53 which made it possible to experience procedurally his theorem of the genesis of the painting, not only as it applied to geometric compositions but also to watercolors with figurative motifs and those that evoke associations of figures (e.g., Sunset in the City, 1922/62; Ambassador of Autumn, 1922/69; Crysta/ Gradation, 1921/88). Christian Geelhaar has noted that these"gradations" reveal a surprising formal similarity to the color and "epidiascopic" films developed by Ludwig Hirschfeid-Mack and Kurt Schwerdtfeger from 1922 on, though no concrete evidence has yet been found that would point to influence of any kind.54

The paintings of squares begin around the same time as the color gradations but are by no means limited to the Bauhaus period, even appearing among the artist's late works. In the works of the early twenties Klee continued the line of the watercolors begun during the 1914 trip to Tunisia, in which, under the influence of cubism and Delaunay's orphism, he divided the picture plane into square and rectangular planes and restricted the figurative element to the merest hints of form (e.g., semicircle = cupola). Eva-Maria Triska has shown that the paintings of squares like the color gradations—should not be understood as an application of theory but as a means of working a theory out.5⁵ Without being able to discuss this in detail, suffice it to say generally that the unpretentious form of the square grid serves to a certain extent as a neutral scaffold for a systematic exploration of the regularities of the pictorial. In doing so, Klee used the square scheme not only for investigations into colors (as Itten did as well in his course on color) but also to demonstrate formal problems like movement, rhythm balance, mirroring, turning, displacement, scale, and proportion—investigations, that is, whose fruits found their way into Klee's course at the Bauhaus and were utilized in the works of numerous Bauhaus students.

The arrow, a polyvalent symbol within Klee's oeuvre, appears in many of the artist's paintings. Because Klee analyzed the arrow at length in his course, we will discuss this motif very briefly, even though as early as 1924 he called for the elimination of the arrow symbol in a lecture: “a symbol in itself is not a pictorial formation. So this sign of an associative agreement has to be overcome; we must go on without the arrow."56 The arrow in Klee's paintings is always a symbol of movement or an indication of a process of movement, for example, in Greeting (1922/78), in which the complementary movements in a color gradation are given visual resonance by means of arrows, or in Hovering (1930/220), where the arrow points to an upward movement that is about to begin or has just begun. In Paul Klee and the Bauhaus Geelhaar tentatively decodes other interpretations of the arrow—for example, as a sign of an inescapable fate, as the symbol of the effects of magical powers, or as an emblem of conquest (also in an erotic sense).

## Teaching at the Bauhaus

On his appointment to the Staatliches Bauhaus, Klee first took over as master of form of the bookbinding workshop, which until then had been led by Georg Muche. In a letter to his wife, Lily, dated 19 April 1921, Klee told her that there were only four Bauhaus students participating in the workshop (along with several apprentices of the master of crafts, Otto Dorfner) and that though the work was solid it was not in the "new spirit." The hope of "bringing in some fresh impetus over time"58 failed because Klee did not assert himself forcefully enough with respect to Dorfner, who was a craftsman of high standing and had his own aesthetic norms and ideas of form. This hesitancy was surely connected to the fact that, despite the idea of an “art school for artisans" that he had confided to Kubin in 1919, Klee was ultimately not interested in applied design but only in the production of autonomous works of art. When the workshop for bookbinding was closed in 1922, Klee was given the direction of the workshop for glass painting, a workshop that, like the bookbinding workshop, remained peripheral to the Bauhaus, not only because of the poor opportunities for outside commissions during the postwar period but also because there was no longer a place for glass painting in the new architecture. (On the question of glass painting at the Bauhaus, see the remarks in “The Artist" in chapter 7.) In Dessau for a time Klee taught form in the weaving workshop run by Gunta Stölzl, but nevertheless the artist's direct involvement in the workshops in terms of concrete management of "applied" design projects was in general relatively minor (one of the exceptions is a carpet design by Gertrud Arndt that uses Klee's basic square grid).

## "Pädagogischer Nachlaß"

Klee's contribution to pedagogy at the Bauhaus did not consist in having introduced new methods of teaching, as was true of Johannes Itten in particular. His teaching method was, like Kandinsky's, traditional: lecture or reading in alternation with practical exercises. Klee's contribution is significant for its didactic aspect—where "didactic" is taken in the narrow sense of referring to subject matter and excluding the methods of teaching and learning. This didactic element, which can to some extent be equated with what is generally characterized as Klee's theory of art, has not been fully explored even decades after the artist's death.63 This is not only a consequence of the enormous quantity of material, some of which is still unpublished, but also with the problems that result from the partial publications of the socalled "Pädagogischer Nachlaß."

The "Pädagogischer Nachlaß," which is held by the Paul Klee-Stiftung in the Kunstmuseum in Bern includes the manuscripts for the courses from the winter semester 1921–22 through the winter semester 1922-23, including the refresher course of the summer semester 1922 (= M.1, with the general title Beiträge zur bildnerischen Formlehre); the courses for the winter semester 1923–24 (= M.2, with several separate courses under the title "Bildnerische Mechanik" [Pictorial mechanics]); a manuscript titled “Klee Gestaltungslehre" ("Klee design theory" = M.3); a manuscript that includes, among other things, the sequence of courses in Dessau and a table of contents dated September 1925 under the title “Anteil Klee an der Grundlehre" (Klee's involvement in the basic course" = M.4); and envelopes with several thousand loose sheets containing brief texts, diagrams, sketches, and finished drawings, usually geometric and constructive (= M.5 and M.6).64

## Theory of Form

In Klee's theory of art, as we have seen, the rational plays an important role, though not the decisive one. The decisive thing for the process of artistic creation is that "infinite spark" that is the essential condition of art "in the highest sphere," that is, of art in a region where the “light of intellect gutters and goes out." The possession of this “infinite" or "secret" spark is a blessing that is granted only to the true artist. It cannot be taught. Thus, Klee, like his colleague Kandinsky, concluded that although "the fine artist likes to work sine ratio," the "examples for school [must be] rationally comprehensible."69

Klee was convinced that this rationally comprehensible aspect manifested in the formal cannot be understood as something freed from worldview but rather as something that must "merge with our worldview."7º In his introduction to "Contributions to a Theory of Pictorial Form" he emphasized: "We are artists, practical craftsmen, and it is only natural that in this discussion we should give priority to matters of form. But we should not forget that before the formal beginning, or to put it more simply, before the first line is drawn, there lies a whole prehistory; not only man's longing, his desire to express himself, his outward need, but also a general state of mind (whose direction we call worldview [Weltanschauung]), which drives him from inside to manifest his spirit in one place or another. I emphasize this point to avoid the misconception that a work consists only of form."71 By including what is here called "worldview," Klee went a step beyond the ahistorical definition of the artistic that can be read out of his "Creative Credo." "Worldview" is no doubt a historical category, and it is evident that Klee was referring to the worldview of a historical period that Kandinsky characterized as the "epoch of the Great Spiritual," in which the goal of art no longer lay in the mimesis of an externally visible reality but rather in making an inner reality visible (see also “The Influence of Theosophy" in chapter 8).72

Much as Kandinsky did, Klee proceeded analytically in developing his theory of pictorial form, and started by examining the elements of form; like Kandinsky he began with the point, and progressed from there to the line, the plane, and space7³ (see “Theory of Design" and "Theory of Form" in chapter 8). The motif that Klee specified for the analytical method is, however, more sensible than is the case with Kandinsky. The purpose of analysis for Klee was not to justify a supraindividual language of design that derives from the ambition to be the expression of the "pure and eternally artistic" but rather "the examination of work with a view to the stages of its coming-into-being. This . . . I call the analysis of 'genesis.'" And: "We investigate the methods by which another has created his work, in order to set ourselves in motion. This approach should save us from regarding a work of art as something rigid, something fixed and unchanging."74 Even so, Klee was not concerned with communicating a specific understanding of the creative artistic process but above all with giving his students the necessary “equipment"75 for artistic design.

Thus he began in the first lesson with the point, characteristically with the "point that sets itself in motion."76In contrast to Kandinsky, who made the point itself the object of extensive theoretical reflections, Klee was interested in the point only to the extent that the line develops from it in a process of motion. Thus, Klee's investigation of elementary pictorial means did not, in fact, take the point as its starting point but rather the line, which not only makes sense in terms of the theorem of movement that is central to his art theory but also seems more appropriate from the standpoints of phylo- and ontogenetics than does Kandinsky's more radical reductionism: "For the present then let us content ourselves with the most primitive of elements: the line. At the dawn of civilization, when writing and drawing were still the same thing, it was the basic element. And as a rule our children begin with it. . . ."77 Klee distinguished between three types of lines: active, medial, and passive. The active line moves freely, without a goal, as if on an aimless walk (wavy line), or it moves between two fixed points, with a goal and temporal limits, more like a walk to take care of business (zigzag line). Both have in common that they divide passive (imaginary) planes. Carola Giedion-Welcker has called attention to the fundamental difference between Klee's active line and Henry van de Velde's understanding of the line. Whereas the Jugendstil artist in his Kunstgewerblichen Laienpredigten (Lay sermons on the arts and crafts; 1902) called the line a "force . . . that is borrowed from the energy of the person who drew it,"78 Klee gave the line an independent life, so to speak, that is, it has "become capable of action and expression on its own. The hand is merely an 'instrument from a distant sphere.'"79 If the line describes a planar form (e.g., square, triangle, circle, ellipse), it loses its mobile character and moves from the active to the medial stage. According to Klee, the line becomes entirely passive when it appears as the outer edge of a color surface. In that case it is the plane that appears active and with respect to the line; "we still see lines, but not linear acts; what we see are linear results of planar actions."80

In the second lesson, starting from the observation of two lines that converge in a point, Klee arrived at a treatment of the third dimension and its perspectival depiction. The specific perspectival operations that Klee demonstrated to his students need not be discussed here. However, in light of one important pictorial problem for Klee—establishing balance in a painting—it is interesting to note that he based the relationship between vertical and horizontal(a fundamental aspect of central perspective) on human orientation in space. As a consequence of gravity, we have “an acute sense of the vertical that keeps us from falling; and if need be (in an emergency) we extend our arms to correct and counterbalance a mistake."81

Thus in the third lesson Klee developed from this outline of the principles of the theory of perspective a symbol of balance, namely, the scale. He demanded artistic harmony in artwork that is, as we have shown, the balance of "primordialmasculine" and “primordial-feminine," of mind and matter, and their formal expression through movement and countermovement: “Every energy requires its complement to bring itself to rest outside the field of force."82 In order to make this clear to the students, Klee made symbolic use of the scale. The simplest case of equilibrium is when there is equal weight on either side of the scale, resulting in symmetry. The reality of the painting is, however, usually more complex and more complicated. For example, if the pans of the balance hold a square of equal size on either side, the left one light gray and the right dark gray, then the right side, which is “weighed down by black energy," must be balanced out by adding a small black field on the left side. Another possibility, according to Klee, is to reduce the plane on the right so that a large light square and a small dark one balance out. The artist emphasized, however, that for such balancing acts there can only be a relative measure, not an absolute one: “The notion that black is heavier than white applies only while we think of a white surface. .. . In physics we speak of specific gravity compared to water; for us there are different kinds of specific gravity: in comparison with white, with black, with medium (andwith every intermediate tone). The colour world brings in still other values, colours on a red background, colours on a violet background, etc.“83

In the fourth lesson Klee treated the problems surrounding structure. His concept of structure was purely pictorial, or based on the syntax of the painting, and it was sharply distinct from that of Moholy-Nagy, who identified structure with the internal makeup of a given material. For Klee, structure obtained when the pictorial element can be repeated as often as one likes: the “most elementary structural rhythms" find their expression “[n]umerically . . . in small numbers"84 (e.g., parallel arrangement of identical straight lines at equal distance from oneanother). Higher-level structures result, for example, when a short and long element (scale), or a light and dark element (weight)alternate. Such a view makes clear the extent to which the artist, who had also taught life drawing in Weimar, differed from the academicism of traditional anatomy for artists.

Then, after treating the process of vision, he moved on to movement as an artistic problem. As we have already seen elsewhere, he interpreted the process of artistic creation as movement in permanence. "Shortly after the first productive movement the first countermovement sets in, a receptive movement. By this same principle of crescendo (and also decrescendo) Klee depicted the movements of the colors at the end of the ninth lecture, ranging from the cold to the warm and from the warm to the cold colors, whereby the arrow symbolized heating or cooling, respectively. Thus at the end of the winter semester 1921–22 the transition to the theory of color was established, and it was the theory of color that Klee would systematically develop for the first time during the winter semester 1922–23 (following the refresher course of summer semester 1922).

## Theory of Color

In the introduction to his textbook on color, Klee emphasized that he “did not limit [himself] to his own observations," but rather “did not hesitate" to draw on Goethe (Farbenlehre), Runge (Farbenkugel), Delacroix, and Kandinsky (On the Spiritual in Art).101 Although this was no doubt true, Klee's theory of color is more than just a patchwork of the theoretical approaches of others; rather, it represents, especially in light of the fundamental and thoroughgoing principle of movement, an original contribution to the theory of color.

Klee saw that his first task was to set up “a kind of ideal paint box in which the colours are arranged on sound principles."102It is no coincidence that Klee started with the naturalphenomenon of the rainbow only to show that it is of limited use in establishing a sequence of colors. He noted that the continuous sequence of colors in the rainbow have generally been “picked out" as follows: red-violet, red, orange, yellow, green, blue, blue-violet. He objected that red-violet and blue-violet are clearly not whole colors but merely “halves." Klee also objected to the"finiteness"103 of this linear band of color; as we have seen many times before, “finiteness" is diametrically opposed to Klee's transcendental inclinations.

The search for a form in which the finiteness of the pendulum movement from red-violet to blue-violet (and back) has been overcome and that holds out promise for infinity led Klee to eliminate gravity in a thought process and in that way to allow the earthly swinging motion of the pendulum to transform into the cosmic turning motion of the top. In the six-part color circle there are three diameters. They not only mark the relevant relation between two complementary colors that lie opposite each other on the periphery of the circle (red—green, yellow—violet, blue—orange) but also point to the penetrating color movements of these complementary colors, which become less colorful toward the center, fading to a gray point at the center. In addition to these three main diameters, of course, "we can draw as many more as we please; they will be no less correct than those discussed, but will mean something different. In contrast to the diametral movement of the pendulum, the color movement that is found on the outer edge of the circle, which Klee called "peripheral," is infinite. As with the diametral movements, the principle of movement proved to be a fruitful one here, and it led to a creative expansion of classical theories from Goethe to Kandinsky. Klee didnot examine the individual colors in isolation for their psycho-physical qualities but rather—starting with the primary colors, red, yellow, and blue—inquired into their range. He recognized that each of these three primary colors takes up two thirds of the circumference of the circle, whereas one third remains free. He demonstrated this first using the color red. In addition to the “red peak" there are two other important points on the periphery, namely, the "warm end of red" (which coincides with the yellow peak) and the “cold end of red“ (which coincides with the blue peak); the third, between the peaks of yellow and blue—that is, the green regian—lies outside the range of red. The same holds true for yellow and blue, so that the artist generalized, “Every color starts ever so gently from its zero, that is its neighbor peak, rises to its own peak, and from there descends again slowly to its zero, that is, the next neighbor peak."109

Incharacterizing these color movements, Klee once again made use of terminology from music. He spoke of a crescendo and a diminuendo and then continued, “But now something else comes in: The colours on this circle do not sound in unison as the chain might lead one to suppose, but in a kind of three-part counterpoint. . . . The voices come in successively as in a canon. At each one of the three main points one voice reaches its climax, another voice softly begins, and a third dies away."110 Consequently, Klee called this polyphonic relationship and harmony of the three primary colors the "canon of colour totality."111

## Klee's Teaching in the Context of Bauhaus Pedagogy

The color sphere, with its basic polarity of light anddarkness, bright and dark, its nèutral gray point in the center, and its wealth of colors rich in connections both on the surface and in the interior is particularly well suited to Klee's striving for harmony and totality. This can, by the way, also be said of Johannes Itten, who published in 1921, as an appendix to the almanac Utopiaedited by Bruno Adler, his planimetric sketchof the color sphere with its seven levels of light and twelve hues (see “The Course" in chapter 5). Ignoring for a moment the inconsistencies that the color sphere has (problems that result above all from the fact that the pure colors at the equator have varying degrees of brightness), the question that remains in relation to his color theory is how Klee made use of a self-contained system like that of the color sphere, which is based on ideas of harmony, in a historical situation in which closure had become a fiction as the result of a fundamental crisis of existence and consciousness, as well as contradictions in society and diverging worldviews. This question brings us back again to Klee's definition of the function of art. We have seen that Klee's thought was directed toward a region that lies beyond quotidian reality, one in which cosmic balance reigns; and we also saw that for him art represented the possibility of taking part in this sphere that is divorced from our imperfect earthly reality. Consequently, the self-contained character of Klee's theory of design is a reflection of a view of existence and conception of art that hardly tolerated imperfection or the fragmentary but instead demanded totality and harmony.

The pedagogical risks inherent in an artistic theory founded in this way are obvious: namely, that the students might fixate on this notion of harmony and, beyond that, on the teacher's artistic toolkit. This can be seen not only in many of the student works that were created at the Bauhaus but also in the works of former Bauhaus students who never succeeded in steppingout of Klee's shadow (e.g., Benita Koch-Otte). Renate L. Bunk noted this critical point very clearly when she remarked that Klee, by “supplyingfixed results robbed the course and the school of precisely those procedural and productive aspects that he valued so highly in his own artistic activity."118 Even so, this must be qualified, because in his course Klee, unlike Kandinsky, was never dogmatic, never asserted an unconditional claim of validity. Helene Schmidt-Nonné remarked that he once left his students at the end of a series of courses by remarking, "This is one possibility—I myself don't make use of it."119And he also expressly warned his students against a blind schematicism: “Such misunderstandings lead to construction for its own sake. They haunt the minds of narrow-chested asthmatics who give us laws instead of works Who have too little air in their lungs to realize that the laws should only be underneath in order that flowers may grow from them. That we look for laws in order to find out how a work deviates from the natural works around us, from the land, animals, and people, but without being foolish about it. That laws are only the common foundation of nature and art."120

Klee's teachings, which in many places seem dazzlingly rational but in fact represent a "highly personal poetics"121 full of speculative allusions and subjective analogies, could only with difficulty be applied at the Bauhaus outside the realm of the fine arts, that is, to applied design, to the design of products (clearly it was most easily managed in the textile department, at least to the extent it did not involve the production of utilitarian fabrics but rather the design of carpets and tapestries of a distinctly pictorial nature). Even so, apart from a few exceptions (e.g., Hannes Meyer, Ernst Kállai), there seems to have been a general consensus at the Bauhaus that the sort of introduction of design basics and the training of"pictorial thinking" found in Klee's courses (and in Kandinsky's) was useful, necessary, and indispensable as a foundation, even if the possibilities of directly transferring it to the practical work of the designer were considered limited. “Not that we want to turn you primarily into draughtsmen and painters! But we shall have to do a bit of drawing and painting together, since these activities necessarily put us in touch with essential ways in which things hang together."122 Thus Klee addressed his students in a course at the beginning of 1924.
