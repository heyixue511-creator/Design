# 9 Animated Isotype on film 1941-7

Christopher Burke

It was fortunate for Otto and Marie Neurath, in terms of their professional re-establishment in Britain, that film maker Paul Rotha contacted them soon after they were released from internment in 1941. The timing was propitious for Rotha, too. He was eager to make documentary films for the British Ministry of Information (Mor), where the situation had recently become favourable for him: since summer 1940 its director was Jack Beddington, who had set up the Shell Film Unit (and commissioned Rotha's first film Contact in 1933); Beddington appointed his colleague at Shell, documentarist Arthur Elton, as Moi Film Officer in January 1941. Rotha seized this moment to establish his own production company, Paul Rotha Productions Ltd, and set about implementing the ideas he had for wartime documentaries. He stipulated to the Mor that he would make films concerning the 'social impact [of war] on civilian life'.¹

Rotha had a deep scholarly appreciation of film, having written the landmark book The film till now (193o) when he was a mere 22 years old, followed by the study Documentary film (1935). In his first book he grouped films into 13 categories, one of which was 'The cartoon film'. He wrote:

To many writers at the moment, the Disney cartoons are the most witty and satisfying productions of modern cinema. Their chief merit lies in their immediate appeal to any type of audience, simply because they are based on rhythm.

He also praised the silhouette animation films of Lotte Reiniger, and especially the quality of her craft, which her imitators' work lacked.³ Rotha's sensitivity to such qualities is to be expected from a man who had studied theatre costume design at the Slade School of Art in 1924. ## A few ounces a day

Work on the film about blood transfusion was soon overtaken by an urgent project to make a film about salvage for the Moi, in order to convince British householders not to waste material that could be useful for the war effort. The production of this film, which became A few ounces a day, is worth documenting in some detail, as it is the only surviving film that consists entirely of animation designed by the Neuraths for Rotha.¹³ In order for them to work on this project it was necessary that they first be cleared by the Ministry of Labour, due to their status as registered aliens. Rotha received a letter from the Moı Films division stating: 'Naturally your request has the support of this Ministry. It is regarded as important that the skilled services of Dr. and Mrs. Neurath should be obtained for this film.'¹ Apparently there were no lingering official doubts about the loyalty of the Neuraths to Britain. Neurath was grateful to Rotha for the confidence shown in them: 'Imagine that we as Anti-Nazis are very glad to have an opportunity to support the war effort, within our own field. You represent your country's friendliness to us.'15

The salvage film was discussed and outlined in a remarkably short time: after a meeting at the Moi attended by Rotha and Neurath at the end of April 1941, Rotha's scriptwriter Wolfgang Wilhelm visited the

In a sense the Neuraths were the directors of A few ounces a day, given that they determined the mise-en-scène when designing it. The film bears no director's credit, stating instead that it was 'designed by Isotype Institute'.2¹ Rotha saw film-making as a task of planned montage. He considered 'preconception of dramatic content by scenario organisation' to be essential, and his films were meticulously scripted, especially those featuring a mixture of dialogue, voice-over, stock footage and Isotype animation. This approach to planning was already familiar in Isotype due to the central role of the transformer. Moreover, Rotha recognized the importance of drawings in making a screenplay more instructive.²² For A few ounces a day Marie Neurath prepared a complete storyboard of the film, which must have been an essential guide for the animation studio, along with sample pictograms and other visual material that were prepared at the Isotype office.23

Rotha was under no illusions about the time and expense involved in making even short animation films. In The film till now he had recognized that the laborious draughtsmanship, necessarily entailed in the production of the thousands of small drawings needed as material for smooth movement, has naturally limited the output of these animated cartoons'.24 The Neuraths, then in the process of reconstructing a small design office in Oxford, did not have the technical means to animate their drawings on film. Rotha suggested initially that animation could be carried out at the Technicolor film laboratory, which had the necessary equipment, but the task of animating A few ounces a day was instead given to Science Films, based in in Letchworth Garden City, about 35 miles north of London. This company worked mainly for the Air Ministry on training and instructional films for the Royal Air Force. A team of three animators there led by Norman MacQueen worked on transferring Marie Neurath's sketches and artwork to celluloid.²⁵

A few ounces a day was completed and showing in cinemas by October 1941. It seems to have been generally well received. Neurath observed that 'the public was apparently satisfied, laughing and applauding, particularly the soldiers'. He added: 'In one cinema I heard my neighbour, a woman in uniform, saying "awfully clever these thing[s]".,2 Rotha reported: 'The film has had great applause at all London cinemas where I have been.'²7 It seems fitting that a report on audience reaction to A few ounces a day was prepared by Mass Observation, the social research organization, which shared methodological aspects with both Isotype and the British documentary movement (see overleaf).
