# Postmodern Design Moves Indoors Arlene Hirst As night follows day, so postmodern interiors were an inevitable offshoot of postmodern architecture. The buildings of CHARLES MOORE (also see under "Architecture"), MICHAEL GRAVES (also see under "Masters of Postmodern Design"), ROBERT A. M. STERN (also see under "Architecture"), ROBERT VENTURI (also see under “Masters of Postmodern Design"), and their peers demanded an interior aesthetic to match. In the United States, the wider design world soon followed. Crumbling arches, free-standing columns, faux neoclassical pediments, classical references, trompe l’oeil, and a pastel palette were all postmodern elements that began to appear in profusion in the late 197os and early 198os. Pedestals were perhaps the defining signature of the style; no postmodern interior was complete without one. Designers used them in every possible material, in an almost unending array of sizes and shapes. It was the easiest way to add architectural detail to a room. Postmodernism made the past popular again, and even modernist designers were lured by its siren song. Classical statues were favorite props and marble became chic, especially when imitated by a skilled painter of faux finishes. Plastic laminate, once relegated to the kitchen and bathroom, moved into the front room, frequently with metallic and textured finishes. While any of these details unmistakably marked a project as postmodern, the interiors could be as stylistically different as a building by Stern and one by Graves. In a story by Suzanne Slesin in the New York Times in 198o, Stern described his approach to one project as a "classical theme with unclassic organization.' But, he added, "Postmodernism is not just a way of having fun with columns."' Stern's interiors to this day are filled with architectural details; these embellishments are his signature, be they pediments, porticos, pilasters, lintels, or all of the above. In a room that he designed for Metropolitan Home's ShowHouse to benefit DIFFA (Design Industries Foundation Fighting AIDS) in i991, he created dramatically overscaled swags of fiberglass-reinforced plaster that provided an eye-popping accent to an otherwise classic formal room. Stern's furniture, frequently of his own design, is generally more classically traditional than his architecture. Graves took postmodernism in a different direction—a style he incorporated into his own life. He filled his home in Princeton, New Jersey, with Biedermeier furniture, Roman antiquities, and reproductions of Romantic paintings. His favored color palette of pale teal and terracotta became a postmodern signature. Graves's ample use of Biedermeier furniture helped initiate a renaissance of the genre. Before then, it was an almost forgotten style, having originated in Germany and Austria in the early nineteenth century, and was virtually unknown in the United States. This warm, Teutonic version of Directoire and Empire style appealed to Graves, who was drawn to its use of honey-colored woqds, espe cially bird's-eye maple. Carol Vogel, writing in New York Times Magazine in 1985, authenticated Biedermeier's popularity: “It's what everyone is gravitating to," she wrote, "It used to be scarce. When MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") came along in i98ı, what was once classic and refined became boisterous and rowdy—a cartoon version of the style. It created a storm of publicity and made postmodernism itself front-page news. The impact of Memphis was perhaps most visible in the production design of movies of the i98os. “It became the production designer's go-to style to communicate Reaganite excess and i98os anti-urbanism," wrote Will Wiles for Dezeen, "The triumph of postmodernist design was to perfectly capture its age. Its tragedy was that the age was rotten."3 For many people, the first image that comes to mind at the mention of postmodern interiors is the i986 film Ruthless People. Lilly Kilvert, its designer, was drawn to the Memphis look, which suited her purposes perfectly. Kilvert has been quoted as saying that a house full of Memphis furniture is more than anyone should have to endure; she wanted Bette Midler and Danny DeVito's home to have a threatening, obnoxious quality, and filled the set with a mother-lode of avant-garde LA art, borrowing furniture from the Whiteley Gallery as well as from the local Memphis member PETER SHIRE (also see under "Design"). Memphis never had a chance in the court of public opinion. In movies, postmodernism was repeatedly cast as the villain and always used for satirical effect. Production designer Stephen Hendrickson turned a white-walled luxury duplex on Manhattan's Upper East Side into a postmodern temple; the transformation is chronicled on-screen as an interior designer, played by Daryl Hannah, decorates it and fills it with allusions of corruption and decadence. Furnishings are savage, with jagged edges and crumbling surfaces. Concrete and rusty iron abound, along with gold leaf, verdigris and faux marbre finishes. While it was clearly over the top. for many of the furniture designers who supplied props for the project, it was also déjà yu. They had real-life Wall Street clientele clamoring for just such pieces. Beetlejuice, Tim Burton's 1988 comedy about ghosts who return to their old house only to find it garishly redecorated, also used postmodernism to dire effect. Burton called it “post-mortemism". Production designer Bo Welch denies that he took the style too far, claiming it embodied the ideas of Richard Meier\*, FRANK GEHRY (see under “Architecture") and PHILIPPE STARCK (see under “Design") rolled into one. And while he admits that his design teetered on the brink of parody, he defends it, saying, "You go so far and think no one would ever do this. Then you got to Long Island and see one that's been taken even further." Wiles has pointed out that in all three of these movies, released around the same time, the rich clients "clearly consider themselves discerning, but their tastes are being held up for ridicule...They are either trashy new money or pseudish cosmopolitans. They are nearly always contrasted against wholesome small-town blue collar types." He postulates that postmodern furniture and interiors never took hold because of the damning treatment it received in the movies.4 But postmodern interiors did take hold for a time. They were seen not only on movie screens, but in restaurant and hotel designs, and were thus introduced in a more positive way to the general public. With its pale green and pink walls and huge clock dominating the top of a central staircase, it was unlike anything seen before. The project soon won him many commissions in the United States. Like most of Starck's work, the Royalton Hotel in New York City felt like a glamorous stage set, with a lobby filled with his spiky, metal-legged furniture and horn-shaped light fixtures. It set a new paradigm for hotel design. Two years later, at the Paramount, Starck acted as a curator, filling the monumental two-story lobby with a dramatic sweeping staircase and a collection of designer furniture not his own, including Marc Newson's famous Lockheed Lounge, which now fetches millions of dollars at design auctions. American designers also got involved, and postmodern restaurant designs appeared all over the country, including Spiaggia in Chicago, a place described by some as a PoMo temple to pizza. Frank Gehry worked on Rebecca's, a Los Angeles restaurant that opened in i986, adorning it with two giant crocodiles suspended from the ceiling, a glass octopus chandelier, and two of his now iconic fish lamps. He emploved a host of local artists to finish the job. A new breed of restaurant designers—Adam Tihany, Sam Lopata, and the firm Haverson/Rockwell among them—turned postmodernism into something exuberant and joyful, extending its reach into the farthest corners of the United States. When the economy went sour, the excesses of the i98os—and postmodernism with them—faded from the scene. Today, however, the movement is showing new signs of life, with a revived passion for Memphis furniture and Memphis-style decorations leading the way. Postmodernism's greatest achievement was its liberation of interiors from the straitjacket of period styles; until it came along, most American homes were done to a turn in everything from French provincial or Louis XV to colonial or Art Deco style. Ironically, the only period still alive and well today is modernism itself. Otherwise, eclecticism reigns, relegating period rooms primarily to museums. It is an approach that postmodernism surely brought about.
