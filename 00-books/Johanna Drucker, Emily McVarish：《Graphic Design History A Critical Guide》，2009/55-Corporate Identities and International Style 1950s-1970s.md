Pop and Protest 1960s-1970s Pop culture sparked a dynamic dialogue between the graphic language of commerce and those of counterculture and protest. Slick photography and self-conscious humor introduced elements of sophistication and complicity into the graphic design of mainstream advertising. Graphic designers began to develop signature styles and aspired to the celebrity status of rock musicians and other media stars. Counterculture designers used ever more readily available means of graphic production to create messages that registered effectively within the media landscape. While graphic designers participated in an increasingly mediated culture, critical approaches to the study of mass communication developed a new vocabulary for “understanding media." Pop designers produced images that circulated as signs in media systems, without necessarily bearing any connection to a reference base in lived reality. 13.1 Doyle Dane Bernbach, Volkswagen ad, 1960. Still famous, this series of ads for Volkswagen marked a major shift in graphic design. A prime example of the "big idea” approach to advertising, the campaign assumed that readers were hip enough to “get" its jokes and grasp the larger point. Seriousness was gone. Cleverness was in. Smart campaigns that appealed to the savvy consumer trumped older convictions about product information or brand loyalty. Flippant copy and polished, ironic photography set a new tone in the industry. Consumers wanted to identify themselves as in-the-know sophisticates, and the facetious image of the VW appealed. The product was sold by association with the “coolness" of ads that inverted the logic of earlier promotional strategies. These advertisements demonstrate how smoothly the establishment (in this case, a major manufacturer) could appropriate countercultural style for its own purposes. Graphic design permeated American and European visual culture in the decades following World War II. The prolific presence of graphic images became a given. But in the 1960s, Pop art and style introduced a new appreciation of graphic media. “Pop" drew heavily on mass media productions shaped by commercial interests, advertising, and the entertainment industry. Film and television celebrities led the way, but artists, politicians, newscasters, business leaders, and athletes—indeed, people in every walk of life—grasped the importance of their "image." Mass media logic became central to contemporary life. This mediated condition of existence was the subtext of pop culture. Self-consciousness about graphic design was one symptom of this emerging awareness of media. Graphic designers forged an idiom from and for the sign systems established in television, magazines, and slick advertisements. Comics, Op Art, media imagery, and psychedelic patterns burst into view along with long hair, short skirts, and a highly graphic in-your-face attitude. Pop design linked commercial graphic art with fast-paced, super-cool, and ultra-hot styles. Witty and playful, the pop impulse defined itself as freespirited in calculated opposition to the straight corporate world with which it was contemporary. The antiauthoritarian, nonconformist stance of the counterculture was identified with rock and roll, fashion, drugs, sex, and radical politics by contrast to the button-down, pin-stripe values of the establishment. Revolutionary rhetoric connected youth culture to real and imagined political engagement. 'The entertainment industry harvested talent and recycled outsider individualism into marketing ploys. Consumers assumed that they were being manipulated by ads and images. Instead of hiding that fact, designers flaunted it in campaigns premised on a knowing wink and nod directed at their audience. As alternative and subcultural graphic design emerged, style choices were used to indicate affiliation with mod, rocker, hippie, or straight culture. In the 1960s, pop graphics helped define identity across a broad spectrum of contexts from high-end advertising and editorial layouts for fashionable publications to do-it-yourself underground, anticonsumerist, activist productions (Fig. 13.2). In the service of an earlier mode of consumer culture, graphic designers had created fantasy images, but their lifestyle narratives and vignettes had still emphasized the brand identity and value of the products they promoted. Pop played on the media system itself, manipulating icons and images for their own sake. Anything could become a sign free to circulate as currency within media culture. Self-conscious graphic design Graphic design became selfconscious in the early 1960s. In recognition of the growing sophistication of consumers, graphic designers discovered a sense of humor. Earlier generations of graphic designers had demonstrated their commitment to style as a way to create an aura of desirability around consumer goods. But, in the 1960s, a chic graphic style became a desirable asset in its own right. Editors and advertisers clearly realized that savvy consumers had outgrown the "message" mode of graphic design. Slick surfaces and high production values Pop art took its imagery and impetus from commercial design, and graphic designers were quick to seize their advantage. Pop artist Andy Warhol had begun his career as a graphic designer. His commercial illustrations gained him a fine reputation in the 1950s. But when he transferred his skills to the art world, his paintings and later silkscreens of the labels and package designs of Campbell's soup cans and Brillo boxes brought international attention to the iconography of graphic art. Graphic designers could appeal to a high-art crowd, too, often working with budgets that fine artists would have envied, but fine art carried cultural validation and cachet. Graphic designers had access to tools that were constantly pushing the industry standard, and fine art became a follower, rather than a leader, in the production of visual culture. The photography in pop campaigns was expertly produced and highly polished. The slickness that entered graphic design in the 1960s was in part, a result of new color printing capabilities and the use designers made of their intensified range of saturated hues and fine screens. Airbrush retouching became a norm, and the tightly focused, super-consumable, high-end photography of fashion shoots became the benchmark for commercial production. A landmark in the history of graphic design, Ken Garland's manifesto struck a note of countercultural consciousness among design professionals. Counterculture and the alternative press The underground press thrived in the 1960s, driven by political and aesthetic impulses and sustained by readily available means of production. Inexpensive offset printing on newsprint provided a platform for a range of expressions aimed at establishing communities of opinion and belief. Readers identified with each other through style. The counterculture's utopian vision combined drugs, rock and roll, and radical politics. Printed offset on newsprint, they often made use of very low-end typesetting equipment and were relatively inexpensive to produce. Artwork was usuaily done by staff or local artists, photographs were reproduced in coarse halftones, and graphic styles and standards showed a complete disregard for the commodified look of high-end mainstream magazines. Publications like the Berkeley Barb, The Village Voice, and Rolling Stone were supported by advertising revenues, but underground comics survived on sales alone. Many alternative publications were produced by amateurs in their garages—or by young print shop workers in their off-hours. Some became established publications. Others flourished only briefly but famously (Fig. 13.9). Independent presses, particularly experimental literary ventures, established a widespread network of alternative publishing. Although they often had little economic success, they were the spawning ground for much original work by artists who achieved considerable recognition later in their careers. Many prided themselves on the rudimentary production that tabletop duplicating machines like mimeographs and xerographs provided. These publications were distinctively produced outside the mainstream establishment.
