OPPOSITE Art Tower, Mito, Japan, 1990. When an architect first gains notice on an international stage with a giant robot, it's a hard act to follow. Arata Isozaki's Demonstration Robot at lapan's Expo ′7o world's fair was a traffic-stopping gigantic mechanical figure that emitted smoke, smells. light, and sound, and whose base became a stage for evening music performances. Undaunted by the challenge, Isozaki went on to design dozens of buildings and objects that were equally avant-garde (though not necessarily as entertaining), becoming one of the best-known and most respected of his generation of Japanese architects. Born in Oita Prefecture, Isozaki graduated in i954 from the University of Tokyo and worked for nine years in the office of Kenzo Tange, the most important architect to emerge from that country in the years after the Second World War. He became interested in the Metabolists, a group of young architects who were developing grand utopian schemes for ideal cities that would grow and change like human organisms, in futuristic concepts akin to those of Italy's Radical Design groups. Isozaki, however, moved beyond the utilitarian concerns of the Metabolists to conceive buildings of striking form but no identifable style. They are a personal interpretation of Modernism that avoids easy characterization, though he is generally considered a postmodernist. Though his earliest work was rectilinear, Isozaki began to explore the vault form, using semicircular precast concrete for barrel-vaulted structures that recall both Japanese and Grecian precedents. He adopted historicist forms to suit each specific project, for example combining the pyramid with the barrel vault in the Museum of Contemporary Art, Los Angeles (i986), using proportions based on the Golden Section in a striking but serene structure that reflects both Eastern and Western aesthetics. The Art Tower Mito (i99o) is a merger of architecture and sculpture, a snake-like twist of triangles inspired by Brancusi. But Isozaki's most unabashedly postmodern project was the Team Disney building in Orlando, Florida (i99ı), a giant cylinder surrounded by a cluster of vari-colored cubic forms. Isozaki's ventures into product design reveal a subtle wit, as in the Marilyn chairs (i973), which add Monroe-like curves to a Mackintosh form that he considered too severe,²² and his robot-like Fuji bookcase-cabinets (i98ı) for MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design"). He also designed jewelry for ACME (see under “Edited Design") and CLETO MUNARI (see under “Edited Design") and was invited to participate in ALESSI's (see under "Edited Design") celebrated Tea & Coffee Piazza project, but failed to complete his design on time. LÉON KRIER b.1946 LUXEMBOURG-BORN ARCHITECT.URBAN PLANNER, AND MOST INFLUENTIAL THEORIST OF NEW URBANISM. BELOW Poundbury, Dorset, England, 1993. An architect who has generally preferred writing about and teaching the discipline to practicing it, Léon Krier has spent his career attempting to define and correct the problems raised by contemporary architecture.²4 He is considered the most important theorist of the New Urbanism, an urban planning movement that was officially founded in i993 but had actually begun as early as the i97os, when architects and planners began to focus on the failings of International Style Modernism. New Urbanism advocates an approach to the development of modern cities that would reject cookie-cutter functionalism, instead reinventing the diverse, walkable coinmunities of the years before the advent of the automobile, Modernism, and suburban sprawl. In proposing inclusive and accessible architecture, Krier believed that there need be no barrier between high art and popular taste. As consultant to Charles, Prince of Wales on architectural issues, Krier was responsible for developing the master plan for Poundbury, the traditional-style (and somewhat controversial) English town extension built in i993 with the Prince as its major supporter. Born in Luxembourg, a modestly sized city of traditional architecture, Krier was nonetheless an early enthusiast of Modernism, though a visit to Le Corbusier's concrete-block Cité Radieuse in Marseilles reportedly alienated him from the style. By the time he followed his older brother Rob to the University of Stuttgart to study architecture, he had decided that the prevailing style was meaningless, seeking change only for its own sake, and that discarding traditional architectural elements was uncongenial to humans. Krier left his studies in Stuttgart for London in i968, and worked briefly as an assistant in the office of JAMES STIRLING (at precisely the time when architects were beginning to look back to historicist styles) before returning to academia to teach architecture and town planning while running his private practice. He contributed a design to the important Strada Novissima project at the 198o Venice Biennale, and his relatively few built architectural works include a tower in the planned community of Seaside\*, Florida, and a town hall in Florida, both historicist forms. Nevertheless, Krier believes there need be no struggle between traditional design and Modernism, asserting that, "The real struggle ought solely to be between good and bad design."25 Despite preferring theory to practice, Krier has completed buildings in Luxembourg, France, Italy, Spain, Germany, the United States, and the United Kingdom. He has also designed furniture for Giorgetti that is clearly influenced by traditional styles. Krier's extensive and influential teaching career has taken place on both sides of the Atlantic, first at the Architectural Association and the Royal College of Art in London and then in America at Princeton, Yale, University of Virginia, and University of Notre Dame. Through his academic work he has been a considerable influence on urbanists and town planners like Andrés Duany\* and Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk\*.
