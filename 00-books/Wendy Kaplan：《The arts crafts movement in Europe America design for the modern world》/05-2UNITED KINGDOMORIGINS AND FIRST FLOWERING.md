# 2 UNITED KINGDOM: ORIGINS AND FIRST FLOWERING ![](images/39849ebf1520f7c2a6637ed750198f391db8aac7e83a1c76c5032713c26a43b8.jpg) 2.1 Charger, 1882–88. William De Morgan hen the Arts and Crafts movement was at its height, around 19o0, the United Kingdom consisted of four countries—England, Scotland, Wales, and the whole of Ireland, not just the northeastern counties. England was always the dominant partner, politically and culturally. An Act of Union between England and Scotland in 1707 had created the United Kingdom of Great Britain, which included Wales, by default. "Britain" and "British," in this essay as elsewhere, apply to this island union. Ireland, long treated as an English colony, had been brought within this system by another Act of Union in 1801, creating the United Kingdom of Great Britain and lreland. The Arts and Crafts movement echoed these relationships. It started in England and flourished there perhaps more than anywhere else. England looms large in this story. In Scotland the movement reflected the ambiguities of the Union, sometimes seeming Scottish and sometimes British. In Wales it hardly took root at all, so Wales does not figure in this story. In Ireland it was definitely Irish, expressing a nationalist spirit. In this essay I have woven the stories of the Arts and Crafts in England, Scotland, and Ireland into a single narrative. ART AND !NDUSTRY In England and Scotland the Arts and Crafts movement was a response to industrialization. (In Ireland, which scarcely experienced industrialization in the nineteenth century, it had a different character, as we shall see.) From the mid-eighteenth century, the factory system, steam power, and complex machinery transformed Britain's productive capacity, bringing greater prosperity to a good part of British society. These changes were endorsed by the intellectual temper of the times, which valued reason, quantification, and the free play of economic forces. But Victorian culture was not monolithic, and voices were raised against the industrial spirit of the age. One of those voices was that of art critic John Ruskin, in the second volume of his history of Venetian architecture, The Stones of Venice, published in 1853. Wanting to define the superiority of Gothic architecture, Ruskin contrasted the precise and perfect moldings of Greek buildings with the rough and lively carving of Gothic: skill and exactness versus feeling and humanity, Suddenly, his angry mind slipped to the present day and to the mechanical skills of British industry: Men may be beaten, chained, tormented, yoked like cattle, slaughtered like summer flies, and yet remain in one sense, and the best sense, free. But to smother their souls...to blight and hew into rotting pollards the suckling branches of their human intelligence, to make the flesh and skin which, after the worm's work on it, is to see God, into leathern thongs to yoke machinery with—this is to be slavemasters indeed.' So many thoughts are crowded into this terrible image of rationalized work. Yet it is also simple. Ruskin is thinking of poor, stumbling, feeling men: Go forth again to gaze upon the old cathedral front, where you have smiled so often at the fantastic ignorance of the old sculptors: examine once more those ugly goblins, and formless monsters, and stern statues, anatomiless and rigid; but do not mock at them, for they are signs of the life and liberty of every workman who struck the stone.² Ruskin's rhetoric created powerful antitheses—between Gothic and Greek, heart and mind, imagination and reason, medieval and modern, hand and machine, art and industry. These are the values of Romanticism set against those of utilitarianism. ![](images/47334ed60e940d0895fd5bc09ea6a1825c3fa905f46c60c21e55151567a9a313.jpg) Anti-industrialism was stronger in the Arts and Crafts movement in Britain than in other countries, and these antitheses were the stuff of it. For many Arts and Crafts people, "industry" meant a landscape of polluting factories, and "trade" meant profit triumphing over beauty and workmanship. They often talked about "the machine" and, given Ruskin's writings, one might have expected them to be machine-haters. In fact, on this issue, they were both subtle and pragmatic. When they talked about "the machine," they were not thinking so much of particular machines as of the whole industrial and commercial system and the way it reduced making to a routine. As William Morris said, "It is not this or that tangible steel and brass machine which we want to get rid of, but the great intangible machine of commercial tyranny, which oppresses the lives of all of us." In their workshops they were undogmatic about machines, using them when it suited their needs, and not when it hindered them. Ruskin set art and industry against each other. Some of his contemporaries hoped to unite them. Owen Jones, architect and designer; Richard Redgrave, painter turned administrator; and Henry Cole, civil servant, were design reformers, as we now call them. They believed that decorative art should follow rational principles: ornament should be conventionalized, patterns on flat surfaces should show only two dimensions. For them, carpets on which lilies and roses seemed to bloom in three-dimensional profusion were an offense against reason. They admired the nineteenth century's industrial achievements; their contribution would be to improve the taste of manufacturers and the public. This was the organizing, rationalizing spirit of the mid-nineteenth century applied to decorative art, and it would be hard to imagine a point of view more different from Ruskin's. The design reformers, however, were in tune with the age, and they directed the government's Department of Practical Art and its successor, the Department of Science and Art. These departments controlled much British art education in the second half of the nineteenth century, and they created the collections of decorative art at the South Kensington Museum (now the Victoria and Albert).' The influence of the design reformers' cultural empire, familiarly known as "South Kensington," could be felt throughout late-Victorian decorative art, including the Arts and Crafts. The heads of most Arts and Crafts people probably contained a healthily unresolved mixture of Ruskinism and design reform. BEGINNINGS, 1860-1880 It is a delicate matter to trace beginnings. This section introduces architects and designers of the 1860s and 1870s who were interested in old-fashioned ways of making things or who believed that decorative art was as important as fine art. These were Arts and Crafts attitudes, but they did not appear as such, for the phrase "Arts and Crafts" had not been invented yet. These attitudes were also part of other movements, such as the Gothic Revival, which reintroduced medieval craft techniques, or the Aesthetic movement, which claimed a new, artistic seriousness for the decorative arts. In 1861 William Morris and a group of artist-friends set themselves up as a firm of decorators, Morris, Marshall, Faulkner & Co. At first they chiefly made furniture and stained glass, often working for Gothic Revival architects (2.4). In the 1870s they began to produce more textiles and wallpapers, meeting an enthusiasm for the "artistic"furnishing of houses encouraged by the Aesthetic movement. This gave scope for Morris's special talent as a pattern designer (2.3). He drew inspiration from English garden and hedgerow flowers, and from old Italian, Persian, and Turkish textiles, with their formal patterns: freshness from nature, discipline from the past. Somehow, the ![](images/7538846c5fdc02567ab16d8f8842ad02a2971580ab3e476289eaa8f8adfcd282.jpg) more disciplined the tulips and the roses are, the more luxuriant they seem. By 1875, when Morris took on sole ownership of the firm as Morris & Co., it had become a fashionable, medium-sized manufacturing and decorating firm, though a rather peculiar one. For one thing, most of the original partners were artists, not businessmen. Morris was best known as a poet; Philip Webb was an architect; and Edward Burne-Jones was a painter. There was a crossing of boundaries here. Decorative art was being moved from "trade' into "art."Morris's exacting attitude as a manufacturer was also unusual. He knew the effects he wanted, and in his case, this often took him into the past. Because he could not get the right colors for his textiles from the new chemical dyes, he went back to vegetable dyes. In printing he used a handpress and the traditional process of damping the paper before printing. This was the only way he could get his rich effects. Morris's attachment to old methods of working is often presented as ideological. It was also technical: he was a manufacturer getting things right. ![](images/3ffca536d98f7e733f07cd5444b693dec14434f8f5b4c50ee83c04a85ad2579c.jpg) ![](images/04a936feaafc25b6471059ecc960a1c46d741d5ce7797e349a4d09e69703a4f6.jpg) ![](images/6d8062e31204f8cb7a52be9f56ea35e95944df43760957564cad2ffaf5080c71.jpg) William De Morgan trained as a painter in the 1860s, but then he met Morris, who encouraged him to turn to the decorative arts. In 1873 he set up a pottery in Chelsea, where he and his craftsmen decorated pots and tiles with colored glazes. He was fascinated by luster, a metallic glaze made famous by the potters of medieval Islam, and Renaissance Spain and Italy (2.1). The technique had been more or less lost, but De Morgan experimented until he had re-created it. He was half artist and half scientist, and his appetite for the craft was an appetite for experiment and technique, the excited happiness of the maker in the workshop, waiting for the next batch of unpredictable lusterware to be fired. There were two designers whose early careers and attitudes were particularly shaped by the movements of the 1860s and 1870s, and who were to play an important part in the Arts and Crafts. After an early success as a children's book illustrator, Walter Crane turned to designing wallpapers, textiles, and other products in the 1870s, becoming a leading designer of the Aesthetic movement. He also produced ambitious, symbolic paintings, which never had the success of his decorative work; people dubbed him "the Academician of the Nursery." Crane resisted the hierarchy that this implied: he saw fine arts and decorative arts as one project. This merging of categories was typical of the Aesthetic movement and would be carried forward into the Arts and Crafts under the banner of "the unity of art." Lewis F. Day began his career as a stained-glass artist, but in the 1870s he became a freelance designer specializing in wallpapers and textiles, and he was a consultant to several manufacturers. Two-dimensional decoration, particularly pattern design, was his forte, and he wrote dry, analytical textbooks on the subject. The spirit of his work and ideas was close to that of the design reformers associated with South Kensington, and in the Arts and Crafts years he would champion design as applied ornament, and the designer's role as collaborator with industry. Then there were two architects whose contrasting habits fed into the richness of the Arts and Crafts. Philip Webb, a good friend of Morris's, was an austere designer who saw his work as "building" rather than "architecture."When he was designing, he thought a lot about the site and local building traditions. The massing of Four Gables was inspired by tower houses that policed the nearby Scottish border in medieval times. In peacetime, people lived in a low wing, but when in danger, they went up into the tower (2.6). Conversely, the Gothic Revival church architect J. D. Sedding offered the pleasures of decoration; he knew how painters, carvers, and metalworkers could enrich a building. Sedding loved the vividness and humanity of medieval wood carvings in English parish churches, "life-revel," as he called them.6 In the 188Os his love of decorative craftsmanship became a program for the Arts and Crafts. Toward the end of the 1870s, some of these attitudes found their way into words. Morris began to give occasional lectures on the decorative arts. He did not enjoy it, but he worked at it, hammering Ruskin's insights into ordinary sentences. Real art, he would say, "is the expression by man of his pleasure in labour."He would take his audience back to the Middle Ages and show them how medieval decorative arts were an enrichment of life: "Time was when the mystery and wonder of handicrafts were well acknowledged by the world, when imagination and fancy mingled with all things made by man; and in those days all handicraftsmen were artists, as we should now call them."8This vision of the past was also a commentary on what was lacking in the present. THE MOVEMENT TAKES SHAPE, 1880-1890 New workshops and studios were set up throughout the 188Os. Phoebe Traquair, an Edinburgh artist and mother of three young children, took up one technique after another, starting with embroidery in the late 1870s, adding calligraphy and illumination in the 1880s, then mural painting in 1885. In 1877 and 1878 W. A. S. Benson got to know Burne-Jones and Morris. At that time he was training to be an architect but was uneasy at the prospect of a deskbound profession. Morris and Burne-Jones encouraged him to start a workshop, as they had done almost twenty years earlier, and in 1880 he took that step. Benson loved tinkering with machinery, and by 1890 he was running what amounted to a factory in Hammersmith, equipped with lathes and stamping presses, producing vessels and light fittings in copper and brass (2.10). These were sold in his shop in London's West End, and he also issued catalogues, principally for the light fittings. Benson's work was very different from Traquair's: hers was handwork, while his depended a good deal on machinery. Both were part of the Arts and Crafts movement. In about 1882 A. H. Mackmurdo, an architect, Selwyn Image, a designer and former clergyman, and Herbert Horne, an assistant to Mackmurdo, set up the Century Guild in London. This was an association of designers and craftsmen, rather than a single workshop, and furniture, metalwork, textiles, and interiors appeared under its name in the 1880s. The swirling forms of some of their designs, such as the Praise of the Soul textile, have been seen as precursors of the Art Nouveau style that developed in Europe in the 1890s (2.7). In 1883 T. J. Cobden-Sanderson, a neurotic London lawyer, took up bookbinding on the suggestion of Morris's wife, Jane, and the physical work helped to calm his soul. He became the leading British Arts and Crafts binder (2.8). In 1885 Christopher Whall, an unsuccessful painter, left London to live on a smallholding in Surrey with his wife. They kept chickens, a pig, and a cow. He wanted to make stained-glass windows, not just design them, and so he taught himself by trial and error, sharing his workshop with the cow (2.9). In 1888 C. R. Ashbee, an idealistic young architect, started a workshop in Whitechapel, the Guild of Handicraft, making metalwork and furniture (2.11—13). This was the Arts and Crafts as social reform, bringing pleasure in work to the poor of East London. In all these workshops, the currents of the Arts and Crafts flowed strongly—the idealism, the sense of exploration, the freshness of design, the healing and creative power of making things. ![](images/cd8334524a7ae04c23a36f346818b383656da9bba0653e9cb949964be640530e.jpg) ![](images/5698a1ddfcc294089c18f13f02f893900131f8c9c32352b68f68e9e4bb32dbff.jpg) ![](images/7a53a750d33ae76d90d8e14a004c4a426599c3be91deebbdb89373337c6f4192.jpg) ![](images/8510304cf3e42ea94cc5fec7651bf2da4eb7388fab33c820c2410595e49c47a1.jpg) This was also the private side of the Arts and Crafts, the decorative artist in his studio, the craftsman in his workshop, negotiating daily with materials. It was the heart of the movement where the tables were made, the books printed, the hopes and imaginings shaped. We have to listen carefully to hear this heart beating in the 188Os, for during this decade the noisy, public side of the movement came to the fore in the busy urban world of organizations and exhibitions. The Arts and Crafts developed alongside other movements of the decade, including a growing public concern with the social consequences of industrialization. The term "Industrial Revolution" was coined in 1880, and the early stages of socialism in Britain belong to this decade. The Arts and Crafts shared some of this spirit, and some of its leading figures, including Morris, Crane, and Ashbee, were socialists. ![](images/01f5af954d8e836bd7d02e39435b927e05cfb0ad972c8d9ae98e0ef3d631a30d.jpg) 2.11 The workshop of the Guild of Handicraft, 34 Commercial Street, Whitechapel, London. Ashbee, 1890 ![](images/c8a05753b092cc974608fca0319a00761b3695a21279d8ff0b960372eaa5f048.jpg) ![](images/64e567122b00781bab2398e938475bb8c04f230c90d7b48ebdf690c44b38990a.jpg) The earliest organization of the Arts and Crafts movement was the Art Workers' Guild, started as an association of painters, sculptors, architects, designers, and craftsmen early in 1884. The idea was that the members were all fundamentally artists and so could learn from each other." Meeting regularly for a lecture or a demonstration, the guild forged loyalties among its members. Since it came to include most of the principal Arts and Crafts architects and designers in London (along with a number of lesser-known painters), it amounted to a representative body, but the members were reluctant to act in public or campaign for their beliefs.'° The guild hovered between the private and the public, speaking out occasionally on issues like the regulation of advertisements, or the building of bridges over the Thames, but it never acquired a public voice." ![](images/3fdb5726c580b13d68c251b15fd4defb8913f62d6a022555ee5de07774d0d454.jpg) The motto of the guild was "Art is Unity" and the best example of this idea in practice was the Holy Trinity Church in London. It was designed by J. D. Sedding in 1888 as a free collaboration between an architect and his fellow guildsmen (2.15). Sedding was a good musician and his church was like a musical work: he was the conductor; sculptors, stained-glass artists, and metalworkers were the players or singers, each making his own contribution to the work; and the whole thing was a chorus of religious praise. This was a British version of the continental idea of the Gesamtkunstwerk, in which different kinds of art, such as painting, sculpture, architecture, and decorative art, are drawn together into a "total work of art." The second organization was the Home Arts and Industries Association, started late in 1884. Most of this essay is about the professional world of artists, architects, and designers. The association takes us to a quite different, mainly rural, world of craft as philanthropy, a world of village halls and ladies of the manor, of classes in simple wood carving and beaten metalwork. The idea was to keep the young men of the village out of the pub or away from the temptations of the local town, to revive local skills, or to provide local employment. These "home arts" classes were not dependent on the professional craft world. They developed independently and the association's job was to coordinate them. But some, like the Keswick School of Industrial Art, which started in the Lake District in 1884, were strong enough to become local industries (2.16). Gradually these stronger workshops merged with the professionals. ![](images/2a9134f58eb2d1c62870382fae10e9d4bfe99e5b48a4de03423c1762e2ec626a.jpg) It is under the heading of philanthropy that Ireland makes its first appearance in this story. There was a great deal of craft activity in Ireland in the 1880s, and almost all of it was philanthropic. This largely agricultural and poverty-stricken country had been governed by England for three centuries, and it was deeply divided between an Anglo-Irish Protestant ruling class and a Catholic peasantry. Philanthropic craft was not new to Ireland, but it took on a new urgency in the 1880s. Alice Hart, an English philanthropist, set up the Donegal Industrial Fund in 1884 to create cottage industries in the famine-ravaged northwest. In the southwest, convents at Kinsale, County Cork, and Kenmare, County Kerry, started lace-making classes for local girls in the same year. In 1888 Florence Vere O'Brien, a talented Anglo-Irish designer, revived a lacemaking industry at Limerick (2.17). The urgency was probably due to the fact that the question of "home rule"—whether Ireland should have a degree of independence—was the most important single issue in British politics in the 1880s. Philanthropic craft appealed to both sides in this debate: as demonstrating the kindliness of British rule, and as fostering the economic life of a country that deserved independence. The Arts and Crafts movement was always closer to politics in Ireland than it was in Britain It is difficult to do justice to philanthropic crafts alongside the professional movement. They were a considerable phenomenon, involving thousands of people, and they had their own identity and purpose. But we do not know much about their history, except that many organizations and classes were set up in the 1880s and lasted well into the twentieth century. They tend to be overshadowed by the more glamorous and well-documented professional movement, and they will continue to be until the history of philanthropic craft is written. The third organization was the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society. The Art Workers' Guild held small exhibitions for its members in the early years, but they did not serve to educate the public or to sell work.1² Early in 1887 several guild members, led by Walter Crane and W. A. S. Benson, talked about forming an exhibition society, and in a later discussion about names, T. J. Cobden-Sanderson suggested "the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society," which also gave the movement its name.'3 The first exhibition opened in October 1888 in the New Gallery, Regent ![](images/419fab247cd7e8ea54af69fb9bf763c51f9902f33ae88907c62f85819c25d7cd.jpg) Street, London. The society's exhibitions involved far more people than the Art Workers' Guild, and their history was, to a large extent, the public history of the Arts and Crafts movement. The first three exhibitions were annual, and the exhibitors at these early shows fall into three groups: about a hundred workshops and studios of the burgeoning Arts and Crafts—Benson, Crane, De Morgan, and others; about fifty individuals who are not familiar from published records, and who may have been amateurs or freelance designers; and about thirty manufacturers from the top end of the decorative arts trades. The trade firms were independent of the movement in their origins and in much of their activity, but at the same time they were closely involved with it. They included Thomas Wardle & Co., dyers and textile printers of the town of Leek in Staffordshire, from whom Morris learned about dyeing; Longden & Co., metalworkers much associated with Sedding; Jeffrey & Co., wallpaper printers who produced designs by Morris, Crane, and Day; and James Powell & Sons, glassmakers who were almost the only makers of Arts and Crafts table glass (2.19). ![](images/ff8caed19e02fd4bc7bdaf6bf8a26347e3850001728f213dfc1a8ca4a0c83c17.jpg) At this point we need to pause to accept a complication in the story. On the one hand, Arts and Crafts people looked down on "the trade," but on the other, firms from the top end were an important part of the movement. This was not just a complication. There was a creative tension between the little workshops and the trade firms, which lasted about twenty years and arguably gave vitality to the movement. Later, as we shall see, it disappeared, and the Arts and Crafts became a simpler, more private thing. The society's exhibitions chart the development of Arts and Crafts designs. The first three (1888, 1889, and 1890) were dominated by pattern design in the form of wallpapers, textiles, tiles, and mosaics, and by objects whose interest lay in their surface decoration—furniture decorated with inlaid or painted panels, brass and copper dishes with designs beaten on their surfaces. The Century Guild charger is typical of such work (2.20). All this was characteristic of the Aesthetic movement and of design reform, and apart from Benson's radical work in brass and copper, there was little of the feeling for simplicity, materials, and structure that we associate with the Arts and Crafts. No major events characterized these years, there were no turning points in the story, but there was a steady process of growth, both in the movement itself in its range of work and geographic extent, and in its influence on the world of architecture and the decorative arts. New workshops continued to be established. In 1891 Morris set up the Kelmscott Press so that he could print books like the fifteenth-century ones he so much admired—strong, intensely physical things with the text standing clear and black on the handmade paper (2.21). In the 1880s Alexander Fisher had experimented with the then-disregarded technique of Limoges, or painted enamels, and in the early 1890s he began to teach it at the Finsbury Technical College in London. Soon Arts and Crafts metalwork and jewelry were glowing with the soft, metallic colors of enameling. In the late 189Os the Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings, a conservation pressure group started by Morris in 1877, began to send out young architects, under the guidance of Philip Webb, to help repair historic buildings. This was a real Arts and Crafts education, learning on the building site.14 In 1898 the young Edward Johnston looked at tenth-century manuscripts in the British Museum and decided that the whole art of formal writing lay in the use of a broad-nibbed pen. Arts and Crafts calligraphy, which had stumbled in previous decades, started on its course. Douglas Cockerell trained as a bookbinder with Cobden-Sanderson, set up his own workshop in 1898, and in 1904 joined forces with the newsagents W. H. Smith & Son in a trade enterprise. They produced fine bindings to Cockerell's personal standards, trade bindings to W. H. Smith's standards, and an improved trade binding Cockerell devised to show that inexpensive bindings could still be strong and pleasant to look at.'⁵ The society's exhibition in 1890 suffered a financial loss, and after that the exhibitions were held about every three years. The exhibitions of the 1890s (1893, 1896, and 1899) differed from the earlier ones in two ways. They were roughly the same size as the earlier exhibitions; the little workshops and studios were still dominant, rising from about a hundred in 1893 to about two hundred in 1899: and the trades remained stable at about thirty participating firms. But the little-known individuals who had been the second-largest element in earlier exhibitions disappeared almost completely. This striking change took place between 1890 and 1893. Nothing in the society's minutes explains it, but the organizing committee may have decided to do without the contribution of amateurs. Second, they differed in the character of the exhibits. There was still plenty of pattern and two-dimensional work, but it was joined now by simple, useful, threedimensional objects, brass and copper wares innocent of any decoration, plain oak tables and chairs, work similar to the sideboard by C. F. A. Voysey (2.22). The best-selling object in 1893 was a simple country rocking chair by Ernest Gimson.'6 We can see these changes as a process of clarification, the society becoming more sure of what was Arts and Crafts and what was not. However, complexity remained. The disappearance of the amateurs left the little workshops and the trades as established partners, but they were not equal, and they were not always of one mind. In 1896 Lewis F. Day, whose loyalty to the South Kensington traditions of design reform made him a natural supporter of the trades, tendered his resignation from the organizing committee, which chose the exhibits, saying that "the Committee as a whole did not seriously consider mere ornament (or pattern) as design; [and] that the manufacturer (as distinct from the working ![](images/e663736ab777a72a85d64cd77c4367c9803e71aedebea195e98f9c76e1a41a5b.jpg) craftsman) was not encouraged."?The values he underlines—design as applied ornament and the importance of collaboration with the trades—were the values of design reform, and his letter exactly expresses the tension in the society, and the movement as a whole, between these ideals and those of the little workshops. Crane and Cobden-Sanderson wrote to Day, attempting to dissuade him from resigning, but to no effect. During these decades the movement spread throughout England, Scotland, and Ireland. In the 1880s it had been mainly concentrated in London, but in the first half of the 1890s it expanded into England's large industrial cities. Here it was generally sustained by the local art school, the city's governing elite, or the local trades. In Birmingham, where it was supported by all three, it took such a hold that it was still as strong in the 1920s and 1930s as it had been about 1900. In Manchester the School of Art appointed Walter Crane as director of design in 1893, and in 1896 artistically minded designers and managers from the city's trades set up a Northern Art Workers' Guild, where their discussions had a design-reforming tone.'8 In Liverpool a special School of Architecture and Applied Art was set up at the city's new university in 1894, supported by the civic elite. By the early 19oOs there were probably few English cities whose art school did not have some connection with the Arts and Crafts.'9 It is strange that such a movement should thrive in the midst of industry, but it seems to have answered to a progressive spirit in these cities. A dedicatory plaque in the Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery reads, "By the gains of industry, we promote art." ![](images/ed095ec8622b3b31eb2bd17b47a059c4425ec43ffdeb644546bc6b67cf6af46c.jpg) ![](images/d0ce27fb3c34fe61c33dcff2e832363e9f7f3094b1c7efa910efff499be11752.jpg) In the 1890s Arts and Crafts people began to leave London for the country. The numbers were small, but their stories are some of the most memorable of the Arts and Crafts. In 1893 Ernest Gimson and Sidney and Ernest Barnsley went to live near Cirencester, among the wooded valleys and bare uplands of the south Cotswolds. The furniture and other work that they produced incorporated features from country woodwork, which celebrated the values they sought. The rail at the back of Gimson's sideboard derives from the sides of country wagons (2.23). In the mid-189Os Maud King and Godfrey Blount started textile workshops in Haslemere, Surrey, employing local women. Haslemere was not far from London by train, but this was a radical move. King and Blount believed that God had made the country but capitalism had horribly mismade the town. Essentially, they were waiting for the urban-industrial world to pass away. Ashbee's Guild of Handicraft was very successful in East London during the 189Os, but in 1902 he moved its workshops, about fifty men strong, to Chipping Campden in the north Cotswolds. Ashbee wanted a healthier environment for his workmen, but he perhaps did not think enough about how a complex city workshop might survive in the country (2.24). The early years of the guild in Chipping Campden were almost idyllic, but around 1905 the workshops were hit by declining sales and could not respond adequately. In 1908 the guild went into liquidation. In Edinburgh Phoebe Traquair continued her joyful exploration of new kinds of work, taking up bookbinding in 1893, and enameling in 1901, in which she did her finest work (2.25). Full of enthusiasm for the Arts and Crafts, the young architect Robert Lorimer opened an office in Edinburgh in 1893. His first job was to repair the house and design a new garden at Earlshall, a sixteenth-century tower house in Fife, for R. W. Mackenzie, an eccentric businessman who drank only whisky and loved the old things Lorimer loved (2.27). The house was repaired in the spirit of the Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings; Lorimer designed simple cottage furniture for the kitchen, and Mackenzie added painted plates from the local pottery. Tall and gaunt, Scottish tower houses were often surrounded by walled gardens, and the original walls at Earlshall had survived. Within them, Lorimer created a sequence of outdoor rooms, sheltered from the bitter winds of the sea, a paradise for the mind. Three of the new style of 1890s magazines, which printed photographs alongside text, were particular supporters of the Arts and Crafts movement. The first and most important was The Studio, starting in 1893. This monthly was the principal showcase for British Arts and Crafts for about twenty years and was widely read in Europe and the United States. The second was the monthly Architectural Review, which was edited from 1896 to 1900 by Henry Wilson, one of the most brilliant Arts and Crafts architects and designers. THE QUALITIES OF ARTS AND CRAFTS OBJECTS We have come far enough in the story of the movement to take a general view of the objects that Arts and Crafts people made. The usual way of understanding such things is to look for common visual features and call them a style. But the illustrations of English, Scottish, and Irish work in this book do not show a single Arts and Crafts style. Newcomers to the Arts and Crafts find this difficult to understand. The truth is that it is not surprising, for what mattered in the Arts and Crafts was not what objects looked like but the spirit in which they were made. Arts and Crafts objects were distinguished not by style, but by qualities that correspond to common Arts and Crafts attitudes. Many Arts and Crafts objects express a self-conscious simplicity. They convey a feeling that it was enough that a table or a dish should be just what it was, and that no amount of ornament or embellishment could add to its essential qualities. This was something subtler and deeper than mere visual simplicity or functionalism, more like a love of things. It was expressed in common Arts and Crafts practices: doing without ornament; relying on the texture of materials, the grain of wood, the thickness and bumpiness of handmade paper; and making much of construction, the obvious joints and heavy structures in furniture (2.43). There is no obvious English word for it, though the German word Sachlichkeit comes close, with its sense of what is there, objective, essential. Sache means "thing," and "thinginess" would be an awkward but expressive translation. ![](images/7b02cee674d3ffb2a766026c433c1275c38e94fd726a51e6623d28b006c930ed.jpg) 2.44 Wall hanging, c. 1900. May Morris ![](images/2633853a567ff5153eed15c2101c44010641710681f962ac7183d5d3a8d5e37b.jpg) Alongside this slightly puritanical affection for the essence of things, there was a joyous and instinctive enthusiasm for the decorative arts and their historical traditions. This is a puzzling attitude, for it is almost the opposite of the first. The Arts and Crafts movement was a love affair with the "object-world" of the decorative arts, with the wealth of old work being gathered at the South Kensington Museum, the re-creation of old techniques, and the sheer pleasure of making. Think of Morris and his dyeing and weaving and printing; of his daughter May's embroideries (2.44); of William De Morgan and his luster glazes; of Alexander Fisher and his painted enamels. Think also of the prominence of purely decorative objects, stained-glass windows, embroideries, and jewels (2.45). This aspect of the Arts and Crafts is sometimes overlooked because it did not have any particular intellectual justification and did not provoke any talk of simplicity or design reform. Yet it was a large part of the movement. And it did not need any justification. A profound, creative relationship with the past was a feature of Arts and Crafts work. It is difficult for a modern reader to understand how natural this relationship was, for the fourhundred-year-old European tradition of drawing inspiration from the past was broken in the twentieth century. Now we think it was somehow odd to look to the past, but it was normal then. Think of Edward Johnston and his tenth-century manuscripts, Christopher Whall and medieval glass, Robert Lorimer and his sixteenth-century tower houses. Edwin Lutyens's design for "The Hut" was inspired by the cottage vernacular of Surrey in the sixteenth, seventeenth, and eighteenth centuries, and it evoked all sorts of romantic historical associations with the countryside, peasant life, and Englishness (2.46). There was also a profound, creative relationship to nature. The flowers and trees and birds that fly through M. H. Baillie Scott's interiors and furniture designs are conventionalized in accordance with the decorative scheme, but they are somehow also vivid and real, the world of nature as a sustaining, beautiful place (2.47 and 2.48). This combination of convention and reality is most powerful, as we have seen, in Morris's textiles. Morris wrote, "You may be sure that any decoration is futile...when it does not remind you of something beyond itself, of something of which it is but a visible symbol."²Arts and Crafts ornament was thus meant to carry the mind out to the countryside, just as the historical inspiration of Arts and Crafts work carries the mind back to the past. Nature and the past were the twin dreamworlds of the nineteenth-century Romantic imagination, the opposites of the city and the modern. The exhibition that year took a loss and brought the society into the red. In the postmortem the committee was split between the older members and the not-so-old. The designer and metalworker Harold Stabler, then aged forty-one, led a subcommittee that reported in favor of the society going out into the marketplace and running a salesroom. The president, Walter Crane, and Christopher Whall and W. R. Lethaby, leaders of the movement then in their late fifties and sixties, opposed this, confirming the society's growing sense of craft as a small-workshop activity, isolated from manufacturing and the marketplace. It was impossible, Lethaby said, for the society to run a shop. In the end, nothing happened.2 In 1915 Stabler and others formed a separate society, the Design and Industries Association, to bring the values of the Arts and Crafts movement to bear on industrial production. This was not exactly a parting of the ways; Lethaby became the association's (slightly problematic) father figure. But now the values of the trades had their own voice. The society was left standing for craft, pure and simple. Loss was the keynote of these years. It was not just the trades that fell away. It was also progressive architects, who had been important in the 1880s, but whose enthusiasms were now for the city, not the country, and for steel frames instead of local materials. It was journalists, too, who left. In 1911 the Architectural Review, which had once been a champion of the movement, referred to "the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society, now dead." Walter Crane wrote protesting that the society was alive, but the report was symptomatic.28 For several decades the movement had achieved a presence in the public world of the metropolis, the press, and exhibitions. In the years around 1900 it was the principal progressive movement in British architecture and decorative art. A decade later, it was going out of fashion. It is sometimes said that World War I brought the Arts and Crafts movement to an end, but in a strange way, the war gave it new life. In 1915 Crane died and the presidency of the troubled Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society passed to Henry Wilson, who had a radical vision for the crafts. He taught Arts and Crafts people to see opportunity in the social and educational reconstruction that would follow the war. In 1916 he organized the most remarkable Arts and Crafts exhibition ever: remarkable because it was held at the Royal Academy, the bastion of fine-art privilege; because Wilson's exhibition design gave a meaning and context to Arts and Crafts objects for the first time; and because he reaffirmed the original meaning of the movement (2.49). There was a room devoted to founders' work—Morris, Burne-Jones, Crane—of which Wilson wrote: "We still hold the ideas of a small group of men who formed this society eight and twenty years ago...ideas that are plain and simple and that lie at the root of life cannot grow old or become ridiculous like last year's clothing."9 For many people the horror of war made the English countryside seem heartrendingly beautiful. For Wilson it made the crafts seem all the more essential. The Arts and Crafts continued quietly in the 1920s and 1930s. The public side of the movement had gone, but its heart survived in many little workshops, and its influence was still strong in art schools. Being out of fashion, it now lived more comfortably on the margins. It was easier to survive in the countryside: in the south Cotswolds so many artists and craftspeople had gathered around Gimson and the Barnsleys that the movement was if anything more populous than it had been before the war.3°It was also easier if you had a niche. Public ceremonies and presentations provided one kind of support for the crafts: Edward Johnston's brilliant career as a calligrapher arced over the war and into the 193Os, thanks partly to many public addresses he was asked to inscribe, His postwar work was arguably his finest (2.50). Conservation provided another: Douglas Cockerell found a niche in conservation bookbinding, and his workshop, continued by his son Sandy, closed down as recently as 1987. This part of the story has no ending date: it is not a simple matter to lay the Arts and Crafts movement to rest, It has many endings. Sometimes it died with the people who made it live. Ernest Gimson died in 1919, before he could carry out his plans for a craft village "making in quantity things of common use at prices within the reach of average incomes."Sometimes it carried on, as a lingering tradition. Irene Wellington, one of Johnston's best pupils, taught at the Central School in London in the late 195Os and remembered the day when she realized she could no longer say to the students, "Edward Johnston said..."32 Sometimes it didn't end at all. Where the Arts and Crafts movement flourished, the names of Ruskin and Morris were invoked. Progressive artists' organizations in Europe and the United States exhibited British Arts and Crafts work, starting in Belgium in the early 1890s. Museum directors and curators bought and proudly displayed it. Progressive shops like Siegfried Bing's L'Art Nouveau in Paris, Keller & Reiner in Berlin, and Sub Rosa in Stockholm sold it. The white-painted, mock-modest houses of C. F. A. Voysey and M. H. Baillie Scott were particularly celebrated. On the whole this was not the result of British or Irish efforts at dissemination, though the international circulation of The Studio helped. The other chapters in this book explain these enthusiasms and their part in the development of Arts and Crafts work in different countries. Those chapters also provide a larger view, a place from which we can look back on the movement in the United Kingdom. In this essay I have focused narrowly on the interplay between Ruskinism and design reform, and between the little workshops and the trade firms, because I think it helps to explain the story of the Arts and Crafts in the United Kingdom. If one takes a wider view and compares the movement in Britain with those in other industrializing countries, such as Germany and the United States, or in the countries of Scandinavia and Eastern Europe, which were not yet industrialized, the most obvious characteristic of the British movement seems to be its anti-industrialism. Part of the reason for this might be found in the British class system. The movement was allied to the British upper middle class. It was not just that it found its market among such wealthy people. It was also that some of the core values of the movement, particularly its romantic sense of the countryside and the past and its disdain for the trade, were the values of this class, "gentry values," as they are sometimes called. Widening the view still more, one might argue that the Arts and Crafts movement in the United Kingdom, though it has often and rightly been seen as a prelude to modern movements in architecture and design, was also, in the context of modernity as a whole, something more tentative, even anxious, a pause rather than a prelude, an attempt to find a breathing space for values that seemed threatened by the developing urban-industrial world. Industrialization had created a material culture that was considered cheap, poorly made, tasteless, and above all, to have estranged the workingman from any control over the work process. Voices of rebellion and reform first emerged in Britain. Great Britain was the leading power in the world politically and economically; it served more than any other country as a model for the young German Empire. The writings of British Arts and Crafts theorists, such as John Ruskin and William Morris, as well as knowledge of objects pro duced in the workshops of Morris, C. R. Ashbee, and other British designers, reached Germany and acted as a catalyst in this early phase of the movement, known as Jugendstil (Youth Style, after Munich's avant-garde journal Jugend). As the end of the nineteenth century approached, German design reform was driven by two pressing concerns: to create goods that could compete economically with other major European powers and to establish a greater sense of national identity following the country's unification of 1871. Britain was the country to learn from. Despite the admiration for Great Britain, the basic conditions for an Arts and Crafts culture in Germany differed in many respects. A major premise in Britain—the idealization of the Middle Ages—had little resonance in Germany. And while the English Arts and Crafts movement originated in large cities, such as London, Birmingham, and Manchester, its most profound associations were with nature, country life, and the country house, where "the simple life" was the paramount ideal. The reformist design culture of the era of Wilhelm Il in Germany (1888—1918) was, in contrast, oriented more toward cities; beginning around 1896, reform experiments developed rapidly in regional urban centers throughout the newly unified German state. From the beginning of the Arts and Crafts movement in England, its modern design culture was shaped by architecture and by the interplay of interior and exterior space. In 1859, for example, William Morris began plans for his home, the Red House, which he, the architect Philip Webb. and other friends designed and furnished. Conversely, the German Arts and Crafts movement gained momentum not from working architects but from artists who had formerly focused on painting, so the development of reforms in Germany took a different direction from those in Britain from the very start. Large-scale interior design projects in the modern style first became an artistic issue in Germany toward the end of the century, when the movement for a comprehensive reform of art and life inspired German Arts and Crafts innovators to begin designing and furnishing houses themselves.² The role played by British publications in disseminating Arts and Crafts ideas in Germany can hardly be overestimated. Works such as Walter Crane's Claims of Decorative Art (1892) and The Basis of Design (1898), along with the monthly London journal The Studio (founded in 1893), were widely read and discussed. Although their influence on individual artists has never been investigated in detail, it's clear that many German Arts and Crafts designers relied on these and other British precedents.3 Contacts with the Arts and Crafts movement occurred on many levels of artistic activity, and they were even spurred by government intervention. In 1896 the German government appointed the architect Hermann Muthesius to its embassy in London as attaché for trade and industry, with the specific assignment of reporting on British architecture and decorative arts. During the seven years he spent in London, Muthesius proved to be an astute observer and investigator. He traveled extensively throughout the United Kingdom and established close relationships with many British architects, designers, and writers. Through the numerous reports and articles he published during these years, Muthesius actively promoted the British Arts and Crafts ideal in Germany.4 His studies resulted in the hugely influential three-volume Das englische Haus (The English House, 1904-5), which extolled the great artistic merit of the Arts and Crafts move- ![](images/b0bb3d1c70b16cc9c02a543fc742ff3cc4fa3961192f521e680beb7c2d9cd5f3.jpg) ment. Muthesius's pioneering book was published after he returned to Germany to take up an appointment in the Prussian Ministry of Trade and Industry, with responsibility for applied arts education. Despite the fact that Muthesius drew upon his British experience in initiating major reforms for crafts training in Prussia, he soon became a strong advocate of standardized German design and emphasized the limits of the English workshop as a model for Germany.⁵ British Arts and Crafts designers also became well known in Germany through exhibitions, competitions, and not least through private interior design commissions. Many German designers traveled to Britain and became acquainted firsthand with the work of their British counterparts. For example, the Swiss-born designer Hermann Obrist, who became active in Munich, took a trip through Great Britain in 1887.His embroidery owed much to the ideas and impetus he gained from English models (3.2). One admirer, Karl Schmidt, the founder in 1898 of the Dresdner Werkstätten für Handwerkskunst (Dresden Workshops for Handicraft Arts) had spent a year in England and later commissioned architects such as M. Between 1907 and 1914, inspired by the German translation (1907) of Ebenezer Howard's Garden Cities of Tomorrow (London, 1902), Schmidt created the first ![](images/15b5aa31505346fc38cfd153bfce220d3962e2426c0114150e7b48173842da34.jpg) German Garden City, Hellerau. The Munich-based Swiss architect and designer Hans Eduard von Berlepsch-Valendas, under the influence of his friend the architect Raymond Unwin, also disseminated English aims of artistic and social reform.8 Design reform ideas in Germany did not generate only from an advanced artistic consciousness; they were also promoted as a means of achieving economic supremacy. For example, in 1899 the sovereign ruler of the grand duchy of Hessen und bei Rhein, Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig, established an artists' colony on the Mathildenhöhe in Darmstadt with the hope that this undertaking would encourage production of high-quality decorative and applied arts throughout his state. An Anglophile, Ernst Ludwig had already introduced the Arts and Crafts to Germany in 1897 by commissioning Ashbee and Baillie Scott to design two rooms at his Neues Palais in Darmstadt, which had been commissioned in 1864 by his grandmother Queen Victoria as a wedding present for his parents." Ernst Ludwig's enlightened patronage provided a valuable example to other German states; a few years later, the Prussian Ministry of Trade and Industry hired modern artists to revive the ailing stoneware industries of the Westerwald, and the court of Weimar commissioned Henry Van de Velde, a Belgian designer who became active in Germany, to work with the local craftsmen of Thuringia to reform and modernize their design styles and production practices. Works in the new style not only had to be conceived and executed, they also had to be mediated to the public. Justus Brinckmann, director of the Museum für Kunst und Gewerbe (Museum of Art and Industry) in Hamburg, aimed to shape the taste of German designers and critics by collecting and exhibiting works of art from the past and the present, including the British Arts and Crafts, and encouraging the artists of his day to study these models of excellence. Brinckmann followed the path of Morris, not by actually working as a designer himself but by setting up opportunities for the new style to flourish. Enthusiasm for Britain peaked around the turn of the century. After 1900 the embrace of British models began to be challenged—a result of the growing self-confidence of the German Arts and Crafts scene. According to the judgment of several art critics, Britain belonged to the "German bloc," thus enjoying greater respect than France, but at the same time it was increasingly regarded as an economic and political rival."° Furthermore, the British belief that handwork was the sine qua non of creativity was not accepted with as much conviction in Germany. Whereas handcrafting was a central (if not always realized) ideal for the English reformers, utility and perceived simplicity were the aspects of the British Arts and Crafts ethos that became most important to German designers. The issue of hand or machine—since Ruskin, a topic of constant debate in Britain—was for Germany a pragmatic option in favor of the machine. Nonetheless, this attitude did not develop overnight. In the quest for new product quality, Germany, too, had to first make the breakthrough via handicraft. Analogous to British precedent, numerous workshops and communities that made unique or limited production objects were set up around 1900. The German designers, however, used the model of the workshop selectively—the path they chose quickly led to mass production, albeit filtered with the British ideals of integrity, utility, and high-quality design for everyday life. An accelerated pace of economic growth after unification ensured that Germany achieved in a few years what had evolved in Britain over several decades; German Arts and Crafts proponents even went beyond the original model of artists' guilds by setting up increasing numbers of workshops with highly developed production technology. By 1907 Peter Behrens was appointed the artistic consultant of the giant electricity firm Allgemeine Elektricitäts-Gesellschaft (AEG) in Berlin, and the Deutsche Werkbund was established as an advocacy association for industry, designers, and artisans. The ideal of handmade beauty, luxury, and spiritual edification was giving way to design professionalism and technical invention. A new, industrially motivated model had developed out of the original goal of the unity of design and execution, with the aim that obiects of whatever kind would be well designed, functional, inexpensive, and above all economic assets suitable for export. Although particular aspirations of the Arts and Crafts movement continued to be significant, Germany had sloughed off the British example and was developing its own new Kultur der Dinge (culture of things)." ART AND LIFE The reform movement of the late nineteenth century aimed to change conditions considered obsolete and incapable of meeting the challenges of the age. The leaders of the new movement in Germany—including designers, artists, architects, patrons, critics, and businessmen—wanted not only to transform the appearance of the products they created but also to improve the taste of the entire population. Traditional academic art had proved unsuitable for realizing this aim, and the applied arts were embraced as the medium by which to convey a new, young feeling for life to the people, and to open their senses to the modern world. One attempt to address this goal was made by the Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig, who in 1899 had sponsored the founding of the artists' colony at Darmstadt. Building upon his successful experience with prior commissions of British artists Ashbee and Baillie Scott, as well as the German artist Otto Eckmann, to design interiors for his palace at Darmstadt, Ernst Ludwig envisioned an artists' colony as a prime opportunity to cast a spotlight on the potential of his capital seat as a center of art, culture, and economic progress. To achieve this, the components of his furniture were made by machine, with only the final finishing and decoration done by hand. Antecedent to Vogeler's volkstümliche Möbel (traditional and popular furniture) was Riemerschmid's so-called ![](images/6e3ad420b4ef232d76537305f99d3260fab738c4fa7cab8906e1e35c7859233b.jpg) Maschinenmöbel (machine furniture) (3.39-3.41).4² Conceived as serially manufactured furniture, it was a breakthrough in meeting the demand to combine design quality with an affordable price. For German design, which was increasingly characterized by sobriety and functionalism, it provided concrete proof that the decisive criterion for improving the quality of life was not found in the elimination of the machine, as Ruskin had demanded, but in the democratic maxim of offering good art for all. Many critics had observed that the English Arts and Crafts movement had experienced the problem of expensive handicraft's incompatibility with wide accessibility; German reformers thought they had solved it. Like no other artist of the reform movement, Riemerschmid created an oeuvre that reflected both the crafted, exclusive, luxury furniture of the haute bourgeoisie, as well as the popular, traditional furniture of the less well-off classes. Riemerschmid's experimentation with simpler furniture began in 1900, when he designed a rustic ten-piece living room suite at a minimal manufacturing price (3.42 and 3.43). The decisive factor here was the market-oriented assignment: narrowing the designer's creative scope and forcing him to work with a simple, yet not stringent, concept.“3 In the following years, Riemerschmid's new ideas were disseminated through a collaboration with his brother-in-law Karl Schmidt, the proprietor of the Dresden Werkstätten and soon to become the founder of the Garden City Hellerau. In 1906 Riemerschmid and Schmidt presented ensembles of machine furniture (each with living room and bedroom suites and kitchen) in several price classes: "An all-inclusive program of this type for three social classes, which could be diversely combined and extended, was an impressive innovation in its logical consistency."\* The use of machine-saw compliant wood that could be easily joined with wooden bolts resulted in an attractive price; also, the furniture could be shipped cost-effectively in a disassembled state, with the components first put together in the workshop branches. The German national economist Friedrich Naumann had proposed the idea of German machine-made Volksmöbel (people's furniture) already in 1904; Maschinenmöbel fulfilled this mandate, so that the Deutsche Kunst und Dekoration could speak of it as an "art for the masses."4s Within the space of ten years, Riemerschmid had progressed as a furniture designer through a range of styles—encompassing Gothic case furniture and reform furniture for the upper middle classes—to home design for the less well-off classes, including a logically conceived, practical, and attractive form of people's furniture. He had discarded the motifs of his native Bavaria and instead created a supra-regional style, which in its simplicity was felt to be universally applicable. In Germany the idea of supplying functional home furnishings and appliances, of educating taste and thus shaping a kind of national identity, was something that carried great weight. Providing quality goods for the consumer was valued higher than showing consideration for the human workforce, which Morris had championed. Correspondingly, the Arts and Crafts scene in Germany never really had a problem with multiple or even mass production, which could potentially alienate men and women from their work. ## EINRICHTUNG Nr. Thus Henry Van de Velde was still able to have court silver made in Weimar and Karl Ernst Osthaus could start up a silversmith's workshop in Hagen after the models of the Wiener Werkstätte in Vienna and the Georg Jensen company in Copenhagen, while around the same time Peter Behrens could design high-technology appliances for the AEG (see 3.19).46 The contradiction of these two principles—exemplified by the artistic, unique piece and the industrial, standard product—erupted in a public argument at the Cologne Werkbund conference in 1914, during which the agenda put forward by Hermann Muthesius as the representative of industry and the promoter of commercial interests clashed with the protest of artists such as Endell, Van de Velde, and Walter Gropius. Muthesius and his supporters saw progress as a rational pact between designer and producer. His arguments were rejected, which led to his withdrawal from the Werkbund executive board." Yet in the end, the ideas of industrial design, standardization, and cooperation between producer and artist were called for more than ever after World War I. In comparison, crafted luxury products appeared more problematic for the future; at any rate, they never managed to answer the needs or ameliorate the condition of the masses. ART AND NATIONAL (DENTITY Germany, like many other European countries around 19OO, was searching for its own national style and cultural identity. A look at the art literature of the turn of the century reveals the considerable attention paid to the topic. What is most striking is the ubiquitous assertion that design reform could create "healthy" German art and combat "signs of decay." This seems like the incantation of a magic spell, conjured up in the hope that the inner unity of the nation would be advanced by a unified concept of art. In nationalist circles, the country had always been perceived as a victim of outside influences. Now, leadership and resolution were called for, so that Germany might command its own fate. As a newly founded empire, Germany had to develop a unique culture, one that could match Britain and France. In general, it was thought that French art should be suppressed as un-German, and British art, despite its estimable qualities, should be considered with a more critical eye. There was a noticeable trend from around 1900 of increasing reservations concerning the British model, for it was perceived to be mired in individual artistry and not headed toward a comprehensive reform of life and society. What were the criteria of national identity? The primary goals of late-nineteenth-century reform had already been formulated by Britain: the return to well-made goods, their greater accessibility through designers collaborating with manufacturers, the desire for logical construction, and the use of materials that would reflect the nature of the region where the objects were made. Germany, however, was not striving, in the long run, for beauty and elegance of construction, but for solidity and a certain core quality with which it could express the basic sense of its national identity: "the over-stimulated palate craves for plain brown bread."48 A nationally entrenched culture had to be democratic: the new art had to possess properties to benefit everyone. Since entitlement to equality sailed under the national flag, it did not involve a risk for the ruling classes. It was obvious: Germany had to define itself as a modern nation; only the raising of standards could prepare it for the future. Even the German states that preferred to distance themselves from Prussia and the German imperial house—for example, Hesse under Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig—shared these intentions. The more prominent the claim to equality, the more expansive became the national base upon which a new Germany was to grow. The historicism of Renaissance grotesquerie and Rococo were scorned and replaced by native vegetal decoration. Traditional rustic furniture, jewelry, and dress increasingly became the focus of museum interest, proving—in the opinion of the advocates of folk art and native traditional art—that there was sufficient creative potential in the people; it had been buried by nineteenth-century industrialization and only had to be revived. Activating these forces would trigger the revival of a homogeneous national culture.49 Allusions to fairy tales, folk tales, and sagas-—invented histories of the mystical dawn of time and great heroes of the past—formed an intellectual context for the discussion surrounding the reintroduction of old techniques. During the period around 1900, design reformers sought consolation in the past as a way of strengthening themselves for the trials of the future.50 Some endeavored to protect the ancient rustic and peasant customs, and to breathe new life into whatever had been precipitated by Heimatkunst (native traditional art and architecture); others wished to revive national traditions by means of handicraft, placing crafts such as ceramics and textiles on a "peasant" basis once more. In contrast, the progressive representatives of art and culture—Josef August Lux, Friedrich Naumann, and Muthesius among them—applied the concept of folk art in an entirely different way. They did not associate it with a renaissance of art in the native rustic style but with a practical, reasonably priced, and highquality utility art that would benefit all classes of people. This included the early standardized furniture lines of Bruno Paul and Richard Riemerschmid, as well as the home appliances of Peter Behrens: prototypes without personal gesturing, involving a strong technical element compliant with the work of an engineer.5' Initially Germany addressed the issue of its new functional art in terms of its educational value, with the main emphasis on the recovery of a healthy society and the raising of taste. Later, in a second phase, the German art industry focused more on the challenge of international competition. This meant that the agenda of the Arts and Crafts movement, which originally seryed as an instrument of reform and part of a healing process, was being suppressed more and more by considerations of cultural and mercantile politics. A modern movement that had been formulated by men such as Muthesius, Behrens, and Riemerschmid could have been the source of an enduring national culture, but the idea existed only briefly, until the outbreak of World War I. After the war, the traditionalists took over the helm once more. When the modern movement energetically and rigorously responded in the 1920s with the Bauhaus, the subsequent backlash proved all the more vehement, even state-ordained. The dream of a unified culture embracing all levels of society, which had seemed within the nation's grasp between 1900 and 1914, was never fulfilled. Translated from the German by Abigail Prohaska
