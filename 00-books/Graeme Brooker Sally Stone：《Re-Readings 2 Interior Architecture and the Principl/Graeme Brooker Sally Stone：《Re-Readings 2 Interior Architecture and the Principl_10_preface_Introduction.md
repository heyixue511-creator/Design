## Introduction

One of the greatest concerns for the 21st century population is the search for a sustainable future. There is a growing global awareness of limited resources, expanding ecological footprint, and climate change caused by excessive carbon emissions. The architect and designer can help facilitate this exploration through the remodelling of the existing structures. Building reuse is an extremely sustainable method of development. The environmental movement’s international slogan, Reduce/Reuse/Recycle, is a manifesto for the way to live in the 21st century, and is especially pertinent to interior architecture and the adaptation of structures. The embodied energy in these elements can be saved through upgrade and reuse, and through adaptation the amount of natural resources required to construct a building is greatly reduced. To take this notion somewhat further: the embodied energy in an original building is huge, in that there was a massive amount of energy expended to produce the original materials and construct the building. If this building is just demolished and replaced with something new, and even if the most efficient current, contemporary building is created in its place, it would take up to 30 years in savings from a badly performing to a prime-performing building just to recover the embodied energy within the original materials.

Sustainable use and reuse is a subject that contains two distinct but linked components. A building can be adapted or remodelled using sustainable methods of construction to provide an interior environment that is energy efficient. The designer or architect can also design a place that can be used in a sustainable manner: a place that encourages social interaction, doesn’t waste energy and contains a healthy atmosphere for the end users.

The old post office in Bolzano, Italy was adapted by the architect Michael Tribus in 2006 into the headquarters for the local government Department of Planning and Environment. It is the first administrative building in Italy to be awarded the energy certification of Passive House and Gold Climate House consumption class.

The original building was a somewhat dreary and dirty three storey concrete structure with vertical channels set between the cast panels. It was constructed in 1954, in that slightly panicky period after WWII. The overriding purpose of the remodelling project was to modify the building to create the most energy-efficient office block possible. The whole process was heavily monitored and the behaviour of the resultant building was also carefully measured.

Two floors were added to the structure, thus creating a building five storeys high. A massive 350mm of thermal insulation was applied to the exterior of the walls; this keeps the building warm in the winter and also prevents overheating in the summer. The over-cladding was painted white to stop excessive solar gain. The external window reveals were then carefully angled to optimise the access of the sunlight in the winter and reduce it in the summer. This has created a dramatic angular decorative effect on the exterior of the structure. The building was also provided with a green roof, a heat-recovery system and an active cooling system.

The changes were not just constructional; the interior organisation was modified to make the most effective use of the natural light at the perimeter of the spaces. This was achieved by relocating the circulation and services towards the centre of the

building. Some open-plan office space was introduced to encourage healthy interaction between the users of the building and the enclosed offices were provided with acoustically insulated walls. These significant measures to save energy have reduced the running costs by 90%, thus creating a building that is environmentally, economically and socially sustainable.

It may not be the easiest approach, but it is a very sustainable process to reuse existing buildings. Built environments that have evolved over many years contain an abundance of memory and can often be multidimensional and diverse. Tangible and intangible culture, events and structures can stimulate social cohesion and the feeling of being part of a community. Specific decisions are important to ecological adaptation. The individual and society can consume less, conservation and restraint can be encouraged, thus demand is reduced and efficiency increased.
