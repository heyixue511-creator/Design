He also studied at the Statens Håndverks- og Kunstindustriskole (National College of Art and Design) in Oslo, graduating in 1949. The same year he began working as a designer for Porsarund Porselænsfabrik, a ceramics factory that was attempting to modernize its product line by re-organizing its design department and recruitina a number of young gifted designers. One of Eckhoff's first successes for Porsarund was his well-known Det Riflede (The Fluted One) tableware range designed in 1952. This white porcelain service recalled the soft forms pioneered by earlier Scandinavian designers such as Wilhelm Kåge in the 1930s and reflected Eckhoff's goal “of arriving at a beautiful, simple and functional solution”. He also designed the fireproof Glohane (1955) and the Regent (1962) tableware ranges for Porsgrund, as well as several other utilitarian services intended for institutional use. Eckhoff was Porsgrund's artistic director between 1953 and 1960, and in this position he had an enormous influence on the creative direction of the factory. In 1951 he won two prizes for his design of decorative and utility glassware in a competition held by Hadeland, which revealed his talent and scope for working within the contexts of both artistic and industrial design. Beyond his work in ceramics and glassware, Eckhoff also gained an international reputation for the excellence of his metalware designs, In 1953 his silver Cypress cutlery (1952–1953) won the Inter-Scandinavian Design Competition sponsored by Georg Jensen to celebrate the company's 50th anniversary. This elegant desian led to him winning the coveted Lunning Prize the same vear. Later, Eckhoff designed highly polished stainless steel cutlery for Dansk Knivfabrik (including the Fuga and Opus ranges) and for Norsk Stålpress (now known as Norstaal). His designs for Norsk Stålpress included Maya (1961), Una (1973), Tiki (1974), and Chaco (1990). Eckhoff also designed metalware for Halden Aluminumvarefabrik and architectural hardware, such as door-locks and mounts, for Trio-Ving, Using the same single-piece injection-moulded plastic seat-shell, he designed the Type 350 and Circle ![](images/f4e39dde57cf784a544f971f3c5f36751055fd76281c852043113cf885cf832a.jpg) armchairs (both 1983) for the Oslo-based furniture company Ana-Tomi. Eckhoff's rational and almost scientific approach to design was tempered by an acute artistic sensibility that ensured that his designs were both functional and beautiful. Over his career he won numerous awards, including five gold medals at the Milan Triennale 1954, 1957 and 1960, and three Good Design medals from the Norwegian Design Council (1962, 1965, 1966). As the most prominent Norwegian designer of the modern era, Eckhoff almost single-handedly helped focus international attention on his country's design output – thus ensuring that Norway was not completely overshadowed by Sweden, Denmark and Finland. △ Tias Eckhoff, Regent service for Porsgrund, 1961
