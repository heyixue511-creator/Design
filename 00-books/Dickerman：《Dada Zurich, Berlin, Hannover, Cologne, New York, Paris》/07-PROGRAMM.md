As part of this effort to forge a broader identity, the group staged the “first public Dada evening,"roo as Ball wrote, underscoring the word public, on 14 July 1916 at Zurich's Waag Hall, an old guildhouse on one of the city's main squares [fig. 1.12]. Hennings read poems, Arp offered a discussion of his paper collages; Ball, Huelsenbeck, Janco, and Tzara performed their simultaneous poem; and members of the group in various combinations recited chants nègres and performed costumed and masked dances [fig. 1.13]. The event also marked a movement away from the margins of culture, seen in the shift within the topography of the city from the seedy Niederdorf, where no respectable citizen would go, to a central area where the bourgeoisie was part of the potential audience. ¹or Yet if it was a step toward a more mainstream identity, it also made the dadaist assault on bourgeois pieties all the more direct.

Along with the other offerings of the evening, the core members read manifestos, appropriating a traditional form of public and political communication aimed at establishing principles.1o² But Ball's text turned on the group itself, mocking its ambitions and suggesting a kind of “dada hubris."ro3 "Just a word," declaimed Ball, "and the word a movement."

Very easy to understand....To make of it an artistic tendency must mean that one is anticipating complications. Dada psychology, dada Germany cum indigestion and fog paroxysm, dada literature, dada bourgeoisie, and yourselves, honored poets, who are always writing with words but never writing the word itself, who are always writing around the actual point. Dada world war without end, dada revolution without beginning, dada, you friends and also-poets, esteemed sirs, manufacturers, and evangelists. Dada Tzara, dada Huelsenbeck, dada m'dada, dada m'dada dada mhm, dada dera dada, dada Hue, dada Tza.1º4

A first public event, a first manifesto, but also an ending, for despite its obscurity, the manifesto was intended and received, Ball wrote, as a "thinly disguised break with friends."1o5 Ball soon distanced himself from the group, leaving Zurich for the Ticino in the first of several leave-takings and bringing to a close the period of his greatest intellectual influence.1º6

With Ball's absence and the closing of the cabaret, Tzara emerged as a new leader, converting Ball's persona as cabaret master of ceremonies into a role as a savvy media

spokesman with grand ambitions. Tzara was "the romantic internationalist," wrote Huelsenbeck in his 192o history of Dada, "whose propagandistic zeal we have to thank for the enormous growth of Dada."1o7 As a result, it was largely through the filter of Tzara's efforts and writing that ideas about Dada were communicated to an audience outside of Zurich. While Ball had not articulated a specific theory or program for Dada, Tzara's writings marked a certain shift, distilling concepts that had emerged at the Cabaret into discernible principles capable of being communicated. Departing from the more mystical aspects of Ball's thought with its religious and alchemical imagery, Tzara, as discussed in the introduction to this volume, offered a declaration of resistance to various forms of social government. In his widely distributed “Manifeste Dada 1918,” he wrote, “To impose your ABC is a natural thing, hence deplorable."ro8

## THE GALERIE DADA

In January 1917, in Ball's absence, the group organized the Première Exposition Dada (First Dada Exhibition) at the Galerie Corray [12]. Works by Arp, Janco, Hans Richter, and Adya and Otto van Rees were shown along with tribal pieces, and Tzara offered three lectures on modern art. Returning to Zurich for a few months,109 Ball surely visited the show for he soon joined the other members of the group in founding the Galerie Dada. Hans Corray relinquished the rooms above the Sprüngli confectionery and chocolate emporium, which he used as his gallery space, and the first of three exhibitions, mixing avantgarde work by the members of the expressionist Sturm group and by the dadaists themselves with primitive and children's art, was opened in March.º The new location represented a significant step away from the sordid Niederdorf district, and there were other signs as well of Dada's newly genteel aspect: programs were printed in advance, guest lists made, invitations distributed, and relatively high entry fees charged. A series of ambitious experimental soirées were planned, but on the weekends only, offering a respite from the relentless nightly performances of the Cabaret Voltaire; midweek, visitors were offered tours and, starting in May, afternoon tea. An evening café on the premises was called the Kandinsky Room. Reflecting on this period of Zurich activities from the stridently politicized atmosphere of Berlin of 192o, Huelsenbeck wrote: “As I think back on it now, an art for art's sake mood lay over the Galérie Dada—it was a manicure salon of the fine arts, characterized by tea-drinking old ladies trying to revive their vanishing sexual powers with the help of something mad.""i12 At the time, however, Ball described the Galerie as a “continuation of last year's idea of the cabaret," but with its “barbarisms” overcome."3 He seemed particularly pleased at the integration of “pictures, music, dances, poems” achieved."4 But in addition, the founding of the gallery clearly signaled a new centrality for dance and the visual arts within the group's activities.

## DANCE

At the opening festivities on 29 March 1917, Sophie Taeuber danced in a costume by Arp to Ball's sound poems [see p. 16] and Claire Walter performed a group of “expressionist" dances. The young women's teacher, Rudolf von Laban, one of the pioneers of the modern dance movement, sat in the audience with a fellow student, Mary Wigman. Laban's presence in Zurich, and the network of personal and creative relationships that developed between the members of the Voltaire group and the Laban dancers, fostered a new attention to experimental dance within the Dada fold. Laban's keen interest in primitive ritual and non-narrative forms created grounds for an alliance. Dance found a place within the larger mandate for abstraction—offering the means, prompted by what was seen as the modern debasement of language, to initiate a shift from verbal communication to corporeal expression

Laban suggested that dance provided access to subrational layers of consciousness. Brought up in Bosnia, Laban spoke of his interest in the dances of dervishes seen in his youth“who perform their prayers not in words, but in body movements and especially endless turnings... at first sight, [they are] completely incomprehensible, even repulsive in the wild whirling which goes on till the dancers froth at the mouth. It all seems quite mad to us, but it is probably in the madness that the sense lies."ös Dances at the Galerie Dada simulated this ecstatic state. In a note on Taeuber's performance in the first issue of Dada, Tzara stressed the transgression of norms, describing Taeuber's dance as “delirious strangeness in the spider of the hand vibrating quickly ascending towards a paroxysm of a mockingly capriciously beautiful madness."ı6 To be sure, one finds many paroxysms of various kinds in Zurich Dada—but it is precisely conjuring a state of consciousness outside of quotidian awareness of the world that seemed, as Laban thought, to offer an antidote to the “warped psyche of our time."117

Not surprisingly, Ball was enthusiastic in his response, writing effusively after Taeuber's opening performance:

Abstract dances: a gong beat is enough to stimulate the dancer's body to make the most fantastic movements....The nervous system exhausts all the vibrations of the sound, and perhaps all the

hidden emotion of the gong beater too, and turns them into an image. Here, in this special case, a poetic sequence of sounds was enough to make each of the individual word particles produce the strangest visible effect on the hundred-jointed body of the dancer.8

For Ball, dance offered an important complement to his own experiments with sound poetry. Rhythm, whether produced by percussive instrument or phonic line of sound poetry, served as a form of direct corporeal stimulation—an almost electrical circuit through the nervous system——which would avoid intellectual mediation and forge instead a nonlinguistic emotional conduit of expression.

## TAEUBER AND ARP

Among the most important contributions in the visual arts were the far-reaching innovations in abstraction developed in the work of Hans Arp and his companion Taeuber. The two met in 1915 during Arp's first exhibition in Zurich at the Galerie Tanner, where he showed with Otto and Adya van Rees, and were soon inseparable. Taeuber, the only Swiss citizen within the Zurich Dada group, was a teacher at the Kunstgewerbeschule (School of Applied Arts). For Arp, the encounter initiated a shift from the type of work shown at the Galerie Tanner, which extended the faceted geometries of cubism to new media and a greater abstraction [2, 4, 5], toward more unprecedented forms of work, which often reflected Taeuber's training and influence. (Arp wrote that in 1915, Taeuber “had already freed herself from traditional art."119) Between 1916 and 1918, the couple developed a broad creative partnership, collaborating on paintings, collages, graphic work, and embroideries. Arp described their common goal as that of breaking with aesthetics” to produce work that would have more of the status of ordinary objects.

As part of this pursuit, Taeuber and Arp challenged the hierarchy between “applied” and “fine" art, often mobilizing the techniques and media of decorative arts in a deliberate shift toward a “minor" key.° For her part, Taeuber continued to devote equal time to both, teaching at the design school as well as working alongside Arp; and she made pillowcases and purses as well as paintings. Both she and Arp deliberately obscured the distinction, creating embroideries that were framed and hung on the walls like paintings [17-20].1² Arp defined their use of a medium with both decorative and feminine associations as a critical strategy, a deliberate resistance to aesthetic hubris: “Sophie Taeuber and I decided to completely renounce the use of

oil colors in our compositions. We wanted to avoid any reminiscence of canvas painting, which we regarded as characteristic of a pretentious and conceited world."2 What Arp described was a kind of programmatic modesty, and this rhetoric of humility permeates his writing.

Taeuber offered a similar challenge to expectations in a highly idiosyncratic group of sculptural works, dating from 1916 to 1918: Chalice, Amphora, Poudrier, and Dada Bowl [27-30]. Made of turned wood, a decorative arts medium, and evoking functional objects through their titles, they nonetheless stood as independent sculpture in a quiet transgression of boundaries.23 Yet if abstraction in painting was still new, the degree of abstraction seen in these sculptural objects was almost unprecedented.124 And extremely rare as well was the use of strict symmetry, here determined by the mode of fabrication. Symmetry is an idea foreign to traditional composition in the fine arts, which depends on the idea of a balance of component elements orchestrated through the subjective judgment of the artist: sculptural contrapposto—the asymmetrical positioning of the figure to suggest movement—stands as a case in point. (Arp, too, experimented with symmetry in a body of woodcuts, which appeared in Huelsenbeck's Phantastische Gebete [Fantastic Prayers] [see p. 426] and in Dada 1.) On both counts— three-dimensional abstraction and symmetry—the decorative or applied provided a radical logic for the fine arts.

A long tradition of abstraction existed within the category of the decorative, but it was precisely this transposition of strategies from one sphere to another that had far-reaching implications. Nowhere is this more in evidence than in the couple's preoccupation with the form of the grid during the Dada years from 1916 to 1918. Arp makes clear that the impetus for this work came from Taeuber herself, writing: "In 1915, Sophie Taeuber's extreme simplifications. her exclusive use of rectangular planes placed horizontally and vertically in the pictures she made at the time, exercised a decisive influence on my work."125 Taeuber's own interest in the grid as a strategy likely came from her arts and crafts training, where the use of all-over patterning was common, and the warp and weft of textiles defined a grid structure But as an idea about a picture, the same gesture was something very new.

In 1918, the pair produced in collaboration a group of "duo-collages" [24], which stand among the first rigorous grids in the history of twentieth-century art, preceding by several months even Piet Mondrian's experiment with the form.26 Arp's description of the process of making the duo-collages stresses the artists' pursuit of a mechanical impersonality. Seeking to create an “anonymous and collective art," the pair jettisoned the use of scissors for the way in which “they all too readily betrayed the life of the hand," and used a paper cutter instead.127 The grid itself further minimized the subjective choice of the artist, creating instead a model of the artist as a restricted actor within a variable system—a counterweight to the valorization of judgment of which the dadaists were so skeptical.

Yet if the grid's structure offered one form of limit on an artist's judgment, chance provided another. Beginning in 1916 or 1917, Arp introduced fortuitousness into the creation of a group of collages [21–23]. Both Huelsenbeck and Richter state that Arp made these works by tearing paper into pieces, which he dropped to the floor and pasted into place where they fell,128 though the delicate balance seen in the finished works suggests that Arp exerted much greater control.129 Nonetheless, these works introduce visual signs of disorder into the orderly system of the grid in the suggestions of random dispersal, the soft jagged edges of torn paper [21], and wobbling structures [22]. Arp sets these noncompositional strategies against each other, using chance as a way of introducing irregularity into the systematic structure of the grid, without fully conceding to the artist's subjective discernment. For Arp, the random and accidental offered a way of letting go of conscious control and a point of access to the subrational. "The law of chance,"” he later wrote, “which comprises all other laws and surpasses our understanding...can be experienced only in a total surrender to the unconscious."13º

In 1917, there is a dramatic shift in Arp's work first seen in ten woodcuts he made for Tzara's Vingt-cinq Poèmes (Twenty-Five Poems) [8]. Moving away from the systematic structure of the grid, the free-form shapes in these images have a certain organic quality, suggesting indeterminate natural forms fixed in a moment of flux. The change seems to have occurred in Ascona, where Arp, Ball, and Janco all went frequently to visit Arthur Segal, Viking Eggeling, Laban, and other colleagues, and was inspired by Arp's encounter with the natural environment there: "In this same year [1917] I turned away from symmetrical woodcuts and embroideries....At Ascona I drew—with brush and ink— broken-off branches, roots, grasses, stones which the lake had thrown up on the shore. I simplified these forms and united their essences in moving ovals, symbols of the eternal metamorphosis and change of bodies."131 Two large ink drawings closely corresponding to this description are

Arp's free-form drawings provided a foundation for what would become a key genre in his career—the abstract wooden relief [34-39, 50–52]. The reliefs were probably initially seen at the Kunstsalon Wolfsberg in September 1918 [fig. 1.14]. In these works, Arp distanced the making of objects not only from his conscious control, but also from his hand. A close friend of the artist related that Arp generated forms from automatic drawings. A carpenter then cut out the shapes with a band saw, and Arp assembled and painted them.3² Each was constructed of three or four layers of wood, and up to seven colors applied in commercial paints. The insistent roughness of these early reliefs—seen in the visible screws that held the works together, their rough sawed edges, and the flat, unmodulated application of paint in a commercial palette—all spoke to Arp's deliberate disregard for technical virtuosity, which would elevate the status of the thing made. Plant Hammer [39], in which amoebalike forms are attached to a painted board, was one of the first reliefs. The backing surface here maintains some of the function of the traditional frame, isolating

the space of artwork and alluding to the metaphor of a view through a window. Yet several of the curvilinear relief forms cross the boundary established by the backboard, suggesting a new permeability between the space of the artwork and the viewer's own. Arp soon dispensed with the backing plane, hanging the reliefs directly against the wall and creating a new hybrid form, which itself transgressed the boundaries between media. Neither painting nor sculpture, they seemed to exult in their status in between.

Such unboundedness is reiterated in the formal character of both the reliefs and automatic drawings—in the lack of ruled lines, and layers spilling over each other. Arp felt the organic provided a new model, which allowed him to replace the pretensions of logic with the productive nonlogic of natural growth, and absolutes with flux, writing: “Dada aimed to destroy the reasonable deceptions of man and recover the natural and unreasonable order. Dada wanted to replace the logical nonsense of the men of today by the illogically senseless.... Dada is for the senseless, which does not mean nonsense. Dada is senseless like nature. Dada is for nature and against art."33 But nature also helped him to articulate a new conception of form in his work that resisted gestalt.34 Warping, buckling, tending toward a loss of boundaries, it offered a counter idiom to the perniciousness of reason.

## THE MASK REDUX

Masks continued be an important source of inspiration in the period after the close of the Cabaret, and for several members of the group, they offered a specific challenge to traditional notions of portraiture based on physical resemblance. Between 1918 and 192o, following the creation of her marionettes, Taeuber made a group of five “dada heads" from turned and painted wood (four are extant [43–45, 47]). With heads sitting on round pedestals, they evoked the genre of the portrait bust——and several were later named “Portrait of Arp." Characteristically deploying the media of applied arts in a critical manner, Taeuber undercut any pretense of high seriousness by flirting with function—Arp is said to have joked that they could always be used as hat stands.35 And instead of resemblance, the heads offered playful abstraction, with facial designs reflecting the influence of oceanic or northwest Indian masks, in a witty refusal to locate individual identity in exterior physiognomy. (The possibility of an even more fully abstract rendering is proposed in a related painting in which the design for one head is transcribed as a projection on a flat plane [48].) A portrait Taeuber arranged to have taken by the studio photographer Nic[olas or Nikolai] Aluf in 1926 [46] suggests how the masklike Dada heads offered commentary on the contemporary. In the photograph, Taeuber's face, wearing a chic hat and veil, appears behind a wooden Dada head lettered with the inscription “Dada 192o." The juxtaposition sets up a critical counterpoint between the civilized façade of fashion rendered photographically and primitivizing abstraction—one a mask of external appearances, the other seen to be more revealing of internal truths.

Hans Richter, who joined the group in late 1916 after sustaining serious injuries on the front, linked the mask and access to the unconscious in a group of paintings shown at the Kunstsalon Wolfsberg [40, 41] in 1918, along with Arp's and Janco's reliefs. Called "visionary portraits," they were executed quickly—at a pace of three or four a day—and at twilight "when the colors on [his] palette were almost indistinguishable." Richter claimed that the darkness forced a retreat from the visible world to an internal one and that the process of painting itself took on the character of “a sort of self-hypnosis."i36 Presented as depictions of Dada comrades, the results were gestural to the point of near unintelligibility, offering an assault on defined form. Yet, what was discernible resembled tribal masks and seemed to reveal the underlying primitive identity of the portrait subject.

## DADA INTERNATIONAL

Definitive changes occurred within the group between late 1917 and what might be seen as the demise of Zurich Dada around the end of 1919. The ending of the war allowed for the increased possibility of foreign travel, facilitating greater contact with the world outside of Zurich and offering the first real opening for an international expansion of Dada as a movement. The arrival in Zurich of two key figures, French-Spanish artist Francis Picabia and the German critic Walter Serner [fig. 1.15], was influential in these late stage developments, introducing a new cynically subversive tone.

Having spent much of the war in New York working closely with Marcel Duchamp and members of the Stieglitz circle, Picabia left for Europe in 1918, staying in Barcelona and Paris, all the while publishing his journal 391 [56, 283, 284], which had already become an international avantgarde forum. Both the journal and his own itinerancy made Picabia an important conduit for ideas. In 1g18 he sought treatment for neurasthenia (and perhaps for alcoholism) in Switzerland, first at Gstaad, then in the spa town of Bex-les-Bains, where he received a letter from Tzara inviting participation in Zurich Dada, copies of Dada 3 [13], and books of poetry as gifts.137 The exchange seems to have prompted Picabia and his wife Gabrielle Buffet to visit Zurich for three

One of the clock images was featured on the cover of Dada 4–5 [54], the last issue to be produced in Zurich. The inside pages offered another drawing by Picabia titled “Mouvement Dada"—a diagrammatic rendering of the avant-garde network linking Zurich, New York, and Paris in which the movement of ideas was presented as a circuit of machinic forces. Already experienced in publishing a journal, Picabia collaborated with Tzara in editing the issue, and it featured a truly international roster of contributors including, along with Tzara and Picabia themselves, Jean Cocteau, Pierre Reverdy, Raoul Hausmann, Huelsenbeck, André Breton, Serner, Eggeling, and Richter. While in Zurich, Picabia also edited the eighth number of 391, which featured the activities of the Zurich Dada group [56].

In their enthusiasm, the dadaists arranged to have Picabia show his mechanomorphic paintings alongside Arp's and Janco's reliefs and Richter's visionary portraits at the Kunstsalon Wolfsberg to be held in September 1918. The radical nature of these works, while strikingly different from anything produced in Zurich, nonetheless confirmed the dadaists' sense of kinship with the itinerant artist: Picabia used technical drawing and diagrams to create machine images, which presented themselves, through their titles and the suggestion of mindless, repetitive motion, as analogies for modern anomie. The director of the gallery, however, was less enthusiastic and returned the paintings to Picabia before the opening. Tzara, embarrassed at the limits to Zurich's avant-garde sensibility, wrote to Picabia seeking to assuage him: “The Director of the Salon is an abominable and pretentious insect whose impudence and lack of talent have been etched like vermin on his feeble intellectual capacity."139 This failed episode aside, Picabia's stay in Zurich forged a first point of contact between the Dada group there and the avant-garde in New York, Paris, and Barcelona.

Serner, who had considerable intellectual authority in German-speaking cultural circles, had fled Berlin for Zurich in 1915 after having abused his professional credentials— he was a doctor of law—to forge a medical certificate for expressionist Franz Jung after the latter had deserted the front. Mingling within the same circles of displaced artists and writers who populated Zurich's cafés, Serner had at first kept a studied distance from the group around the Cabaret Voltaire. In Sirius, the cultural and political journal he founded with Christian Schad while in Zurich, Serner launched a scathing critique of Ball's and Huelsenbeck's proto-dada Berlin performances of 1915, deriding them for “blasphemy against the intellect... and its words."4º By 1918, however, he had abandoned his defense of rationality Together with Arp and Tzara, Serner began collaborating on several poems under the group title “Société Anonyme pour l’exploitation du vocabulaire dadaiste" (The Exploitation of Dadaist Language Company) and produced a fake press release about a duel between Tzara and Arp—the latter marking the invention of a key dadaist genre, which implicitly signaled the group's growing interest in both exploiting and intervening in a new media culture. In dozens of skillfully placed fake newspaper articles, Serner pursued a media pranksterism that undermined the objectivity and veracity on which modern journalism purported to be based.

On 9 April 1919, Serner joined Tzara and Richter— all that remained of the earlier Zurich group—on the stage at the soirée held at Kaufleuten Hall [see fig. 1.11], the final large-scale Dada event in Zurich. According to Richter's account, the audience, eager to be shocked, found the opening events—a lecture by Eggeling on abstract art, dances by Suzanne Perrottet in a mask by Janco, poems written by Huelsenbeck and Kandinsky—somewhat disappointing in this regard and watched with polite amusement. There was some improvement with the performance by twenty people of a simultaneous poem by Tzara and the “tunes or anti-tunes” of Hans Heusser, but Serner finally provided the provocation sought. He came onto the stage dressed in a morning suit, carrying a headless tailoring dummy, to which he offered a bouquet of flowers. He brought a chair, sat astride it with his back facing the audience and began to read his manifesto "Letzte Lockerung" (Final Dissolution). Now truly shocked, the audience verged on rioting, pelting Serner, who calmly continued reading, with insults and trash.14 The published version of this text takes the combustible dadaist manifesto genre to a new extreme, characterized by a mixture of bitter irony, obscenity, and crude aggressiveness, with more than a hint of violence. Serner's lines transform earlier dadaism's ethical despair into a new note of overarching cynicism:

Everything is a confidence game ("You see EVERYTHING is rastaquouèresque, my dear friends"); everything equally ridiculous (“That everyone falls metaphorically on his ass. And sneezes"), and the pretensions of privilege must be debunked (“The pancakes, the Bible sayings, the girls' bosoms, the percentages, the Gauguins, the snot-rags, the stocking garters, the spirits, the lavatory seats, the waistcoats, the bugs—all the stuff that simultaneously they do, think of and pore over—must be shoved, one after another, so sharply in front of the curve of their chins until finally their feeling of well-being is as great as the mere feeling of wooziness they had experienced until then.")142

Labeled “a breviary for a confidence man” in its first publication, the manifesto introduces a new modern type, restless in his search for stimulation, who gains notoriety for his public acts, but whose motives are inscrutable. A critical notice, which appeared shortly after its first reading, spewed: "Messrs. the dadaists, who were never more unnecessary than presently, should confine their manifestoes to their numerous magazines... and not burden the public with them." But this protector of the public's sensibility was none other than Serner himself.143

In accordance with Tzara's ambitions for a broadbased movement, Serner attempted to found a branch of Dada in the fiercely anti-German city of Geneva, organizing there the First Dadaist World Congress in late 1919 and the Grand Dada Ball in 192o [57]. Both efforts proved financially disastrous. In hindsight, the most productive legacy of Dada in Geneva may lie in the support Serner provided for the work of Christian Schad, who seemed to have understood Dada as a committed battle against dogma in artistic practice; the two may have had a romantic alliance.44 Schad had shown paintings and woodcuts of a mystical expressionist bent at the Zurich Kunsthalle in October 1915, but in Geneva he began producing work that offered a nuanced reading of Arp's recent efforts. Schad created abstract wood reliefs—the Alsatian artist's signature genre—which developed on the principle of creating an object neither rarified through materials nor trained artistic technique by incorporating components associated with machine technology heavy enamel commercial paints, mass-produced metal hardware, and bits of metallic paper [58-60]. Schad's strategy of laying these elements on the surface seems to have led to another development more wholly his own. Reclaiming the photogram, a technique known since the discovery of photography but hitherto unused for artistic purposes, Schad created what are among the first intentionally abstract photographs [61–65]. (Tzara, who took a group of these images to Paris, most likely showing them to Man Ray, later dubbed them Schadographs to distinguish them from Man Ray's photograms called Rayographs.)145 Schad covered the light-sensitive surfaces of photographic paper with tiny compositions of the most quotidian detritus of modern life— scraps of fabric, lace, and rubber; printed paper and even balls of dust—and left them to develop on his basement windowsill, before bathing them in fixative. Schad recalled: My penchant for everything lying around on the streets, in shop displays, in cafés, even in waste bins was at its height."146 The resulting images took the new genre of collage a step further, automatizing the technique to create a trace of direct contact—a collage, that is, without the glue. Schad delighted in the way that the process, which minimized the artist's subjective investment and which, in the unpredictability of the result, allowed for a certain amount of chance, offered a challenge to traditional composition, producing instead images of a “lawless simplicity."147 Serner commented with satisfaction on the “dawning of technology in art."148 On many of these images, Schad cut a jagged border “to free them," he said, "from the convention of the square."149 Resembling the asymmetrical forms of his reliefs, these borders draw attention to the close relation between the two types of work, which combine technological elements, found materials, and abstraction in the first important reading of Zurich innovations.

The Kaufleuten Hall soirée stood as Zurich Dada's last hurrah. A quiet distance had been growing between the writers and media spokesmen Serner and Tzara, on the one hand, and the artists Arp, Janco, and Richter on the other. Soon afterward efforts at reformulation were seen, which codified the group's dissolution. Arp, Janco, and Richter founded the short-lived Bund Radikaler Künstler (Alliance of Revolutionary Artists), which was committed to abstraction as an expression of socialist political ideals in solidarity with the recent rebellions in Munich and Budapest, and later the less overtly political exhibiting society Neues Leben (New Life). By the end of r92o, Huelsenbeck had left for Berlin, Tzara for Paris, and Richter to his parents' estate at Klein-Kölzig with the filmmaker Eggeling. Ball was in his village, and only Arp and Janco remained in Zurich.
