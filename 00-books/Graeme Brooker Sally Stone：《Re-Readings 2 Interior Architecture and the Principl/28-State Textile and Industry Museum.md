There are many parallels and similarities to be drawn on the relationships between textile design, fashion and the construction of interior architectural space. Gottfried Semper, in the Four Elements of Architecture, considered the close relationship between fabric and weaving and the construction and making of

enclosures with textiles. His writing has ensured an enduring relationship and interest for designers in exploring connections between body, space and dress. The design of a textile museum offers a unique opportunity to examine once again the relationships between making spaces for the display of garments, textiles and pattern, and the construction of architectural elements.

The Augsburg State Textile and Industry Museum (TIM) is situated in the Augsburger Kammgarnspinnerei, a Worsted-spinning mill. Worsted is a particular woollen yarn, which this mill specialised in making. Friedrich Merz founded it in 1836. It was the fi rst major industrial company in the city, and it

employed up to 2,000 workers. It was the largest Worsted-spinning mill in the Federal Republic of Germany until it closed in 2004. The Austrian architect Klaus Kada restored the museum in order to present the history of the Bavarian textile industry. Atelier Brückner were commissioned to design a 2500 square metre exhibition dedicated to the textile world of fabrics, colours and patterns.

This exhibition space was organised into three distinct zones: pattern books, histories and processes. The core of the museum’s inventory was the huge collection of pattern books of the New Augsburg Cotton Factory. This rich and unique archive, which comprised over one million patterns from three centuries, was presented as the core spatial link between all zones of the display.

The designers achieved this by displaying the pattern books in cases, but they brought them to life by utilising interactive digital technologies. In the central zone of the exhibition, staged like a catwalk, were three large mannequins. The over-scaled sculpture-like qualities of the fi gures adopt the classic silhouette shape of the pattern-making and sampling objects. In this zone the archive of pattern books was brought to life by a series of projections onto the three larger-than-life fi gurines. The so-called ‘Three Graces’, named after the mythical classical statues, were connected to digital screens that allowed the visitors to select patterns from the books and then have them projected onto the mannequins. The infi nite richness of the patterns became interactively accessible and animated the central area of the display. The light show was reminiscent of a catwalk of giant models,

The narratives of the production processes and the history of the development of the textiles were exemplifi ed with two parallel routes of exhibition, both surrounding the core of the central zone. The production process examined the journey of the material as it begins to be manufactured from the raw fi bres, to the yarn, and then to the fi nalised woven or knitted fabric. This journey was articulated through the exhibiting of the historic machine park.

This incorporated a collection of machinery used to produce textiles. Guided tours through the park showed people through the space and exemplifi ed how the material was refi ned and then made into textiles. Finishing processes, and the handmade area of the exhibition, brought to the textile-making process through the incredible sound of the working looms and machines. This made it a comprehensible as well as an exciting and visceral experience.

The second storyline runs parallel to the production processes, and consists of a series of rooms displaying the history of textile production. The visitors experience the development of the beginnings of the weaving trade in this room, with the last room showing future textiles and smart fabrics. The visitors can explore and test the latest ideas in textiles for themselves in a hands-on exhibition environment. When combined together all three aspects of the museum bring to life the designed textile object.

## Light

## Introduction

When articulated and deployed thoughtfully, both natural and artifi cial sources of light have the potential to enrich and transform interior space.

The power of light lies partly in its abilities to sculpt, shade and foreground space, poetically realising the abstract properties of an interior whilst communicating its numinous qualities and dimensions to its users. The symbolism of light, and its magical dimensions, can be manipulated to instil supernatural sensations in onlookers. Light also embodies authority, and through the sciences of the understanding and application of lumens and lux values, a detailed and structured framework of hard data and facts will decisively relay how a space for particular uses will need to be illuminated. Whichever way light may be tactically used, and quite often it may well be in both of the ways that have been outlined – atmospherically and scientifically – it is fair to say that without light, space cannot be visualised. It is a design material that has the ability to significantly enhance and suppress mood and emotion.

As well as a mood enhancer, light will be deployed in order to illuminate and animate space. The main stair of the Casa Batllo, by Antonio Gaudi, was designed to be an expression of organic sinuous curves, fashioned from wood, steel and mosaic tile. Linking the living spaces, the stair gently curved and slithered between the floors, as though a snake. As it rose between the levels of the building, the wooden balustrade was expressed as a twirling ribbon, exploding into a vertical twist of thin steel, crowned with a glowing glass crystal ball neatly encapsulated in the winding taper.

Figure 3.50 Casa Batllo. Barcelona, Spain. Antoni Gaudi.

The dynamic energy of the stair was animated with a wash of natural light, delivered from above via two large sculptural lozenge-shaped lead-glassed skylights. Natural light was used to enhance and bring to life the dynamism of the stair, as well as illuminating the way through the space, as the occupants and their guests moved vertically through the building.

The effects of light, its articulation and deployment, will give form and meaning to a space. Its careful material expression, through a light fitting of some kind, or through a very focused attenuated source of illumination, can provide a highlight and a focus for any kind of interior. From the outside of a building an illuminated interior can appear inviting and atmospheric. In the startlingly inserted addition to the Elsa Morante Public Library in Varese, Italy, by DAP studio, both existing openings and the new windows in the addition, were illuminated so that at night they would provide a welcoming view to the people of the village. The powerful dialectic between old and new is echoed in the uncompromisingly contemporary addition to the building. At night subtle lighting softens this stark distinction and whilst still ensuring that the new element is different from the old, the interior lighting unites the inside spaces of the new library.

The dialectic between old and new can be expressed in light through an effect akin to chiaroscuro, or the treatment of light in painting and drawing, often utilised to dramatic effect. This can be exemplified through the manipulation of the contrast between shade and light:

The beauty of the Japanese room depends on a variation of shadows, heavy shadows against light shadows. – Junichiro Tanizaki, In Praise of Shadows. <sup>29</sup>

The 17th century Katsura Imperial Villa, outside Kyoto in Japan, is often considered to be a precursor of Modernist spatial tendencies. Its harmonious balance of landscape and buildings, site, view, composition, proportion and order, offer an epic spatial and time-based experience. The Shokin-tei Pavilion, the fi rst of four shelters on the path through the garden, was designed for a tea ceremony. Access was via a stone bridge where guests could wash their hands in the water of the pond before the ceremony commenced. Typical Japanese trees inspired each of the tea pavilion names, and ‘Shokin-tei’ evoked the pine that surrounded it. The interior of the pavilion was arranged around three tatami, with a combination of screens and walls that could be slid back and forth, both to modify the views of the garden and to accentuate the light and shadows of the interior spaces during the tea ceremonies which took place throughout the day and also at night.
