In light of the concrete problems of the relationships between architectonic form and its treatment incolor thisprogram remained strangely undefined and remote from the discussions of the day, for example, in the de Stijl movement or in the circle around Bruno Taut. Kandinsky's real pedagogical effect at the Bauhaus was surely not through the workshop for wall painting but in the required course that was initially offered for the first semester and later in part for the second and then at the end of the twenties extended to the fourth semester.4⁶ The goals, content, andmethodology of this course are discussed in several written sources by Kandinsky himself, and these are the primary basis for the discussionthat follows: in addition to the essays “The Basic Elements of Form,"47 "Color Course and Seminar,"48 “The Value of Theoretical Instruction in Painting,"49 “Art Pedagogy,"50 and "Analysis of the Primary Elements of Painting,"51 there is his book Point and Line to Plane (1926), and the manuscripts compiled from the course that were published in 1978 under the title Cours du Bauhaus.

The distiliation of Kandinsky's teaching at the Bauhausis, without a doubt, the compendium Point and Line to Plane (even if problems with color are treated only in passing), and an analysis of the notes andpractical exercises of the students found in the Bauhaus-Archiv in Berlin confirms the view of Max Bill that “the materiallaid out in Point and Line to Plane [formed] more or less the basis of his teaching, both in the basic course at the Bauhaus and in the 'free painting classes' he taught, a kind of master studio for free painting."52 (The establishment of such“free painting classes" by Kandinsky and Klee in the late twenties, which—institutionally isolated from the energetically encouragedproductive work of the Bauhaus—indulged in a remote ivory tower existence, is in Kröll's view one of the main indicators of the "phase of disintegration," as he characterized the late years of the Bauhaus.)53

Another important source that supplements Point and Line to Plane is Cours du Bauhaus, the work basedon handwritten and typednotes by Kandinsky and translated into French by former Bauhaus students Suzanne and Jean Leppien: its value, however, is diminishedbecause the frequently abbreviated notes are neither chronologically nor thematically arranged.54

## Instruction

Kandinsky's instruction for the preliminary course was dividedinto two main sections: an introduction to abstract elements of form and the course “analytic drawing."55 His teaching method—in keeping with the dualistic approach sketched above—was based on the inseparable relation of analysis and synthesis—"no'either-or,'but’and'"56 —whereby the analysis was understood not as a goal unto itself but as “a means to synthesis."57 We can illustrate this with a number of statements from various essays by the artist:

The method applied to these tasks must be both analytical and synthetic. These two methods are closely bound up with one another, and the second is unthinkable without the first.58

Thus, every individual form of study has before it two important tasks:

1. The analysis of given phenomena, which must as far as possible be regarded in isolation from other phenomena, and

2. The relation of those phenomena which have just been analyzed in isolation to one another— synthetic method.

The first should be as narrow and as limited as possible, the second as broad and as unfettered.59

The main aim of all teaching should be to develop the capacity for thought in two simultaneous directions:

1. the analytical and

2. the synthetic.60

Clearly drawn into the whirlpool of the increasingly rationalistic and scientistic basic orientation at the Bauhaus, Kandinsky spoke out in 1926—the year of his sixtieth birthday, of the publication of Point and Line to Plane, and of the completion of the new Bauhaus building—especially pointedly in favor of the analytic method:

Until now, no planned analytical thought has in fact existed in questions of art, and to be able to think analytically means to be able to think logically. . . . Even “pure" art needs today a more precise, consistent scientific basis. The one-sided emphasis upon the intuitive element, and the "aimlessness" of the art associated with it have frequently led young artists . . . to ungainly conclusions that distract one from art. "Neue Sachlichkeit" [the new sobriety] would suffice as an example from the present day, trying to give art “political" aims. . . .

The young artist, especially when starting out, must from the outset accustom himself to objective, i.e., to scientific thought. . . . By absorbinghimself in those elements that are the buildingblocks ofart, the student acquires—apart from the capacity for logical thought—the necessary inner feeling for artistic resources. . . .61

In this text Kandinsky attributed an integral pedagogical function to the instruction in painting at the Bauhaus: painting shouldnot be understood as l’art pour l’art, asa specialized, marginalized subcategory (like the free painting class of 1928 on) but as a“co-organizatory force" that enables students to break the boundaries of genre and to create an aesthetic synthesis. The statement that "at the Bauhaus, painting should be the most suitable educatress" because it“has, for several decades, advanced at the forefront of all other art movements and has fertilizedthe other arts—especially architecture"62 where evidence that it was Kandinsky the painter speaking, offeringan interpretation that consciouslycontradictedthe view of the primacy of architecture advocated by Gropius in keeping with his own call for universal validity.

It is interesting to see that Kandinsky's sympathy for the analytic principles of the Bauhaus period was accompanied neither by unqualified positivism nor by an elimination of the intuitive components. Although Kandinsky had stated in Point and Line to Plane (in contrast to his convictions during his early years in Munich) that composition is "nothing other than the logically precise organization of those living forces encapsulated within the elements in the guise of tensions"63 and the style of his paintings had clearly transformed from the dramatic and explosive paintings of the prewar years to the cool, precise, geometrically constructed compositions of the twenties, he nevertheless soon realizedthat a composition could never be purely rational, since it could never manage without the corrective of intuition.64 He drew the same conclusion for art pedagogy: "Great periods in art, however, had always had their 'doctrine' or 'theory' whose necessity is just as self-evident as was and is the case in science. These 'doctrines' could never oust the intuitive element, because knowledge is barren in itself. It must content itself with the task of passing on materials and methods. Fertile is intuition, which employs these materials and these methods as means to an end. The end cannot, however, be attained without the means, and in this sense intuition might likewise be considered infertile."65 The same issue of bauhaus in which that essay originally appeared also contained—certainly not by coincidence but rather from a shared concern with the threat to the original spiritual quality at the Bauhaus that was posed by an increasing orientation toward rational, goal-oriented activity— Paul Klee's now famous remark “We construct and keep on constructing, yet intuition is still a good thing."66

In psychology intuition is a form of problem solving characterized by solutions that occur to someone suddenly and without special effort.67 This stands in marked contrast to an approach to solutions that proceeds "step by step ... without any noteworthy tinge of emotion."68In “Reminiscences" (1913) Kandinsky described in passing the intuitive creative act that all forms came "of their own accord": "presenting themselves fully fledged before my eyes, so that I had only to copy them, or else constituting themselves actually in the course of work, often to my own surprise."69 In Paul Plaut's book Die Psychologie der produktiven Persönlichkeit (1929), he spoke of "so-called 'spiritual' experiences" that poured out of him as ideas that "sometimes 'appear to one' so rapidly and continuously that one has no time to fasten on to them, even to a limited extent. Even this expresses itself in different ways: sometimes one sees only fragments, isolated juxtapositions, pictorial, linear 'sounds,'sometimes the whole picture, which only has to be held fast, i.e., must be given material form."70 This description is Kandinsky's response to his critics, who had accused him of "working too 'consciously,' too much, or too exclusively with [his] 'head'"71 in his boldly tectonic compositions of the twenties, that is, that they lacked intuition or that it played too small a role.

Summing up our discussions so far, we are led to the conclusion that the dualism of the analytic and the synthetic principles mentioned several times above can in some ways be depicted in terms of Kandinsky's view of design as a balance between calculation and inspiration, between rationality and intuition. Nevertheless, in teaching design—that is, when given the pedagogical goal of a propaedeutics of design's elementary means— Kandinsky tended unequivocally to the rational method (and in this respect differed especially from Itten of the teachers at the Bauhaus), as the following citation demonstrates:

## Connections to Gestalt Psychology

Before moving on to a more concrete discussion of Kandinsky's teaching and his theory of design, we should comment on the observation made above, which was perhaps not immediately comprehensible, that, despite the "positive"73 analysis of pictorial means that Kandinsky called for and to some extent also achieved at the Bauhaus, Kandinsky ultimately did not give in to pure positivism.

We will recall that the search for the "figured bass," a dictionary of elements, and a grammar of pictorial means (which had already been attempted by Adolf Hölzel, among others) was the result, in Kandinsky's case, not simply of interest in the perception of form but was based, as we have seen, on the intention to give adequate expression in pictorial language to the idea of the "pure and eternally artistic" that was metaphysically conceived but that nonetheless claimed to be objective. If the artist in his early years was not able to find his way to use forms that resulted logically, that is, not purely emotionally,74 by the time of

On the Spiritual in Art he had become aware of the need for rational, constructive compositions.75 A decisive step in this direction is marked by the analyses that appeared in 1919 in the Russian journal Iskusstvo, nos. 3–4, “Little Articles on Big Questions: On Point; On Line."76 These analyses clearly served as the basis for the first section of the later Bauhaus book Point and Line to Plane. If the first section of Point and Line to Plane is dominated by a more positivistic view of the problem along the lines of a detailed investigation of pictorial means, the second section (in which Kandinsky treats surface area)reveals a more holistic point of view that reveals remarkable parallels to the antipositivistic Gestalt psychology that was developing in Germany at the time. L. D. Ettlinger and later Paul Overy have devotedconsiderable energy to demonstrating such parallels or even direct correspondences between Kandinsky's paintings and writings of the Bauhaus period and the ideas of the Gestalt psychologists. Whether or not Kandinsky read the writings of the leading Gestalt psychologists in the twenties (i.e., Max Wertheimer, Wolfgang Köhler, and Kurt Koffka), Overy proceeds from the hypotheses that these ideas were constant topics of discussion in the intellectual atmosphere of the Bauhaus and that they would have been especially attractive to Kandinsky."7 (It seems significant in this context to mention that under Hannes Meyer psychology became part of the Bauhaus curriculum and that the lectures on psychology given by Karfried Graf von Dürckheim were based on Gestalt and holistic approaches.)78

Gestalt psychology's approach to science may be broadly defined as antipositivistic because, in sharp contrast to the older elemental psychology, the process of sense perception is understood not as the combination of atomistic, individual sense units that can be counted and measured—that is to say, not as elementary and additive—but as complex and gestaltlike. "Instead of reacting to local stimuli by local and mutually independent events, the organism reacts to an actual constellation of stimuli by a total process which, as a functional whole, is its response to the whole situation."79

Thus, for example, according to Gestalt psychology, a triangle is not perceived as the addition of three lines and three corners but as a holistic gestalt that is more than the sum of its parts. It may be assumed with great certainty that this movement in psychology would have had strong appeal for Kandinsky given its basic antipositivistic stance, its synthetic approach to gestalt, and its assertion of intercultural validity of design principles. This is not the place to go into the details of the principles of design elaborated by Gestalt psychology—closureor completeness[Geschlossenheit], proximity or contiguity [Nähe], good form [Prägnanz], gestalt pressure [Gestaltdruck], figure [Figure], and ground [Grund]. It is enough to point out that Kandinsky's artistic oeuvre during the Bauhaus period and the text and illustrations in the book Point and Line to Plane frequently reveal a remarkable proximity to these gestalt principles. It is unimportant whether a direct influence of Gestalt psychology exists here or whether it might be an example of simultaneity of events; the fact remains that in his search for a figured bass of painting Kandinsky began to formulate at the Bauhaus a design language whose claim to validity could be supported and legitimated by the research of Gestalt psychology—more seriously and convincingly than would ever have been the case with theosophical theories.

## Theory of Design

The main weight of Kandinsky's instruction was placed on an introduction to abstract form elements, his true theory of design (in addition, Kandinsky gave a course on analytical drawing, which will be discussed later). The program of this introduction to abstract form elements retained the same basic structure over a period of years. It began with an introduction that critically analyzed the intellectual situation of the day and determined the standpoint and function of the new synthetic art. Then the following themes were discussed systematically:

1. Color theory (isolated color)

2. Form theory (isolated form)

3. Color and form theory (relationships of colors and forms)

4. Base.80

The course was highly theoretically oriented and—to use a current expression—teacher-oriented, in contrast to the much more task-oriented teaching that was otherwise typical for pedagogy at the Bauhaus. Nevertheless, the lectures were supplemented by small-scale, practical exercises for the students that were intended to aid in the practical acquisition of the theoretical material. The tasks that Kandinsky gave were as a rule so narrowly conceived that the range of possibilities was radically constrained; despite all that, a variety of individual solutions was possible (this was also the principle of Albers's preliminary course). Many of these assignments can be found in the Cours. We cite here just one representative example from the notes of a participant in Kandinsky's course:

In Kandinsky's class we were given a real vacation assignment, but it isn't bad. A square, 30 cm by 30 cm, is to be divided up into 5 by 10 cm rectangles. The following colors are to be used: 3 primary, 3 secondary, and 3 uncolored (black, white, gray). The arrangement of the colored rectangles is entirely up to you as long as they are horizontal and vertical, not diagonal. It's also up to you how often you use each color, so long as every color is used at least once. The task is as follows:

1. emphasize the center

2. balance top and bottom.

In the final session he did not say much new, just something about the tensions in the square; I myself did not understand it completely, andI think it's not that important anyway....81

The last sentence would seem to be not untypical, and it suggests that, despite all of Kandinsky's efforts to give a rational basis to design, it was clearly not always easy for the students to follow their teacher's thinking.

## Color Theory

As part of his introduction to abstract elements of form at the Bauhaus, Kandinsky regularly began with color theory. His pedagogical justification for this was that in an analytical study of elementary pictorial means the phenomenon of“color" would be more accessible at first for students of the first semester than problems of form (here "form" is taken in the strict sense).82 To clarify the terms here, Kandinsky used the word “form" in both a broad and a narrow sense. In the broad sense his concept of form includes color, because—"since neither surface nor volume can exist without color,"83 or in other words, color is always connected to form or itself establishes the form—color is, in Kandinsky's view, one of the abstract elements of form. Form in the narrow sense refers to the basic elements, like the point, the line, the plane, the body, and space, independent of their color.84

As a painter, Kandinsky considered the problem of color not only in practice but also in theory in an extraordinarily intense way. This occupation found systematic form in his epochal theoretical magnum opus, On the Spiritual in Art (1912).

The theory of color Kandinsky explicatedat the Bauhaus, which was profoundly inspired by Goethe, underwent certain modifications that were, however, hardly reflected at all in Point and Line to Plane (1926). In his second major theoretical work Kandinskymade his central focus an analysis of form (in the narrow sense)—an analysis he had called for, but left unfulfilled, in On the Spiritual in Art—without fundamentally revisiting the theory of color. All that is new in Point and Line to Plane is the methodological explorations of the correspondences between forms and colors, which will be discussed in more detail below.

"When the spirit emerges from within it appears as color, as sound. Color and sound are nothing but pure spirit; exactly the same thing that we discover within us when we truly understand ourselves."85 This quotation from Rudolf Steiner may help to suggest the horizon against which Kandinsky developed his theory of color in On the Spiritual in Art. His interest was not to develop a theory of color that could be applied instrumentally. that would make it easier for thepractitioner to mix paints, or that would provide insights into the relationships among the colors, but rather to explore color as a means to have “a direct influence upon the soul."86In such an investigation, whose results—as Kandinsky emphasized—are based on "empirical-spiritual experience and... not upon any positive science,"87 colors are studied first individually, that is, in isolation, to discover their particular psychological effects.88 The parallels to Goethe's Farbenlehre (Theory of Colours), specifically to the didactic section (section 6, "Effect of Colour with Reference to Moral Associations"), are unmistakable, which has led some critics to claim that Kandinsky's color theory is merely a paraphrase of aspects of Goethe's theory.89 In the brief discussion of Kandinsky's color theory that follows, the relevant chapter from On the Spiritual in Art is supplemented by pertinent passages from the Cours du Bauhaus where the latter help to complete or clarify the former, especially when they provide new perspectives.

Whereas Goethe in his much more comprehensively conceived theory of color dealt in detail first with the origin of colors and especially the basic colors yellow, blue, and purple (polarity and intensification) before exploring the effects of colors individually and then the "harmonic," "characteristic," and"characterless combinations" of colors, Kandinsky began immediately by characterizing the effects of individual colors. He made the fundamental distinction between the "warmth and coldness of a color" and its "lightness or darkness," resulting in "four main sounds":90

1. warm-light

2. warm-dark

3. cold-light

4. cold-dark.

Kandinsky defined the warmth or coldness of a color very generally as its inclination toward yellow or blue and designated this color pair the "first great contrast." The warm (yellow) moves toward the observer; it is a movement toward the physical, because "yellow is the typical earthly color." The cold (blue) withdraws from the observer; it is a movement toward the spiritual, because "blue is the typical heavenly color."91 Although the proximity to the ideas of romanticism generally and Goethe's reflections in particular is striking, what is new with respect to Goethe is that yellow is also characterized by an eccentric (centrifugal) movement and blue by a concentric (centripetal) one. Sixten Ringbom has convincingly demonstrated that Kandinsky was inspired here by Arthur Osborne Eaves's book The Colour Cure (1901; translated into German as Die Kräfte der Farben in 1906), an occult book on chromotherapy, i.e., on the healing powers of colors, in which blue is described as, among other things, "astringent."92

These sorts of "characteristics" of colors—in this case, of yellow and blue—document once again the synesthetic abilities or tendencies of Kandinsky the artist and theoretician. It should be immediately obvious that they remain largely subjective; this could be easily demonstrated by any psychological investigation, for example, using the methodology of the so-called semantic differential.

As part of his exploration of the "second great contrast," that of light and dark, Kandinsky arrived at the "noncolors" white and black. He localized these noncolors in the proximity of the colors yellow and blue, respectively (cf. Goethe, §§ 765, 766, 778); like yellow and blue, according to Kandinsky, white and black have “the same movement towards or away from the spectator, although not in dynamic, but in static, rigid form."94 In his Bauhaus manuscripts he spoke in this context of a movement in spe, a movement in embryonic state, and in terms of temperature he characterized white as internally warm (proximity to yellow) and black as internally cold (proximity to blue). Whereas the silence of white, in Kandinsky's view, "is not dead but full of possibilities," black seems like "an inner sound of nothingness bereft of possibilities, a dead nothingness as if the sun had become extinct, an eternal silence without future, without hope. . . . Black is something extinguished, like a spent funeral-pyre, something motionless, like a corpse, which is dead to all sensations, which lets everything simply pass it by. It is like the silence of the body after death, the close of life. Kandinsky described gray as a mixture of the two noncolors, as "toneless and immobile." This disconsolate aspect increases the darker the gray is, whereas a light gray retains a touch of hope.97 Both in On the Spiritua/ in Art and in the Bauhaus manuscripts there is, with reference to Delacroix, a remark that gray can also result from an“optical mixture of green and red."98

Green and red mark the "third great contrast." In “absolute green," understood as an “ideal balance" between the diametrically opposed colors yellow and blue, both the horizontal and the circular motions of these two original colors cancel each other out so that green seems motionless and the epitome of calm, passivity, and self-satisfaction: "Thus, pure green is to the realm of color what the so-called bourgeoisie is to human society: it is an immobile, complacent element, limited in every respect."99 Red, green's complement, is on the surface as motionless as green, that is, it has neither eccentric nor concentric, neither protruding nor receding movement. Internally, however, red seems to be “a highly lively, living, turbulent color . .. of immense, almost purposeful strength. In this burning, glowing character, which is principally within itself and very little directed toward the external, we find, so to speak, a kind of masculine maturity."100

Thepresentation of the effects of the red ranging from "ideal red" turning to "warm" and then "cold" red (an inclination to yellow or blue, respectively) will not be discussed here. If yellow is added to the warm red, blue to the cold red, the result is the "fourth great contrast," namely, that between orange and violet. In both cases, the same movements obtain as for yellow and blue but to a lesser extent. Orange “resembles a man sure of his powers, and for this reason evokes a particularly healthy feeling." Violet, by contrast, has "something sad, an air of something sickly, something extinguished about it (like a slag heap!). It is not for nothing that this color is considered suitable for the clothes of old women. The Chinese in fact use it as the color of mourning."101

Kandinsky's depiction of the four great contrasts in the form of a circle (Orobouros motif as the symbol of infinity and eternity) seems problematic for two reasons. First, because yellow and blue (l), red and green (Ill), orange and violet (IV) are opposed on the circle in such a way that the resulting sequence of colors defined by the mixture of pigments is wrong: yellow-greenviolet-blue-red-orange. Second, because the contrast of white and black (ll) remains outside the circle; thus, although the contrasts are visible, the complex relationships of the colors to one another are not—as they are for example in Runge's color sphere.102

Now is the time to briefly call attention to some conceptual differences between the version of Kandinsky's color theory in On the Spiritual in Art and in the Bauhaus manuscripts:

1. Kandinsky's epistemological position seems to have changed at the Bauhaus. As the author of On the Spiritual in Art, a work based exclusively on the results of "empirical-spiritual experience"—as he put it then—Kandinsky ignored both physics and the "material conditions" of pigments; now he more frequently introduced discoveries from physics into his course and, more importantly, started with pigments, that is, with the “material substance of colors."103

2. A second profound difference is that Kandinsky at the Bauhaus no longer exclusively focused on the contrasting pairs of colors in his teaching but rather started with the three primary colors—yellow, blue, and red—as had been the general practice since the eighteenth century. This was not only in accordance with Itten's theory of color, which was inspired by Hölzel, but also with the dogma of primary colors advocated by Mondrian (whose ideas, like Kandinsky's, were indebted to theosophy), Theo van Doesburg, and other members of de Stijl. This sort of correspondence should not immediately lead us to believe it was a case of influence; in the work program for Inkhuk of 1920 Kandinsky—surely independently of Itten, Mondrian, or van Doesburg at this point—called for the exploration of the "elementary color triad" yellow-blue-red.

At the Bauhaus, Kandinsky considered red not primarily in its opposition to green but in its relation to yellow and blue. Red is thus the color that "fills the gap between yellow and blue," that lies "in the middle between yellow and blue," and that results from intensifying yellow and blue (cf. Goethe §§ 699 ff.).104 Among the "great contrasts" that Kandinsky explored in On the Spiritual in Art only the green-red pair was truly complementary, that is, only these two colors become gray when mixed. Only by returning to the three primary colors at the Bauhaus and starting out from the material properties of pigments was Kandinsky able to overcome the inconsistencies that adhere to the theory of color formulated in On the Spiritual in Art. From that point on he was working with the secondary colors (mixtures of two primary colors) that are derived from the primary colors yellow, red, and blue—orange, green, violet—and the tertiary colors (mixtures of two secondary colors), citrine, russet, and olive. (This terminology is reminiscent of Hölzel's color theory, to which Kandinsky may have been introduced at the Bauhaus by Hölzel's students Johannes Itten and Oskar Schlemmer.) The complementary color pairs (pairs of the primary and secondary colors that produce gray when mixed) are yellow-violet, red-green, blue-orange. Kandinsky spoke in these cases of the "great contrasts." When the primary colors are replaced with tertiary colors, the following pairs result: citrine-violet, russet-green, olive-orange. In their greatly weakened form, these contrasts of secondary and tertiary colors result, according to Kandinsky, in a "small contrast."105

3. These brief remarks on certain combinations of colors and their contrasts bring up a third point in which the earlier version of Kandinsky's color theory differs from the later one. Both Goethe and Runge concerned themselves more or less intensively with problems of a theory of harmony for colors. Both took it for granted that certain color combinations are harmonious while others are less so. Thus Goethe distinguished between "harmonious," "characteristic," and "characterless" combinations; and Runge distinguished between "harmonious,""disharmonious," and "monotone" ones. Goethe treated only dyadic color relationships, whereas Runge also had triadic ones in the case of disharmonious combinations.

Although in On the Spiritual in Art Kandinsky clearly considered it practical for methodological reasons to isolate the colors and examine them individually, he was of course very clearly aware of the fact that color is never an isolated stimulus but always occurs in context. "Form alone,"Kandinsky said, “can exist per se. Not so color."106Despite this recognition, however, he didnot attempt to go systematically through the relationships among colors and to examine their possibilities for harmony. In contrast to Goethe, Runge, or Hölzel, he was of the view that in a time of great spiritual antagonisms—he seems to have taken little notice of socialantagonisms—"harmonization on the basis of simple colors is precisely the least suitable."107 Consequently, he recognized that combinations of colors that, following traditional aesthetic rules, have generally been considered dissonant contain possibilities of new harmonies: "For example, the juxtaposition of red and blue, these physically unrelated colors, is today chosen as one of the most strongly effective and most suitably harmonious because of the great spiritual contrast between them."108 Apart from this one concrete example, however, in On the Spiritual in Art Kandinsky retained his fundamental reserve with respect to a harmony theory of colors, tha is, he did not take the trouble to analyze the relationships of colors to one another based on a new, revised concept of harmony. More or less in passing, he merely remarked that colors seem dull, weak, and powerless against white, whereas most seem precise, strong, and powerful against black;109 and that red is especially "beautiful" as a complementary color to green in folk art and in folk costumes.110

In addition to the exploration of the corresponding "internal sound" of the individual colors on the basis of introspection and phenomenology, there was also a more in-depth study of the relationships of colors to one another at the Bauhaus, as is clear both from the Cours and the student works produced in the class. For example, Kandinsky had them systematically examine how the effects of colors change according to the size of the plane and the color of the surroundings.111 In relation to the three dimensions "brightness," "size," and "temperature" he selected several examples from the universe of possibilities to show how a small fleck of color (above the line) on colored ground (below the line) changes its apparent color based on the surrounding color:112

We can only make guesses about Kandinsky's view of the color harmony problem during the Bauhaus period. It can be said, however, that the Cours has no hint of a skeptical attitude toward the question of harmony like that found in On the Spiritual in Art. On the contrary, in his course Kandinsky referred several times to Ostwald's "quantifying theory of color" and the latter's book Die Harmonie der Farben (The harmony of colors).113 According to Ostwald, the "main principle of color harmony" is that "Only those colors can be harmonious or compatible whose features stand in certain simple relationships to one another."114 Harmony represented for Ostwald a system of order based on measurement, and it is certainly plausible that in his search at the Bauhaus for the figured bass of painting Kandinsky might have agreed with this conception of harmony. This is supported by an idea already expressed in 1912 and reinforced in Point and Line to Plane of 1926, namely, that "the future theory of harmony for painting" was to be found where the relationships among the elements may be “expressed in a mathematical form," a thought culminating in the statement that "in every art, number remains the ultimate form of abstract expression."115

## Theory of Form

This presentation of Kandinsky's theory of form (like the ones below on the "Theory of Color and Form" and the "Picture Plane") follows in its main aspects the corresponding passages in Point and Line to Plane both in sequence and argument. Material from the Cours is introduced only in cases where amplification seems necessary.

Kandinsky's theory of form comprises the investigation of the elementary pictorial means of the point and the line and the three basic forms that result from these elements, namely, the circle, the triangle, and the square.

The geometrical point is, according to Kandinsky, invisible, nonmaterial. In language it indicates silence; it is the “symbol for interruption, for non-being . . ., and at the same time it is a bridge from one entity to the next."116 Pictorially, the point is the result of the tool's first contact with the material surface, with the picture plane. This contact of the tool with the surface implies that the point, which in mathematics remains invisible and nonmaterial and exists only as a thought construct, obtains a certain size and definite outline as part of its pictorial materialization so that it can be distinguished and defined against its surroundings. Although Kandinsky believed that the point can be defined as the smallest of theelementary forms, this “smallness" is relative. Its relativity results from the ratio of point to picture plane and from the relation of the point to other pictorial elements found on the picture plane.117 With respect to its outline (its form), the point can be thought of in the ideal as round, that is, as a circle. In practice, however, it takes infinitely many forms. Even so, the point was for Kandinsky “inwardly [the] most concise form. It is introverted. . . . Its tension is ultimately always concentric. ... The point burrows into the surface and establishes itself for all time." Because it has no inclination to move, the factor of time is almost completely excluded so that it may be considered the "temporally most concise form."118

The simplest composition for Kandinsky, then, is when a point is placed at the center of a quadratic picture plane. Here the tensions between point and plane are absolutely balanced, the dyad of point-plane takes on the character of a unison. Only in the case of a central placement of the point can a dyad be heard: the absolute sound of the point and the sound of the surrounding picture plane. Adding points would establish simple rhythms at first and then, as the points pile up and grow dense, surface aspects.119

One suggestion of the analytical and elementary approach that characterizes Point and Line to Plane, as discussed earlier, is Kandinsky's understanding of the line as the trace of a point's motion. As Clark V. Poling has shown, the artist is here following a thesis published by the physicist and physiologist Hermann von Helmholtz in 1876 that “a point in movement describes a line."120 This means that the line, though fundamentally different from the point in character, nevertheless owes its existence to it, and thus it is a secondary pictorial means. Under a self-imposed constraint to arrange the elementary pictorialmeans according to their degree of complexity into a logical; hierarchical system, it seems to have escaped Kandinsky that as a pictorial means the line, not the point, is both phylo- and ontogenetically primary. This can be seen equally well in the art of primitive peoples and the drawings of children (on this, see Klee's definition of the line in his "Contributions to a Theory of Pictorial Form," discussed in “Theory of Form" in chapter 9).

If the line is understood as a point in motion, it means that energies or forces have acted on it. Kandinsky distinguished between two fundamental possibilities: (1) the effect of a force that leads to straight lines; and (2) the effect of two forces that result in either zigzag or curved lines.

The three basic types of straight lines, of which all other straight lines are merely modifications, are the horizontal, the vertical, and the diagonal. The horizontal was, for Kandinsky, a "cold, basic support"; the vertical, by contrast, was "warm"; and the diagonal, because of its "equal tendency toward each of them," was described as "cold-warm."121 It is typical of the axiomatic form of Kandinsky's theory of design that he abjured from explaining or justifying the properties he assigned to the three types of lines.

“Axiom" is not understood here as a claim whose truth has been confirmed so frequently by experience that it can be considered absolutely certain but as a claim about whose truth nothing is known; thus it is placed at the beginning of a theory to be constructed in order to tèst the consequences that result. In both cases, in contrast to straight lines, “a far greater sympathy with the surface" arises, so that these types of lines “have, indeed, something surface-like about them."126 This characterization corresponds to what Klee called the “medial stage" of the line (see "Theory of Form" in chapter 9).

## Theory of Color and Form

In this section of the course Wassily Kandinsky was not attempting to subject colors and form to an isolated analysis as elements but rather—from the point of view of an aesthetic synthesis—to discover correspondences between colors and forms. It is precisely from these synthetic perspectives, then, that his theory of color and form deserves special interest. We need not go into the conditions under which Kandinsky's ideas of synthesis were formed, as they were already discussed at the beginning of the chapter; thus we can proceed directly to the presentation of the relationships among forms and colors.

Kandinsky's central concept for working out these relationships was temperature. That colors are associated with temperature is a commonplace in the history of color theory since at least the time of Goethe. What is new, however, is that Kandinsky established axiomatic assignments of specific temperatures to specific formal elements. One significant step toward formulating a figured bass of painting was to bring these“form temperatures" into a system with the “color temperatures" in a way that was as accurate as possible and in this way to achieve form-color correspondences that would be valid at least within the system. To do this, Kandinsky began with the three basic types of lines (horizontal, vertical, and diagonal) and their characteristic temperatures (cold, warm, and coldwarm). Even in On the Spiritual in Art he had characterized black as internally cold(proximity to blue) and white as internally warm (proximity to yellow) so that now in his theory of color and form he concluded: “If the schematic straight lines——in the first instance, horizontals andverticals—are examined for their color characteristics, we are faced with an obvious comparison with black and white."131 Kandinsky assignedred to the coldwarm of the diagonals, since in his theory of color he had placed it between blue (cold) and yellow (warm). In contrast to the three schematic straight lines, which according to Kandinsky lie firmly on the plane, the independent straight lines are inclined to break free of the plane, and Kandinsky associated this with the protruding and receding of yellow and blue.

Although in his own paintings from the twenties Kandinsky frequently made use of the basic forms of the triangle, the square, and the circle, their form-color relationships were more likely to reveal these "new possibilities," so they do not reflect the scheme of triangle-yellow, square-red, and circle-blue. Nevertheless, this dogma seems to have been considered unassailable in his course. Oskar Schlemmer commented on this in 1923, not without an ironic undertone: "Kandinsky's classes: scientifically strict study of color and form. For example: the search for the three elementary colors that correspond to the three basic forms (triangle, square, circle). Instinctively I always make the circle red and the square blue."135In order to confront skeptics with data, Kandinsky conducted surveys not only among the students but in the general population that were intended to contribute to a statistical clarification of the relationships between basic forms and basic colors. Hirschfeld-Mack reported that an overwhelming majority decided in favor of yellow in the triangle, red in the square, and blue in the circle.136 It is obvious that this result is very much open to methodological criticism. For example, it must be considered rather naive to conduct such surveys at the very place where this form-color dogma was being taught, namely, at the Bauhaus. A lack of awareness of methodology is also suggested by the fact that the questionnaire gave the three basic forms in order (triangle, square, and circle), and it contained instructions to fill these three forms with the three colors yellow, red, and blue. It does not require any special insight into the theory behind constructing questionnaires to recognize that this wording and the three colors in the sequence that corresponded to Kandinsky's pedagogical view all but suggests the desired result. It is difficult to escape the conclusion that the goal of these surveys, for all their appearance of objectivity, was more to confirm Kandinsky's theories than to make an unprejudiced assessment of reality.

## Picture Plane

After having examined the basic elements point and line in isolation in the theory of form—their relationship to each other and to the plane are only suggested occasionally—in the section of his course (and his book Point and Line to Plane) entitled "Picture Plane" Kandinsky treated the material bearer of the picture under that name (abbreviated "pP"). In doing so, he explored the picture plane both in terms of its internal tensions and in light of its relations to the elementary pictorial means.

From a variety of possible picture planes he chose three "schematic" cases: the oblong format (which, in keeping with his characterization of the horizontals, he called a "cold" format), the upright format (consequently a "warm" format), and the square (which he called "objective" because of its relative balance between cold and warm and therefore took as the starting point for his further discussions).

He divided the picture plane (square) into four force fields: above, below, right, left.

“Above” conjures up an image of greater rarification, a feeling of lightness, of emancipation, and ultimately, of freedom. . . . Constraint is reduced to a minimum.

"Below" has entirely the opposite effect: density, weight, bondage.. . . Constraint attains its maximum.138

Overy observed that this "double sound" of gravity and weightlessness corresponds to a primitive habit of vision. He refers to the following passage in James J. Gibson's standard work The Perception of the Visual World:

An out-of-doors world is one in which the lower portion of the visual field ... is invariably filled by a projection of the terrain. The upper portion of the visual field is usually filled with a projection of the sky. Between the upper and lower portions is the skyline, high or low as the observer looks down or up, but always cutting the normal visual field in a horizontal section. This is the kind of world in which our primitive ancestors lived. It was also the environment in which took place the evolution of visual perception in their ancestors. During the millions of years in which some unknown animal species evolved into our human species, land and sky were the constant visual stimuli to which the eyes and brain responded. In the typical indoors world of civilized man, a ceiling and walls take the place of the horizon and sky, but the floor is still an equivalent to the ground.139

This accords with Kandinsky's, as he put it, "literary" association of up with the sky and down with the earth.140

According to Kandinsky, the characteristics of the left side of the picture plane correspond to those of “above" (light, relaxed, free) but in altered form, that is, with a greater degree of density; by analogy, the right side is inherently close to "below" (heavy, dense, bound) but with a lesser degree of density: "movement toward the 'left'— toward freedom—is movement into the distance ... movement toward the 'right'—toward captivity—is a homeward movement."141 What Kandinsky described metaphorically seems to be connected with the way vision is conditioned in Western culture by reading from left to right. ## Analytical Drawing

Kandinsky—who expressly emphasized that his course on analytical drawing was based on the same principles and methods as his instruction on abstract elementsof form148conceivedthis course for the first semester not as an introduction to drawing from nature or things but as a propaedeutics of“precise observation" andof constructive pictorial organization. The goal of this drawing class was thus not to depict the external appearance of the world of things in a way as close to nature as possible but to provide a representation “of the laws that govern the forces (= tensions) that can be discovered in given objects and of their logical construction—an exercise in clearly observingand clearly reproducing relationships."149 This quotation demonstrates yet again that Kandinsky's concern in art was with an "inner perception," not with a mimesis of external reality of any particular concrete aesthetic form. (It is therefore hardly surprising that in his course Kandinsky frequently criticized realistic tendencies in the art of the twenties, particularly the painting of Neue Sachlichkeit.) This "inner perception" does not, however, preclude analytical drawing from treating objects, namely, in the form of still life. It is well known that still life—which had been common as an independent genre in painting since the early sixteenth century—was frequently used as an occasion for formal design in modern art ranging from impressionism through Cézanne to cubism. Similarly, still life in Kandinsky's course represented a means of formal analysis. This meant that his course on analytical drawing ignored both the sensory stimulation of the various materials that coalesce in the still life——and whose depiction was of such extraordinary interest to Itten in his preliminary course—and the possibility of exploiting the still life as a vehicle for expressing certain kinds of subject matter (as was done, for example, in the vanitas still lifes of seventeenth-century Holland). The still life is well suited to formal analysis not only because of the lack of movement but also because it can be arranged at will, so that even the structure can conform with the artist's intentions for the design. As a rule, the students in Kandinsky's drawing course, too, constructed their still lifes themselves. In doing so, they consciously gave precedence to elements that easily lent themselves to reduction to·geometric forms and couldbe subsumed to a rigorous pictorial architecture. ## Critical Objections

Gropius and Kandinsky were agreed on two points: (1) that art was “not teachable" (Gropius) or “could not be learned" (Kandinsky); (2) that a school for design like the Bauhaus had to have a propaedeutic course to ensure that students would acquire those very general insights into the basics of design that were equally important for fine and applied arts. In this sense, the elementary language that Kandinsky formulated as part of his teaching system at the Bauhaus was of central significance not least becausé his course was required for all first-semester students, and for a time even second- and fourth-semester students. Precisely because Kandinsky's design course ignored problems of practicaldesignwork and lacked any specific subject matter—apart from that of finding and interpreting the tensions inherent in a form or composition——it was clearly accepted for years as the general propaedeutic course in the sense of presenting the most elementary notions of form and color. Only under

Hannes Meyer did the relevance of this design course begin to be questioned, particularly by Communist students. A hectographed pamphlet published by the latter in July 1930 states, among other things: "What is the content and purpose of analytical drawing? Still-life, concise scheme, tensions, structural net, and as the ultimate objective—'free composition of three-dimensional, formal energy tensions.' This kind of instruction is bound to lead to individual, abstract, creative work, as is borne out by the various distinctly individual renderings of one and the same still-life. This kind of drawing is entirely unsuited for the work in the workshops, since it does not allow for an objective approach."152 If one tries to situate Kandinsky's design course within the overall context of pedagogy at the Bauhaus, it can be seen as the contribution of a “rational irrationalist," something that can also be said of Klee. For someone like Itten, by contrast, the irrational ·aspects dominate, as the rational ones do in the case of Moholy and Albers. Despite his metaphysical and transcendental inclinations, Kandinsky established a pedagogical edifice that is logically and deductively structured and clad with strict rationality, but whose fundamentalaxioms nevertheless elude a rational approach. In the same way, Kandinsky's true goal—the formulation of a design language that conforms to a principle of internal necessity—was founded on a stratum of irrationality. The strong emphasis on the analytical principle—the call for a rational penetration of the means of design, for their reduction to basic forms and colors, and for “microscopic" exploration of the inner tensions inherent in a form or a complex of forms—did not, as Kandinsky saw it, preclude communicating the spiritual and emotional qualities that are inherent in the forms and colors. It is obvious that the realization of this goal was often hampered by the “purely cerebral exercises," which necessarily harbored the risk of ossifying into a schematic formalism, resulting in a merely external, recipe-like set of rules acquired. There can be no doubt that there was at least the possibility of stunting students' sense perceptions through this fixation on a primarily cognitive approach to design, through this tendency to rigorous cerebralism. And this is confirmed by the dominance in numerous student works of a design language that places strict restraints on emotion.

Equally problematic is Kandinsky's insistence on design principles that he claimed were timeless and intercultural (as did Itten, following Hölzel)— a result of his search for a figured bass of painting as an instrument for the pictorial realization of the pure and eternally artistic. By postulating universal principles, Kandinsky, like the Gestalt psychologists, turned perception and design into categories that claimed to be independent of cultural, political, social, and other concrete conditions. It goes without saying that such a view has an inherently conservative aspect, and thus it is no surprise that leftist critics, both at the Bauhaus itself and from the so-called critical art pedagogy movement of the 1970s, have tried to disqualify Kandinsky's teachings as formalistic or hostile to emancipation. They did not, however, succeed in unsettling the outstanding historical importance demonstrated by his fundamental contribution to the pedagogy of design.
