## 134.

Youth Children's Story Series, 1961

The stories in this magazine came from both classic Chinese literature and Western stories. The target audience, students in the middle elementary grades, were treated to easy-to-read text, lively drawings, and positive messages. A typical story was Little Shui-hu Hero, a famous mythological tale (Outlaws of the Marsh) altered by making the main character a child.

135–138.

Children's Story, 1962

Originally a supplement to Little Friends Pictorial and geared to higher elementary and middle school children, this magazine was mainly text, but had some color illustrations. Features included The Story ofAhcheung, The Story of Little Detective Siu-fok, and The Story of Siu-yan.

139–141.

Little Angel Pictorial Monthly, 1962

Little Angel Pictorial Monthly was published by Children’'s Paradise and produced by the same writers and artists. Its contents were similar to Children's Paradise, but its illustrations were weak, and it included translated Western comic strips.

## 142.

Good Children's Pictorial Bimonthly, 1963

This was another imitator of Children's Paradise. It had very similar content, although it was targeted to Chinese children all over Southeast Asia, not just in Hong Kong. The wide distribution led to an interesting feature, a letters to the editor column full of notes from readers around the region.

## 143.

New Friends of Children Pictorial Bimonthly, 1965 Like the other children's magazines, this one had uplifting and positive themes running through its features, which included stories, science trivia, and games.

## 144-148.

## Little Baby and Little Po Po, 1975

Both of these comics were “candy books," a term used for inexpensive comics bought by young children. The characters were adorable, and the stories had a positive spin and educational content. Adults encouraged their children to read comics like Little Po Po and Little Baby,which is perhaps the reason they were published for almost twenty years. The art and scripts were by Hui Guan-man.

## 149–150. Children's TV, 1975

Most of this comic book's characters came from Japanese animation (anime) on television, which were a big hit in the early and mid-1970s. As this kind of programming continued to grow in popularity, the style of drawing and action began to influence Hong Kong manhua.

## 151-152.

## Happy Together Children's Pictorial, 1975

To appeal to the young fans of Japanese anime, Hong Kong manhua artists created stories using popular Japanese cartoon characters. This magazine's signature character is the round-faced TV Girly. Its stories are retellings of episodes of the Japanese show Robocon, which revolves around a red robot that can talk and work.

## 153–157.

## Ding Dong Bimonthly, 1976

Ding Dong, which first appeared in Children’s Paradise in 1975, is a translation and adaptation of the Japanese manga Doraemon, by Fuijo Fujiko. The main character, a nuclear-powered robot cat named Doraemon, became Ding Dong, and other places and character’s names were changed. The story is a heartwarming fantasy about Tai-hung, a little boy who is the master of Ding Dong. With Ding Dong's help, Tai-hung can fly (powered by a propeller on his head), fight evil classmates with special gadgets, and even travel through time via his desk drawer.

Readers of Children’'s Paradise loved Ding Dong, and it was soon published as its own comic book. However, it was an unauthorized copy of Doraemon, and copyright considerations led to its demise in 1981. Soon after, Dareaemon’s Japanese publisher began printing an official Chinese version that preserved the pirated comic's character names. The title was finally changed to Doraemon in 1999, and it remains one of the most popular Japanese children's comic books in Hong Kong.
