# CHAPTER 10 # "The Club" and Postwar Other companies not actively engaged in defense production were offering lines of new products for business expansion after the war. The Herman Miller Furniture Company, under the direction of Gilbert Rohde, introduced its Executive Office Group, the first integrated line of modular furniture that could be assembled in various configurations, the forerunner of postwar open office systems. Dave Chapman, Inc., advised a number of client companies in adapting to peacetime production, including Parker Pen Company, International Harvester Company, Brunswick-Balke-Collender, and Scovill Manufacturing Company. Norman Bel Geddes designed a postwar line of radio cabinets for RCA, Coca-Cola vending machines for Mills Industries, and domestic ranges for Floyd Wells Company. He also conducted development work on the Nash automobile.1 During the war, the Crane Company, a manufacturer of domestic bathroom fixtures, made steel valves for the U.S. Navy (each ship required 1,500 to 15,000 valves per ship). Because Crane had discontinued making domestic products for the duration of the war, it did not need the services of Henry Dreyfuss, its design consultant, but continued to pay his retainer so he would not work for a competitor. Dreyfuss set up a satellite office in the Crane building in Chicago to study the company's entire line. His staff chose products that should be carried over and designed new products to be introduced after the war. So when the war was over Crane would have a new line of products ready to go immediately.² Libbey-Owens-Ford (LOF) had been challenged by National Geographic magazine to create a kitchen of the future. H. Creston Doner, head of LOF's department of design, accepted the challenge, and the result was the Kitchen of Tomorrow, built in the home of an LOF executive, which was featured prominently by major magazines and accompanied by a promotional campaign that included a short feature film by Paramount to be shown in movie theaters. Three full-sized models toured nationally for two years, starting in 1944. The kitchen promoted the use of glass extensively, but also proposed appliances and furniture for a“peacetime revolution of convenience and comfort." The kitchen and dining area were joined in an open space arrangement, made possible by a dining table that flipped up to attach to a wall, a pass-through counter area, and a refrigerator accessible from both kitchen and dining area. The glass-front refrigerator and cabinets brought dinnerware and food into view. The traditional stovetop was replaced with a unified wall unit that included a glass-walled oven/rotisserie grill, built-in toaster, mixer/juicer/meat grinder, waffle maker, and digital clock/thermometer, as well as recessed cooking/baking vessels. As mentioned earlier, college campuses during the war were at a low level. By 1943, there were very few male graduates or applicants, since the draft swept up many high school graduates. Some schools contributed to the war effort, such as the faculty at Pratt, by designing camouflage for military installations. Others were planning for an expected postwar resurgence and determining how to educate students of the new profession, industrial design. The American Designers' Institute (ADI) was under the leadership ofits president, Alfons Bach, and its education committee, headed by Alexander Kostellow, and with members László Moholy-Nagy and Donald Dohner had surveyed its membership and solicited the advice of John Vassos, Ben Nash, and George Kosmak (b. 1905) to develop a prototype educational program for industrial design. In 1944, ADI announced its Educational Program for Industrial Design, a four-year program combining technical, business, and academic courses. It in fact would become a template for the many new programs of industrial design that would be initiated after the war. In 1944, many designers were repositioning themselves for postwar business opportunities. Harold Van Doren expanded his firm, already with offices in Toledo and Philadelphia, to include a New York office, Van Doren, Nowland, & Schladermundt, which he established in collaboration with designers Roger Nowland and Peter Schladermundt. Donald Dailey, of Van Doren's office in Philadelphia, designed the familiar Boston pencil sharpener, which would be used in millions of schoolrooms over the next 50 years. J. Gordon Lippincott opened a design office in New York with Walter P. Margulies, titled Lippincott & Margulies. He hired Read Viemeister as director of styling. Donald Deskey designed a bowling center ball-return system for the Brunswick-Balke-Collender Company, which would become an integral part of hundreds of modern recreational bowling alleys that attracted families after the war. Harley Earl established an independent styling studio in Detroit, Harley Earl, Inc., to design nonautomotive products, but continued as vice president of styling at General Motors. John “Jack" Morgan had left Sears, Roebuck in 1943 and in 1944 opened his own office, Jack Morgan Associates, in Chicago, with a small staff. Jon Hauser (1916–1999) replaced Morgan at Sears. Peter Müller-Munk left his position as head of the industrial design program at the Carnegie Institute of Technology (CIT) in order to devote full time to his commercial design studio, Peter Müller-Munk Associates in Pittsburgh. Richard I. Felver, a Pittsburgh industrial designer, replaced him at CIT. John Gordon Rideout had closed his Cleveland office in 1941 but reopened it in 1943 in Chagrin Falls, Ohio, near Cleveland. In 1944, he joined with architect Ernest Payer to combine industrial design and architecture. That year, Edwin W. Fuerst, head of design at Libby Owens Glass Company, left Libby to open his own design office in Toledo, specializing in package design, which would operate until about 1952. In February, 1944, the International Business Machines Corporation (IBM) Automatic Sequence Controlled Calculator (ASCC) was delivered to Harvard University by IBM, which had engineered, built and funded the project, but it had been conceptually designed by Harvard University's computing pioneer Howard H. Aiken (1900–1973). The ASCC used 765,000 components, 500 miles of wires, three million connections, and was 51 feet long, eight feet high, and two feet deep. It was the industry's largest electromechanical calculator and the first operating machine that could execute long computations automatically. The computer was later called the Harvard Mark I. Futuristic industrial designer Norman Bel Geddes, who had been hired by IBM for \$50,000 to do the job, designed its enclosure. Aiken considered the elaborate case to be a waste of funds, which he thought could have been used to build additional calculator equipment. IBM was not happy because Aiken claimed himself as the sole "inventor” in the press release for the formal presentation to the university in August, ignoring several IBM employees who had helped build various components. Raymond Loewy Associates (RLA) was the formal organization formed in 1944 by Loewy to recognize his partners: A. Baker Barnhardt (in charge of products, automotive, and packaging); Jean Thomson Loewy, his first wife; Jack Breen (business manager), William Snaith, considered by Loewy “a brilliant partner." Miss Helen Peters was Loewy's personal secretary. RLA designed an Electrolux B6 floor polisher as well as a 1,000 hp diesel engine yard switcher locomotive, the FM H-10–44, 195 units of which were produced by Fairbanks-Morse for the American Railroad. Gone was any evidence of streamlining. The engine was a simple black and orange rectangular box, distinguished mainly by a protruding roof visor mounted on the rear cab. That same year, Gilbert Rohde, while still heading design at Herman Miller Furniture Company, passed away at age 50. In 1945, Herman Miller would appoint George Nelson as the company's new director of design. Nelson had become co-managing editor of Architectural Forum magazine in 1943 with Henry Wright and had that year written an article, “Grass on Main Street," in the Saturday Evening Post, which introduced the innovative idea of pedestrian malls. In 1945, Nelson and Wright developed a unique concept of storage wall systems, an outgrowth of their joint book, Tomorrow's House, published that year. The storage wall was featured in Life magazine, attracted national attention, and led to Nelson's position at Herman Miller. As his first project as director of design, Nelson designed a collection of 70 pieces. He would become an innovative and influential leader in postwar modern furnishings design. Since 1941, the membership base of the American Designers' Institute (ADI) in Chicago had been shifting to New York. In 1942, its New York chapter questioned whether the organization should still be incorporated in Illinois. The ADI board apparently agreed, for in 1943, the Illinois secretary of state dissolved the corporation for failing to file an annual report as required. In May 1944, the American Designers Institute (without the apostrophe) was granted a new charter in Delaware based on an application filed by Alfons Bach, Ben Nash, and George Kosmak. ADI's administrative offices were promptly relocated to New York, and its new president for 1944 was Belle Kogan, who had been secretary/treasurer during the previous term of office.⁵ Most prominent industrial designers of the 1930s practiced in New York, and their wellpublicized financial success over the decade had attracted the attention of New York State tax authorities, who decided that industrial design earnings were taxable under the unincorporated business tax statute. Accordingly, in 1940, Henry Dreyfuss, Walter Dorwin Teague, and Raymond Loewy were billed nearly half a million dollars in back taxes for the years 1935, 1936, and 1937. The three argued that their earnings were similar to architects, derived from services rendered rather than the sale of goods. So the Big Three shared costs to engage attorneys to represent them in court, with Teague as their representative standing trial on April 30, 1941. The attorneys argued for exemption of Teague's income from the unincorporated business tax because "the vocation of industrial design is aprofession, rather than atrade' or ‘business, and section 386 of the tax law ‘excludes the practice of law, medicine, dentistry, [and] architecture, which under existing law cannot be conducted under corporate structure, and in any other case in which more than eighty percent of the gross income is derived from personal services rendered by the individual." The case was dismissed. This victorious result has been taken as legal evidence that industrial design is, in fact, a profession. From that time, subsequent attempts to tax industrial designers under the same law were unsuccessful. However, after about 1976, when taxes for "professional corporations" would be implemented, all professions, including architects and industrial designers, would be subject to taxation similar to business corporations. However, the tax challenge of 1941, and the intent to protect their legal identity as an individual service provider, probably explains the traditional obsession of designers such as Loewy, Dreyfuss, and-Teague in requiring only their own name to be credited on all the work produced by individuals on their staff, a restrictive and resented practice that essentially disappeared in the 1970s, when tax codes were modified. After the tax issue was resolved, New York's leading designers, including Dreyfuss, Loewy, Bel Geddes, Deskey, Arens and Wright, met informally to discuss how the profession could further its obiectives of professionalism, influence the business world, and establish standards of practice. There were serious concerns regarding the American Designers Institute (ADI), the only such organization of national scale, as an appropriate organization for industrial design. First, the term “designers” in its title did not specifically refer to “industrial designers," the now commonly accepted specific term for the group of professionals who had successfully established the profession in the business and public worlds. That meant that any kind of "designer" could join ADI, not just industrial designers. Second, Chicago furniture designers who were specialists in a single industry had formed ADI. Some still were, and yet, many ofits members worked in many different, unrelated industries. The consensus of the leading designers mentioned above was that a national organization should specifically refer to “industrial designers," should be more exclusive, and should be restricted to those practitioners who had demonstrated proficiency in a broad range of products, not just one. At the time, there were about 2,000 industrial designers, but many were not well qualified in mass-production experience or skills. On February 7, 1944, 15 industrial designers met to discuss and conclude the formation of a new organization in accordance with these objectives. They defined an industrial designer as "one who has successfully designed a diversity of products for machine and mass production." This was the fundamental philosophical difference between the two groups, as many members of ADI specialized in furniture or home furnishings. The Society of Industrial Designers (SID) was thus formed. On August 20, 1944, at the first annual meeting of the SID, the group elected Walter Dorwin Teague as president, Henry Dreyfuss as vice president, Raymond Loewy as chairman of the executive committee, Harold Van Doren as treasurer, and Egmont Arens as secretary. Philip McConnell, formerly with the U.S. Treasury Department, and former secretary to the New York World's Fair Design Commission, was appointed executive secretary of the new organization. In addition to those mentioned above, founding members included Donald Deskey, Norman Bel Geddes, Lurelle Guild, Ray Patten, Joseph B. Platt, John Gordon Rideout, George Sakier, Joseph Sinel, Brooks Stevens, and Russel Wright. Wright recalled that George Nelson, Charles Eames, and several other members of the ADI were considered for membership, but were thought to be not acceptable at the time, probably because their work was limited to furniture design. Each of the SID founders invited one additional designer to join the next year, and after that, membership would be open to qualified designers. Some called the new organization "the Club," an exclusive organization led by the "Big Three," Teague, Loewy, and Dreyfuss. The qualifications for membership in SID were spelled out in a 1945 fAyer: "Membership ... shall be limited to practicing industrial designers ... [who have] practiced ... for several years [and] must have had three or more of his products manufactured and offered for sale. He must have ... served three or more manufacturers." These criteria specifically excluded businessmen, specialists, handicraft designers, and engineers. Each candidate for admission was required to submit a portfolio to the board of directors, where one negative vote could disqualify. Dues were levied according to individual income.7 So now, in 1944, there were two major design organizations, both of which had founding members who were pioneers of the profession. The ADI founders in 1938 were primarily furniture designers (or fabrics, ceramics, or other products in the traditional so-called art industries), but some ADI members later worked in a diversity of industries. SID founders were primarily designers in a diversity of industries (usually in the traditional so-called artless industries), but some later specialized. As the postwar era evolved, these distinctions would become blurred or irrelevant. They would all be industrial designers. But in 1944, with the solidification of this new and influential profession, there was considerable confusion and controversy about the formal definition of industrial design and industrial designers. This confusion extended to educational institutions, which were faced with the new challenge of how to educate industrial designers. In 1944, Richard F. Bach, dean of education at the Metropolitan Museum, hosted a conference for administrators of art schools to discuss the matter. Invitees included Alexander Kostellow and James C. Boudreau of Pratt, as well as administrators from 44 other art schools. A summary of the conference stated that “the conference carefully avoided definition of the term ‘industrial design, fearful that the effort to arrive at a definition might constrain discussion. Indeed it might. Most art schools' design programs were quite broad in scope and were not strictly industrial design programs. Only 15 of the 44 art schools had programs specializing in industrial design. Major changes in curriculum recommended by industrial designers included courses in the physical and behavioral sciences and the addition of workshops and laboratory courses similar to institutes of technology. Industrial design model building instruction required well-equipped workshops with heavy equipment such as lathes, band-saws, planers, drill presses and vented paint-spraying booths, as well as model instruction in soft and hard clay, plaster cast and forming, plastic heat forming, mold making, woodworking, metalworking, and high-quality finishing techniques, all requiring heavy expenditures. Specialized training in concept sketching and presentation rendering using pastels or gouache was also required to meet the standards of the profession. These potential changes challenged the traditional “applied art" mentality of art schools and would require new specialized equipment and faculty. For art schools to recognize industrial design as a unique entity would be to separate it from existing curricula and require new faculty, or it could lead to the establishment of independent schools of industrial design, something art schools did not want to see.8 Art school administrators argued that these sorts of changes would drive away some artistic students, which was probably true. As a result, many new industrial design courses in art school programs would not be as effective as in more technically adapted schools and would often be taught by existing art faculty with no experience in the field. Walter Dorwin Teague, attending a later meeting of this same group, commented, We have created a profession, and some of [you educators] are delighted to teach it, with only the vaguest idea of what it is all about.... By our practice we will create a definition of industrial design and I trust the conference committee will be acute enough to discern it.9 What Teague was saying was that practitioners would define the profession as it evolved, and educators needed to figure out how to teach it. This is pretty much the way the issue was resolved over the next 50 years. In fact, the profession would constantly evolve and expand into all aspects of design (graphic, interior, architectural, etc.) as well as into dozens of related technical fields such as psychology, science, engineering, sales, and marketing. Every new technology would present new opportunities for industrial designers, who always sought to make technology understandable and user friendly. Industrial design would forever defy a fixed definition, and industrial design programs would continue to vary in quality, scope, and structure. The invasion of Europe began in June 1944. On the day that the American troops entered liberated Paris in August of that year, Raymond Loewy, now a naturalized American citizen, was invited by the U.S. Department of State to speak on the radio to his former countrymen in French and was received in private audience by President Franklin D. Roosevelt.10 In May 1945, the war ended in Germany, and in August with the atomic bombs in Japan. Military personnel by the millions returned home to begin civilian life anew and needed housing, education, and jobs. Many married and went to school under the G.I. Bill. Those who had stayed at home had been deprived of new consumer products for four years. Aging cars, furniture, appliances, bathrooms, and kitchens, many from the 1930s, needed replacement or modernization. Perhaps more significantly, the national mood was much deeper than simple relief that the war was over. Many had been economically deprived since the Depression began in 1929, unable to afford the many new products and advances of the 1930s, which were then unavailable during the war. To these people, they had endured 15 years of scrimping and making do without any of the wonderful modern goods of the "World of Tomorrow" that had been promised at the World's Fairs of both 1933 and 1939. Now, many had enjoyed good jobs and steady income during the war. Average weekly pay had doubled since 1940, from \$24 to \$44 in 1945. A large number of families had double incomes, because many women had worked on defense jobs. Millions had accumulated considerable savings, and they were eager to spend it The country struggled to make a rapid transition from wartime production back to domestic, which was, in a sense, almost as difficult as it was to make the opposite switch in 1941. New designs and tooling were required, both of which took time. But America was the wealthiest and most powerful nation in the world with the greatest manufacturing base and the largest consumer market. An economic boom began that would last for the next 15 years. From 1945 to 1950, annual housing starts increased from 200,000 to 1,154,000. Developers were building new suburbs using crews of masons, carpenters, plumbers, electricians and painters who followed each other down streets and completed 30 homes per day. These mass-produced homes included a yard, a garage, shrubbery, shutters, and built-in appliances, including refrigerator, stove, washer and dryer—the perfect new owner package. All that was needed to start housekeeping was furniture, lamps, dinnerware, accessories, lawnmowers, ladders, and a car to drive to work. One of the popular accessories in such houses was a clothes dryer with a unique window in the front door that Brooks Stevens had designed in 1941 for the Hamilton Manufacturing Company. The demand was high for the design of mass-produced housing, but most architects did not contribute because of their concern that it could reduce their commissions on individual houses. But a number of nonarchitect entrepreneurs stepped in with ideas, among them Buckminster Fuller, who in 1945 redesigned his original Dymaxion house of 1929, which had evolved into his 1942 wartime Dymaxion Deployment Unit. Now, his final design used a central vertical stainless steel strut on a single foundation, which supported bicycle-wheel-like structures that hung down to support the roof, while beams radiated out to support the floor. Roof, ceiling, and floor were formed of wedge-shaped fans of sheet metal, creating a round floor plan. The entire exterior was aluminum, with many components built by the Beech Aircraft Company, which was seeking new postwar products. Several prototypes, including the Wichita House, were built, incorporating a prefabricated bathroom, water storage, heating, air conditioning and a convection ventilator built into the roof. Reactions to the prototypes were positive, but it was never produced industrially because Fuller considered the prototypes as "unfinished." Potential buyers found other options, and Fuller's company went bankrupt. Industrial designers were again in high demand by manufacturers as they developed and tooled up for high-volume domestic production. The profession was attractive because of its reputation for high salaries or fees, because its leading practitioners were national celebrities, and because graduates were in high demand. In March 1946, the Society of Industrial Designers (SID) published an educational bulletin listing schools with programs in industrial design. There were 21 such programs, with another half dozen under development. But graduates from these programs would not reach the market before about 1949 or 1950. At this point I need to remind you of something I mentioned in the preface. After 1934, and particularly after World War II, the number of industrial designers increased significantly. Since the subject of this book is the machine designers of the early 1930s, those who were the earliest pioneers prior to about 1935, these are the designers who are primarily followed throughout the book. To follow the careers and work of all industrial designers after 1935 would regretfully have been beyond the scope of this book. Although some are mentioned, information about them and their designs is limited. Accordingly, if you fail to find details of the many well-known and highly successful designers who have influenced the profession since 1935, or of their designs, this is the reason for their necessary omission. Immediately after the war, of course, the supply of designers was limited to older prewar practitioners already at work, or to young prewar graduates who were returning from military service. This meant that the large design firms and companies that were in existence before and during the war, led by the machine designers of the 1930s, would be handling the lion's share of postwar demand for industrial design, and would be enjoying tremendous success. Among those pioneer design firms was Raymond Loewy Associates (RLA) of New York, which in 1945 opened offices in Los Angeles, California, and Chicago, Illinois. For the latter, Loewy hired Richard S. Latham (1920–1991), a returning serviceman who had started before the war as a designer at Montgomery Ward. RLA designed a streamlined diesel locomotive, built by Fairbanks-Morse in General Electric's Erie, Pennsylvania, facility and introduced in 1945. The “A" units had an engineer cab above the engine with an automotive-style split windshield, similar to the 1937 Electro-Motive Super Chief, with which it was competing on the Atcheson, Topeka & Santa Fe Railway. The “B” units were cabless. Eighty-two A units and 29 B units would be built for American Railroads over the next four years. RLA in 1945 was on retainer to no fewer than 79 American, British, and Swedish corporations as a design consultant. Britain was at the time considering its own renewal and regeneration after the war and invited Raymond Loewy to offer an American designer's view of British industry. He responded with a letter published in the London Times in November that expressed the typical philosophy of American industrial design, titled "Industrial Design: The Aesthetics of Salesmanship—an American View": In America the industrial designer has developed a design technique that has been successfully tested in the field during the last 15 years, and American products most generally carry with them the advantage of collaboration between the professional consulting designer and the industrialist. This partnership with industry and our philosophy of design are based on securing greater sales appeal—increased trade. I have heard much here in England about the Aesthetics of design. This to me is strange. We seem to find that the aesthetics of an industrial product will take care of themselves automatically after we have provided a balance between function, simplicity, and utility. In the average manufacturer's mind the word aesthetics has annotations of philanthropy and culture after office hours. Industrial design did not become standard practice in America because of aesthetics. There were too many other good reasons for using it—increased sales appeal of products and increased trade. And the final result is no less beautiful for having been designed on sound, unemotional business lines." This philosophy, which embraced the purpose of design as a business strategy to increase product sales by appealing to the tastes and desires of common consumers, was the antipathy of those at the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) and other designers who saw the fundamental purpose of industrial design as an artistic, sculptural, and personal expression by an individual artist. This difference was the root cause of MoMA's criticism of streamlining, its general disregard of market value, and its dismissal of popular taste. It was why MoMA promoted product designs on the sole basis of aesthetic superiority, a quality judged by MoMA alone. This, of course, was the traditional mission of art museums for centuries——to educate the general public how to appreciate and understand the best of fine art. MoMA's acclaimed historical fountain of “good design," the Bauhaus, was founded with the legitimate objective of marrying art and industry. Now that this had been successfully accomplished in America, MoMA wanted to reseparate them into traditional, and unequal, categories of “good design” and “commercial design." Fortunately, the public would not recognize this elite distinction. In 1944, Paul Block, Jr., publisher of the Toledo (Ohio) Blade newspaper, had commissioned Norman Bel Geddes to prepare a study suggesting future developments for the city of Toledo, which had lost population and appeared to be in decline. It was to serve as a model for urban planning, not just for Toledo, but for other cities in Ohio as well. Bel Geddes was well known for his work in city and traffic planning, for his City of Tomorrow for Shell Oil in 1937, for his Futurama at the 1939 World's Fair, and for his 1940 book, Magic Motorways. Geddes was offered a fee of \$50,000 plus travel expenses to complete the exhibit within three to six months. 'The final cost would be closer to \$75,000. In response to his commission, Bel Geddes created Toledo Tomorrow, a giant model fiftynine feet in diameter depicting Toledo transformed by a major reorganization of the city's transportation infrastructure. At a scale of one-inch equals 100 feet, the model showed an area of about 150 square miles, with limited-access freeways crisscrossing the metropolitan area. A new passenger depot located near city center served air, rail, and bus travelers in a single location. The model was created in Bel Geddes's New York office in 47 panels, each four by six feet, and shipped to Toledo for public unveiling on July 4, 1945. Visitors walked around the model on an elevated walkway to simulate viewing the city from an altitude of 10,000 feet, and a special sound system provided audio descriptions at five points. The audio assured visitors that “if only a part of this master plan were brought to fruition, your Toledo would be a better city." As a dramatic effect toward the end of the visitors' walk, the lights were dimmed to suggest night, and special ultraviolet lamps were turned on to illuminate the model. Ultimately, while a few changes in the city resulted from the Bel Geddes model, they were not very noticeable, and the proposed central air-rail-bus terminal was never built.12 Also in 1945, Bel Geddes designed motorboats for Gar Wood Industries, luggage for Atlantic Products, and'model-photography stories for Encyclopaedia Britannia on the Great Pyramid and the Panama Canal. However, in 1946, the new management (Henry Ford II) engaged industrial design consultant George Walker to work on Ford's first new postwar design, which would become the 1949 Ford. Walker, who had designed Nash cars as a consultant since 1937, had hired Joe Oros (1917–2012) in 1940, and just recently, Elwood P. Engel (1917–1986) in 1946. Henry Ford II had invited both Gregorie and Walker to submit competing design proposals, and Ford chose one of Walker's submissions. Hibbard, an automotive designer since 1915, left General Motors to replace Gregorie as head of Ford styling and would also contribute to the design of the 1949 Ford. In 1946, Raymond Loewy Associates' (RLA) Chicago office, largely through the work of Richard Latham and working with Greyhound president Orville S. Caesar, developed a series of concepts for a 40-foot Greyhound double-decker Scenicruiser bus to provide a high view of scenic landscapes from a second-level dome, similar to those popularized by railroads for cross-country travel. After some years of development by the General Motors Truck and Coach Division, the final design based on RLA's concepts would be introduced in 1954. Over a thousand would be built over the following two years and would prove to be very popular with the traveling public. RLA also designed the most innovative and modern-looking postwar upright vacuum cleaner in 1946, the Magic Carpet S-2, for the Singer Company. Instead of the typical “"broom”- type single handle, the S-2 had twin chrome handles that spread apart as they descended to the cleaner head, straddling a flat, closed, plastic housing for a cord reel. The motor housing was no higher than a pack of cigarettes, accomplished by turning the motor on its side, which enabled the cleaner to go easily under furniture. The cleaner could also be hung on a closet wall, saving considerable storage space. That same year, Richard Latham of RLA designed logical layouts for “ham" shortwave radio receivers I-20 and No. S-38 for the Hallicrafters Company. Another diesel locomotive in 1946 came from Ray Patten, head of General Electric's Appearance Design division. Along with his assistant director, Gordon Florian, Patten designed passenger (PA) and freight (FA) versions of the American Locomotive Company (Alco) and GE diesel locomotives. Between 1940 and 1953, these companies jointly made and marketed large road locomotives. Patten described the design as "a locomotive so distinctive and powerful looking that it actually helps railroads sell their services to passengers and shippers." By 1946, Patten's design group at GE numbered 50 people, including 15 to 18 model makers.1⁴ Gordan Florian soon left GE to open his own design office in Bridgeport. Otto Kuhler had designed more locomotives and railroad cars than Raymond Loewy, Paul Cret, and Henry Dreyfuss combined. But after the war began, his German accent became a liability, and he abandoned freelance work. In 1944 he worked for the American Car and Foundry, developing double-deck sleepers and subway cars, but was laid off in 1947. He retired to a ranch near Pine, Colorado, where he painted western scenes, exhibited them in museums, and raised cattle. After the war in 1945, Teague, like Loewy, changed his sole proprietorship to a partnership, allowing senior staff, including C. Stowe Myers, with Teague since 1934, and Frank Del Guidice (1916-1993), who later became the company's president, to be partners in Walter Dorwin Teague Associates (WDTA). Del Guidice represented WDTA in seeking commissions from the Boeing Company, which met with success, and WDTA established a branch office in Seattle, Washington. Teague established an engineering division to handle the complexity of the projects at Boeing. They installed vinyl-clad panels for an easy-to-clean surface, introduced pleasing colors, and added cabin lighting as well as call buttons for flight attendants and overhead sections with individual reading lights and oxygen masks, all standard features of today's planes. The result was called the Stratocruiser, introduced to commercial service in 1946. Brooks Stevens designed Ben Hur, the first home food freezer, which was blue, unlike all previous white home appliances. George A. Beck became manager of industrial design in General Electric's new electronics department in Syracuse New York. Donald Deskey in New York expanded his firm, adding architectural, product design, and packaging departments. In 1945, Henry Dreyfuss had moved to California and opened a second office near his home in Pasadena. His office in New York, managed by a partner, William F.H. Purcell, and an associate, Robert H. Hose (1915–1977), both hired in 1946, designed RCA radios, televisions, and phonographs in 1946. In California, Dreyfuss's new client, Convair in San Diego, was seeking entry into the postwar aviation boom with a flying car developed by Theodore P. Hall, Convair's chief engineer. Styled by Henry Dreyfuss, it would be introduced in 1947. The Model 118 ConVairCar was a four-seat, two-door car with an aircraft engine and wings attached to its roof. Convair planned to build 160,000 of them, costing \$1,500 each, and rent them at airports. The first prototype, Model 116, made 66 successful flights, but Model 118 crashed during a demonstration flight, injuring the pilot. The pilot had mistaken the auto fuel gauge for the aircraft's fuel gauge and ran out of fuel in midflight, which is what happened to Convair's plans. Most of the automobile industry was flying, not to get in the air, but to get back to making cars for a market that was desperately in need. Millions of prewar cars were at the end of their life span, with rust, worn-out parts, or dated looks.
