In the ad “Bauer Beton," for the Bauer Type Foundry, Brodovitch once again utilizes his favorite tool from Paris, the airbrush. Fizzing across a skeletal illustration of a crane, the speckled paint from the airbrush provides a fluid contrast to the stark angularity of the drawing. Where the paint crosses the illustration we see a shift from positive to negative in the crane's structure that is reminiscent of his Bal Banal poster. Finally, with the crane actually lifting the letter “A" out of the word “Bauer," Brodovitch is once again making explicit his interest in the interaction of type, illustration, design, and the ideas of the machine age. With all these designs, we are witness to the fact that Brodovitch was one of the earliest translators of modernist ideas into the American popular culture.

Recognized for the exceptional work he had performed for Pléiade and Blackmore Press in Paris and London, Brodovitch was also invited to design several book covers during his first decade in America. These included Christopher Bush's publication Dead Man's Music (1932), Carolyn Wells's Fuller's Earth (1932), André Steeman's The Night of the 12th-13th (c. 1933), Tom Maloney's 3 Poems (c. 1935), and The Jazz Record Book by C. E. Smith (1942). Of these designs, Dead Man's Music is the most graphically inventive. Similar to the design “Maximum Payload" Brodovitch utilizes an “X-ray" style, this time of a man's hand.

Brodovitch designed numerous book covers during his first decade in the United States, using type, illustration, and airbrush to create the illusion of depth and narrative.

It is a technique that creates the illusion of a three-dimensional narrative within the two-dimensional graphic space. The transparent hand provides a sense of depth above and below the illustration. This depth enables a series of associations to be made between the different designs. The piano keyboard trapping the skeletal hand, which in turn is positioned over a grave-like outline of a piano, perfectly illustrates the book's promotional slogan: "The clue was music. The crime was murder." We can see this tool utilized in many other Brodovitch designs, including the New Jersey Zinc advertisement. Achieving this construction of a narrative flow through a two-dimensional space was something Brodovitch would push to its logical extreme on the pages of Harper's Bazaar

## Harper’s Bazaar

Brodovitch's contribution to the commercial arts did not go unrecognized. From 1931 to 1935 he was awarded various medals, honorable mentions, and prizes for his advertising work by the Art Directors Club of New York. Of these awards, his designs for Steinway and Climax Molybdenum were singled out for widespread recognition. The members of the club were so impressed by this work that in 1934 they asked Brodovitch to design their “13th Annual Art Directors Exhibition"on the thirty-fourth floor of the Rockefeller Center, New York. As with his other design work, Brodovitch enlisted the-help of his students to construct the exhibition.

The photographer Ralph Steiner had been attracted to the language of graphic design since the early twenties. His work had been featured in the 1927 "Machine Age Exposition," where he had also been a member of the artists board. Steiner photographs of billboards and posters had received much praise and in the early thirties he was regularly invited to photograph shoes and other items for the accessory pages of Harper's Bazaar. Because of his interest in modern design, Steiner was obviously aware of Brodovitch's work for Steinway and Climax Molybdenum. Recognizing the potential of Brodovitch's work for Bazaar, he invited its editor-in-chief, Carmel Snow, to the art directors exhibition. Steiner introduced Brodovitch as a designer dedicated to the values of originality and innovation. Snow's now famous response illustrates her concurrence with that characterization:

I saw a fresh, new conception of layout technique that struck me like a revelation: pages that led beautifully, cropped photographs, typography and design that were bold and arresting. Within ten minutes I had asked Brodovitch to have cocktails with me, and that evening I signed him to a provisional contract as art director.24

The confirmation of this offer was, however, dependent on the approval of the owner of Harper's Bazaar, William Randolph Hearst. To attain this, Snow asked Brodovitch to put together a dummy issue of the magazine, which she would then take to

Hearst's castle in Wales. Brodovitch hurried back to Philadelphia where he once again requisitioned a group of students to assist him in the creation of dummy Bazaar layouts. The students who assisted him in the Van Pelt Street studio were Raymond Ballinger, Nelson Gruppo, Susan Campbell, Roy Faulconer, and Mary Fullerton. These designs are now lost, but a description of their creation offers an insight into their possible appearance. Brodovitch and his students produced two "dummy magazines, composed of double-page spreads in categories of fiction, fashion photography, fashion drawing, accessories, and cosmetics."25Obviously aware of the established content of magazines such as Vogue, they incorporated all the habitual features on bags, shoes, and jewelry. For Brodovitch, however, the repetitive presentation of skirt lengths and different colored gloves had, in some way, to be reinvigorated. Each design had to be fresh. Searching for “shock value" on every page, Brodovitch constantly referred to those artists who influenced his formative years in Paris. He would encourage his students to “appropriate' a particular style, with statements such as “Do it in the manner of Cocteau, but do not imitate Cocteau."26 They worked on the layouts for over seventeen days.27 Flitting from his flat upstairs to the studio below, Brodovitch and his students cut “paper dolls out of patterned paper, or illustrat[ed] perfume bottles to look like high key photography - whatever was unlike other fashion magazines was tried."28

William Randolph Hearst was notoriously conservative, with his views on design being no less conventional. Personally accompanying the layouts to Wales, Carmel Snow obviously recognized the importance, for the future of the magazine, of having Brodovitch on board. When Hearst saw Brodovitch's designs the “layouts must have seemed brash, unruly, and foreign, being imbued with the strange flavor of the European avantgarde."29 Nevertheless, it seems in matters of design Hearst valued Snow's opinion above his own, since, albeit grudgingly, he said to Snow: “Well, if you want this man, go ahead and get him."30 To one contemporary of Brodovitch's it was “sheer

Another notable commission that resulted from one of Brodovitch's yearly trips to France was his hiring of famous posterist A. M. Cassandre. Born three years after Brodovitch, in 1901, Cassandre was a fellow Russian émigré from Kharkov who had migrated to Paris in 1915 to study at the Ecole des Beaux Arts. His posters began to appear around Paris in 1923, around the time of Brodovitch's 1924 Bal Banal illustration. Cassandre's advertising work had an immediate impact on Paris and obviously influenced the young Brodovitch. Prior to Brodovitch's arrival at Harper's Bazaar in 1934, the established cover illustrator for the magazine had been Erté. Supplying a steady diet of Art Nouveau imagery, Erté had been illustrating Bazaar for nearly two decades, his designs changing little over this period. In 1937, it was left to Carmel Snow to carry out the long overdue termination of Erté's contract. In his autobiography Things I Remember, Erté notes that he effected the impending dismissal when he admitted to himself that what had once been a “challenging and rewarding arrangement had ended by becoming a frustrating chore."35

Cassandre's first cover for Bazaar was the March 1937 issue “The Key to the Paris Openings." He would continue to illustrate

Unlike his famous posters L'Intrans (1925), Nord Express (1927), and L'Atlantique (1931), Cassandre's work for Bazaar contains little of the Art Deco style he became celebrated for in Paris. The chief influence on his Bazaar work seems to come from the imagery of Salvador Dali. Like Dali's landscapes, Cassandre's illustrations contain a broad range of flaccid shapes and biomorphic blobs that are set in an environment of deep perspectives. Many of his best designs are announcements of Paris openings, signifying this subject through a dreamlike assortment of opening doors, keys, eyes, and zips. In the March 1937 issue we can also see one of Cassandre's first ventures away from the poster. In this issue Brodovitch commissioned several artists to design hat boxes. Cassandre's design maintains a surrealist theme with several elongated fish encircling the box. Brodovitch also created a hat box for this issue. His design is an illustration of a woman's hand, duplicated many times. Always fond of recycling his own work, Brodovitch utilized the hand motif in various other designs during the 1936-37 period. It can be seen in one of his infrequent Bazaar cover designs (January 1936), in a poster he created for the Pennsylvania State Police, and for the cover of the 1937 “New PosterInternational Exposition" catalogue - an exhibition that also featured the work of Cassandre.
