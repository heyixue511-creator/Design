While these ideological underpinnings would likely have appealed to Otto Neurath (who had played an important role in the council movement in Munich),³³ it was, above all, the implications of figurative constructivism's visual language for the design of standardized pictograms that were paramount in Neurath's decision to recruit Arntz for the Vienna Method project. Arntz's formal approach already corresponded to Neurath's demand that educational pictures eliminate extraneous detail and provide the most important information within the viewer's first few glances at the picture.³⁴ Furthermore, the duplication of forms and their ordered arrangement in rows and columns – features characteristic of Vienna Method charts – were already recurring motifs in Arntz's own woodcuts 35

The critic, photographer, and art historian, Franz Roh (a long-time friend of Neurath), was the first, in 1926, to suggest the suitability and potential application of Arntz's work to the Vienna Method of Pictorial Statistics, and appears to have arranged the first meeting between them. Neurath had come to Düsseldorf that year to exhibit charts in the Austrian pavilion at the ‘Gesolei' exhibition,3⁶ though he was not yet satisfied with the graphic solutions that had so far been developed. Roh was in agreement and, in an article for Das Kunstblatt, suggested Arntz's stylistic approach as a potential improvement:

Where the necessity arises to find somebody capable of depicting coloured graphic symbols for industry or of presenting that new vivid form of statistics, as the hall of Austrian economy at ‘Gesolei' aspires to do with sensuous yet not always irreproachable stylization, one should consider this still young man.³7

Arntz remained in Düsseldorf for an additional two years after this initial meeting, collaborating on pictogram design with Neurath by post. Finally, in September of 1928 Arntz moved to Vienna to begin work at the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum – initially on a temporary basis – and was hired the following January for a permanent appointment as director of the Museum's graphic department. Alma and Tschinkel, both of whom had only come into the Progressives' circle in the previous year, joined the team in Vienna the following summer on Arntz's recommendation.³⁸

Even before Arntz's arrival in Vienna, however, the design of pictograms at the Museum began to conform to graphic conventions derived from his woodcut prints, which Neurath had begun collecting and making available at the Museum since their first meeting.³º Beyond his contribution to exhibition charts and publications, Arntz's characteristic pictorial style also reached the Viennese public through the posters, models, and large-scale wall paintings that he produced for the Museum after his arrival in 1928. In his triptych depicting the city's newly constructed Amalienbad swimming hall, as well as in a Vienna cityscape (titled ‘Sozialarbeit der Stadt Wien’), Arntz imagined Viennese society as a continuous architectural cross-section (see pp. 232-3 below).4º This device (first developed in woodcuts such as Ruhe und Ordnung'; p. 224 above) allowed Arntz to suggest a complex set of relationships by bringing together a diverse group of social settings and contexts within a single pictorial framework.

Alongside the cross-section presentation, the standardization of figural types represents another point of overlap between figurative constructivism and pictorial statistics. The three figures at the left side of Sozialarbeit der Stadt Wien', for example, function in a manner similar to statistical pictograms, indicating social class through variations in their respective uniforms: the worker on the left, his arm outstretched and holding a single coin, is unadorned; in the middle stands a figure in identical pose, but adorned with a bowler hat and suit jacket – a petit bourgeois – his arm in the same position but holding a mediumsized money bag; the third figure (on the right) retains the same pose and proportions but, in addition to the suit jacket, wears a vest and top hat, and holds a larger money bag.4 Thus, through minimal variation, these figures were able to suggest a wide range of meanings.

In depicting themes relevant to the Museum's work, and in making reference to the programmes of the Viennese municipal government (which largely financed the Museum's projects), Arntz's paintings served to link figurative constructivism with both the Museum and the socially progressive agenda of the city of Vienna. It should be noted, however, that there were also significant differences between the position taken by Arntz and the other Progressives, who expressed support for the revolutionary workers' council movement, and that of the Viennese Social Democrats, who advocated a program of 'revolution through reform'. Nonetheless, the social-educational mission of the Museum itself, and its origins in Neurath's own efforts to promote self-management programs among the Viennese working class, had great appeal for Arntz and the Progressives.⁴²

In addition to artworks by Arntz, Neurath also collected graphic work by other Progressives, including Alma, Tschinkel, and Seiwert. As an expression of the themes that these artworks shared with the Museum's statistical presentations, Neurath and several members of the Progressives circle began at this time to describe figurative-constructivist prints and drawings as sociological graphics.'4³ In several articles Neurath praised the Progressives for their collective vision, and on occasion used the Museum as a venue to promote their work.⁴⁴

Likewise, the artists viewed their relationship with the Museum as an opportunity to publicize and exhibit their work, and reach a still greater audience. Tschinkel, for example, in an article about the Museum that appeared in a 1929 issue of the Czech arts journal Výtvarné Snahy (Artistic endeavors), made a point of mentioning the Museum's 'collection of sociological graphics, from which exhibitions are occasionally staged'.4s This collection was drawn on extensively in the following year, when the Museum contributed several print series from its holdings to a major exhibition of international contemporary art, 'Die Kunst in unserer Zeit' (Art in our time), held from March to May 193o at the Künstlerhaus in Vienna, and organized by the Gesellschaft zur Förderung moderner Kunst in Wien (Society for the promotion of modern art in Vienna).“

Perhaps the most significant articulation of the partnership between the Progressives and the Museum came in the form of an announcement that appeared in the November 193o issue of A bis Z, describing the creation of a new 'Department of sociological graphics' at the Museum, with the goal of 'connecting the Museum's work to the most progressive formal and critical artistic creation of our time'.47 While this department was likely a relatively informal affair, the announcement suggests that the relationship between the Progressives and the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum was soon conceived in broader terms than just the design of charts and pictograms – even if this remained the primary and most enduring aspect of their collaboration.
