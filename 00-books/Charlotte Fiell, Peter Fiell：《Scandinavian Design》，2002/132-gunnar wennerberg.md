Gunnar Wennerberg, Iris vase for Gustavsberg, 1897 ![](images/947bdc190bc22b1df7ae8a81935c137aa8f7bc0a8fed25fc96a50473783fb5bd.jpg) ▶ Gunnar Wennerberg, Gullviva and Liljekonvalje (Cowslip and Lily-of-the-Valley) plates for Gustavsberg, 1897 # gunnar wennerberg (b. 1863 Skara, Sweden – d. 1914 Paris, France) ![](images/ca407743236a47d3b5f5db9b455fe3e36d69328a767d720f9f705485fe9b6b17.jpg) The Swedish painter, ceramics, glass and textile designer Gunnar Wennerberg studied painting under François Bonnat (1811–1894) and Henri Gervex (1852-1929)in Paris from 1886, and also trained at the Sèvres Porcelain Factory. On returning to Sweden in 1895 he was appointed art director of the Gustavsberg ceramics factory. During his thirteen-year tenure there, Wennerberg introduced a new stylistic direction to the company that drew inspiration from the natural world. As a gifted painter of flowers, Wennerberg produced a number of exquisite designs that were decorated with Art Nouveau style floral motifs. HisGullviva (Cowslip) and Liljekonvalje (Lily-of-the-Valley) plates were meticulously hand-painted in soft natural colours with stylized representations of wild flowers native to Sweden. These designs were displayed at the 1897 Stockholm “Konst- och Industriutställningen”(Arts and IndustryExhibition) and revealed not only the growing influence of the “New Art”, but also the respect with which nature was commonly heldin Sweden: while the flowers Wennerberg depicted were stylized, they did not display the frenzied swirling and almost tortured quality of similar floral motifs found in French Art Nouveau or German Jugendstil designs. Wennerberg also executed a number of designs that incorporated high-relief modelling, such as his Iris vase of 1897. Between 1898 and 1909 he also designed cameo glassware for the Kosta factory, which bore more than a passing resemblance to designs byÉmile Gallé(1846–1904), but the floralmotifs Wennerberg embellished these with were treated in a much more naturalistic way. From 1902 to 1908 Wennerberg taught painting at the Konstfackskolan (University College of Arts, Crafts and Design), and from 1908 he designed ceramics for the Sèvres Porcelain Factory and woven textiles for Handarbetets Vänner. He returned to Paris in 1909 and lived thereuntil his death in 1914. As one of the leading Swedish designers at the turn of the century, Wennerberg's simplified and poetic interpretation of the Art Nouveau style was of much influence in Scandinavia. ![](images/a41af80cfb34a8dd18e4336d1cdbf9515729a83bdc6c7d08b2caf3b580b7cf17.jpg)
