And rigorous ![](images/d0e4108b9d1ab16f6cc2c525614f7fe786c216880d6ca458bb916fcdaa031e1c.jpg) ## PAULA SCHER b.1948 DESIGNER, EDUCATOR, AND PENTAGRAM PARTNER WHO USED TYPE FOR DECORATIVE IMPACT. As the first female member of the internationally known firm Pentagram, Paula Scher has broken new ground in more ways than one, creating memorable brand identities for major corporations like Coca-Cola and institutions including the Museum of Modern Art and the New York Public Theater, as well as teaching graphic design and, in her spare time, painting intricate map-and-text artworks that have been exhibited internationally. Scher was born in Virginia and grew up knowing she wanted to be an artist, at a time when graphic design was barely identified as a profession beyond basic commercial art. She took a traditional BFA at the Tyler School of Art in Philadelphia, graduating in i97o and moving to New York to begin her career. She worked in the children's book division of Random House but, like several others who found their métier in graphic design, was given creative opportunities in the flourishing music industry. After working for CBS Records, she was hired by Atlantic Records in 1974 as an art director and began designing album covers. At a time when pop music was flourishing and MTV didn't exist, covers made the difference between a hit and a flop, and Scher's assertive designs were an immediate success. Hired back by CBS, she designed ı5o albums a year over an eight-year period—an impressive output by any standard, and a well-received one as well; several of her designs won Grammy nominations. Scher's graphics introduced a variety of historical typefaces rather than the standard modern forms then popular. Her bold vocabulary, inspired in part by Russian Constructivism and Art Deco precedents, used contrasting typefaces, often set along the diagonal. She was a pioneer in a new generation of graphic designers who used type for its decorative value as well as for communication. After leaving CBS to establish her own practice, Scher went into partnership in 1984 with Terry Koppel, taking on a variety of assignments that included advertisements, packaging, and book jackets. That year, she designed a now celebrated poster for Swatch, inspired by the work of Herbert Matter, the Swiss designer who became art director of Knoll\*. She also contributed frequently to Print magazine.
