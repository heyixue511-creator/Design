![](images/e96c546fac6806319bcc66222b82abb21e22da3933c331a687fafe82002548aa.jpg) Ewald Dahlskog, wine glasses for Kosta, 1927 # ewald dahlskog (Swedsh 1811950) Ewald Dahlskog studied at the Centraltryckeriet (Central Printing Office), Stockholm (1905–1908), and later at the Kungliga Tekniska Högskolan (Royal Institute of Technology). Stockholm (1908–1912), and the Kungliga Konsthögskolan (Royal University College of Fine Arts), Stockholm (1913–1917). Around 1920 he took several study trips abroad including visits to France, Italy and Tunisia, and fror 1924 to 1926 he lived in Paris as a freelance painter, illustrator and journalist, On his return to Sweden in 1926 he began producing designs for the Kosta glassworks, such as his stemmed cutglass bowl with highly stylized motifs of minotaurs and palm trees, which showed the influence of the Art Deco style. The following year he designed a range of wine glasses with inverted cone bowls and faceted stems that had a more modern aesthetic, as well as several free-blown vases and a decanter that were vernacular in style. Dahlskog also received widespread recognition for his marquetry panel designs, particularly for those he designed in 1926 for the Stockholms Konsterthus (Stockholm Concert Hall) which were adorned with stylized scenes of shepherds and soldiers. Three years later, Dahlskog began designing ceramics for Bobergs Fajansfabrik in Gefle, including his wellknown series of faïence flower vases and garden urns. The bold geometric forms of these ribbed vessels signified the increasing influence of the Modern Movement on Scandinavian design – having been exhibited at the landmark 1930 Stockholm Exhibition they were described as "functionalist” in the Swedish press. But while these moulded earthenware pots did reveal the formal influence of the Modern Movement, their chunky robustness owed more to the strong craft heritage in Scandinavia – a synthesis of the contemporary with the traditional that is so often at the heart of Scandinavian desian. Dahlskog continued designing for Bobergs Fajansfabrik until his death in 1950. ![](images/4af76628e22fa6e8b19b897c826f116a9bd5ac0a4ad2c2b758424844f92205bf.jpg)
