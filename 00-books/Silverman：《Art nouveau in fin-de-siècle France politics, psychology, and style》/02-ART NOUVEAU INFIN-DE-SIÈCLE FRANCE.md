# ART NOUVEAU IN FIN-DE-SIÈCLE FRANCE

## POLITICS, PSYCHOLOGY, AND STYLE

AT THE PARIs ExPOSITION of 1889, the French proclaimed the wrought-iron colossus of the Eiffel Tower an art nouveau. By 19oo, however, they assigned that term to the decorative art of intimate, luxurious interiors. In this imaginative and innovative work, Debora Silverman explores the shift in the locus of modernity in fin-de-siècle France from technological monument to private interior. What assumptions about modern life were expressed in an art of organic, irrepressibly curving forms? And why were these assumptions so compelling at the century's end?

Silverman addresses these questions in a multidimensional analysis, exploring the political, economic, social, intellectual, and artistic factors specific to the French fin de siècle that interacted in the development of art nouveau. She uncovers in the connections between contested republican liberalism, intensified economic competition, the rise of feminism, and the discovery of the psychological interior the motives and meaning for a modern style conceived as a nature style of interior decoration. Drawing on a wide range of sources, she discusses art nouveau as part of a broader institutional initiative for French design reform led by the Central Union of the Decorative Arts. In this union republican politicians, museum curators, aristocratic collectors, and luxury artisans became allies in a quest for stylistic innovation that was based on the rediscovery of the eighteenth-century rococo arts
