not get quickly to the meaning, which in itself provides enough interest?

There is a Social and Economic Museum in Vienna that consists almost entirely of pictures made with the 'Vienna Method'. Here each individual picture is not a curiosity, rather a part of the whole. The knowledge that this Museum mediates is always assumed as common knowledge but it is rarely made known to ordinary people, and in other respects it is difficult to make it known to them: the face' of our environment (division of labour, land use, population density, and a thousand other things that lend a country its character or its appearance), which we all know in isolated, incomplete facets but can never completely know from personal experience. The newspapers, for example, report little about this face'; they explain more about its contortions and scars. In order to understand the reports in the newspapers, each reader must have access to an overview, which, without requiring special effort, informs them of the essentials about lands and peoples. The goal of this Museum is a kind of statistical travelogue in pictures, of which the tendency is indicated by the example above. The Vienna Method has worked out statistical visualization as a strictly systematic hieroglyphic script for the sake of rapid orientation.

Picture script has swiftly conquered wide areas during recent years: major exhibitions cannot do without it any more; nutritional information, charts about the spread of broadcasting, growth in branches of industry, ways of employing the earth's surface, population statistics – all of these can be shown in a clear and easily comprehensible form with it. Increasingly, picture script becomes a framework for exhibitions, around which everything else can be organized.

# Social and economic museum of Vienna, 1925-1934

## FOUNDING

The Österreichische Verband für Siedlungs- und Kleingartenwesen (Austrian association for settlement and allotment gardens), General Secretary Dr Otto Neurath, had a permanent exhibition, 'Museum für Siedlung und Städtebau' (Museum for settlement and town-planning), Parkring 12; the material originated from an open-air exhibition in the Rathauspark, 1923.

Otober 1924: Neurath led city councillor Hugo Breitner through the museum and suggested the founding and creation of a Gesellschaftsund Wirtschaftsmuseum (Social and economic museum). Before the end of the year the district council decided to support and subsidize the association of the 'Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in Wien'.

Work began on 1.1.1925.

COLLEAGUES Otto Neurath (Director); other employees: Josef Jodlbauer (administration), Marie Reidemeister (office work, practice in handling statistics, under Neurath's direction). The work in graphic communication carried out by Otto Neurath and his associates – now commonly known as Isotype – was conceived and developed in the 192os as 'the Vienna Method of Pictorial Statistics'. This approach to designing information had from its inception the power to grow and spread internationally. Political developments in Europe played their part in its development, and production moved to the Netherlands (1934) and to England (194o), where the Isotype Institute continued to produce work until 1971.

The Austrian, Dutch, and English years of Isotype are described here freshly and extensively. There are chapters on the notable extensions of Isotype to the ussR, the usA, and Africa. Isotype work in film and in designing for children is fully documented and discussed.

Bringing together the latest research, and based on a wealth of archival material, this book is the first comprehensive, detailed account of its subject.
