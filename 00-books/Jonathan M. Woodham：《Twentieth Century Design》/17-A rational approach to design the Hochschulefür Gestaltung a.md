# A rational approach to design: the Hochschule für Gestaltung at UIm: 1953–1969 Penny Sparke suggested in her book Consultant Design: While Germany sells design in the name of science, Italy in the name of art, Scandinavia in the name of craft and the USA in the name of business, all these national images of design were necessary strategies in the highly competitive markets of the immediate post war years. The role of the designer was to help develop a marketing strategy which would give his product a special place in that market.18 The scientific, rational, and efficient outlook associated with German design in the later 195os, as typified in the austere aesthetic of Braun products of the period, was very much boundup with the innovative curriculum at the Hochschule für Gestaltung at Ulm (HfG). Inge Scholl, whose brother and sister had been executed by the Nazis in 1943, wanted to establish a new international school of design as part of an express commitment to social and moral reconstruction in the postwar years. In fact many German universities had shown considerable continuity in personnel and outlook with the prewar years, and so the envisaged school was intended to reflect a distinctive alternative personality. Scholl established a foundation in 195o in order to bring the school into being, and funding was also secured from the US government, German federal and regional government, and the business sector in the Federal Republic. Opening its doors in 1953 as a private institution, and thus free from the constraints of the local and national education system, its original impetus was focused on the idea of a new Bauhaus: visiting teachers included Johannes Itten, Josef Albers, and Mies van der Rohe, all of whom had contributed to the original Bauhaus ethos in the 192os. In 1955 the HfG moved into new buildings designed by Max Bill, who had been installed as director in the previous year. Students and staff were involved in their construction and furnishing, much as the Bauhaus students had been under Gropius at Dessau in the mid-192os. However, within a short space of time ideological differences between Bill and several of his younger colleagues began to emerge. These centred on their strong desire to emphasize the theoretical dimensions of the curriculum at the expense of the studio- and workshop-based studies upon which Bill placed a much higher priority. He resigned as director in 1956, and the running of the school was placed in the hands of a rectoral board of five (later three) members, renewable on an annual basis. From then there was an increasing emphasis on science and other disciplines which cast a fresh perspective on design thinking and practice, leading to Bill's resignation from the school in 1957. His departure was also exacerbated by personal as well as curricular conflicts with Tomás Maldonado who had joined the staff in 1956. Maldonado played a key role in theoretical debates, particularly through his many contributions to, and editorship of, the HfG's magazine Ulm, which was published between 1958 and 1959 and from 1962 to 1968. Other HfG theorists included Claude Schnaidt and Giu Bonsiepe who, following the closure of the school, became an important figure in South American design. Richard Hamilton, who had visited the HfG in 1959, put into words the distinctiveness of the institution thus: The major difference between the HfG and any other school of design in the world is precisely in the rejection ofthose principles which gave the Bauhaus its meaning and almost every school now available for the design student.19 By the later 195os mathematical, statistical, and analytical methods, together with sociology, anthropology, physical and behavioural psychology, and twentieth-century culturalhistory, were considered sufficiently important elements to rank alongside the more practical aspects of the curriculum. In the early 196os methodology and theory assumed a dominant role in many areas of work, creating further tensions with those who placed a greater priority on the practical elements. This state of relative ideological confusion led to the creation of a new constitution for the school in 1962, when once more its direction fell under the control of a single person, initially Otl Aicher. It was also in this period that the radicalism of the institution came under attack in the press, accompanied by a funding crisis. Faced with loss of status and autonomy the staff and students at the Hochschule chose to close the institution in 1968. ## Interpreting the HfG Robin Kinross, in a very useful survey of literature20 on the HfG published in the 198os considers that it is perhaps too soon to be able to evaluate the institution's true significance. He drew a number of parallels between Ulm in the 198os and the position of the Bauhaus in the late 193os: most of those involved in both institutions were still alive, there was self-representing documentation in the form of the Ulm magazine and the Bauhaus Books, as well as a number of selfrepresenting exhibitions put on by members of both institutions. None the less it is quite clear that the HfG made a considerable impact on design education and led to a greater acceptance of designers in German industry as well as an expansion of design education.21 The institution's own links with industry were limited: the volumeof work from Braun, the electrical appliance manufacturer, was modest and other commissions, such as that for the corporate identity scheme for the West German national airline, Lufthansa, were sporadic. None the less, the HfG curriculum, with its balance of theory and practice, embraced a move away from intuition to method, from component to system [117], from product to process, and from the individual to an interdisciplinary design team as an appropriate means of solving problems. Such a definition of the designer's role anticipated the outlook of many of the larger consultancies which were to place an increasing premium on design management. ## Design methods The significance of design methods as a means of solving problems systematically emergedas an area of great interest and concern to the more progressive factions of the design profession from the early 196os. The first conference in the field, entitled ‘Systematic and Intuitive Methods in Engineering, Industrial Design, Architecture and Communications', was held at Imperial College, London, in 1962. Literature on the subject had begun to appear during the late 195os and 196os, with frequent articles in the COID periodical Design, the everincreasing publication of conference papers in Britain and the United States, and a proliferation of books. These included L. Bruce Archer's Systematic Method for Designers22 of 1965, revealing links with the HfG where Archer had taught industrial design before becoming a Research Fellow at the Royal College of Art [118].23 ## Ergonomics and anthropometrics: data for designers In the early 196os there was an increasing range of potential tools to aid the rationality of the decision-making process in design. As a result many designers were eager to reconcile emergent disciplines such as ergonomics, anthropometrics, cybernetics, marketing, and management science with design thinking. In such shifts away from the intuitive, micro- rather than macro-, means of problem-solving designers increasingly began to make use of interaction charts and matrices. Such a rational outlook had its roots in the application of scientific techniques known as OR (Operational Research) which had been used for problem-solving in the Second World War, having evolved alongside developments in ergonomics. Both British and American armed forces had produced extensive guides for their designers, with American versions being published for general use in 1954.24 Ergonomics, 'human engineering'as it was known in the United States, or'biotechnics'on the continent, was of considerable interest to design and industry around the world, resulting in a proliferation of societies, university courses, and international conferences within a short space of time.25The field of anthropometrics—the systematic study of measurement of the human form—was also of increasing interest and value to designers as was emphasized by Henry Dreyfuss ![](images/73d8a542076b0061712d6dab6dddf109fdf485298349860034048d26c2cc3ddd.jpg) ![](images/1da056d4e1dd2da1de9917a86234b1dcff7bda1e9d52bfc77901ab08f3a36ec8.jpg) ## 119 Henry Dreyfuss Joe and Josephine anthropometrics charts, 1960s Anthropometrics (the scientific measurement of human form) and ergonomics (the systematic analysis of work efficiency relating the user to his or her environment) became increasingly important tools for the designer after 1945. In some respects related to this growing concern for method and system in design was the emergent field of design management, which has been touched upon in relation to the increasing depth of expertise offered by consultancies. In Britain it was recognized by the Royal Society of Arts in its Presidential Awards for Design Management, which were initiated in 1965: Heals, Hille, London Transport, and Conran were among the early recipients. An early exponent in the field was Michael Farr, who set up his own design management consultancy, Michael Farr (Design Integration) Ltd., in Tottenham Court Road, London, writing Design Management28in 1966.
