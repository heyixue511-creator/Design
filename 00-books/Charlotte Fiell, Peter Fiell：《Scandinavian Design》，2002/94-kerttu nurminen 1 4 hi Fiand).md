![](images/8913a8f2bf9aecf7a42e99eaf3bf40650f112bb75ab52e5b8e3f5cc5d8c12909.jpg) Kerttu Nurminen, Lumikuru (Snow Canyon) bowl for Nuutajärvi, 1985 # kerttu nurminen 1 4 hi,Filand) ![](images/57bfa7e9574d18e3fb4e661e0a00e439172c3ed3fc668cc0f6fed5bcdeca5390.jpg) The Finnish glass designer Kerttu Nurminen studied at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu (University of Art and Design) in Helsinki from 1966 to 1970. In 1972, she began designing glassware for the Nuutajärvi glassworks and received the Coburger Glass Prize in 1977. Later in 1985, Nurminen designed her colourless Lumikuru (Snow Canyon) range, which included various bowls and platters as well as a small fondue dish and an eggcup. This line of partly sandblasted pressed glassware was designed as a replacement for the Luna range (1971) that had been designed by Kaj Franck. For the Lumikuru glassware, which was intended for industrial mass production, Nurminen skillfully combined simple functional formswith the subtlest use of decoration – a simple band of glass contrasting with the almost velvety quality of the sandblasted finish. The range was also sold with a smooth rather than sandblasted finish under the name of Kuru (Canyon). After Nuutajärvi was merged with littala, Nurminen produced designs for both brands. For the Nuutajärvi Pro Arte collection she designed her well-known Lampi glasses and decanter (1993& 1995), which through their subtle bands of green and blue embodied “the lucidity of northern nature, water, sky, and the forested horizon”. Her slightly later Milla and Poppy glass sculptures were also part of the exclusive Pro Arte range of studio glass, but with their leaved stems and petal forms they were more literal and playful in their representation of nature and, like the flowers they portrayed, had a fragile fleeting quality. In contrast, Nurminen's production glassware for littala was more functional, while retaining a sense of originality and creativity. When launched in 1988, her industrially-producedMondo glassware brought Nurminen international recognition, and with its flame-like striations of fluid colour can be seen as an evolution of her earlier Lampi studio glass. With a “passion and unwillingness to compromise”, Nurminen brings the artistic sensibilities associated with studio glassmaking to the realm of industrialized factory production so as to realize unpretentious yet high-quality, democratic, and timeless objects – a quintessentially Scandinavian approach to industrial design. ![](images/5f1b29814cb501f001b4ec7e0c735545bc67d906e171c2ad1136d0e62dfcad27.jpg) ▶Kaj Franck, Prisma vases for Nuutajärvi, 1954 ▶> Kerttu Nurminen, Palazzo glass range from the Pro Arte collection for Nuutajärvi, 1998 - This collection pays hommage to the craftsmanship and traditions of Italian glass blowing, which has so inspired Finnish glass designers working at Nuutajärvi since the 1950s onwards ![](images/276ef0a0aa13af17ee780bc79130eb9564f9c9b5561f3da9bbbb268dedcc12c2.jpg) ## nuutajärv 1 l, Filand ![](images/42e618349f97abf3025d732a255ea27ac229353a744d5681f4d0b40549725b82.jpg) Nuutajärvi, Finland's oldest surviving glassworks, was founded by two Swedish entrepreneurs, Captain Jakob Wilhelm De Pont and Harald Furuhjelm, in 1793. Initially, the venture operated under the Swedish name Notsjö (later becoming known as Nuutajärvi Lasi), and during the early years it manufactured simple household glass, bottles and window panes. Around 1851, the factorywas purchased by Adolf Törngren, who subsequently modernized it by implementing more efficient methods of production. After touring a number of European glassmaking centres, he decided to introduce pressed glass processes to the manufacturing operation and also began using more refined raw materials so as to enhance the quality of the company's products. Additionally, Törngren recruited the French glass blower Charles Bredgem under whose direction the company began manufacturing both pressed and filigree glassware. TheNuutajärvi-Notsjöfactory went on to become one of the leading Nordic producers of glassware and in 1858 it opened its own sales outlet in Helsinki, which was named after, andrun bythe glassworks' accountant, G.F. Stockmann (now the largest and bestknown department store in Scandinavia). From 1862 to 1878Nuutajärvi also briefly manufactured ceramics. In the early 1900s, the company decided to acquire its own designs for contemporary glassware. To this end, it held two glass design competitions in 1905 and 1906 and subsequently received submissions from a number of Art Nouveau designers, including Valter Jung (1879–1946), Helena Wilenius, Carl Johan Boman(1883–1969), Gustaf Strengell (1878–1938), and Jussi Paatela. It was not until after theSecond World War, however, that the firm began manufacturing truly innovative production and studio glassware. Between 1946 and 1948, Gunnel Nyman designed a number of influential art glass pieces for the factory, such as herCalla vase(1946), which predicted the expressive natural forms of the later work of Timo Sarpaneva and Tapio Wirkkala. Often, Nyman would incorporate trapped air bubbles or swirls of opaque glass in her thick-walled crystal vessels, as in her Serpentiini (Serpentine)vase of 1947, or use simple organic forms withfaceted edges, as in her Facett 1 dish of 1941. After a devastating fire the glassworks was rebuilt in 1950 and acquired by the Wärtsilä group, which also owned the Arabia ceramics factory. The same year, the head of the utility ware design department at Arabia, Kaj Franck, was given the responsibility of re-designing Nuutajärvi's utility glassware products. The following year, he was appointed the design director of the glassworks, and under his careful guidance the company's reputation for creative excellence was solidified. Franck had previously designed glass for littala, but while there had been frustrated by the company's lack of technical experimentation. At Nuutajärvi, however, he was able to freely explore new techniques and coloured pigments and indulge his desire for greater creative expression. To this end, he recruited two Venetian glass blowers from Murano whose technical know-how and expertise greatly influenced the glassworks' output. Franck's colourfully striped “spun” plates, for example, show the unmistakable influence of Italian post-war glass. During the 1950s, Nuutajärvi set itself apartfrom the other two main Finnish glassworks, littala and Riihimäki, through its abundant use of bright colours and simple yet often expressive forms. In 1952, Saara Hopea became Franck's assistant and shortly thereafter designed her well-known stackable Model no. 1718 tumblers and Model no. 1716 pitcher (1953), which were highly functional yet radiated a lively aesthetic through their bright colours. During the 1950s, Franck revived the production of filigree glass and designed a number of rusticstyle pieces using bubbled glass. At Nuutajärvi, Franck was also able to pursue his interest in pressed utility glass, while enjoying the freedom of experimentation offered by the development of art glass pieces, such as his small bird sculptures. During the 1960s, the emphasis moved away from utility blown-glass towards high-quality pressed glass products, especially after the factory purchased new state-of-the-art pressing machinery in 1968. Oiva Toikka's Kastehelmi (Dewdrop) range eloquently testified to this change of direction, while his curious Lollipop sculptures and candlesticks denoted the factory's continued interest in producing éxpressive and boldly coloured art glass. In 1968, Heikki Orvola joined the glassworks and later executed a number of important designs, most notably his elegant Aurora glasses (1972). Around the same time, KerttuNurminen began designing both art and production glassware for Nuutajärvi, often using sandblasting techniques, asin her Lumikuru (Snow Canyon)range of 1985. In 1981, Nuutajärvi art glass began to be marketed under the Pro Arte trade name, and from 1988 to 1990 the glassworks was a subsidiary of littala. Like littala, Nuutajärvi became part of theHackman Group in 1990 and three years later, in recognition of the factory's bicentennial anniversary, a new range of art glass was introduced known as the Pro Arte collection. Today, Nuutajärvi is solely concerned with the small-scale production of exclusive yet exquisite art glass pieces designed by, among others, Sami Lahtinen (b. 1967), Tiina Nordström (b. 1957), MarkkuSalo (b. 1954), Rolf Svartström (b. 1966), HarriKoskinen, andOiva Toikka. The factory's output continues to exemplify the successful collaboration of talented designers and skilled craftsmen, which for decadeshas been such a defining feature of Finnish design.
