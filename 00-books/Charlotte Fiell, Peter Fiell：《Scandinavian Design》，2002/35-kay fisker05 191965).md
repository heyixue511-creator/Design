Michelsen, 1928-1929 & 1927 # kay fisker(Danish 193-1965) ![](images/fffa38bd12b7256e2aa9ecd00990f5a64fb9707feaa9643babc00a63d88b21b5.jpg) Kay Fisker worked for the Danish architect and designer Anton Rosen from1912 to 1916 before studying architecture at the Kongelige Danske Kunstakademi (Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts) inCopenhagen. Aftergraduating in 1920, Fisker established his own architecture practice and in 1921 was awarded a gold medal in Ghent for his work. Between 1919and1927 Fisker was also the editor of the journal Arkitekten, and from around1925 he began designing silverware for A. Michelsen in Copenhagen. As a memberof the younger generation of avant-garde designers in Denmark, Fisker set out to modernize Danish design by developing forms that were strongly geometric, such as those of his cocktail shakers from the late 1920s. Form and decoration were often utterly inseparable in his designs, as his bonbonnière for A. Michelsen(1926)or his beech wingback chair from the 1930s demonstrate. Fisker also designed book covers and exhibited his work at the seminal1925“Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes” in Paris. In 1928, he was awarded the prestigious Eckersberg Medal and later in 1947 he received the C.F. Hansen Medal. He became a professor at the Kunstakademiets Arkitektskole (Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts, school of architecture), Copenhagen, in 1936 andheld this position until 1963. His design work was the subject of several one-man shows held in Charlottenborg (1934 and 1953), Paris (1949), London (1950), and Århus (1953). Like many other Danish designers, Fisker's skill lay in his ability to update traditional forms by striking a delicate balance between historicism and outright Modernism. As one of the most influential architect-designers working in Denmark during the 1920s and 1930s, Fisker mastered a wide range of materials so as to produce not only luxury goods, such as his exquisite silver cocktail shakers, but also more mundane yet useful products, most notably his cast-iron stove that has an understated beauty derivedsolely from functional purity. ![](images/64c31c88012d004b50f5c3d926f502ce0c16c815fca7d1cd0852e8b2d87cd5b7.jpg) Kay Fisker, interior of the Danish pavillion at the 1925 “Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes" in Paris ∇4Kay Fisker, cast-iron stove for Støbejern, c. 1920 ∇ KayFisker, wingback chair (unknown maker), 1930s ▶ Kay Fisker, wine pitcher for A.
