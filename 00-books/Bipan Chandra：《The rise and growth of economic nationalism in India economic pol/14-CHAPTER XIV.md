# CHAPTER XIV

# Indian Political Economy

Theory is only enlarged Practice, Practice is Theory 'studied in its relation to proximate causes.

— JUSTICE RANADE

The given conditions of society and the prevailing conceptions regarding its future and contemporary interest, determined the economic ideas that received the approbation of thinkers and the acceptance of statesmen.

— G. SUBRAMANIYA IYER

During the period under study, some of the Indian national leaders evolved the concept of Indian Political Economy' which was fully worked out in a scientific form by Justice Ranade in his classic address on 'Indian Political Economy', delivered at the Deccan College, Poona, in 1892.1 His basic formulations are too well known in Indian economic literature to need an elaborate statement here.2 What is perhaps not so well known is the wide scale on which they were subscribed to in the ranks of the Indian national leadership. Ranade's views on the subject may be summarised as follows:

The claim that the postulates of Classical political economy were universally and absolutely and demonstrably true for all times and places and for all stages of economic development was far from true.3 Classical economics was based on assumptions which were not universally applicable; they had been born out of specific conditions of England; and, in fact, they were true of no existing society.4 In particular, they had hardly any validity in a backward, stagnant agricultural country like India where not competition but status and custom and tradition ruled.5 Secondly, the past practice of British statesmen and the contemporary practice of other nations did not at all conform to the precepts or assumptions of Classical economics.6 As a matter of fact, even in India the British did not strictly adhere to these precepts in giving guarantees to railways and help to plantation industries or in claiming state ownership over land or in trying to preserve through legislation petit culture by tenants.7 Thirdly, the scientific claims of Classical economics had been seriously questioned by European and American economists8 and were under attack even by several English economists;9 moreover, some of the Classical economists themselves had been at pains to avoid making claims of absoluteness and universality for their science.1o Fourthly, Classical economics might at the most explain static economic conditions but could hardly cope with the processes of dynamic economic development.11 Keeping all these factors in view, the conclusion was inescapable that the principles or laws of economics were not abstract and universal like those of physics and mathematics but were relative and historically conditioned and derived; they differed in time and space.1z Hence, the specific stage of economic development of a people and not abstract economic theories should be the decisive factor in determining economic policies for a country.

It may be noted that Ranade did not deny the value of economic theory or the validity of economics as a science.13 On the contrary, he specifically repudiated those who would reduce it to an Art, to 'the position of a rule of thumb'.14 He only wanted to make it more ‘subtle and scientific' by basing it on practice.15 He said that economics being a social science its principles were to be derived historically through the study of specific economic activities and not deductively so that they were closely linked with historical experience, practical observation, and social reality. Or, as D. G. Karve has put it, ‘a comprehensive, sociological and constructive view of economics was alone acceptable to Ranade'.i6 In 1881, Ranade had criticised English economists for looking at the question of Free Trade 'solely from the economical point of view' and ignoring 'the political and social elements .which enter into the question', and averred that the economic aspect ought to be subordinated to the higher interests and aspirations of a people ‘if political economy is to be anything more than schoolmen's metaphysics'. In fact, he pointed out, that is why one talked of ‘applied, i.e. political economy' and not of 'pure economics'.17 In this respect Ranade remained for once true to the practice of Classical economists and more often than not referred to economics as political economy.

In course of time Ranade's critique of Classical economics led to the foundation of the 'national' or 'Indian' school of political economy. This development was, we believe, in the main the product of two factors: (a) Ranade's conclusion derived from a belief in the relativity of economic doctrines, in their close dependence on social reality, and in their quality of reflecting economic practice.-that since India's economic interests and conditions differed from England's the principles of economics applicable to it must also be different;18 and (b) the acceptance of Ranade's premises and conclusions by a large number of other nationalist contemporaries.

Thus, as early as 1877, K. T. Telang quoted the English economist T. E. Cliffe-Leslie to show that the doctrines of political economy were not true of all countries alike.18a He also said that in deciding practical questions principles of economics should form only one of the considerations, other considerations of social and political welfare being equally important.19 In his paper on ‘The Poverty of India', Dadabhai argued that political economy might be ‘all right in a Nativegoverned country', but was not strictly applicable to a dependent country which had to make 'home remittances and charges'.2o Prithwis Chandra Ray too condemned any attempt 'to take the abstract principles of political economy and apply them cut and dried in a country, without considering its sociological conditions', and cited the experience of America, Australia, and the Continent of Europe as a proof of the error of the 'complacent dogmatism'of English economists.2i G. V. Joshi did not deal directly with economic theory, but his treatment of the questions of the role of the state, Free Trade, etc., clearly brings out his close adherence to the fundamental theoretical framework of Ranade.2z R.C. Dutt did not comment on economic theory; he did, however, notice that Ricardo, Cobden, Bright, and Robert Peel had ignored the heavy duties on Indian goods entering England at the very time when they were agitating for Free Trade in England. Nor did he fail to praise European economists for taking ‘a more unbiased view of the situation'and for unerringly pointing to the double standard practised by English statesmen at home and in India.23

G. Subramaniya Iyer dealt at length with problems of political economy in his work, Some Economic Aspects of British Rule in India, to which he attached a long Appendix on 'English Economists and India'. Even though he was obviously following in the footsteps of Ranade, his treatment of the question is historically significant. Dealing with the question of Free Trade, he pointed to the protectionist policies of Germany and the U.S.A. to show that economic policies of nations were determined by national needs and not by economic theories, and remarked that the theory of Free Trade suited England because of its peculiar conditions and that, on the other hand, Indian conditions resembled those of Russia and the U.S.A. in this respect.24 Coming to problems of theory, he said that Classical economics was 'one-sided and imperfect' and not applicable to other countries.25 Moreover, he remarked echoing Ranade —'the truths of economic science are not universally applicable, irrespective of particular stages of national growth and of peculiar environments and conditions.'z6 He also pointed out that European political economy had never stood still; it had undergone 'steady changes in its conceptions, theories, and practical application, since the dissolution of medieval times'.27 Adam Smith himself belonged to a particular point of time.28 Stressing the social origins of economic thought, G. S. Iyer wrote: 'The given conditions of society and the prevailing conceptions regarding its future and contemporary interest, determined the economic ideas that received the approbation of thinkers and the acceptance of statesmen.'It was therefore- clear to him that the principles of orthodox economics had to be modified when applied to India since 'the economic interests of India and the conditions under which they have to be safeguarded' were different from those of Europe and America.29 He also noted that the practice of the English in India provided many exceptions to their adherence to orthodox economics; unfortunately, however, he pointed out, such departures from dogma were made only 'to uphold and protect the interests of the ascendant class'.3o

The relativity of economic doctrines and of their application to the peculiar conditions of India was also sometimes asserted at the popular level of the press. Thus, for example, the Amrita Bazar Patrika had written, as early as 1 December 187o, that 'the condition of India is in many respects peculiar and the same law which holds good in other countries may not hold good here'. The Hindu of 7 December 1883 observed : 'In the application of the European economic laws to Eastern countries, the special conditions must be taken into consideration....Your political economy is not after all an end in itself.'The Mahratta of 2 April 1899 criticised Adam Smith and Cobden for trying to make of political economy a science like chemistry. They started out rightly, it said, with the practical question how to increase the wealth of England. Unfortunately, in their hands the question 'gradually lost its specific nature and the change from particular to the general was easy and swift'. In any case, it asserted, the economic condition of India, being different from that of England, 'calls out for the application of a different set of principles'. It further declared : 'India is not to be sold for the fads of economic doctrinaires'; and asked the Government of India to 'proclaim once for all that it is not going to borrow its economics from England'. In conclusion it pleaded for 'the formation of national economics'.31 Bipin Chandra Pal's New India also dilated, in its issue dated 21 April 19o2, on the relativity of economic doctrines on account of 'the wide divergence that exists between one people and another both in their social structures, and in their climatic and historic environments', and, pointing to the social roots of economic theory, observed that economic ideas were 'the result of particular industrial conditions, existing in any country or at any time, and must, therefore, vary from age to age, and between one country and another'.3² Many other nationalist leaders referred to the relativity of economic principles in the course of the agitation against the application of Free Trade principles to India.33

Most of the Indian leaders cited above made a distinct advance over Ranade's position in one respect. They were able to discern that it was in its political condition that India differed most not only from England but also from the other nations of Europe and America, for India's economy and finances were controlled by an alien power.34

## I. ROLE OF THE STATE

The laissez-faire theory of the functions of the state was one of the tenets of Classical economics that was challenged vigorously and consistently by the leading nationalist economists. They rejected the idea that the state should confine its activities to the maintenance of law and order35 and pleaded for the widening of the horizons of state activity so as to encompass all matters in which individual effort was likely to be less effective than national effort.36 The state should in this respect act as the collective organ of the national will for national purposes.37 They further maintained that in an economically backward country like India the obligation on the state to act as the guardian of national economic and industrial interests was even greater than in the advanced countries of the West, since it had to help people overcome inherited weaknesses, their own inertia and inferior competitive position vis-a-vis the powerful foreigner.38 G. V. Joshi, in particular, felt that it was the duty of the state in India to so regulate the inexorable working of economic laws of competition and control and guide the inevitable transition from handicrafts to modern industries as to minimise the resultant economic dislocation and misery.39 There was, however, some difference of opinion between Justice Ranade and G. V. Joshi regarding the limits to which state' intervention in economic affairs could go. Ranade opposed the setting of any theoretical limits and said that the national needs should be the only criterion. 'The question is one of time, fitness, and expediency, not one of liberty and rights', he said.4o G. V. Joshi, on the other hand, believed that the state action was an extraordinary measure and should, therefore, be hedged in with restrictions lest instead of aiding private effort it suppressed and even replaced it.41

In order to justify the right of the state to interfere in economic affairs, Ranade and G. Subramaniya Iyer also attacked the doctrine of the social supremacy of the ‘individual and his self-interest that lay at the heart of the concepts like the economic man' and self-regulating economy, and propounded in its place the doctrine of the primacy of collective welfare over individual interests. Ranade put this view as follows:

Modern Thought is nearing to the conclusion that the centre round which the Theory should revolve, that the true centre is the Body Politic of which that Individual is a Member, and that Collective Defence and Well-being, Social Education and Discipline, and the Duties, and not merely the Interests, of men, must be taken into account, if the Theory is not to be merely Utopian.42

Similarly, G. S. Iyer criticised Adam Smith for treating the individual ‘as a purely egoistical force, working uniformly in the direction of private gain' and not paying due regard to the fact that man should 'cultivate sympathy with his neighbours, with his nation, and with humanity at large'. Echoing the German historical school, he said :

...the immediate private interest of the separate members of the community will not lead to the highest good of the whole, and is frequently opposed to it....On the maintenance of the unity and progress of the nation depend the security, well-being and civilization of the individual, and private economic interests, like all others, must be subordinated to the completion and strengthening of the nationality.43

G. V. Joshi made the interesting point in this context that the philosophy of laissez faire had arisen in England as a reaction to the state helping one section of the community at the cost of another. He would concede that objection might be rightly taken where the state was found 'favouring a class and promoting only sectional, instead of national, interests'. But the case was quite different in India, where, he said, 'there is no such conflict of class-interests; the classes are not divided, as elsewhere, by hard lines'. Here the state was not being asked to help onę class against another nor to favour the capitalist class as such; the state was being asked ‘to help the entire community in its present helplessness'. All the classes of Indian society were suffering at the hands of competition by powerful foreigners; all of them were equally interested in the rapid economic development of the country.44

Basing themselves on the twin doctrines of the state's right to interfere in economic matters and the predominance of communal welfare over individual interest, the Indian leaders demanded that the Government of India should follow a policy of direct, deliberate, and systematic promotion of industrial development which was in their eyes the sine qua non of nation's economic welfare.45 Many of them also asked for various forms of state assistance to agriculture.46 Ranade applied this doctrine of the state being the guardian of the entire community to fields where there was need for ‘protection of the weak against the strong'.47 He supported legislation to protect tenants and to regulate money-lending.48 He also advocated a more equitable distribution of national wealth.49 Similarly, G. S. Iyer advocated state intervention in favour of the workers on the ground that the state had the responsibility of protecting the weaker side in the unequal struggle between labour and capital.5o It may, however, be repeated here that quite a few other Indians took strong objection to any state interference with 'the quiet working of private industrial enterprise'.51

A note of warning may be sounded at this stage. Care should be taken not to confuse the nationalist economists' theoretical defence of state interference in economic affairs and their demand for state aid to industry — which might in some cases even take the form of direct state enterprise —with a desire for socialism or even for a welfare state.52 Far from advocating socialism or even state capitalism, these economists merely wanted the state to provide an impetus to and give a helping hand in the transition of the agrarian economy towards industrial capitalism — all they desired was to induce the state to make up the economic deficiencies of private enterprise in a backward country. That this was the role assigned to state aid by Ranade is clearly brought out by a perusal of his essays on Netherlands India and the Culture System', 'Iron Industry — Pioneer Attempts', and 'Industrial Conference'.53 Light is also shed on his attitude by a little-known act of his in 1886. While he talked in general of producing ‘in State Factories all products of skill which the State Departments require in the way of Stores',s4 in the course of his minute of dissent to the Report of the Finance Committee of 1886 he strongly objected to the Committee's proposal for removal of restrictions on jail manufactures as it 'encourages the state officials with the help of public funds to bear down all private competition'. In particular, he criticised the utilisation of jails 'to drive steam machinery and work presses, and tents, and paper, and carpets with the help of State funds'. In other words, he objected to jails being used to manufacture goods which the Indian private enterprise also produced.s5

G. V. Joshi repudiated any suggestion of state socialism or state capitalism even more strongly and explicitly. Thus he wrote in 189o: 'Our scheme emphatically is not a scheme of State-socialism, such, for instance, as was attempted with fatal ill-success by the Provisional Government in 1848.'s6 Firstly, he repeatedly remarked that the only objective of state action was to redress the balance in the unequal struggle of the resourceless Indian entrepreneur with the powerful and go-ahead foreigner' so that the former was not nipped in the bud'.57 Secondly, he stressed that industrial .development was basically the function and prerogative of private effort, the state's role being temporary and limited to that of preparing the private entrepreneur to assume independent responsibility. As soon as the latter eventuality was realised the policy of state aid would lose its raison d'etre and would have to be given up. It would in fact be wrong at that stage for the state to try to monopolise the industrial field.58 Thirdly, apprehending that state aid might 'degenerate into aggressive State action, supplanting private effort', he proposed surrounding it with strict 'limitations of scope, direction, and time'.59 Lastly, he criticised the government for attempting in respect of irrigation, forest conservancy, and taqavi allowances 'to accomplish by its own agency the work which private native enterprise should be trained and encouraged and even subsidised if necessary to carry out'.6o

## II. LAND REVENUE: A TAX OR RENT?

Another field in which the Indian leaders questioned the official theoretical framework was that of land revenue administration. The main points of difference related to the nature of land revenue and the ultimate ownership of agricultural land. The dominant view, among the Indian administrators was that in India the state was, since times immemorial, the ultimate owner of the soil and the cultivators or other landholders were in essence its tenants; in consequence the land revenue paid by the landholders to the government was in the nature of rent and not a tax.61 The nationalist economists and other leaders repudiated this contention in toto. They said that the Indian landholder was as much a private owner of his land as a landholder in any other part of the world, and that to assert the contrary was to misinterpret the past and ignore even the principles underlying the existing land tenures. They therefore maintained that what the government charged as land revenue was a tax that it collected for its needs in the manner of governments in other countries.62 Interestingly enough, while the official spokesmen often stressed that in claiming to be the universal lords of the soil the British rulers were merely following the practice of the previous rulers of the land, G. V. Joshi insisted that the claim was put up because 'Western Theories are recklessly applied to the facts of Indian life in all their logical rigours'.63

Ranade and Joshi also attacked the Ricardian basis of the Indian systei of land revenue. Underlying the official ideas. and practice was the assumption that the tenant of the state or the zamindar in India was a capitalist farmer who paid economic rent,64 that is the price differential between the produce of superior land and the produce of the poorest land at the margin of cultivation on which the cost of production was just covered by its produce. The cost of cultivation under this definition covered the cultivator's labour costs as well as his capital costs at the prevailing rate of profit.65 It was clear that according to this theory of rent the state as the landlord in India could absorb the whole of this economic rent as land revenue without raising the cost of production and without affecting prices or wages which were determined by the cost of production on the marginal land.66 Ranade and Joshi did not criticise the Ricardian theory of rent as such, but they denied that it was valid in India. 'In classical political economy the rent of a capitalist farmer was determined by the competition of capital, the size of the population, and the varying fertility of the soil.'67 Ranade's objection was that, since the state had become the sole landlord in the country, the cultivator paid not a competitive but a monopoly rent which the government was in a position to pitch as high as it liked and which frequently encroached upon the profits and wages of the cultivator.68 Joshi's objection was even more fundamental. He pointed out that Ricardo's theory assumed the existence of capitalist farmers who just did not exist in India. The Indian cultivator, both in the zamindari and the ryotwari areas, was more in the nature of a cottier tenant than a capitalist farmer moved by considerations of economic profit. According to him, there were two basic factors in the situation : (a) the immediate object of the cultivator was sheer subsistence and not profit on capital invested; and (b) there was intense competition for land among the cultivators because of the grø ing population, gradual disappearance of cultivable waste, a 'failure of non-agricultural industries'. In consequence the ryot, driven by 'cruel economic necessity' and sooner than part with his only source of living—his patch of landand become a landless labourer, agreed to almost any terms imposed by :. state or the private landlord even if it meant living belr subsistence level.69 I9T

## II. FREE TRADE VS PROTECTION

m the beginning the nationalists were deeply interested in question of Free Trade vs Protection. As early as 1877, K. T. lang had remarked in the course of a long paper on 'Free 0 ade and Protection—from an Indian Point of View': 'It is subject so intimately connected with the whole future of our intry—no less in its social and political than in its industrial

pects—that it is impossible to exaggerate the importance of aving correct views upon it.'73

The nationalist leaders with near unanimity demanded that n the interest of healthy economic development the Governent of India should help the nascent industries of the country granting them protection.74 Thus, for example, G. Subrama-¿ Iyer wrote in 1903:

1 To bring into existence a variety of non-agricultural occupations is the most paramount want of the country and this can be done... by the adoption of a policy of Protection. ...

so long as the present so-called Free Trade and unequal competition continue, India has no hope of a future which will be distinguished by material prosperity.... To preserve the foodgrains and raw materials that she produces for consumption in her own domestic markets, by taxing their exports, and to prevent the import of competitive manufactures ov (RN protective duties in view to the growth of indigenous indes tries—this is the policy that alone will save India.7s

Strangely enough, however, the Indian National Congress did not demand protection even once during the first 2o years of its existence. This was perhaps partly in deference to its Radical allies in England and partly due to its reluctance to put forward a demand that was, in view of Britain's stern adherence to Free Trade, obviously 'unrealisable' politically.76

Seeing that the doctrine of Free Trade was a major hindrance in the path of industrial progress through protection, the nationalist leaders set out to disprove its validity in Indian conditions and to show that, on the other hand, protection would be useful and not harmful to the long-range interests of India. The earliest and the most scientific effort in the direction was made by K.T. Telang in 1877 in the paper referred to earlier. Many later writers seem to have relied on this work.

The main nationalist argument against Free Trade was its unsuitability to India.77 The Indian leaders said that it might have benefited England, but in India it had only caused, and was causing, industrial havoc.78 They further maintained that Free Trade could exist only among equals; between India and England it was something like ‘a race between a starving, exhausting (sic!) invalid, and a strong man with a horse to ride on'.79

Some of the Indian leaders also refuted the assertion that protection restricted the freedom of the consumer and the liberty of the entrepreneur. They said that liberty and freedom could not possibly mean freedom to foreigners to ruin India's nascent enterprises through unlimited competition and that, in fact, in Indian conditions real liberty would come only through protection and 'artificial nourishment', while Free Trade meant giving protection to England, the stronger party.8o As far as the cost of protection to the consumer was concerned, they were convinced that the nation would be quite willing to bear it especially since the long-term benefits of fall in prices, increase in wages and employment, and general growth of national income would far outweigh the cost.81 Telang also had little difficulty in showing that in India protection would not lead to the diversion of capital from its natural channels but would instead help mobilise the country's idle hoards.8z As far as labour was concerned, he continued, the consequences of protection and the resultant growth of industry would be entirely beneficial; it would lead to an increase in the price of labour because of the increased demand for it. This led him to say that even if protection did diminish the aggregate production of a country it would still be desirable in a country like India in' order to secure a more equitable distribution of national income. ‘After all,'he wrote, ‘it does not do much good to a country to have a few bloated fortunes in the midst of a dead level of indigence here and there chequered with squalid misery. It is better, I humbly conceive, to have fewer large fortunes if the population at large be at the same time removed above the needs of starvation and misery.'83

Several Indian economists questioned the chief premise of the Classical economists that Free Trade led, through the working of comparative costs, to the most efficient geographical division of labour. They refused to accept as ‘almost a Providential dispensation' any division of labour that would consign India for ever to be a producer of raw materials, and rejected the assumption that 'India should continue to export the raw material to England and import the manufactured goods from England'.84 They asserted that India was by its history, geography, and resources eminently fitted to be a great manufacturing country.85 In any case, temporary protection was needed to test whether or not certain industries were better suited to new countries than to their old habitats.86 Moreover, there could be no fair and mutually beneficial division of labour till the full capacities of every nation were known and developed. To have universal Free Trade just at the moment would stereotype the existing pattern of international division of labour and mean that those countries which had developed earlier would for ever remain industrially advanced while others like India would be further reduced to an agrarian status pure and simple.87 In this context, K. T. Telang warned against a country being reduced to a single-industry country since any change in fashions, tastes, and needs affecting its products would spell disaster for it.88

Several of the Indian leaders also argued that protection to infant industries for a limited period of time was not opposed. even to the tenets of laissez faire, and pointed to the writings. of John Stuart Mill as having approved of protection in the type of situation prevailing in India.89 In fact, many of them claimed to be free traders in principle who were favouring protection for India as a special case.9o

Some of them brought forward a very ingenious argument to prove the correctness and justice of protection in India's case.. Referring to the heavy duties imposed on the import of Indian. manufactures in England up to the middle of the 19th century K. T. Telang, for example, asserted that 'the agency employed to destroy our indigenous manufactures was this very Protection' and claimed that all the Indians asked for was that 'the same instrument which was used as a sword against us should now be used as a shield for us'.91 Similarly, R. C. Dutt remarked caustically: 'But in India the Manufacturing Power of the people was stamped out by protection against her industries, and then free trade was forced on her so as to prevent a. revival.'92

To strengthen their case for protection, the Indian leaders appealed to the practice of nations as against the theories of political economy and pointed out that nearly all the free nations of Europe and America and Britain's own colonies like Canada and Australia had protected their newly started industries whenever these were threatened by foreign competitors.93 They also very acutely noted that England itself had reared its modern industries under the fostering care of protection during the last decades of the i8th century and the opening decades of the 19th century, and that it was only after its industrial machine had been properly geared up for successful competition abroad that it had proclaimed to the world the doctrine of Free Trade.94 Also, R. C. Dutt and G. Subramaniya Iyer did not fail to notice that while the British economists like Adam Smith had been loudly preaching the doctrine of Free Trade from the end of the i8th century onwards, the British statesmen turned free traders much later and only when it suited Britain.95 Many of the nationalist leaders went to the extent of saying that the British did not sincerely believe in the principles of Free Trade and were using them as a cover-or, as G. V. Joshi put it, a 'metaphysical framework'—to push their products in India and to prevent the growth of an industrial rival.96

In spite of their criticism of Free Trade and advocacy of a protective umbrella for industries, none of the prominent nationalist leaders adopted a doctrinaire position in favour of protection. Their attitude in the matter was highly eclectic. This was but natural for their rejection of Free Trade was also not doctrinaire. As in case of their other economic attitudes, their tariff policy was not the product of general principles of economics but sprang from, and was closely related to, concrete economic issues. This eclecticism, inherent in their very defence of protection, was sometimes clearly articulated. For instance, R. C. Dutt wrote in 19o1 : 'I do not pin my faith to Free Trade, and I do not pin my faith to Protection. I hold that the policy most conducive to the prosperity and happiness of the people of India is the policy which should be adopted for India.'97 In fact, as some of the nationalist leaders openly stated during the first years of the 2oth century, what they desired was not so much protection as the right to frame an independent tariff policy.98 At this time a few Indians also began to discriminate between different types of protection and see clearly that the wrong-type of protection could result in a large scale influx of foreign capital which would be able to use protection to monopolise the industrial field. Thus the editor of the Hindustan Review and Kayastha Samachar wrote in November 1903:

... with the present political and economic subjection of India to England, the adoption of a policy of Protection is most likely to be abused in favour of the Anglo-Indian producers and alien exploiters generally and... the. struggle will be between the British capitalist and the foreign capitalist and not between the Indians on the one hand and all aliens on the other.99

## Similarly, the Hindu of 13 October 1903 observed :

## IV. SOME OTHER OBSERVATIONS

In conclusion attention may be drawn to a few other important aspects of the doctrinal outlook of the nationalist economists.

An important aspect of the economic thinking of the nationalists was its social orientation. As has been shown above, the nationalist economists realised that economic ideas. and policies had to be subordinated to socially derived goals and perspectives. In fact the entire economic thought of the nationalists was pervaded by this understanding; and this notion of subjecting economic thinking and policy to socially integrated goals perhaps forms the specific core of what is. known as Indian economics. This view of economic ideas and policy also established from the very beginning close interrelation between Indian economics and politics. It enabled the nationalist leaders to link nearly every important economic question with the politically dependent status of the country and to take note of the most imporant political and economic aspect of the Indian reality—that India was being ruled by a foreign power for the purposes of economic exploitation.

Apart from this, while their economic thinking was ahead of contemporary English thinking and in tune with thinking in Europe and America—in stressing that the same propositions of economics could not be applied to countries at different stages of economic development and that for each country these propositions should be formulated in the context of the general economic needs of the country concerned —it did not yet result in the emergence of any new 'Indian' system of economic ideas or theories.1oi In spite of the use of adjectives like 'Indian' and 'national' before their economic thought, the Indian nationalist economists did not discover any new economic laws which would arise out of, and be applicable only to, the specific and peculiar conditions of India. What we get from them is an amalgam of the ideas of Classical economists and their later-day critics. It might almost be said that Indian Political Economy was more an attitude of mind, an approach towards Indian economic problems, and a method of economic reasoning than a system of economic thought.

This was largely due to the fact that the nationalist economists' interest in economic theory was secondary or derivative. They were not professional economists interested in developing economic theory or in evolving an Indian version of it. They, therefore, made no attempt to grapple with theory per se. Their comments on economic theory have, in fact, to be gleaned from a vast mass of writing on contemporary economic problems. Their political economy arose out of a very practical consideration: because the rulers of India used the authority of Classical economics to oppose nationalist demands, the nationalist leaders in turn felt it necessary to challenge the universality of Classical economics and its applicability to Indian conditions. This rather mundane origin of their economic thought was sometimes consciously expressed by them. Thus they said that they had to demolish the doctrine of laissez faire functions of the state as it came in the way of state assistance to industries,1o2 the Ricardian theory of rent because it was used to justify the high pitch of land revenue, to reject their demand for the permanent settlement of land revenue, and to deny their allegation that the level of taxation in India was very high,1o3 the doctrine of Free Trade because it was in part responsible for the repeal of cotton import duties, imposition of cottón excise duties, and the refusal to grant protection to nascent industries.104

But even though there was nothing new in the economic thought of the nationalists, mere statement of the Indian point of view and adaptation of European thought to Indian questions. had an importance of its own. After all the very act of choosing between different theoretical positions constitutes an act of theorising and is even theoretically significant. The Indian leaders revealed that, though the products of English education, they were at no stage overwhelmed by it and were not afraid of attacking the shibboleths governing the English mind. Moreover, they also deserve credit for taking a comprehensive and integrated view of economic science. Though they did not develop a consistent economic theory or political economy of growth, they did do the next best thing—tliey gave a consistent, integrated, interrelated picture of Indian economy, its diseases, their relation to the alien rule, and the renedies to be applied. We may go so far as to say that they tried to evolve some sort of political economy of growth in which developments in industry, foreign trade, fiscal policy, and agriculture were closely intertwined and which was geared first of all to rapid industrialisation of the country. In this connection, it may also be noted that the relativity of economic principles and the notion of Indian 'exceptionalism' were utilised by the early nationalist leaders not to justify or conserve the purely indigenous or pre-modern pattern of Indian economy but to accelerate the speed of its modernisation. Classical economics was rejected not because it was disrupting the age-old pattern of Indian social life but because its rigid and indiscriminate application tended to perpetuate the 'old legacies and inherited weaknesses',105 and 'the ancient bondage of Feudalism and status';1o6 and an 'Indian Political Economy' was brought forth to promote the rapid transformation and modernisation of the national economic life.
