![](images/8e4c902d7704c3464106c28692c6102bf508a512dc77f0f90fc8d1e0601c76aa.jpg) ▶ Sven Palmqvist, Fuga bowls for Orrefors, 1954 ▶ Sven Palmqvist, Kraka vase for Orrefors, 1944 # sven palmqvist (b. 1906 Lenhovda – d. 1984 Orrefors, Sweden) ![](images/0dba4b9c87e4d3a69d47e7fbf43e6bcc19d738196a36c92e8203309e226ae2b6.jpg) The Swedish glass designer Sven Ernst Robert Palmqvist trained from 1928 at the glass engraving school that had been established at the Orrefors glassworks. Following this, he studied at the Konstfackskolan (University College of Arts, Crafts and Design) and the Tekniska Skolan (Polytechnic), Stockholm, from 1931 to 1933 and then later attended the Kungliga Konsthögskolan (Royal University College of Fine Arts), Stockholm, from 1934 to 1936. During these years, he also studied under Paul Cornet and Aristide Maillol (1861–1944) at the Académie Ranson, Paris, as well as in Germany, Czechoslovakia, Italy and America. In 1936, Palmgvist returned to the Orrefors glassworks, where he developed the Kraka technique, in which a grid-like pattern of white or coloured glass was sandwiched between layers of clear glass. Later he exhibited his work in Paris at the 1937 "Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne" and showed his designs at the 1939 New York World's Fair. In 1954, he invented a method of centrifugally spinning molten glass in a mould so as to eliminate the need for hand finishing, and at the 1957 (XI) Milan Triennale he was awarded a gold medal and a Grand Prix for his Fuga domestic glassware, which utilized this highly innovative technique. During the late 1940s and early 1950s, he developed his Ravenna glassware in which coloured geometric glass segments were surrounded with sand-sprinkled air channels to produce a mosaic-like effect. He used this technique for a free-standing glass wall entitled Light and Dark, which was designed for the Union Internationale des Télécommunications, Geneva, and comprised twò hundred blocks of Ravenna glass. Palmqvist remained at Orrefors until 1972 creating exquisite “industrial studio glass”, and later worked as a freelance designer for the glassworks. Palmgvist's art glass epitomized the graceful functionalist forms so characteristic of Swedish design, while his development of pioneering techniques ensured breathtaking creative innovation, Captivated by the visual poetry of glass, Palmqvist stated, “With glass you can sculpt with light, paint with spectral colours with the aid of the sun.
