# WILLIAM BLAKE “The Divine Image” from “Songs of Innocence” (1789)

![](images/3be74f2fc63b664d5c83b62731081a4c2a23b26c1292a5b60962f4f5a11ff1ce.jpg)

William Blake

Our search for the origins of Art Nouveau leads us back to the turn of the eighteenth century, to the visionary painter-poet William Blake (1757-1827),21 who anticipated the leitmotiv of Art Nouveau completely. An extended flowing movement, singularly gliding rhythms, as well as asymmetry and a closed graphic form, all these appear everywhere in his drawings or illustrations, where a biomorphic figurative substance has recently been observed. The laws of gravity and of perspective seem abolished, bodies are scarcely rounded, light casts almost no shadow. Blake's imaginary world remains two-dimensional as far as this is possible for figurative art and as long as the latter does not become a pure outline or a surface silhouette as it did in Art Nouveau.

Many elements fuse in the art of Blake, which yet remains original to such a degree that only recently has one begun to detect the presence there of certain influences.22 All that is essential is genuine, especially in his form. There is not a single feature in his work that does not bear the sign of exaggeration—“exuberance is beauty"— and only seldom is a disconcertingly amateurish trait missing. Art, for him, was “a means of conversing with paradise”; in his pictures one breathes the atmosphere of an undiscovered planet. Like his poems, they are visions and, as an art, imaginative to the highest degree, springing from within and nourished on the elements of another world. But Blake's fairy-like, ecstatic world of images is at the same time ruled by a strange elegance and an instinct that delights in ornament and decoration. The art of Blake is at once ingenuous and sentimental, weak and strong, simple and sophisticated. It is eminently romantic: on the one hand it is filled with sweetness and an exalted child-like faith in fairy tales—an art “for angels"23—on the other hand it expresses all the violence and discord of the German Sturm und Drang of Blake’s own age.

Blake's pictorial art is extremely literary and always refers to a text or to lettering, his images being frequently combined with characters. Many of his subjects were chosen from the Bible—preferably from the Old Testament—from Milton, or from Dante. Besides, a great number of his graphic works illustrate his own lyrical or prophetic poems. It is a religious art, but of a rebellious kind, individualistic and heretical: “I know of no other Christianity and of no other gospel than the liberty both of body and mind to exercise the divine arts of Imagination: Imagination, the real and eternal world of which this vegetable universe is but a faint shadow." Behind the forms of nature Blake pcrceived the sources of life; beyond the established forms of material appearances, he recognized the ever-renewed spiritual drives whereby everything is constantly being metamorphosed.

The fairy-tale charm of the small book-page, Infant Joy (plate 27), leads us back to the world of children's songs which characterized Blake's earliest period. The illustration that surrounds the poem is as lyrical as the poem itself, and the idea of the illustration displays the same kind of benevolent fantasy as the form which expresses and sustains it.

The idea of the fire-flower is one of Blake's own creations. The heavy flower is carried here by a frail stem that is curved in the form of a C and grows out of a lawn which slopes down on one side. The balance of the composition seems uncertain. The mass of the flower is split open and thereby translated into movement, so that its relatively compact forms harmonize with the delicate character of the other elements of the picture. The small leaves, with their jagged and feathery edges, not only fill the lower half of the page in the sense of a compelling need for decoration, but also intervene between the movement in the figure and the geometrical rectangle of the page itself. With its curved lines and bodies, the outline of the arabesque forms a square which, although open on all sides and not definitely framed, can yet be recognized as a square and, across an empty space, maintains its distance from the limits of the page. This device was later developed into a distinctive style by Art Nouveau, but remained totally unknown in the more selfconscious style of eighteenth-century Rocaille (plate 29) and was first used by Blake.

The sensitiveness of the organism both to neighboring forms and to the limits of the page gives birth to the complementary form that is so typical of Art Nouveau. In the jagged lines of white background that interlock with the canopy of flowers above the woman's figure, in the pointed negative forms between the leaping petal-flames, above and on the right-hand side, in the lancet-shaped empty spaces that introduce themselves above the horizontal chalices from the opposite direction in reversed but identical form, everywhere, more or less clearly, the outline forms two figures, delineating them both with equal ambiguity. The intervals, at least where they approach the positive forms, acquire the nature of cmpty reflected forms. This is brought about by a bold, simplifying, and ornamental outline, as the “realistic” contour of the little group attests. Here, the empty space outside the contour of the group is not recognizable as an opposed form; all that remains is a kind of "form scrap” of passive background. But, apart from the figurative scene, everything is conceived as if there were neither space nor light, juxtaposed or superimposed in a pattern in terms of surface, so that even linear intersections scarcely ever occur, and, as in most of Blake's illuminated pages, text is subservient to design.

This page comcs from the Songs of Innocence, one of Blake's most beautiful “illuminated books." Blake not only illustrated his own poems here, but printed them himself according to his own typographical methods.24 He was conscious of having invented a device for simultaneous printing of the text and the illustration from the same plate, in a style more “ornamental” and “uniform” than had ever been used before. In addition to rendering the text and the illustration uniform and equal in value, Blake made the content of the page appear clear and complete. It is not the text alone, but the uniformity of text and illustration that conveys the whole meaning to us, both having been conceived at the same time. With Blake, poetic inspiration occurred very often simultaneously with visual inspiration.25

The meaning of Infant Joy may be understood on several levels: firstly, the scene taking place in the flower-represented as the situation in which the dialogue between the two stanzas occursand the flower itself as a decorative, fairy-like figure, can both be understood as the very atmosphere of the lyrical poem. But from Blake's system of symbols, we may also deduce a more precise meaning for this content, as an “annunciation" that occurs in the "flower of love." In a deeper sense too, the flamboyant flower signifies the “chalice of the womb" itself, into which the angel with the “butterfly-wings of Resurrection” has penetrated as “life-agent of the father" in order to participate there in the holy act of procreation by arousing to life, through a question which must be answered, the unborn child in the womb of the mother, who is both Mater and Matter.26

The burning flower as the “chalice of the womb" is not merely a poetical metaphor; it also suggests that all preceding life, even though less differentiated and of lesser value, is still present within the most highly developed species. One thus feels inclined to brcak open the hard, closed form of existence in order to attain the stream of life and penetrate the mystery of ever-renewed conception of growth. With Blake, a plant or one of the elements can symbolize the organic principle of something pre-human or within man himself. The meaning conveyed here relates not only physical substance and movement, but also the same substance and movement of the spirit, the still unreflected unity of substance and idea, analogous to the aesthetic and more evident unity of text and image. St. Francis of Assisi had already preached to the birds; but Blake included the amoeba and even primordial protoplasm in his vision of Christian life.

Sometimes, but only rarely, Blake considers nature only from the exterior and represents it, for instance, in a landscape. If ever he undertakes to do so, as in the lyrical and bucolic woodcuts of his small Virgilian landscapes (plate 38), their rhythm is quite asymmetrical and nearly all seem to be of a soft and animated basic substance where objective forms are only half-differentiated, scarcely yet crystallized in their variegated consistence. In these nocturnal scenes the topographical facts also seem to bathe in a general element of nature into which life can penetrate. The art of representing landscape is one of space, the painting of landscapes presupposing a primary interest in what occurs in space and in its unyielding structure, as well as in the appearances of a given reality. Neither Blake nor Art Nouveau felt this interest; they both prefer to reproduce nature in more symbolical forms.

The poetry of Blake, in addition to the testimony of his work as an artist, reveals to us how he evaluated nature and her forces. He wrote about his prophetic poem Europe: “The whole poemn is an allegory of the sleep of Nature during the eighteen hundred years of the Christian era... and the awaking of forgotten joy, when ‘nature felt through all her pores the enormous revelry.'"27 In Jerusalem, Blake says: “No individual can conform to rules, for they mean death to every energy of man and seal the sources of life"; and, in the Marriage of Heaven and Hell: “Jesus was all virtue and acted from impulse, not from rules" and, again, “Energy is eternal delight."

## Blake and Rocaille

The fire-flower, the “watery flames," the almost nonobjective but still expressive tangle of lines, and the whirlwind, all these are patterns that developed quite logically out of Blake's own handwriting as an artist. Chronologically too, they stand at the very beginning of his own art, that is to say of his mystic style. In Blake's earliest works, however, these features are missing and his close affinity with Art Nouveau is not yet apparent. These earlier works are figurative compositions where the tame classicism of the period is aped again in an amateurish and half-skilled way. What was it that suddenly unsealed the secret sources of Blake's inspiration and then allowed his own style to flow and develop freely?

The time of this change in his style is well established. Blake's own style is first revealed in the pages of his collection of poems, Songs of Innocence (plates 25-27), produced in 1789, the first year of the French Revolution. The title page of this volume, which was obviously the first to be created, provides a decisive indication (plate 26): here, for the first time, there appears one of Blake's flame-arabesques, though it still reveals itself frankly as a derivative element, borrowed from Rocaille style. A Rocaille form adheres to the lettering and both of them are thereby transformed.

What had Blake discovered? The Rocaille style (plate 29) is an animated and dynamically tense ornamentation where asymmetry is stressed to the utmost. It consists mainly of C-shaped curves which, in fact, as they follow their courses, join in S-shaped curves but remain, as individual curves, exactly demarcated. At the same time, there is in Rocaille a rhythmic play of constant starting and stopping, of seeking and avoiding, of curves which are alternately concave or convex, narrow or broad. Entirely closed on one side, these curves frequently bifurcate, radiate or open up like fans on the other side, while their ends curl backward and roll upward in the manner of volutes. This abstract basic figure, owing to its nature and function, may develop into the most variegated forımns of more or less figurative patterns. On the whole, the characteristic elemnent consists in the complicated interruptions of the design and the extremely open form which is composed of many smaller parts.

Rocaille conceives of ornament in plastic terms. Whether carved in the wood of wall panels, cast in ormolu, or modeled in stucco, it remains a body of ornament, developing in three-dimensional space. Even if applied to the surface of a panel, it appears in relief, with strong contrasts of light and shadow. Owing to its richly differentiated, sharp-cornered, and streamlined profile, it appears, even if more subtle and jewel-like, as a variety of the “mass in movement” of the Baroque style. Its sculptural substance is broken up in narrow, curved ridges and ruts that, almost blending into each other, are often juxtaposed in a parallel arrangement of sheaves. The delicacy of filigree work here seems to unite with a more sluggish quality, as though there were something shapeless and doughy underneath the hard surface of the metal. Only on the surface, not within the mass itself, is the substance supporting the outer surface differentiated; beneath the accurate chiseled modeling of the outer relief the bulk of the inner mass remains amorphous.

Blake was unable to achieve so complicated and ingenious an idiom. Wishing to give his title page an ornament in addition to the illustrative scene, he had reverted to Rocaille, which had been out of fashion. What he finally made of it seems at first to be nothing but an incredible vulgarization or simplification of Rocaille; but something else had also occurred.

What is different and absolutely new here is that Rocaille forms are now united with lettering. Pure Rocaille could never be combined with lettering because the latter had always consisted of linear signs set on a one-dimensional surface whereas, in engravings or other prints, Rocaille had always been represented as relief in an illusionistic three-dimensional style. As Blake did not conceive ornament in relief, but had transformed Rocaille into a broadly superficial idiom of form, the basic condition for blending it with lettering or calligraphy was brought to fulfillment.

However it is impossible to translate the effects of three-dimensional relief as such into the effects of a two-dimensional plane surface. The transformation achieved by this leveling also brings about other transformations, and Blake thereby rendered Rocaille less active by softening its outer and inner contours. The delicately chiseled relief melted, thereby reducing the internal pressure of the surface tension that had been maintained within the form of swelling substance. With the disintegration of the rigid relief, the sluggish mass that had been imprisoned within its rounded surfaces was set free. As it escaped it flowed beyond the confines which before had been so stringent and exacting, and this caused the curled-in terminals to unfold. In general, Blake liked to ease the tension of rounded patterns by devising his forms in flat bands, such as, for instance, his transformation of the acanthus capitals of slim pillars into the tops of living palm trees.

In a French design for a carpet, dating from around 175o (plate 24), we may observe this same type of softening of Rocaille that made it less sharp as the design unfolded. This is one of the rare examples that is not by Blake, and it clarifies how far these transformations result automatically, so to speak, from this transposition in terms of surface. In this carpet design we may see the relief-like nature of Rocaille not so much illustrated literally as expressed, to a certain degree, within a two-dimensional plane. In this carpet design one also finds, besides the rudiments of a hard surface, the curves of the soft substance of Rocaille overflow their more rigid and broken edges. The tenseness of the relief relaxes and form and rhythm begin to enjoy more freedom. Obviously, such a design is already very closely rclated to Art Nouveau.

In Rocaille there were already hints of the complementary relationships of positive and negative forms. Blake exaggerates these with partiality. In the flames and flame-shaped intervals of one of his latcr works, such a reciprocal relationship is clearly developed in the way Blake liked. In the catalogue for an exhibition of his pictures, he says: “Clearness and precision have been the chief objects in painting these pictures. Clear colors unmudded by oil, and firm and determined lineaments unbroken by shadows which ought to display and not to hide form." As against the voluntarily confused, painterly, and polyphonic form of Rocaille, all that matters to Blake (and to Art Nouveau) is the clear legibility of the form as an event in itself.

The transformation that began in the title page of Songs of Innocence (plate 26) continued throughout the othcr pages of the book. Without the key provided by the title page, one would scarcely be able to detect a relationship to Rocaille in the pages designed only a short while later. In Cradle Song (plate 1 14), there is a still greater dispersion of forms appearing in the chaos of creation, whereas on the page entitled The Divine Image (plate 25) a grcat flaming tree surges with magnificent clarity. These forms, liberated from anything that might shroud or hide them, soar upward like the pure vital spark, or the impulse of life itself. But Rocaille, especially late and naturalistically interpreted Rocaille like the hollow tree stump (page 34) from a series of copperplate engravings by Crusius, suggests something that has been left over and cast off; in fact it is like the negative of a form wherein the substance which had once lived has literally rotted like old wood. Even in its heyday, Rocaille had something indeed autumnal about it, like rustling leaves, whereas in Blake's work (plate 28r) the vigorous and vernal branches, or the luscious acanthus volutes in the back of an armchair, remind one of his beloved “source of life."

Five years after the creation of Blake's first significant title page, the new one for the now combined Songs of Innocence and of Experience (plate 28) shows that no longer can the trace of Rocaille be detected. Here it is clear that the process of transformation has been completed. In abolishing Rocaille, Blake found his own language of form. Thus, from a historical point of view, his work illustrates the transition between Rocaille and Art Nouveau.

## Blake and Mannerism

In this alchemy of structure, still another factor appears: the linea and forma serpentinata of Mannerism, contained in Rocaille, are liberated. Both the art of Blake and Art Nouveau are supported by this fundamental current that was latent in anti-Classicism and in anti-Baroque art. Around 18oo, Blake's curves which, as we see in the flaming flowers, had at first been somewhat more closely allied to Rocaille and consisted of small elements, now became, under the influence of high Classicism, more continuous and with a closed outline. Blake even says: “The more distinct, sharp and wiry the bounding line, the more perfect the work of art." Becoming increasingly simple, Blake's figures show with ever increasing clarity the Manneristic instability of their structure, their Manneristic lack of space, and their Manneristic reliance on torsion too. Great surging tracks twist, forming lasso-like curves (plates 34 and 1ro). No more are we sure of what is superimposed or underlying, and it is now as if the forms could be twisted in any direction, which suggests to us the same feeling we also experience with many sixteenthcentury paintings. The figures that are either plunging downward or hanging as if weightless in the void (plates 33, 34, 110, and 3or), the parallelograms of limbs and their distribution in space which we cannot comprehend rationally, all these are, with Blake, features inherited from Mannerism. The setting of Blake's pictures is indeed so narrow and so flat, though conceived in terms of trompe-l'oeil in those compositions where there are figures, that these serpentine-shaped figures can scarcely turn around and seem flattened out. In general, Blake tends to transpose a surging movement in space into a two-dimensional surface movement and to treat the subject matter of a picture as if it were a microscope preparation pressed between two glass slides.

The substance with which or out of which Blake created his forms can also be suspected of having a close relationship to those “ectoplasm-like convolutions" which André Malraux recognized in the figures created by Primaticcio, Rosso Fiorentino, or Niccolo dell'- Abbate.28 They also remind us of qualities that Aldous Huxley saw in the figures of El Greco: “No less disquieting than the narrowness of El Greco's universe is the quality of the forms with which he filled it. Everything here is organic, but organic on a low level, organic at a point well below the limit of life's perfection . .. In life we are not attracted by protoplasm in the raw or by individual organs separated from the organism as a whole. But it is with forms suggestive of precisely such objects that El Greco fills his pictures. Under his brush the human body, when naked, loscs its bony framework and even its musculature, and becomes a thing of ectoplasm —beautifully appropriate in its strange pictorial context, but not a little uncanny when thought of in the context of real life. And when El Greco clothes his boneless creatures, their draperies become pure abstractions, having the form of something indeterminatcly physiological."29

Blake's creatures secm to be made of a similar matter and, in organisms like the “sea anemone tree" in one of his watercolor illustrations for Dante's Purgatorio, the character of his matter reveals itself clearly as being related to the substance that lies under the reptilian surface of Rocaille, and renders possible the hybrid forms created both by Blake and by Art Nouvcau: the flameflowers and the water-flames, and, on the other hand, the innumerable mermaids, Undines, and creatures of hybrid origin employed by Art Nouveau and including Toorop's womcn “with their writhing ectoplasmic hair"30 (plates 1 16 and 1 17).

It is because of this material nature that Blake's figures could so easily be melted to form many-figurcd ornaments. His supple, draped figures move with an elasticity that bodies with bones and joints could never achieve. This also explains why, in spite of the heraldic rigidity of its symmetry, his ornaments made of narrow stripes seem to find themselves in constant flux, like sprays of water where the form remains unchanged while their mass flows constantly. However, as Blake wished to represent bodics like those of Michelangelo, the overdistinctly modeled muscles and tendons project, as if they were freely exposed, through the close-fitting garment that reveals rather than conceals the anatomy, in accordance with the forms of Mannerism.

In its appearance and substance, the human figure as conceived by Blake is related to the anti-Classical figure of Mannerism. A cold-blooded amphibian ideal was opposed to the heavily corporeal humanistic figure as represented by Raphael, or to the warmblooded fleshy types painted by Rubens, whom Blake hated. In contrast to the normal human figure, hybrid forms occupy more and more space (plate 33). Turning away from the “normal," such a trend leads to the kind of eccentricity, pervcrsity, or lasciviousness that Mannerism and Art Nouveau knew how to suggest with the wiles of a Satanic seducer.

## Blake and Art Nouveau

The substance of Blake's forms also explains how the symbiosis of lettering, ornament, and illustration that he achieved had been at all possible. The fusion of lettering and Rocaille created in 1789 in the title page of Songs of Innocence produced also a transformation (plate 26) in the style of lettering when Blake forccd the anonymous and pcrfect calligraphy of the eighteenth century to become an individual handwriting and made the sharp engraved lines of etching much more similar to the thicker strokes of a woodcut engraving. His characters appear to us to contain likewisc the pulsation of biomorphic substance and movement, and this mctamorphosis is also completed in his title page of 1794 (plate 28). Clearly, the same kind of strokc delincatcs both lettering and figurative rcprcsentation, both of which twist and turn with the same movement and appcar to scnd out the same kind of shoots. Just as Art Nouveau ornament was born in Blake's work, so can we also detect .therc the birth of Art Nouveau lettering. The latter indeed developed out of the same creative association of a new substance and a new rhythm, achieving thereby the transformation of the dissimilar into the similar and the fusion of lettering, ornament, and illustration into a single homogeneous ensemble. By obliterating the distinctions between tcxt and picture, by fusing what is intellectual with what is visual, Blake went even further than any of the artists who followed him chronologically. If one compares individual works of Blake and of Art Nouveau artists which offer significant similarities, Blake's style now seems more purely Art Nouveau than Art Nouveau itself (plate 1 14 and page 48).

In a title page that Van Rysselberghe designed for an Almanach of 1895, a bunch of long-stemmed morning glories grows out of the L in the title, enclosing on the one side the asyımmctrically displaced blocks of the lettering in its embrace. Ornaments and lettering, lines and surfacc-bodies are, to a great extent, all assimilated. In the four flowers in the middle of the page, the reckoning of form and counterform causes the inversion that assigns an active role ainong the dark forms to the interior design formed by the star-shaped blanks of the white background. Blake had also tried his hand at such inverting of positive and negative forms, but never went as far in their exploitation. Notwithstanding their mutual assimilation, all the forms in this Art Nouveau design are perfectly developed and isolated one from the other and, in spite of the softness of the curves, each line is sharply defined. One feels that the problems of layout, of proportions and of their relationships to the intervals, have been solved here according to a theoretical principle.

On the other hand, Blake's Cradle Song seems to be filled with all the immoderate extravagance of genius. The whole page flickers as if with flames; all is one, just as the child in the poem is still one with the universal spirit. A message is slumbering in the germs of the ornament, and the words seem to have only just condensed as an articulate communication. If, in Art Nouveau, on the one hand, the form is elevated to the level of rational clarity and of the transparency conferred by technical perfection, Blake, on the other hand, renders transparent the primitive germ and the process of creation. The price of the more rigorous development of the principle of form thus consists of a certain emptiness; as Voysey had stated in The Studio as early as 1893: “We have a language of ornament and yet nothing to say."

But Blake did not anticipate Art Nouveau only in ornament, illustration, and lettering, nor indeed in the ensembles of books and structures, in rhythm, conception, and signification. He indeed anticipated, or at least forestalled, most of its other characteristic features too. Were one to think of him, in the terms used by William Morris, as a universal artist who was not only poet, painter, book illustrator, and printer of his own works, but who might also have designed furniture and everything else for the home, one would be able to visualize all these various objects only as borrowing the most imaginative forms of Art Nouveau. In his pictures, Blake seldom depicted an interior and furnished it even then but summarily, with Biblical parsimony. But in these rare cases we find combined pieces of furniture” of a surprisingly independent and inventive style, and objects too, such as the vase-like pitcher on Lot's table, which Gallé or Tiffany might have dreamed of (plates 36 and 37). Twisted and serpentine in themselves, with uninterruptedly flowing outlines and obliquely revolving whirls of opalescent materials, bulging or extremely extended or, in another place, ending in a sharp point, these objects still retain the forms of blown glass, with the same kind of organically dynamic growth. They thus belong, like distant relatives who yet know nothing of one another, to one and the same general family of forms.

## Proto-Art Nouveau About 1800

Blake's proto-Art Nouveau was not unique in his time. In the decades before 18oo, various features of Art Nouveau appeared independently in England, Italy, France, and Germany, all emphasizing the super-personal character of this new conception of style. True, some other works with a close relationship to Art Nouveau were also born under the immediate influence of Blake. During the last years of his life, Blake was indeed surrounded by a whole group of very sensitive, Romantic-minded artists who admired him as his disciples and whose exquisite art sprang mainly from his own. Blake's Virgil landscapes (plate 38) were thus the source of the art of Edward Calvert (1799-1883) and of Samuel Palmer (18os-81), the two most important artists of this group.32 Their work was mostly graphic in character and they both, though perhaps more or less unconsciously, exalted likewise the instinctive fecundity of nature.

In Calvert's fine pictures (plate 39), the Manneristic element is even more obvious than in Blake's. The lawns of his landscapes are covered with strange mushroom-like flowers that have no stems. Vine branches with grapes hanging from them, some so full and heavy that they drip with juice, are entwined around mossy tree trunks. The thatched roofs of hidden cottages somehow adapt themselves to the soft outline of the hills and the treetops; fences of gardens or wooden beams in an interior make us vaguely think of biomorphic forms. Milk and honey seem to flow in Calvert's pictures which are filled with the repressed tenderness of Pan-like eroticism, the idyllic earthly paradise at time's beginning being his subject matter. His lettering also goes back to the origins of writing: Calvert was the only artist of his period to use a kind of runic script.

Samuel Palmer's landscapes are as lyrical and as soft, but of a more closed, less manneristic or elegant form (plate 4o). Palmer surrounds his hills, fields, and haystacks with simplifying black contours. He integrates similarly filled areas in his compositions, such as bushes, with the same foliage; or fields with the same crops, all in a single unit which, as a picture, is organized in flat sections looking like those of a stained glass window. Yet Palmer and Calvert have more pictorial sense than Blake. Their tones are shaded and they sense space-values, though, like Blake, they both express space more or less in terms of a two-dimensional plane, juxtaposing or superimposing things upon it instead of showing them successively in perspective.

Blake's successors were forgotten as he too was at first forgotten. But, unlike Blake, they were not destined, even later, to exert any influence on the early phases of Art Nouveau. In the realm of High Art Nouveau, they were only rediscovered as a consequence of the rediscovery of Blake, their ideas being then further developed in the works, for instance, of Charles Ricketts and Thomas Sturge Moore.

But the work of Philipp Otto Runge (1777–181o) had no influence at all on Art Nouveau or, more specifically, on German Jugendstil.33 However great the difference between the two artists, Runge, in a curious way, is closely related to Blake; but there is no reason to believe that either of them knew anything about the other. Independently of each other, they were both guided by the same artistic intentions. For Runge, the symbol of law and order in the seemingly tangled life of nature is the arabesque, which he illustrates in the exemplary forms of plant life (page 98). But, with Runge, leaves and flowers which are true to nature also lead back to the basic patterns of their growth and development and, at the same time, reveal the geometry that is concealed within the laws of nature. In spite of all his stylization in order to achieve ornamental elements, the abstract and ideal image of the plant appears in every one of his concrete forms. Like Blake, Runge also breaks up the baroque plurality of “foliage" into the forms of single, isolated leaves where design and draftsmanship dominate the summary way of visualizing things from the optic and painterly point of view which characterizes the eighteenth century, from Watteau to Gainsborough, and also the nineteenth, from Constable to Renoir.

From the grotesque designs of the Louis XVI period, which in turn stem from those of Mannerism, Runge develops symmetrical ornaments that are all alien to nature out of human figures which, however, are not reduced to a figurative ornament as is the case with Blake. Runge then arranges them on a plane in the foreground of his picture, set against a background of a landscape of great depth (plate 282). Children emerge from flowers or are integrated into subterranean roots. In his symbiosis of plants and children, Runge also refers to the mystery residing in the identity of man and nature, in the unity of all organic life. The grotesque element reappears here in the allegorical, symbolical, hermetic, and decorative character of the frames with which he surrounds his pictures. Here for the first time painting achieves its unity with the frame, together from the point of view of content; grown together, the two form, on a small scale, a total work of art. Lilies, poppies, and swans, all favorite themes of Jugendstil, can already be found in Runge's work. He conceived a fantastic floral type of architecture in a design for a monument that might well have been invented around 19oo. A simple shoot, a plant in its natural relationship, is transformed by him in his sketches into ornaments that one might well imagine having seen in Pan or in Die Jugend.

Even Ossian's Dream by Jean Auguste Dominique Ingres (178o-1867)³4 belongs to this domain. The cloud of figures with its parallels, its melting outlines, its ectoplasmic substance, and its whole bizarre and fabulous atmosphere, despite all individual dissimilarities, also stands halfway between Mannerism and Art Nouvcau, like Blake's and Calvert's works. But the figures of Ingres and of Runge are more corporeal, morc modeled and compact (even if they are floating), and more static, more closcly connected with the solid structure of the picture as a wholc. A national constant, the English inclination for weightless, almost unsubstantial and unstatic figures, finds its most extreme expression in the very flat and unstable creations of Blake.35

This new style of design asserted itself, however, not only in paintings or in the depicting of pieces of furniture or of other objccts—we might, for instance, mention, in addition to Blake's works, the plant-like and budding supports for a table top in one of Henry Fuseli's pictures—but also in objects crcated in the field of the applied arts and even in the architecture of around 18oo.

An English chintz of 1802 (plate 44) is printed with a pattern of long, flowing stems and soft leaves and star-like flowers, the simplified and closed forms of which are disposed on a two-dimensional planc. Firmly outlined, they even seem to present a double outline, the full spaces of the intervals being set apart from the figurative forms of the design by white edging. Here we find a very early example of the complementary relationship which is so typical of Art Nouveau.

Proto-Art Nouveau even adopts the appearance of abstraction in two English glass vessels of about 182o (plates 45, 46) which regress to the basic forms of the jug and the bottle. The craftsman who made them may indeed have thought of antique Roman glassware, but these two pieces still have an entirely independent character that reminds one of Art Nouveau. A soft and gliding rhythm makes itself felt in the contours as well as in the flowing and undulating stripes which produce the pattern. The ornament, developed from the inorganic material and the technique of its production, approximates the delicate irregularities of organic life. At the same time, we already find an anticipation of the strong dynamic lines of High Art Nouveau: parallel and complementary stripes which are disposed in a preordained relationship to each other arc determined by the material itself, being genuine “surface-bodies." Decisive features of Art Nouveau thus appear in these pieces of glassware without achieving the typical mood of Art Nouveau in the sense of the work of Tiffany or of Gallé.

In pieces of furniture designed by Schinkel, and in an upright piano of 183o (plate 284), we again find forms which approach those of Art Nouveau. In the balustrade of a staircase in Canova's Tempio Canoviano (1819), there are mctal “stripes" hanging in relaxed curves. The row of houses of Lansdown Crcscent in Bath (1794) cuts through the landscapc like a whiplash (plate 42). Suggested by the English crescents of the eightecnth century, with their houses built in a semicircle facing an open square, this street in Bath adapts itself to the topographical situation like the winding paths of an English park. It is an undulating wall, softened in itself, like the one that the illustrator Doudelet invented a hundred years later to illustratc one of Maeterlinck's poems (plate 43); after 1900, Gaudí also achieved this effect in the terrace of the Güell Park (plate 16) as well as in the façade of the Casa Milà (plate 218). The flexible substance and the denuded forms that one finds so often in Art Nouveau appear moreover in an almost grotesque manner in the Exerzierhaus in Berlin, built in 18oo by David Gilly. The body of the building looks like a form that has been carved, as if the building had been produced by slicing it from a mass of dough.

In general, proto-Art Nouveau has a closed, simplified, and fluid form, approaching more or less closely the characteristic rhythm and the curves of High Art Nouveau. Figures disposed in a plane are treated in surface, the single forms appearing as “surfacebodies," either with firm outlines or without any contours, in the manner of forms that have been cut out and mounted. As to the significance of such a picture's content, proto-Art Nouveau tends to emphasize the power of instincts and of growth inherent in nature and to communicate to us a sense of the relationships existing in all organic life. In this, proto-Art Nouveau is closely allied to the Romantic attitude toward nature; in spite of a preference for pure, clear, simple, closed, and graphic forms, proto-Art Nouveau form thus generally remained within the limits imposed both by Classicism and by the contemporary neo-Gothic style. In his watercolor, Angels Hovering over the Body of Christ (plate 3or), William Blake even makes a Gothic broken-vault ornament out of the bodies of the angels. To a certain extent, proto-Art Nouveau seems literally to be founded on a fusion of Classicistic and Gothic elements.

Simultaneously with the extreme swing of Blake's curves, there appears, on the other hand, an equally extreme but utterly geometrical new style of architecture in the works of Ledoux, Gilly, and Canova. The Tempio Canoviano reveals both styles: the cylindrical body of the building and the sweeping curves of the staircase balustrade. Around 18oo, two possibilities are thus presented which were later destined to represent the limits and alternatives imposed or offered to Art Nouveau. In English early Art Nouveau, the curved and the geometrical lines are indeed developed side by side; later, they relieve each other in the linear curves of High Art Nouveau, which is primarily decorative, and in geometrical and rectangular late Art Nouveau, so strongly influenced by architecture.

The new style of proto-Art Nouveau is to be found in its most concentrated form in Blake, but appears also in some independent works, though without noticeably affecting our general conception of the pcriod around 18oo. After 183o, however, these features became submerged in historicism. Without any clear separations, and with much overlapping of periods and many transitions, historicism shifted in various forms from the Gothic style to Renaissance, then to Baroque; in interiors, we often see a return to Rococo and, in the eighties and nineties, concurrently to some extent with Art Nouveau or Jugendstil. The nineteenth century culminated in the syncretism of the studio-style, where the entirc historicizing masquerade is carried away in a whirling confusion of various periods.

## Latent Art Nouveau

During the long interval between the proto-Art Nouveau of around 18oo and real Art Nouveau of the end of the century or early English Art Nouveau that first appeared in 1849, various symptoms can be detected, between 1830 and 189o, which prove that Art Nouveau continued to subsist as a kind of underground current. The applied and graphic arts appear to be more receptive to its influences. But, in opposition to its comparative “pureness" in proto-Art Nouveau, it often reveals itself now in an “impure" form, adulterated by historicism or naturalism. Obvious features of Art Nouveau then mix with elements suggested by entirely different criteria. Nearly always of more modest and often anonymous origin, such works can express otherwise latent Art Nouveau only in areas that are dominated by the historicism of the period.

The glass vase, taken from Felix Summerly's Art Manufactures³⁶ of about 1847, was designed by Redgrave (plate 47) and can be distinguished from an example of High Art Nouveau only by a touch of early Victorian style. The closed flowing outline, the extremely prolonged neck and the curved mouth leave nothing to desire from the standpoint of Art Nouveau, which makes its presence felt in the long, stylized, and streaming leaves and in the wreath of flowers. Only the gilded rim of the mouth and the pattern of the base reveal that the vase is in early Victorian style.

In a silver christening mug, also designcd by Redgrave for Summerly's Art Manufactures, another resemblance to Art Nouveau appears, in spite of something early Victorian in its prcdilection for the Gothic (plate 49). Almost in the spirit of Blake (plate 1 12), although in high relief, a row of angels is presented in an ornamental manner. Their gliding and relatively closed forms, the bulbous and swinging curves between the angels, the praying children, kneeling over flowers in the style of Runge (and thus expressing the symbolic "language” of the vase), and above all the handle, twisted like a snake and divided into two skeins in its lower part, all remind us of Art Nouveau. Features of Art Nouveau expressed in the neo-Gothic style are also to be seen on the façade of the Sayn foundry in the Rhineland, where the framework is not only openly displayed in its different parts, but is also used for ornamental effects and as an expression of symbolical energy.
