## Introduction

The English word window derives from wind and eye; this etymology helps defi ne the window aperture as a complex instrument that both admits light and ventilation to an interior and enables the eye – that is, one’s vision. – Georges Teyysot, A Topology of Everyday Constellations. <sup>37</sup>

Openings, both imposed and found, impact signifi cantly on the design of the interior. A carefully crafted view through a space, via a series of openings such as doorways or windows, will imply connectivity and promote movement. The delineation of these apertures will often entice the user of the space to move and circulate in an interior, in order to interact with what is being offered through the opening. Therefore it is important to understand that a critical element of the tactic of creating or reusing an opening in an interior is that it is a device that is closely associated with movement.

Once this is understood, openings can be used as rhythmic devices, punctuations in space that can organise circulation through focusing fl ow in an interior space. For instance, in the Canaletto apartment by Morpurgo de Curtis Architetti Associati, three apertures were cut into a screen wall that divided two rooms. One aperture is opaque, and it is a source of light for both spaces; one is a mirror, refl ecting the view of the room back into itself, and one opening allows a very particular framed view of the next room. Movement between the rooms is then facilitated by a doorway, placed under a mezzanine level, a spatial organisation that requires the occupant of the space to advance to the corner of the room. Even in a small space such as this, a series of strategically placed openings ensured that view; fl ow and circulation were tightly controlled, highly complex spatial manoeuvres.

The considered use of devices such as openings can enable the designer to heighten the senses of the user of the space. This approach can sometimes increase the tension of the visitor in a space through subtle means. This can be done in a number of ways but will often revolve around the development of the threshold between different environments. In the Cathedral Museum in Milan, Guido Canali utilised the rooms and corridors of the existing building in which to create an incredible journey that unfolded as a

sequence of movements through a series of spaces.

Various constraints, not least the deep and complex plan of the building, meant that the route would often need to double back on itself, through the same rooms that the visitor had traversed earlier, in order to make the museum work. Canali deployed a very sophisticated composition of objects and views, chronologically arranged, and facilitated by a series of openings, such as doorways and apertures. These were positioned to offer very specifi c views, through the interior space. The visitor would often journey through a room at least twice. Sometimes they would view a space, yet wouldn’t immediately access it, as they were circulated elsewhere via guiding balustrades or display vitrines. This method of movement through the space heightened the tension of this journey, especially as Canali used these views in a variety of ways, and combined with more straightforward access points into the journey through the museum.

Objects such as long friezes, transported from the cathedral outside, would be placed in a room that consisted of a series of thin tall openings. Other fragments of buildings would be placed into infi lled arched openings. All of these tactics considerably increased the sense of intrigue, as elements such as the frieze appeared to travel through the wall. This approach allowed the visitor to view into the next room, yet required them to move in a counter-intuitive circulation pattern, outside the room, in order to physically access it. The sensation of moving away from the space that the visitor wanted to access heightened the tension and intensifi ed the experience of moving through interior space. When this pattern of movement was undertaken numerous times, as it was in this museum, the openings were used to create very focused and atmospheric interiors.

In reuse there is always this coincidence of things that seem incompatible. – Superuse. <sup>38</sup>

As objects in their own right, the reuse of windows and doors provides a very different view on the tactic of openings. Bureau A took this idea to its extreme when they created a garden pavilion, entitled la fabrique, from salvaged doors and windows. The garden folly was inspired by Buster Keaton’s film One Week in which a newly married couple attempts to build a kit-house on a plot of land. The arguments and disorganisation that ensue became a metaphor for the project, mostly in the emphasis placed upon the inconsistencies arising from salvaged elements, and the difficulties faced when trying to reconcile these differences into a coherent whole in one built object. As Bureau A designer Daniel Zamarbide stated:

La Fabrique is built with a similar mixture of seriousness and lightness. The pavilion made out of recycled windows found on demolition sites is designed and built by BUREAU A and the inhabitants in a couple days. The lightness comes from the direct relation between thinking and doing.

– Daniel Zamarbide, quoted in Dezeen. <sup>39</sup>

Whichever way openings, apertures, doors and windows are deployed, their strategic and tactical use will ensure the designer creates intense sequence and movement in an interior space.
