## La simplicité active.

L'impuissance de discérner entre les degrés de clarté: lécher la pénombre et flotter dans la grande bouche remplie de miel et d'excrément. Mésurée à l'échelle Eternité, toute action est vaine — (si nous laissons la pensée courir une aventure dont le résultat serait infiniment grotesque — donnée importante pour la connaissance de impuissance humaine). Mais si la vie est une mauvaise farce, sans but ní accouchement initiel, et parceque nous croyons devoir nous tirer proprement, en chrysantèmes lavées de l'affaire, nous avons proclamé seule base d’entendement: l'art. Il n’a pas l'importance que nous, reîtres de l'esprit, lui chantons depuis des siècles. L'art n’afflige personne into the terrain of modernity, Dada conversely often resurrects ritual in fragmentary and transfigured ways. Hugo Ball's dirgelike performances of abstract verse, the faux tribalism of Cabaret Voltaire spectacles insistently uncoordinated among performers, the cathedrallike space of Schwitters’ Merzbau, housing relics but closed to any public, all provide examples. It is the loss of community, rather than simply its nonexistence, that haunts Dada—a distinction that emerges within Dada artwork in the proliferation of memorial structures, the incorporation of desecrated fragments of earlier belief systems, and the inscription of the failure of new collective forms.

Given its iconoclasm, and its often-ephemeral productions, the irony of displaying Dada within the museum has been frequently noted. Yet, the dadaists were in the end profoundly historical creatures. Ball's diaries are written with keen consciousness of a future reader,53 and Huelsenbeck described the motivation for Dada's first publishing venture, the Cabaret Voltaire anthology [7], as that of “want[ing] to document."54 Other ambitious compilation projects such as Dadaco, 1919, the Anthologie Dada, 1919 [53, 54], and the Dada Almanach, 1920, followed. By 192r, Tzara published his poetic account “Chronique zurichoise";55 Huelsenbeck had put out not one but two histories of the movement, each in different city-centers,5⁶ and Breton had sketched an intellectual history of Dada in Paris for his patron Jacques Doucet.57 Shoring up boundaries was certainly part of this thrust: innumerable texts disputing the meaning of the word Dada, patrilineal claims to origin, and the inclusion or exclusion of individuals within the group's ranks appeared. Within a few years of the movement's demise, others added their voices, writing memoirs that sought to give meaning to events.58 This self-historicizing impulse reemerged with a vengeance in the postwar period: Richter and Huelsenbeck, now émigrés, penned memoirs for an American audience, and Marcel Duchamp organized a broad showing of dadaist work at the Sidney Janis Gallery in New York in 1953.59 In all these efforts, the dadaists defined their activities in relation to the times—as a moral and social phenomenon within the field of art. Yet, the pains taken to explicate and justify betray a paradoxical anxiety that Dada would be historically incomprehensible. In both Dada period texts and in hindsight, the group relentlessly questioned what it meant to be a group: what bound them, when in principle they were “against principles"?6° And they constantly measured themselves against history, implicitly asking how they would be judged.
