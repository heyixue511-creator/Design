# MARCO ZANINI b.1954 ITALIAN ARCHITECT AND DESIGNER KNOWN FOR CERAMICS AND GLASS PIECES, CONTRASTING COLORS. WITH PLAYFUL SILHOUETTES AND Although he was trained as an architect and has designed a number of furniture pieces, Italian Marco Zanini is best known for the shapely and colorful ceramic and glass objects he designed for MEMPHIS (see under "Edited Design") in the i98os. His appealing pieces had a whimsical charm and their almost anthropomorphic forms were a counterpoint to the angularity of most postmodern design. Zanini studied architecture at the University of Florence, graduating in i978, but while still a student worked freelance for companies in Los Angeles and San Francisco. After meeting ETTORE SOTTSASS (see under "Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") at a workshop organized by the Radical Design group Global Tools\*, he was invited to join Sottsass Associati. From there he was recruited, along with fellow employees ALDO CIBIC, GEORGE SOWDEN, and MATTEO THUN, to become one of the original members of the Memphis collaborative. He was among the most prolific of the group, showing work at all of the annual exhibitions, from the Dublin sofa in i98ı to Roma, a plastic armchair suggesting a classical throne, in 1986. Zanini's well-received glassware designs were unorthodox combinations of conflated shapes and contrasting colors. They include the Cassiopea vase (i98o) and Alpha Centauri (982), which layered segments óf clear and blue glass. Moving to ceramics, he continued to play with form, combining two silhouettes in a single piece and sometimes topping them with a cockscomb motif, as in the Baykal vase (i982). In i983 he created three intriguingly shaped teapots. Colorado, Sepik, and Mississippi, all more likely to be used as decorative accessories than as functional objects. In contrast to the raucous colors of most Memphis furniture, Zanini's ceramics and glass were usually pastel-toned, perhaps to temper the disconcerting effect of their quixotic silhouettes. While at Sottsass Associati, Zanini designed a variety of objects, including jewelry, graphics, industrial machinery, and accessories. He also worked with Sottsass on store designs for the American firm Esprit\*, a popular chain of stylish, mid-priced clothing stores that in the i98os made use of postmodern interiors and graphics to establish an avant-garde brand identity. But the two are almost inextricably connected. Both were rebelling against a restrictive establishment and a style born at the Bauhaus, both turned to historical precedent for inspiration, and, perhaps most important, both sought to assert the importance of their practice as means of communication. Postmodernism, in every area, erased the boundaries between high and low culture, melding the vernacular and high art to suit a diverse and multicultural society. This was particularly apparent in graphic design, where old rules were broken, experiments ran rampant, and weird became acceptable.' Unlike furniture, a printed piece did not have to stand up, hold things, or support weight, but just had to be noticed, and type's only function was to be readable, though sometimes legibility seemed to take a back seat to special effects.² Graphic design as a profession had not existed before the mid-twentieth century; practitioners in what would become that field were called commercial artists, and worked for design studios, as freelancers, or for advertising agencies, designing relatively conventional works to suit conventional clients. Those who sought more creative outlets turned to fine art and galleries, or sold their work privately. The prestigious Switzerland schools that taught typography (as derived from the Bauhaus, or what became designated as Swiss Style) and their American counterparts at Yale and a few other colleges were thought of as trade schools; the skills they taught were considered applied, not fine, arts. The postmodern revolution in graphic design began—and continued--on a parallel path with the Modernism practiced by midcentury (and later) designers like Paul Rand, Herbert Bayer, and Massimo Vignelli. Emerging in small cohorts of like-minded rebels, some European-trained and some converting by chance or circumstance to the feld, they applied their talents to whatever came their way. The most interesting opportunities were in designing pop-culture artifacts like album covers, posters, and magazines that, in the days before the Internet and social media, became the key forms of communication for a generation of street-wise young rebels. Combinations of vintage and new typefaces, bright colors, and asymmetric designs that mixed images and type had transgres sive appeal and seemed an ideal expression of the zeitgeist. For the designers, these vehicles were personal showcases and promotion pieces, as well as a means to support a career in an uncertain new profession that until the i96os lacked even a name. Graphic design is more closely allied with commercial interests than other disciplines. In being inseparably linked to selling, it is not considered as culturally significant as architecture or industrial design. It is also generally short-lived—paper tends to be read and discarded—but that is precisely what makes it an immediate reflection of its time, and even an influence on public taste.
