# Ler Devagar Bookstore

Location Lisbon, Portugal

Remodelled 2009

Designer Aurora Arquitectos

![](images/1203ef67a711e8da0575c16396848c7900f6b86f25a6b2f98ec0cd39162a1637.jpg)  
Figure 3.39 Immovable printing presses were incorporated into the new design of the bookstore, creating a unique set of interior spaces.

Working with existing buildings will always entail the transformation of what is already in situ. The material condition of the existing building will often provide some of the impetus for change, and its careful scrutiny will expose matter that can be considered useful in the ongoing processes of instigating modifi cations and ultimately the possibilities for transformation. Not only will the existing fabric of a building need to be altered to accommodate a new use, any residual material that is waste, or matter that is either left on site or required to be removed, will have to be dealt with in a considered fashion. Sometimes this may not be easily remedied. A budget may not stretch to radical alterations that are costly and time-consuming. And sometimes material that is left on site is just too

awkward and diffi cult to remove. If so, then the designer of the space is left with few options, one of which is just to work with what is already there by incorporating whatever is left behind into the new design. This can take the form of objects that are considered to be immovable, and therefore need to be incorporated into any factors for the design of new interior space.

Aurora Arquitectos were commissioned to make the Ler Davagar (read slowly) bookshop in the LX Factory in Lisbon. The bookstore is just one of over 150 creative companies located in and around a 23,000 square metre sprawling estate of buildings and enterprises in a 19th century Lisbon textile and weaving mill in the city district of Alcântara. Abandoned until 2008, with the injection of some funding and government support the complex has now become a ‘creative village’, a space for social enterprises and small businesses. Design studios are situated alongside bars, restaurants and music and arts venues, creating a thriving economy and a buzzing hub of activity for the outer suburb of the city.

One of the early additions to the complex was the bookshop, housed in a warehouse unit that had previously contained a printing company. The spatial legacy of the company took the form of some leftover redundant machinery and in particular some huge rotary printing presses. Recognising the potential of maintaining a connection to the past through the origins of book production with the presses, as well as aware of the pragmatic concerns of the budget and that the cost of removing the machinery would be extremely prohibitive, the designers decided to retain the massive sculptural printing press machinery in the warehouse and incorporate it into their design work. Thus the design processes became an approach that was set out to work with, in and around, as well as upon the printing presses in the large open space.

The bookstore announced its new presence to the LX Factory complex with the design and construction of a large black steel door with a complementary yet startling bright yellow entrance. The stack of presses was set back from the door, allowing visitors to adjust to the overwhelming spectacle in front of them, as the three storey wall of machinery dominated the central hall of the large open warehouse space. The bookshop, two bars, counters, and rows upon rows of shelving, groaning under the weight of all kinds of publications, were then arranged around the machinery and appliances as well as the structures supporting them. The designers conceived the space in an ad hoc

fashion, with niches and spaces between the machinery becoming locations for book display, some shelving, or a bar, recessed into the structure supporting the heavy weight of the presses.

This contingency-based approach allowed the designers to create an improvised feel to the interior and in particular the relationship between new and old. A series of convenient staircases, previously allowing workers access to the presses, were utilised for getting to the layers of shelving. Satisfying codes and regulations for the change of use to a public building, the bookshop visitors can clamber all over the machinery, pick up books and fi nd a space to stop and read, have refreshments, or just marvel at the spectacle of the now silent equipment. At the top of the enormous contraptions, the structures of the machinery have afforded the designers some leeway to insert two meeting room levels. Formed in steel, they are conceived as open ‘trays’ within which to meet, rest, read, or merely take in the view of the surrounding spectacle and to catch your breath after you have managed to ascend the mountain of metal and machinery.

## The Bath Room

Location London, UK

Remodelled 2010

Designer Ben Kelly Design

![](images/f4a56c6dbe9b7affd2f7908bd0283906eb181a55e11ba675d37f3b6cd770e048.jpg)

Details and services such as materials, fi nishes, lighting, handrails, bathrooms, can often get overlooked in the design process. Yet all are fundamentally the elements and objects that the user of any space will physically come into contact with the most. In a bathroom, objects such as toilets, sinks, baths, taps, shower units, mirrors and lighting, along with surfaces such as tiling, come in a huge range of formats,

types and styles. The choices made when specifying them have huge ramifi cations for the design of the interior and in particular the creation of specifi c identities for both public and private spaces. The thoughtful design of spaces in which to explore and imagine how these might work, to consult with experts, and to select and arrange examples, along with being given assistance on how to deploy these objects, are practically non-existent.

The Bath Room is a studio space for designers and anybody creating bathrooms or service spaces with which to view, examine and explore how they can be designed, selected and then specifi ed. As its designer Ben Kelly states, it’s not a

traditional showroom, nor an exhibition, but instead a hands-on studio where objects and artefacts from a number of ranges of sanitary ware can be taken off the walls, or off the screen, played with, or seen in actual settings. In order to realise this type of space Kelly conceived the interior as a thought-provoking cabinet of curiosities – a playful space that promotes interaction with the objects themselves and invites guests to explore and engage as well as experimenting with the composition of all of the different ranges of products. The designers do all of this through the creation of an environment that mixes off-the-peg materials, such as mobile shelving units, steel piping, and bright orange plastic sheeting, with the readymade objects of the toilets, sinks and plumbing. The effect is the creation of a vibrant interior space, one that invites investigation and the hands-on testing of sampled objects.

The bathroom occupies 280 square metres of a second fl oor space in amongst the creative industries that it seeks to inspire in Clerkenwell, East London. In order to reinforce the distancing of this space from other more traditional showrooms, the entrance lobby is treated as an ‘air lock’: a space to decompress the visitor from their own preconceptions of a toilet showroom before they enter The Bath Room. Lit with blue neon the lobby acts like a symbolic ‘plunge-pool’: a threshold that is cleansing the visitor of their presumptions, as they are bathed in blue light before they enter the well-lit second fl oor exhibition show space.

The single-fl oor space was organised into a series of zones, a strategy used not only to order the goods for display, but also designed to enact a noncompartmentalised method of exhibiting products. This was an important consideration because it not only allowed spaces to fl ow into each other, dispelling

the notion that the private space has to be enclosed and hidden away, but most importantly allowed customers to see objects against each other, making possible unusual or unforeseen juxtapositions across the open space. In order to enhance the opportunity for chance connections, the layout of the products and the environments used to contain them was unusually innovative and not seen in a traditional sanitaryware showroom. For instance, the designer range was located next to bathing and wellness, and were all contained in an off-the-peg mobile shelving system. Usually found in archives or libraries, the system is on a track that is moved by a hand-wheel in order to open each cabinet to reveal a series of bathroom appliances and increasing the chances of surprise. The slow slide of each cabinet opened up not just a different set of appliances but also a riot of different orange and grey interior fi nishes, contrasting sharply with the well-mannered brushed steel exteriors of the cabinets.

The visual language of plumbing was used to emphasise the dynamism of the small compact interior. Tracks and other connecting devices such as pipework, normally hidden away in walls or fl oors, were exposed and utilised as directional instruments for fl oor, wall and ceiling. Track systems for fl ooring and lighting also unite the mobile shelving system, and combined with the lighting and piping connected the different zones of the showroom to become an instrument of movement, signalling the fl ow of products and people through the space. The enclosed presentation room was conceived to be a transparent box wrapped in glass panels and lifted off the fl oor with bright orange-bracketed feet. Although bolted to the fl oor they look temporary, and appear to also be fl uid, promoting the sense of movement in a

space that appears to be constantly on the move.

There is nothing in this interior that has been overlooked; as every element, object, detail and fi nish has been very carefully considered, specifi ed and then creatively applied. It affords the visitor the impression of an exquisitely well-crafted room of objects and spaces, one made to engage with and promote the idea of looking and enjoying the often-overlooked details of one of the most unnoticed rooms.

## The Mistral Wine Store

Location São Paolo, Brazil

Remodelled 2012

Designer Studio Arthur Casas

![](images/046d77eb06ccdc9d772f4849097e9b7387324583e749bd5db20b4da4a3d82e53.jpg)

Figure 3.44 A reading room is enclosed by books on wine held in-between fl oor-toceiling timber beams at the back of the main double-height space of the store.

Figure 3.45 Plan: The organic language of the design is derived from the fl uid nature of the product it is displaying. Visitors are moved through a series of rooms, in a journey that carefully orchestrates a number of back and forth routes.

The form and also the content of an object can provide the inspiration for the design of a new interior space. Wine retailer Mistral commissioned Studio Arthur Casas to design a new store, one that would physically represent the ideas and values of a company that did most of its business on the internet. Without losing the digital dimension that the company valued so dearly, the new space needed to exemplify the virtual brand of the company.

As well as this, it needed to extol the more visceral delights of sampling fi ne wines. In short, the design of the site needed to attract new clients as well as retain existing customers, elevating and inspiring them to ensure that they felt like a clientele of knowledgeable wine tasting connoisseurs. The small, 100 square metre, store was situated in a busy upmarket shopping centre in São Paolo. The double-height space needed to house a sales area, cellar, storage, interactive gallery, a reading room and places for wine tasting, as a small group or as part of a larger party.

Studio Arthur Casas were infl uenced by the sensual and poetic qualities of the wine they were displaying and selling, and the bottles in which it was being contained. Aware of the amount of programming they needed to organise in such a small space, they utilised an organic, sinuous, curved design language for the interior. This was to ensure that both the poetic and the pragmatic dimensions of the project could be satisfi ed and ultimately resolved into a cohesive whole.

The designers organised the interior as a journey, a route or path between the fi ve spaces of the shop. At the beginning of this route was a tall, double-height curved wall. This enticed the shoppers to journey into the store and engage with each aspect of the sequence of interior spaces. From the walkway of the mall, a glass wall, enclosing the sales area, contrasted with an inviting curved corridor: a space lined with wine bottles. The designers created the gently curved entrance in order to invite customers deeper into the space and to discover the unique brand of the company, and hopefully the contents of each bottle. The gradual curve created a path to the centre of the store where the fi rst sales table was positioned. From here doorways revealed the next part of the journey into the reading room, or the interactive gallery. The display wall of bottles appeared to be fl oating, as they were suspended by their necks and thus projected bottom-end out into the organic shaped corridor, exaggerating the curve of the space.

Between the backlit opaque glass of the wine display wall, and the tall vertical timber strip upper wall, a dark stripe of black glass concealed several screens: each was activated by guests touching the wine bottles placed within each section. Once a bottle was picked up or even just touched the screen revealed general information about the wine. This clever device ensured that the wall became animated with information, images and stories of the wine. The bottles were organised by region, and were designed to provide a visual clue to shoppers outside the store in the mall, as well as provide a constant undulating wall

![](images/d986607ad4e086a1e42da756964b16c4338674bd4de7ce92ba168f3a8153991a.jpg)

of texture and animated graphics at the beginning of the interior journey. All the technical equipment was hidden within the walls. Where the curved wall of the entry was separated from the structure and walls of the existing mall site, a room was formed. This created a small double-height cellar with its own air-conditioning system. Separated from the main corridor by a glass door, this was not open to the public. It was designed to be a secret place to store rare wines, which only the chosen few could access.

Inside the gallery space, wines that were displayed on the wall could be brought to an interactive table. This was designed to showcase a monthly selection of special wines. Sensors underneath each bottle allowed customers to place it on the table and get information related to the wine, data that was projected onto the fl at screen. Effectively, when positioned on the surface, the bottle became the equivalent of a keyboard mouse. When it was twisted, information such as location, interviews with producers and notation on taste and other information would appear on the fl at surface of the table.

![](images/43f289ac594f24f03efd3b9e0cd43f1302d66b3021b490ce0e69d26ad410f553.jpg)  
Figure 3.46 A sales table surrounded by wine bottles punctuates the initial part of the journey through the interior.

A reading room was created at the rear of the store. Books on wine were held between fl oor-to-ceiling wooden laths. A Lina Bo Bardi chair invited customers to relax and read next to the double-height wall that was covered with bottles. Secreted in the wall, and separating the entrance and the interactive gallery, was a hidden stair. This ascended to the tasting space in the mezzanine, where invited guests could sample wine away from the hustle and bustle of the shopping mall below.

In every space the bottles of wine were presented in surprising ways. Even though omnipresent, the object of the bottle was never rendered monotonous. Instead it becames a motif and a texture that was used to distinguish the ambiance of each context in which it was carefully positioned, whether as giver of information or as decoration. Wine holds sensations and stories that are extremely diverse. The designers used this multiplicity of possibilities, inviting the discovery of the content of each wine bottle, the starting point of the design project. In Mistral, the presentation of the product cannot be dissociated from the information it contains. Therefore the truly innovative aspect of this project consists in trying to bring together architecture, product, information and interaction into one single interior entity.
