![](images/555be3fa81c384982d9b8188c22556a8ed4d695384ef34074990ab9259a83b8e.jpg) HeikkiOrvola,Aurora glassware range for Nuutajärvi, 1972 – later produced by littala # heikki orvola (inish b. 1943) ![](images/2a6a7ea5f8b2ef59b3da3b75fe16caa90855f68287e71e9ad10b9905713997fc.jpg) The Finnish glassware designer Heikki Orvola studied ceramics at the Taideteollinen Korkeakoulu (University of Art and Design) in Helsinki between 1963 and 1968. Following this, he was offered a summer placement at the Nuutajärvi glassworks byKaj Franck. He was also offered a position in the product design department at Arabia, but opted instead for the greater creative freedom afforded by Nuutajärvi. As both companies were part of the Wärtsilä group, Orvola's first design for Nuutajärvi was a range of glassware that was intended to complement a ceramic tableware service that Richard Lindh (b. 1929) had designed for Arabia. Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, Orvola designed several ranges of utility drinking glasses, such as the elegant Aurora(1972), Lili (1980) and Saturno (1984), as well as bowls, vases and a candle lantern that were massproduced using blow-moulding or pressing techniques. In 1976, he studied at the Pilchuck Glass Workshop in Stanwood, Washington, which had been founded by the renowned glass artist Dale Chihuly (b. 1941) andwhich was, accordingto one of its students, “an artist's heaven, and you don't even have to die”, While in America, Orvola also became friends with the glass artist Marvin Lipofsky(b. 1938), who introduced him to the glass artist community in the San Francisco area. The highly creative and artistic nature of American art glass profoundly influenced Orvola, as did his travels to Mexico, China, Russia, Nepal and India. During this period he produced some of the most expressive art glass created by any Scandinavian designer working at the time. He used sandblasting, cutting, lustre finishes, bubbling, filigree techniques and casting, all to dramatic effect. His designs ranged widely in style and execution, from his delicate swirling filigree plates (1980) to his free-blown lidded bowls (1983), which had curious abstracted vegetal forms and incorporated the Ravenna technique that had been pioneered by Sven Palmqvist. His later limited edition pieces, such as the Maya vase (1994) for Nuutajärvi's Pro Arte collection or his sculptural totemlike Grand Cana/ bottles (1998), were explorations of material, form and decoration that clearly showed the influence of Post-Modernism. As Orvola noted, “abundant decoration is not a typical phenomenon in Finnish design. I reckoned it was time to offer people a few colourful items of tableware." In ![](images/c48fdcd2c6adbc8320635d65f7bf690b475ceac467c4f78371cbf7cbed2c67c0.jpg) contrast to his one-off glassware, Orvola also designed production ceramics for Arabia, such as theMoreeni (1992)and the 24hr(1996) tableware ranges, which have basic, undecorated functional forms that project a sense of homely simplicity. Orvola's career, which is typical of many Scandinavian designers, demonstrates hisextraordinary ability to produce designs of high integrity for both one-offstudio manufacture and industrial mass-production. Beyond glassware and ceramics, Orvola has also designed textiles for Marimekko and enamels for Järvenpää. He received the Pro Finlandia Medal in 1984 and his work was the subject of a major retrospective exhibition at the Helsinki Museum of Applied Arts in 2000 – ranging from glass sculptures to well-honed, practical tools for living. As Tapio Periäinen has observed, “Heikki Orvola is a tangible example of the way in which a designer or artist can express and thus show what kinds of industrial utensils are durable, timeless, and conserve nature, and in spite of this are beautiful. ... Orvola helps to point out a way for us all to what is called a sustainable future, an environmentally friendly way of life."
