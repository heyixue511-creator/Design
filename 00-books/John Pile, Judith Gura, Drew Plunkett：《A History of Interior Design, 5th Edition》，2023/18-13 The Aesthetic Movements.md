The Shaker aesthetic evolved from interpretation of Christian doctrine dating to the eighteenth century and the renunciation of, what they considered to be, worldliness—the flaunting of unnecessary acquisitions. Somehow, and collectively, they evolved artifacts that required no training in design or manufacture to appreciate them and to be moved by them. The Victorians had their own interpretations of the same Christian doctrines, which they expressed through acquisition and display, but their artifacts found their roots in medieval ecclesiastical architecture. Victorian taste was instinctive, ostentatious, and seen to be evidence of the fruits of honest labor. Those fruits also supported a leisured class who had time to absorb elitist aesthetic sensibilities. This leisured class was uncomfortable with popular taste and factory production, and gravitated to objects made by hand by skilled craftsworkers, usually to designs created by wealthy bohemians. That work would come to be classified as Arts and Crafts. ## BRITAIN: ARTS AND CRAFTS MOVEMENT The Victorian interior, bulging with artifacts, crossed barriers of class and income. A description of a cottage parlor in a Welsh ironworks noted a “French-polished chest of drawers covered with a profusion of glass and other articles . . . a glass-fronted cupboard filled with china and glass and displaying ostentatiously silver sugar tongs and set of spoons . . . a mahogany table with a bright copper tea-kettle reposing on it.” In Britain, the children of early industrialists lived comfortably on the proceeds of their parents’ enterprise. Some, disinclined or ill-equipped to join family businesses, declared themselves to be artistic. They disparaged and despaired of popular taste and the factory-made objects designed to satisfy it. These beneficiaries of nineteenth-century prosperity imagined that they could see virtues in living a simpler life, surrounded by handmade objects crafted by contented artisans. Factories could make facsimiles of medieval objects but they thought mass production responding to popular taste was distorting Gothic purity, while handmade artifacts were easy to like because they eschewed excessive decoration, and hand crafting meant that they bore the physical marks of their production, which added an aura of authenticity. ## Arbiters of Taste and Virtue Artistic gentlemen and social reformers, such as John Ruskin (1819–1900), remained enthusiasts for medievalism and a return to craft production by the nineteenth-century equivalents of medieval craft guilds that would hand down skills and safeguard aesthetic standards. Ruskin was an independently wealthy patriarch able to pay for craft skills and could afford to believe that good design could not be produced by a machine. He and like-minded reformers also presumed that by improving living and working conditions they would improve working-class morals. He condemned retailers who, he said, deceived customers into buying aesthetically questionable items. Home, he said, was “the place of Peace . . . a sacred place, a vestal temple, a temple of the hearth. . . .” He suggested “making all retail dealers salaried officers in the employ of trade guilds” who would dispense Ruskin-approved advice on taste. Charles Eastlake (1836–1906) in Hints on Household Taste in Furniture, Upholstery and other Details (1868) also worried that an aesthetically innocent public were in danger of being exploited by shopkeepers. Customers abetted by shopkeepers continued to buy what they liked, a Darwinian process in which the popular, however meretricious, survived. For the less wealthy and less idealistic, machine-made wallpaper could transform a room cheaply and quickly but there were conventions: flock papers were appropriate for drawing rooms, sitting rooms, dining rooms and libraries; marble patterns with a gloss finish were correct for halls, stairwells and corridors; moire —suggestive of watered silks—or small-patterned flocks were favored in bedrooms. W. M. Thackeray, the author of Vanity Fair (1838), writing in the British humorous magazine Punch in 1848 said, “. . . the public wants a thing, therefore it is supplied with it; or the public is supplied with a thing therefore it wants it.” ## Morris William Morris (1834–1896) was the driving force of the Arts and Craft movement. The son of a wealthy businessman, he was a typical young Victorian of independent means who presumed himself suited to artistic endeavor and became a poet, a novelist, a translator of Icelandic sagas and a socialist activist. While a student at Oxford, he was inspired by the city’s medieval buildings and began to oppose industrialization. Although he would become an atheist, he was influenced by Ruskin’s Christian Socialist writings and used his inherited wealth to set up rural craft communities. In 1861 he formed a decorative-arts company with three Pre-Raphaelite painters Dante Gabriel Rosetti (1828–82), Edward Burne-Jones (1833–98), and Ford Madox Brown (1821–93). They were unlikely businessmen but were joined by the engineer Peter Paul Marshall (1830– 1900), the academic Charles Faulkner (1833–92), and the architect Philip Webb (1831–1915). They called themselves The Firm. They were ideologically committed to craft techniques and explained to customers the vulgarity of the machine-made. The Firm established itself as a radical purveyor of furniture, wood and stone carvings, metal work, stained glass, and murals. It aspired to have handmade decoration accepted as a fine art and to introduce a guild to control the quality of artisan output. It recruited apprentices from the Industrial Home for Destitute Boys in Euston, London. Inevitably, given their backgrounds, the original partners diverged. In 1859–60 Webb designed the Red House for Morris (13.1), who designed parts of the interior (13.2). ![](images/33cfb53dfd4adf38e0b41bea1212a580a7c199bbc3b235557b11e7a8866fdbea.jpg) Philip Webb, Red House, Bexleyheath, Kent, England, 1859–60. While the stretched newels of the stair and the elongated finials that crown them might be seen as Gothicisms, the simplicity of the solid banister, the exposed soffit of the stair, the simple architrave and skirting moldings all demonstrate a movement away from Gothic Revival flamboyance to an aesthetic shaped by Arts and Crafts principles. The Firm became Morris and Co. and flourished under the patronage of artistically inclined and wealthy customers. It was not the clientele Morris might have hoped for. Around 1870, Webb designed a chair with an ingenious adjustable back that Morris and Co. manufactured. It became known as the Morris chair (13.3) as it is not unknown for the partner with the higher profile—or indeed a husband in partnership with his wife—to be given credit for work produced in a partnership. ![](images/7348b56ecac05541d9afa3d0dee190908e179408f314bdc84ac40aad7e3a6494.jpg) Phillip Webb, adjustable chair, manufactured by Morris & Co., England, c. 1870. A precursor, in an early version, of the modern adjustable chair that became known as the Morris Chair because it was made by the firm founded by William Morris. In the midst of setting up and funding social experiments, creating and funding the Kelmscott Press, for which he wrote twenty-three of the fifty-three handmade books it published, Morris designed the textiles and wallpapers (13.4) that made his reputation and continue to help sustain the press. These were distinguished from other products of the period by their restrained simplification of plant forms. ![](images/50ae96f370530a5802d136156b9c85804b5556f4184432c7e6fa64ef7fafacc1.jpg) William Morris, Pimpernel wallpaper design, manufactured by Jeffrey & Co., for Morris & Co., England, 1876. The Pimpernel design, with its rich blues and greens could dominate any interior. ## Ashbee The Arts and Crafts movement flourished and the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society was formed in London in 1887. It held its first show in 1888. The same year, architect and designer Charles Robert Ashbee (1863–1942) established the Guild and School of Handicraft, a cooperative based on the model of a medieval guild, to train and improve the lot of a few disadvantaged inhabitants of London slums. The guild employed around fifty men and, in 1902—still in pursuit of the ideal of a rural community of healthy, well-behaved artisans—relocated to the bucolic Cotswolds. There, Ashbee designed jewelry and silverware that was made by craftsmen and proudly bore the marks of their hammers. The objects were exquisite but expensive. It is impossible to say if, had they cost less, their elegance would have triumphed over the market-led outpourings of industry. ## Voysey The most complete and accomplished exponent of Arts and Crafts principles was the architect Charles Francis Annesley Voysey (1857–1941) who designed every fixture and fitting in the irresistibly pretty houses he created for prosperous middle-class clients. Typical of these is Hollymount of 1907 in Knotty Green, Buckinghamshire, for which he designed all the furniture and fittings. He made subtle variations on individual pieces so that his interiors seem to have been assembled over time—but by one discerning eye (13.5). He worked extensively as a consultant to manufacturers over a wide range of products, from beautiful yet simple cast-iron fireplaces (13.6) to a comprehensive range of wallpapers, carpets, rugs, tiles, and textiles. Like Morris, he favored “flatness” in motifs derived from nature, explaining that “an attempt at realism provokes [unfavorable] comparison with the subtleties of nature” (13.7). ![](images/777cddcd472567b5f422e956979fa80c0e285ca0e7b4748e2928bc9f7d58234f.jpg) 13.5 Charles Francis Annesley Voysey, Hollymount, Knotty Green, Buckinghamshire, England, 1907. His design in c. 1914 for a music cabinet has painted decoration reminiscent of work by Morris. He was not averse to stylistic shifts, and traveled from Neo-Tudor to Neo-Georgian by way of Arts and Crafts (13.8). ![](images/17e62e326526a034729a8a16b03b278629b0d7e3affb3d77041afac718c72de1.jpg) M. This cabinet is covered with painted ornament in the manner that Morris had introduced fifty years earlier. ## Asian Influences In 1853 the U.S. navy under Commodore Matthew Perry sailed their steam-powered, paddlewheeled frigate into Japanese waters with orders from President Millard Fillmore to oblige the Japanese to open their ports to American trade. Coercion worked and Japanese goods began to filter through to the West. For those in Britain who had had enough of Neo-Gothic excess the elegant restraint of Japanese artifacts offered an alluring alternative, even pieces produced for the Western market were made by artisans with traditional tools and skills and appeared to be suffused with a pagan spirituality that offered an alternative to the Christianity that shaped the nineteenth century’s social and cultural mores. ![](images/39fbaa4802034ef9218ecd98a5c26808f56f1c6768b6049866e5eccd60de9d06.jpg) Edward Godwin, sideboard, London, England, c. 1870. The influence of Japan is clear in the thin, ebonized mahogany frame and silver-plated handles. Architect and designer Edward William Godwin (1833–86) deserted his polychromatic Neo-Gothic palette for ebonized finishes and delicate, fragile furniture. (13.9). His Art Furniture Company produced furniture, textiles, wallpapers, and tiles, all influenced by Japanese examples, and were sold in Liberty & Co. He designed a studio house for, and probably in collaboration with, James Abbott McNeill Whistler (1834–1903), an American painter infatuated with Japanese art. Whistler had been involved in the decoration of the dining room in a London House that became known as the Peacock Room (13.13). Originally created in 1876–7 by architect and designer Thomas Jeckyll (1927–81), another enthusiast for things Japanese, it had Godwin furniture, leather-covered walls and an intricate system of shelves idiosyncratically arranged to display Japanese blue and white porcelain. Whistler painted the room, including its doors and window shutters, with decoration based on peacock feathers and called it A Harmony in Blue and Gold; he had called his portrait of his mother Arrangement in Gray and Black No. 1 (1871). ![](images/c95b8152dbd3eb9ae437e0d0a15af217fc98bf70b0c13597e42c156e79a6b032.jpg) 13.13 Thomas Jeckyll and James McNeill Whistler, Peacock Room, London, 1876–7. Within this dining room, Whistler introduced Japanese themes into an Arts and Crafts environment. The room, with shelves to display Japanese porcelain, had been designed by Jeckyll. Whistler’s blue and gold wall painting was inspired by peacock feathers. Peacock feathers were a popular device among artists and designers of the Aesthetic movement that marked the descent of Arts and Crafts into fey self-regard during the 1890s. Its figureheads were the flamboyant playwright and arbiter of taste Oscar Wilde and artist Aubrey Beardsley whose drawings, undoubtedly influenced by Japanese prints, were—when not obscene —undoubtedly perverse. Wilde was said to have complained, “I find it harder and harder every day to live up to my blue china,” at a time when imported blue china—from China—was definitively chic. As a diligent aesthete, Wilde would have been distressed by the British designed and manufactured quasi-Chinese Willow Pattern range that was exceptionally popular in humble homes. However degenerate its proponents might appear, the Aesthetic interior was ascetic, denying itself the visual gluttony that had devoured the High Victorian interior. The author and cartoonist Sir Osbert Lancaster explained that “cheerful magentas and sulfur yellows were banished . . . in favor of sage green, peacock blue, and every variety of ocher.” Decadence was not confined to London. In France, writer Joris-Karl Huysmans published his novel A Rebours (Against Nature, 1884), in which a reclusive aesthete, contemptuous of bourgeois society, retreated into a world of studied aestheticism—the interior of his home. Huysmans wrote that it was about a man “seeking refuge in illusions of extravagant fantasy, living alone, far from his century, among memories of more congenial times, of less base surroundings.” The hero was said to be based on Robert de Montesquiou, a French count and aesthete who had curated his own world within his Paris home and who declared that “an apartment is a mood” so suggesting that an interior need not conform to prevailing taste but should defy it to define the personality of its inhabitant and creator. Whistler painted Montesquiou’s portrait and called it Arrangement in Black and Gold (1891–92). ## ARTS AND CRAFTS IN THE UNITED STATES ## The Craftsman Movement Strong links between Britain and the United States made it likely that an Arts and Crafts movement would appear in the United States, a reaction, as in Britain, to the ornate mass production for the popular market. The leading figure in what came to be called the Craftsman movement was Gustav Stickley (1858–1942). His family owned furniture factories and he began his career by selling historic reproductions before becoming interested in the writings of Ruskin and Morris. In 1898 he traveled to England and to Paris and, when he returned to the United States, he began to design and produce solid oak furniture that was simpler in form but large and bulky in the American way. Ornamental detail was provided by the pegs, and mitered, and dovetailed joints of traditional furniture making (13.11). The style was also known as Mission, because of a perceived similarity to furniture made for Californian missions or Golden Oak after the yellow-brownish tone given to the wood by “fuming,” a smoking process that darkened the wood and accentuated its grain. ![](images/6d5e0aaaedbafe0075dc2ee2bc557f16d6ba978004463d4098e7f866230666ed.jpg) Gustav Stickley, armchair, United States, 1905. An illustration from a catalog from L. and J. G. Stickley’s Onondaga shop where many Craftsman-style designs were produced. In 1901, Stickley founded The Craftsman magazine to promote Arts and Crafts ideals with illustrations of “Craftsman” houses (13.12). It also carried articles on women’s rights, childcare, and social justice, along with photography, poetry, fiction, and other material that Stickley thought might appeal to his likely audience. The master of English Arts and Crafts, Voysey, contributed an article that was illustrated by photographs of his work. ![](images/90ab6457f013587b56b71c747a2a9ac46c465dfd70936b1291de1ae19d47dcb9.jpg) Dining room, United States, 1904. The magazine Craftsman, from which this illustration is taken, promoted the Craftsman movement. The chairs, the table, and sideboard shown are from Gustav Stickley’s shops. The room itself with its wooden wainscot and simple window detail is in strong contrast to popular taste of the time. Stickley was briefly joined in 1903 by Harvey Ellis (1842–1904) an architect and architectural illustrator, and unsigned illustrations of furniture that appeared in late 1903 in The Craftsman have been credited to him. The designs show less bulky forms and ornamentation that suggest the influence of Voysey and other British designers. However, given Ellis’s short time with Stickley it is thought more likely that the change of detail was made by his established employees such as LaMont Adelbert Warner (1876–1970), who was Stickley’s designer and head draftsman and responsible for many of the new designs. Warner and his wife, Emma V. Smith (1876–1923), designed and built a Craftsman-style house in Bronxville, New York in 1908. When it was featured in Good Housekeeping in 1909, the magazine noted that Warner also designed much of the furniture—including the electric-light fittings. Stickley’s commercial success encouraged other manufacturers to follow Craftsman principles and he found himself in competition with the Onondaga, New York, shops of his younger brothers, Leopold (1869–1957) and John George (1871–1921). Will H. Bradley (1868–1962) was a commercial illustrator who developed an enthusiasm for the Craftsman style and similar work from Britain. He was commissioned by the popular and influential Ladies’ Home Journal to develop ideas for houses, rooms and furnishings (13.14). His accomplished illustrations were rich in detail depicting lamps with stained-glass panels, metal objects in hammered copper, and art-pottery pieces. 13.14 ![](images/ce2729d9742d02a9ffc3265089242f981cf79b1f4b75105512c2271519e09e45.jpg) Will H. Bradley, interior illustration, 1902. Renderings of Bradley’s interior designs became familiar to an extensive American public through publication in the popular magazine Ladies’ Home Journal. His support for designs in the Arts and Crafts and Craftsman style, and his references to the work of Charles Rennie Mackintosh and the Vienna Secession designers (see Chapter 15), helped lead to acceptance of the furniture that came to be known as Mission style. Elbert Hubbard (1856–1915), who had begun as a traveling salesman for the Larkin Soap Company, set up his own community and a craft-based venture called Roycroft in New York state. Inspired by Morris’s Kelmscott Press, Hubbard published elegant handmade books and pamphlets, many of which he wrote, on art and literature. Roycrofters produced Mission furniture and artifacts, and Roycroft became something close to a cult built around Hubbard, but it, and its products, faded in importance after he and his wife died aboard the RMS Lusitania when it was torpedoed by a German U-boat in 1915, an event that brought the United States into World War I (1914–18)—and precipitated the end of the community. Traces of Craftsman influence survived after the war in the small single-story timber “bungalows” that had evolved in Bengal, India (hence the name) as comfortable homes for colonial administrators. Like many Indian ideas, the bungalow was imported into England, where the first example was built in 1869. Typically, they were wooden structures with shingled walls, porches, and verandas under overhanging eaves that were supported on wooden posts. Stickley saw the bungalow, with well-executed Arts and Crafts detailing, as perfect accommodation for the enlightened, and the artistic and the modest structure was well suited to embellishment by natural materials, in harmony with its location. It benefited from benign climates and became a popular building type in California and familiar in the backgrounds of early silent films that were shot on location in the suburbs of Los Angeles. ## Japanese Influences Charles Sumner Greene (1868–1957) and his brother Henry Mather Greene (1870–1954) established an architectural practice in California that drew on Craftsman tradition, Stick style, and the bungalow vernacular. Their wooden houses had low, pitched roofs and long overhanging eaves but it is the quality of the interiors, in the Robert R. Blacker House (1907) and the Gamble House (1908) in Pasadena (13.15) that distinguishes them. Wood is used with careful and intricate joinery details that draw on Japanese precedents and Arts and Crafts respect for quality handwork. ![](images/e57a96eacfcedc4cb91fa3081f016b00a5d2536ee3206c4bb1bf15565f790058.jpg) Greene and Greene, Gamble House, Pasadena, California, 1908. The Greene brothers’ work is based on an understanding of craft ideals, with woodwork detailing influenced by Japanese traditional design. Finely detailed furniture, lantern-like light fittings, and stained-glass inserts in windows generate original interiors that have a sense of tradition. In 1893 the brothers had visited the World’s Columbian Exposition in Chicago and saw examples of Japanese architecture. They were impressed by Japanese commitment to refinement of detail and Henry cited the example as important in the development of the practice they established a year later in Pasadena—even if the connection was not obvious. When Charles saw more Japanese work at the St. Louis World’s Fair of 1904, the influence became clearer. They exposed structural components, with each element clearly articulated without added decoration. Ornament is present in their interiors but restrained, panels of stained glass, pendant electriclight fittings and simple elegant, beautifully crafted furniture fill spacious entrance halls which flow, often unobstructed by doors, into major public rooms. Color comes principally from wood tones, mahogany, teak, rosewood, ebony, and maple, finished by polishing or oiling. Reds, blues, and greens feature in their stained glass and rugs. Bernard R. Maybeck (1862–1957) was born in New York and studied at the Ecole des Beaux-Arts in Paris before moving to California in 1892. His output was eclectic; he believed that each project deserved to be treated in a style appropriate to its function. He produced bungalows in what became known as the Western Stick style but, when he considered it appropriate, introduced traces of Gothic Revival, and Beaux-Arts Classicism. This mélange is obvious in his Christian Science Church of 1910, considered his greatest work (13.16). The ostensibly classical entrance portico also suggests Japanese influences with its clear articulation of structural elements. The interior dominated by the cruciform roof structure of raw, gently curved, timber beams, which might be found in a warehouse but are decorated by intricately carved wooden panels. The pulpit stands in front of a pierced screen that looks Gothic. An almost anarchic mix of rough-hewn timber roof beams incorporating carved panels and a pulpit that stands in front of a wooden screen that might be Gothic Revival. ## EMERGENCE OF WOMEN DESIGNERS In the nineteenth-century world of architecture and, to a lesser extent, interior design, the interior decorator was usually a woman and regarded as an amateur. Many women slipped into the activity because friends and acquaintances admired their work and then asked for advice thus offering creative opportunities. Most women decorator/designers had useful social connections and the self-confidence to deal with conducting and expanding businesses. ## United States ## Wheeler The emergence of professional woman designers began toward the end of the nineteenth century. Candace Wheeler (1827–1923) was a textile designer who became an early authority on interior decoration, working first in Colonial Revival, then the Arts and Crafts and Aesthetic movements. In 1877 she established the Society of Decorative Art in New York to help impoverished women support themselves through the decorative arts. Another founder member of the society was Louis Comfort Tiffany, and he and Wheeler formed Tiffany & Wheeler and collaborated on the interiors of significant private houses and public buildings. Yet Wheeler’s most significant job was the design of the interior of the Woman’s Building at the World’s Columbian Exposition held in Chicago in 1893. The building was designed by Sonya Hayden (1868–1953) the first female architectural graduate at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and was filled with exhibitions of women’s fine arts, crafts, and industrial products. In the same year, Wheeler wrote Household Art and, in 19o3, Principles of Home Decoration. ## Wharton Wheeler was not the first American woman to write about interior design. In 1897 Edith Wharton (1862–1937), before she had produced any of her novels and appalled by what she considered to be the vulgarities of the newly rich, collaborated with American architect and interior decorator Ogden Codman Jr., (1863–1951) to write The Decoration of Houses. It became a standard in American interior design. ## Britain ## Agnes and Rhoda Garrett In Britain, two campaigners for women’s right to vote were prepared to take on the men who had, incongruously, monopolized interior design. Agnes Garrett (1845–1935) and her cousin Rhoda Garrett (1841–82) were at the forefront of the emergence of specialist interior practice in both residential and commercial work (13.17 and 13.18). They moved in artistic social circles and in 1874 founded A. & R. Garrett, House Decorators, the first British interior-decorating business to be run by women. Two years later, they published Suggestions for House Decoration in Painting, Woodwork and Furniture. They facetiously advised readers to take advice offered by decorators “cum grano salis” (with a grain of salt) and then, among other opinions, advised against making “a thing or a material look like what it is not,” against hiding the “construction of your house or of any part of your furniture,” for the use of a “considerable amount of plain neutral color in your rooms,” and, significantly given the year, against “overloading . . . rooms with ornament.” All are sentiments that might have been voiced by the architectural reformers of their time—and later. ![](images/0a572e472aa594e1319c95a0aa2915fa142e15cd614727b3136ffa3255f05630.jpg) A fragment of the ceiling hand-painted by the Garretts for their home and studio at 2 Gower Street, close to the Senate House Library of London University where the ceiling has been stored, in fragments, since 1975. The movements that emerged, in Britain and the United States, as reactions against the excesses of Gothic Revival shared with it an inclination to look for a new aesthetic in the past—with the revival of hand skills and creative communities. Both found their models in medieval Europe but they also found something in the unfamiliar artifacts and attitudes of Japan. Both examples encouraged restraint but restraint could not counter the popular appetite for excess, and, as Gothic Revival faded, that appetite was satisfied in increasingly eclectic ways.
