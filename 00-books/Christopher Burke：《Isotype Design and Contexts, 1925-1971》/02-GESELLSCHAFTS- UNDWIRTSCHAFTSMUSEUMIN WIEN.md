Vereinfachte Mengenbilder Sich merken, ist besser als Genaue Zaklen vergessen.

Neben die geschichtlichen Museen, die Vergangenheit bewahren, tritt im 20. Jahrhundert das Museum der Gegenwart. Zu den Museen der Technik und der Hygiene gesellt sich heute das Museum für Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft, Die modernen Menschen wollen sich über ihre Lebensbedingungen Rechenschaft geben. Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft konnte bisher mit den Mitteln der Museumstechnik nicht allgemein verständlich dargestellt werden. Die neue, von Wien aus rasch verbreitete Bildstatistik vermag es. An die Stelle schwer deutbarer Tabellen und Kurven tritt das Mengenbild aus Reihen gleich großer, farbiger Symbolzeichen, Bunte Tafelbilder, Magnettafeln, Leuchtbilder prägen sich dem Gedächtnis durch Form und Farbe ein. Zum starren Bild tritt das bewegte, Zeichenfilme des Museums auf Grund der Wiener Methode.

The existing, permanent exhibition of the Museum (at Parkring 12) remained open, containing the department of 'Housing, settlement and town-planning'.6⁵ The Museum had also acquired exhibition rooms for its section of‘Social hygiene and social insurance' in a special wing of the people's apartment building' named Am Fuchsenfeld.6 This was one of the largest communal blocks erected by the Vienna municipality, which spanned several streets in the city's twelfth district and contained 6o4 apartments, 29 shops, baths, laundries, a kindergarten and day-care centre, a clinic, and meeting & lecture halls.

N42, 45 Municipal apartment block Am Fuchsenfeld, Vienna. Constructed 1924-5. Architects Heinrich Schmid and Hermann Aichinger.

In some ways, museum' is a misleading word to describe the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum: the nature of its exhibits made it an unconventional museum (even by today's standards), and it certainly was not an art gallery. Neurath offered an alternative description that is perhaps most fitting:

It is a popular educational institute for social enlightenment, which seeks to spread social and economic education through exhibitions, publications, lectures, slides, films and other aids, primarily in Vienna & Austria but also abroad.

He was conscious of reshaping the definition of a museum:

The Museum direction is of the view that a decentralization of museums is appropriate in a big city. It currently has exhibitions at three sites and, when possible, will have more. Museums should not be centrally situated, monumental installations, rather movable constructions, so that one can easily redesign them on changing the exhibition.6⁸

Neurath's ambition to reach the broad masses was perhaps best realized in the fourth of the Museum's Viennese locations: a small, street-corner showroom at the head office of the Städtische Versicherungsanstalt (Municipal insurance service) in the heart of the old city.

It was called the ‘Zeitschau' – a 'topical show', although Neurath later translated it as ‘march of time', alluding to the well-known American newsreel. Entry was free and he claimed that it received 2,ooo visitors per day.6ª In addition to charts dealing with the Museum's typical themes, this venue contained so-called psycho-technical apparatuses', which Neurath described as having the purpose of 'self-teaching'.? Marie Neurath explained later the populist intention behind the Zeitschau:

Rousing a quick reaction is different from the long lasting influence or stimulation which is the aim of education. Our museums and exhibitions were in general of an educational type and rather severe in subject matter, though colourful and attractive. Visitors were not numerous, though certain individuals came again and again and brought friends along. Otto Neurath knew that other attractions must be added if the number of visitors should be larger. He did so at a certain corner show in Vienna where he had not only recent events in photo and diagrammatical explanation; he had certain apparatus displayed where people could test their skill or intelligence. This place was crowded each lunchtime."1

The daily newspaper of the Social Democrat Party, Die Arbeiterzeitung, reported on the opening of the exhibition:

So one passes from chart to chart without noticing that one has entered a museum, because museums are all about hallowed halls, colonnaded passageways, plush carpets and liveried attendants. For instance, if people were really expected to visit a museum of economic statistics, most would turn away in boredom. This is precisely one of the purposes achieved by setting up this shop: that people should absorb pictorial material in passing. This is a novelty here. The concept originates in Austria but comes to us via America. From there, over the great water, the director of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum, Dr Otto Neurath, was approached about doing something for clients waiting in banks. The pointless sitting around makes waiting rooms unpleasant places. The Americans, however, wanted their customers to have a good memory of their bank building or shop premises. And so he suggested transforming these waiting rooms into little museums, in which one can gain all kind of useful knowledge while waiting. The idea struck a nerve and now we have such a museum of the street here in Vienna.7²
