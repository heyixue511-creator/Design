# STANLEY TIGERMAN b.1930 OUTSPOKEN CHICAGO ARCHITECT WHOSE UNCONVENTIONAL DESIGNS USE VISUAL PUNS AND ALLEGORY. BELOW Vernacular-style weekend home clad in corrugated steel, Lakeside, Michigan, 1983. Known for a propensity to speak his mind, Stanley Tigerman is equally known for conventionchallenging architecture, despite his continued admiration for the pure aesthetic of Mies van der Rohe.38 The contrary streak surfaced early in his career, when he was one of the Chicago Seven\* architects, an anti-establishment group that lobbied for greater diversity in Chicago architecture. Though almost all his work has been in and around that city, where he was born, his reputation is international. After a year at Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Tigerman worked for several Chicago firms and served in the United States Navy before returning to school to complete his architectural studies at Yale, receiving both a BArch and MArch. He opened his office after graduating in 1961. Resisting any attempts at self-promotion—"I see architecture as an ethical pursuit"39—he developed the practice gradually. In i986 it became Tigerman McCurry Architects, in partnership with his wife, Margaret McCurry (the two work independently on most projects). Unlike most architectural offices, the firm operates as a teaching environment as well as a practice, encouraging both individuality and collaboration among its employees. In a city steeped in architectural heritage both classical and contemporary, Tigerman's work makes its own statement; his thought-provoking buildings almost always meld classic modernist themes with elements of high-tech and the postmodern vocabulary. Tigerman's buildings are generally based on geometric solids, with little ornament to dilute the strength of the forms, and he is particularly fond of the grid, which reappears as both ornament and structural element in his buildings and interiors; he used it to dramatic effect in 1986 in a showroom for FORMICA (see under "Edited Design"), highlighting the firm's ColorCore laminate in two interlocking, three-dimensional grids that create a labyrinth effect.4º He is fond of allegory and humor, giving projects titles like Kosher Kitchen for a Jewish-American Princess (1978) or Dante's Bathroom Addition (198o) in a 2o12 exhibition of his work. Some of his puns are visual; his coffee service for Alessi has hands gripping the tray in place of conventional handles, and a chair for Herman Miller has a footrest that suggests a stuck-out tongue. He also designed several tabletop pieces for SWID POWELL (see essay under "Edited Design"). Although much of Tigerman's product design has this subtext of playfulness, his penchant for satire emerges unfiltered in his "architoons," imaginative sketches that poke fun at architectural icons and that have been widely published. Disliking the constriction of most architectural study programs, in 1994 Tigerman co-founded Archeworks, a multidisciplinary, graduate-level design school that functions more as laboratory than classroom.
