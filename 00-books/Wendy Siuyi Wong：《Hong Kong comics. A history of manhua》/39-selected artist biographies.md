# selected artist biographies

## AU-YEUNG, Craig (1961–)

Au-Yeung Craig, often known simply as Craig, began drawing comics in 1981 while he was still in school. As a student, his work was published in Ming Pao’s Campus and Nonsense pages, and in Students’ Times Magazine. After receiving a degree in graphic design from Hong Kong Polytechnic in 1987, Au-Yeung worked as a disc jockey and art director for a radio station. His first graphic novel, Three Times Seven Equals Twenty-one, was published in late 1995. Since then, Au-Yeung has been a full-time comic artist; his work appears regularly in magazines and newspapers, and in comics such as Three Times Seven Equals Twentyone, My Own Private Heaven I to III, Siu Ming, and My Angel. In 1999 he established Cockroach Tenicolor Comix Quarterly, a quarterly venue for the work of young, alternative comics artists.

## BAO, Dik-cheung (1928–)

Known as Lo Fu, Hueng Chou, Uncle Seven, and Uncle Bao, Bao Dik-cheung came to Hong Kong around 1951, where he cofounded East Comics with Lee Waiman. Two years later, his popular comic Little Angeli, appeared; it was soon followed by other titles targeted at younger readers produced by Bao and his two brothers. Bao also served as editor of a children's cartoon supplement for Sing Tao Daily, and in the 1980s he briefly taught cartoon drawing at an art school.

## CHAN, Chi-dor ( ?)

Beginning in 1934, Chan's work was published in mainland newspapers. After World War II he moved to Hong Kong, where his comics, drawn under the pen name Uncle Luk, appeared in Sing Bao. He remained active in the comics industry for several decades; in the 1960s he was involved with the popular title Cartoons World.

## CHENG, Ka-chun (1918–2000)

Cheng Ka-chun published his first four-panel comic strips at the age of fifteen. During the Sino-Japanese war, Cheng, whose aliases included Cho Chi and Szeto Yan, was active in a Guangzhou antiwar artists group. After the war, he became the editor of Wah Kiu Daily and published Manhua World with Lee Fan-fu and other artists. An exhibition of his fifty-year manhua career was held at the Hong Kong Arts Center in 1988. Cheng also published a book on the history of Hong Kong manhua in 1992.

## CHEUNG, Man-yau (1957–)

Cheung Man-yau began working as an assistant to Wong Yuk-long at the age of seventeen. During his long association with the artist, he worked on several of Wong's popular comics, including Drunken Master and Buddha's Palm. Later Cheung became the chief artist on Oriental Heroes. He is known as a great draftsman, though his own works, such as Tournament and Warrior Horse, have not sold particularly well.

## CHOA, Yat (1968–)

Choa Yat, whose real name is Leung Chung-kei, was trained as a teacher and worked in that profession for three years before he returned to college for a bachelor’s degree in fine arts. His comics first appeared in the newspaper Ming Pao in 1991. Since then, his published comics have included Sam's Manhua Series and Leung's Saga Series.

## DONG, Pui-sun ( ?)

Dong Pui-sun, also known as Chou Si-wai, Chou Sun, and Fung Yan-king, worked as an illustrator for the World of Wu-hap in the 1960s before beginning to draw comics for major newspapers in the mid-1970s. His works include the Hai-dai Chau series in New Daily, The Nonsense in Tin Tin Daily, and Boss in Oriental Daily.

## FUNG, Chi-ming (1960–)

After joining the industry at age sixteen, Fung Chiming worked for various penny papers, including Hei Bo, Ching Bo, and Lianhuantu Daily. His best-selling work, Dagger, Sword, Laugh, was published in 1987. Recent works by Fung, include Enemy of Heaven, King of Dagger, and Tribe of X.

## HEUNG-SHAN, A-wong (1938–)

Heung-shan A-wong started his career in the 1950s in the mainland's manhua industry. Since he emigrated to Hong Kong in 1964, Heung-shan, whose real name is Wong Siu-tao, has been a regular contributor to Manhua World, Sing Tao Evening Daily, Oriental Daily, Ming Pao, Express Daily, and other newspapers and magazines. His best-known work includes Dummy Seven and Beauty, which first appeared in 1966.

## HON, Man (1949–)

Hon Man began working for the penny papers at the age of fifteen. In the early 1970s, Hon, whose real name is Cheung Chun-man, began to specialize in ghost stories. At Jademan Comics in the 1980s, he was responsible for titles such as High Spirit of Ghost and Strange Story Collection. He left Jademan in 1988 to publish his own comics, including Hon Man’'s Night Stories, then joined Kwong's Publishing, where he worked on Ghost World. He began to fade from the manhua industry after that and worked as fortune-teller.

## HuI, Guan-man (1930–)

Hui Guan-man started to submit his work to newspapers in the early 1950s and quickly became one of Hong Kong's most popular and well-paid manhua artists. His works include Uncle Choi, Joke's Comics, Grandpa and Granddaughter, Young Couple, Miss Silly, and Handsome Duck. Hui retired in the 1980s.

## HuI, King-sam (?)

Hui King-sam first worked as an assistant on Ma Wingshing's The Chinese Hero. His own comic, The King ofStreet Fighter, became one of Jademan Comics’s bestsellers and spawned several imitators.

## KAM, Siu-man (1962–)

Kam Kin-man, known as Kam Siu-man, joined the comics industry at age sixteen as an assistant and later joined Jademan Comics. In the mid-1980s he gained acclaim for Tai-kung Pao, which appeared in Yuk-long Manhua Biweekly. After leaving Jademan, Kam went to Siu-keung Publishing, where he worked on Siu-man Classic Four Frames Works. Since 1997 his manhua strips have appeared mainly in newspapers and magazines.

## KHOO, Fuk-lung (1964–)

Khoo Fuk-lung joined Wong's Jademan Comics in 1980, where he worked on Siu-keung Manhua Collection, and Yuk-long Manhua Biweekly and drew covers for several other comics. His first individual work, Steel Warrior Adventures, appeared in 1989. In 1993 Khoo redrew Wong's famous work Solar Lord, which had been popular in the 1970s. By then, Khoo had become well-known for his own fighting comics.

## LAI, Tat-wing (1971–)

Lai Tat-wing, also known as Tat-tat-wing, first published his comics in Sau-sing Chai (Longevity Boy) when he was still in junior high school. His work was later used for theater-company publicity materials, and his short stories appeared in several magazines. In 1995 his first self-published work, Man-man Chu, Dak-dak Gao, received a positive response from alternativecomics readers. Since then he has drawn several other titles in the spirit of Man-man, and his works have developed a more mainstream audience.

## LAU, Lee-lee (1966–)

After graduating from Hong Kong Polytechnic with a degree in graphic design, Lau Lee-lee worked for a nongovernmental organization for five years. She began to draw comics for nonmainstream magazines and in 1998 became a full-time comics artist. Her first collection, Mom's Drawer Is at the Bottom, spurred discussions about feminism and gender-equality issues. Lau has since published comics in several major newspapers and contributed to Au-Yeung Craig’s Cockroach Tenicolor Comix Quarterly. Other titles by Lau include This Is How Star Should Really Be, Two Sunny Side Up, Tossing and Turning, The Beginning of the End, and Flat World.

## LAW, Guan-tsiu (1918–)

Law Guan-tsiu, who received formal art training in Guangzhou, came to Hong Kong after World War II. In 1953, he established the first full-color children's magazine in Hong Kong, Children's Paradise, which he worked on for thirty years. After retiring from the magazine, he started a studio, where he taught drawing classes. In the mid-1990s, Law was elected the visual artists representative to the Hong Kong Arts Development Council.

## LEE, Chi-ching (1963–)

Lee Chi-ching joined the penny paper Ching Bo in 1981 and later worked for Jademan Comics's Manhua King Weekly and Ghost Stories Collection. After Khoo Fuklung left Jademan, Lee worked on Khoo's Steel Warrior Adventures. Lee's most recognized work, The Story of Three Countries, was the first Hong Kong manhua to sell well in Japan. His most recent works are manhua versions of Jing Yong's epic sword-fighting novels.

## LEE, Dak-hong (1957–)

Lee Dak-hong began publishing comic strips in Hong Kong's major daily papers and magazines in 1980. His individual works include Manhua by Lee Dak-hong and Little Funny Booklet.

## LEE, Fan-fu (1906–1967)

The career of Lee Fan-fu, whose real name was Lee Tak-chun, began in Guangzhou in the late 1920s. In 1929, he worked with Ye Yin-quan to organize that city’s first comics magazine, The Sketch. Lee's most famous works from this period are Young Master and Old Master Ho. After World War II ended, Lee moved to Hong Kong, where he continued to draw Young Master; it was published in Sing Bao until he died. He also contributed to Manhua World, one of the most important comics in Hong Kong.

## LEE, Ling-hon (1928–)

Lee Ling-hon, whose real name is Lee King-wah, began to publish his work at the age of seventeen in mainland China. He continued to draw comics after moving to Hong Kong in 1949. Lee contributed to various newspapers through the 1950s and 1960s, as well as to such manhua publications as Caricature Weekly and Man-hua Daily. Lee moved to Canada in 1982.

## LEE, Shek-cheung (1928–)

Lee Shek-cheung came to Hong Kong in 1946 and began working for New Children's Biweekly Magazine. In 1959 he helped found Hong Kong's longestrunning children’s magazine, Little Friends Pictorial. A few years later, his four-panel strip Dai-wah and Siuwah began to appear regularly in a pro-China newspaper. That strip has been in print for almost twenty years.

## LEE, Wai-chun (?)

Lee Wai-chun is one of the very few well-established female manhua artists. Her first work, Miss Fa Fa (Miss Flower), was not a big hit, but it did lead to her next work, 13-Dot Cartoons, which was first published in July 1969. 13-Døt was very popular with female readers and remained in print until 1980. In July 1996, 13- Dot was republished as a “cartoon classic” by the biggest publisher in Hong Kong, Publication (Holdings) Limited. This version ceased in 1999.

## Li, Chi-tat (1965–)

Li Chi-tat has worked in the manhua industry since he was seventeen. He is best known for his realistic drawing style, which resembles the work of the Japanese manga artist Katsuhiro Otomo. Lee's works include Black Mask and The Assassination of Emperor Qin.

## LIAO, Bing-xiong (1915–)

Born in Guangzhou, Liao Bing-xiong went to a teacher’s college, then worked as a teacher and an art editor before starting to draw comics. During the Sino-Japanese War, Liao specialized in antiwar manhua; after the war he was a member of Renjian Huahui, a Chinese comics artists orgànization in Hong Kong. He returned to China in the early 1950s, and there he became one of the mainland's most important manhua artists.

## LUI, Yu-tin (1927–)

Lui Yu-tin, who was born in Hong Kong, drew comics for various Hong Kong and Guangzhou newspapers. His most famous work, Wu-lung Wong (King of Blunders), first appeared in Chum Daily in Guangzhou. After the war Wu-lung Wong became the most popular comic series in southern China and Hong Kong, and it inspired several movies during the 1950s and 1960s. Lui moved to Canada in the 1980s.

## MA, Long (1952–)

Ma Long, whose real name is Yuan Jao-chiu, began working in the comics business in 1983. His first comics appeared in Sun Pao, Ming Pao, and Sing Tao Daily Ma’s individual works include Fifty Steps, Ma Long’s Political Cartoon Collection, The Transition Period 1991–1992 Manhua Collection, and Humorous Cartoons by Ma Long. In 1991 he worked as an editor for Fan Dou Magazine, and in 1998 he edited Special Administrative Region Manhua Magzine, funded by the Hong Kong Arts Development Council.

## MA, Wing-shing (1961–)

Ma Wing-shing began his career at the age of fifteen at Ching Bo, where his comics included The Experiment of the Devil, Five Brothers, and Charming. In the 1980s Ma joined Jademan Comics and created The Chinese Hero, which became the all-time best-selling comic in Hong Kong. In 1989 he started his own company and published Tin Ha Pictorial, which featured two of his comics, The North and South Poles and Wind and Cloud. Ma has also worked in collaboration with other artists to create Black Leopard (with Siu Kit), God of Kings (with Yuan Ga-bo), and Enemy of Heaven (with Fung Chi-ming). Both The Chinese Hero and Wind and Cloud were made into movies.

## MAK, Ching (1934–)

Mak Ching migrated from Macau to Hong Kong, where he served as the editor of Manhua World from 1957 to 1965. He continued to be a productive artist through the 1970s, publishing his work in Caricature Weekly, Manhua Daily, and Baby Comic.

## NG, Sheng-gwan (1935–)

Ng Sheng-gwan's work first appeared in Kung-sheng Daily in the 1950s, and later in Express Daily and the Sun Dan Evening Post. His comics include Careless Young Master, Happy Fruit, and Big Brother Sunny.

## OW, Man (1960–)

Ow Man, whose real name is Man Kai-ming, began working in the manhua industry at the age of fourteen and has served under both Seung-gun Siu-bo and Wong Yuk-long. At Wong's Jademan Comics in the 1980s, he was responsible for the drafting work on Buddha's Palm. In 1993 Ow formed his own company, King's Fountain Limited, and published several gangster manhua set in Hong Kong’s red light district, including Teddy Boy and Portland Street. Teddy Boy was made into a movie in 1996; four sequels followed.

## SEUNG-GUN, Siu-bo (1955–)

Seung-gun Siu-bo, born Kwong Tong-yuen, entered the manhua industry at age 11. His early works, drawn in the late 1960s, include Unbeatable Golden Dagger, Black and White Little Demon, Little Drunken God, and Little Bar Girl. Seung-gun's most successful comic, the kung fu manhua Lee Siu-lung (Bruce Lee), debuted in 1972. The manhua empire he built, which included the penny papers Hei Bo and Ching Bo, made him Wong Yuk-long's major competitor throughout the 1970s. Wong convinced him to work at Jademan Comics in the 1980s, though that arrangement lasted only a short while. In 1986 Seung-gun established Kwong's Publishing, where he has continued to publish Lee Siu-lung and other kung fu manhua.

## SEUNG-GUN, Siu-keung (1950–)

Chan Kwok-yin, who publishes under the name Seunggun Siu-keung, has worked in the comics industry since he was sixteen. In 1973 he began to work on 73- Manhua, which published his strip Sau-sing Chai (Longevity Boy). The following year he joined Jademan Comics and continued the title. In 1988 Seung-gun left Jademan to start his own company; its titles include Sau-sing Chai and Siu-keung Manhua Collection.

## SEUNG-GUN, Siu-wai (1950–)

Seung-gun Siu-wai, whose real name is Kwong Namlum, is Seung-gun Siu-bo’s older brother. He started working in the manhua industry in 1963, when he was thirteen. Ten years later, he started the magazine 73-Manhua, and two years after that he began collaborating with his brother on Hei Bo. Like his brother, Seung-gun joined Jademan Comics in the 1980s, where he contributed to King Manhua Weekly. After leaving Jademan, he formed his own company.

## SIU, Kit (1963–)

At the age of fifteen, Siu Kit assisted Ma Wing-shing in the production of Charming. He continued to work with Ma at Jademan Comics and later became the first assistant on Ma’s The Chinese Hero. When Ma Wingshing established his own company in 1989, Siu once again worked as his key assistant, this time for Tin Ha Pictorial's North and South Poles and Wind and Cloud. Siu's other joint works with Ma include Black Leopard.

## SUNG, Sam-long (1926–1999)

Sung Sam-long's work first appeared in Manhua World in the 1950s. His most famous comic is Dumb Detective Manhua Series; he also worked as an art editor for various newspapers during the 1970s and 1980s.

## TOU, Sam (1950–)

Tou Sam's comics have been appearing since 1975 in such venues as Sing Tao Night Daily, Oriental Daily, City Magazine, Children's Daily, and Ming Pao. His best-known titles include One Is Nice and Three Little Guys.

## WAN, Yat-leung (1967–)

Wan Yat-leung, alias Fat Leung, joined the industry at age nineteen as an assistant at Kwong's Publishing. After working as a producer and script writer on King of Ghost Book II: If I Am the King, he left Kwong’s to self-publish End of the World, which lasted only a few issues. His most popular works include The Legend of Black Panther and Sea Tiger I–III.

## WONG, Chak (1922–)

Wong Chak, whose real name is Wong Kai-hei, moved to Hong Kong in the 1950s to work as an illustrator for a Catholic magazine. In the late 1950s he began submitting his work to Manhua World and the comics page of Sing Tao Daily. Shortly after that, he created the manhua characters Old Master Q, Big Dumb, and Mr. Chun, who appeared in Wong's most successful title, Old Master Q. This comic has been in print since 1964, which makes Old Master Q Hong Kong's longestliving manhua character.

## WONG, Kwok-hing (?)

Wong Kwok-hing joined the industry in 1976 as an assistant to Seung-gun Siu-bo. His first individual work was a kung fu comic titled Grasshopper Boy. Wong also contributed to Yuk-long Manhua Biweekly and in 1988 published Comic Pig.

## WONG, Sze-ma (1940– 1983)

Wong Sze-ma, born Wong Wing-hing, started drawing newspaper comic strips in 1961. Four years later he began publishing the four-panel strip Father and Ms. in the newspaper Ming Pao. After Wong died in 1983, Publication (Holdings) Ltd. released collections of his major works, including Ngau-chai, Gorgeous Susan and Debussy.

## WONG, Yuk-long (1950–)

Wong Yuk-long, known as Tony Wong in English, was the major force in Hong Kong manhua during the 1980s and 1990s. Wong started his comics career at age thirteen at Epoch Comic Weekly. By the time he was twenty-one, he had already tried to start his own company four times. He succeeded in 1971, establishing Yuk-long Picture Book Company; his works from this period include Little Vagabond, The Son of Ultraman, and Solar Lord. The next year saw the debut of his best-selling kung fu manhua Little Rascals (Siu Lau-man), later renamed Oriental Heroes (Lung Fu Mun). Wong also established two penny papers, Sang Po and Golden Bo Daily, in 1975 and 1977, respectively. About this time he set up a formal system for hiring assistants, which helped the manhua industry become more organized and more like other media industries. In the 1980s Wong established Jademan Comics and began to consolidate the industry by hiring his main competitors, including Seung-gun Siubo and Seung-gun Siu-wai. Jademan eventually had a virtual monopoly on the Hong Kong comics industry. In July 1986 Jademan (Holdings) Limited was listed ón the Hong Kong stock market—it was the peak of Wong's career. In October of 1987, the stock market crashed and brought his company down along with it. He was pushed out of Jademan, and in 1991 he was sent to prison for two years for forgery. After his release, Wong established Jade Dynasty and began publishing comics again.

## YEN, E-king (1933–)

Yen E-king, alias Old Monster or Ah-chun, never had a fixed drawing style. His best-known works are the editorial cartoons on social and political issues that he drew in the 1960s for Express Daily. In 1968 these were collected and published as The World ofE-king Yen. In the 1990s, under the alias Ah-chun, he published a series of comics done in the style of traditional Chinese paintings.

## YUAN, Bou-wan (1922–1995)

Yuan Bou-wan, whose real name was Yuan Chi-tang, began publishing his work in Guangzhou newspapers before World War II. The southern Chinese drawing style he picked up there later influenced Hong Kong manhua. Yuan's most famous characters include Kiddy Cheung, Miss Lau, Hot-Tempered Leung, Arrogant Chiu, and Wong Chai. Kiddy Cheung and Miss Lau were the most popular comics, and were made into movies during the 1950s.

## YUAN, Yat-mok (1957–)

In 1975 Yuan Jao-tai, known as Yuan Yat-mok, Yatmo, and Local Boy, helped organize an experimental comic daily and later drew comics for newspaper and magazine columns. Most of his work has criticized politics and current events. Yuan's individual works include Rather Die Than Change.

## ZUNZı (1955–)

Zunzi, whose real name is Wong Kei-kwan, began drawing comics in 1980. He has had regular editorial cartoon columns in Ming Pao, People’s Monthly and Next Magazine, and Apple Daily and has published stand-alone comics such as Black Material, Grandpa Dang, 100 Caricatures of Hong Kong, Taiwan, and China Political Figures, and A Biography of Mr. Legislator.
