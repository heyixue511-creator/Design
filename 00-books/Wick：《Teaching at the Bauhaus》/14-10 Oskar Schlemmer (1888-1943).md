# 10 Oskar Schlemmer (1888-1943)

## The Human Being as Theme

Oskar Schlemmer figured at the Bauhaus as a man in the middle. He avoided extremes. He was always skeptical of dogmatic definitions. He was always concerned with balancing out radical views. And no one was more concerned than he with the possibilities of a thorough transformation of the Bauhaus idea into practice. He was an artist in the space between the great movements of the periods before and after World on 3 February 1921 in a letter to his friend and fellow painter Otto Meyer-Amden that the Bauhaus was "building" in an entirely different direction as expected, namely, toward the human being: "Gropius seems very aware of this; to his mind the academies make a grave mistake by neglecting the formation of the human being. He wants anartist to have character, and this should come first, art later."2 For Moholy-Nagy, too, as we have seen, the human being was central—we need only recall

War I—cubism, expressionism, Dada, ccnstructivism, Neue Sachlichkeit (new objectivity), surrealism—with a basic intellectual attitude found in the disputed area between classicism and romanticism. Thus he succeeded in overcoming and sublimating the antagonisms, typical of the time, that beset the Bauhaus—between a subjective desire for expression and an objective intention for design, between metaphysics and rationality, between a spiritually oriented Kunstwollen (will to art) and a pragmatic orientation toward action, between fine art and applied design—in an allembracing theme: the human being.

While this theme occupied Schlemmer during his Bauhaus years exclusively—intellectually, artistically, and pedagogically—it did not simply correspond to his "mission"1 as an artist; it was also something like a permanent reminder--certainly an irritating one for some people—that the true goal of this “art school of modernism" did not lie in the interesting avant-garde exercises (which often led to pure formalism). Rather, the fundamental meaning of the Bauhaus should be defined with the human being as measure: all design should start from and work toward the human being. The idea of the development of a new human being for a new society was among the essential points of the founding utopia of the Bauhaus, and Schlemmer observed with satisfaction his slogan "Not the product, but man, is the end in view"³—and yet Moholy was representative of all those at the Bauhaus whose design practice took the human being into consideration but as a pictorial theme rather than an artistic subject. They thought it a sign of definite progress to have emancipated themselves from the representation of the human being—a theme that was weighted down with tradition throughout the history of art—and to be able to explore purely pictorial means with any figurative considerations; this gave them the confidence that they were "modern." By contrast, Schlemmer saw no discrepancy between the goal of pictorial articulation of a highly specific image of man and the goal of modernity. His effort was aimed at the formulation of an "up-to-date" and yet "timeless" canon of figures, a “universally valid type of design."4 For him, the human being was neither the bearer of psychological particularities (as in expressionism, for example) nor the representative of particular social milieus (as, for example, for Otto Dix or George Grosz). Stripped of all physiognomic, psychological, and social differences, the human being led back, as Schlemmer saw it, to its essential ur-form and thereby was removed to a "sphere of idols."5 Because Schlemmer attempted to grasp the human form in its ideal, and thus doubtless ran the risk of losing sight of the historicity of the human being, he was surprisingly close to all those at the Bauhaus whose pictorial thought was based on “abstract elements of form”. (Kandinsky) and who operated with these formal elements in a similarly ahistorical way by viewing the square, circle, and the triangle as elementary design categories of universal and timeless validity.

Despite these parallels, two important differences remain: (1) in terms of content, Schlemmer was the only one who made the human being (more precisely, the human being in space) the theme of his art and his teachings with the utmost consistency, thèreby giving the humane aspirations of the Bauhas their most evident expression (it is no coincidence that the official Bauhaus logo, which shows a\stylized, constructivist human head, was designedby Oskar Schlemmer); (2) in terms of form, Schlemmer only rarely conformed to the ideology of the right angle and the imperative of the cube that were dominant at the Bauhaus. Georg Muche remarked on this in his obituary for Oskar Schlemmer: “He evidently knew what we had all forgotten: that there is no cube in the visible appearance of the surfaces of nature. We at the Bauhaus hadplaced ourselves under the sign of the right angle. The square was the preferredform for our creativity. ... Oskar Schlemmer placed himself under the sign of the arc, the curve, and the circle. From these he formed his image of the human being in the equilibrium of harmonious movement."6 The fundamentalimportance of the wavyline in Schlemmer's work has been thoroughly analyzed by Wulf Herzogenrath in his examinationof the artist's wall paintings. We will return to this as part of our presentation of Schlemmer's intellectual background(the sections“TheMetaphysician" and "The Classicist"). First, however, we will sketch very briefly the stages of his artistic development from his time at the academy in Stuttgart until his departure from the Dessau Bauhaus.

## Stages of Artistic Development

Schlemmer, born in Stuttgart in 1888, received his artistic education from1906 to 1910at the Akademie der bildenden Künste (Academy of fine arts) in Stuttgart, after a two-year apprenticeship in a marquetry workshop and a semester at the arts and crafts school in his native city. The works of the academy students—nude studies, portraits, landscapes, interiors——can, with the exception of the small oil painting Illuminated Head of a Girl (ca. 1909, CW G 16),7 be passed over. Both in its flat palette andin its tendency to depersonalize and standardize the face,8 this early painting already points to later developments, for example, the Female Head in Gray (1912, G 79), which was the result of Schlemmer's encounter with Cézanne and cubism during his stay in Berlin (1911–12), and reveals an advanced stage of a reduction to formal types. By deciding in favor of the formal rigor of cubism as opposed to the exuberance of contemporaneous expressionism, Schlemmer set the course for the development of his entire oeuvre. Thus in 1913 he noted in his diary: “what I love above all: the astringent, severe style; not flowery, fragrant, silky, Wagnerian, but like Bach, Handel."9 And one year later he wrote in a letter to

In 1912 (or 1913)11 Schlemmer became a master student of Adolf Hölzel, that "pioneer of abstraction and groundbreaking theoretician"12 at the academy in Stuttgart, who provided important stimuli for artists like Nolde, Baumeister, Itten, Otto Meyer-Amden, and Ida Kerkovius (who would later attend the preliminary course at the Bauhaus as well). (For other references to Hölzel in this book, see "Notes on Itten's Artistic and Pedagogical Development" in chapter 5 and "Theory of Design: Theory of Color" in chapter 8.) Schlemmer's attitude toward Hölzel, especially in later years, was marked by some ambivalence, but that will be a subject for the final section of this chapter (“The Possibilities and Limits of Artistic Teachings"). It is certainly correct to see the years as a master student, between 1912–13 and 1919, as .years of emancipation from his teacher and the development of highly personal conceptions of form and pictorial ideas, and yet Hölzel's influence on Schlemmer's objective of a unity of law and emotion in art is unmistakable.13

During the war years, while serving on the front first in France and then in Russia, after which he was placed with a cartographic unit in Alsace, Schlemmer arrived at the first prototypical formulations of his theme: the human being. He tirelessly emphasized the importance of this theme: "The depiction of the human figure will always be the great parable for the artist. Here the human being of flesh and blood with the mystery of its existence, there its interplay in art. The conditions and laws of its physical existence are not the same as those of its artificial and artistic ones. They are the ones most foreign to our physical organism: brush, pencil, plaster, wood, stone."14 He considered the human being the "most noble subject,"15 the object to which he could attach the spirituality that he had sought, but not found, in formal experimentation and aesthetic innovation. In his diary—alongside his letters to Otto Meyer-Amden, the most reliable source of information on Schlemmer's thoughts and activities—there are numerous allusions to what he felt to be the Achilles heel of modern art—the chasm between aesthetics and ethics (Søren Kierkegaard)—and to the deficit of spiritual substance and normative power accompanied by an availability of formal means: "Then comes knowledge of the medium Some days we may find ourselves fully equipped. The hand, the eye. . . . We have everything we need, but nothing to say. Nothing to express. A creative conception is lacking."16 That same year (1915) he noted in his diary: "I would like to have such a mastery of technique that the actual painting would present the least challenge. And indeed this is the case: I have the form; what I lack is the vision. At one time the opposite was true. Now find my hands full and my heart empty. Yet I remain convinced that the idea is the source of form; the spirit guides the hand."17 As an example of "the mind, the idea which forged itself a form,"18 Schlemmer mentioned in his diary the figure of Apollo of Tenea; and this model of ancient sculpture did indeed play a prominent part in the artist's attempt to bring about a “marriage of form and content."19 Although he certainly approached this goal in paintings such as Divided Figure (1915–18, G 107), Composition in Pink (1916, G 110), Homo (1916, G 114), Man with Fish (1916–18, G 115), and Plan with Figures (1919, G 116), he himself had doubts until the early twenties: "The fate of today's artists: to have form, a wealth of form, without content to match; we have the techniques but lack a conception."20

The paintings just mentioned, executed between 1915 and 1919, are all abstractions of the human figure that have in common a reduction of the body to a formula (frequently the typical violin contour) and a strict, tectonic connection to the surface. In this way, the figures seem to be arranged in a coordinate system that gives them stability and support on the pictorial surface; this is particularly clear in the compositions Divided Figure and Homo, the latter a painting that is considered an incunabulum of Schlemmer's representations of the human figure. Hans Hildebrandt has written of Homo, the prototype of the artist's later figurative compositions (cf. the wall decoration in the workshop building at the Weimar Bauhaus, 1923; the wall decoration for the Rabe House in Zwenkau, 1930–31): “The human being as idea, embodied in the form of a naked man. The sitting point has been selected, because in the contrast of the upright upper body to the lying, stretched-out limbs it reflects the double nature of the human being: active-moving and passive-resting. The vertical-horizontal contrast is at the same time a symbol of the forces active in the right-hand corner. Such a double interpretation results from the sketching in of a coordinate system that is placed over the figure and ovér the free space."21

What Schlemmer had in common with, for example, Kandinsky's systematic explorations of form is an interest in the symbolic meanings, in the metaphysical quality, in the psychology of elementary pictorial means.24 What distinguished it, however, is the insistence on the theme of the human figure, so that as a rule the constructive form serves the pictorial formulation of a human figure that has been abstracted of all contingencies and made as universal as possible. This can be seen from the fact that Schlemmer very soon abandoned experimenting with purely abstract, geometrical, and stereometric forms (for'example, in Ornamental Sculpture in a Divided Frame, 1919– 23, P 1) in favor of his concern with and goal of a more moderate figural abstraction, for example, in the series Relief H (1919, P 4–P 6) and Relief JG (1919–21, P 7-P 9, P 11-P 12). Whereas into the early twenties this rigorously planar aspect was a main feature of Schlemmer's paintings (and this is still true of the wall decorations for the workshop building of the Weimar Bauhaus of 1923), a clear move away from this becomes evident around 1922–23in theartist's Gallery Paintings. After Gropius—clearly impressed by Schlemmer's tectonic pictorial conception in his series of Architectural Sculptures(P 10)—appointed Schlemmer to the Bauhaus at the end of 1920, the artist's interest as a master of form in the wood and stone sculpture workshop was initially focused on relief and round sculpture. The first paintings came around1922–23, after a pause of three years (the last from the earlier period had been Plan with Figures of 1919) and they differ radically from the earlier ones in that the planar pictorial structure was replaced by spatiality and corporeality. That is to say, the figures appear sculpturally rounded andare shown in perspectivally formed spaces. The major works that exemplify this development areThe Gesture: Female Dancer (1922, G 118), Dinner Party (1923, G 124), Resting Place (1925, G 134), and Five Figures in Space: Roman (1925, G 137). Although the space in these paintings has depth, the perspective follows the rules of Euclidean geometry only partially, so that there is no harmonious spatial continuum of the sort found in the systematic space of the Renaissance but rather an unreal, metaphysical space that can be traced back to the Italian "pittura metafisica" of Carlo Carrà and Giorgio de Chirico.25

It is hardly surprising that Schlemmer's turn to perspectivalspatialrepresentationwouldencounter criticism at the Bauhaus, where, under the influence of abstract constructivism, the “ideology of the flat" was then popular. Art critics and fellow artists perceived his return to the third dimension as conservatism, a betrayalof modernism. For Schlemmer, the “renunciation of modernity in its formal aspect"meant a gain in “intelligibility" and “classicality,"26 especially as the figure was no longer thought of as analyzed in its construction but as a self-contained unity ofa corporeal, fleshed-out outline form. Thus the artist himself always distinguished terminologically between the analytically faceted"differentiation people" of the Stuttgart period and the "outline people" of the Bauhaus period. The treatment of depth and plasticity are, of course, not identical with the rejection of abstraction and the association with a verism (which Schlemmer considereddubious) that one finds in the twenties with the Neue Sachlichkeit as the antipode to constructivist trends. Rather, Schlemmer was abstract in the sense that he reduced natural forms to stereometric forms and avoided characterizing individual features in favor of representation based on types.

Whereas the Female Dancer (1922), which relates thematically to Schlemmer's choreographic experiments dating back to 1912, creates an impression of tension and dynamics with its abrupt foreshortening and tapering, the painting Five Figures in a Room: Roman, with its statuesque structure of figures, evokes the clarity and classicality that would continue through the cycle for the fountain room of the Museum Folkwang in Essen (1928–30),27 even if some of these compositions already reveal something of the transition to the baroque phase of the Breslau period.

Karin von Maur sees Schlemmer's so-called gallery paintings of the Bauhaus years, which were permeated by a metaphysical mood as “models . . . , that served the Bauhaus program," which sought to create a "symbol of community" and function as an artistic anticipation of a new human being in a utopian architecture.28 The question of whether they could actually fulfill this function must remain unanswered here, but the following two sections, which clarify Oskar .Schlemmer's intellectual position, should be able to help expand on what has been said so far and contribute to a better understandingof the artist's pedagogical intentions.

## The Metaphysician

Schlemmer was the sort of artist who did not create direction from a spontaneous impulse but rather for whom spontaneity was refracted through reflection. Although emotion and intuition were very important to him—indeed, primary²9his compositions never seem like expressive jottings or purely subjective psychograms but were always filtered and clarified by his effort to establish regularity. By inserting ratio between intuition andmaterialization as an instrument of control, he succeeded in objectifying the subjective and thus approaching his goal of the design of an ideal type of human being. In doing so, he was concerned with what he himself called "mystical objectivity,"30 an objectivity that, despite all the things in existence that follow laws and can be calculated, is also aware of the "incomprehensible, the unnamable."31

Because Schlemmer was uncommonly well read, we can only mention several of the major sources from which he drew ideas in developing his concept of "mystical objectivity"—from Paracelsus by way of Jakob Böhme through to German Romanticism (Heinrich von Kleist, Carl Gustav Carus, Philipp Otto Runge, Caspar David Friedrich) and beyond (Otto Flake, Ricarda Huch). Numerous diaryentries show that Schlemmer regularly thought about questions of the mystical or metaphysical. Thus, in 1913 he surmised "that a deeper mysticism [might be] created by way of natural representation," namely, "the mysticism of painterly means."32 In 1915 he saw a "universal aspiration toward the mystical"33 that some artists sought in rules, others in chaos. After clearly interceding for the pragmatic goals of the Bauhaus and severely criticizing its inadequate implementation (on this, see the section "Schlemmer and the Idea of the Bauhaus"), he noted in 1922: "Rededication of art. ... What remains is the metaphysical: art."34During the twenties he frequently made use of Otto Flake's maxim that it is essential "'to bind the metaphysical,' to give it substance andclothe it in comprehensible language."35 Referring to Philipp Otto Runge, who along with Caspar David Friedrich was one of his favorite painters, Schlemmer coined the term "metaphysical mathematics"36and his artistic interest at the time—as we have seen—was moving in the direction of "metaphysical spaces, metaphysical perspectives, the metaphysical figure."37

This series of similar-sounding statements could be extended almost at will. If we choose not to do so, it is because we wish first to attempt to determine exemplarily, using just one painting, Schlemmer's intellectual standpoint. The painting is Paracelsus the Lawgiver (1923, G120), a painting that doubtless has programmatic character. Schlemmer was not concernedwith portrait likeness, as a comparison to historical representations of Paracelsus demonstrates, but with apictorial compression of that which he personally saw in Paracelsus (1493–1541). "Only in general typology" is this fictive portrait “oriented around the portraits of humanist scholars and Reformers, with their imposing frontality and eloquent hand gestures."38 Here, too, then, as in Schlemmer's other figurative works, there is a "tendency to single out colors and forms"39 with the goal of distilling the essentials. Schlemmer's portrait of Paracelsus is an homage to an intellectual idol esteemed by the artist. Schlemmer had studied the writings of Paracelsus and was as impressed by the latter's cosmological concept of a concordance of microcosm and macrocosm, which was already familiar to Schlemmer from the romantics, as he was by Paracelsus's tripartite division of the human being into an earthly, visible body; a heavenly, invisible life spirit; and a divine soul that was the source of knowledge, morality, and salvation and elevated humanity above simple earthly circumstances and explained his likeness to God. Karin von Maur has shown, on the basis of markings in the Paracelsus edition by Hans Kayser (1921) that Schlemmer used, that the artist was particularly interested in Paracelsus's remarks dealing with the localization of art in the unconscious foundation of existence in the mystical and magical. Thus he saw in Paracelsus's aphorism "Art cannot be taken from me, for it is hidden in me and is an ungraspable thing"40 a confirmation of his own view of art.

Schlemmer encountered the idea of a unity of humanity and cosmos—or, more precisely, of microcosm and macrocosm—and thus the notion of man's likeness to God in his study of Jacob Böhme (1575–1624), who also inspired and influenced Johannes Itten, as was suggested in “The Background of Itten's Pedagogy" in chapter 5. Schlemmer owned the1922 book Christliche Theosophen (Christian theosophers) by Freiherr von Schröder, in which Böhme is treated at length and in which Schlemmer found statements that would have bolstered him in his view of humanity. It reads: "The renewed human being as God's likeness is no longer an individual but a genre; he is beyond gender and dual gendered at the same time: inter-sexual. For the 'severance' into the sexes meant, in Böhme's view, the end of divine likeness, and in Adam's desire for a helpmate, in the desire for 'otherness' and 'separateness' he saw the first phase of the turn from God whose last consequence would be the Fall. The spiritual man is a likeness of the one and only God and thus transcends the difference of gender. . . ."41 It is clear that this image of the human being as an ideal exemplar of a genre, without any specific gender, is one that Schlemmer portrayed both in his paintings and in the types of characters he realized onstage.

Despite the enormous distance in time from Böhme to the ideas that Heinrich von Kleist propagated in his 1810 essay in dialogue form, “Über das Marionettentheater" (On the marionette theater), it is just a tiny step; and it is characteristic of Schlemmer that he scarcely let pass an opportunity to mention this latter work. The Fall caused man to lose his innocence, his harmony with divine creation—a loss that is revealed in his inability to move with grace, poise, and harmony. After the Fall, such movement—in Kleist's view—was only possible for the mechanically functioning, unfeeling, and unconscious jointed puppet, the marionette, or for God: "thus, only when knowledge has passed through the infinite, as it were, does grace return; so that, at the same time, it appears most purely in that human body that is either not one at all or that has an infinite consciousness, that is, in the jointed puppet or in God."42 Kleist's "Über das Marionettentheater" seems to have fascinated Schlemmer for two reasons. First, because he recognized as his own the problem the essay raised of grace being imperiled by the reflective conscious. Second, because this essay was “probably the most powerful stimulus for the creation of his ’artificial figure.'"43

We should also mention the influence of the metaphysical anthropology of Carl Gustav Carus (1789–1869), who was a nineteenth-century romantic natural scientist in the tradition of Goethe and Schelling as well as a painter close to Caspar David Friedrich, Johann Christian Clausen Dahl, and Georg Friedrich Kersting (and equally admired) whose contributions to psychology were given renewed currency at the beginning of the twentieth century by Ludwig Klages. Schlemmer, who called attention to both of Carus's major works, Psyche and Symbolik der menschlichen Gesta/t (The symbolism of the human form) in his course "Der Mensch,"44 found confirmation in Carus both for his own conviction of the importance of the unconscious and for his goal of designing an ideal for the human being. The role of the unconscious in the creative process had long been clear to Schlemmer; in 1918 he wrote that he was searching for "the undercurrents, a'theory of the unconscious.'"45 Five years earlier, in 1913, he had already written in his diary: “Respect the unconscious! The handunconsciously succeeds wherecalculating reasonwouldremain baffled."46 It is reasonable to assume, then, that Schlemmer would have entirely agreed with the fundamental thesis, one that anticipated later depth psychology, expressed by Carus in the introduction to Psyche: "the key to knowledge of the essence of unconscious life of the soul [lies] in the region of the subconscious."47 (This assumption about Schlemmer is also supported by the fact that he recommended that his students read the small book Das unbewußte Denken [Unconscious thought] by Aloys Wenzel.)48

Schlemmer would certainly also have agreed with Carus's ideas that everything is related to everything else, that beyond the sensory lay the supersensory and behind the physical the spiritual,49 and that the "human being is a symbol of the divine idea of the soul."50 The condition for this symbolism was, however, as Carus saw it, the human being in his "purest manifestation."51 This meant that "the arbitrary and transitory must necessarily be distinguished from the essential and permanent,"52 that is, it must be abstracted from the natural circumstances and the ideal human form defined by a basic scale, a module. It is not significant for our purposes that in his theory of proportion based on morphology and physiology Carus assumed the natural ur-scale, as the "organic module," to be one third of the length of the spine (i.e., eight dorsal vertebrae), but rather the fact that his system of proportions is

the basis for all symbolism of the human form; for only now has its true starting point been found, which will show the purely human to be a genuine ideal and thus create the opportunity immediately to comprehend all of the particular differences in constitution, temperament, and spirit more completely as they reveal themselves in infinite nuances through the transformation of this form. Such a pure center, it goes without saying, will remain in its essence an abstraction, something that can never be found as such in nature—in short, as the irrational it will stand opposite nature as the rational. . . . We need . . . in every architecture, and thus also in the organic one of the human body, as a ruler only a simple diménsion that can be divided into equal parts."53

The extent to which Schlemmer identified himself with this interpretation is clear both from the fact that this passage from Carus was the only one he underlined in pencil and marked in red in the margin in the Theodor Lessing edition that he used and because he expressed very similar ideas in 1930 with regard to his wall paintings in Essen.54

## The Classicist

We return now to the concept of “mystical objectivity." Although Schlemmer can be included in the tradition of mystical and romantic thought— which led up to a contemporary like Ricarda Huch, whose interpretation of the cosmos as a trinity of spirit, nature, and soul he adopted exactly for his study sheet for the course“Der Mensch"56he did not see himself as a romantic.57 Rather, he sought to represent the“most romantic idea in the most austere form."58It is characteristic, then, that he loved Runge's statement that "strict regularity" is of utmost necessity precisely for those artworks that originate from the imagination and the mysticism of our souls.59 The insistence on this "strict regularity" seemed necessary to Schlemmer in order to address a danger that is without a doubt inherent in romanticism, namely, the danger of an enraptured or exalted surrender to the transcendental.

Despite his intellectual proximity to romanticism, Schlemmer was closer to the classical ideal in his artistic realizations, which speak more to a search for the great, clear, valid, and objective form. It was Nietzsche60 who, with the paired terms “Apollonian——Dionysian," gave Schlemmer the terminology with which he would characterize his occasional swings from the rigorous to the ecstatic during the second decade of the 1900s.61 Our depiction of the stages of his artistic development has already demonstrated that the artist ultimately decided in favor of the Apollonian pole, for “number, scale, and law,"62 for the classical. Here, too, Nietzsche had set the direction for him:

"The classicist desires the possible, and out of economy remains within the limits of the possible. ... The grand style results when the beautiful carries off the victory over the monstrous."63 And: "The greatness of an artist is measured . . . by the degree to which he approaches the grand style. ... To become master over the chaos that one is, to compel one's chaos to become form: to become logical, simple, unambiguous, mathematics, /aw. . .. The classical style represents in essence this repose, simplification, abbreviation, concentration."64

In addition to Nietzsche it was Goethe who influenced Schlemmer with his call for a "figured bass of painting" and--even more so—with his ideas on style. Style for Goethe (as for Schlemmer) was fulfilled neither in simple "imitation of nature" nor in "mannerism." Mannerist subjectivism, which Goethe also characterized as "convenient mysticism," is just as false as a naturalism that shows things almost mechanically in their external surfaces, in their arbitrary forms, in their temporal determinedness. In the latter, everything is merely natural; in the former, everything is supernatural. The goal of great, true art is to be both simultaneously, that is, the artist is expected "to give his artwork a shape, a form, such that it seems natural and supernatural at the same time."65 This goal is to be approached by striving for a style that has as its condition a visually perceptive penetration into things and that leads to the second, "mature" nature that points beyond both the "immature nature" of naturalism and the “immature poetry” of mannerism.66 As for Goethe, so for Schlemmer, too, style was the desirable synthesis of the imitation of nature and "formal manner." Seen in terms of the movements of the twenties, Schlemmer saw Neue Sachlichkeit as a dubious naturalism and abstract constructivism as merely “manner," epitomized for him in Malevitch's Black Square.67 It seems to have escaped Schlemmer's notice, however, that for Malevitch in particular the square was anything but "formal manner"; rather, like an icon, it was spiritual form in its most concise shape. In contrast both to naturalism and constructivism, Schlemmer saw himself-with his figural abstractions that strive to be the expression of a cosmic idea—as searching for style in Goethe's sense: "For me, abstract means style pure and simple; and style for me, as we know, means the last form, the most complete possible. The path there goes by way of the overcoming of naturalism . . . to an ever greater precision of the idea."68

195 Oskar Schlemmer, Schematic diagram Nature-art-human being," 1928–29; wash on cardboard

Goethe's view of art can also help us to understand the wavy line that in Schlemmer's thought has various meanings as an object and to some extent as an autonomous form as well. Wulf Herzogenrath has dealt extensively with the wavy line in Schlemmer's oeuvre and has shown, with reference to statements by Jean Paul (whom, along with Runge, Schlemmer valued most among the romantics) and Wilhelm Worringer (whose book Abstraktion und Einfühlung [Abstraction and empathy] Schlemmer read), that the wavy line, with its flowing and organic character, appeals more to emotion, whereas the angular line originates more from reason and is also grasped by reason. As wavy lines in Schlemmer's work are frequently juxtaposed with angular lines—often as axes in strictly horizontal or vertical arrangements—it seems probable that the two represent the antitheses “intuitive—regular" or even "Dionysian—Apollonian," antitheses that connect “only in the completed human being and the perfected artwork."69 In support of this thesis, Herzogenrath enlistsGoethe's theoryof art and points to its classification of artists as “undulists," who prefer the "snaky line," and "skeletists," whose oeuvre consists of "mere operations of reason." Only by connecting these two “manners" can style, the supreme goal, arise.7º Thus Schlemmer's works, with their stock of elemental forms, prove to be the expression of the desired unity of the emotiona) and the rational, of the intuitive and the regular. And it is precisely this striving for unity—along with the connection to the theme of the human being—that marks the back-drop against which the artist's teaching activity also developed at the Bauhaus (and later in Breslau and Berlin).

## Schlemmer and the Idea of the Bauhaus

Schlemmer came to the Bauhaus in early 1921. After having experienced, as a student representative, the failure of the reform of the academy in Stuttgart and the rejection of Paul Klee as a successor to Adolf Hölzel, he now turned his hopes to the creative engagement with the progressive ideas formulated in the Bauhaus program. What made him sympathize with the Bauhaus idea, after working for many years as a painter, sculptor, stage designer, costume designer, and choreographer, was above all the idea of overcoming the established borders between the genres and the goal of the Gesamtkunstwerk, although unwilling to accept the "subservience to architecture."71 This last point is clearly demonstrated both by Schlemmer's figural wall decorations for the Weimar workshop building of 1923—whichof course take the existing architecture by Henry van de Velde as a point of departure and incorporate it without becoming dependent on it—-as well as by the fact that in choosing the colors for the interior design of the Stadttheater in Jena designed by Gropius in 1922, Schlemmer submitted to the Bauhaus director's conception only unwillingly.7²

Schlemmer's diary entries andletters—the seismographic records of events at the Bauhaus— not only reflect the inner tensions and internal difficulties of the art school of modernism, especially in its founding phase, they also shed light on how great the rift between program and reality had become at the Bauhaus. The artist saw through both the ideological and the utopian characters of the Bauhaus with uncommon perception—whereby, following the terminology of the sociologist Karl Mannheim, “utopia" refers to ideas that transcend existing reality that have the potential to be realized, and “ideology" refers to ideas that transcend existence that de facto never become reality. In Mannheim's view, such an attitude finds itself inclined to ideology because the activity aimed at bringing about change “falls away" from the imagined intent of such change, and thus the falsity becomes manifest in the incongruity between the idea and the action.73

In Schlemmer's view, the ideological character of the Bauhaus, understood in these terms, manifested itself in the following points:

1. Contrary to all programmatic objectives, "building, even in the most utopian sense" was hardly conceivable74because of the lack of an architectural course or workshop,7⁵ which should, in fact, have formed the very heart of the Bauhaus: "I never understood why this most important workshop of all did not exist at the Bauhaus; I have long been calling for a strong architecture workshop, and I feel that it must be established, no matter what the objections."76 This remark in 1924 is merely a stronger formulation of a demand he had already made three years earlier: "What I would like to see is this: more architecture at the Bauhaus, more discipline in the other fields."77

2. The lack of focus on architecture during the early years led Schlemmer to come to the conclusion (a disappointing one for him) that the Bauhaus was nothing but a “modern art school" that happened to have several workshops in which "students chip and whittle away, following their aesthetic stirrings, somewhat better than at an arts and crafts school."78 Here, too, the artist was diagnosing a tendency to ideology in the fall away from the Bauhaus's original, self-imposed goal of a rigorous implementation of art school reform. In this context, Schlemmer's resolute critique was aimed against two workshops in particular, ceramics and weaving, which, although they contributed little to redeeming the founding utopia of the Bauhaus, were nevertheless "well on their way to becoming the hallmark of the Bauhaus. . .. The label of a good art and crafts school should thus come as no surprise."79

3. In 1922, under the title “Hausbau and Bauhaus," Schlemmer sketched out the “real utopia"80 of a transparent metal-and-glass architecture that recalled the pre-World War I ideas of Paul Scheerbart and the buildings of Bruno Taut (glass pavilion for the 1914 Werkbund exhibition in Cologne) and Gropius (Fagus factory in Alfeld an der Leine, 1911) and Mies van der Rohe's design for a glass skyscraper in Berlin (1922) and anticipated the ideas of Gropius's glass architecture for the Dessau Bauhaus building of 1926. The goal , of this architectural “real utopia" was “to demonstrate to the Bauhaus that it was in no way prepared to realize the house of the future because its workshops—weaving, ceramics, cabinet-making—were committed to both outdated materials and traditional genres of the crafts."81 Schlemmer's ruthlessly pointed critique of particular workshops ("Stone sculpture?.. . I do not go in for decorative doodads.... Ceramics? 'Boorish.' Out of the question. . . . Metal workshop? I have no sense for ornament; it is immoral, when we lack the essential. ... Glass painting? Why such medieval labels... ?")82laid out the artist's fundamental objections to the anachronism of the rhetorical evocation of the spirit of medieval crafts and historically outdated means of production. Although after leaving the Bauhaus, Schlemmer expressed the view in retrospect that it would always remain an achievement of the Gropius and the Bauhaus to have made "the decisive step of linking the question of modern art with the reality of handicraft,"83 during the founding phase of the Bauhaus he was the sharpest critic of any rapturous romanticism of the crafts and one of the most pointed opponents of any attempt to move closer to industry:

I do not believe in craftsmanship. We cannot restore the crafts of the Middle Ages, any more than we can restore the art of the Middle Ages, not even in an approximate modern equivalent. It has been made obsolete by modern developments. Handmade objets d'art in the age of the machine and technology would be a luxury for the rich, lacking a broad popular basis and roots in the people. Industry now provides what the crafts once provided, or it will when fully developed: standardized, solid functional objects made of genuine materials. .. . I do not believe that craftsmanship as practiced at the Bauhaus can transcend the aesthetic and fulfill more serious social functions. "Getting in touch with industry" will not do the trick; we would have to commit ourselves and merge completely with industry. But we cannot make that our goal; it would mean turning our backs on the Bauhaus.84

It would be difficult to make a clearer critique of the false consciousness that was already evident in the Bauhaus manifesto and its problematic (or at least ambiguous) ideology of the crafts. At the same time, Schlemmer—at least in a dialogue with himself, for it is important to distinguish precisely between his private diary notes and his official positions at the institution—went a step beyond Gropius, who in 1922 spoke cautiously of establishing first contacts with industry, before trying since 1923 to get the Bauhaus to commit to a guiding thesis of “art and technology, a new unity." Schlemmer saw clearly that the merger with industry that he described as tantamount to the neutralization of art could not be his task—the task of an artist—and that it would ultimately lead to his departure from the Bauhaus. That he nevertheless remained at the Bauhaus until 1929 (when he was called to the academy in Breslau) is another matter.

Recognizing that, as an artist, it would be wrong to merge with industry—even though in 1921 he had cited Oswald Spengler, who advised "the poets to join the merchant marine, and the artists to become engineers"85—and seeing that, owing both to the economic difficulties of the time and the changing technologies of architecture (glass and metal construction), the artist lacked "noble tasks"86 (the application of modern art to architecture), Schlemmer drew three conclusions. First, a turn to the "illusionary world of the theater,"87which promised to be a substitute for his idea of the Gesamtkunstwerk. Second, a "[r]ededication to art. .. . I should not try for what the industry can do better, or which the engineers can handle more expertly. What remains is the metaphysical: art."88 Third, an insistence in his teaching on treating the human being as the indispensable measure even—indeed, especially—in the solution of highly technical design tasks that are determined by machines, which after 1923 had more and more become the focus at the Bauhaus. To which Schlemmer replied: "Not machine, not abstract—always man!"89

## The Teacher

The range of teaching tasks that Schlemmer took on at the Bauhaus was unusually broad. During the founding phase of the Bauhaus he was responsible for leading the workshop for wall painting as master of form (from 1922 Kandinsky, later Scheper); from 1921 to 1923 he was in charge of the stone sculpture workshop (1921–22 together with Itten); from 1921 to 1923 he was, together with Itten, master of form in the metal workshop (succeeded by Moholy-Nagy); and then from 1922 to 1923 he was also the artistic director of the wood sculpture studio (1920 to 1922 Muche). In 1923 he took over the Bauhaus stage (1921 to 1923 Lothar Schreyer), which became legendary under his leadership, and until Schlemmer left the Dessau teaching staff in 1929 it was one of the most active departments and central to the life of the community at the Bauhaus. In additional to practical and experimental stage work, from 1926 on he taught a course in stage theory that was required for students in the department. As early as 1921 Schlemmer was teaching life drawing, which was expanded in the 1927–28academic year to “figure drawing” and in 1928 became part of his ambitious course “Der Mensch."

This list alone is enough to demonstrate the variety of Schlemmer's desire and will to play an active role as an artist-teacher in the realization of a Bauhaus program that was far from any sort of narrow specialization. At the same time, it also shows that Schlemmer—starting during the Weimar period from an orientation that was very much focused on practice (work study in the workshops)—significantly contributed to the theoretical teachings at the institute in Dessau as science became increasingly dominant. In late 1920, shortly before joining the Bauhaus, he wrote, “I will not have to be a real teacher, to give systematic instruction. ... I foresee that the students . . . will work with me."90 In 1928 he found himself having to obtain “all kinds of books on the topic" in order to be able to treat his“future subject of instruction. . . 'Man'. . . an enormous area"91 in a manner well-grounded in facts.

Not only is theavailable information on Schlemmer's workshop instruction sparse, and since the responsibilities for leadership of the workshops were not yet sharply divided during the founding phase, the student works from that period are of limited use for trying to reach conclusions about the pedagogical influence of individual masters of form. A survey conducted by Wulf Herzogenrath in 1969 of former members of the Bauhaus who worked in the workshops for wall painting and sculpture under Schlemmer's direction revealed that they could recall hardly any well-requlated teaching activity but certainly could recall intensive discussions.92 This seems to agree with what Schlemmer noticed at the time, not without concern, namely, that "the students apparently have little enthusiasm for practical craftsmanship."93 "What is actually done—by the students—in terms of art and handicrafts is minimal. Almost frighteningly so.. .. The Bauhaus is a beautiful facade, a concept, an idea. . . .94

Schlemmer's activity in the workshop for wall painting is documented only in a single assignment given to students Hermann Müller and Schmidt (1922). It is found in Schlemmer's unpublished papers and can be identified with the decoration (first version, later removed) for the auditorium of the Stadttheater in Jena, which Gropius was renovating. The task was to “arrange and dispose small, regular squares of different colors such that from a distance the effect is homogeneous and tranquil."95 This sort of formal and aesthetic task was by no means atypical of the early years of the Bauhaus. It was, however, atypical when viewed in terms of Schlemmer's own formal language, which was based primarily on curves.

The wall reliefs that Joost Schmidt—at the time already an apprentice in the workshop for wood sculpture—designed for the main building of the Bauhaus on the occasion of Bauhaus Week of 1923 can also be related stylistically to Schlemmer, even though they do not make use of Schlemmer's figurative themes (see "Biographical Notes" in chapter 11).

It is, moreover, difficult to concur with the judgment Wingler made regarding the stone sculpture workshop, namely, that Schlemmer's artistic volition could be read out of a large portion of the student works.9⁶ Rather, a look at that workshop and the sculptures by Otto Werner and Kurt Schwerdtfeger illustrated in the book Staatliches Bauhaus Weimar, 1919–1923 would reveal a stylistic pluralism that ranges from a relatively traditionally interpreted torso to an almost neoplastic (i.e., influenced by Theo van Doesburg) relief that, at best, might suggest Schlemmer's influence in its strictly horizontal and vertical axiality. To the extent that these works can be associated with Schlemmer at all (in Design and Form, for example, Itten claimed that Schwerdtfeger's cubist architectural structure was the result of the instruction in form that Itten gave in the stone sculpture workshop), it is simply to show that Schlemmer was in no way concerned with imposing his will to form on his students but, on the contrary, with ensuring them of the latitude for experimentation necessary for them to tackle on their own the formal problems of the day.

One can only speculate about Schlemmer's pedagogical influence as a master of form in the meta/ workshop. Karin von Maur sees the "highly stylized slender forms or the curved handle forms"97 of the metal vessels of Pap and Tümpel— and Jahn should be added as well—as traces of the master. Nevertheless, one should perhaps be cautious of associating everything curved or rounded with Schlemmer alone.

The wall designs for the workshop building of the Weimar Bauhaus that Schlemmer created in 1923 served to a large extent to give the artist an opportunity to demonstrate publicly the possibilities of a synthesis of painting, relief, and interior architecture. In parallel with this, from 1923 the Bauhaus stage provided Schlemmer with an additional platform that enabled him to realize step by step the idea of a Gesamtkunstwerk. It is not the purpose of this book to portray or even interpret Schlemmer's stage work at the Bauhaus.98 But we can say briefly that the same principles applied to Schlemmer's conception for the stage as applied to his work as a fine artist. The stage served, in his view, "the metaphysical needs of the human being by instituting a world of illusion and by creating the transcendental on the foundation of the rational."99 Theater could fulfill this metaphysical function only as a "stage of types" that differed equally from both literary and political theater this is the same basic standpoint reflected in Schlemmer's "painting of types." The establishment of types on the stage by using masks and costumes is analogous to the process in Schlemmer's paintings in which a human type is depicted according to a regulated canon. In the beginning the costumes and masks were frequently grotesquely exaggerated and spatially elongated; later, in Dessau, the actors usually wore simple tights, for example, in the "three primary colors, yellow, red, and blue, which corresponded to the three basic temperaments: sanguine—choleric——melancholic. And the masks were “adapted to the basic form of the face so that a universal, typical head resulted."100 Because for Schlemmer, as we have seen, the metaphysical was realizedin an order that conformed to a law, the movements of the actors in space followed a strict geometry, a "dancer's mathematics":101

In the production workshops of the Dessau Bauhaus, the challenge to provide optimal solutions and rational production methods led to a push for teamwork. In the stage department, it was Schlemmer's unconditional demand for precision, which derived from the basic concept of a "dancer's mathematics," that required the students to be especially willing and able to cooperate, to become part of the stage collective. This in no way meant that Schlemmer suppressed the individual traits of the students but simply that he worked to connect these traits to the shared task. This was, if you will, the artist's mosaic-like contribution toward realizing the goal of the social utopia of the Bauhaus. Schlemmer also offered his students opportunities to develop their own creativity, to experiment on their own, as can be seen from numerous stage works that are only loosely related, or even entirely unrelated, to the master's notions of form. The latter is the case with the Reflective Plays of Light that Ludwig Hirschfeld-Mack, Josef Hartwig, and Kurt Schwerdtfeger developed in 1922 outside of the stage workshop; as early as 1923, however, in the book Staatliches Bauhaus Weimar, 1919–1923, the Plays were included in the chapter on the stage workshop. Schlemmer and especially Moholy-Nagy, whose essential interest in problems of lighting design were discussed above (“Aspects of His Artistic Work" in chapter 6), acknowledged these students at the Bauhaus for their use of “modern technology" for design.105

## The Human Being as Object of Instruction

As in his oeuvre in painting, sculpture, and drawing, and of course as a teacher, Schlemmer's true interest in his work for the stage was in the theme of the human being. Though he frequently had to downplay this theme in the work study in consideration of the particular inclinations and possibilities of individual students, he was able to concentrate entirely on this, for him, central topic in the drawing class that was independent of the workshops and in the theoretical basis for .it.

But first we have to clarify the terminology. For the 1969 book Der Mensch, published in the series Neue Bauhausbücher, Heimo Kuchling put together more than 200 A4 format sheets from Schlemmer's literary estate relating to the “Der Mensch" course. Kuchling's use of the term "preliminary teaching" [Vorlehre] for this part of the instruction at the Bauhaus seems misleading, even indefensible. Schlemmer's drawing course had nothing in common with the intentions and functions of the real preliminary course. On the contrary, the course in life drawing, which Kuchling calls "the first preliminary course at the Bauhaus to be assigned to Schlemmer"107 took for granted the basic qualifications in drawing and design that were meant to be acquired in the preliminary course. Labeling the course “Der Mensch," which was established in the 1928–29 year as a required course for the third semester, must be attributed to a kind of inflation of terminology, since this course was anything but a propaedeutic class in view of the level of expectation and thus its intended audience, namely, the students who had already completed the two-semester preliminary course.

In 1921, at Gropius's suggestion, Schlemmer took over the leadership of the “evening nude" (sculptural nudes). In a phase during which the Bauhaus was increasingly oriented toward the principles of abstract constructivism, this gave the students an opportunity (in addition to life drawing with Itten and Klee) to grapple with the problems of depicting the human figure—under the leadership of a sovereign draftsman. It was clear to Schlemmer, who had “accepted with alacrity,"108 that life drawing risked being accused of conservatism, especially at the Bauhaus, with its emphatically antiacademic inclinations:

Drawing from the nude has fallen into disrepute among the moderns. The odium of academicism clings hard to it, no wonder, when the art now in vogue aims at the dissolution of form, deformation, anti-naturalism. So what do we look for from the nude today? Learning to draw at least—drawing from the nude takes this for granted—this can be learnt initially from inanimate nature and the living things of everyday life. It does not need to be the nude model for the student to feel after a form and reproduce it. Nor the manner of the reproduction—the ability to draw, in this or that style. The student must master allthis before he confronts the nude. Understand the nude as, so to speak, the highest nature, the finest articulation and organization, as an edifice of flesh, muscles, bones. The greatest variety of attitudes are possible, l wouldwish for the most objective Matisse: to be able to draw like Dürer before expressions are composed. Therefore set expressions aside, and so work towards objectivity. (Example Matisse.)“Expression too is objective." True, if by that is understood marked rhythm, elaboration of certain parts which are essential andspeciallystriking—theinner, imaginary structure, not the scientifically correct dissecting of bones—the muscular system, too, not anatomically exact, but rather the bearer of the functions of the fleshy edifice. The more objective the study, the greater will be the student's success in elaborating this. Knowledge of the actual muscles alone enables the artist to intellectualize them and make them serve purposes beyond mere correctness.

In front of the model, therefore, draw as correctly as possible and create the expression from memory.

No cheap effects, trying out of mannerism—in India ink, let alone in colour. Draw to as large a size as possible, not too small. Relate the drawing to the size of the surface. This is already composition, as far as it goes.

Those who have the attitude, should seek to satisfy its demands as precisely as possible, those who have not should try to find the one that suits them.109

Evèn this diary entry, which has a programmatic character, shows how much Schlemmer's view of figure drawing differed from the traditional norm of the academy, on the one hand, and from a problematic form of expressionism, on the other. Perhaps Schlemmer's rejection of expression when confronted with the nude model110 also contains an indirect criticism of Itten, who had introduced a highly expressive note into life drawing during the early years of the Bauhaus, as can be seen from Itten's own drawings from the period from 1918 to 1920 (see "The Course" in chapter 5).

Because Schlemmer's course on life drawing did not have him walking through the rows of students like a schoolmaster, looking over their shoulders to control them, or even intrude in their drawings in order to make corrections (evidence of his high pedagogical ethos, which consisted of an unconditional respect for the integrity of the students' creations) but rather took part by drawing himself, his own nude studies that were done at the Bauhaus are the best illustration of Schlemmer's antiacademic approach. In contrast to the deliberate, dry, detailed manner that was then still typical at many art schools, one is struck by the relaxed and quite unconventional style of representation, for example, in the free draftsmanship in sketches made as the model walked about the room, which capture the typical rhythms of movement, or in the drawings that reveal a perspective very unlike the academic and sober one in their unusual lighting effects, such as footlights. Heimo Kuchling accurately captured the difference from the academic view of the nude in noting that Schlemmer's nudes "do not spring from a vain endeavour to provide an optically 'faithful' rendering of the model. Schlemmer's 'objective attitude' consists in the fact that he fixes the static or dynamic structure and the plastic contour of the body with a largely unblurred line used as'pure means.' Use of the pure line compels the artist to a process of abstraction."111 In keeping with Schlemmer's art theory and its basis in Goethe, the point here is not to imitate nature but to strive for style.

The circumstances under which Schlemmer executed his life drawings were also antiacademic. For example, in Dessau the stage was usually used as the site for life drawing, which made it possible to strip the exercise of its sterile character by changing the lighting and sometimes even using gramophone music. Moreover, the drawings were not done using professional models in practiced, stereotypical poses; the students themselves took turns as the model. When they were not drawing nudes in motion but rather poses of oneto two-hour sittings, during the breaks Schlemmer would analyze the position at the blackboard “by emphasizing the essential, the basic forms, the axes of movement, the skeletal and muscular structure, light and shadow"112 in order to give the students the necessary stimuli for the process of discovery and learning.

Early on it became clear to Schlemmer that the study of the nude alone was not enough to convey an image of the human being in all its aspects. Consequently, as early as 1922 he proposed to the council of masters, as part of a consolidation of the instruction in form, placing “the human figure at the center of my investigations. 'Man, the measure of all things' provides so many possibilities for variation and for relationships to architecture and craftsmanship that one would merely have to extract the essentials. Therefore: measurement, proportions, and anatomy; typicality and special features. The various guiding ideals of the different artistic styles. The dynamics of the body. Movement. Dance. Kinesthetic sense. Man in his relationship to the world about him."113 What is remarkable here is not just the coupling of the theme "human being" with architecture and craftsmanship but also the fact that it amounts to an outline in anticipation of the course “Der Mensch," which would only be realized six years later.

Not until the fall of 1927 did Schlemmer take another step toward his goal of expanding life drawing in the direction of the more comprehensive theme "the human being": "We have no continuation for the advanced fourth-semester students. I suggested 'figural drawing' which was well received and adopted at once. So I will now probably have to give that once a week. I hope to work up this subject, which I like so much and the students greet eagerly, with the idea of formulating a 'Compendium of the Figural.'"114 And a month later: "The figural drawing is going well. Excellent training in clarity and self-awareness."115 In the wake of a revision in the curriculum of the coursework at the Bauhaus in early 1928, which sought toplace more emphasis on science and training—a trend Schlemmer regardedwithdiscomfort (“In general it's becoming very pedagogic"116 and “I don't like playing the schoolmaster"117)—the artist managed to give life and figure drawing the greater theoretical emphasis he had long sought for it, with the introduction of his broader course “Der Mensch." .Whereas in 1927 the elective figural drawing course clearly put the focus on more specialized areas like theory of proportion, anatomy, and biomechanics, in 1928 the course“Der Mensch," now required for the third semester, emphasized an overarching anthropological basis for all teaching at the Bauhaus:

For the "new life," which ought to represent a modern feeling for the world and life, knowledge of the human being as a cosmic being is essential. The conditions of its existence, its relations to its natural and artificial surroundings, its mechanism and organism, its material, spiritual, andintellectual manifestation, in short: the human being as a physical and mental being is both necessary and meaningful as a field of instruction.

The course is divided—like the graphic representation, the scientific structure, and the transcendental worldofideas—into three parts: the formal, the biological, and the philosophical. During the instruction the three parts alternate before finally uniting in the totality of the term "human being."118

The core of the formal section was figural drawing, which included the following subtopics:

—theory of the systems of scale and proportion (e.g., Golden Section, the canon of Polyclitus, Leonardo's diagram of proportions, Dürer's Unterweisung der Messung [Instruction in measure, translated as The Painter's Manua/], systems of scale according to C. Schmidt, G. Buschan, and C. G. Carus, Schlemmer's "box person");

—theory of the construction of heads and of types of heads;

—research into the mechanics and kinetics of the human body, both body movements "in themselves" and as movements in space, especially in built and constructed space—research that has since become a fixture of training for architects and, under the catchword "ergonomics," for designers;

—research into the representation of figures in ancient and modern art.

Without overestimating the reach of design methods based on measurement and construction, Oskar Schlemmer certainly recognized that intuition alone was not enough and also that knowledge of certain schematic and constructive possibilities of representation could be useful to the student, especially when studying the human figure. Schlemmer's efforts should be interpreted in this sense, as reducing the natural shapes to basic geometrical forms in order to obtain in this way “simple orgąnizational schemes, the autonomy of which, like the laws governing their assemblage, could be apprehended and could therefore be applied with great consistency. . . . They are a specific foundation for the (imaginary) construction of a human figure and yet are so general and so little binding that individual formulations can be evolved out of them. .. . With this method Schlemmer won anti-academic ground for figure drawing too."119

![](images/4dc6780d52e4618811f94c044f866d4d64a0ad272c6a45601db042ea7db7917f.jpg)

It is interesting that in the formal section of the course“Der Mensch" Schlemmer was essentially building on the “anthropocentric constructivism" of his Stuttgart period, for example, where he developed the statics and dynamics of the human figure systematically from schematic structural lines (starting from vertical lines, the letter i, and vertical lines that have begun to vibrate),120 or where he, following Dürer and the Italian mannerists (e.g., Luca Cambiaso), constructed the figure as a “box person” made up of cubes (also of cylinders and spheres or circles) in order to demonstrate its physicality and tectonic structure using simple means.

Here again we see the tendency, so characteristic of the Bauhausas a whole, to reduce things to geometrical figures. It is related to a process that began at the latest with the beginning of the modern age, with the Renaissance, which Rudolf zur Lippe has convincingly analyzed as the “geometrization of the human being."121 Very briefly, this is the view that the human being, under the dictate of functional rationality, is subjectedto increasingrestrictions in hisoriginal physicality. The most enduring effects have been the result of industrialization, with its processes based on the division of labor (Taylorism), but the process existed even earlier, for example, in ceremonies at court, in the military, in dance, andin sports, where naturalmotions are divided into subprocesses and then reconstructed according to predetermined geometrical configurations. In art the effects of the geometrizationof the human being can be seen most clearly in the introduction

If we were to apply this extraordinarily interesting interpretative approach to the evaluation of art or to Schlemmer's teachings, the result would have to be negative. The artist's figures would have to be interpretedas the corollary to the human being denatured by technology and industry, as a soulless robot--an interpretation that certainly accords with the tendencies to social criticism found in art of the period. With respect to Schlemmer's goals, however, the misunderstanding could hardly be more grave, and the artist himself always remarked on the danger of such a misinterpretation: "so now I try to construct the human body from the simple mathematical bodies and their combinations. The simple mathematical body is both the origin and the completion of every physical form. By doing this I am struggling to overcome the ever-threatening mechanism that would make the human being into a machine.... People often object that my 'human beings' have no 'faces,' at most 'puppet faces.' Unfortunately, they don't understand that that is the intention, that it cannot be otherwise. Before we can paint 'faces,' before we are allowed to do so, we must recognize the type, the impersonal. That is truly not a 'loss of individuality.'"122

Schlemmer's humane aspiration is clearly manifested in the scientific (biological) section of his course as well, where he spoke on questions of the origin of life; of cell and seed theory; of anatomy, the mechanics of the body, and the function of organs; of problems of nutrition, hygiene, and clothing. This "theory of life activities," which points beyond the limited field of physiology, offered Schlemmer a transition to the philosophical section that he considered especially important. Here his intention was to lay out the major systems of thought from antiquity to the modern period, "focusing on discussions of the concept of substance, notions of space and time, and last but not least the essence of the soul," ending "with the metaphysical knowledge, to clarify questions .of aesthetics and ethics."123

With his comprehensive course “Der Mensch," which exploded the boundaries between fields, Schlemmer hoped to give his students what he felt to be most lacking at the Bauhaus, namely, a worldview; and hence in May 1928 he was "curious to see . . . what picture of the world will emerge by the end of the semester."124 It is clear that the course “Der Mensch" not only reflected very precisely Schlemmer's striving for totality that was nourished on the intellectual tradition of romanticism but also corresponded to the basic Bauhaus idea of reconstructing a totality of life by means of educating generalists rather than training specialists. That the goal of “totalization" (to use Klee's term) in an industrial society based on a high degree of division of labor and differentiation of function is, of course, extremely controversial, something that we can only mention but not discuss in detail. The only certain thing is that we are hardly more likely to reach this goal by overloading education with encyclopedic details of partial aspects from the most varied academic disciplines. Thus the critique of the course “Der Mensch" applies not to the formal (drawing) portion—here Schlemmer was sovereign as both artist and pedagogue—but to the biological and philosophical portions. There he not only clearly demanded too much of his students but seems to have taken on too much himself, despite his unusual knowledge of the literature and his intensive engagement with the material. The fact that this course“could not do justice to its universal proportions and high goals in all of its parts" can only be partially explained by the limitęd time available to prepare for it.125 Rather, it must be attributed above all to the fundamental impossibility that an individual in the twentieth century could make good on such a claim on totality.

Nevertheless, it is still Schlemmer's historical contribution to pedagogy at the Bauhaus to have been the only one to have placed the human being so completely at the center of his teachings and to have made it the theme for the design of the everyday world, both in terms of metaphysics and scale. The emphasis on the human being was an endeavor which also appears in the popular Bauentwurfslehre (first published in 1936) by the Bauhaus member Ernst Neufert and in The Modulor by Le Corbusier, who published his highly influential system of measure based on the proportions of the human body in 1948.

## The Possibilities and Limits of Artistic Teachings

Schlemmer was active as a teacher for just over twelve years: almost nine years at the Bauhaus, around three years at the Academy of Art in Breslau, and (until his dismissal by the National Socialists in 1933) half a year at the Vereinigte Staatschulen für Kunst und Kunstgewerbe (United state schools for art and arts and crafts)in Berlin. He was a teacher but no schoolmaster, and as a teacher he thought deeply about the possibilities and limits of teaching art: about what can be taught and what cannot. His thoughts on these issues will be outlined in conclusion in terms of a central point for pedagogy at the Bauhaus, namely, the problem of a theory of harmony in modern art and in the training of artists.

As a master student of Adolf Hölzel at the academy in Stuttgart, Schlemmer was familiar with his teacher's efforts to find a theory of harmony analogous to that of music, a counterpoint of painting. He played an instrument himself and loved Bach especially (like Itten and Klee), and his fascination with this idea of counterpoint or figured bass, which he shared with Kandinsky, is demonstrated by numerous passages in letters anddiaries, journal articles, interviews, and speeches. One is, however, immediately struck by an ambivalence in Schlemmer's attitude; on the one hand, as we have seen, he sought "strict regularity" (Runge), "number, scale, and law,"126and a seemingly supraindividual formal language; on the other, he approached the goal of counterpoint in the fine arts with critical distance: "Counterpoint in painting will never be discovered, or it will be, in contrast to that of music, dryly schematic. The creative processes in the two arts differ entirely."127 Further:

It is, however, still a strange fact that even today no universally valid canon for the laws of painting has been found—unlike in music, which is so bound to laws. Goethe certainly hoped that the contrabass could be found in paintingas well; certainly in Dürer's work and later in that of someone like Caspar David Friedrich a significant degree of law of color and form has survived; certainly Delaunay and Hoelzel of the ultimate academy of painters where the rules of art. finally discovered, would now besafequarded. But what is even the theory of form and color of someone like Klee or Kandinsky theones who have surely realized the dreams most completely, by creatinga well-ordered pedagogy—what is it other than the key to their own kingdom and—in the pedagogical sense— the ABC and the first aids for the composition of form, color, plane? For beyond the scaffold of form and the scale of color the imponderables begin: the immeasurable, the inconceivable, the mystical aspect of the work of art, which the more original it is raises the question of the law again. .. . Why does law in painting, if not practiced by a superior mind, so easily become a constricting dogma? Why is the result there so often the sterile stylization rather than the fulfilled style?128

Ofthe diverse reasons that makethe prospect of a figured bass of modern art seem dubious, Schlemmer recognized the central one with all possible clarity. It is a social question, and it pertains to the fact that the "basis ... for something shared, for an agreement"129no longer exists in a pluralistic society, and consequently the search for a binding theory of harmony is of necessity futile from the outset. So Schlemmer drew the only adequate conclusion for artistic theory—and here he agreed with his teacher Hölzel—that the knowledge of particular rules, of particular principles of design, may be helpful for the student, but these rules represent nothing more than a“skeleton of an artistic theory," and their schematic application necessarily leads to "sterile stylization," to “manner."

According to Schlemmer, no art can exist that is controlled entirely by a system of rules that can be rationally calculated, because “geometry, the Golden Section, the theory of proportions . . . are dead and fruitless if they are not experienced, felt, sensed. We must allow ourselves to be surprised by the miracle of proportion, by the magnificence of the relations of numbers and the correspondences and to form laws from these sorts of results."130 Art, then, not as a simple act of applying a law but as an innovative act of creating a law, is a process that in Schlemmer's view should progress from emotion to law, from intuition to construction, from the subjective to the objective. In this respect Schlemmer differed fundamentally from Hölzel, who in his own compositions started from pictorial laws, from a theoretically considered, rationally constructed pictorial framework, in order to free himself from it step by step and disperseinto unpredictable“accidental effects."131 By contrast, Schlemmer started out from the primary sensation and allowed "form . . . [to develop] out of the conditions set by the materials; the result is order"132 that is freer and newer than anything that had existed before. It is clear

## Biographical Notes

The artistic career of Joost Schmidt, born in Wunstorf near Hanover in 1893, initially followed the academic paths usual at the time. In 1910 he entered the Grand Ducal Saxon Academy of Arts in Weimar, where he was a student in Max Thedy's painting class, received his diploma in painting in 1913, and was named a master student in 1914. After

fighting in the war in France, where he was seriously wounded and taken prisoner by the Americans, he returned to Weimar in 1919, attracted by Gropius's Bauhaus manifesto, to learn at first hand about the palpable revolutionary mood and the innovations that were being discussed everywhere. According to his wife, Helene Schmidt-Nonné, however, “the modern trends and teaching methods were not to his liking, and so with some of the other former professors he joined the rival academic institution, which was re-established in the same building of the Bauhaus in the spring of 1921."3

When he finally did join the Bauhaus at the age of twenty-seven, having been accepted into an apprenticeship in sculpture, it seems to have been in connection with the work on the SommerfeldHouse, home toBerlinlumberman. Gropius, in collaboration with his colleague Adolf Meyer, had designed a wood house—stylistically related to the prairie houses of Frank Lloyd Wright—for which the client wished to have teakwood paneling and carvings made for the interior. It was Joost Schmidt who accepted the difficult task of executing the desired carvings for the doors, the stairwells, and the gallery on the upper floor. Schmidt was restricted in his themes by Sommerfeld's request for symbolic representations of his sawmills in Schneidemühl, Kolberg, Danzig (Gdansk), and other sites in eastern Germany. Only in the designs for the doors did Schmidt have a free hand. Here "decorative geometry was the formula for the design,"4 and it is obvious that he drew inspiration from Itten's theory of the socalled characters of forms (triangle, circle, square characters, see ,“The Course" in chapter 5). This is also confirmed by Itten's claim in his book Mein Vorkurs am Bauhaus (translatedas Design and Form) that these works resulted from the influence of his course. Even in these early reliefs it is possible to see the direction that Joost Schmidt would take, namely, “towardabstraction based on the spatial relationships of simple geometric elements."5

Joost Schmidt's work as a fine artist has hardly been studied at all. Indeed, his intensive activity in applied graphic arts and his extensive teaching left very little time for his own art. In 1932 the Bauhaus in Dessau was closed. ## From the Lettering Course to the Elementary Design Course

As both teacher and person, Joost Schmidt was extraordinarily well liked at the Bauhaus. The students in particular liked him very much for his teaching and design abilities, his personal modesty, and his inexhaustible willingness to help. Especially in the years from 1928, which have been characterized as a phase of disintegration (see chapter 3), through his all but inexhaustible pedagogical engagement and the enormous range of courses he taught, Schmidt played a decisive role in keeping Bauhaus pedagogy from dissociating into the separate domains of learning and doing and did as much as possible to maintain the integration of the two as originally intended. This is evident in his efforts to combine the elementary design experience gained in the plastic arts workshop with the professional qualifications obtained in the advertising workshop into the special applied field of exhibition design while also establishing close connections between the advertising department and the photography department led by Walter Peterhans. The same intention can also be seen in his effort to expand the required course on type and lettering into an elementary design course that would complement the offerings of Albers, Klee, and Kandinsky.

In addition to teaching basic skills in lettering and the construction of type, Schmidt's course on type and lettering undertook explorations of the fundamental determinants of lettering and typefaces (consideredfrom the perspectives of language, the psychology of expression andoptics, technology, and economics).14 He was sharply critical of the bewildering variety of letter forms and especially ofGerman particularism in this area (the continued use of Fraktur, the German blackletter style): "Historical and national vanities and prejudices, along with all the corresponding forms of letters, belong in the junkyard of culture."15His goal was to simplify radically the forms of letters and to set international standards in order to establish the minimum conditions for a less problematic way to communicate among nations. The search for a systematic approach to the forms of letters was by no means rare during the twenties; for example, Kurt Schwitters worked to develop such a system as part of his interest in applied graphics.16

In contrast to several other Bauhaus masters who considered teaching a burden (this is true not only of Feininger and later Klee but also of junior masters such as Herbert Bayer), Schmidt showed a genuine interest in it. The background of Schmidt's concept of education was not so much his own aesthetic practice and theoretical reflections, as was generally thecase with the artistteachers already discussed, but rather the students, whom he sought“to develop" by working from their “abilities and skills."17 He did not merely trust in spontaneous self-realization but was convinced of the need for systematic instruction. Though we may be justified in doubting whether the details of the division into three categories—"teaching program," "teaching method," "teaching goals"—are always correct, Schmidt's teaching concept certainly seems to be the summa of everything that constitutes Bauhaus pedagogy. It contains the idea——shared by all the Bauhaus masters—of the double determination of design by understanding and feeling, reason and intuition (although, as we have seen, the different masters certainly weighted the two differently). In addition, we see topoi of Bauhaus pedagogy in the wording of such goals as “liberation from dead conventions," "development of creative energies," and "development of manual skills," as well as in methodological principles such as "learning by doing," "from the simple to the difficult," and“from the elementary to the complex." Schmidt's teaching concept corresponds in general outline to the subject matter of Bauhaus pedagogy as well: design exercises with geometrical or stereometric basic forms; developing artistic principles of order and of the experience with design rules (scale, proportion, weight, quantity, quality, and so on); confronting problems of form, space, color, and materials; finally, very generally: increasing the sensitivity of our perception of our surroundings, intensifying our experience of things, and encouraging the ability to articulate individual artistic ideas.

What Schmidt's basic instruction consisted of in practice can be reconstructed, at least in part, from student works that have survived, from drawings by the artist's wife, and from the recollections of former students. However, it is often difficult to distinguish clearly between the products of this propaedeutic design course that developed from the obligatory courses on type and lettering and the practice works from the advertising workshop.

A characteristic feature of the design exercises from Joost Schmidt's courses is a standardized grid of three by three squares into which the students had to place nine variant solutions to the assigned task. This exercise was based on the idea of training creative thinking, thinking in alternatives, and also of making the students aware that there could be more than one solution to a design problem, a view that Schmidt shared with Josef Albers. The course began with exercises in the planar subdivision of assigned form elements like rectangles, circles, squares, points, lines, bars, and so on, in order to give the students experience in the variety of possible combinations even in a drastically reduced repertoire of drawing. These exercises took place against the background of a general theory of contrasts of the sort that Itten had taught during the founding phase of the Bauhaus (for example, ordered-disordered, big-small, round-angular, strong-weak, near-far, static-dynamic). Subsequent exercises in contrasts applied the same principles to individual letters or meaningless syllables, including studies in which nine contrasting variants of letters were to be found for a given, constant symbol. In accordance with the methodological principle of increasing difficulty in small, gradual steps, the level of difficulty of the assigned tasks increased from section to section as the course progressed. For example, using collage elements, the students were asked to design not only formal contrasts but also thematic ones; and, as Helene Schmidt-Nonné has shown, “a degree of social critique"19 can be seen in the selection of the materials used for the pictures and even more so in the combinations.

In addition to these exercises in contrasts and the straightforward instruction in type and lettering, the students in Joost Schmidt's course were given a solid introduction to the methods of perspectival representation (parallel and vanishingpoint perspective) that at the time—according to Kranz—had become an aesthetic goal unto themselves in all of the Bauhaus departments.20 This emphasis is also demonstrated in the studies of the geometry of the human figure (see above), which, like the exercises with type and lettering, were sometimes executed spatially (i.e., using isometrics or central perspective).

Finally, Schmidt also assigned exercises in color theory, though admittedly they were not nearly as intellectually in-depth as those of, say, Klee or Kandinsky. In contrast to the philosophical and metaphysical foundations of the latter, Schmidt's instruction on color was decidedly sober and practical. In the above-mentioned program of 19 January 1928 one of the goals was called the “development of a feeling for color, ideas of color." The way in which this goal was to be reached is clear from a passage in the catalog of the International Congress for Educators in the Arts in Prague in 1928, which remarks on Joost Schmidt's teachings on color at the Bauhaus: "Color—color system according to Ostwald; color studies."21 Though this program point may seem obscure, the mention of Ostwald speaks volumes. Whereas Klee and Itten—in the latter case, following Adolf Hölzel, his teacher in Stuttgart—vehemently rejected the scientifically conceived, quantifying color theory of the chemist and Nobel Prize winner Wilhelm Ostwald (1853–1932) and rejected it as useless for the arts, Gropius had begun as early as 1923, following the first far-reaching change of course at the institute, to display Ostwald's color circle in the rooms of the Bauhaus. In 1927 Ostwald was invited to the school for lectures, and in 1931 he was even accepted into the circle of “Friends of the Bauhaus." Kandinsky, who had led the workshop for wall painting in Weimar, had often referred his Dessau students to Ostwald (see "Theory of Design: Theory of Color" in chapter 8); and in the Dessau workshop for wall painting, led by Hinnerk Scheper, Ostwald's arrangement of colors was displayed for purposes of demonstration. From about 1923 on, advertising in Germany enjoyed an unprecedented boom based on the arqument that it “improved quality and awakened egalitarian needs" and also functioned“in the serviceof democratization."24 To date just a few of the most important events: in 1923 the magazine Gebrauchsgrafik (Applied qraphics) was established; in 1925 the Union of German Window Dressers was founded and began to publish the magazine Schaufenster: Kunst undTechnik (Display windows: Art and technology); in 1926 Roy S. Durstine's book Making Advertisements and Making Them Pay (New York: C. Scribner's Sons, 1920)appeared in German translation; and in 1929 the International Poster Exhibition took place in Munich.

In Germany it was Moholy-Nagy, Herbert Bayer, and Joost Schmidt and, outside the Bauhaus, Max Burchartz, Jan Tschichold, and Paul Renner; in Russia, it was Rodchenko and El Lissitzky, among others, who made decisive contributions to the artistic distinction of the New Advertising that was flourishing at time, based on the principles of constructivism and the postulate of objectivity. In contrast to the raucous deportment of the usual rhetorical advertisements, the practice of these men was based to a large degree on ethics. Their motto was “Neither suggestion nor information"—that is to say, their goal was not to inveigle someone into something but to persuade“by means of a clear presentation of facts, of economic and scientific data."25 Along with students in the advertising department and in collaboration with the workshop for interior furnishings at the Bauhaus, Joost Schmidt put these principles into practice most convincingly in his avant-garde and groundbreakingexhibition buildings in Berlin (1929)andDresden (1930). These “three-dimensional, accessible diagrams or graphic demonstration sites"26were meant to form a synthesis ofart andcommerce—an idea that had already been propagated by the German Werkbund before World War I.

## The Plastic Arts Workshop

Thę students of the plastic arts workshop, which was also run by Joost Schmidt, took part in the works created for these exhibition stands as well. The information available about this workshop is sparse. Even the date of its creation has not yet been firmly established. Wingler dates it from the fall of 1925;²7 Eckhard Neumann comments that Schmidt led the plastic arts workshop “from the beginning in Dessau";28 Helene Schmidt-Nonné reports that the workshop began to be established in the spring of 1927, a date that fits with the details given by another member of the Bauhaus, Heinz Loew;29 in other relevant literature the date 1928 is found.30 The following remark from the notes of Helene Schmidt-Nonné might help to clear up these discrepancies: “The stage workshop and the plastics arts workshop were not separated initially; Joost Schmidt was master of form and work for the plastic arts workshop; for the stage, just technical master. Because his conception did not coincide with that of Schlemmer, he ceased work in the stage workshop and concentrated his work on the plastic arts workshop"; in addition, she notes that “at first this workshop did not have a budget and even later it had only a minimal, symbolic one."31 Thus, Schmidt apparently did lead the plastic arts workshop from 1925 on, but initially it existed only as an appendage to the stage workshop—it is no coincidence that Schmidt produced designs for a mechanical stage at that time—and only later regained the institutional autonomy it had had as the sculpture workshop before the move to Dessau. There seems to have been a gradual process of emancipation that found its culmination in 1928 with the formal declaration of its parity with the other Bauhaus workshops.

The renaming of the Weimar workshops for wood and stone sculpture as the "plastic arts workshop" was more than just a rhetorical maneuver designed to shed terminology that was considered outdated and that evoked the fine arts. It corresponded precisely to the change of direction in the Bauhaus program from about 1923 that we have mentioned several times. In that context, it not only makes sense that no sculptures were produced in the plastic arts workshop during the first years in Dessau but also that this workshop would have been subsumed under the goals of the stage workshop (the production of threedimensional stage sets and props). It was Joost Schmidt's personal achievement to free the plastic arts workshop from this functional context and bring it the attention unquestionably merited by the design of three-dimensional objects in the teaching program of the Bauhaus. This was all the more necessary in 1928, because Moholy-Nagy, who had placed great emphasis on sculpture in his preliminary course, had left the Bauhaus and so—apart from Albers's preliminary course—there was hardly any opportunity left for a systematic focus on the phenomena of bodies and space.

Thus, in contrast to the work in other workshops at the Bauhaus, which focused on applications and consisted in part of commissions, the emphasis in the plastic arts workshop was on the "elementary study of three-dimensional, sculptural relationships."32 That is, it was not about “autonomous art" or about "art at the construction site" or about the production of three-dimensional utensils but instead provided a general education in the plastic arts. Its goal was "to awake, to develop, to intensify the ability to imagine space and the consciouș experience of spatial sense perceptions, and to realize spatial ideas."33

In keeping with the formal reductionism of basic geometrical forms that dominated the Bauhaus, and based on his previous experience with the design of the vestibule reliefs of 1923, Joost Schmidt rigorously limited the field of tasks to

systematic exploration of elementary spatial figures: the cube, the sphere, the cylinder, the cone, and so on. At first the figures are produced by the students, that is, they are modeled in clay, molded in plaster, and become objects of experiment. A period of almost scientific laboratory work begins: examination of scale relationships, proportional relations, strength, the possibility of changing positions. ## 12 Preliminary Remarks on the Reception of the Bauhaus in Germany

A comprehensive history of the reception of the Bauhaus has yet to be written. Perhaps this is because it spread internationally, and the ramifications to which the ideas, principles, and methods of the Bauhaus were subject became too elaborate to summarize, so that such a gigantic task seemed all but impossible.1 This is true not only of any attempt to present the influences of the Bauhaus in the fields of architecture, design, and the fine arts—that is, at the level of material culture— but also in the field of education. A historical assessment of the reception of the pedagogical ideas for art and design at this art school of modernism, even if confined to Germany, would be a project that would exceed the framework of this book and would have to be the subject of its own study. In chapters 2 and 4 we saw it would be impossible to provide a prehistory of theBauhaus and its pedagogical premises that would be even approximately complete; a history of the recep tion of the Bauhaus that could claim to be thorough is equally impossible.² Consequently, the discussion that follows, which because of the need for stringent selection can do little more than cast spotlights and sidelights, must be accepted from the start as a mere fragment. This is true even though those who were involved in this history in some form but who are mentioned not at all or not adequately here might become sensitive or suffer wounded pride.

## The Weimar Republic

The historical effects of Bauhaus pedagogy which can be especially clearly recognized in the principle of workshop teaching, the value placed on architecture, the concept of the preliminary course, and the permeation of elementary design theory in arts education in the schools—did not begin, as is often assumed, with the closing of the Bauhaus in 1933 and the subsequent emigration of numerous Bauhaus masters and students to the United States in particular (see chapter 13) but is already evident during the Weimar Republic both in professional training for artists and in aesthetic education in schools. It is, however, important to distinguish precisely here. For it would be a mistake in a historical review to ascribe to the influence of the Bauhaus everything that at first glance might seem to be a concept or effect typical of this art school of modernism, even if it was the most important school of its kind. It is essential to recall that, like the Bauhaus, many art schools and applied art schools of the interwar period were indebted to the ideas of the debate on art school reform that were outlined in "Bauhaus Pedagogy in the Context of Art School Reform" in chapter 4; and that after World War I these schools deeply influenced by ideas that circulated first in the Arbeitsrat für Kunst (Work council for the arts) and the Novembergruppe (November group), for example, and later more broadly in the German Werkbund. For that reason, one cannot simply speak of a blanket dependence on the great model of the Bauhaus, even though it did, of course, have an influence. Such influence was most direct in cases where members of the Bauhaus themselves took with them to other schools the concepts of education and training they had developed as teachers or experienced as students.

This sort of complex set of problems can be seen, for example, in the case of the Academy of Art in Breslau, under its directors Hans Poelzig (1903–16), August Endell (1918–25), and Oskar Moll (1925-32). Hartmut Frank has shown, in an essay with the apt title "Ein Bauhaus vor dem Bauhaus" (A Bauhaus before the Bauhaus), that in Breslau under Poelzig "the concept of the reform of arts and crafts training that would later be considered the characteristic creation of the Bauhaus"3 was already in practice. For example, Poelzig considered the workshops—alongside the courses in particular subjects—as the most important places for learning at his school; and even before Johannes Itten introduced the preliminary course at the Bauhaus, the Breslau academy had a preliminary class. Moreover, Poelzig considered it essential to integrate all of the genres of the visual arts with all of the branches of the crafts under the primacy of architecture; this was, of course, an idea that Gropius later postulated as his central concern in the Bauhaus manifesto of 1919.

In contrast to Poelzig, who was a tireless force behind art school reform even before World War I, August Endell, one of the outstanding Jugendstil artists of Germany, supported from 1918 on "the transformation of the Breslau school from a reformed applied arts school to a pure art academy . . . that also had workshops."4 Although Endell was accused of weak leadership and a lack of vision, it is interesting to note with respect to support for the Modern Movement that the appointment of Adolf Rading, an active supporter of the Neues Bauen (New building) movement, as professor of architecture occurred during Endell's directorship. It should also be recalled that Rading in turn led to the appointment of another proponent of this movement, Hans Scharoun (who as an expressionist architect had been a member of the Arbeitsrat für Kunst), to the Breslau academy in 1925.

Under the direction of the painter Oskar Moll from 1925 on, the academy in Breslau became “a gathering place of the artistic avant-garde in Germany, . . . a place for an . . . undogmatic form of modernism."5 This meant that the school created an environment in which elements of the Bauhaus could flourish alongside expressionists like Otto Mueller andmasters of NeueSachlichkeit like Carlo Mense and Alexander Kanoldt; for example, Oskar Schlemmer was appointed director of the stage class in 1929 and continued to teach the project “Der Mensch" that he had begun at the Bauhaus, and Georg Muche was a member of the teaching faculty as professor of painting from 1931 to 1933. We should also mention Johannes Molzahn, who was not a member of the Bauhaus in the strict sense, but who was closely associated with the early Bauhaus and was active in Breslau between 1928 and 1932. Rather than go into detail about the subjectsandmethods of these artists as teachers, it must suffice to cite a statement Schlemmer made that casts a spotlight on the pedagogical principles at the academy in

Breslau during the late twenties and early thirties: "Bauhaus teaching (Klee, Kandinsky): making the personal universal. Applied to Breslau, extremist: creating, participation of the students, involvement in their own larger projects (the old system of the 'master studio'). Thus an egoistic principle: work as the best means to develop oneself and others. Basically the principle that goes without saying: the best things happen when someone enjoys something, i.e., is totally engaged in it. That can only happen with one's own work. For the academy, then, the point is to harness this egoistic element for the universal. The whole teaching plan must be built on that."6 This “individualistic" conception was also reflected in the fact that Breslau had two preliminary classes side by side, one for fundamentals of drawing and another for painting, which in their increased specialization and the closer connection of the teachers to artistic practice were conceived as far less general than was the case for the preliminary course at the Bauhaus. The material studies in drawings from the preliminary class of Paul Dobers, which aimed at knowledge of the “secret life, [the] uniqueness" of things,7 reveal certain parallels to student works from the preliminary course as taught by Itten and Albers, but the academy at Breslau lacked the comprehensive goal of a general introduction to elementary principles of design that was sought at the Bauhaus.

If at the academy in Breslau under Endell and even more so Moll the accent was clearly on the fine arts, the trend at the Kunstgewerbeschule (arts and crafts school) in Halle, better known as Burg Giebichenstein (Giebichenstein castle), under its director, the architect Paul Thiersch, was emphatically on applied arts in the period between 1915 and 1928. Very much as Gropius did initially, Thiersch strongly emphasized the crafts, though, it seems, not so much with a utopian romanticism of the medievalBauhütte (masons' lodge) but with a more realistic andpragmatic orientation; this was almost a given, as the school had evolved from the artisans' school of the city of Halle.8 In keeping with this inclination, the workshops were systematically built up beginning in 1919, as teaching workshops at first, but already in 1920— much earlier than at the Bauhaus—as productive or production workshops. In Halle, however, the industrial orientation that was typical of the late Weimar Bauhaus and especially of the Dessau Bauhaus did not play a significant role. The “Burg," as it was called for short, "remained faithful even after 1925 to the workshop program and myth of the handicrafts that it had developed in the early twenties."9 The small craft-production series, not industrial mass production, was considered the standard, and the products designed and produced in Halle were distinct from the design's of the Dessau Bauhaus in their “moderate modernity," so that, despite all the similarities, "the Burg could be called an anti-intellectual pendant to the Bauhaus."10

Several members of the Bauhaus who came to the Burg beginning in the midtwenties were also representatives of this kind of moderate modernism. Gerhard Marcks did not make the move from Weimar, or rather Dornburg an der Saale (where the Bauhaus ceramics workshop was a kind of annex), to Dessau and instead accepted an appointment as director of the sculpture workshop (from 1928 to 1933 also interim director) at Halle. Benita Otte, who was at the Bauhaus in Weimar from 1920 to 1925, became artistic director of the workshop for hand weaving in 1925. Marguerite Friedlaender also made the move from Weimar to Halle in 1925, becoming director of the ceramics workshop (and later the porcelain workshop as well). Erich Dieckmann—alongside Marcel Breuer one of the outstanding furniture designers at the Bauhaus—took over the cabinetmakers' workshop at the Burg Giebichenstein in 1931, following an intermezzo at the Bauhochschule (School of Architecture) in Weimar. Hannes Wittwer, who was for a time Hannes Meyer's partner in his Swiss architectural office and a teacher at the Bauhaus from 1927 to 1929, became Paul Thiersch's successor as director of the architecture class in Halle in 1929. Erich Consemüller, who was at the Bauhaus from 1922 to 1929, taught at the Burg from 1931 to 1933 in the architecture class and in the advertising department; and Walter Herzger, a student at the Weimar Bauhaus from 1921 to 1924, led the graphic workshop from 1930 to 1933. Despite this brief sketch of the personal interconnections between the Bauhaus and the Burg,11 and despite certain stylistic influences, the school in Halle should in no way be seen as an offshoot of the Bauhaus. Rather, the Burg had its own version of the reform ideas typical of the time, even if it may not have been as radical as the Bauhaus in artistic matters or as intellectually sharp in conceptual ones, and perhaps even lacked its rigor and structural logic in pedagogical aspects as well.

It is not possible to discuss all the public art schools during the Weimar Republic that adopted elements of Bauhaus pedagogy. We will mention only the preliminary instruction that Max Peiffer Watenphul gave at the Folkwang-Schule in Essen from 1927 to 1931,19 the preliminary classes at the Landeskunstschule (Regional art school) in Hamburg given by Fritz Schleifer (a member of the Weimar Bauhaus) and Alfred Ehrhardt (a member of the Dessau Bauhaus; on whom more below) from 1930 to 1933,2º and the course given at the Kunstgewerbeschule in Stettin by Kurt Schwerdtfeger, Vincent Weber, and Else Mögelin.21 A quick look at several private art schools of the period that are of interest in the context of Bauhaus pedagogy would have to include Édmund Kesting's school Der Weg (The path) in Dresden (in Berlin as well from 1926 on) and the Itten-Schule in Berlin. The basis of both schools was not so much the idea of workshops that had been propagated with such vehemence by art school reformers and members of the Werkbund but rather the idea that the point was to use the medium of art to educate people to become creative (not necessarily to become artists or designers). This is understandable when we recall that Kesting, like Itten, was close to the expressionistic tendencies of the Sturm circle; thus, both started out with a very specific image of the human being and understanding of the world that, in contrast to the aesthetic ideology of the Werkbund, aimed more at developing and nurturing the individual.

The Dresden school Der Weg, which had the programmatic subtitle Neue Schule für Kunst (New school for art) and thus signaled its antiacademic aims even in its name, was founded in 1919, at almost exactly the same time as the opening of the Staatliches Bauhaus. As with the early Bauhaus, Kesting's school was dominated by expressionistic tendencies and the search for a “new human being" on a spiritual basis. A 1926 pamphlet reads:

Every human being travels a different path

But all of them should find a single goal.

We try to give all of the power to enable the path to be completed.

We summon all who want to know the reality of the spirit.

We summon all who want to explore the experience of creative laws.

We summon all who want to find themselves.

We summon all who want to find the other human being.

We summon all for whom art is life.

We summon all who seek life.

Because we walk the path to the human being.

Because we walk the path to art.22

Kesting found a companion to walk this path in the Sturm artist Lothar Schreyer, who had led the expressionistic Bauhaus stage from 1921 to 1923 and who not only taught at the Weg-Schule in Dresden from 1924 to 1927 but also led it together with Kesting, just as Hans Haffenrichter, a student at the Bauhaus from 1921 to 1924, took over the directorship of the Weg-Schule annex in Berlin in 1926. In a prospectus for the school from the midtwenties, the following global pedagogical goal was expressed: "The goal is to allow the students to develop individually and to place before them the rich variety of means of artistic expression."23 In addition to artistic subjects like drawing, painting, graphic arts, modeling, composition, and design, there were also courses in diverse branches of the applied arts (for example, textile design, metalworking, bookbinding, advertising, construction for trade fairs, interior design, and stage design), areas in which the abovementioned goals, somewhat effusive in their formulation and certainly no longer up-to-date in 1926, were given a realistic interpretation.

The situation was similar with the private art school run by Johannes Itten, which opened in 1926 as the Moderne Kunstschule Berlin, but was renamed the /tten Schoo/ in 1928 and was closed in 1934.24 Following his departure from the Bauhaus in the spring of 1923, Itten first spent several years at the Swiss Mazdaism center in Herrliberg, but he returned to Germany in 1926 to establish his own art school and realize his vision of another Bauhaus as a "school of the fine arts"25 (seę “Between Art and Design" in chapter 5). The teaching was on the basis of the concept of a holistic art training that Itten had developed during his years in Vienna and continued at the Bauhaus in Weimar. Consequently, the goals, topics, and methodological approaches in Berlin did not differ fundamentally from what Itten had practiced earlier. Thus a 1926 prospectus for the school read: "The goal of the instruction is to help develop the creative forces in the human being and to teach the means of the visual arts. To reduce . . . inhibitions and to develop physical abilities; to train the ability to think artistically and the powers of the soul, to teach the means of the visual arts."26 The artist-teacher Itten did not, of course, give up his spiritual and mystical orientation²7 in Berlin.

Nevertheless, the student works from his school in Berlin often display a more strict, constructive pictorial language than most of the studies from the preliminary course at the Bauhaus, which owe more to the spirit of expressionism. More so than in Weimar, where Itten emphasized the emotional understanding of the essence of things and the transformation of that understanding into art, his course on art was now permeated by rational elements. This can be seen most clearly in the textbook on design the Itten School published in 1930 under the title Tagebuch: Beiträge zu einem Kontrapunkt der bildenden Kunst (Diary: Contributions to a counterpoint of the visual arts). The subtitle clearly signaled a very specific ambition, namely, that of establishing a set of rules for art analogous to those of music that could provide the student of design with orientation and structure. It is equally obvious that Itten was building on the work of his Stuttgart teacher Hölzel, who spent his life searching for the "figured bass of painting."

Itten's private school in Berlin can be thought of as a very specific variant of the Bauhaus; this is clear not only from the founder's views on art pedagogy but also from the fact that practical subjects like architecture, photography, advertising, and graphic arts were taught alongside the fine arts. Unlike the Bauhaus and the reformed arts and applied arts schools of the period, Itten's school did not have workshops itself but rather supported the idea that "the students should, on completing the basic training, work as interns in the field."28 Elements of Bauhaus pedagogy flowed into the Itten School, in part because of the courses taught by the teachers who came from the Bauhaus; thus it acted both to preserve and multiply certain Bauhaus ideas. We should mention above all Georg Muche, who came from the Sturm circle, was appointed to the Bauhaus in 1920 on Itten's initiative, and joined the Itten School in 1927, before accepting an appointment to the Breslau Academy in 1931 (see above). Former Bauhaus students like Gyula Pap, Max Bronstein, Fred Forbat, and Ernst Neufert belonged to the teaching staff in Berlin; in addtion, Otto Umbehr (better known as Umbo) taught the photography class from 1928 to 1930, followed by Lucia Moholy, László Moholy-Nagy's first wife, who taught the class until her emigration in 1933.

In addition to the appropriation of elements of Bauhaus pedagogy in public and private art schools in the interwar period, the permeation of Bauhaus pedagogical conceptions into art and handicraft courses in the primary and secondary schools of the Weimar Republic represents a second strand relevant to the history of the reception of the Bauhaus. In handicraft courses in particular, according to Antje Middeldorf, the influence of the Bauhaus was “broad and anonymous,"29 though she qualified this with the remark that such crafts training was oriented more around the goals of the German Werkbund and not so much around the principles of the Bauhaus, which would have seemed too intellectual and formalistic to a typical schoolteacher of the time like Georg Stiehler. In 1925 Stiehler characterized a Bauhaus chair as "properly built—'like 2 x 2 = 4'— but also 'horribly impoverished, lacking all feeling.'"30 In this context it should, of course, be emphasized again that the Bauhaus, in turn, at least from 1923 on, was deeply permeated by Werkbund ideas. Nevertheless, Middeldorf identified in crafts training during the twenties such Bauhaus maxims as fidelity to the task, economy, and utility but also noted that the treatment of ornamental techniques and the "attempt to raise the pure utilitarian form to an art form" was very much “unlike the Bauhaus."31

In arts education in the Weimar Republic roughly a decade, after the founding of the Bauhaus, the design principles that dominated at the school seem to have found at least some footing, as evidenced by the student works exhibited at the convention of arts educators in Breslau (Wroclaw) in 1930: abstract compositions, photo montages, photograms, “cubist cities,"32 and so on. Determiningwhether—andif so, to what extent—these students works should be seen as a general trendwouldbe worthy of a separate study, for the distinctly irrationaland anti-intellectual (and also conservative) currents within arts education in the schools and in the pedagogical discourse of the time should not be overlooked or underestimated.33

Of the more famous arts pedagogues of the Weimar Republic who “wanted to make use of” the “emphatically rationalistic, abstract conceptual scheme" of the Bauhaus “within the framework of arts training that emphasizes the intuitive, the irrational aspect of the child's creative act,"34 we will mention only Christoph Natter and Hans-Friedrich Geist.

In the case of Christoph Natter, an arts instructor in Jena (i.e., in the immediate region of the early Bauhaus), we must distinguish carefully between the first edition of his book Künstlerische Erziehung aus eigengesetzlicher Kraft (Artistic education based on the strength of inherent laws) of 1924 and the second, revised edition of 1931. Although the jacket design of the first edition was by an artist of Moholy-Nagy's intellectual caliber, Natter missed no opportunity to polemicize against the “hegemony of reason″35 in general and in arts education in particular: “A one-sided intellectual education must prove ill-fated for our people. . . . Human reason. . . is creatively dead; for it has nothing in common with the life that comes from God. It is eclipsed."36His conclusion is: “Artistic education is for cultivating sensibility."37 It is thus necessary to descend“to the level of the soul," on which “everything relating to design, to the creative, to the harmonious" is based.38 Despite certain echoes of the somewhat effusively mystical rhetoric of the early, expressionistic Bauhaus, it is difficult to harmonize Natter's decidedly antirational views with the pedagogical maxims of the Bauhaus. Moreover, the student works that Natter illustrated in his book are too heterogeneous for it to be possible to assign them -definitively to the context of Bauhaus pedagogy. This view is also confirmed by the internal contradictions that are typical of the book as a whole. On the one hand, the author opposed the dictatorship of reason and called for arts education in the higher grade levels that "works purely artistically" in order to counter the “demands máde by scientific education";39 on the other hand, he made themes of elementary pictorial categoriès like line, form, space, brightness, and color, as well as formal laws like movement, rhythm, symmetry, and tension. In doing so, he made use in part of the terminology of so-called formal art history, as founded by Heinrich Wölfflin; for example, where he distinguished between the "painterly" and the "linear,"40 or where he takes up a groundbreaking and then highly influential theoretical work like Das Problem der Form in der bildenden Kunst (The problem of form in the visual arts; 1893) by the sculptor Adolf von Hildebrand in order to explain the principles of the sculptural.41

If the first edition of his book, apart from these sorts of excursions into the realm of reflections on formal analysis, stood under the sign of a powerful anti-intellectual effect paired with diffuse appeals to make “the creative" the "only principle of art"42 and to turn against every form of unproductive intellectualism, Natter seems to have made a clear change in course with the second edition of 1931, moving toward an artistic rationalism. Now he spoke of "primal artistic laws,"43 of “counterpoint,44 of scale and number; more than that, he alluded favorably to the exemplary achievements of the exponents of the Modern Movement, such as Malevitch, Tatlin, van Doesburg, Dexel, Kandinsky, Le Corbusier, and Gropius.45 Admittedly, he continued to make the criticism that in the "modern man" the intellect suppresses "the creative flow that comes from the depths of the unconscious,"46 but now his position was distinctly less one-sided. For example, he cited an essay by Gropius from 1923 in which the founder of the Bauhaus unmistakably argued for a balance of the rational and the irrational: “Reason must know the laws and guide the building hand in order for a creative thought to become manifest. Until the laws have been mastered, the idea remains stuck in chaos."47 There was but little time left for this second edition to find a wider reception among teachers before a new attitude toward art arose and with it another form of arts pedagogy, namely, that of the National Socialist state.

The influence of the Bauhaus on Hans-Friedrich Geist, who published his ideas on art and arts pedagogy in progressively inclined journals like bauhaus, Das Kunstblatt, and Das Neue Frankfurt,4⁸ is only indirect and can be most closely associated with the name Paul Klee. Geist, who was a teacher at a Volksschule (secondary school) in Altenburg and Meuselwitz in the twenties, saw his "main task in the reorganization of arts education at the level of the Volksschule."49 Although after the Nazi seizure of power he placed himself entirely in the service of the National Socialist ideology, his educational program around 1930 sounded progressive and democratic: “The sequence of classes at the primary school, which is determined by prejudices about talent, should be replaced by an organic school of cultural life for children with the goal of fulfilling the children in terms of their central willingness to perform functions and their general life needs."50 In the wake of the arts education movement around 1900 and inspired especially by an influential book of the early twenties, Der Genius im Kinde (The genius in the child) by Gustav F. Hartlaub,51 this included a conception of arts pedagogy that aimed at liberating the child's primal design energy. In contrast to the earlier reformists (Georg Kerschensteiner, for example), Geist's efforts toward a renewed education in the arts were not directed so much at a realistic or naturalistic "depiction true to form"52 as the supposedly highest level of the young draftsman's development but rather at encouraging the direct, more or less “primitive" expressive gestures of children and young people. He believed he had found an important ally in Paul Klee in his own rejection of the so-called naturalistic prejudice. "Klee's 'childlike' art"53to borrow the title of an essay by O. K. Werckmeister—cannot be examined more closely here. It seems adequate simply to note that the early Klee was certainly convinced that the “'primal origins of art' were to be found ’at home in the child's nursery' [and] that modern artists should take the’art' of children seriously as 'examples,'"54 but later he decisively rejected any connection between his own works and those of children, emphasizing instead the fundamental difference between the art of artists and the art of children.55 Geist, by contrast, always emphasized the “kinship" that, in his view, “existed between PaulKlee's works and the works of children."56 In this way he tried to use the supposedly childlike art of the Bauhaus artist, who was then already recognized in society and even well established, as a means to legitimize his own efforts in the pedagogy of the arts.

Very much as was the case with Schwerdtfeger later, Geist's arts education was based on a two-phase developmental scheme. In this scheme, design in the first, early-childhood phase was based on totality and gestalt: “Things seen, things dreamed, daily experiences, and exuberant emotion all become one." In the second phase “a refined feeling for line and form develops slowly. The .. . phase is characterized by an attitude of criticism and reflection. Rational thinking and judging become evident, and these things lead to design that is objective and true to form."57 In this phase, material studies and the study of elementary forms are the focus, but without compelling the students to commit to ideas of form typical of the Bauhaus and without recourse to the rational operations that were characteristic especially of Klee's course on form in Dessau. "The freely designed, expressive, imaginatively improvising element .. . strongly determines the impression, . .. [so] that methods of the design course at the Bauhaus were not employed primarily as a logical education in formal order but rather—obviously successfully—to liberate the child's natural, original creative energies."58

By contrast, Alfred Ehrhardt's Gestaltungslehre (Theory of design) of 1932 is very much indebted to the formal repertoire of several Bauhaus masters (especially Kandinsky) and certain exercises from the Bauhaus preliminary course (for example, Itten's rhythm studies, Moholy's equilibrium studies, Albers's matter and material exercises). In the early twenties Ehrhardt had studied painting and graphic arts at the arts and crafts school in Hamburg, was active thereafter as an art instructor at the Landerziehungsheim (Regional education center) in Dahlenburg near Hanover, studied in 1927–28at the Dessau Bauhaus, returned to the Landerziehungsheim in Dahlenburg, and in 1930 took over direction of one of the two preliminary classes at the Landeskunstschule (Regional art school) in Hamburg. It is highly problematic not only that his Gesta/tungslehre transplanted design principles and teaching methods from the Bauhaus to the field of aesthetic education from children and young students between five and twenty-one but also that his conception of arts education, which was based on Bauhaus theory in formal respects, hada strong background of social criticism⁵9 and subscribed to a view of humanity that Middeldorf stigmatized as “faschistoid"60 because of its biologistic implications. For example, Ehrhardt was concerned with educating a “truly modern human being" whom he described as “oriented toward the future," "full of energy," "intellectual," and at the same time “capable of lived experience" and contrasted to the“type so often encountered today: nervous, incapable oflived experience, self-satisfied, and easily exhausted," that is moribund, decadent, “at most a transitional type but certainly not the modern type."61 His credo of the “modern age," marked by electricity, air travel, neon advertisements, and accompanied by a "new feeling for life,"62 as well as his emphasis on the intellectual, placed Ehrhardt squarely in the opposite camp to the notorious anti-intellectualism of German arts pedagogy during the interwar period. Wolfgang Reiss has rightly noted that this turn should not only be seen as a “reaction to the new demands of society that no longer wished to close its mind to contemporary visual phenomena" but also as an "attempt to respond to the problem that arts pèdagogy had failed to solve: how to extend the sign

In Ehrhardt's view, the point was to encourage design with a wide variety of materials in order to train such virtues for work as "thoroughness, exactness, precision, honesty to oneself and to the materials"64 as well as “economy in time spent, energy exerted, and materials used" and "discipline in the working process . . . and responsibility in the overall design."65 These sorts of formulations may be reminiscent of Albers's "Werklicher Formunterricht" (Instruction in form through work) or even more so of Kerschensteiner's idea of artistic education as a “propaedeutics for a German work ethos"66and thus with a—rather conservative aspect of the ideology of the German Werkbund (of which Kerschensteiner had been a founding member). Without a doubt, it was one of the inconsistencies in the politics of education and culture in the Third Reich that Ehrhardt's Gestaltungslehre, despite its latently proto-National Socialist ideology of education, was denounced by the new powers-that-be as cultural bolshevism just a year after its publication because of its orientation around the Bauhaus in questions of form. As a former member of the Bauhaus, Ehrhardt himself was dismissed from the staff of the Landeskunstschule in Hamburg.

In general, the influence of Bauhaus pedagogy on arts education in the schools of the Weimar Republic should be considered minimal. If there was a partial adoption of the material practices of the Bauhaus in terms of the practical training that had been a cornerstone of the curriculum there since 1922, the design teachings of the Bauhaus, with their rational tendencies, remained relatively limited as an influence on arts education in the schools, given that the ideas dominant in the arts education of the twenties had focused on developing and later preserving as much as possible the genuine creative energies of the child. Overall, it is fair to speak of a “broken relationship″67 between arts educators at the time and the Bauhaus.

## The Third Reich

Whether—and if so, to what extent—the educational concepts of the Bauhaus may have survived its final destruction at the hands of the National Socialists in the Third Reich, either informally or in disguised form, is very difficult to determine, given the uncertain nature of the sources.68 Ekkehard Mai has spoken of "very different forms in which its effect continued . . . : not only literal emigration but also inner emigration, work continued inconspicuously and a wide variety of ways of securing its existence in schools."69As early as 1933 "all the teachers of the Bauhaus generation [had] lost their positions,"70 a fate that they shared with a number of former Bauhaus students who were already teaching at art schools in the Weimar Republic—for example, Herzger, Haffenrichter, Schwerdtfeger, Schleifer, Ehrhardt, and many others. Naturally, the result was such a serious bloodletting that it is no longer possible to speak of a continuation of the pedagogical principles of the Bauhaus within the aesthetic education of the Third Reich. This is especially so given that the arts andcrafts schools, which prior to 1933 had partially integrated ideas of reform pedagogy that were certainly reminiscent of the Bauhaus, had either been destroyed by the Nazis or converted into "master schools of German crafts" and compelled to follow the aesthetic program of völkisch (nationalist, popular) arts and crafts. Even so, according to Magdalena Droste, it would be easy to “offset the group of dismissed teachers . . . with a list of those who kept their positions or found other employment after 1933,"71 either because they were not considered suspicioús politically, because the new powers-that-be had no quarrel with their art, or because they became active National Socialists. Georg Muche, Joost Schmidt, and Walter Peterhans hadopportunities to introduce Bauhaus ideas into their courses at Kunst und Werk (Art and work), the name given to what had been the Reimann School in Berlin7² (one of the most important private art schools of the Weimar Republic), which was led by the architect Hugo Häring from 1935 on. /tten, who was forced to close his private school in Berlin in 1934, was the director for the Höhere Fachschule für textile Flächenkunst (Advanced technical school for the textile arts) from 1932 to 1938, which he sought to run according to Bauhaus principles.73 From 1939 on, as director of the master class for textile art at the Textilingenieurschule (School for textile engi neers)in Krefeld, Muche was able to preserve maxims of Bauhaus design education in a rather inconspicuous and quiet way even during the Nazi period.74 Whereas Itten ultimately managed to escape National Socialism by returning to his native Switzerland, andMuche used the freedom and protection offered by the textile industry in Krefeld to remain out of politics, there were others who quicklypaid allegiance to the new system. To name just two examples, Peter Röhl, a former Bauhaus constructivist, de Stijl artist, and antifascist, was able to keep his teaching position at the art school in Frankfurt as a member of the National Socialist Party and a realistic draftsman; and, despite having been dismissed by the Nazis fromhis professorship at the Pädagogische Akademie in Elbing in 1933, Hans Haffenrichter (of Der Weg) seems not to have had any problems quickly documenting his approval of the new system by producing a realistic bust of Hitler.75 The fact that he was able to take a new teaching position after World War Il, as director of the painting department at the Werkkunstschule (Applied art school) in Wiesbaden (1949–52) is cause for seeing his biography as rather typical for Germans in the twentieth century.

This already opens the door somewhat to the following section, which is devoted to the reception of Bauhaus pedagogy in Germany during the postwar period. First, however, we should recall an especially tragic facet of the continued effect of Bauhaus pedagogy in the National Socialist state: namely, in the drawing courses that Fried/ Dicker-Brandeis gave at the concentration camp in Theresienstadt (Terezín), Between 1916 and1919 Friedl Dicker attended Itten's courses at the latter's art school in Vienna and studied at the Bauhaus in Weimar from 1919 to 1923. She was deported as a Jew to Theresienstadt in 1942. As part of the illegal courses offered in the camps, she organized instruction in drawing and design for children and young people who were living in the ghetto isolated from their parents, until she was deported to Auschwitz (Oświęcim), where she died in the gas chambers. The primary goal of this instruction was "to help [the children] escape the desolation and the wretched horror of the ghetto .. . [and give them] solace and hope. Effort, talent, and skill alone were not enough. It was necessary to lead the children, teach them, show them how to express themselves, how and what to communicate."76 To that end, Friedl Dicker-Brandeis applied virtually the entire pedagogical program of her teacher of many years, Johannes Itten: from the elementary geometrical exercises in form to the rhythm exercises, nature studies and still lifes, and the color studies and collage pictorial analyses of Cranach, Titian, Vermeer, Cézanne, van Gogh, Matisse, and others."7 It is not simply the fact that the teaching methods and subjects of the early Bauhaus could be applied directly, even toward the end of the Third Reich, to arts education for children and young adults with some measure of success—some 4,000 children's drawings from the camp have survived—that justifies the historical significance of Friedl Dicker-Brandeis's course but also the exceptional circumstances under which it occurred. This is an unparalleled example of aesthetic education being sustained by the principle of hope in the face of the Holocaust.

## After 1945

## The Federal Republic of Germany

Following twelve years of National Socialist totalitarian rule and a near total breakdown, the Federal Republic of Germany, founded in 1949—by contrast to the German Democratic Republic founded that same year—accepted the Bauhaus with relative fluidity. There was a modest exhibition in Berlin in 1950, 22 Berliner Bauhäusler stellen aus (22 Berlin members of the Bauhaus exhibit), organized by Bauhaus student Hubert Hoffmann, and a large exhibition in Munich that same year, Die Maler am Bauhaus (The painters at the Bauhaus), which was organized by the art historian Ludwig Grote, who had been a friend and active supporter of the Bauhaus since the Dessau period. The site of the exhibition was, of course, no coincidence. A little more than a decade earlier, in 1937, the Bauhaus artists had been branded by the National Socialists as "degenerate" in an exhibition at an annex of the Haus der deutschen Kunst (House of German art), as it was then known; now, in the spaces of the Haus der Kunst, stripped of the adjective "German," it was possible to rehabilitate them in splendid style.

The image of the Bauhaus that began to form in the wake of these exhibitions in the fifties was, however, anything but comprehensive. In retrospect the Bauhaus seemed to be mythically transfigured as a radiant Olympus upon which several of the illustrious artists of the twenties— Kandinsky, Klee, Feininger, Schiemmer—gathered. This apotheosis of several outstanding painters and the fixation on art, which went hand in hand with a tendency to overlook architecture and formal and industrial design, frequently led to a lack of insight into the genuinely progressive ideas and goals of the Bauhaus, into the basic concept of an artistic and social synthesis, and into the educational program of the Bauhaus as a whole.

This image of the Bauhaus duringthe fifties—merely sketched in outline here, with its one-sided emphasis on the fine arts—is very closely related to the fact that variants of the Bauhaus preliminary course could be established, with relatively few problems, as part of the training at academies and appliedart schools in West Germany. Under circumstances that had completely changed with respect to the twenties, the social motives that underlay the work of, say, Moholy or Albers at the Bauhaus quickly disappearedfrom view so that most of these new preliminary courses remained fixated primarily on the goal of free development of the individual's creative possibilities and training in the fundamentals of formal aesthetics. Otherwise, the terminology and the ideas of the Bauhaus were unmistakably the point of reference for these developments. For example, Gustav Hassenpflug of the Landeskunstschule in Hamburg wrote in 1956: “This basic instruction has the task of introducing students to various materials and techniques, developing their sense for genuine forms and visual design, and awakening their creative energies. It was devised according to the model of the Bauhaus preliminary courses of Itten, Albers, and Moholy."78 Jupp Ernst of the Werkkunstschule in Wuppertal emphasized explicitly as early as 1950 an idea typical of the Bauhaus, though later questioned by critics, of a basic instruction that coveredallsubject areas comprehensively: “Form in the crafts and in the arts is developed from the material, the spirit, the purpose, and the method. For that reason, this aspect common to all branches of design should be taught in a common basic instruction that is required of all students."79

Thepositionopenwas that of director of the preliminary course and the list of qualifications for the desired candidate was defined unambiguously: “Preference will be given to students of Itten, Muche, Klee, Kandinsky, and Albers."80 It was, consequently, only natural that during the process of rebuildingWest Germany former Bauhaus members or those close to it functioned to spread the aesthetic positions andpedagogical principles of the Bauhaus. They included Max Burchartz in Essen, Hanns Hoffmann-Lederer in Darmstadt, Fritz Christoph Hüffner in Kassel (later Darmstadt), Gerhard Kadow in Krefeld, Kurt Kranz inHamburg, Hannes Neuner at theart academy in Stuttgart, Boris Kleint in Saarbrücken, and Maximilian Debus at the Technische Hochschule (Technical university) in Stuttgart (the lastnamed a student at Itten's private art school). All of this has yet to be examined historically, but stil one can assert cum grano salis that these derivatives of the classical Bauhaus preliminary course were by no means slavish copies of the historical models but in most cases attempts at modifications or even creative extensions of it. Thus, it is not possible to agree without reservation with Heimo Kuchling's assertion that these basic instructions were merely aestheticized diminutions of the preliminary courses at the Bauhaus that emphasized only the “aesthetic and playful."81 It cannot be denied, however, that all of these preliminary instructions gave primacy to form, grammar, and syntax while neglecting content and semantics (and also pragmatics).82 Certainly we should take very seriously the argument of such an intimate connoisseur of the problems involved as Fritz Seitz, who felt that the basic instruction could only fulfill its principal goal if it kept free of any claims on content.83 To the extent that that meant keeping free of "statements about existence,"84 as Seitz put it, and perhaps of political convictions or personal commentary on the reality of life, one can endorse this view without proviso. It should not be overlooked, however, that every form, no matter how abstract, and every material per se has unique semantic qualities or symbolic meanings, potentials for association, or expressive features, which are binding at least in some fashion, basedon cultural convention (and thus supraindividual), and thus can (or must) also be the subject of the basic instruction. If this fact is ignored—and that seems to have been the case not infrequently after the war—the charge of formalism that was regularly brought against these basic instructions was completely justified. Moreover, it must be recalled that making pure formal problems, that is, concern with form as form, a theme—though it may correspond to a position that Hölzel advocated in his dictum that art lies in the means—cannot in any way be considered representative of the Bauhaus, for Itten and Schlemmer (though students of Hölzel) and Klee and Kandinsky were always searching for the spiritual aspect inherent in the form or hidden behind it. It would be a gross oversimplification and impermissible abridgment to evoke the pedagogy at the Bauhaus in order to justify excluding content from these basic instructions.

We must return again to the first decade of the postwar period. In contrast to the practice common to most of the art academies, in which from the overall context of teachings at the Bauhaus only a single structural element was extracted—namely, the preliminary course——the Werkkunstschulen (applied arts schools) of the fifties strove to revitalize the Bauhaus idea in a way that was not limited to partial aspects. Already a few months before the end of the war, Georg Muche wrote to the British military government to call for the Bauhaus to be reopened ("reopening of the 'Bauhaus' in Germany, representing the best type of the modern Academy of art and architecture"),85 by which he certainly did not simply mean a traditional art school with a basic class organized around the Bauhaus model but rather a comprehensive effort at reconstruction. Be that as it may, the time was not yet ripe for it. Not until February 1949, three months before the founding of the Federal Republic of Germany, did eighteen former arts andcrafts schools(Kunstgewerbeschulen) that had been transformedinto master schoolsofGermancrafts(Meisterschulendes deutschen Handwerks) meet in Hanover to found the working community of applied arts schools (Werkkunstschulen). The name Werkkunstschule had been proposed by Hans Schwippert, but the term Werkkunst, in the view of Christian Grohn the result of a simple transposition of the syllables in Kunstwerk8⁶ (artwork), was not an invention of the period following World War Il or even Schwippert's own creation. The roots of the term extend back to the founding phase of the German Werkbund, and it occupied a prominent place in Walter Gropius's vocabulary even before the founding of the Bauhaus. It was an expression of the turn from fine arts to applied arts that was typical of the time, in which the emphasis on the practical, on the work process, and on so-called fidelity to the work was at least as important as the artistic aspect. According to Fritz G. Winter, the director of the Werkkunstschule in Krefeld from 1949 to 1971, Werkkunst was “a concept that saw 'work' not only in manual labor but also in the technical labor that was intended to blend together with the design principles of art. Integration was the goal."87 That was very much a Bauhaus idea. In addition, the name Werkkunstschule was meant to distinguish the schools from the academies; to avoid the term Kunstgewerbe, which had been discredited; and to signal the effort to do more than merely offer training in the crafts. Although we cannot here go into detail about these applied arts schools, which would include interesting special developments,88 we can concur with Christian Grohn's assessments that the basic idea of these schools and their efforts to unify art and handicrafts or art and technology was founded in the same intellectual tradition as the Bauhaus concept and that this also explains their structural parallels to the Bauhaus.89 These parallels included the introduction of a basic class or a so-called preliminary semester, practical training in workshops, and the creation of classes—with an eye to supporting industry—industrial design. Although this plan for a totalistic approach in the Bauhaus manner was implemented in different ways at the various applied arts schools (according to an overview of 1956, only four of the schoolshad departments for architectureandindustrial design),90it appears that it wasabove all the basic classes, whose fundamentalpurpose as a propaedeutics of seeing and making was undisputed at first, that seem to have been the cause for concern in the overall structure of the curriculum. Thus Gustav Hassenpflug, a student at the Dessau Bauhaus from 1950 to 1956and later director ofthe Landeskunstschulein Hamburg (which was, by the way, a gatheringplace for formerBauhaus students,includingEdgar Horstmann, Kurt Kranz, Otto Lindig, Gerhard Marcks, Else Mögelin, Fritz Schleifer, and Wolfgang Tümpel), complained that the "outstanding results of these basic classes . .. have a decisive disadvantage at almost all the schools: they do not find the proper, organic continuation in workshops and professional classes. They remain merely an interesting experiment without precipitating rich results for actual practice."91 This was a problem that, as the minutes of the council of masters demonstrate, was recognized and regularly discussed even during the early years of the Bauhaus.

The most thorough attempt to reconstruct the Bauhaus was, no doubt, the founding of the Hochschule für Gestaltung in Ulm (College for design; abbreviated HfG) in the fifties by former Bauhaus student Max Bill. It seems justified and worthwhile, then, to examine this new school in somewhat greater detail.92 In 1952, a year before teaching began, Bill wrote: "The College for Design in Ulm sees itself as the true continuation of the Bauhaus."93 This is not the place to discuss the prehistory of the school, which goes back to the activities of the adult education center (Volkshochschule) in Ulm in the late forties,94 nor the antifascist impetus behind the founding of the project, which was originally planned as the Geschwister-Scholl-Hochschule für politische Bildung (Scholl siblings college for political education). It must suffice to note that the idea of continuing the tradition of the Bauhaus was proposed as early as 1949 in the planning group around Inge Scholl, the sister of Hans and Sophie Scholl, who were executed in 1943 for their anti-Nazi resistance efforts.95 In 1950 Inge Scholl wrote to Theodor Heuss, a member of the German Werkbund since 1913, its president for a time, and in the meanwhile the first president of the Federal Republic of Germany: "the idea is to continue the tradition of the Bauhaus."96 In the discussions for planning a new college dedicated to democratic education, the legacy of the resistance movement combined with the idea of reviving the Bauhaus, which had been closedin 1933 under pressure from the National Socialists.

Even so, it was not until 1950, when the internationally renowned Swiss artist, designer, and architect Max Bill—a former student of the Dessau Bauhaus—joined the planning group, that it was decided to focus the school on design, abandoning the original idea of a political academy. During the preparatory planning meetings, which were now largely influenced by Bill, the idea of making the school a direct successor to the Bauhaus gradually crystallized. It went so far that Bill, now "rector designatus" (designated rector), even considered naming the school “Bauhaus Ulm." In 1951 he wrote a letter to Walter Gropius in the United States asking, “Could we, if we chose, call the College for Design in Ulm the Bauhaus'?"97 The founder of the Bauhaus responded positively, although, with an allusion to Moholy-Nagy's experiences with the New Bauhaus in Chicago, not without skepticism: "I would personally not be against your using the name Bauhaus Ulm. . . . I leave it to your decision because you have to carry the brunt now, and I want to be helpful."98 It seems to have been primarily the graphic artist Otl Aicher, a childhood friend of the Scholl family who had been involved in the construction of the college in Ulm from the beginning and who married Inge Scholl in 1952, who prevented the school from being called the “Ulm Bauhaus," because, unlike Bill, he was by his own admission not concerned with "making a second Bauhaus" but with deliberately setting the new school apart.99

In the end, Bill, too, recognized that some two decades later and under completely different political, economic, cultural, and social circumstances a direct connection to the Bauhaus was hardly possible. Even so, as a graduate of the Bauhaus he was not prepared to stray too far from his ideas of a new Bauhaus. In the end, however, there were no painting or sculpture classes and no goldsmith workshop, as Bill originally planned, because of forceful resistance from several members of the planning committee. In particular, it was Walter Zeischegg, whom Bill had called to Ulm in 1951, and Otl Aicher, both of whom had abandoned the sculpture class at their respective art academies, who were unwilling to tolerate even the seed for the creation of a college of the fine arts. Only graphic arts, product design, and architecture—that is, the classical fields of the so-called applied arts—should be taught at the new school. Despite all the disputes, Max Bill initially remained the dominant figure and the Bauhaus remained the clear point of reference. Even without the use of the name "Bauhaus," the direct reference to the historical model was unmistakable, as the official subtitle of the Bauhaus in Dessau was Hochschule für Gestaltung.

On 3 August 1953—the date also marks the beginning of Max Bill's rectorship—teaching began in the classrooms of the Volkshochschule in Ulm with the first basic course, led by Wa/ter Peterhans, In 1927 Peterhans had taken over as director of the newly instituted photography class at the Bauhaus, which was made a part of the advertising department. In 1938 Mies van der Rohe invited him to teach the visual training course at the Illinois Institute of Technology in Chicago. Now, at Max Bill's recommendation, he came to the HfG as part of an exchange program with American professors to spend three months as a quest lecturer and teach the visual basics of design. Peterhans was succeeded in December 1953 and January 1954 by Josef Albers (see chapter 7), who had emigrated to the United States shortly after the Nazi seizure of power in 1933, and who was a guest lecturer at the HfG again from May to early August 1955. Helene Nonné-Schmidt, a student of the Bauhaus and the wife of Bauhaus master Joost Schmidt (see chapter 11), taught several courses in Ulm between 1954 and 1957, introducing above all Paul Klee's "theory of pictorial form" and her husband's "elementary design theory" to the young HfG. Last but not least, Johannes Itten (see chapter 5) gave a one-week course on color theory at the HfG in April 1955.100 We mention all of this here in order to document how strong the connections to the Bauhaus were, even in terms of personnel, during the initial phase of the school in Ulm under Max Bill's direction. One other detail can help fill out this picture: Bill had originally proposed Bauhaus member Wilhelm Wagenfeld as the leader of the product design department, but ultimately Walter Zeischegg was appointed in his place andwould remainassociatedwith the school until its closing in 1968.101 It is also worth noting that Mies van der Rohe visited the school in

1953 and that the constructivist painter and designer Friedrich Vordemberge-Gildewart—who, while not a member of the Bauhaus per se, at least shared an affinity with it as a member of the de Stijl group—belonged to the teaching staff of the school in Ulm from 1955 to 1962. All of this is good reason for assuming that the new college was a legitimate successor to the Bauhaus. The extent to which this assumption is true, or in need of correction, will be discussed briefly in what follows.

In his function as designated rector of the HfG, Max Bill wrote in 1952:

In myview, the Bauhaus of Walter Gropius achieved the ideal situation for that time, in which leading artists were prepared to work together to solve practical tasks. So a highly qualified teaching staff gave priority to daily problems without questioning the value of true artistic achievement. This connection between true progressive art and true progressive problem solving in the areas of architecture, the design of utilitarian goods, typography, and so on, was the crucial aspect of the Bauhaus. .. . A further development of the Bauhaus idea can only follow this tried-and-testedbasis. Iam convinced, now as ever, that the fine arts should be given primacy in the field of design. .. . We view art as the highest level of expression in life and we seek to make life an artwork. We want . . . to fight against the ugly, with the help of the beautiful, the good, the practical. As a successor to van de Velde's art institution in Weimar, the Bauhaus had the same goal.102

Two aspects of this quotation deserve to be emphasized in particular: first, the affirmation of accepting the legacy of the Bauhaus and continuing it; second, the demand to make the fine arts the starting point and reference point of all design.

If one asks which historical phase of the Bauhaus Max Bill was referring to, it is clear that he meant the period in which the expressionistic and utopian founding pathos and the romanticism of the crafts of the early years were giving way to a more pragmatic tendency—a tendency that found its most succinct (and classical) formulation in Gropius's 1923 slogan "Art and technology, a new unity!"103 But, rigorous functionalists would ask, what is the sense of a unity of art and technology? What reason could there be for constructing and designing an object produced using technology and intended for daily use—produced, that is, to serve a particular purpose—-by any other principles than those determined by that purpose? Would the participation of art in such cases even be appropriate? Bill was aware of the fact that function must be in the foreground of the design process. Even so, in his view, a piece of furniture (an example he often used) is not only determined by its function but

it must have a form, for despite all the comfort, despite all concern for technical and economic possibilities, the effect of this chair is that of a sculptural object in space, that of a form, together with its user, together with other things like it.104

That is to say, the external manifestation of the obiect was, for Bill, its eminent meaning. He was convinced“that form is always judged as more beautiful or less perfect than something else, and that ultimately, both for form and for art, perfect beauty is the measure. So ultimately form means beauty."105 Because “beauty" knows no objective criterion, however, but rather depends to a large degree onsubjective judgments and personal preferences, the designer's intuition, experience, and specific ideas for the design are the determinant factors. The form of an object is, according to Bill, decisively determined by the personality of its creator. This position is highly reminiscent of Gropius's remark that "we must consciously see to make the personal factor more objective, but every work of art carries the signature of its creator. From the multiplicity of equally economical situations—for there is not just one in each case— the creative individual selects the one that suits.his personal feelings and taste."106As an artist and passionate proponent of the primacy of art, Bill would surely have largely agreed with this view. Hannes Meyer's version of the Bauhaus, on the other hand, whichannulled the predominance of the fine arts, can hardly have been considered Bill's model. (Be that as it may, the school building for the HfG on the Oberer Kuhberg that Bill constructed between 1953 and 1955 clearly echoes the school for the Allgemeiner Deutscher Gewerkschaftsbund [General Association of German Unions] that Hannes Meyer built in Bernau near Berlin between 1928 and1930.)

Max Bill's artistic orientation corresponded with a didactic concept—to the extent it can be considered a concept—that consisted of teaching and learning in individual freedom without a fixed teaching program. Only the introductory, one-year basic instruction, which—following the Bauhaus model—was required of all beginning students, revealed a somewhat clear curriculum, even if Eva Seckendorff emphasizes that the “practice of the first-year basic instruction was defined less by a clear program than by the individuals whom Bill was able to attract as guest lecturers for the course"107and that meant overwhelmingly, as noted above, former teachers and students from the Bauhaus. (The "transitional teaching plan" for the basic instruction in the academic year 1953-54 lists as lecturers—in addition to such former members of the Bauhaus as Albers, Bill, Nonné-Schmidt, and Peterhans—Otl Aicher, Max Bense, Hans Curjel, Adolf Portmann, and Walter Zeischegg.) As at the Bauhaus, there was no direct attempt to prepare the students for their later work in the specific departments. (The four departments were: (1) Product Form,later Product Design: (2) Visual Design, later Visual Communication: (3) Architecture, later Building, then Industrial Building; (4) Information.) Rather, there was, as Bill described it, a general "aesthetic training" that he considered indispensable as an introduction. The goal was "to develop the individual abilities and activities of the students in an atmosphere of free action. The students should learn step by step through multifaceted elementary exercises, cast off preexisting opinions, and work independently and impartially."108

In order to tighten up the fundamental instruction and bring it to a common denominator, on 1 January 1955 Bill assigned the Argentine painter and graphic artist Tomás Maldonado, whom Bill had invited to Ulm, to take over the direction of the basic instruction. Maldonado, who that same year published a book on Bill, did not delay in freeing the basic instruction of its Bauhaus touch (which for Maldonado meant "artistic") and focusing it more on the sciences. Although at the Dessau Bauhaus—especially under Hannes Meyer—scientific disciplines had a definite place in the canon of subjects, these fields were not initially part of the basic instruction. Even the departments that followed Bill's design concept did not see a significant role for the sciences in their work. The creative signature remained the main criterion in design, which stood above any possible demands of the production engineers or manufacturers for whom the designer was working. The former were supposed to concern themselves only with properly executing the designer's plans. Bill, who behaved like a highhanded “hegemon"109 and barely tolerated divergent opinions, rejected the notion that the engineers or manufacturers should cooperate or have a say in the actual design. In contrast to Bill's “arbitrary design based on intuition and artistic genius"110 Maldonado, Zeischegg, and Hans Gugelot (who was active at the HfG from mid-1954 on) called for science and technology to be introduced into the teaching program in order make it possible for the design to work on a secure, objective basis. Similarly, some thirty years earlier, Gropius and then—even more emphatically—Hannes Meyer had focused their efforts on a rationally conceived theory of design that would take into account the social, economic, and technological conditions of an advanced industrial society. Bill, who reacted negatively to this and later spoke of "technological degeneracy,"111 stepped down as rector on 31 March 1956.

An important impetus behind the increasing role of science at the HfG was the exemplary cooperation, begun in 1955, between the director of the Product Form department, Hans Gugelot,112 who was free of the baggage of artistic training, and Braun, a manufacturer of electrical products113 (design of building sets and systems), which would later be continued in other, pioneering cooperative projects with industry, such as Otl Aicher's jobs for Lufthansa. This development necessarily had consequences for the basic instruction as well, into which Maldonado was introducing more and more mathematical operations and scientific subjects (for example, mathematical logic, theory of symmetry, topology, combination theory, physics, and chemistry). That is to say, he conceived "a basic instruction with a high degree of quantifiable design steps . . . with the goal of creating a purely technological design."114

After Bill resigned as rector in 1956 and the directorship of the school was taken over by a board consisting of Aicher, Gugelot, Maldonado, and Vordemberge-Gildewart, the effort to disassociate from the Bauhaus tradition became increasingly evident. Even so, it is not difficult to see that, especially in the Building Department (led by Herbert Ohl), there were elements of a tradition that could be traced back to Gropius's and Hannes Meyer's experiments in the area of so-called industrial architecture (e.g., the Dessau-Torten housing colonies, 1926–28 and 1928–30). As a technocratic and positivist ideology andincreased emphasis on mathematics in the design gained the upper hand, there was a fundamental reform of the teaching program—both in terms of structure and content—which led to the rigorous removal of the shellac of the Bauhaus teachings, now seen as obsolete. In 1958 Maldonado's general attack on the Bauhaus and its pedagogy read as follows: "The educationalphilosophy of the schools for product design is now completely outmoded. It is identified with a tradition that is primarily artistic: the Bauhaus tradition. Although the geometric teapots ‘Bauhaus 1924' are consideredmuseum objects today, some would oblige us to view the pedagogical ideas of 'Bauhaus 1924' as extremely relevant." And further: “The viewpoints that determined the ideology of the Bauhaus are now, a quarter century after it closed, difficult to translate into the language of today's problems. More than that: we must reject several of these viewpoints . . . with absolute conviction but also with absolute objectivity."115

For Maldonado, the teachings of the Bauhaus were not a cultural fetish that should be blindly worshipped but a historical phenomenon that should be critically examined to see whether they were still relevant to present-day design education. The result of this examination was first of all a decided redefinition of the functions of the basic instruction: "It introduces the students to the work in the departments, especially to the methods on which the division of labor is based. It familiarizes the students with the most important questions of our technical civilization and imparts the horizons of the concrete design tasks. It trains the cooperation of various disciplines and thus prepares the students to work as a team."116 In contrast to the pedagogical goals of the basic instruction at the Bauhaus and Max Bill's ideas (which were derived from them), these definitions of its function were distinguished by an instrumentalistic terminology that may have been viewed as a sign of progressiveness by the new leadership at the HfG, which was increasingly made up of advocates of mathematics, design methodology, and communications theory (like Horst Rittel). The wide-ranging teaching offerings at the Bauhaus of the initial phase, which were intended to produce creative generalists, were now replaced by the education of specialists who were efficient, capable of working on a team, and open to the dictates of scientific and technological rationality. In 1923 Gropius had postulated a unity of art and technology—a thesis to which Georg Muche raised pointed objections in 1926 (see chapter 3); now the HfG propagated a unity of science and technology, celebrating "technology as technology."117 Part of this was a methodical procedure that tried, as much as possible, to exclude any random elements or individual design preferences: "The function of a method consists . . . in regulating the unbridled imagination, directing it down particular paths."118 Industrial design was in no way to be understood as a form of applied art or craft activity using technology, as had typically been the case at the Bauhaus. Tomás Maldonado remarked on this:

The opening manifesto of the Bauhaus . . . proclaimed. . . the unity of art and crafts. . . . A few years later, however, the Bauhaus hadmade its own adjustments to the rationalistic current. . . . Not only the craft products but also industrial products represented from then on a problem of form, a problem of form that was supposed to be solved artistically. . . . For the present—the year was 1958—Maldonado continued: “A new educational philosophy is coming. Scientific operationalism serves as its basis. ... At the moment there is only one school that is dedicated to educating this new kind of product designer: the Hochschule für Gestaltung in Ulm. It is the first example of this new educational philosophy. I am convinced that sooner or later other schools . . . will set out on the same path."120

The distance between the Bauhaus and the HfG in the post-Bill phase was clear not only in the pedagogy and the increasing"methodolatry"121 but also in the spectrum of products designed there, which were all distinguished by an ascetic, neutral design that avoided all formal extravagances: the so-called Ulm style. These included tape recorders, light meters, travel buses, hand mixers, gasoline pumps, dental instruments, forklifts, street lamps, and other technological and industrial objects whose design required significant competence in engineering and thus differed significantly from the Bauhaus objects, which were primarily for home use.

With the increasing emphasis on the sciences at the HfG in the late fifties and early sixties, the school, whose history could be described as a series of crises, found itself subjected to a real test. The figures who had been called to Ulm to provide a scientific basis—such as the mathematician Horst Rittel or the sociologist Hanno Kesting—threatened more and more to become too powerful and to dominate or even put aside the work of the designers. What had been intended as a—quite reasonable—complementary relationship between design and science led to polarization of factions. In the academic year 1961–62 the students of the "first year of study" (the name "basic instruction" had been eliminated along with the institution itself) protested against the excess of "book learning" and argued that they did not wish "to become sociologists, physiologists,psychologists, and certainly not theoreticians of structure, statisticians, analysts, or mathematicians but rather designers!"122 As Horst Rittel's most fervent opponent, Otl Aicher used the opportunity to combat the preponderance of theory at the HfG and to find a way back to the genuine goals of the school, namely, to educate designers in the areas of industrialproduction, visual and verbal communication, and architecture. In doing so, he expressly contradicted the notion, which had in the meanwhile become generally acceptedin Ulm, that design was science. He left room for the relevant sciences, but only to serve the subsidiary function of supporting the education of designers.

The step taken at the beginning of the academic year 1961–62 to eliminate the general basic instruction, which until then students of all departments had participated in together, in favor of courses on fundamentals that were specific to a department was one that Aicher could not, or did not wish to, recant. The elimination of the basic instruction was a final logical step in the direction in which Maldonado had set out during the fifties and at the same time was the definitive good-bye to the legacy of the Bauhaus at the Ulm school.

The rejection of the general basic instruction was also intended to solve a structural problem that had also existed at the Bauhaus: the continuity of the preliminary course with the practical training in the workshops (in the case of the Bauhaus) or the basic instruction with the practical design work in the departments (in the case of the HfG). Now students in Ulm had “to decide at the beginning of their studies which department to enter, and there they received a specialized basic training that was designed to meet the demands of theparticular speciality."123 This did not, of course, mean that one ceased on principle to teach or learn elementary rules of design but rather, first, that these basics were taught in a way specific to a department and, second, that, rather than taking place in advance of specialized study (and thus being ultimately independent of it) they were intended to accompany the entire course of study, with continually decreasing emphasis. Fritz Seitz depicted clearly in a diagram this model, which Maldonado had introduced in the field of product design, related it to the preliminary course at the early Bauhaus and the basic class of the art schools of the postwar period, and supplemented it with an original proposal to solve the problem discussed here, the goal of which was that “the work on the fundamentals" would “from the sidelines constantly support and augment the activity in the departments and specialized courses."124

The reception of Bauhaus pedagogy in the arts education and arts instruction in the schools of the Federal Republic of Germany deserves a more detailedinvestigation. Several references to the problems involved have already been made in the presentations of the individual pedagogical conceptions at the Bauhaus (chapters 5 through 11), so they need not be repeated here. Among the best-known authors whose didactic efforts benefited from the stimulus of Bauhaus pedagogy are Kurt Schwerdtfeger, Ernst Röttger, Reinhard Pfennig, and Gunter Otto, who can all be seen, more or less, as proponents of the "formal art instruction" that dominated the sixties, which was aimed against the irrationalism of so-called artistic art education (Musische Kunsterziehung), although Schwerdtfeger took an unusual middle position between these two conceptions of art pedagogy (see chapter 5). When the positions of these authors are subjected to a comparative analysis, one is left to conclude that Gunter Otto's rather distanced approach to particular teaching approaches at the Bauhaus contrasted with Schwerdtfeger's rather uncritical borrowing of elemęnts from the design courses of Itten, Moholy, Kandinsky, and others, with Röttger's reinterpretation of Bauhaus pedagogical efforts as mere "play with artistic means," and with Pfennig's stubborn clinging to the concept of “pictorial thinking" that was inspired by Klee.

Kurt Staguhn can certainly be criticized for heeding the call of certain Bauhaus teachers to examine "the theory of perception for its significance to artistic laws" and concluding “that artistic laws are identical with the categories of perception."125Or: “The teaching of artistic laws and their integration into pictorial orders must therefore become a central part of art education. For this reason it is necessary to introduce into art education ... the pictorial theory that the Bauhaus elaborated."126 Staguhn committed the same error that Hölzel, Itten, and Kandinsky did before him by believing that “artistic laws" are universally valid "for all art" and thus could be justified by "categories of perception that always remain the same."127 He overlooked that perception processes are to a large degree determined by cultural, historical, social, and other intervening factors, and thus it is hardly possible to equate "laws of perception" and supposedly intercultural and timelessly valid "pictorial laws." (We should note in this context that the influence of the Bauhaus and the theories of design developed both within its circle and in its wake decreased to the same degree that art itself moved away from the claims to validity by laws of perception that were developed above all in Gestalt psychology; we need only recall the many varieties of antiart, from Dada to action art.)

## The German Democratic Republic

These necessarily fragmentary notes on the reception of Bauhaus pedagogy will conclude with a few spotlights on the former German Democratic Republic. The former Bauhaus student Hubert Hoffmann was one of the first to find that a continuation of the pedagogical traditions of the Bauhaus in the Soviet-occupied zone after 1945 was not possible, despite hopes that were initially justified. Hoffmann had been given the task by the Social Democratic mayor of Dessau, Fritz

Hesse, to revive the institute. The timid beginnings of the school's revitalization—which under Hoffmann contained strongly defined biological and ecological components—was soon nipped in the bud when the SED (Sozialistische Einheitspartei Deutschlands, the German socialist unity party) took over the government. Once again, as under the Weimar Republic and Hitler's fascism, the Bauhaus was considered politically undesirable. This time it collided with the dogmas of Socialist Realism, and Hoffmann himself was labeled reactionary and forced underground.130

Moreover, even at the Hochschule für Baukunst und Bildende Künste in Weimar131 (College for architecture and fine arts)—seemingly another historically predestined site like Dessau—there was an effort to create a new Bauhaus, but it was also doomed to failure. After former Bauhaus member Ernst Neufert had refused the assignment of reorganizing the school along the lines of the early Bauhaus, the architect Hermann Henselmann accepted the task in the summer of 1945. Although he did not know the Bauhaus from personal experience, he had been strongly influenced by the ideas of Gropius, as demonstrated by his 1946 opening speech, “Idee und Aufbau der Staatlichen Hochschule für Baukunst undBildende Künste" (Idea and structure of the State college for architecture andthe fine arts). The fact that he appointed former membersof theBauhaus like Hanns Hoffmann-Lederer andPeter Keler to lead the two preliminary classes, Rudolf Ortner as teacher for the practical study, andGustav Hassenpflug for thechair in urban studies also demonstrated his commitment to theBauhaus. He tried to do justice to the idea of a unified art school by establishing a lively exchange between the architecture and fine arts departments, and the same approach was intended between the fine and applied arts. In 1947 Gustav Hassenpflug was still able to publish in the journal Bildende Kunst (Fine arts), which was published in the Soviet sector of Berlin, a call for “the two essentially newprinciples of the Bauhaus—the pedagogical principle of the education of creative people and the practical principle of forming people who will mediate between art and industry, between intuition and precise research . . . to be continued in the new Germany."132 After the founding of the German Democratic Republic in May 1949, however, the mood clearly turned against the possibility of carrying on Bauhaus conceptions. The lightdark studies based on Johannes Itten's course, the material studies, and the exercises in structure and form in the preliminary classes of Keler and Hoffmann-Lederer were soon subjectedto criticism and accused of formalism and lack of relevance for actual practice, especially for the tasks of future architects. Although the Bauhaus had only recently been considered a victim of fascism and thus honored, it was now subject to the turbulence of a complete change in the ideological weather. For example, a newspaper article on the new direction of the Hochschule in Weimar read: "From the former pseudo-scientific, idealistic Bauhaus, an arts and crafts school of the reformist variety, the logical consequence of a precisely functioning college has been derived, with the task of establishing teaching and research on the basis of the exact sciences with the goal of the progressive development of our society today. All holdovers of feudal-bourgeois representation, reformist tendencies, and formal experiments will be cast aside."133 A typical example for the political blinders in the GDR that continued to affect the assessment of the historical value of the Bauhaus into the late fifties can be found in an essay by Herbert Letsch, “Zur Frage des Klassencharacters der ästhetischen Konzeption des Bauhauskonstruktivismus" (On the question of the class character of Bauhaus constructivism's aesthetic conception) The author presents the thesis that the Bauhaus, or Bauhaus constructivism, is “a (not immediately obvious) manifestation of imperialist ideology," or more precisely, the aesthetic doctrine of the constructivists was “in its objective content nothing other than the theoretical sanction of the ultimately economically determined decline of bourgeois art in the imperialist developmental stage of capitalism."134 If one takes a standpoint that class implies party allegiance, then of course it is not difficult to identify bourgeois elements at the Bauhaus. Representing the Bauhaus and Bauhaus constructivism in general, which Letsch fundamentally misinterprets as pure l’art pour l’art, as “specific manifestations of imperialist ideology“135 is, moreover, an assessment that is so inaccurate it can only be interpreted against the background of ideological blindness within a political system struggling to assert itself. There can be no doubt that the Bauhaus ultimately failed in its aesthetic and socially progressive goals precisely because of the conditions of an imperialistic and capitalistic social system and thus may be considered a victim, but certainly not a “manifestation," of imperialism.

However, even though circumstances in the GDR in the fifties were extremely unfavorable to the prospect of continuing the Bauhaus maxims of arts education, Günther Regel has shown convincingly with reference toHerbert Wegehaupt that it was nonetheless possible.136 Wegehaupt (1905–59), who—following evening courses at the art academy in Breslau—had studied in 1926–27 at the DessauBauhaus, among other places, was appointed professor for theory and practice of artistic design at the Institut für Kunsterziehung (Institute for arts education) at the University of Greifswald. There, quietly and almost unnoticed by the officials, he was able to assure the survival of certain Bauhaus conceptions. In order not to raise suspicion, he “did not speak much of the Bauhaus or of Klee . . . , but he had so internalized the perspective and attitude of the Bauhaus, as well as its insights into what about art makes it art, that he was not obliged to take on any particular procedures or methods from the teachings of the Bauhaus. He did not think much of preliminary courses or special courses to teach basics of form and design—of basic classes in any form. . . . Even so, he was concerned with the students learning and gradually perfecting the language of forms and colors, and properly dosed grammar was a part of that but not something to be saved up, not isolated from the genuine activity of art." This is well demonstrated by “[n]umerous artistic explorations, systematic series of experimęnts and studies, that not infrequently resulted in small treasures with artistic character"137 and revealed the continuation of the Bauhaus tradition.

A more objective assessment of the Bauhaus than the one Letsch expressed in his essay was offered by the Soviet author Leonid N. Pazitnov in his book Das schöpferische Erbe des Bauhauses, 1919–1933 (The creative legacy of the Bauhaus, 1919–1933), whichwas published in German translation in East Berlin in 1963. In it, the author credited the Bauhaus with having developed “a truly maximal program for its time,"138 but he also expressed the view that the implementation of this program would have required a radical transformation of the existing social relationships and a total break with the principles of bourgeois aesthetic consciousness. He characterized the “creative legacy" of the Bauhaus and the progressive elements of its pedagogy as follows:

The system worked out at the Bauhaus during the twenties for educating artistic designers of industrial forms has achieved tremendous relevance. The fact that its fundamental principles went far beyond the framework of bourgeois society and its own system of artistic education does not, of course, mean that we must now, forty years later, mechanically adopt it as a model to imitate for our own circumstances. A critical approach to the Bauhaus experience is necessary. It is equally necessary, however, to take everything from this experiment that could not be realized under the circumstances of bourgeois society but that can be realized in the actual practice of building communism. This is as true of the educational system of the Bauhaus as it is for the principles of its aesthetics and practice.139

It is clear that this work by a Soviet author marked the beginning of a more intensive reception and more positive evaluation in the German Democratic Republic of the Bauhaus, its aesthetic positions, and its pedagogical conceptions. In addition to a qrowing number of journal articles, two overall depictions of the Bauhaus appeared in the GDR in the seventies that not only helped to close gaps in the information about the Bauhaus but also contributed new perspectives on the problem. In his 1965 book Das Bauhaus, 1919- 1933 Lothar Lang came to conclusions that large-Iy agreed with those of Pazitnov. With a touch of pathos, he spoke of the "sublime goals of the Bauhaus" that were dashed by the reality of capitalism, attested to the Bauhaus's productive transformation of "abstract constructivism (suprematism and de Stijl) into concrete functionalism," and concluded that the Bauhaus has "a secure place in the history of modern art" and is of "epochal significance."140 In the introduction to his 1966 book on the Bauhaus, Diether Schmidt offered a fundamental revision of earlier positions when he made the criticism that in the discussions on art in the GDR during the fifties the Bauhaus tradition was all too one-sidedly dismissed as "formalistic" and“cosmopolitan."141

Despite the enormous amount of research on the Bauhaus conducted in socialist Germany since the seventies—by the time the wall fell in 1989 there had been five large International Bauhaùs Colloquia at the Hochschule für Architektur und Bauwesen in Weimar alone—one-sided emphases and biased assessments remained the order of the day. Hannes Meyer, who was long treated with condescension in the old Federal Republic of Germany, was given prominent attention for decades in the Bauhaus literature in the GDR, right up to Klaus-Jürgen Winkler's Meyer monograph of 1989. No other Bauhaus master was given as much individual attention in the relevant specialist literature of the GDR as this Marxist architect who sought to put into practice the political and social content of the Bauhaus founding utopia. The Bauhaus historiography of the GDR also gave preference to Gropius, Moholy-Nagy, and others whose dedication to social causes was unmistakable. This decided turn toward the Bauhaus was legitimized by reference to a statement of principles issued by the SED:

![](images/670730f4baa91c2f67fc4807e150462630ed92629c90d7ea7524772a95de9d0e.jpg)  
258 Herbert Wegehaupt, Study series on the artistic means of point, line, and plane, 1950s

[T]he socialist culture of the German Democratic Republic is committed to the rich legacy that was created throughout the history of the German people. “Everything great, noble, humanistic, and revolutionary is held in honor and encouraged in the German Democratic Republic by placing it in relation to the tasks of the present!" We take it for granted that the creative achievements and progressive ideas of the Bauhaus belong to this legacy that is worth preserving and building upon.142

This rhetoric, which was meant to justify historically “real existing socialism," casts a light on the way that history was made to serve as a political instrument in the former GDR.

Ignoring the detours and ideological acrobatics that were necessary to arrive at an at least somewhat positive assessment of the Bauhaus in the GDR, we must acknowledge that the reception of the Bauhaus in socialist Germany had from the beginning an approach based on social theory—even if it was completely one-sided. One would, of course, be right to question whether the corset of the Marxist-Leninist theoretical structure and the materialist philosophy of the history and theory of art always profited an adequate reception of the Bauhaus. However, the GDR was never subject to the way of thinking that long dominated in the old Federal Republic, namely, that art and design could be analyzed from the perspective of purely inherent aspects as part of a history of form, style, and evolution, as if aesthetic objectivationsandtheir socialdeterminants could be disassociated at will.

Naturally, the changes in perspective on the Bauhaus in the GDR from the sixties on had consequences for the reception of Bauhaus conceptions of design pedagogy. At a time when the HfG in Ulm had long since divorced itself from the idea of artistic design andsteppedout of thepedagogical shadow of the Bauhaus, the socialist part of Germany was gradually beginning to discuss the question of whether and to what extent Bauhaus pedagogy could be a model or even set the quidelines for the education of architects and designers. Principles of Bauhaus pedagogy-—especially the basic course—were a fixed institution in the education of architects and industrial designers in the former GDR.143Beginning in the mideighties the building, andeven more so the genius loci, of the Dessau Bauhaus was used for international design seminars. And even the pedagogical maxims of Johannes Itten, which were sharply attacked in the West German literature on arts pedagogy in the seventies for their tendencies to individualism and lack of social connection, found an attentive reception andgracious approval in socialist Germany.144 Whereas the arts pedagogy of West Germany and West Berlin began to move away from the tradition of the Bauhaus after 1968 under the influence of the student movement, the German Democratic Republic could now claim—clearly belatedly but with no less commitment—to have realized “the fundamentals and environment for a design education" for which “the Bauhaus [had to] struggle during its existence."145
