The splendor of the stair, the marble columns, the gilt detail, and the painted ceiling area are visual overtures for the experience of the opera to come. Architects and designers of the nineteenth century picked their styles from the past and—apart from the adjustments necessary to accommodate new functions—stayed largely true to the original model. By the end of the century, new social and economic structures created a climate, and a confidence, in which assiduous respect for the past seemed less deserved. The development of new materials and techniques at a greater and faster rate than ever before encouraged an increasingly relaxed and eclectic approach to the fusion of the familiar and the unfamiliar. This encouraged creative borrowing that shaped the extraordinary build-ings of the latter part of the century as in pursuit of an appropriate style—for skyscrapers in particular—architects and designers experimented with collaging elements of different styles within single buildings. If they were skillful, the hybridized Eclecticism, had coherence—and panache. The post-Revolutionary French government wanted physical expressions of national unity and commissioned Jean-Nicolas-Louis Durand (1760–1834) to evolve standards for public buildings. In 1800 he published Recueil et Parallèle des Edifices de Tout Genre Anciens et Modernes (Collection and Comparison of All Kinds of Ancient and Modern Buildings) and established rules for every building type, whether assembled from masonry or cast-iron components. Plans were simple diagrams, sections and elevations followed the logic of construction. Provincial towns benefited from well-resolved buildings and the formulae gave Paris Les Halles (1853–70) the iron and glass pavilions of its wholesale food market. When these were demolished in 1971, their destruction—and the crass shopping mall that replaced them—made a convincing argument for the preservation of utilitarian buildings that, with age, acquire romantic glamour. Eugène-Emmanuel Viollet-le-Duc (1814–79), the great restorer of French national monuments, excited controversy when he used cast-iron components in traditional structures, most notoriously in restoring the flèche of the cathedral of Notre Dame in Paris in 1859. In drawings, for a concert hall (14.2) and a theoretical interpretation of Gothic structure (14.3) he suggested how cast-iron structural strength could be married to traditional interior architecture. It was an indisputably eclectic approach. ![](images/83e3b9007c868a02e5e8ccb01aa815682a35fff6d3788280e34aff4d6b0a077d.jpg) Eugène-Emmanuel Viollet-le-Duc, design for a concert hall, Entretiens sur l’Architecture (Discourses on Architecture), 1863–72. 14.2 A speculation about the structural strength of cast iron in the cause of improving sight lines. ![](images/26c33a380989db38c160f44adf190cc346f6fa9ee77b3a2d39df3b2a86bb820f.jpg) Eugène-Emmanuel Viollet-le-Duc, sketch of cast-iron structural members, Discourses on Architecture, 1863–72. The skills to master this hybridization of traditional form and modern function were learnt at the Ecole des Beaux-Arts in Paris. The Ecole taught rational planning, with a preference for symmetrical composition, and a compendium of primarily classical detailing. Beaux-Arts teachers were also the designers of the great buildings of late nineteenth-century France that defined the school’s philosophy. Pierre-François-Henri Labrouste (1801–75) created the unprecedented libraries of S. Geneviève (1844–50) and the Bibliothèque Nationale (1859–67). The interiors of both exploited the potential of cast iron, as a structural and a decorative material. The Geneviève demonstrates Beaux-Arts clarity of planning (14.6). In a large entrance hall, with rooms for book stacks and special collections on either side, square Neoclassical piers support iron segmental arches that carry a plain, flat ceiling. The entrance hall passes through the building to a double stair leading to the reading room, lined with bookshelves. A row of thin iron columns, on the central axis of the building, supports decoratively perforated iron arches that carry two plastered barrel vaults. Labrouste recognized that iron had potential for more delicate decoration than carved stone. ![](images/2c592dd3453e9ce14b3262b23f45f2a45c164d8b38d8f2e6d40d95726fffc721.jpg) 14.6 Pierre-François-Henri Labrouste, Bibliothèque S. He was more interested in taking advantage of cast iron’s structural and decorative potentials than in replaying ancient motifs. Both buildings are heroic, with modest details defined by the capabilities of cast iron. Both suggest that a new decorative language was waiting to be discovered. Beaux-Arts’ greatest monument, the Paris Opéra (1861–75), by Jean-Louis-Charles Garnier (1825–98), is a more conventional adaptation of classical principles but overwhelming in its scale and richness of decorative detail (14.1, 14.4, and 14.5). The Petit Palais exhibition building (1897–1900) by Charles-Louis Girault (1851–1932) and the Gare du Quai d’Orsay railroad station (1898–1900; 14.8) by Victor Laloux (1850–1937) demonstrated how architectural rhetoric could glorify buildings dedicated to modern functions. House styles were eclectic: Colonial, Norman, French Renaissance, half-timbered Tudor, Gothic, Spanish Mission, Ranch House, or combinations of some or all. While necessity and financial restraints shaped most American architecture, immensely rich American industrialists, railroad magnates, and bankers were uninterested in practicalities. They wanted houses to rival those of the asset-rich, cash-poor European aristocrats to whom they married their daughters, and they needed architects who understood and could adapt European precedents. Richard Morris Hunt (1827–95) was the first American to study at the Beaux-Arts, from 1846– 55, and with that cachet he attracted clients such as the Vanderbilt family, who were rich from building railroads and steamboats, and stock speculation. For William K. Vanderbilt, he adapted an early French Renaissance château in an eclectic interpretation of the style forced by the exigencies of its city-center corner plot. He used the style again for George Washington Vanderbilt II’s country house in North Carolina. He used it yet again, around 1895, for The Breakers, Cornelius Vanderbilt II’s Newport, Rhode Island, mansion although there he adapted Italian Renaissance for the dining room (14.9). He employed a more eclectic conglomeration of arches, dormers, mansard roofs for the eight stories of the New York Tribune Building (1873–5) to which he added a skinny clock tower that rose another four floors. ![](images/be968828a952057ae41527165b35b3c846a588b8c59e53fbaec1221b8565f6ca.jpg) ## 14.9 When Hunt returned to the United States from France, he brought back his enthusiasm for the classicism of his Beaux-Arts training. In The Breakers, he created his own variation on Italian Renaissance design. ## Building High As cities grew, distinct districts were devoted to commercial activities, for ease of communication and commuting. Specialist support businesses were drawn to such concentrations of potential customers. “Business districts” commanded high rents and high land values. Taller buildings maximized profits and they could go taller with the invention of the passenger elevator. Until the introduction of cast-iron columns and beams, the height of buildings was limited by the structural potential of masonry walls and columns. The world’s first iron-framed building was the five-story-high Ditherington Flax Mill (1797) in Shrewsbury, England, and the technique employed there became common for the factories of the Industrial Revolution. Stone or brick remained the preferred options for external walls, however cast iron was strong and fire-resistant and could be poured into molds to make ornamental detail. In the United States, James Bogardus (1800–74) developed the iron-framing techniques introduced at Ditherington and established a factory in New York to make cast-iron cladding panels, in various Italianate styles for his own and other architects’ projects. These story-height units accelerated construction and allowed larger window areas. Floors remained wooden and vulnerable to fire, and inadequate fire escape stairs made them more dangerous. Fire safety was improved when brick or terracotta, laid in shallow arcs for compressive strength, replaced masonry. Fire-proofed structural elements and light tile cladding made twelve-story-high buildings feasible. In 1856, a British engineer, Henry Bessemer (1813–98), invented the Bessemer process for producing steel economically. The stronger steel columns and beams superseded their iron equivalents and the “skyscraper” would grow taller by the end of the century. The world’s first passenger lift was installed in the Equitable Life Company headquarters building in New York in 1870. By 1890, the electric elevator, invented in Germany in 1880, was sufficiently refined to make very tall buildings feasible. The prevailing Beaux-Arts aesthetic determined the aesthetics of the open tracery of the elevator cage that allowed passengers to view the spectacle as they were hoisted through central atria that ran the full height of buildings (14.10). The first examples, such as that in Los Angeles’ Bradbury Building of 1893, were spectacular if rudimentary. However, as the Beaux-Arts obligation to decorate asserted itself so the open metal traceries of the sides of the “birdcage” elevator cars became elements that designers attacked with relish. The monumental stair that in low-rise buildings had been centrally located on plan and led to the principal floor, had no effective role to play in the skyscraper. It was replaced by a battery of elevators, preferably on axis with the front doors. They were less imposing but indisputably practical and satisfied the Beaux-Arts preference for symmetrical planning. They also offered opportunities for extravagant decoration on the doors that confronted waiting passengers and the walls of the cabin that enclosed them. In the twentieth century, as passenger cabins were enclosed for safety and users’ peace of mind, internal decoration became perversely bland. ![](images/e87a5ad56fee3853c6fbec3907f9b53188ad137476c37a4f9dd19e4ce6de493b.jpg) George Herbert Wyman, Bradbury Building, Los Angeles, 1893. The emergence of the modern, large office building poses new problems for architects of the eclectic era. Wyman introduced the skylights over a central atrium to provide light for the perimeter galleries that replaced dark corridors and gave access to offices on each floor. The elevators moving in open cages and the stairs connecting the various gallery levels present an image more functional than eclectic. ## Office Interiors Office interiors were utilitarian with rows of small rooms ranged along corridors with external windows for light and ventilation. Floors, ceilings, partitions, and furniture were usually wooden, in shades of somber brown oak. Glazed partitions between offices allowed some daylight to reach rooms without external walls. Patterned glass gave visual privacy and opening panels provided cross ventilation. Directors’ offices and boardrooms might be distinguished by a rug or leathercovered upholstered chairs, perhaps some pictures. As corporations grew, so general offices evolved to accommodate hives of clerks, stenographers, and typists (14.11). Specialist equipment including filing cabinets, rolltop desks, swivel chairs, typewriters, adding machines, and time clocks became common. The telegraph and then the telephone changed basic communication and the nature of work. As gas gave way to electricity, improvements in artificial lighting reduced the need to locate offices on the external walls. ![](images/5f1cadd212af74bdcc85a1a2cf4d9c7c1681f050d747636b5703a033cf8b5637.jpg) 14.11 Illiustration of the office in a publishing company, New York, c. 1890. Principal rooms, with street views, had rich timber paneling, monumental fireplaces, parquet floors, generous allocations of stained glass, and other prestigious materials: sliding pocket doors created generous areas for entertaining. Second Empire style, the public architecture of France during the reign of Napoleon III (1852–70), was admired and emulated in the interiors of Philadelphia’s enormous City Hall (1872–1901; 14.14) by John McArthur Jr., (1823–90). The interiors have a bold vitality that eschews restraint. U.S. government buildings by Alfred B. Mullett (1834–90), such as the massive State, War, and Navy Building (now Eisenhower Executive Office Building) in Washington, D.C., (1871–88) follow a similar model. ![](images/329dc68e132fbb4cf01afceb08adc5d7326938bf283bb692c9b90b2542d2f2f8.jpg) 14.14 John McArthur, Jr., and Thomas U. Conversation Hall, as it is known, is a monumental interior in the huge building that was the governmental center for the large city Philadelphia had become. Admiration for the French Second Empire style had led to excessive grandeur in the public buildings of a democratic society. The work of Frank Furness (1839–1912) was underpinned by Second Empire influence but it was also tuned by a robust monumentality that drew freely on Gothic Revival, Stick, Shingle, and Arts and Crafts precedents. The brick and stone dressings of the library’s perimeter have the materials and scale of exterior walls, but the enormous fireplace dominates the room. ## Furniture and Fittings in Excess Factory production in the Victorian age made extravagantly decorated objects relatively inexpensive. New materials and techniques were developed and whole new categories of object became possible. The ancient Egyptians and Greek had discovered that by gluing thin panels of wood, with grains of alternate layers running at right angles, it was possible to produce a panel more resistant to warping than natural wood of the same thickness. In 1797 an English naval engineer, Sir Samuel Bentham (1757–1831), patented a number of machines capable of cutting very thin layers, or veneers, of wood by hand and described the gluing together of several veneers to form a stronger single sheet. Fifty years later, the Swedish engineer Immanuel Nobel (1801–72) invented the rotary lathe, which cut much thinner, much larger veneers from a log that was rotated as on a spit. In 1928 the standard size of 8 × 4 feet (2,440 × 1,220 mm) was established for all sheet materials and endures. Iron and brass tubing, which had first been used for utilitarian pipes, were promoted to make ornate bedhead and foot boards (14.17). They were much lighter and often more convoluted in shape than their wooden counterparts and therefore more desirable to the Victorian eye. ![](images/c46f3d412aeb78249ba50fd9ca46ef59637a4ec3662205c4c20903302dbe5623.jpg) 14.17 Sears Roebuck, page from catalog, United States, 1901. This page from a mail-order catalog shows a “massive” bed of iron tube and steel spring construction, the result of the adoption of industrial materials for domestic use. The Austrian–German cabinetmaker Michael Thonet (1796–1871) developed the technique of using steam in pressure chambers to bend strips of wood, which he then glued to make chairs that, without heavily upholstered bulk, went some way to satisfying the Victorian enthusiasm for visual complexities. In 1851 he received a bronze medal for his bentwood chairs at the Great Exhibition in London. In 1853 he received a silver at the Exposition Universelle (Universal Exhibition) in Paris. Following financial problems, he transferred the business to his sons and it continued as Gebrüder Thonet (Thonet Brothers). A chair produced in 1859, and known as Komsumstuhl Nr. 14, became ubiquitous in modest cafés and restaurants (14.19). No. 14 pioneered industrial chair production. It was assembled from a number of components that could be exported in space-saving packages across the world; components for thirty-six chairs could be exported in a box of 35 cubic feet (1 cubic meter). The chair won the Thonets a gold medal at the Paris World’s Fair in 1867. More than 50 million units of the model had been sold by 1930 and it remains in production. The seats and backs of Thonet chairs were usually of woven cane, which afforded a certain amount of cushioning, or plywood that could be shaped and decorated with cut-out patterns in the cause of decoration (14.18). ![](images/8dd76a5ba0535c501bb3df4b110374b912275356514df8562d2993930b578b18.jpg) Thonet Brothers, page from catalog, Austria, 1904. More than 50 million chairs have been sold since its launch and it remains in production. ## Furnishing the Home In his New York workshops, the German-born cabinetmaker John Henry Belter (1804–63) produced chairs, sofas, and tables that incorporated extravagantly curved and heavily upholstered elements, which were further embellished with elaborate floral carvings (14.22). Much of his output was made by mechanized processes in which slivers of shaped plywood were glued together to produce intricate carved ornament. The complexity of these and pieces by other makers imitating his style and techniques, have been labelled Rococo Revival. ![](images/95136113e7795f62d5e20adb9a6203ec88e6937da6abe7985990e6badd879cf3.jpg) John Henry Belter, sofa, rosewood and rosewood laminate, New York, c. 1850. German-born Belter developed a technique for bending and laminating rosewood, cutting and combining layers of laminate to create elaborate openwork carvings in the Rococo Revival style. The undulating silhouette and intricate floral ornament made his designs among the most popular of their time. At the end of the nineteenth century, Gustave Herter (1830–98) and Christian Herter (1839– 83), who were also born in Germany, emigrated and founded their eponymous company in New York with cabinet and upholstery workshops, a design office to provide an interior-decoration service, and a reputation for high-quality work in the most expensive and fashionable houses. Different lines were given names that suggested interpretations of historic styles: Renaissance Revival, Neo-Grec, Aesthetic movement, and Anglo-Japanese. The quality of the Herters’ work, both as designers and manufacturers, is typified by a mahogany and marquetry sideboard of c. 1880 in their Renaissance Revival style (14.21). 14.21 ![](images/6c3d72d166fc01e7f9d9227e0b591e5a516e6d3b5a44f2c90a9ac7e107efed4c.jpg) Herter Brothers, Renaissance Revival sideboard, mahogany with marquetry ornament, New York, c. 1880. The Herter Brothers designed and made furniture and interiors for some of the most lavish homes in the United States. Their revivalist styles included Renaissance, Neo-Grec, Aesthetic movement, and the Anglo-Japanese style. Well-padded upholstery provided comfort and signaled opulence. Thickness was emphasized by quilting, tufting, and padding pulled tight by buttons to create regular patterns of indentations. Metal springs beneath cushioned elements increased softness. Fabrics were usually colorful with elaborate woven designs but leather and woven horsehair, usually in black, were alternatives. Leather was favored in rooms considered to need a “masculine” atmosphere: dining rooms, smoking rooms, billiard rooms, and studies. A side chair of 1846–47 by upholstered seat and back demonstrated how the vogue for Gothic Revival furniture in ecclesiastical architecture influenced furniture in the home (14.20). The lighter touch of Rococo Revival, typified by the work of Belter and the Herter Brothers, was thought more appropriate for parlors and boudoirs. Size was prized, and large, mirrored hat racks appeared in halls and vestibules. Pianos made in rectangular, grand, and upright forms were status symbols embellished with rich and complex ornamentation, their flat surfaces offered additional opportunities for displays of ornaments and framed photographs. ![](images/96dbd4c7a76d8f55fbeb82c750a5e4380d98c40f66cbb0dd363b7cfb8b8d9406.jpg) Thomas Brooks, side chair, black walnut, New York, 1846–7. At a time of enthusiastic churchgoing, the new Gothic Revival style would have been familiar, particularly to members of the numerous and prosperous middle class. Its influence is apparent in this chair, which was probably intended for a dining room or a library. Something less ecclesiastical and more Rococo Revival would have served in a drawing room or boudoir. Textiles that had been produced slowly by hand lent themselves easily to factory production. Powered looms in efficient mills could produce large, elaborate and colorful patterns on woven or printed cloth. Floral designs were particularly popular and braiding, fringes, and tassels added to the luxuriance. Lace curtains provided auxiliary privacy when heavier curtains were opened, and those with integral woven patterns satisfied the Victorian need for decoration and, by filtering daylight, contributed to the Gothic gloom that characterized the Victorian interior. Carpets were generally produced on power looms and, like furnishing textiles, favored repeat patterns of natural forms, arabesques and scrolls. Standard lengths could be sewn together and laid from wall to wall. Linoleum was invented in 1855 in England by Frederick Walton (1834– 1928), using solidified linseed oil, pine resin, and ground cork dust on a canvas backing. It was easily adapted to incorporate floral patterns and imitations of wooden parquet and tiled floorings. Printed wallpapers were the most economical way of dispatching plain walls and could match the colors and patterns of fabrics while offering more delicate detail. Floral and geometric patterns, Chinese and Japanese themes, even scenic motifs were popular. Some papers were embossed for a slight bas-relief effect and printed paper strips replicating egg and dart and Greekkey motifs would be applied just below ceiling level as a cornice, with deeper triangular short sections used at corners. The illustrations in The Grammar of Ornament (1856) by English designer and architect Owen Jones were brightly colored and, at first, had encouraged the use of strong hues. However, within a short time they were considered frivolous and were replaced by duller, darker colors. Browns, olive greens, and mauves were considered more tasteful and muted in candlelight and gaslight. After 1854 and Captain Perry’s ventures in Japan and China, references to Asia were rife. Bamboo—and imitations of bamboo—furniture became popular both in the United States and Europe. Bamboo objects were imported or made locally from imported material. Folding screens with painted or fabric surfaces on wooden frames had an ostensibly practical function but were principally prized for their decorative potential. Wicker furniture made from woven rattan (thin strips of palm-tree bark) or similar flexible materials was popular in less formal rooms such as porches, conservatories, or children’s bedrooms and nurseries ## Shopping The new cast-iron framed buildings served many functions most obviously factories and warehouses but also the “dry goods” stores that were forerunners of the department stores (14.23) that first emerged in the cities of the East Coast offering comprehensive selections of goods that allowed Victorian shoppers to compare, select, and buy everything necessary for home decoration. Oil lamps, from the simple to the very ornate, were the most common lighting devices until supplanted by gaslights, which tended to replicate the design of candle brackets. Chandeliers, often ornamented with brass work and hanging glass prisms, were popular and usually as big as a room could accommodate. With the introduction of electric light, gas brackets were converted to take the new filament bulbs that Thomas Edison invented in 1879. The first electric fan appeared in 1889 and in 1893 a fully electric kitchen was displayed at the World’s Columbian Exposition in Chicago. ![](images/fdea180a66a04e1df9c1d178f56f9a0b62ffa20e1202bf17a30b527f98724129.jpg) 14.23 Schuyler, Hartley & Graham shop, New York, 1864. Guns and military goods were in demand during the U.S. Civil War (1861–5). The shop interior has counters, cases, and cabinets in the vernacular of the period, while the upper walls and ceiling are richly decorated. As the United States began to colonize its own empire in the west, and frontier towns and cities became respectable, retailers invented ways to service their household needs. Mail-order catalogs by Sears Roebuck, Montgomery Ward, and many smaller companies illustrated the products necessary to set up a respectable home far from the department stores of the East Coast. At a prosaic level, plumbing fixtures and water pumps, cast-iron stoves for heating, and ranges for cooking that, inevitably, sported decorative flourishes were shipped west. Generally, the more expensive the item, the more it would be decorated. Bathroom fixtures, including flushing lavatories, bathtubs, and washbasins were offered in a plethora of styles, from the utilitarian to the flamboyant. Decoration and furnishings in the most isolated homes needed to pass muster with aesthetically inclined visitors. As transport improved, so coal replaced local wood as fuel and, as towns became cities, gas superseded coal. ## High Style Sites in the commercial centers of prosperous cities increasingly became fewer, but technology made tall buildings an option. Early examples of what became known as “skyscrapers” show architects’ reluctance to move away from pre-industrial conventions but tall buildings caused aesthetic problems. There were five classical orders of architecture and, initially, buildings higher than five stories posed aesthetic conundrums. Classical columns had been conceived to rest on the ground and support a pitched roof and the orders were not designed to be mixed or stacked. A reversion to the Georgian plainness that had evolved for tall London townhouses made sense and worked for the Monadnock Building (1889–91) in Chicago by Daniel Burnham (1846–1912) and John Wellborn Root (1850–91). Its sixteen stories were totally unornamented with load-bearing, red brick walls that were enormously thick at lower levels to take the weight of the upper levels. The wall of the lowest floor projected slightly to make a “plinth” and a simple projecting “cornice” finished the fourteen identical floors between with a modest flourish. Internally, the iron elevator structure and cages provided the only decorative elements. After Root’s death in 1891, Burnham hired Charles B. Atwood (1849–95) to complete the Reliance Building (1895) and he replaced load-bearing masonry with a steel structure clad in “curtain” walls of large windows and white, glazed terracotta panels. The Reliance also offered electricity and telephones to all its tenants. A central top-lit courtyard provided daylight and ventilation. The Rookery (1886), a Chicago office block, had a central court covered at groundfloor level with a glass and iron-framed roof, designed by Frank Lloyd Wright in 1905. An alternative solution, typified by the Bradbury Building (1893) in Los Angeles (see here), was to install the glass roof at the top of the building and to use the huge central void for elevator cages, stairs, and access balconies, all of which gave opportunities for cast-iron decoration. Ernest Flagg (1857–1947) applied Second Empire style to the forty-seven floors of the Singer Building in New York, which was designed and constructed in phases between 1897 and 1908 (14.24). It was, briefly, the world’s tallest building at 612 feet (187 m). A mansard-topped tower rose from a larger block in which lower floors were open to the public, with monumentally ornate stairs and balconies under vaulted ceilings. ![](images/0469597957a21485a5e4928e163ea5c758943e5f5a06addb4996681da0c40022.jpg) Ernest Flagg, Singer Building, New York, 1897–1908. The monumental interiors of the lower floors, of what was briefly the tallest building in the world, were open to the public. In 1913, the Woolworth Building (14.25) in New York by Cass Gilbert (1859–1934) became the tallest building in the world at 792 feet (241 m). Its steel frame, elevators, and sixty stories of offices were clothed in white glazed terracotta tiles among the tracery and pinnacles of French Gothic ecclesiastical architecture. The public interiors included spacious elevator lobbies, stairs, balconies, and arcades, all detailed in a Gothic/Byzantine hybrid style. Executive office interiors sported tapestries and an eclectic mix of ornamental furniture and wood carvings. Gargoyles caricatured Gilbert holding a model of the building and Frank Winfield Woolworth, the founder of the F. W. Woolworth Company retail chain, clutching money bags labeled “5c” and “10c” in reference to the prices of goods in his cut-price stores. ![](images/b9b8970cf2a6eebd8eccf5daf5dddd0a1d35fa459b4721775bc0b7e992007edf.jpg) Cass Gilbert, Woolworth Building, New York, 1913. On its completion, the Woolworth Building became the world’s tallest building and, like the Singer (14.24), its entrance lobbies were open to the public. It became standard practice for shop units in lobbies to be rented to independent businesses that relied on the custom of people who worked in the building. ## Ocean Liners Interior design reached remarkable levels of opulence in the interiors of the great ocean liners (14.26). On the British RMS Mauretania (1906) passengers in first class could enjoy halls, lounges, and smoking rooms in Italian Renaissance and Francis I French styles designed by the British architect Harold Peto (1854–1933), who specialized in lavishly eclectic interiors for town houses, country mansions, and hotels. The smoking room of the British RMS Franconia (1923) masqueraded as a Tudor half-timbered hall with a huge brick fireplace. The Great German pre-World War I liners excelled in excess eclectic decor. The swimming pool of the German SS Vaterland (1914) later renamed Leviathan was “Pompeian” with Roman Doric columns two stories high around the tiled pool. The main lounge of the Italian liner Conte di Savoia (1931) recreated the gallery of the seventeenth-century Colonna Palace in Rome, complete with statuary and fresco painting. ![](images/722be15265d812d5708139447ad29b32f290bb6cad3f866817f8ec58a3b320da.jpg) ## 14.26 The ocean liner SS France, 1910. Some of the most spectacular eclectic interiors were found in first-class accommodation on ocean liners. The grand stair and dining room with rich—supposedly Baroque—decoration was intended to suggest the comforts of a grand hotel and to minimize the effect of the rigors of an Atlantic crossing. ## Movie Theaters Eclecticism continued deep into the twentieth century. Film, the new mass entertainment, and spectacular Hollywood film sets prompted eclectic design within movie theaters, which themselves became a part of the visual experience. Traditional theaters and opera houses had always been elaborately decorative, but now a mass audience could find lobbies loaded with decoration. Thomas W. Lamb (1871–1942) specialized in creating vast auditoria suggesting exotic and fantastic settings—Spanish, Persian, Indo–Chinese, or an amalgam of styles. Lamb created more than 300 theaters in the 1920s and 1930s. John Eberson (1875–1954) specialized in “atmospheric theater,” where the ceiling performed as a sky with moving clouds, stars, and a moon floating above complex architectural and sculptural detail. Grauman’s Egyptian (1922) and Chinese (1927) theaters in Hollywood, by Meyer and Holler, were extravaganzas that became nationally famous. At the Egyptian theater (14.27), the open-air forecourt was flanked by a “bazaar” of shops and, during screenings, an employee dressed as a Bedouin would intone the title of the feature. The proscenium surrounding the screen was lavishly decorated and gilded in the manner of the Egyptian king, Tutankhamen, whose tomb had been uncovered in 1922. The rest of the interior was simple but monumental, still referencing ancient Egyptian tombs and temple architecture but almost modernist in its aesthetic. Hotels and restaurants also drifted toward historical pastiche. ![](images/9e3c18b324e67168df96a76263d332c8cae7daebf534148b83758948e2ceb9df.jpg) 14.27 Meyer and Holler, Egyptian theater, Hollywood, 1922. Inspired by the opening of Tutankhamen’s tomb—or the success of Rudolph Valentino’s film The Sheik (1921)—the columns and “hieroglyphics” live up to the theater’s name. ## EUROPE In Europe, eclecticism was not unknown but did not develop on the scale it achieved in the United States. Perhaps the presence of real historic buildings and interiors gave imitation less appeal but a few bombastic examples, such as the Palace of Justice in Brussels (1866–83) by Joseph Poelaert (1817–79) or the monument to Victor Emmanuel II in Rome (1885–1911) by Giuseppe Sacconi (1854–1905), were exceedingly grandiose. Classicism came to be the official style of governmental design in Europe in the 1930s. The combination of formality and tradition, with a touch of modernism and a sense of efficiency and economy, contributed to its appeal. An example is the Finnish Parliament House in Helsinki (1927–32; 14.28) by Johan Sigfrid Sirén (1889–1961). Its façade of fourteen classical columns, at the top of a monumental flight of steps, fronts a symmetrical plan generated by the circular legislative chamber at its center. In the 1930s, it became the favored style of Adolf Hitler in Germany and Benito Mussolini in Italy. Demagogues understand the political gains to be made from giving people their preferred architectural style. ![](images/a8d893e8da64e8a5dfb0922e926c7a87b013220875ba63e8166c81e4d0f90ebd.jpg) 14.28 Johan Sigfrid Sirén, Finnish Parliament House, Helsinki, 1927–32. Sirén stripped the building of all the conventional signifiers of classical architecture, except for the fourteen columns on the entrance façade but even these were stretched so that they lacked the load-bearing solidity of their ancient antecedents. The flat roof—and the consequent lack of a pediment—brought the building close to modernist massing even if the symmetrical organization of plan and elevations contradicted that. Eclecticism was primarily an American phenomenon; perhaps it was a way of not only acquiring the pedigree of established European styles but of having them express something of a new confident country on the verge of dominating the old continent in the next century. Eclecticism had been less strong in Europe where old buildings, in a variety of styles, were abundant. Europeans in search of a new style were searching for something more radical. Some of them believed that a response was needed to the technological changes being pioneered in the United States, others continued to look for inspiration in nature.
