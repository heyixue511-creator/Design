Using mass media to shape public opinion is a theme inherited from nineteenth-century reform (see Chapter 4) and was renewed during World War I with increasing emphasis upon the power and impact of visual images and short accompanying catchphrases. There is a remarkable similarity among such war posters regardless of which side of the conflict they promoted, for they rely upon a combination of romantic idealism and monumentality inherited from the Renaissance that identifies war with heroism, as seen in Jules Abel Faivre’s (1867–1945) 1916 poster image of a young soldier with arm raised high in a plea to buy war bonds (below, top). War posters blurred boundaries between art, advertising, and patriotism, contributing to the enormous role that the advertising industry, political propaganda, and illustration assumed during the 1920s and 1930s. In Germany poster artists sometimes explored more reductive and sophisticated techniques for engaging the viewer, approaches that laid the foundation for modern graphic design. Deutsches Historisches Museum, Berlin. ## Dimensions of Interwar Design Part IV encompasses the years 1918 to 1944, and includes three touchstones of modern design history: the long-awaited Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes in Paris (1925) from which the label “Art Deco” derives; the foundation of the German school for design education known as the Bauhaus (1919–1933), whose curriculum, publications, and exhibitions shifted from a craft- to a factory-based model amid similar and related contemporary initiatives taking place in France, Soviet Russia, the Netherlands, Eastern Europe, and Austria, motivated at times by a desire to address working-class needs and living conditions; and the 1939 World’s Fair in Flushing Meadows, New York, displaying an array of retail products and imagined futuristic projects, the fruits of an emerging industrial design profession in the United States in the 1930s, navigating the competing and often conflicting aesthetic, commercial, social, and political dimensions of design during this period. Design functioned prominently in the major historical events of the era: the suffering and lingering trauma of World War I; economic recovery in the 1920s; a crippling economic depression accompanied by social unrest in the 1930s; the rise of repressive authoritarian regimes in Europe; collectivization in the Soviet Union; and large-scale social welfare and infrastructure projects in the United States. With the advent of radio communication and faster means of transportation by rail, steamship, and auto, the design enterprise during this period was more global than ever, but the design history story has for the most part remained stubbornly Eurocentric, with non-Western nations generally relegated to the margins of the historical narrative. And as broad and encompassing as the three touchstones may appear, they do not include an even wider range of design activity during this period, in particular those areas that lie outside the collections of art and design museums and are more likely to be found in trade journals and mass advertising that reached a broader consumer market. Moreover, there is a tendency to view these three landmarks as distinct as well as monolithic, pitting, for instance, the luxury of Art Deco against the reductive approach to design at the Dessau Bauhaus or the “consumer engineering” of American industrial design. As the following pages demonstrate, there was considerable overlap and crossover among these approaches to modern design during the interwar period, corresponding to complex attitudes toward technology, work, leisure, and lifestyle. We might begin thinking about the expanded role of design during this period as varied responses to the experience of World War I (1914–18) and its aftermath. The origins of the style known as Art Moderne or Art Deco may be traced to the years just before the war. But the persistence of French organizers to host an international exhibition devoted to modern decorative and industrial arts in 1925, originally planned to take place in 1915, suggests that the active promotion of French pre-eminence in the design and manufacture of luxury goods to accompany a “modern” lifestyle that embraced travel, tourism, entertainment, and hygiene was motivated by a combination of renewed nationalism and economic reconstruction in the wake of the Great War that extended to many of the countries that participated in the exhibition. The majority of Art Deco designers who took part in the exhibition saw themselves as artist-craftsmen, proud of the equality they had earned and shared with fine artists and cognizant of an exclusive and discriminating international market for their goods. Marketing, advertising, and promotion through posters, merchandising, and lifestyle magazines such as Vogue expanded the audience and commercial exposure of Art Deco. The Bauhaus presents a complex and shifting institutional history. Its first director, architect Walter Gropius (1883–1969), was a World War I veteran. The school’s first home was the city of Weimar, capital of the republican government that replaced the imperial state following Germany’s defeat. The Bauhaus manifesto (see here) proclaimed a utopian faith in the creativity and unity of the arts culminating in the art of building, the exaltation of craft, and an ideal integration of art into life. In the early 1920s the Bauhaus mission shifted its focus, championing industrial materials and production, eliminating overt signs of handwork, and adopting the mantra “Art and Technology: A New Unity.” The shift was apparent in the appointment of new faculty, in the construction of an austere factory-like building for the school’s new home in Dessau in 1926, and in the design of prototypes for furnishings that eliminated ornament in favor of spare forms emphasizing an underlying, elementary geometric abstraction with universal aesthetic and social pretensions. The shift at the Bauhaus was contemporary with (and to an extent influenced by) international efforts of artist-designers in Soviet Russia and in other countries who, under banners such as Constructivism (Russia), l’Esprit Nouveau (France, see here and fig. 9.5), and De Stijl (the Netherlands), embraced prefabricated building techniques and materials, and espoused a reductive visual vocabulary for design as a basis for an egalitarian society. Such radical and utopian views, often referred to by the label “Modernism” (with a capital “M”), were meant to replace outmoded individualistic and nationalistic values that were viewed as contributing to competition, international tensions, and eventually conflict and world war. The experimental use of modern industrial materials for furnishings, elimination of carved or molded ornament, and use of sans serif typography as well as photomechanical processes for printing are often associated with these movements, but such technologies and practices were not limited to them nor to their particular aesthetic, social, and political ideologies. Clean lines, smooth surfaces, undecorated forms, experimental typography, and asymmetrical page layouts in printing emerged in the 1930s in commercial as well as radical experimental contexts. Depending upon the specific historical circumstances, “Modernism” could signify a variety of attitudes toward modern life, from austerity in response to economic hardship, to practical, socially responsible middle-class modern living, to utopian projects for radical collective or communal living, to an embodiment of the spiritualized experience of non-objectivity, or to a more transparent, “natural” lifestyle freed from convention and conformity to tradition. It is no wonder that in assembling material for its 2006 exhibition devoted to Modernism, the Victoria and Albert Museum catalog could only conclude that Modernism was a “loose collection of ideas” rather than a consistent approach to design. The United States emerged after World War I as an international leader in the mechanized mass production of automobiles and related products of industrial manufacturing such as kitchen appliances, owing to the unparalleled success of Henry Ford’s moving assembly line in the production of the standardized, unchanging Model T (see here). But in the wake of the nation’s participation in the war, America’s foreign policy turned inward and isolationist, and included withdrawal from the fledgling League of Nations founded in 1920 and worries at home over liberal immigration policies. After the mid-1920s, the market for furnishings and industrial products began to decline and needed to be sustained through aggressive advertising as well as through design innovation. The emergence of Art Deco in the United States was stimulated by the emigration of European designers after World War I in search of creative and economic opportunity and the receptivity of manufacturers and retailers in providing consumers with modern luxury goods and furnishings. Business magazines such as Fortune touted the application of modern art to industrial and workshop production achieved through the efforts of artists, collectors, museums, manufacturers, and retail merchandisers. One outcome was a modern design style sometimes known as “Skyscraper,” inspired by the tall buildings that were beginning to dominate the skylines of North American cities, the excitement of city nightlife, the rhythms and improvisational spontaneity of jazz music, interpreted visually using Cubist fragmentation, and marketed through the popular cinema, museum exhibitions, department stores, and illustrated magazines. The Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) in New York, founded in 1929, exhibited and promoted a more austere approach to architecture and design, showing architectural models by Le Corbusier, Walter Gropius, Mies van der Rohe (the last director of the Bauhaus), and other European and US architects, proclaiming an “International Style” based upon the use of modern industrial materials (steel, reinforced concrete, large expanses of glass [curtain walls], and the idea of the elimination of ornament), which a curator noted “would seem to mar rather than adorn the clean perfection [italics added] of surface and proportion.” The appearance of the professional industrial designer in the United States, beginning in the later 1920s and 1930s, was primarily a product of industrial manufacturers’ desire to stimulate consumer interest for their products and appliances through an appeal to novelty and fantasy in an increasingly competitive economic climate. Desire for household appliances was often advertised in the language of paternalistic reform: that is, as a contribution to social progress, hygiene, and the application of domestic science and technology to everyday life, clearly expressed in the modern style known as Streamlining. The planners of the 1939 World’s Fair in New York saw design as encouraging employment, economic growth, and improved living standards for the middle class on a vast scale with large corporations and government as sponsors of ambitious urban and suburban planning, including an interstate highway system. Organizers and manufacturers hoped that a streamlined vision of the future would reduce social ills amid the hardships of the Depression, and would act as a bulwark against populism and the attraction of fascist dictatorships in Europe as well as socialism and the threat it posed to capitalist free enterprise. The Fair’s vision of the “World of Tomorrow” combined exhibits to attract the attention of consumers such as electric dishwashers and the first television sets, with sci-fi novelties such as robots, the General Motors Company’s Futurama, and the space-age Trylon and Perisphere that housed a future miniature “Democracity.” The Americas, the subject of Global Inspiration IV, exhibit the complexities that a global perspective brings to bear on the history of modern design, from early colonial ties to Spain as displayed at World’s Fairs, to the search for (or construction of) an Indigenous national identity in Mexico in the wake of independence and its marketing in the United States through tourism and the film industry; to an engagement with international Modernism that includes the education of Latin American designers in Europe and the emigration of Europeans to the Americas. Scholars have argued that competition from antique dealers and manufacturers offering period furnishings for the luxury market may have eroded the market for Art Nouveau, and it is also possible that department stores selling less expensive imitations and reproductions of the style may have reduced its appeal to the high-end market for modern decorative arts. Competition also came from Germany: French writers at the time praised examples of German design at exhibitions for their modern practicality and restrained decoration as debated by the Werkbund (see Chapter 7, here). Emerging attitudes toward modern living valued efficiency, seen in the success of scientific management in business and industry, in the writings on domestic science by Christine Frederick, and in the recommendations of taste-makers such as Elsie de Wolfe (1865 1950), who advocated the cultivation of uncluttered interiors, light, and good ventilation in her 1913 book The House in Good Taste. Even as designers explored more practical attitudes toward modern living in a variety of goods and interior ensembles, the values that nurtured Bings and Meier-Graefes showrooms persisted, particularly as they encouraged innovation in materials, original decoration, and a belief in the unity and equality of the arts. In France these values persisted under the auspices of the Société des Artistes Décorateurs (S.A.D.) and the annual Salon dAutomne where fine and decorative arts were exhibited together. In addition, periodicals devoted to design (Art et Décoration, for example) promoted the work of modern designers for a luxury market. French designers continued to embrace the reputation for individuality and originality they had fought for as well as continuity with a distinguished national heritage of craft excellence prior to and after the French Revolution. Designers explored a wide variety of new sources of inspiration, including contemporary painting and sculpture, original adaptations of restrained late eighteenthcentury Neoclassical furniture and fashion, along with sensational entertainments in Paris such the Ballets Russes and the jazz and dance clubs that emerged after World War I, associated with a spirited and youthful modernity. Maurice Dufrènes (18761955) upholstered mahogany armchair (fig. 8.3), based upon an eighteenth-century type known as a bergère (see here) and exhibited at the 1913 Salon dAutomne, exemplifies the new direction pursued by French artist-designers in the years just before the outbreak of World War I. Dufrène began his career as a painter, worked as a furniture designer and manager at Julius Meier-Graefes La Maison Moderne showrooms, helped to found the Société des Artistes Décorateurs in 1901, and managed the retail design studio known as Maîtrise for the Parisian department store Galeries Lafayette beginning in 1922 that marketed artistic designs for the retail trade. Dufrènes furniture reveals continuity with highly skilled workshop production, and from the second decade of the twentieth century embraced a robust yet controlled modern sensibility. In the armchair, for instance, the broad contours of both the back and seat are comfortably balanced and embellished by the carved spiral scroll at the termination of the armrests and the seams of the silk upholstery to create a design remarkable for its harmony and unity of structure and decoration. The broad, sweeping curves of Dufrènes designs recall the ripe fullness of nature found in the still lives and interiors of Henri Matisse, and also bring to mind Matisses analogy between painting and furniture in his 1908 essay Notes of a Painter. In this essay, the artist compared the tranquil mood he hoped that his canvases would provide his bourgeois urban audience with the comfort of a good armchair: What I dream of is an art of balance, of purity and tranquility, devoid of troubling subject matter, an art which could be, for every mental worker, for the businessman as well as for the man of letters, for example, a soothing balm, a mental calmative, something akin to a good armchair which eases his physical fatigue. ![](images/17e1a3a7f50c702996bce5374bfb4cda229fcf6662b414732840774cf9b0f4b4.jpg) Maurice Dufrène, chair, mahogany and upholstery, 30 × 23 in (76.2 × 48.4 cm), shown at the Salon d’Automne, Paris, 1913. The suggestion that paintings and furniture bring relief from the pressures of whitecollar work and the routines of daily life (Matisses remarks stem from a similar statement made by French writer Charles Baudelaire; 18211867) reinforces a shared milieu for modern artists and high-end artist-designed furnishings in common pursuit of original creative expression that contributes to the joy and sense of well-being found in everyday acts of modern living for a discriminating audience with sufficient leisure and imagination (and financial resources) to find aesthetic pleasure in everyday experience. Like several of his contemporaries with careers in design, Frenchman Jacques-Émile Ruhlmann (18791933) studied to be a painter but turned to the decorative arts around 1910. Many of his designs are free interpretations of French Neoclassical or Empire furniture, augmenting the French patrimony of virtuoso marquetry using expensive and rare materials and involving the most exacting standards of craftsmanship. As seen in a desk from c. 1919 (fig. 8.4), his furniture is replete with invention, employing veneers of exotic woods such as amboyna imported from Laos and macassar ebony from Indonesia (both were French colonial territories at the time), with ringed drawer pullers of ivory attached by green silk thread accenting a grayish-green sharkskin writing surface (Ruhlmann used other materials such as crocodile skin for cabinet doors in place of glass or fabric). The desk is supported by gently curving thin legs that project outward from the desktop and are delicately tapered (known as fuseau). Ruhlmann was a member of S.A.D.; in his view, luxury and artistic freedom were the bedrock of modern French design. In an interview from 1920 he stated: Only the very rich can pay for what is new and they alone can make it fashionable. Fashions do not start among the common people. References to the decorative arts in the early Cubist paintings of Georges Braque (18821963) and Pablo Picasso (18811973) consist primarily of the incorporation of pieces of cheap printed wallpaper and the borrowing of wall painters techniques for imitating wood grain. Such borrowings constitute encroachments from the industrial products of ordinary domestic life upon the territory of high Art, in terms of their lower middle-class associations and the implied blurring between mechanical reproduction and the individual, original, and exclusive work of fine artists. But while Cubist paintings appear to challenge the exclusivity associated with fine art, early twentieth-century critics did not generally acknowledge this provocative crossing of boundaries. More often they found parallels for the geometric shapes and abstract, compositional strategies of Cubist artists in contemporary luxury craft such as those ensembles displayed at the 1912 Maison Cubiste that reinforced the elevated aesthetic views of S.A.D. One can detect a debt to Cubism in the furniture and furnishings designed by Frenchman Pierre Chareau (18831950) who marketed his custom designs through a boutique he owned and operated in Paris beginning in 1924. The beveled contours of the desk and angles of back and armrests of the desk chair suggest Cubist abstraction, and both pieces are devoid of carved decoration or marquetry. Also Cubist-inspired is Chareaus standing lamp with intersecting, faceted pieces of smooth, translucent alabaster attached to a metal armature, adapted as well to desk lamps, chandeliers, and sconces. Chareaus boutique catered to an elite audience and used expensive materials, including imported woods as well as labor-intensive craftsmanship, with wrought iron produced by master craftsman Louis Dalbet, who collaborated with Chareau on many of his commissions. At the Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes, 1925 (International Exposition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts), a tall painted mural by Robert Delaunay (18851941), featuring a Cubist rendering of the Eiffel Tower, was included in one of the interiors for the exhibitions model French embassy, but a more persistent link between the fragmented and dynamic style seen in this and related paintings by Delaunay and the decorative arts was made by Delaunays Ukrainian-born wife, Sonia Terk Delaunay (18851979). Sonia Delaunay used overlapping or interlocking abstract geometric shapes and prismatic color for the design of interiors, fabrics, clothing, and other furnishings. The Delaunays used the term simultaneity to describe the dynamism and transcendence of solid form that characterized the aesthetic experience of modernity, and in 1925 a Boutique Simultanée was included as one of the Gallery of Boutiques featured at the Paris Exhibition. The following year, the Delaunays collaborated on the costumes and set designs for a new production of Cléopatre performed by the Ballets Russes in Paris. Cassandre likened his task as a poster artist to that of a telegrapher who was the instantaneous conduit of condensed information to be seen rather than read. Cassandres designs were highly selective in spite of the matter-of-fact tone of his selfassessment: he drew upon abstract elements in contemporary fine art such as Cubist painting and abstract sculpture as well as popular forms of visual communication including film sequencing and branding. He also incorporated sophisticated verbal as well as visual puns that helped to integrate text and image. His mural-sized 1932 poster featuring Monsieur Dubo for Dubonnet wine (part of a series of posters he designed for the company) features a seated figure in a bowler hat in three stages of pouring, sipping, and pouring a second glass of the beverage (fig. 8.11). In each iteration the letters of the brand are completed as Monsieur Dubos form fills out. The successive words beneath the first two images play on the French word for good (bon) that was already in use by the company (Good Dubonnet), ending with the brand name itself. The deft combination of repetition and variation to attract and maintain viewer attention is remarkable. There are subtle variations in pose, as well as in color. Cassandres little man (aka Monsieur Dubo) is composed entirely of straight and precisely drawn semicircular curved lines, whose regular contours are repeated from part to part in each of the three variations, creating a strong sense of visual unity within each poster and as a sequence that is read left to right. The little mans torso and legs remain in the same position while the upper body, head, and right arm reveal slight variations in the sequence. Straight lines are generally parallel or drawn at a 90-degree angle. Other angles create parallel lines, such as the pouring bottle in the third of the three images, which matches the angle of Dubos head and his bent left leg. The angle of the head is at 90 degrees in the first image in the series, turning more acutely in the middle drinking image. In each of the three posters the viewers eye follows a diagonal path downward from upper right to lower left and then continues across horizontally with the letters of the word Dubonnet, suggested in the numerous horizontal contours and shapes of the little man, table, and chair. Circular and semi-circular shapes are found in Dubos tightly fitting bowler hat, with echoes in the shapes of the wine glass, elbow, eye, eyeball, sleeve buttons, and the fingernails of the left hand. They also are found in the counters of the geometric sans serif, upper-case letters and the shapes U, B, O, and D. Unlike poster advertisements for alcoholic beverages in the later nineteenth century, or even ads for wine or beer on television today, Monsieur Dubo drinks alone rather than in the represented or implied company of others. Typical and yet at the same time remarkable, even personal when triggered by one of its attending objects, the café celebrates an aspect of our experience of modern time, offering a respite (whether private or social) from the demands and obligations of work (when time is not our own) and the hurried pace of the day, a small indulgence that reminds us of our autonomy, lending significance to unimportant moments rather than dramatic or climactic ones. Cassandres work helped to define the profession of graphic design in the interwar years, eschewing more conventional narrative advertising illustration in favor of bolder, reductive ways to identify products and create an immediate and memorable visual impact. In 1936 the Museum of Modern Art in New York devoted an exhibition to Cassandres posters; he was the first graphic designer to be so honored; an earlier exhibition at MoMA devoted to design (1934) was entitled Machine Art (see here). A similar sophistication, integrating fragmentary or pictographic images with blocks of text to create visual variety and interest, is found in books and high-end journals in Paris in the interwar period, as practiced by publisher Alfred Tolmer (18761957). In 1930, Tolmer issued a book entitled Mise en Page that helped to define new techniques that explored creative approaches to visual communication in the publishing industry. An examination of glass design in the period around 1910 reveals a lively interest in a variety of techniques to permit individual and original expression in the medium. Perhaps as a result of Gallés success and reputation (see here), early twentieth-century glass does not always make a distinct break with Art Nouveau. The enameling technique known as pâte de verre, produced by refiring colored ground glass in molds, resulted in translucent surfaces of varied color whose rich effects were reminiscent of the earlier movement, even if the technique, to be successful, required thick walls and more patient attention to the cooling process. After experimenting with the process of pâte de verre, Marinot explored effects using transparent as well as colored glass. René Lalique (18601945) operated his own glassmaking facility near Paris for the manufacture of limited editions of his designs, many of which are manufactured today in boutiques that bear the founders name. Lalique trained as a jeweler, and earlier in his career created some of the most celebrated examples of Art Nouveau jewelry using precious metals, stones, and enamel. Before the end of the first decade of the new century he began working with glass as an inexpensive industrial material rather than using precious gems, purchasing a lightbulb factory in 1911 to work directly with the medium. Lalique explored glass primarily as a transparent rather than translucent medium, etching with acid to achieve contrast in texture. Critics admired Laliques work for its purity and perfection, seen in the Firebird lamp (fig. 8.13), which took its name from the title character (half-woman/half-bird) of a well-known production (with music by Russian composer Igor Stravinsky; 18821971) at the Ballets Russes, staged first in 1910 and performed again in 1926. For instance, Swiss-born Jean Dunand (18771942) came to Paris originally to study sculpture. His teacher, Jean Dampt (18541946), advocated egalitarian views toward the arts, and with his mentors encouragement Dunand pursued and achieved critical success in the field of dinanderie, referring to small vases and utensils manufactured from nonprecious metals such as copper and brass, applying patina for textures and colored surfaces as well as the repoussé technique to introduce relief patterns based upon geometric and schematic floral forms. Occasionally the organic shapes of Dunands early dinanderie vases recall the irregularity of Art Nouveau pottery or glass, but often his works exhibit a preference for more regular contours and classically inspired forms (such as the Greek amphora), as well as spiral decorative motifs. In 1912 Dunand studied the technique of applying lacquer to a variety of hammered copper dinanderie under the guidance of a well-known Japanese craftsman named Seizo Sugawara (d. 1940) who was working in Paris at the invitation of French jeweler Lucien Gaillard (18611942). Lacquer is a colored or transparent substance made from the sap of trees grown primarily in East Asia. A long process of refinement and purification is required to produce lacquer, and its application to surfaces of wood or metal objects is equally painstaking. Although lacquer was used primarily as a protective coating to preserve and protect materials (with Dunands assistance the French air force used lacquer to coat the surface of plywood propellers for airplanes during World War I in order to prevent the dangerous deterioration of the material in wet or humid weather), Dunand became interested in lacquers potential for design, which he developed more fully after the war. His exacting methods produced layered surfaces of great subtlety and brilliance on a variety of wooden screens, metal bowls, and furniture. An idea of the variety of surface effects in lacquer is seen in a composite photograph in figure 8.14. After World War I, economic reconstruction and a renewed sense of patriotism in France helped to expand the commercial scope of Art Moderne. Yet the period just before World War I and lasting into the 1930s also witnessed tremendous political ferment and dramatic upheaval, during which the relationship between design, technology, and progress was redirected, often toward broader, utopian ends. In addition to the destruction wrought by a four-year international conflict in which close to 40 million military and civilian lives were lost, the period witnessed the 1917 Revolution and ensuing Civil War in Russia, political revolution in Hungary (1919), widespread political upheaval and revolution in Mexico (191020), the end of the Qing dynasty in China (1911), and the post-World War I partitioning of the vast Ottoman Empire. Art historians often use the term Modernism (with a capital M) to refer to the loose collection of movements, manifestos, journals, and projects (planned and less frequently realized) that arose in response to the tensions of the era. The champions of Modernism argued that the future they envisioned demanded a rejection of, rather than continuity with, the past whether in aesthetic, social, or political terms. As a result of architectural exhibitions in Stuttgart (1927) and New York (1932), Modernism became recognized as an international style, based upon principles believed by their designers and advocates to be universal (see here), independent from the national contexts in which they emerged as well as from subjective expression and the commercial practices of fashion. While we may refer to Modernism as a movement in design, it was not unified. There were differences and contradictions among various Modernist individuals and groups as well as areas where distinctions between Art Deco and Modernism became quite fuzzy, for instance, both embraced physical fitness, exercise, and hygiene, and associated casual clothing, and had an aesthetic preference for elemental geometry linked at times to machine-like precision. But polemical rhetoric was often a Modernist strategy, imparting a sense of urgency, opposition, and missionary zeal to efforts that aimed to reinvent society and redefine human values and behavior, constructing images of the new man and new woman and their place in the future mechanized world. The seeds of Modernism were sown in revolutionary talk and nourished by manifestos and exhibitions that demonstrated impatience with the status quo, but again, the politics of Modernism were quite varied, veering at times to the left, center, and right. Imagining and designing utopia was one thing, but realizing the future was another, and might require a concentration of power in revolutionary or elected governments in the 1920s, faith in autocratic dictators and the power of propaganda in the 1930s, or compromise aiming to achieve more modest goals within existing capitalist economic structures. ## Futurism In the years both preceding and immediately following World War I, a number of artistdesigners in Italy turned their attention to machines, factories, and cities rather than to nature as sources of inspiration for their work. They developed a new visual language that embodied the dynamism of air, railroad, and automobile transportation, and heroized the factory and industrial workers who operated machines, provided the physical labor that built the infrastructure of the modern industrial city, and devoted their leisure time to dance halls and weekend sports. There are numerous references in the poetry of Italian poet Filippo Tommaso Marinetti (18761944) to the particular beauty and excitement of machinery as symbols of speed, freedom, and the harbingers of revolutionary change, suggested in a passage from his Manifesto of Futurism, published in the French newspaper Le Figaro on February 20, 1909: We declare that the splendor of the world has been enriched by a new beauty  the beauty of speed. A racing car with its bonnet draped with exhaust-pipes like firebreathing serpents  a roaring racing car, rattling along like a machine gun, is more beautiful than the winged victory of Samothrace. We will sing of the stirring of great crowds  workers, pleasure-seekers, rioters  and the confused sea of color and sounds as revolution sweeps through a modern metropolis. We will sing the midnight fervor of arsenals and shipyards blazing with electric moons; insatiable stations swallowing the smoking of the smoke; bridges flashing like knives in the sun, giant gymnasts that leap over rivers; adventurous steamers that scent the horizon; deep chested locomotives that paw the ground with their wheels, like stallions harnessed with steel tubing; the easy flight of aero-planes, their propellers beating the wind like banners, with a sound like the applause of a mighty crowd. Futurist artists derived their strategies for visualizing the excitement of speed and change from the fragmentation and spatial dislocations of Cubism (see here and fig. 8.7) as well as the experiments of Jules Marey (18301904) and others with time-lapse or chronophotography, a technique that showed movement as a sequence of overlapping and abstracted linear patterns. In the medium of painting, efforts resulted in the frenetic rhythms in works by artists such as Gino Severini (18831966) and Giacomo Balla (18711958), and were the inspiration for American artist Earl Horters (1880 1940) painting Rhapsody in Blue (1927), reproduced as an advertisement for Steinway pianos (see fig. 10.15). Crossing the boundary from fine to applied art was a natural extension of Futurism. Marinetti was a tireless activist in promoting the Futurist cause, using the popular press as a vehicle for communicating his ideas in print, and addressing the reader with an aggressive, imperative tone borrowed from the persuasive techniques found in contemporary print advertising. Bright electric street lamps, the internal combustion engine, and the wonders of early air travel suggested a spirit of liberation that removed barriers to the possible, whether the conquest of darkness or of space. Such developments in turn called forth new techniques of expression in art that dematerialized form and demanded freedom from convention and tradition, pitting the liberated future against a stagnant, moribund past, the latter impeding the progress of the former. Marinetti hoped to move beyond the salon, the gallery, the museum, and the refuge of the bourgeois domestic interior, identifying with and appealing to an audience of industrial workers and youth in factories, on streets and athletic fields who exulted in the vitality of the city and reveled in its intoxicating state of flux. He praised Italys large industrial manufacturers as paving the way to the future, including the Pirelli tire company, Olivetti typewriters, and the Agnelli family, one of the founders of the Fiat motor company. One might say that the industrial proletariat were both the inspiration and the imagined market for the Futurists  a youthful public who might mobilize to tear down and supersede all that was traditional and static, both in art and in the status quo that maintained existing political and social institutions and resisted radical change. Marinettis tone was revolutionary, utopian, and apocalyptic: building the future and tearing down the past went together. While rooted in poetry and finding an outlet in the fine arts, his vision for Futurism extended to visual communication and other areas of design in the political sphere during World War I and in its turbulent aftermath in Italy. Marinettis medium for spreading the gospel of Futurism to the masses was the written word, not as phonetic symbol but as expressive visual image. In his poetry he built upon the lead of Symbolist poet Stéphane Mallarmé (18421898) in arranging type on the page to visually convey the meaning of words or sensation of sounds. Different sizes of type within the same line, and different fonts within the same word, liberated the printed page from its usual directional order and traditional standard spacing. An example is the poem At Night in Her Bed from a published collection entitled Les mots en liberté futuristes (Futurist Words in Liberty) dating to 1919 (fig. 9.1), describing a letter from an artillery soldier to his girlfriend, who lies supine at the bottom right of the page. Sounds of gunfire disrupt the handwritten words into fragments, to which are haphazardly added abstract shapes and words such as explosion and simultaneity. The readers attention shifts from place to place without clear or prescribed organization aside from a tendency for the eye to wander downward. While earlier examples of Marinettis Futurist poetry operate within the regularizing framework of parallel vertical and horizontal spacing of letterpress typography, At Night in Her Bed appears to have been composed somewhat like a collage, with portions of cut-out text pasted on the page along with other cut-out shapes and handwritten words. The collage was then photographed and exposed as a negative on to a metal plate for offset printing. This technology permitted far greater freedom for typographic experimentation, and was employed by graphic designers increasingly in the later 1920s (see page208 onward), often with a freer but still hierarchical order of information. Just as Western painters in the early twentieth century were challenging the window as a paradigm for the art of painting, experimental poets were challenging the grid of the type form and printed page as a paradigm for the written word: in Marinettis printed poetry, words and letters are not just the equivalent of spoken words but operate visually as images and symbols; they are meant to be seen rather than read. ![](images/21e358aae3958b8d4819f78d274b5492fe9ab0c2a0b642a061377788a94e1f8e.jpg) Filippo Tommaso Marinetti, “At Night in Her Bed,” fold-out poem, 15 × 10 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>8</sub> in (38.1 × 25.9 cm), from Les mots en liberté futuristes, Milan, 1919. From 1912, Futurist painter Giacomo Balla took an active interest in fashion (mainly mens) and published treatises on the subject beginning in 1914. Asymmetrical, geometric, conical, spiral, and elliptical patterns printed on or applied to clothing mirrored the fast-paced rhythms of urban life as seen in a vest with appliqué patterns designed by Balla and made in 1915, or a drawing of the so-called anti-neutral suit of 1914, designed by Balla for Futurist poet Francesco Cangiullo (18841977) to wear and to attract attention at demonstrations supporting Italys entry into World War I (fig. 9.2; at first aligned with the Central Powers but remaining neutral at the outbreak of war in August 1914, Italy joined the war on the side of Britain, France, and Russia in 1915, hoping to gain territory then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire). According to Balla, modern clothing should avoid faded and murky colors and symmetry, strive to liberate emotion and action by being aggressive, agile, and dynamic, and should permit free movement. Balla and other Futurists appreciated the fashion worlds seasonal changes that provided continuous opportunities for creativity for designers and excitement for consumers. The Futurists attraction to consumer goods differed from views on fashion by other Modernists who argued for permanent, universal standards for designed products based upon function that might mold a more collective future society. In other media, Futurist artists Tullio dAlbisola (born Mozotti, 18911971) and Giacomo Balla each designed earthenware vessels and plates decorated with speeding motorcyclists or colorful asymmetrical shapes in the 1920s, manufactured in clay-rich areas of Italy traditionally known for ceramic manufacturing. ![](images/17f867750026bb024ce67c126bfc9cd50289a0d41f5562cee3d7cc882232dade.jpg) Giacomo Balla, illustration of the “anti-neutral suit,” Futurist Manifesto, published in Volantino della Direzione del Monumenti Futuristi, 1914, Milan. The Futurists formed their own political party in 1918 to promote the role of modern art and design in Italy, and Marinetti and other Futurists supported the government of Italian fascist military dictator Benito Mussolini (18831945) who seized power in late October, 1922, and ruled Italy for the next twenty years. Nationalism, militarism, machismo, and faith in modern industrial technology as an instrument of change drew the two movements together, along with impatience toward slower, more democratic efforts to bring about economic, technological, and social progress in Italy. Futurist artist Fortunato Depero (18921960) installed a room of his work (Salone Depero) at an exhibition in Monza in 1923 and again at the 1925 Paris International Exposition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts (though not in the Italian Pavilion), and promoted his services as a graphic designer through the publishing company Dinamo Azari in Milan in the 1920s. He was unsuccessful in securing work from Pirelli, but designed advertising for the Italian distiller Campari, manufacturer of a refreshing rose-colored aperitif, along with the single-shot, carbonated version of Campari whose conical bottle echoes the shapes Depero used in his advertisements. For Campari, Depero created a series of high-contrast, black-and-white newspaper advertisements without copy or product representation that made use of simplified angular shapes for letterforms, the cone-shaped drinking glass, and a mechanical, toylike addressing figure (fig. 9.3). The product name in this example is composed of letters in descending size, resembling a megaphone and visually corresponding to the reverberating blare of a shouting vendor. Deperos playfulness and visual puns were part of a strategy that was only marginally political, suggesting that the visual arts could contribute to instilling Futurist attitudes and behaviors in viewers and consumers. He was the co-author, with Balla, of a treatise entitled Futurist Reconstruction of the Universe (1915) arguing that Futurist toys would foster imagination and spontaneity, and included the following advice for adults: The Futurist toy will be very useful to adults too, keeping them young, agile, jubilant, spontaneous, ready for anything, untiring, instinctive and intuitive. While perhaps not the direct call to political activism envisioned by Marinetti, the Futurists expressive manipulation of letterforms and the nervous energy generated by sharp contrasts and the rhythmic repetitions of geometric shapes found an outlet in advertising in the later 1920s, communicating the liberating spirit of a Futurist sensibility to a popular audience. The Wolfsonian Florida International University, Miami Beach. ## France: L’Esprit Nouveau and Purism While the Société des Artistes Décorateurs (S.A.D.), the couture fashion industry, and other luxury boutiques and pavilions dominated Frances contributions to the 1925 International Exposition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts in Paris (see here), a more practically oriented, functional approach to design and modern living also emerged, whether in Elsa Schiaparellis casual summer and sporting outfits (see fig. 8.22) or Pierre Chareaus and Francis Jourdains spartan (if still exclusive) Physical Culture Room for S.A.D.s model French Embassy (see fig. 8.23). ![](images/78541b7650bbeaed25b9fcc8353a1325e6e049eb5e669b76dc8da21fb4b73bd3.jpg) Le Corbusier, Pavillon de l’Esprit Nouveau, Paris, 1925. Equally austere but more ambitious than these examples was a model unit for lowcost housing titled the Pavillon de lEsprit Nouveau, designed by Swiss-born architect Le Corbusier (18871965). This structure was the most radical French expression of new attitudes toward design and the application of construction technology to modern living to emerge after World War I, combining a commitment to the use of standardized, prefabricated modern building materials and construction with a rejection of almost all decoration as a vestige of outmoded craft methods of production (fig. 9.5). Housing shortages after World War I, sympathy for returning war veterans (and the millions whose lives were sacrificed along with civilian casualties), and concerns over social justice all provided an impetus for designers to imagine and pursue a broader, inclusive vision for design in shaping the nations future with renewed urgency. The furnishings of the Pavillon included bentwood chairs and modular built-in storage units as well as tables supported by thin, straight, dowel-like legs that merged living, dining, and study areas into a single continuous space. Modularity was a key concept for Le Corbusier, permitting flexible units of space that could be repeated to construct living spaces on an industrial scale for affordable public housing; the concept also played a role in the architects Unité dHabitation project in Marseilles after World War II (see here and fig. 11.34). While visionary in terms of its proposed mass scale, much of the apartments furniture was available commercially through office-supply catalogs (rather than for homeowners; but for the exhibition, the pieces were custom-built to better conform to the units compact proportions and the size of the doors), and the Thonet bentwood chairs were more commonly found in cafés, theatres, and restaurants than in domestic living- or dining-room spaces. Le Corbusier preferred the term equipment rather than furniture, with chairs defined as machines for sitting. After 1925, Le Corbusier teamed with interior designer Charlotte Perriand (19031999) to design original furniture using tubular steel; examples include the swivel or rotating chair, based upon square and circular shapes and emphasizing lightness and transparency (fig. 9.6). ![](images/a1cc0809236ce5f45be8dccc148d30618593085b159d8b2fbdf5cc28d393c98e.jpg) Charlotte Perriand, Le Corbusier, and Pierre Jeanneret, Swivel chair, chrome-plated tubular steel and leather, manufactured by Thonet Frères, Paris, France, 29 ¾ × 22 × 21 ¼ in (75.4 × 55.9 × 54 cm), 1928. Museum of Modern Art New York. Le Corbusier thought of furniture and furnishings as type-objects, that is, basic, anonymous forms that developed naturally from a process of gradual refinement based upon utility and economy  the beauty of such objects was not the result of added or integrated decoration but rather from an appreciation of their purity and permanence. His affinity for the typical shares much with the notion of Typisierung embraced by members of the German Werkbund (see here) whose exhibitions Le Corbusier praised. The architect used the term mechanical selection to describe his approach, asserting design as an inevitable process leading to final forms, conducive to mass production for a variety of man-made products. Described as a machine for living in, and compared to similar descriptions of automobiles as machines for transportation and airplanes as machines for flying, Le Corbusiers Pavillon de lEsprit Nouveau was an appropriate model for modern living, based upon the simple fulfillment of universal functional requirements rather than luxury and individual expression in the traditional home or apartment. Satisfying those requirements depended more upon the skills and working methods of the engineer and the practical sensibility of the modern architect than upon the methods of fine artists  a rebuke to the values of the Société des Artistes Décorateurs. As with earlier design reform initiatives, architecture provided a framework for Le Corbusiers functional standards: structural steel and expanses of glass had rendered load-bearing walls unnecessary, and reinforced concrete was a more efficient and industrial building material than traditional stone or brick. As an example of product design as the inevitable outcome of technical progress, he offered the industrially manufactured wine bottle, a type object that fulfilled its function without need of decorative embellishment, operating efficiently and independently from fashion or fine art. Le Corbusier linked his approach to design with the modern accomplishments of engineers in the construction of ocean liners, airplanes, roadsters, and other products of modern technology, recommending prefabricated mechanized mass production as the solution to meet urgent housing needs in the aftermath of World War I. His polemical tone emerged in his 1923 book Vers une architecture (Towards a New Architecture), which concludes with the rhetorical question Architecture or Revolution? His hope, it appears, was that government investment and support from industrial manufacturers would combine to fund and implement his proposal for lowcost housing on a mass scale in order to stave off social unrest and political instability in France. Some manufacturers and large building-supply companies helped to underwrite the cost of Le Corbusiers pavilion and recognized economic advantages to standardization, but the architects vision for modern design conflicted with the traditional practice of most manufacturers, retailers, and consumers in a competitive commercial market. For Le Corbusier, designers should search for underlying, elementary forms based upon the type-object principle, not subject to the whims of fashion, the traditional role of decoration, or the elitism of artist-designers. Along these lines, he and Jourdain had reprinted Adolf Looss 1908 Ornament and Crime essay in a 1920 issue of their L’esprit nouveau journal (see here). The Pavillon de lEsprit Nouveau and the pavilion of the Soviet Union at the 1925 Paris International Exhibition provide a sense of competing design visions for shaping the experience of modern living that would intersect throughout the interwar period. The definition of progress varied, as did the relationship between design and technology. Technology might serve as the vehicle for the mechanized mass production of standardized goods to satisfy the perceived living requirements of the many, or equally as a tool for aesthetic experiment and invention, or metaphorically for the liberation of the individual from traditional values and lifestyles. Each interpretation might be considered modern, and all were part, at times, of a polarized design discourse during the interwar period. The projects and ideas generated by Le Corbusier gained adherents during the midand later 1920s. Le Corbusier and Amédée Ozenfant (18861966) coined the term Purism to refer to the simplified abstract paintings they produced, beginning c. 1918, and Le Corbusier published essays about art, architecture, and design in the journal L’esprit nouveau, co-edited with Ozenfant and poet Paul Dermée (18861951). In their writings, the authors explored pairs of polar concepts such as intuition and logic, the eye and the mind, the individual and the universal to argue for the existence of permanent standards for beauty in all forms of art, defined as harmony, proportion, and clarity, and independent of fashion. They claimed that manufactured goods, including architectural construction, were governed by principles of logic and economy, determined as it were by the law of mechanical selection analogous to the process of natural selection proposed in Darwins theory of evolution, and resulting in an elemental aesthetic purity and functional efficiency, along with equal attention given to space as well as mass, solid as well as void: From all this [previous discussion and observations] comes a fundamental conclusion: that respect for the laws of physics and of economy has in every age created highly selected objects; that these artificial objects obey the same laws as the products of natural selection and that, consequently, there thus reigns a total harmony, bringing together the only two things that interest the human being: himself and what he makes. Despite polemical rhetoric and the presentation of selective evidence, the principle of mechanical selection was largely symbolic: aside from ship cabins, railway compartments, and the standardized office furniture ordered (and modified) by Le Corbusier to furnish the Pavillon de lEsprit Nouveau, neither Le Corbusiers logic nor claims of universality had much appeal beyond private, custom commissions for wealthy clients who shared the aesthetic rather than radical social and political aspirations of LEsprit Nouveau. ## The U. A. M. In the years following the Paris exhibition a group of designers including Le Corbusier, Jourdain, Chareau, and even Puiforcat (whose use of precious materials precluded his designs from reaching all but the very wealthy) questioned S.A.Ds focus upon luxury and the organizations perceived failure to consider the broader social benefits of industrial materials and standardized mass production, in short entering the debate over the relationship between art and industry. Polarized attitudes and claims were more theoretical than actual in many ways, since the aims of department stores and other retail distributors of Art Moderne were certainly to promote goods beyond an exclusive market, and the work of designers such as Puiforcat and Chareau was often produced on commission for wealthy clients. Nevertheless, in 1929 a group of dissenters split with S.A.D. and formed their own design organization known as the Union des Artistes Modernes (U.A.M.), displaying their efforts at several exhibitions during the 1930s. The work of U.A.M members centered around factory assembly, industrial materials, the use of interchangeable parts, and eliminating overt signs of specialized skilled craftsmanship associated with decoration. The group does not seem to have articulated a particular social or political agenda, nor collaborated with manufacturers or governments on projects for low-cost social housing. The tall dressing cabinet, dating between 1926 and 1929, and designed by Irish-born designer and U.A.M. member Eileen Gray (1878 1976) for her villa at Roquebrune on the Mediterranean coast, illustrates one example. Constructed of aluminum, cork, glass, and cellulose (fig. 9.7), the cabinet also functioned, in place of walls, to divide the homes master bedroom from the bathroom for privacy, and to complement other fitted shelving and tables that created more open interior space. Vitra Design Museum, Germany. ## De Stijl De Stijl (The Style) was the title of a journal published in the Netherlands, founded in 1917 by painter-designer Theo van Doesburg (18831931). Contributors included Dutch painters Piet Mondrian (18721944) and Vilmos Huszar (18841960), Belgian sculptor George Vantongerloo (18861965), Dutch furniture-maker and later architect Gerrit Rietveld (18881964), and architects Robert van t Hoff (1887 1979), J. J. P. Oud (18901963), and Jan Wils (18911972). Although De Stijl designs were not exhibited in the Dutch pavilion at the 1925 Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes, designs by Van Doesburg, Rietveld, and other artists associated with De Stijl were shown in 1923 at the Galerie de lEffort Moderne owned by art collector and dealer Léonce Rosenberg (18791947), where they were seen by Le Corbusier. Furthermore, Mondrian lived and worked in Paris both before and after World War I, and Van Doesburg spent time in Weimar, interacting there with faculty and students at the Bauhaus in the early 1920s (see here onward). The term movement can only be used loosely in connection with De Stijl: no group exhibitions of De Stijl were ever held, and maintaining shared ideals and a spirit of collaboration among its participants proved challenging, especially in light of Van Doesburgs strong personality. Indeed, for one reason or another many contributors to early issues of De Stijl severed their relationship with the journal over differences with Van Doesburg during the course of its eleven-year history. Then again it could be argued that neither the U.A.M. or the Bauhaus maintained consistent, unified approaches to design. The manifesto published in an early issue of De Stijl cites individualism and nationalism as the causes of the conflicts that led Europe to World War I, and proposed a new balance between the individual and a new, universal world view, with modern art in the vanguard: There is an old and a new consciousness of time. The old is connected with the individual. The new is connected with the universal. The struggle of the individual against the universal is revealing itself in the world war as well as in the art of the present day. The war is destroying the old world with its contents: individual domination in every state. The new art has brought forward what the new consciousness of time contains: a balance between the universal and the individual. Despite the radical rhetoric of the De Stijl manifesto, the work of the journals contributors recalls the work of Dutch architect Henrik Berlage (see here), who had used the phrase unity in diversity to advocate a balance between unique and standardized elements in modern architecture and design. Another Dutch architect, J. L. Mathieu Lauweriks (18641932), promoted designs based upon carefully constructed geometric compositions governed by mathematical relationships of parallel and perpendicular planes. As noted by Philip Meggs, Lauwerikss influence as a member of the faculty at the Düsseldorf School of Arts and Crafts may be seen in the consistent and standardized layouts for promotional material developed in Germany by Peter Behrens for the AEG (see fig. 7.4). Berlage, in turn, praised the designs of Frank Lloyd Wright (see here). The American architects use of flat roofs, wide overhanging eaves, open living spaces, and the interpenetration of interior and exterior space were circulated through print publication and were admired in the Netherlands and by De Stijl contributors. Dutch designers incorporated elements of this approach in many of their own architectural projects. They extended the principle analogously to the equality between figure and ground in painting and between solid and void in sculpture and architecture; that is, in each of these media they noted the active role of negative space as an aesthetic principle, with built-in furniture replacing standing cabinets, furniture that reduced mass, and sliding partitions that created more flexible living spaces in open interiors. Blurring the boundaries between fine and applied arts by suggesting their unity through underlying formal principles was a fundamental tenet of De Stijl, demonstrating the ultimate integration of painting, sculpture, typography, furniture, interior ensembles, and architecture. For Van Doesburg, furniture was sculpture for the interior, and architecture a sort of walk-in painting, which placed the viewer within painting instead of in front of it and thereby enable[d] him to participate in it, that is, to integrate aesthetic experience into the acts of everyday living. Mondrian expressed such ideas in utopian language, writing that architecture and the applied arts would more fully realize what painting had achieved in its own but more limited way, that is, the attainment and enjoyment of harmony and balance, based upon the rejection of representation and naturalism, and achieving an equilibrium among elementary rectangular shapes of primary color created by the intersection of black strips of uniform width. Mondrians numerous essays on this topic employ dualities similar to the writings of Ozenfant and Le Corbusier on Purism (see here), though without explicit reference to engineering or mechanical selection. Paired terms such as individual/universal, conscious/unconscious, subjective/objective, and mutable/immutable argue for equilibration between opposites, realized through the interplay of positive and negative space and the asymmetrical balance of rectilinear elements in walls, floors, furniture, and windows. Mondrian referred to his new conception of art as Neoplasticism or the New Plastic, and anticipated, through metaphor, its application to the built environment in essays published in De Stijl and elsewhere: The new spirit must be manifested in all the arts without exception ... As soon as one art becomes plastic expression of the abstract, the others can no longer remain plastic expressions of the natural. The two do not go together: from this comes their mutual hostility down to the present. The New Plastic abolishes this antagonism: it creates the unity of all the arts... Our age has reached the climax of individualism: the mature individual can now increasingly find equilibrium with the universal. When our mentality actually attains this equilibrium, it will also be clearly expressed in every aspect of outward life, just as it is expressed abstractly in the new plastic. Mondrian also insisted that there should be no distinction between art and life, that the work of art should not be separate from living, an idea articulated in an essay of 1923. Before proceeding to develop the theme that both the machine and the urban environment are demonstrations of progress from the natural to the abstract, he writes: Indeed, the evolution of art consists in its achievement of a pure expression of harmony: art appears only outwardly, as an expression that (in time) reduces individual feeling. Thus art is both the expression and (involuntarily) the means of material evolution: the achievement of equilibrium between nature and non-nature  between what is in us and what is around us. Art will remain both expression and means of expression until (relative) equilibrium is reached. Then its task will be fulfilled and harmony will be realized in our outward surroundings and in our outward life. Perhaps the best-known De Stijl project is the Schröder House in Utrecht, built in 1924 to 1925 for a widow (Mrs. Truus Schröder-Schräder) and her three children, and designed by Gerritt Rietveld (figs. 9.14 and 9.15). Schröder-Schräder contributed many suggestions that Rietveld incorporated into the design, including the use of folding and sliding partitions that moved on tracks cut into the floor. Rietvelds open design was meant to encourage shared family time after Schröder-Schräders husband died and to limit the isolation of family members as they dealt with loss. With its geometric areas of floors, furniture, and window moldings painted in bright blue, red, and yellow, projecting balconies with tubular-steel railings, a flat roof, expanses of square and rectangular glass and corner windows to suggest continuity between the built and natural environment, rectangular slab construction (actually brick-faced with cement rather than standardized prefabricated units of reinforced concrete), the Schröder House fulfilled many of the principles embraced in De Stijl, in particular the attempt to create a living work of non-objective art. According to art historian Paul Overy, Schröder-Schräder was concerned that the furniture be simply constructed (like the Red-Blue chair, assembled rather than requiring specialized craft skills) and that the décor specifically avoid associations with luxury. And while the proposed use of reinforced concrete suggested economical industrial building materials and modular prefabrication, the house was in a sense customized to meet specific individual family needs, and, through color and its vocabulary of rectilinear elements, promote aesthetic experience rather than serve as a model for low-cost public housing. Beginning in 1931 Rietveld built a series of privately funded housing units only a short distance from the Schröder House. Known as Erasmuslaan, these connected units did not employ color, used steel, concrete, and glass, and were designed with a large open interior living space sparsely furnished with lightweight tubular-steel chairs and tables designed by Rietveld along with fitted shelving (fig. 9.16), and are still occupied today. ![](images/3ddb33a3e805185c957c50716c6c7ae7ed847c5bf35bbf235d252f25dd60540b.jpg) 9.14 Gerrit Rietveld, Schröder House, Utrecht, 1924–1925. ![](images/f30c7a51862ea7581613527c30927d42bfbf8933b3ff5e8d77d4dc4596011520.jpg) Gerrit Rietveld, first-floor interior of Schröder House, Utrecht, 1924–1925, restored 1985–1987, photographed 1987. ![](images/5587b179f248fd9e13ebd81ba04468cfb556f67545bd5da486bc266fa50e34d9.jpg) Gerrit Rietveld, interior, Housing Block Erasmuslaan 9, Utrecht, The Netherlands, 1931. These included the Soviet Pavilion designed by architect Konstantin Melnikov (18901974), which projected a distinct modern identity for the new Russian communist state in contrast to the majority of displays on view (see here onward). The Workers Club was a rectangular reading room containing two long, plain wooden tables with hinged leaves that tilted to hold newspapers and journals, and a series of chairs with curved, open backs assembled from eight standardized components painted in gray and white to match other furnishings in the room and lacking carved, painted, or inlaid decoration. Rodchenko also designed and built a pair of armchairs, each with a shelf to the side that could be attached to form a chess table when the chairs were placed facing one another. The room included a moveable platform with an elevated speakers rostrum, folding table for displaying pamphlets and books, and a folding screen for mounting posters or projecting films. The Workers Club was a shared communal rather than private space, whose design aimed to define the leisure time of industrial workers, eschewing the overt signs of personal comfort, luxury, and skilled craftsmanship. Working-class leisure consisted of reading materials, posters, film, and a speakers rostrum that promoted communist working-class values following the Russian Revolution. The austerity of the Workers Club was relieved by a lively and bright color scheme of white, red, and gray that helped to create an inviting modern interior space. ![](images/6694b63bae1f24917a3940520a4a6da9103d9952cda47f3884f7cbe7a523b243.jpg) Aleksandr Rodchenko, Soviet Workers Club, 1925, reconstructed at Tate Modern, London, as part of the Defining Constructivism exhibition, 2009. The Workers Club was a mature product of Constructivism, one of several evolving movements initiated by artists who wished to participate directly in the reshaping a new Soviet society following the revolutions of February and October 1917, and the Bolshevik victory in the civil wars that ended in 1920. In 1918 the new communist Russian government established a ministry of culture known as the Peoples Commissariat of Enlightenment (Narkompros) under the direction of Anatoly Lunacharsky (18751933), a Communist Party official as well as playwright who was tolerant of experimental artistic ideas in the belief that they could contribute to the molding of public opinion for the new Bolshevik government. Narkompros and its various institutes and schools replaced Russias more academic, exclusive, and conservative pre-revolutionary predecessors, permitting the participation of progressive artists (including women and Jews) to put into practice utopian projects that linked nonobjective abstract art with working-class revolution and the promise of a new enlightened society that would replace the vast autocratic Tsarist Empire. Many revolutionary artists shared the goal of liberating the masses from the tyranny of the past, and for a time they affirmed together the hope that art and design would radically reshape the values and conditions of modern life. Founded upon Marxist ideology, revolution was viewed as the inevitable outcome of the working-class struggle against capitalist greed, inequality, and oppression. Achieving social justice might require sacrifice, and all efforts were directed toward imagining a future society in which shared collective goals replaced individual aspirations for personal wealth and luxury. Despite years of civil war and a devastating famine that claimed millions of lives between 1917 and 1921, by 1924 the new Soviet Union had reoccupied territories lost during World War I, was recognized as a sovereign state internationally, supported nascent communist parties abroad as part of an international communist workers movement, and embraced the use of propaganda to promote its goals, create an identity, and inspire support among the working classes in other nations through publications and participation in international exhibitions. As with the other Modernist movements included in this chapter, abstraction played a significant symbolic role in the early formation of Constructivism, as the rejection of representation signified a radical break with the past, a clean slate upon which to construct a new, utopian future. Abstractions most articulate spokesman in Russia both before and after the revolution was Kasimir Malevich (18781935), who used the term Suprematism to refer to his non-objective style of painting. Malevich defined Suprematism as the expression of feeling in art, and noted the formal similarity between the simple geometric squares and rectangles of his canvases and the forms of modern technology and industry in their transcendence of organic naturalism. Malevichs paintings manipulated abstract geometric elements to suggest states of mind, seen in titles for his works such as Suprematist Composition Expressing the Feeling of Movement and Restraint or Suprematist Composition Expressing Magnetic Attraction. He encouraged the integration of art into life, and through his association with the art organization renamed UNOVIS (Affirmers of the New Art) in 1920 and a Peoples Art School (1919) located in Vitebsk helped to coordinate efforts to use Suprematist compositions for signs and banners on tram cars and for the embellishment of temporary architectural constructions such as theater stages and speaker platforms, often referred to as agitprop. An example is Nikolai Suetins (18971954) design for a tram car in Vitebsk, consisting of the slogan Long Live the Revolution accompanied by a bold red circle (based upon Suetins sketches) and a darker wedge shape to attract attention (fig. 9.18). The expanded agenda for art within UNOVIS most likely prompted members such as Vasily Kandinsky (18661944), who had pioneered an expressive abstract nonobjective style in Germany before World War I and returned to Russia after the revolution, to adopt a more precise geometric vocabulary in his paintings, and to approach abstraction analytically as well as intuitively, experimenting with the psychological effects of shapes and colors. According to art historian Christina Lodder, Kandinsky even conducted a survey of his colleagues to study their psychological responses to colors and other formal elements of design. ![](images/b3ca267c992b69b51d2d72a8cb0c528c00bfeb726a8e08cdb860f2d52fdc1606.jpg) Nikolai Suetin, design for a tram car, photograph, Vitebsk, 1918. Malevich used the term collective creative art to promote Suprematism as an agent of change, and encouraged UNOVIS artists to exhibit works anonymously. The idea that a poster, banner, costume, or set design could function as both propaganda and as nonobjective abstract art suggests Suprematisms underlying aesthetic basis: the experience of non-objectivity might be found on the street as well as in the gallery or the home. In a similar vein Vladimir Tatlin (18851953) used ordinary industrial and mass-produced materials to create abstract collages, where works of art become objects and are grouped together with tools and other commodities as equal elements of the everyday proletariat environment. Strongly influenced by examples of Cubist collage that he had seen in Paris as early as 1913, Tatlins work after the Russian Revolution combined ordinary materials such as wood, metal, and stucco in abstract relief compositions. Although hardly functional, such experiments, sometimes referred to by their creators as laboratory work, were intended to provide a basis for future utilitarian products that reinforced an abstract aesthetic sensibility. The collage technique emphasized the imagination of the constructor rather than sophisticated artistic skills and suggested that the making of art could not only be appreciated widely but also made by everyone, that is, a liberating, democratic art. In the decorative arts, the former Russian State Porcelain Manufactory came under the control of the new Soviet government after the revolution, and a new group of revolutionary artists joined the designers and artisans at the manufactory. In addition to continuing to produce more traditional work, the manufactory introduced Suprematist designs by Malevich, Kandinsky, and Suetin as propaganda. Sergei Chekhonins plate from 1918 (fig. 9.19) offers a compromise, with the Soviet Unions hammer and sickle symbolizing agriculture and industry, along with a semicircular cog amid a floral background, suggesting an integration of Russias traditional agrarian economy and vast peasant population and the increasing role of industry and its urban workforce who constituted the future focus of optimistic post-revolutionary economic planning. The survival of Suprematist porcelain in museum collections today is due in part to its export and display at exhibitions abroad. ![](images/a0a1a0e3d05695fda89cf309d9ff85718d19e388bbe34a779ae8228b1dac4e89.jpg) ## 9.19 Sergei Chekhonin, plate, blue emblem with flowers, diameter: 9 <sup>2</sup>/<sub>3</sub> in (24.5 cm), 1918. Malevichs efforts were closely associated with an organization known as INKHUK (the Institute of Artistic Culture) in Moscow beginning in 1920, and with an exhibition that took place there in 1921 known as the Third Obmokhu, named for the Society of Young Artists. From these organizations a Working Group of Constructivist Artists was formed. Constructivists viewed their efforts as being more in line with Marxist ideology that embraced a materialist view of culture (as opposed to a spiritual or aesthetic view) as most appropriate and urgent to address the collective needs of a new working-class society. The emphasis upon production among the artists at INKHUK created a tension with those who felt that imposing practical restrictions in design represented too narrow an approach compared with earlier interpretations of Constructivism that focused upon the integration of art and life. As a result of this growing conflict concerning the role of artists in the new communist state, some artists left Russia for the West, including Kandinsky (who joined the faculty of the Bauhaus, see here onward) and sculptor Naum Gabo (18901977), who also moved to Germany in 1922. INKHUK members became actively involved in the Higher State Technical Workshops, established by the Communist Party in 1920 to further integrate fine artists with production. By this time Rodchenko had come to question the compatibility of painting and sculpture with the evolving practical direction of Constructivism. He promoted the training of artist-constructors, a new kind of versatile and non-specialist creator who might integrate a new vocabulary of simple geometric shapes with prototypes for the efficient manufacture of clothing, furniture, lighting, architecture, and other basic goods and furnishings for the working class. Rodchenkos wife Varvara Stepanova (18941958) designed lightweight two-piece athletic outfits, advocating sport as a healthy, active communal leisure activity for working-class men and women (fig. 9.20). The loose-fitting garments allow for freedom of movement with colorful geometric designs in both the top and the shorts angling inward at the waist. It is to this phase of Constructivism that Rodchenkos Soviet Workers Club at the 1925 Paris exposition belongs (fig. 9.17), designing and furnishing spaces to house appropriate leisure activity for the new men and women of the collective, egalitarian future. The designers role was envisioned as more akin to the methods of a draftsman than those of the fine artist. The furnishings were designed to employ modern industrially manufactured materials and a minimum number of standardized parts. ![](images/982e0ec4e97a1a9904815a85c7b334a72d54c50530f0982b767ee983440c1962.jpg) Evgenia Sokolova (Zhemchuzhnaya) wearing an athletic outfit designed by Varvara Stepanova, photograph, 1923. Photograph by Alexander Mikhailovich Rodchenko. Despite being designed with mechanized mass production in mind, the furnishings for the Workers Club were one-of-a-kind products, and few if any prototypes were produced by industrial manufacturers, whose activities were hampered by a depressed economy and the lack of necessary investment and support to produce the new designs. It is unlikely that workers were consulted in Rodchenkos design process that focused upon an imagined, utopian future. To judge from the indifferent reaction to Rodchenkos project within Vkutemas and later attempts to replace traditional representational textile designs with decorative patterns based upon themes of industrialization and collectivization (fig. 9.25), the public (and eventually the Communist Partys) reception of Constructivist design was equivocal. In addition a number of younger artists and former Constructivists during the early 1920s had turned (or re-turned) to realism. These artists embraced easel painting and an idealized naturalistic style as appropriate to an art of mass persuasion to build support for official policy along more traditional lines, employing techniques consistent with narrative illustration and advertising; in short, propaganda. By the mid-1920s, tolerance for the utopian pretensions of abstract art and Constructivism during the early years of Narkompros had diminished. Increasingly Soviet artists were encouraged to comply with a state policy demanding an idealized naturalism (or socialist realism) depicting working class, athletic, or military activities designed to provide hopeful images of heroic individual and collective achievement to gain the support of the Russian masses. In this less tolerant environment, artists such as Rodchenko and El Lissitzky (the latter working primarily in Germany) continued to produce graphic designs using photography that still permitted a measure of experimentation while satisfying state oversight and the conformity it required. El Lissitzky (born Lazar Markovich Lissitzky; 18901941) studied architecture in Germany and later worked with Malevich (as well as Marc Chagall) at the Peoples Art School in Vitebsk, marshaling abstraction as an effective means to communicate confidence in Soviet strength and industrial progress, primarily through publishing and exhibition design. He directed a short-lived program in architecture at UNOVIS, and later created his imaginative series of PROUNS (acronym signifying for the new art), a group of paintings and lithographs composed of abstract geometric shapes with an ambiguous suggestion of perspective and three-dimensional projection to suggest future applications to the built environment. El Lissitzky referred to PROUNS as stations of interchange between architecture and painting, affirming the utilitarian possibilities of geometric abstraction. His identification of artists with engineers and workers was almost a commonplace among Constructivists both in Russia and elsewhere in Europe. Some of El Lissitzkys best-known contributions to Constructivism were in graphic design, showcasing the ability of print to communicate ideas visually and dynamically connect text with symbols and photographic images. His work includes a book of poetry by the revolutionary poet Vladimir Mayakovsky (18931930) entitled Dyla Golossa (For the Voice or To Be Read Out Loud), designed for public performance and audience participation. ![](images/973469cf43845ed7fb15a8f0d77e23a6f3126c2c0eb5e7bd7762c5b08e30795a.jpg) El Lissitzky, cover for Vesch/Objet/Gegenstand, 12 ${ ^ 5 } / _ { 1 6 } \times 9 { ^ 5 } / _ { 1 6 }$ in (31.3 × 23.5 cm), Berlin, 1922. In 1922 El Lissitzky began to publish an international journal of the new art entitled Vesch/Objet/Gegenstand. Translated simply as Object in English, the journal featured articles and photographs of works of fine art, decorative art, and industrial products, including projects by De Stijl artists as well as by Le Corbusier (work by El Lissitzky was featured in a 1922 issue of De Stijl as well). The use of half-tone photographic reproductions rather than etchings or engraving conveys Lissitzkys interest in promoting photomechanical process rather than craft-based techniques as a form of mass communication. The cover of one of the journals issues from 1922 (fig. 9.21) reinforces abstract relationships between bold sans serif typography, asymmetrical compositions of related geometric shapes, and industrial forms, in this case a locomotive with a cowcatcher diagonally connecting to the Russian word Vesch. The vertically and diagonally oriented letters, horizontal and vertical printers rules used as separation, and use of different letter sizes recall the typographical experimentation of the Futurist poet Marinetti (see fig. 9.1). But whereas Marinetti used such elements to make the page into a kind of poetic image to communicate a frenzied sensibility characteristic of the dislocations and simultaneity of modern life, Lissitzky and other Russian Constructivists tempered such explosive dynamism with discipline in order to communicate clearly, quickly, and dramatically. This required attention to page layout, in which text, symbol, and image worked together to actively engage and direct the viewers attention. For the international 1928 Pressa (Press) exhibition in Cologne, El Lissitzky designed the USSRs display: it featured a photomontage and accompanying fold-out booklet documenting the latest innovations in the speed, volume, and distribution of printing juxtaposed with images of groups of industrial workers, the Red Army, and party leaders, often photographed from below as if on pedestals or above as part of public demonstrations of military might and solidarity. Klutsiss posters often employed official and often-reproduced photographs of modern machinery with dramatic changes in scale to stimulate eye movement as well as to communicate increase in production, as in a poster for the development of transport from 1929 (fig. 9.23). Klutsis juxtaposes a diminutive figure of a camel in front of a modern locomotive to contrast past with present, with smaller locomotives suggesting stages in the progress of transportation as a growth chart. The red star and use of red behind the other engines refer to Russia, while the diagonal letter points to the future to reinforce the visual hierarchy in a dynamic asymmetrical arrangement. Constructivists saw photography as inherently modern because it was both reproducible and mechanical rather than being tied to illustration and fine art. Stalins tight control of propaganda in the Soviet Union beginning in the later 1920s made it difficult for Constructivists to pursue their experimental approach to graphic design. While slogans proclaiming faith in the industrialized future remained, idealized illustrations of workers and peasants co-existed with photography and the dynamic asymmetrical compositions that closely related text and image more interactively. The more illustrational approach is usually referred to as socialist realism and represents a form of communication designed to reach the Russian masses, but was decidedly not the kind of modern abstract visual language that inspired the Constructivists, whose efforts were marginalized. In some cases artists were seen as threats to the state: for unknown reasons, Gustav Klutsis was arrested in 1938 and executed three weeks later. The overthrow of the Tsarist Empire and success of the Bolshevik Revolution provided the political context for artists to design and implement utopian projects with state support. A lasting legacy of Constructivism was in graphic and exhibition design: photomechanical reproduction, photomontage, dynamic asymmetrical compositions, striking photography made possible by portable cameras, the integration of type and image, all were marshaled as propaganda to communicate a collective consciousness for a new social order, inspired by faith in Marxist revolutionary ideology, mechanized mass production, and the state-controlled economic planning that would realize its promise. The graphic design strategies developed by Rodchenko, El Lissitzky, Klutsis, and others contributed to Jan Tschicholds New Typography, where they served a far wider variety of purposes that included business communication and advertising. ## The Bauhaus The Soviet Workers Club at the 1925 Paris exposition was but one indication of the international reach of Constructivist activities: El Lissitzkys essays on modern design were published in issues of De Stijl, and the short-lived journal Vesch/Objet/Gegenstand contained texts by Van Doesburg, Mondrian, Le Corbusier, and other designers advocating the use of reinforced concrete, tubular metal, and standardized mechanized industrial production. These ideas also found expression in a school of design established in Weimar, Germany, known as the Bauhaus. The Bauhaus (from the German verb bauen; to build) opened in 1919 from the consolidation of Weimars Academy of Fine Arts and the citys Arts and Craft School (Kunstgewerbeschule), both of which had closed during World War I. The schools first director was Walter Gropius (18831969), an architect who had worked in the office of Peter Behrens prior to the war. Gropiuss architectural commissions included the well-known Fagus shoe-last factory at Alfeld an der Leine near Hildesheim, designed in 1911 in collaboration with Adolf Meyer (18811929), and often viewed as an early example of modern steel-frame construction, exhibiting large expanses of plate-glass, curtain walls, and an absence of traditional moldings and decoration. Gropius was also active in the Deutscher Werkbund and had designed railroad cars with efficiently equipped standardized compartments and fitted furniture. Within the Werkbund, however, he supported artistic freedom and individuality rather than Typisierung and the industrial logic advocated by Hermann Muthesius (see here). Composed during the liberal Weimar Republic that governed Germany after World War I from 1918 until Adolf Hitlers appointment as German Chancellor in 1933, the idealistic text of the Bauhaus manifesto (1919) shows little interest in specific standards for design or a particular application of industrial technology, and is concerned instead with championing the collaboration of artist and craftsman, pointing to the medieval building as a collaborative model to overcome the opposition between fine and applied art and to create a living art integrated into rather than separated from life: Architects, painters, sculptors, we must all return to crafts! For there is no such thing as professional art. There is no essential difference between the artist and the craftsman. The artist is an exalted craftsman. By the grace of Heaven and in rare moments of inspiration which transcend the will, art may unconsciously blossom from the labor of his hand, but a foundation of handicraft is essential for every artist. It is there that the primary source of creativity lies. Let us therefore create a new guild of craftsmen without the class-distinctions that raise an arrogant barrier between craftsman and artist! Let us together desire, conceive and create the new building of the future, which will combine everything  architecture and sculpture and painting  all in a single form which will one day rise towards the heavens from the hands of a million workers as the crystalline symbol of a new and coming faith. The model of the medieval guild, and with it the idealistic legacy of William Morris and the Arts and Crafts movement, emerges in this strongly utopian passage in which the products and activities of design will transform both the material and more broadly human conditions of society. The guild model also emerges in Gropiuss practice of calling the Bauhaus faculty masters rather than professors, and in the manifestos cover illustration for the school, featuring a woodcut by early faculty appointment Lyonel Feininger (18711956), depicting a church bathed in the light of three stars, symbolizing perhaps the collaboration and essential unity of painting, sculpture, and architecture in the Gothic cathedral. Such ideas formed the founding basis of the philosophy of education at the Bauhaus, even though architecture was initially not among the fields of study offered at the school in its early Weimar years. Although Gropius inherited a small number of faculty from the Weimar Academy of Fine Arts, many of them were hostile to the revamped curriculum for the Bauhaus and lobbied successfully to maintain their independence from the new school. As a result the director was able to appoint several new masters for the school. The new faculty were almost exclusively fine artists, who embraced abstraction and freedom of expression as the basis for art, but who at the same time were committed to a less elitist role for artists that encouraged the integration of art and life. The shared goal among the faculty and director seems to have been a healthy collaboration between artist and craftsman in educating new designers to reinvigorate the production of useful goods culminating in the construction and interior design of buildings, informed and conditioned by the discipline and unity implied in the architectural enterprise, and contributing to German economic recovery and cultural renewal. Faculty appointments in 1919 included Johannes Itten (18881967), Gerhard Marcks (18891981), and Feininger, as well as painters Paul Klee (18791940) in 1921 and Kandinsky (see here) in 1922. Kandinsky had worked as an artist and teacher in Russia after the 1917 revolution, but departed for Germany late in 1921. Klee was born in Switzerland but studied painting in Munich, and it is difficult to believe that he was unaware of the artistic milieu of that city in the heyday of the Munich Secession and Jugendstil (see here). His views on the social role of the decorative arts in promoting a broad-based audience for aesthetic ideas emerges in a letter he wrote to a fellow artist in 1919, revealing the charged political climate of the time: Of course, exaggerated, individualist art is not suitable for the general public  it is a capitalist luxury. But I think we are more than just curiosities for wealthy snobs. And all that in us which is in some way or the other is striving for eternity would be more likely to be encouraged in a communist community ... this new art would grasp craftsmanship and bring forth a great new flowering. For there would no longer be academies, but only art schools for craftsmen. A key element in the curriculum of the Bauhaus was a required common first semester of study (Vorkurs) before students selected one or more areas of specialization in the Bauhaus workshops (ceramics, glass painting, mural painting, furniture, metalwork, textiles). Munich-trained painter and printmaker Georg Muche (18951987) assisted with the Vorkurs, and other mandatory but non-specialized courses in form and theory were taught by Klee and Kandinsky. The purpose of the Vorkurs under the direction of Swiss-born artist and educator Johannes Itten was to encourage students direct contact with materials and textures, drawing techniques, and forms so that their innate creative energy might be best directed toward study in a specialized workshop. Surviving examples of student work demonstrate contrasts of textures among different materials in an abstract composition, drawings from life that exaggerate a sense of rhythm in nature, and underlying formal relationships among geometric shapes in the analysis of works of art. While male students might choose freely among areas of specialization following the Vorkurs, women were almost always directed toward the weaving workshop, viewed by the faculty as appropriate for female students (only with difficulty did Marianne Brandt obtain permission to pursue her apprenticeship in the metal workshop). In fact, so strict was the gender bias that a separate Vorkurs was taught to the women students at the Bauhaus. One of the important elements of the Bauhaus Vorkurs was its role as a foundation for all of the workshops. Implied is the notion of an underlying basis connecting all of the arts that might be applied to a variety of forms in architecture, ceramics, metalwork, furniture, or printing: principles that had their basis in elementary, abstract geometric forms and that culminated in the built environment. This intellectual or even spiritual part of the Bauhaus curriculum was balanced by later specialization in the workshops, but even then a Form master (fine artist) collaborated with a technical master to provide continued guidance to students. Bauhaus graduate Grete Marks (18991990) owned and operated a ceramic workshop in Marwitz, Germany (near Berlin, but now Marwice in western Poland), with her husband and brother-in-law (both were killed in an automobile accident in 1928), named the Häel Workshops for Artistic Ceramics from 1923 until she left Germany for Jerusalem in 1933 (after Hitler was named Chancellor), moving to Britain in 1936 where she continued to design and manufacture modern ceramics. Until the later 1920s the workshop was a successful commercial enterprise, employing as many as 120 workers. Scholars have been careful to point out that the early Vorkurs did not seek only to familiarize students with the underlying elements of all the arts, but also to draw connections between artistic activity and the discovery of unconscious reality. Itten practiced a form of mysticism known as Mazdaznan, conducted breathing exercises, and imposed a vegetarian diet for the schools kitchen. Not all students (or faculty) adhered to the Mazdaznanian (as well as racist) elements of Ittens teaching that appear to have ended with his dismissal in 1923. Despite the fact that a non-specialized preliminary course had been introduced in some form earlier by Peter Behrens in the Düsseldorf Kunstgewerbeschule and also formed part of the curriculum in design education in Soviet Russia at INKHUK, the preliminary or foundation course tends to be associated with the Bauhaus. This is perhaps a result of the emigration of Bauhaus faculty and their students to the United States and Britain in the 1930s, where the concept became institutionalized as a cornerstone of design education after World War II. Design education was envisioned as a problem-solving activity, unified by faith in the underlying basis of all artistic activity, balancing creativity and experimentation with technical and aesthetic decisions based upon a consideration of materials and production. The foundation model for art and design education persists to this day in the United States and worldwide, though it has been increasingly challenged by designers and educators who believe its basis in craft as well as the notion of designers being able to do it all are both anachronistic and ill-suited to address urgent needs for action in the face of social and environmental threats that pose impediments to progress in the twenty-first century. Results from the Bauhaus in the early Weimar years may be summarized in the construction and interior design and decoration for the Sommerfeld House in Berlin, a residence (now destroyed) commissioned privately from Gropius but constructed with the collaboration of the Bauhaus workshops (fig. 9.28). The client was shipping industrialist Adolf Sommerfeld, who provided a large quantity of teak wood normally used in boat building; the project demonstrates the early Bauhaus emphasis upon craft techniques and the integration of construction and original abstract decoration. The horizontal emphasis of the elevation and the strongly projecting eaves of the roof of the Sommerfeld House recall the Prairie houses of Frank Lloyd Wright (see here). The doorway and staircase of the entrance area are decorated with wooden relief panels that develop the angular construction of the teak paneling, exploring relations of geometric forms in two and three dimensions, as well as a variety of textures created by sanding and chiseling. These motifs, in either regular patterns or asymmetrically balanced compositions, are repeated in a series of leaded glass windows behind the staircase as well as in embroidered appliqué curtains, all produced in the Bauhaus workshops. Like Wrights Prairie houses, the Sommerfeld House remains strongly based on handicraft despite employing abstract geometric shapes rather than organic forms in decoration. Despite the collaborative effort on the Sommerfeld House and some limited commercial success in the weaving and ceramics workshops, the Bauhaus struggled for adequate funding from the Weimar government and battled local resistance directed at its unorthodox approach to applied arts education. In addition, the school was criticized by designers more aligned with Constructivism who felt the schools methods and products were allied too closely to craft production, rather than to mechanized industrial production in the service of more efficient, socially responsible modern living. Detractors claimed that the Bauhaus produced the kind of individual and indulgent design that was at variance with the inclusive aims expressed in the 1919 manifesto. One of the most vehement attacks appeared in De Stijl. The journals editor, Theo van Doesburg, moved to Weimar in 1921 and attended an International Congress of Constructivism held there in 1922. Although Van Doesburg never officially taught at the Bauhaus, he was acquainted with Gropius who seems at one time to have considered the Dutch artist for a teaching position. Gropius was sensitive to criticisms of the schools shortcomings. He had already been in contact with Dutch architect J. J. P. In response to financial, political, and ideological pressures, Gropius began to shift the focus of the Bauhaus mission and curriculum. One of the most important changes that took place was Gropiuss decision in 1923 to force the resignation of Itten and to hire Hungarian painter and Constructivist László Moholy-Nagy (18951946) as his replacement. The two men had met in Berlin, where Moholy-Nagy was living, having left Hungary following the nations short-lived 1919 communist revolution. Moholy-Nagy took responsibility for the Vorkurs and for a more industrial focus that began to inform Bauhaus education and activities. He brought with him a strong commitment to mechanized mass production and new material technology in design, reducing the importance of craft specialization and traditional workshop training. To emphasize the point, in what has become part of the canonical literature of Modernism, Moholy-Nagy designed a series of abstract geometric paintings and communicated instructions by telephone to assistants to have them produced. His efforts parallel Rodchenkos call for a new conception of the designer as the artist-constructor rather than artist-craftsman, and Moholys series of paintings share a vocabulary of intersecting planes floating in a projected space similar to El Lissitzkys series of PROUNS (see here). The new approach de-emphasized unconscious and spiritual associations with the creative process. In their place, Moholy-Nagy identified the artist with the engineer, creating designs for factory production, promoting aesthetic unity and control through the use of modules and the grid. Moholy-Nagy placed photography on an equal footing with the fine arts and experimented with kinetic sculpture. He praised the machine as a liberating force both in creative and in social terms: Constructivism is not confined to the picture frame and the pedestal. In 1928 Gropius resigned as director of the Bauhaus, naming the Swiss-born Hannes Meyer (18891954), who served as professor of architecture, as his successor. Meyer was an unpopular choice at the outset. His approach to design was more narrowly circumscribed than his predecessor, proposing that design was a product of function × economy. He placed design solely in the service of universal working-class needs based upon the use of economic materials and efficiency in standardized production, to be brought to fruition through participation of the workshops in real projects for low-cost public housing and home furnishings. Meyer tended to view design at the Bauhaus as a largely deterministic process, resulting more or less automatically from considerations of climate, hygiene, economics, and engineering. His approach marginalized the value of fine art in the design process, forcing the resignation of several members of the faculty. Ironically, under Meyers directorship, the Bauhaus became more commercially successful and financially independent through commissions and sales of its prototypes to manufacturers, including wallpapers, textiles, advertising, and lighting. Bauhaus designs produced under his directorship (regardless of his political ideology) found a niche in a capitalist consumer economy in Germany, were reproduced in fashion magazines and were manufactured at the Bauhaus workshops or companies who purchased rights to produce and market them. Although equally committed to technology and the social mission of the Bauhaus, Moholy-Nagy resigned quickly after Meyers appointment as director. Moholy-Nagy held that modern design required an accommodation to standards and uniformity to maintain a sense of social responsibility, but that versatility and more open-ended exploration of new technology and materials preserved a necessary sense of intuition and creative expression in the design process. In Moholy-Nagys view, versatility and experiment helped to relieve the fear of mindless conformity to dehumanizing factory work and narrow specialization. Until the end of his career, he maintained the value of a creative role for design and design education rather than how that value might be compromised too heavily by more pragmatic economic or social considerations, whether consumerist or collective. The differences between Meyer and Moholy demonstrate that Modernism was far from a unified movement, but was rather a loose collection of ideas with differences, contradictions, and strongly held, often polemic, opinions among its theorists and practitioners. Was the Modern designer an engineer? A worker? Or an artist? Today perhaps we need not choose among such options. Yet the stakeholders in these debates were often unbending, as the stakes were felt to be high: shaping a future with little or no reference to the past, proposing solutions to urgent social problems on a vast scale in a climate of political unrest that often seemed to demand positions of firm authority rather than democratic compromise and reform. Despite inconsistencies and ambiguities, the term Modernism remains part of the literature and history of design, tied to many of the most canonical names, institutions, and iconic products of the first half of the twentieth century. But even in the politically volatile interwar period, there was a gap between intention and public reception of Modernist movements and their designs, and the meaning of undecorated products and furnishings, and the efficient use of space was in constant flux. Perhaps the most enduring aspect of interwar Modernism was not the trajectory of its various groups and organizations, but rather its flexibility, an ability to be adopted, and adapted, to a variety of individual, social, and political agendas. In time Meyers position became untenable. Gropius had always attempted to avoid political confrontation with government officials in the interests of the schools survival, but Meyers leftist political views (despite his accommodations of commercial considerations) were radical; under his directorship, political theory was taught as a classroom subject and a communist student group was formed. Nationalist sentiments in Germany raised government suspicions toward Bauhaus politics. Meyer resigned in 1930, relocating to Soviet Russia, where he hoped his design activities and principles would find more ready acceptance. His place was taken by the well-established German architect Ludwig Mies van der Rohe (18861969), who moved the school from Dessau to facilities in Berlin. In 1933, the National Socialist (Nazi) Party came to power in Germany and the Weimar Republic ended. This governments strong anti-communist position continued to cast suspicion upon the international sympathies of the Bauhaus despite Miess efforts to solicit architectural commissions from the Nazi government. The schools association with left-wing radical politics was linked to the influence of foreigners and Jews, bringing about its demise, 14 years after its opening in 1919, coinciding with the rise and fall of the Weimar Republic. The importance of the Bauhaus is not the result of its success in developing prototypes for industrial production, which was limited, but rather of the compelling nature of its ambitious ideal of collaboration between art, craft, and industry in the education of designers. This contribution is perhaps best exemplified in the career of Moholy-Nagy, who is remembered less for particular masterpieces of art or commercially successful industrial products than for his embodiment of the new and versatile artist-worker-engineer, engaged in the creative process of design to address a broad range of human and social needs. Also the emigration of Bauhaus faculty to other colleges and universities outside of Germany, the respect for many of its faculty and students among institutions such as New Yorks fledgling Museum of Modern Art, and its celebration in countless exhibitions and anniversaries (most recently the centenary of its founding) have all added to the continuing power of a Bauhaus image that may vary somewhat from the institutions actual history and underwent considerable change in the 14 years of its existence. Nevertheless, and in the face of calls for the revision of its craft-based curriculum, the Bauhaus continues to exert an influence upon postsecondary education in the visual arts and design, where many of its features, including some sort of common core, still form the basis of art and design curricula. Despite a recognition that the school perhaps never realized the utopian vision of its directors and spokespersons, the Bauhaus provided an environment in which artists were encouraged to design for industrial production and to shape and enhance the experience of modern living; the boldness of that effort has remained a significant legacy. ## Beyond the Bauhaus A small number of Bauhaus faculty remained in Germany after the schools closing in 1933 and designed products as consultants to industrial manufacturers. Despite closing the Bauhaus in 1933, the Third Reich benefitted from the efforts of former students and faculty who remained in Germany and from aspects of its approach to industrial design. Like the Volkswagen, the Peoples Radio was a government-sponsored initiative that served the needs of the working class with a basic, affordable, standardized manufactured product using modern materials that provided mass access to the Führers broadcasts and limited access to other programming  a combination of entertainment, propaganda, and censorship. Taylors theory of scientific management, maximizing productivity in factory work through the study of movement in the performance of a variety of tasks (see here). Books such as American author Christine Fredericks Scientific Management in the Home (1915) proposed that the placement of sinks, cabinets, serving tables, ovens, refrigerators, and work surfaces should derive from a systematic study of food preparation, food service, and storage, defining a new dimension to research-based design that might be coupled with industrial production (see here). Fredericks research runs parallel to the success of Henry Ford and was adopted in Germany and elsewhere. Housing shortages after World War I and a sluggish economy suggested to local officials in some areas that new construction should maximize efficiency and fulfill affordable minimum requirements for working-class families, and that manufacturers should adopt standards based upon such requirements. In an era when fewer families could afford servants, a rational approach to housework reduced its menial connotations and added an element of science and cultural progress to tasks and responsibilities usually assumed by women. Indeed, home economics was once a required subject for young women in high schools across the United States. In addition to efficiency, the designs for kitchens and office furnishings considered the effect of glare and brightness in lighting as well as posture in the design of furniture, maintaining some commitment to design standards based upon considerations of human factors. One of the goals of such research was to make objective criteria the basis for a science or engineering rather than an art of design, circumscribing the role of subjective expression. Adopting such practical considerations for design often required the backing of local governments, as in the city of Frankfurt in the late 1920s, when Ernst May (1886 1970) held the position of City Architect for the Social Democratic Party that was in power under the Weimar Republic at the time. May designated inexpensive land on the outskirts of the city for the construction of housing estates to address urgent needs for affordable housing built on a large industrial scale, using prefabrication, flat roofs, zigzag elevations, and purpose-built beds and cabinets, seen, for instance, at Bruchfeldstrasse, built in 1925. Eventually more than 15,000 housing units were built under Mays tenure. One influential result of housing in the city was the Frankfurt kitchen (fig. 9.38) designed under the direction of Grete Schütte-Lihotzky (1898 2000). The kitchen (a mere 6.2 × 11.2 ft.) featured moveable track lighting and continuous work surfaces with built-in storage cabinets and sliding doors. The contracts for low-cost, Existenzminimum housing (minimum existence: a term used to emphasize new standards in housing design and a vast improvement over older, more cramped dwellings) enabled designs for furniture and fittings to be mass-produced inexpensively in large quantities according to standard measurements. Much as European steel companies invested in the manufacture of tubular-metal furniture, large industrial type foundries in Europe and the United States expanded their range of new typefaces in the interwar period to promote their products to the commercial printing industry. Lifestyle magazines, such as Vogue (first published in 1892), incorporated photography and asymmetrical layouts to present contemporary fashion and leisure pastimes for the modern, active woman, independent of radical social or political agendas, and industrial manufacturers adopted photomontage, striking layouts, and new approaches to information design to promote their products. The absence of serifs and other calligraphic features of typography, as well as the integration of type and image, and the reductive language of pictograms, all had a symbolic meaning for the movements associated with Modernism in the interwar period. For instance, the sans serif typefaces designed at the Bauhaus by Bayer (see fig. 9.32) embodied the principle of generating a variety of letterforms from a limited number of related modular units as a metaphor for standardized mechanized production, even at times at the expense of legibility. The major foundries had introduced versions of sans serif faces primarily for newspaper headlines and advertising around the turn of the century, contemporary with the Werkbund in Germany and the introduction of efficiency standards in other industries, for instance, in the sizes of pipefittings or electrical wiring, adapters, and sockets. In printing, this effort also produced national standards for envelope and paper sizes, invoices, and other common business needs, including the dimensions of office filing cabinets. In Germany these reforms took place under the auspices of the national Institute for Standards, known as the DIN (Deutsches Institut für Normung), founded in Berlin in 1917. Terminology in the field of typography is at times confusing, as the sans serif faces were known in Europe as Grotesk and in the United States as Gothic. Franklin Gothic (fig. 9.42) was designed for the American Type Founders Company (ATF) and cut by Morris Benton between 1903 and 1905. Although it lacks serifs, Franklin Gothic retains Latin letterforms for the characters a and g, and rounded letterforms narrow as they approach vertical stems rather than being of a uniform width (or weight) throughout. In Germany, the Berthold Foundry of Berlin introduced the closely related Akzidenz Grotesk font at about the same time. Developing sans serif fonts for more widespread use required control of letterforms, spacing, and weight. The commercial foundries addressed concerns of legibility and precision to meet the varied printing needs of the market. Foundries developed large, extended families of fonts in a variety of weights as well as in expanded and condensed forms. In 1927 Munich-based typographer and teacher Paul Renner (18781956) introduced the influential sans serif typeface Futura, including bold, light, condensed, and expanded variations demanded by the commercial printing industry (fig. 9.44). The font was produced by the Bauer Foundry in Frankfurt. Futura is known as a modular sans serif because several of its letterforms share the same element, for instance, the circular lower-case o is used for the letters a, b, d, e, p, and q; and geometric because all strokes are of a single width (also known as monoweight). The modular principle is certainly inherent in earlier experimental typefaces but was applied with greater attention to legibility and the effects produced by modern automatic typesetting equipment. Futuras modernity derives, of course, from its name, from the precision of its modular, geometric rather than calligraphic letterforms, approximating symbolic associations with interchangeability and mechanized production. Other foundries responded with their own versions of modular sans serifs typefaces in Germany; for instance, Rudolf Kochs Kabel (Cable) for the Klingspor Foundry in 1927 (fig. 9.45). One difference between the Futura and Kabel typefaces was Kochs preservation of features of the Latin alphabet, for instance, the a as well as the slanted crossbar of the e, a characteristic found in the earliest Venetian fonts of the later fifteenth century. Despite the identification between sans serif and Modernism in typography, this type of letterform has a history going back to early nineteenth-century display faces, renewed with expanded technology and the explosion of mass-circulation printed materials in newspapers, magazines, and catalogs requiring headlines in the early twentieth century. Tschichold was trained in calligraphy in Leipzig, using his skills to design display lettering in newspaper advertising, and brand names for products and services. As a young man he visited the Bauhaus exhibition in Weimar in 1923 (see fig. 9.29). Tschichold was enthusiastic about the printing on view there, and saw sans serif typography and asymmetrical page design as exciting and effective means toward modern and direct communication of information to readers for a variety of printing applications, including advertising. He embraced a new approach and discipline for the composition of text and image without traditional decorative borders and flourishes. The trademarks he developed from the later 1920s onward, often commissioned by publishing companies, appear somewhat conservative by present-day standards, but reveal an ability to combine letterforms and abbreviated, recognizable images into unified pictographic symbols, as seen in an example for the Bücherkreis (Book Circle) of Berlin (fig. 9.46). In a manifesto published in 1925 and again in book form in 1927 as The New Typography, Tschichold questioned traditional approaches to printing and advocated principles of clear and direct communication favoring the use of sans serif typography, half-tone reproduction of photographs rather than hand-drawn illustration, and asymmetrical layout. In The New Typography, Tschichold explained the Modern context for a disciplined approach that defined the emerging profession of graphic design: Every part of a text relates to every other part by a definite, logical relationship of emphasis and value, predetermined by content. It is up to the typographer to express this relationship clearly and visibly, through type sizes and weight, arrangement of lines, use of color, photography... He also explained the rationale for asymmetry in light of stimulating eye movement for the modern viewer who is often too busy for leisurely reading and demands a more active engagement with printed material for effective communication: Reading presupposes eye movement. The New Typography so designs text matter that the eye is led from one word and one group of words to the next. So a logical organization of the text is needed, through the use of different type-sizes, placing in relation to space, color, etc. For posters, rectangular elements relating to the frame dominate, while the use of diagonal type and shapes, or other deviations from the strict discipline of the sans serif letters, create tension and arouse interest (fig. 9.47). Tschicholds New Typography and related articles published in trade journals provided practical information for printers to adapt asymmetrical layout and an ordering of elements based upon content within the structure of a geometric grid to the needs of title pages, stationery, announcements, invitations, and other prosaic printing tasks, known as jobbing. The grid, still based upon the process of letterpress printing, ensured precise relationships between parallel and perpendicular elements on the page. Elements of this approach became the basis for consistent identities developed for corporations working on an international scale and for Swiss graphic design after World War II. The New Typography imposed a certain degree of conformity upon the designer, but still required careful adjustment rather than the rote application of a formula. As a result, Tschicholds designs, much like Mondrians abstract paintings, have an inherent sense of rightness in their abstract balance and in the feeling for the relationship among the various elements of the page in relation to a desired hierarchy of information. The guidelines for the New Typography could be listed as rules or even described as laws, but they could not be programmed; in addition to the logical and effective communication of information, the abstract aesthetic pleasure of a New Typography poster also offered an independent, aesthetic level of appreciation to the viewer. The reproduction of photographs opened up new possibilities for the printing industry and provided the basis for much experimentation. In many ways, book and type designers in England in the interwar period continued the tradition of great typographers from the eighteenth century described above and revived by William Morris in the late nineteenth century. Chapter 9 introduces many canonical examples of modern design in the twentieth century. Throughout the chapter the words international and universal appear, for instance, in the name of Herbert Bayers universal typeface (see fig. 9.32) or the title of the Museum of Modern Arts 1932 exhibition of architectural models whose curators identified and promoted an international style (see here). But just what did these designers and critics mean by these terms? Were they thinking or projecting globally? Given the selection of examples from the MoMA exhibition and Bayers Latin letterforms, one might be wary of employing either international or universal to their efforts. Despite universal pretensions and an avowed rejection of the past and nationalism, the expressions of the international style were not only varied (see above on Scandinavia) but also deeply Eurocentric, stemming from a faith in a particular and selective adaptation of industrial technology, minimizing difference and offering a solution to resolve Western polemics, whether between fine and applied art, art and engineering, or individual and society. Such distinctions do not always apply in non-Western cultures, and they tend to ignore, for instance, Chinese furniture as a source of inspiration for the furniture of Danish designer Kaare Klint. ## Chapter 10 Design, Industry, and Advertising in the United States In the United States, craft-based industries tended to remain conservative in terms of design until the mid-1920s, unaffected by the self-consciously modern aesthetic approaches of Art Deco designers in France and elsewhere in Europe. Following World War I, the United States pursued a policy of isolationism, limiting earlier waves of immigrants amid fears of social unrest and communist agitation in the immediate wake of the Russian Revolution. On the recommendation of then Secretary of Commerce Herbert Hoover, the United States declined to participate in the 1925 Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes in Paris because, in Hoovers opinion, manufacturers were unable to comply with the conditions that specified displays of objects that were modern and original. The United States did, however, dispatch a large delegation to the exhibition, headed by the President of the American Association of Museums, Charles Richards, whose charge included organizing a traveling exhibition of works selected from the Paris exhibition. The traveling exhibition, and others sponsored by institutions such as the Metropolitan Museum of Art, stimulated public and commercial interest in newer designs, aimed mostly at a sophisticated audience. By the later 1920s, ensembles of interior furnishings by Jean Dunand and other French designers of modern luxury goods could be seen in the windows of New York department stores such as Lord and Taylor and Macys. American retailers and advertising executives began to recognize and publicize the commercial benefit of contemporary artistic designs in a wide range of consumer goods. Museums promoted modern styles along broadly educational lines, emphasizing the integration of art into the lives of the middle class and the ability of the decorative arts to raise the level of public taste and contribute to cultural uplift. This general receptivity to modern design in the United States was accompanied by a wave of émigré designers from Europe including Peter Muller-Munk (19041967; Germany), Raymond Loewy (18931986; France), Ilonka Kárász (18961981; Hungary), and Alexey Brodovitch (18981971; Russia, by way of Paris) who began successful careers as consultants in a wide range of media from the mid-1920s. In addition, magazines such as Vogue and Vanity Fair used photography and illustration to promote an idealized image of modern life associated with fashion, sport, travel, entertainment, and other active leisure activities. Whether contemporary or traditional, consumers embraced design in the United States in the 1920s as proof of material and social progress, and the connection between design, progress, and modern life was strongly reinforced through advertising. Mechanized mass production extended to the manufacture of household appliances in the 1920s, and was admired almost without reservation by capitalist as well as socialist governments in Europe and the Soviet Union. Standardized production vehicles such as the Model T Ford and electric appliances demonstrated the social benefits offered by machine production and assembly, extending the comforts and conveniences of modern living, formerly reserved for the wealthy, to the mass market through technology with less concern for individual consumer choice. Such products exemplified the democratization of culture and widespread improvement in the general standard of living. The Ford Company opened an automobile factory in Germany in 1924, and by 1929 the United States was responsible for more than 80 percent of automobile production worldwide. American industrialization was seen as the basis for prosperity and economic growth. Praise of business, the machine, and the assembly line had overtly religious overtones: skyscrapers were dubbed cathedrals of commerce, and Calvin Coolidge commented: The factory is a temple  the worker worships there. In the Soviet Union, Communist Party observers also praised the assembly line but criticized private ownership of the means of production, the exploitation of workers, and the uneven distribution of wealth under capitalism. As argued above (see here), in the Soviet Union industrialization was linked with collectivization, requiring sacrifice of individual ownership in exchange for higher levels of production and equal distribution of its benefits for all by the state, to be realized through ambitious five-year plans. The revolutionary success of the Ford Motor Company took place within a decade, and by 1927, when its production halted, more than 15 million Model T Fords had rolled off the assembly line, virtually identical in their black lacquer finish and traditional carriage-based body form. Sometimes dubbed Fordism, this approach to industrial production was summarized by Henry Ford himself, with echoes of Adam Smiths encomium to the division of labor in a pin manufactory (see here): The way to make automobiles is to make one automobile like another automobile, to make them all alike, to make them come from the factory all alike, just like one pin is like another when it comes from a pin factory. The Model T suppressed elements of individual choice, artistic invention, and product differentiation associated with art and design  all effort concentrated upon the reduction of cost and the acceleration of routine and worker productivity in the plant under the strict supervision of management under pressure to meet ambitious production quotas. Capital investment in equipment and the rationalization of labor mitigated against product differentiation; and, as pointed out by several observers, this combination of factors produced lower costs. In 1916 the Model T sold for \$360, less than one-half its cost in 1910, and well below the cost of other domestic automobile manufacturers; in 1919 the cost for each model was \$265! Without real competition in the marketplace, Fordism succeeded in delivering on the promise for personal transportation to an expanding middle- and working-class market. And productivity was hardly limited to the automobiles themselves: service stations, paved roads, real-estate prices in vacation areas, and mechanics for service and repair represent only a handful of ancillary industries and services that blossomed as the direct result of the Model Ts success. Unlike the German Werkbund, there was no movement or organization in the United States to forge an Industry Culture that considered a unified or national approach to the aesthetics of the products of mechanized mass production. To help offset the pressures of the assembly line and the unrest caused by accelerated production schedules and the repetitive routine of the assembly line, Ford introduced a profit-sharing plan labeled the five-dollar day for workers in 1914. In time, and through credit-purchase, factory workers were able to own their own Model T, turning workers into mass consumers. Further concessions in the form of reduced working hours and benefits to labor occurred as the result of union organizing in the interwar years. After World War II, paid vacations and a government-sponsored interstate highway system contributed to the emergence of a robust tourist industry. As more Americans became owners of second cars and as the automobile became integrated into the leisure as well as work activities of the public, comfort, luxury, and styling increasingly influenced consumers decision to purchase new vehicles. General Motors, formed in 1918 through the consolidation of a number of individual manufacturers under the leadership of its new president, Alfred P. Sloan (18751966), responded to these consumer trends by establishing stronger ties to its retail dealers. In time Sloans strategy was adopted by Ford, who could not fail to notice that market saturation and competition had slowed the pace of Model T purchases and that usedcar sales were beginning to outstrip demand for new cars (after all, older Fords were virtually identical to the most recent models). General Motors began to distinguish their vehicles first through the introduction of variety in color, made possible by new enamel paints that dried almost as quickly as the standard black lacquer. The company also introduced a series of minor styling changes and options for buyers to customize their purchases. These are first seen in the 1923 Chevrolet Superior (fig. 10.13), the contours of which appear more unified and compact than those of Fords Model T. General Motors also invested more heavily in advertising as a means to stimulate sales, a strategy with important ramifications that are more thoroughly discussed below. In short, the automobile became not simply a means of transportation but, like fashion and other consumer goods for the home or for clothing, a reflection of personal taste, individual identity, and a means to measure self-improvement. ![](images/651e669bc6294cba490a2775e8731c8549e2cf9e0da53ce5df97aa60a82964ee.jpg) Chevrolet Superior, 1923. General Motors Corporation, Flint, Michigan. ## Advertising, Art, and the Selling of Modern Design in the United States The history of modern design in the United States in the interwar period cannot be written without stressing the importance of advertising in the formation of a culture of consumption. The overwhelming achievements of materials technology and mechanized mass-production technologies could not be sustained without exciting desire and channeling it through the sale of products. Advertising functioned to erode traditional resistance to spending based upon the virtue of self-denial grounded in religious faith and social obligation. Loosening the social constraints on leisure and consumption is among the themes of English author Elizabeth van Arnims 1922 novel The Enchanted April, in which a young married woman persuades an acquaintance to join her in renting an Italian villa (without their husbands) to escape dreary winter weather at home for the freedom to enjoy the pleasures of a fragrant springtime by the Mediterranean, enticed by a newspaper advertisement that began To those who appreciate wisteria and sunshine. Before the advent of television, the tools of advertising included commercial radio as well as print communication and its effective, provocative use of text, illustration, photography, layout, and typography. Museum initiatives supporting modern decorative arts and a closer connection between fine and applied art dovetailed with the strategies of advertising agencies to build and strengthen the link between products and the hedonistic lifestyle of confident and energetic participants in the ever-changing modern scene. In the 1920s, advertisements were decidedly upscale, promising charisma and success in exchange for the purchase of a wide variety of products ranging from toothpaste to toasters. An example is a 1923 advertisement from American Vogue for a Dodge Brothers automobile (fig. 10.14). The copy mentions the speed of the vehicle as well as the enthusiastic response of hunters, golfers, and polo players for the car, which acquired its very own aggressive personality to match that of the prospective owner: Wielders of the polo mallet, hunters of the moose, hard hitters from the tee  are enthusiastic about this car. For them its virility and boundless eagerness to go have irresistible appeal. ![](images/0bb0f954c3244d5f0b7e25b3ddc8a1003b5314a7850d744a114fe3e7cfd96755.jpg) Advertisement for Dodge Brothers Senior Line, Vogue, 1923. Advertisers defined an emerging good life, grounded in physical activity and experienced by an elite group of people engaged in active and social leisure pursuits with whom an increasingly diverse audience could identify through the products they purchased, the books and magazines they read, and the films they watched. The triumphs and conflicts of this group formed the subjects of popular fiction in masscirculation magazines such as True Story and Ladies’ Home Journal, and included color illustrations to augment the frequently serialized stories. Companies and the advertising agencies they hired to promote their products turned to artists and illustrators to provide suitable, usually idealized addressing figures for products in magazines and even on large billboards that lined the boulevards and highways of a more mobile American society. Fueled by the success of persuasive posters in government-sponsored public relations campaigns to enlist recruits or sell war bonds during World War I, advertising agencies saw illustration as a vehicle to shape the consumer values shared by an expanding and increasingly heterogeneous mass market, although racism continued to limit opportunities for economic and social advancement for Black Americans. Some companies promoted the use of fine art in advertising, part of an initiative to familiarize a broader audience with modern art and link companies with cultural uplift and education. In 1927 General Motors (GM) hired Harley Earl (18931969) to head an Art and Color Section within all brand divisions of the giant corporation, based upon Earls success in redesigning the 1927 Cadillac Lasalle (fig. 10.18), a less expensive variation of the companys traditional luxury sedan. Earl came to GM as a designer of custom automobiles, in which lower profiles and a more unified approach to body design, found in expensive European automobiles such as the Hispano-Suiza, were substituted for the traditional high chassis and collection of unrelated elements such as bumpers, wheel covers, and headlights of more standardized American cars. One of Earls contributions as a designer for the automobile industry was to introduce new features that rarely required significant engineering changes or substantial capital investment in new machinery. Providing choice and variety in the automobile market without significantly raising production costs constituted an industrial design strategy that was in line with a consumer-oriented business and manufacturing practice. The competitive, freeenterprise economic climate of the later 1920s made manufacturers recognize that Fordist uniformity and standardization in industrially manufactured goods were not sufficient to continue to guarantee profits: the need for change was a necessity for generating continued growth. Significantly, the Art and Color Section of General Motors was renamed the Styling Division in 1937: the new name appears to reflect the corporations acknowledgment of its strategy for frequent model changes, as well as its understanding that design functioned to generate desire, and in effect was a branch of advertising. Later dubbed planned obsolescence (see here320), the strategy encompassed the annual or seasonal introduction of new colors, patterns, or forms that made existing industrial products appear outmoded and encouraged consumers to purchase the latest, most modern item. It helped to reduce the risks of introducing substantively new products. Obsolescence was part of a new business model that used design as an investment to help stimulate consumer sales. Although some writers criticized this approach as a form of consumer manipulation and acquiescence to market pressure (it certainly deviated from Le Corbusiers search for permanent objets types), planned obsolescence operated on what Roland Marchand has called the new consumer ethic, a recognition that consumption fueled production, putting people back to work and bolstering buyer confidence. Many industrial manufacturers began to think of this approach to design as an integral part of their business, and turned to advertisers, retailers, and designers to suggest new or modified products. The Ford Motor Corporation commissioned the painter and commercial photographer Charles Sheeler (18831965) to produce a series of photographs of the Ford Motors River Rouge facility that coincided with the introduction of the Model A, intended to communicate the inspiring grandeur and efficiency of the machine-dominated environment (fig. 10.19). Historians have noted that in these photographs, as well as in a series of paintings by Sheeler of the Rouge plant reproduced in the new business journal Fortune (first published in 1930), the human presence is often entirely absent or overwhelmed by the machinery the worker operates (for comparison, Diego Riveras 1933 series of frescoes in the garden court of the Detroit Institute of Arts titled Detroit Industry celebrates the power of industry but with workers and managers playing a more active role). This in turn reflects the view that the progress guaranteed by mechanization, for better or worse, diminishes the agency or requires the submission of the individual worker to carefully monitored routine tasks controlled by managers and owners, a telling indication of an inherent tension between man and machine as well as between labor and management in the capitalist factory system. Indeed, the success of Fords moving assembly line was due to the replacement of skilled by unskilled labor, with increasing emphasis upon the performance of single tasks in required sequences measured against the clock and under the watchful eye of factory foremen. But while the focus of Sheelers River Rouge photographs from the later 1920s was upon the power and productivity of machines, the commercial strategies of large manufacturing corporations, including Ford, were moving toward the consumer and the need to stimulate desire through promotion, advertising, marketing, and merchandising, all of which defined and expanded opportunities for designers. As noted above, the alienating effects of the assembly line and the general mechanization of the workplace were balanced by consumption in which workers now participated and the myths of personal and social progress that the purchase of commodities promised. The power of Sheelers River Rouge photographs lies in their combination of documentary truth with the reinforcement corporate authority. ![](images/669d0d23ce15b2d4984150edd32343a8a7de84f1587a2c0043a42658142e1f24.jpg) 10.19 Charles Sheeler, Stamping Press, photograph of Ford Plant at River Rouge, Michigan, 1927. Library of Congress. While General Motors and Ford used in-house styling departments and advertising to direct their design activities, some companies looked to independent or consultant designers to modify and market their products. The phenomenon of the consultant industrial designer is significant in the history of American manufacturing in the 1930s. It indicates that aside from in-house styling departments created by the automobile industry in the later 1920s, industrial manufacturing corporations had to look outside of their own personnel to introduce eye-catching design or respond creatively to surveys of consumer preferences. In the business language of the time, companies thought in terms of product engineering, focusing upon what Jeffrey Meikle describes as the functional arrangement of a products mechanical parts instead of those features of a product that might generate consumer appeal. The stock market crash of October 1929, precipitated by inflated stock prices and widespread speculation on company growth and rising real-estate values, marked the beginning of an economic depression with high unemployment (reaching nearly 25 percent in 1933), falling domestic production, and an erosion of consumer confidence and spending. During this period the United States government took a stronger role in providing welfare and funding large-scale infrastructure projects as well as public art and the Social Security Act. To encourage people to spend rather than save, more manufacturers turned to designers in an effort to bolster sales of their products. In the 1920s, advertising executive Elmo Calkins argued in business journals that design added appeal and value to products  during the 1930s this strategy was not only adopted but also refined to produce consumer engineering that sought to guarantee desire, and in which designers played a significant part. The emergence of a business model for stimulating consumption helped to define and promote industrial design during the 1930s in the United States. Kem Weber (born Karl Emmanuel Martin in Germany; 18891963) traveled to California in 1914 to assist with the German Pavilion for the Panama-Pacific International Exposition in San Francisco in 1915 (celebrating the opening of the Panama Canal and its promise for increased trade for Californias port cities) and remained in the United States following the outbreak of World War I. He opened an industrial design office in Los Angeles in the early 1930s, having worked in the later 1920s as a draftsman for the Barker Brothers Department Store in Los Angeles. As a consultant for industry, Weber designed an electric clock with continuous numerical readout (rather than traditional dial) housed in a copper and aluminum case (fig. 10.20), manufactured by the Lawson Time Inc. of Alhambra, California, in 1934. The rectangular form of the clock is swept back at the left edge in a curve rather than at a 90-degree angle to produce a smooth, streamlined look suggestive of flowing movement rather than the angles and abrupt staccato rhythms of Cubist or set-back Skyscraperinspired forms of the 1920s. Historians have noted the use of such forms in the buildings and project designs of German architect Erich Mendelsohn (18871953), whose work, such as the 1928 Schocken Department Store in Stuttgart, was also admired by Frank Lloyd Wright and exhibited at the Museum of Modern Art in 1932. Such forms may also be seen in the lower stories of the 1932 Philadelphia Saving Fund Society Building designed by the firm of Howe and Lescaze. The electric numerical readout offered a sense of precision to the owner, and the flow of Webers case suggested smooth, continuous mechanical movement like that of the assembly line. ![](images/72b2c24cc826520e73b8f72bdf7fab47c9e3bdab3c63f823b22fffc4723fab76.jpg) 10.20 Kem Weber, Zephyr electric clock, brass, bakelite, $3 { ^ 1 } / _ { 4 } \times 8 \times 3 { ^ 1 } / _ { 8 }$ in (8.3 × 20.3 × 8 cm), manufactured by Lawson Time Inc., Pasadena, California, 1934. A number of consultant industrial designers were artists who had worked directly in advertising or were successful in fields such as stage and set design for the theater. Often recommended to manufacturers by advertising agencies, industrial designers absorbed an eclectic array of sources for inspiration in their work, defining a middle ground between advertisings concerns for visual appeal and broadly humanistic concerns with efficiency and progress through improved performance and the introduction of art into everyday life. Industrial design consultancies often employed large staffs, and in addition to redesigning products were responsible for package design, labels, advertising, and merchandising. Although the success of the profession in the 1930s was often based upon the value and marketability of individual names such as Raymond Loewy, whose portrait appeared on the cover of a 1949 issue of Time magazine, industrial design office practice generally involved collaboration and compromise among many individuals working together as a team according to a division of labor and a dialogue between competing viewpoints and objectives. The team-based approach of industrial design offices approximated the existing conditions and complexities of the manufacturing industry more than the ambience of the artists studio or the idealism of the academy, where isolation often produced compelling ideologies but few massproduced goods or realized grandiose schemes for future planned societies. Designers such as Loewy and Norman Bel Geddes (18931958) used their celebrity to publicize the emerging profession and its value to consumers and manufacturers alike through books and magazine articles; articles and advertisements in journals such as Fortune mediated the advantages of design to the business community and a wider readership. ![](images/d6f4876021256f010320329d9009c0b7ecd8fd457dd2f988aa434d9ce51f1c03.jpg) Advertisement for gas range, Geo. D. Roper Corporation, Rockford, Illinois, from Good Housekeeping, August 1923. Studies of the projects of American industrial designers in the 1930s reveal the dynamic interrelationships between aesthetic, commercial, user-centered, and manufacturing considerations. For example, Bel Geddes was hired by the Standard Gas Equipment Corporation in 1933 to redesign its kitchen ranges in the hope of stimulating consumer demand. Market research revealed that ease of cleaning was the primary consideration of housewives when purchasing a new range. Bel Geddess response was to use large rectangular panels of enamel-coated sheet metal (including a panel to fit over the burners) rather than cast iron to create a more enclosed and unified, essentially round-edged boxlike form, eliminating open areas that collected dust as well as the clutter of decorative handles and other hardware (fig. 10.21). Bel Geddes hung the panels from a sturdy tubular-metal frame, as if constructed as a model of gridlike Corbusian architecture. The new model presented a more integrated, unified housing or envelope, while advertisements identified smooth polished surfaces and unobtrusive form with improved hygiene as well as reduced housework. Many of these characteristics are also found in a 1933 stove manufactured under the brand name Magic Chef for the American Stove Company of St. Louis, Missouri, where the thin, tubular-steel frame is left partially exposed (fig. 10.22). ![](images/f649a93bb5654df096acc856ab8b61d1e4e8432c572a1f0024cd950a073559e3.jpg) Anonymous, gas range, “Magic Chef,” enameled steel, chromium-plated steel, cast iron, stainless steel, plastic, 60 × 45 × 27 in (152.4 × 114.3 × 68 cm), American Stove Company, St. Louis, Missouri, c. 1935. This approach, balancing unified housing with mechanical or structural change for improved performance, appears to have been the ideal toward which many consultant industrial designers directed their efforts, that is, to a point somewhere beyond mere superficial styling or cosmetic change but squarely within a free-market competitive economic model. It also created a connection and balance between design and engineering (rather than advertising), between the creativity of the designer and the objectivity and problem-solving associations of an applied scientist. Another oft-cited example is Loewys design for British manufacturer Sigmund Gestetners duplicating machine of 1929. Gestetner came to Loewy in New York for assistance in improving the appearance of his machine. Loewy provided a unified housing that concealed many of the machines working parts. Whether by intention or happy accident, the new shell reduced cost by making the nickel-plating of exposed mechanical parts unnecessary and improved performance by reducing the amount of space between parts where dust tended to accumulate and lead to malfunctions. Walter Dorwin Teague (18831960) worked as a staff artist for an advertising agency in New York before designing products for industrial production. Many of his designs aimed to reduce complexity as well as improve the quality of life through an awareness of taste and discrimination. He redesigned several cameras for the Kodak Company, including its line of Brownie models, introducing smooth plastic housing with vertical ribs to each model, as well as the small 35-millimeter Bantam Special or bullet model of 1936 (fig. 10.23). Teague enclosed the lens in a unified shell with rounded sides and thin, horizontal metal ribs, explaining that the ribs functioned to reduce the cracking of the casings black lacquer finish  as much a solution to a technical problem as of modern styling, achieving a balance between novelty and improvement. ![](images/81a42c56b97dddd6f6364afd7f10763945f44dfa61136b82860558d4f46904af.jpg) 10.23 Walter Dorwin Teague, Bantam Special camera, painted brass, $3 ^ { 1 } / _ { 2 } \times 4 \times 1 ^ { 3 } / _ { 4 } ( 9 \times 1 0 \times 4 . 4$ cm), Eastman Kodak Company, Rochester, New York, 1936. For instance, Loewys 1935 Coldspot refrigerator for Sears Corporation (fig. 10.24), as well as an earlier 1933 refrigerator designed for General Electric by Henry Dreyfuss, integrated the condenser element into the appliance housing. Such changes conformed to the strategy of obsolescence (see here), making each years model new, and identifying the new with the improved. The Coldspot broke records for sales, even though the US economy was steadily approaching pre-Depression manufacturing levels by 1937. The title of Loewys autobiography, Never Leave Well Enough Alone, offers examples of his approach to design, acknowledging its connection with the need for change to maintain levels of production and consumption in a competitive economy. Henry Dreyfuss (19041972) shared with his fellow industrial designers of the period a belief that successful and more or less final solutions to design problems for many products were possible. He defined a products survival form as being simple and having a satisfying aesthetic that fulfilled its practical, production, and commercial requirements. This theory, less doctrinaire than Le Corbusiers objets types, allowed for changes while retaining a memorable feature or form that created product identity and promoted familiarity and appeal. The Type 300 combined handset for the Bell Telephone Corporation, designed in 1937, is one of Dreyfusss best-known pre-World War II projects (fig. 10.26). The combined handset replaced earlier upright models with separate speakers and microphones in the later 1920s. Not subject to competitive market pressures, the design could be developed on the basis of ease of use, stability, ease of cleaning, and durability. When he learned that he would be unable to consult with engineers when preparing a new telephone design for AT&T, Dreyfuss initially withdrew from the competition. After the company rejected proposals from other designers, Dreyfuss was offered the commission for the new phone. The Type 300 was more unified than its predecessors, and indeed appears to adopt some of the basic (survival) features from a combined handset phone designed by artist Jean Heiberg (18841976) for the L. M. Ericsson Company of Stockholm, Sweden, in 1930. After World War II, Dreyfuss made several improvements to his design, switching from a metal to a lighter-weight plastic housing, and placing the numbers outside rather than inside the dialing mechanism for legibility and reduced wear. Perhaps the most important improvement to the post-war phone was its receiver, whose lightness and flattened handle enabled the user to cradle it between ear and shoulder, freeing the hands to multi-task while talking on the phone. ![](images/d8b7276491b8a20719913b2c6b955b4cade0322df0d4eb523e946fa13d62d0a8.jpg) Henry Dreyfuss, Type 300, handset, black phenolic resin, Bell Telephone Corporation, 1937. ## The United States and International Modernism During the 1930s, the Museum of Modern Art in New York (MoMA, founded in 1929) embraced and promoted a more European-based model of industrial design that selectively incorporated elements of Constructivism, the Dessau Bauhaus, and the theories of Le Corbusier. The museum preferred a more restrained functional architecture, referred to as the International Style and featured in a well-publicized show in 1932 titled Modern Architecture: International Exhibition, rejecting decoration and embracing well-lit open interior spaces, fitted furniture and the elimination of mass through thinner, lighter-weight materials. MoMA favored visual similarities over differences and even contradictions in local contexts, lumping together the aesthetically pure and exclusive private homes designed by Le Corbusier and Mies van der Rohe with the efficient public housing projects of Oud in the Netherlands and Ernst May in Frankfurt as exhibiting a common vocabulary of formal elements deriving from modern building materials and new construction technologies. What was important was the rejection of revival styles and the substitution of superficial modernistic (Art Deco) decoration and sentimental attachment to the past with the clean lines, smooth surfaces, modular units, and elemental geometry. In 1934, MoMA housed an exhibition entitled Machine Art, curated by architect Philip Johnson (fig. 10.27), and published a catalog with an introductory essay by museum director Alfred H. Barr, Jr. The exhibition featured more than 400 objects, drawn from the scientific laboratory, institutional kitchens, machines, and machine parts, along with tubular furniture and bathroom fixtures for office or home. The catalog essays acknowledged the contributions of the Dessau Bauhaus and of modern abstract artists such as Constantin Brancusi. Many objects were lent by industrial manufacturers such as Alcoa (Aluminum Company of America), who also served as one of several sponsors. Machine Art advocated an approach to design for modern living that renounced handicraft in favor of machine production and emphasized the elemental, enduring beauty of the geometric forms of machines and the goods they produced. At times elemental geometry was not intentional, as in a bearing spring, while at other times it was a matter of selection and a modern aesthetic sensibility. The exhibition organizers argued for a universal machine aesthetic, promoting geometry, utility, new materials, and the rejection of decoration and signs of hand-crafting. In the museums view, ornament was a vestige of craft and of privilege, unnecessary to the practical needs of the modern family; not being necessary, decoration was therefore extravagant, even irresponsible in the context of widespread unemployment and hardship during the Depression. Barr wrote that in order to end the divorce between industry and culture, we must assimilate the machine aesthetically as well as economically. It is worthwhile remembering that Machine Art revived, or updated, a strain of modern design history that includes William Morriss recommendation that machines not try to imitate handwork, and Frank Lloyd Wrights equation of machines with simplicity. ![](images/16a57e46f96678e2fc05013a9def3369108b1941156f1cdf2f65230daa19fcaa.jpg) 10.27 “Machine Art” exhibition at the Museum of Modern Art, New York, 1934. In promoting the relationship between geometric purity and the methods of industrial production, Machine Art presented a reductive and somewhat limited interpretation of modern design and the role of the designer or artist-engineer. Divorced from function by being placed on pedestals and hung on walls with dramatic lighting, objects in the exhibition such as trays or pitchers were accompanied by price lists and could be appreciated for their abstract, geometric purity as machine-age works of art as well as commodities to be purchased for the home; in other words, an exercise in design reform and the elevation of public taste  of selling the advantages of modern design with the museums authoritative stamp. In furniture the curators relied almost exclusively upon European designers using tubular steel, ignoring creations of American designers no less innovative in their use of modern industrial materials. Among those ignored was Warren McArthur (18851961), an engineer who created chairs and tables constructed of lightweight tubular aluminum, ingeniously joined by a system of patented internal cables that produced clean joints (fig 10.28). McArthur received a patent for his invention and also designed numerous versions of aluminum seating for aircraft for the US Army during World War II. Gradually an expanded International Style of modern industrial furniture and furnishings, exhibited at MoMA and at other museums around the country as good design, gained broader acceptance after World War II, incorporating standards reminiscent of reform in their balance among individual expression, restraint, comfort, and practical advantage. The context and genesis of this approach to industrial design is a focus of Part V. ![](images/2044f1e81adfdc99e3edda4dcc6949c6b966971dcc835975d8bc2ed4db251864.jpg) Warren McArthur, chair, aluminum and leather, c. 1932. More traditional craft-based manufacturers in the furniture and ceramics industries hired contract or in-house designers to develop modern products for industrial manufacture during the 1930s based upon smooth forms, new materials, and restrained decoration. Gilbert Rohde (18941944) designed modern displays and furnished rooms for the 1933 Worlds Fair in Chicago, but is perhaps best known as design consultant for the Herman Miller Furniture Company of Zeeland, Michigan, from 1932 until his premature death in 1944. Miller furniture was sold primarily through department stores such as Macys in New York and John Wanamaker in Philadelphia, and on a contract basis to architects in the design of office and other institutional interiors. During the years he spent at Miller, Rohde developed a series of furniture designs based upon sectional or modular rectangular units for seating, storage, and shelving (fig. 10.29). D. J. DePree, President of Herman Miller and who hired Rohde, promoted the relationship between modern design and socially responsible values of modesty and efficiency, especially relevant to the business community (and its reputation) in the economic climate of the Depression. ![](images/ead56ae65c94a4f635d89b5fa1e83cfa18cc1dd0428148cfd5386bc551373ecd.jpg) Diagram for modular desk units based upon designs of Gilbert Rohde, Herman Miller Company, Zeeland, Michigan, c. 1935. One popular domestic application for practical modern design was in production ceramics, seen in the line of table service known as Fiestaware, designed in 1936 by British-born ceramicist Frederick Hurten Rhead (18801942) for the Homer Laughlin China Company in West Virginia, where he worked as artistic director during that time. Fiestaware (fig. 10.30) eschewed traditional connections with patterned decoration, historical styles, sculptural contours, and relief decoration in favor of plain circular shapes with slightly raised concentric ribbing. Fiestawares uniformity invoked the standardization of industrial manufacturing, but such uniformity was balanced by a wide variety of brightly colored and highly durable glazes developed by Rhead. Certainly the name Fiestaware evoked associations with gaiety (rather than austerity), and a similar palette is found in contemporary Mexican ceramics that were also manufactured on a smaller scale in California, where Rhead had spent time as a studio potter prior to working for the Laughlin China Company. Fiestaware retained some traditional elements such as the handles or feet for serving dishes and the concentric circles that suggest the technique of the potters wheel. With his wife, Mary, Wright published a Guide to Easier Living (1950). Wright also was a pioneer in finding product applications for aluminum. Private collection. ## Streamlining A related area of American interwar industrial design concerns the broad topic of Streamlining in the transportation industry. During the 1920s, wind-tunnel tests by engineers demonstrated that teardrop-shaped forms, rounded in front and tapering toward the rear, reduced wind resistance, enabling both faster speeds and improved fuel consumption for land-based and air travel. By 1930, these results were adopted in the aviation industry, where bullet-shaped noses, farings placed over landing gear, and wings molded to the body of aircraft appeared in airplanes such as the Douglas DC1 in 1933 or the earlier German Junkers F-13. The American railroad industry adopted streamlining for locomotive design in the 1930s. Threatened with increasing competition from the automobile industry and commercial aviation, railway companies had seen a steady decline in passengers and profits since the 1920s. Early examples of streamlined passenger trains were shown in the second year of the 1933 Century of Progress Worlds Fair in Chicago. They employed lighter construction materials such as corrugated steel (rather than heavier steel of a uniform thickness) combined with diesel engines to improve fuel efficiency. Broad curved front ends and reduced space between cars were features of the Budd Manufacturing Companys three-car commuter train for the Burlington Railway Company in 1934, appropriately named Zephyr. Streamlining also appears in the bullet-shaped Union Pacific locomotive illustrated in figure 10.33 from 1934. Adapting the benefits of wind-tunnel research to the automobile industry proved elusive, but several prototypes and limited production vehicles emerged in Europe as well as in the United States. Hungarian designer Paul Jaray built a teardrop-shaped automobile as early as 1922, and in Britain A. E. Palmer designed a car in 1930 with a rear engine permitting lower ground clearance that also incorporated the new teardrop form. Also, visitors to the Victoria and Albert Museums 2003 Art Deco 19101939 10.33 exhibition were treated to the streamlined Tatra car, manufactured in Czechoslovakia in 1937. Inventor Richard Buckminster Fuller (18951983) designed a Streamlined three-wheel vehicle that achieved media notoriety at the Chicago Worlds Fair in 1933, but it was never put into production, while Streamlined prototypes for automobiles, boats, and aircraft were designed by Norman Bel Geddes and promoted in his journal Horizons beginning in 1932. While the teardrop may have promised efficiency for production automobiles on the basis of research and testing, the American automobile industry was hesitant to invest in such a radical retooling of its factory equipment. Bel Geddes did serve as a consultant to the Chrysler Corporation for the 1934 Airflow, which adapted a more unified approach to the construction of the grill, hood, and windshield to traditional front-engine production technology with flat sides. The advertising campaign for the Chrysler Airflow, featuring photographs of the auto alongside a Union Pacific locomotive (fig. 10.33) generated excitement and numerous orders, but negative publicity stemming from flaws in the first units to reach the market doomed the car, and manufacturing ceased in 1937. ![](images/58d13a9ddae519eefa666b7b01f175e2391c4fd01f46f1c9f2895ac8aea4e243.jpg) Airflow Imperial Coupe, manufactured by Chrysler Corporation, 1934, with Union Pacific Streamline Express, 1934. While Streamlined automobiles in the United States remained experimental or limited to the custom market (in Europe the Volkswagen was an exception, though not manufactured widely until after World War II, see here), the tapered forms and lightweight materials adopted in aircraft design during the 1930s found popular expression in the Airstream trailer, first built in 1936 in Culver City, California, by Wally Byam (18961962). Intended for leisure travel, the lightweight aluminum Airstream Clipper featured a distinctive tapered bullet shape to lessen wind resistance and increase fuel efficiency, along with its unified body and chassis (fig. 10.34). Production halted when the United States entered World War II in December 1941 and aluminum was needed for military production, but the success of the Airstream during the Depression in the later 1930s illustrates the popular appeal of Streamlining. The business of trailers as mobile homes for retirees and for vacation travel prompted a lengthy illustrated feature story in the monthly business magazine Fortune in March, 1937, citing trailer building as a growth industry for modern living, economical for owners, and making efficient use of limited space. 10.34 ![](images/f815c681bfcb8a08b3b03df834496e5602b29f5ba03b090dca98b256c614a00a.jpg) Airstream Clipper aluminum trailer, manufactured by Airstream Inc., California, 1936–1940. Streamlinings appeal extended to a host of products unrelated to transportation. Kem Webers electric table clock (see fig. 10.20) exhibits the smooth surfaces and swept-back contours of the style, while the Electrolux Model 30 vacuum cleaner designed by Lurelle Guild (18981985) dating to 1937 (fig. 10.35) used lightweight aluminum for the body that rested on chrome-plated steel skids that glided across the carpet. Electrolux vacuum cleaners were manufactured in the United States in Dover, Delaware, though the companys origins were in Sweden before World War I. Whether a clock, vacuum cleaner, or pencil sharpener, Streamlined housings communicated graceful, effortless movement, without tension or friction. In a complex world with mounting social tensions and economic challenges in the later 1930s, Streamlining provided consumers with a combination of hope and escape, fueled by manufacturers and designers emerging attention to the varied motivations behind consumer behavior. Criticized by the Museum of Modern Art as modernistic rather than Modernist, the idea of a Streamlined future fueled the imagination of consumers, offering freedom from resistance whether in an airplane, the sight of a shiny Airstream trailer, a table clock, or a radio. ![](images/22e307f992c604336184394fb0de3efd606b481424efb99584b228f2818334d8.jpg) Lurelle Guild, Electrolux Model 30 vacuum cleaner, chrome-plated steel, aluminum, vinyl, rubber, 8 <sup>1</sup>/<sub>2</sub> × 23 × 7 <sup>3</sup>/<sub>4</sub> in (21.6 × 58.4 × 19.7 cm), Electrolux Corporation, Dover, Delaware, 1937. Brooklyn Museum, New York. ## The New York World’s Fair The 1939 New York Worlds Fair centered upon the theme of Building the World of Tomorrow. Industrial designer Walter Dorwin Teague was on the planning board for the fair; Norman Bel Geddes designed the popular General Motors Corporations Futurama exhibit and ride, where viewers on moving chairs experienced a vision for an American city in the year 1960, with suburbs connected to the city by networks of multi-lane highways, and automobiles defining the Streamlined patterns of tomorrows modern living. For both of these men, the Worlds Fair provided an opportunity to extend the range of their industrial design practice beyond consumer products and appliances and to realize, if only ephemerally and in miniature, a unified conception for a radically designed future that improved the conditions of everyday life. This vision was embodied in the centralized design of the fair, where several multicolored avenues converged in a circular space that contained the symbolic Trylon and Perisphere. Both the avenues to either side of the central circle were separated thematically for various activities such as transportation, international exhibits, and amusement and entertainment. Democracity, a miniature world of tomorrow, was housed within the large globe-shaped Perisphere, viewed from two rotating balconies and connected by walkway to the other symbol, the tall pyramidal Trylon (fig. 10.36). Designed by Henry Dreyfuss, Democracity was divided into zones separated by wide streets designed for vehicular traffic and tree-lined walkways for pedestrians that were elevated to avoid accidents. Residential communities were located between the city center (Centerton) and farmland, and were named Pleasantvilles or Millvilles (the latter a factory town). Democracitys accompanying brochure touted the elimination of the depressing sight of slums, pollution, and crime, whose design promised a manageable future based upon the common good achieved through cooperation and interdependence, a utopia recalling William Morriss News from Nowhere (see here) for the age of industry. ![](images/e76c85452649fecc047a000414af49087e6ed429ee75ce564e9527bd5887b81c.jpg) 10.36 Aerial view of New York World’s Fair, 1939–1940. Like Democracity, Bel Geddess Futurama exhibit, sponsored by General Motors, also managed traffic flow in the modern metropolis by multi-lane highways and banked exit ramps, pedestrian walkways that did not interfere with vehicular traffic, and by a radiocontrolled system that monitored speed and distance between vehicles on the highway (less individualized than features on todays cars to manage cruise-control). The exhibit stressed the importance of highways, and paralleled the lobbying efforts of the automobile industry, tire manufacturers, and oil companies to expand government funding of the nations road system and further stimulated consumer desire for personal mobility. Conspicuously absent were mass-transit systems (other than buses). Like the utopian vision of Le Corbusiers Voisin plan from the mid-1920s, a single style prevailed in the Futurama, defining a controlled world that applied the efficiency and logic of large-scale modern technology and materials to erase differences, contradictions, conflicts, and irritants. Many contemporary historians have noted the contrast between these managerial visions of tomorrow and the realities of unemployment, urban decay, and racism affecting industrial cities in the United States. In turn, such overarching control of space and movement demanded compliance, the surrender of individual freedom and agency to patterns of movement akin to the predictable and efficient rhythms of industrial machinery. Charlie Chaplin poked fun at such a mechanized future in his 1939 silent film Modern Times with a series of sequences in which the assembly-line worker (Chaplin) becomes a programmed victim rather than beneficiary of modern industrial technology. In addition to the utopian Democracity or Futurama, other manufacturers displays commodified the future through the display and working demonstration of products and appliances, translating the clean, Streamlined vision of the future into a present rather than distant reality, often with an emphasis on the relationship of technology to leisure. Exhibits documented the steady and sure progress of science, the attainment of frictionless speed in the transportation industry, for instance, and tested how machines reduced toil and increased free time. In one exhibit for the Westinghouse Corporation, designed by Gilbert Rohde (see here), Mrs Drudge washed dishes by hand while her counterpart, Mrs Modern, loaded an automatic dishwashing machine. Still in the wake of the Depression and hardship caused by high levels of unemployment and poverty, and fearful of mounting tension and conflict in Europe (already present in the exclusion of Nazi Germany from participation and the inability of a number of European nations to participate or having to modify their pavilions, such as Czechoslovakia, occupied by the Nazis beginning in 1938), the products and demonstrations of the fair exhibits provided some kind of tangible proof of progress through modern, efficient living. Modern-day critics of the 1939 New York Worlds Fair have commented that its planners conditioned the experience of visitors, aiming through dramatic devices such as lighting and perspective to stage a sequence of particular responses leading to a climax that gave substance to the vision of tomorrows world today. Such devices were the stock in trade of architects and interior designers such as Morris Lapidus (19022001), who was a successful storefront designer during the interwar period. Lapidus employed techniques such as mirrors to create the effect of spaciousness and curving windows beckoning shoppers toward the entrance by removing sharp corners or barriers. He installed bright signage and lighting to create contrast or what he called the moth principle. Such devices may be seen in Lapiduss 1939 storefront for Hoffritz Cutlery in Manhattan (fig. 10.37). The mingling of utopian ideology and consumer engineering was characteristic of the 1939 Worlds Fair, representing the strategies of advertising agencies and the balancing of competing, even contradictory priorities that characterized the profession of industrial design as it became part of the experience of modern living in the United States during the interwar period. ![](images/9dc9e3c1265ac6b447ad4b959b0cd5aaaa57c89c590291e7622eefc2cdaa333c.jpg) Morris Lapidus, storefront for Hoffritz Cutlery, New York, 1939. Planners and promoters expressed optimism that the provocative exhibits of the World of Tomorrow would continue for five years, but even with publicity in newsreels and in print, the exhibits closed before the end of 1940. Like much popular entertainment, the 1939 Worlds Fair caused great excitement initially but failed to sustain interest beyond the initial fleeting impact of glitter and a sense of escape from harsher realities. Some of the organizers believed that the fair was educational in shaping public opinion and offering solutions and even proof of reduced hardship, alienation, and conflict, but its longevity depended upon ticket sales from a public who appeared to see the fair as a short-lived collection of novelties with little meaning beyond the immediacy and initial excitement. As an experience based in part upon techniques derived from the advertising industry, it was, in a sense, a victim of the planned obsolescence that helped to shape it  without continued novelty and progressive change, the World of Tomorrow too quickly became yesterdays news. Interestingly, the idea of a more limited but nonetheless progressive fair resurfaced during the 1950s with the traveling Motorama showcases sponsored by General Motors (see here) or the Monsanto House of Tomorrow, featured at Disneyland (see here). ## Photography and Graphic Design In the mid-1930s, photography began to compete with and gradually to eclipse illustration for advertising. Illustration persisted for magazine and book covers, along with illustrated fiction. Although advertisements formed part of his extensive oeuvre, Norman Rockwell (18941978) enjoyed a long relationship with the popular weekly mass-circulation magazine Saturday Evening Post. Rockwells color illustrations for the Post presented a variety of familiar themes drawn from common white middle-class experience, frequently involving seasonal activities and holidays. Rockwell was familiar with the conventions of narrative art in the Western tradition, relying upon pyramidal composition to provide focus or climax as well as to guide the viewers eye through the carefully orchestrated compositions. Frequently his covers and posters seem to suggest continuity with the sense of community and comfort provided by family and neighborhood, an antidote to the impersonal modernity of factory and office, and absent from the utopian visions of Democracity. The use of reassuring shared experiences and techniques of naturalistic illustration to communicate those values were Rockwells hallmarks. A Post cover from November, 1933 (fig. 10.38) illustrates a young mother spanking her child on a chair that is a simple design based upon a Neoclassical model, and less frequent in Rockwells illustrations than carved and more heavily upholstered furniture inspired by earlier eighteenth-century examples. Notwithstanding the political incorrectness of implying any form of physical abuse or threat to children in our own day, the story is clearly and simply told. The child has broken a piece of fine decorative china with a hammer, and the mother responds with a traditional form of punishment. Her response, however, is not the result of an emotional outburst or of inherited child-rearing practice: while she raises her hand, she holds a psychology book that has guided her actions. In fact, the read response is precisely the same as the spontaneous one, and modern psychology has recommended a solution with which the reader is already familiar. The illustration makes the reader feel more confident with some of the discomforting information that makes us feel insecure in dealing with everyday existence. ![](images/4e0a41aef0fbae904dae3dd8fddf56e969f5264840996518cab4567390f80c24.jpg) Norman Rockwell, “Child Psychology” cover, Saturday Evening Post, November 25, 1933. As Michele Bogart has suggested, Rockwell was ambivalent about his own success, despite the celebrity he attained; his attempts to experiment with more expressionistic approaches to painting were rejected by his editors at the Post. It may also be helpful to view his success in the context of mass images that seek at once to reflect as well as to shape behavior in periods of great social and political change. Rockwells poster illustrating a festive family meal is one of four from The Four Freedoms series, in this case Freedom from Want (fig. 10.39). The composition suggests references to countless images of the Last Supper that lend formality, even solemnity, to the meal. Rockwell executed the series in 1942, in the midst of World War II, amid government-imposed rationing and shortages in support of the war effort that raised more than thirty-two million dollars in war bond contributions. Like advertising illustrations connecting products with the good life, Rockwells images steered away from troubling social or political problems, and his illustrations indulge in escapism as elements of mass culture. Indeed, until the 1960s, minorities, for instance, find little place within Rockwells oeuvre, and popular appeal at the time seemed to preclude representing Black people or commenting on racism or social unrest. ![](images/008b4faf859ee24641c994625b3c28858e93c2a9b376ab6276046731e8b4c9b8.jpg) 10.39 Norman Rockwell, Freedom from Want, oil painting, 1943. The Norman Rockwell Museum at Stockbridge, Massachusetts. Other examples of contemporary public art seemed to include or at least reference more directly the hardship of many Americans during the Depression. The murals painted for the federally sponsored Work Progress Administration (WPA) provided artists with an opportunity to work, most often in a traditionally accessible naturalistic style (Arshile Gorkys 1937 murals for Newark Airport, New Jersey, are an exception). Thomas Hart Benton (18891975), for instance, was responsible for several murals in post offices and other public buildings and institutions during the 1930s. Benton used swelling shapes and sweeping curvilinear lines to invest his figures with energy and movement, often lending a heroic character to the depiction of ordinary everyday activities of work and recreation during a time of high unemployment in the hope of building national unity and optimism (fig. 10.40). ![](images/e9b3715b1cb1b5d6ce0588e124ed72df800fa6647dc55014fd1bc732809ad945.jpg) Thomas Hart Benton, Arts of the West, tempera, 7 ft 10 in × 13 ft 5 in (239 × 409 cm). The New Britain Museum of American Art, New Britain, Connecticut. Beyond Rockwells appealing illustration and Bentons message of solidarity (and racial inclusion, as in his 1931 series of murals for the board room of the New School for Social Research in Manhattan) stood other artists whose realism moved in alternative directions. The less narrative and more introspective realism of artists like Ben Shahn (18981969) and Edward Hopper (18821967) reveal an experience of alienation and isolation (physical or spiritual) in the modern world. Only rarely, however, did their paintings reach the mass audience that read or subscribed to the Saturday Evening Post during the interwar period. After the war, progressive art editors such as William Golden (19111959) and Cipe Pineles (19081991) invited Shahn and other contemporary American artists to contribute illustrations for stories in journals such as Seventeen and in print advertisements for CBS radio and television. While illustration continued to find a place on the covers of weeklies such as the Saturday Evening Post, the launching of Life magazine in 1936 and Time (first published in 1923) provides evidence of a gradual shift toward photography (photojournalism) as the most compelling medium disseminating news, entertainment, and advertising. Even earlier in the 1930s, a number of art directors turned to photographic reproduction for its sense of immediacy and visual impact. Advertisers were attracted to the medium for the same reasons, as well as by the ability of commercial photographers to both simulate and manipulate narrative situations in order to make products appealing. Instead of relying upon copy to sell products or report events, art directors and the advertising industry turned to photographs to reach an expanding readership. In addition to product advertisements (which often accounted for 50 percent of their content), mass-circulation magazines carried regular features on contemporary fashion, literature, travel, and entertainment. Photography played a key role in presenting such material, in particular studio portraits by Edward Steichen, Charles Sheeler (fig. 10.19), and other photographers. ![](images/c0458e3f0decd538e3d7322e99acc2e995ab924c6e0b236c8272f31bc631770d.jpg) 10.41 M. F. Agha (art director), Edward Steichen (photographer), two-page layout from Vanity Fair, 1935. With the hiring of European-trained artists to oversee art direction, monthly magazines began to acquire a more self-consciously modern, integrated presentation of photography, text, and display titling in the later 1920s. At Vogue and Vanity Fair, art director Dr Mehemed Fehmy Agha (18961978) adapted some of the distinctive asymmetry of the New Typography to cover design and page layout. Agha was born in Ukraine of Turkish parents and was working as art director for German Vogue when he was asked by Vogues publisher, Condé Nast (18731942), to be the magazines American art director in 1929. Nast hoped that Agha would bring a new look to American Vogue, a feeling for modernity as present at the 1925 Paris Exposition but generally absent in American mass-circulation magazines. Agha was concerned with the overall expressive arrangement of every element on the page, often across a two-page spread, utilizing contrasts and harmonies among shapes and tones to create striking rhythms and vivid effects. The strong contrast and dramatic lighting employed by wellknown photographers such as Edward Steichen were integral features of Aghas art direction for Vogue and Vanity Fair, seen in a two-page spread from a 1935 issue of Vanity Fair, featuring Steichen photographs of the fashions for that spring season (fig. 10.41). Vogues content throughout this period focused upon fashion and the leisure activities of international celebrities including royalty, film stars, writers, musicians, and artists. Technically and creatively demanding as an art director, Agha brought to this lifestyle content the sensation of novelty, excitement, and the drama of the unexpected in its visual presentation. His association with Condé Nast publications lasted until 1943. In covers for Vanity Fair, Agha used simplified abstract forms together with text in subtle ways to convey meaning for content that included contemporary political and social concerns. Vanity Fair introduced color photography and illustration in the early 1930s. An example is a 1933 cover designed by Cipe Pineles (fig. 10.42), using the technique of collage to portray two figures against a dark background that includes a mass of red ink. The figures, one bloated and smoking a cigar (1929), the other thin and rumpled (1933), are cut out of newsprint from the stock-market pages of the business section. The tall hat and goatee of the thin figure identifies him as Uncle Sam, whose profile appeared frequently on the covers of Vanity Fair throughout this period. Pineless focus upon compositional unity among the elements of page design led to both clarity and concision, initially compelling at a glance as well as sufficiently subtle to sustain visual interest and communicate symbolic meaning. ![](images/fd1018dc13652c39fef30f8a288da6876d66625af88af2651824628b7363e603.jpg) M. F. Agha (art director), Cipe Pineles (artist), cover for Vanity Fair, October 1933. In the 1930s, Vogue competitor Harper’s Bazaar hired Russian émigré Alexey Brodovitch as art director. Brodovitch had worked as a stage designer for the Ballets Russes in Paris prior to coming to the United States, and continued to oversee art direction at Harper’s Bazaar well into the 1950s. The method of expressively orchestrating text and image as abstract elements on a magazine page or cover describes the activity of graphic design as practiced by Agha and Brodovitch and that was increasingly acknowledged in the commercial printing industry. Using photography and adapting the experimental strategies of the European-inspired New Typography, interwar graphic designers set high technical and aesthetic standards for their publications. Characteristics such as the reinforcement of diagonal movement across the page, contrast of images with white space, asymmetrical composition, unframed photographs that bleed to the edges of the page or blend completely with the white paper, and the juxtaposition of images helped to create visual interest in layouts and advertisements throughout the 1930s. Art directors such as Agha and Brodovitch were not publishers, political propagandists, or social activists. Their approach to visual communication involved a recognition that attracting modern readers attention demanded creativity and originality rather than cliché. They applied their awareness of modern art, photomontage, and the principles of the New Typography to serve the commercial needs of the publishers who employed them, engaging the reader in a new awareness, appreciation, and identification with modern life that focused increasingly upon consumption as a vehicle for self-realization. American artist Lester Beall (19031969) employed characteristics found in Russian Constructivism, the New Typography, and modern abstract art in a series of government-sponsored public-service posters promoting the use of electricity through the Rural Electrification Administration (REA) beginning in 1937. Bealls approach appears in a poster illustrating a bright white, electric washing machine dominating a pictogram of a home with white arrows symbolizing electric current against a background of blue (sky) and yellow (earth) (fig. 10.43). The sense of contrast is strong and reductive imagery is striking and operates without need for text or detailed illustration. As art historian Michael Golec has noted, Bealls approach drew upon and complemented the brochures of Rudolf Modley, a disciple of Otto Neurath and trained in the use of Isotype (see here), who also worked for the REA. Using pictograms to present statistical information to promote rural electrification, or the increase of electrification as a sign of improvement in the standard of living, is an example of information graphics, but information is rarely neutral: its presentation helps viewers to form opinions, often in line with the intentions of their sponsors. The REA provided low-interest loans to electrical suppliers who in turn marketed labor-saving appliances to homeowners. ![](images/4d9a01e73d81666bb1e0426ca8cee9c48d771a5d935c4e583510ef42b6c1f1f9.jpg) Lester Beall, “Wash Day,” poster, Rural Electrification Administration, 1937, poster, silkscreen, 40 × 30 in (101.6 × 76 cm), 1937. Through illustration, photography, and graphic design, mass magazines and the advertising that underwrote production costs forged a modern visual culture in the United States. Advertising was a necessary corollary to sustaining the growth of an industrially based capitalist economy. The advertising profession gained recognition and respectability through its connection with elevating public taste through art and reinforcing shared beliefs and values, linking these public-minded interests to products. Sales, whether of magazine subscriptions or of the products advertised on their pages, came to depend upon seductive images and asymmetrical layouts to attract attention and create distinction. In this way, the New Typography gained ground in the competitive commercial context of interwar America, in which the new and the fashionable were interchangeable. Techniques that first emerged in the service of radical utopian ideologies and political revolution were marshaled in the interests of businesses and governments. As explained by Roland Marchand: Pictures surpassed copy not only in their ability to intensify emotion but also in their capacity to say several things at the same time. Visual imagery effectively reached the less literate portions of the population. It also conveyed dense messages. By setting up harmonics of atmosphere and imagery that resonated at a variety of levels, visual images also obscured problems of internal contradictions or irrational association. Such harmonics were exactly what gave style products their added value beyond pragmatic utility. Evocative pictures upped the consumption ante by inducing the customer to obtain the fullest satisfaction by buying the ad along with the product. ## Industrial Design and Austerity Alvar Aalto (see here onward) designed the Finnish Pavilion for the 1939 New York Worlds Fair, and in that same year the Museum of Modern Art sponsored an exhibition of Scandinavian design that featured a selection of Aaltos own modern furniture. MoMA reacted in the 1930s against what they referred to as the modernistic commercial motivations that influenced American industrial design, and advocated responsible standards based upon suitability to purpose, material, and process of manufacture. Such standards, echoing the European tradition of design reform, were implied in the 1934 Machine Art exhibition (see here) and were embodied in the household products chosen for a series of traveling exhibits entitled Useful Objects under Ten Dollars. In 1940, MoMA sponsored a competition entitled Organic Design in Home Furnishings. By organic curator Eliot F. Noyes (19101977), an architect who later was responsible for formulating a unified design policy for the IBM corporation, meant molded forms that were manufactured using industrial processes and materials such as laminated woods. Flexibility was also organic when understood as units or modules for furniture and storage pieces that might be easily constructed from prefabricated components, expanded, and configured to meet a variety of needs. Department stores agreed to display the winning designs and were committed to finding manufacturers to produce and market them. One of the winning designs was a molded plywood chair upholstered in foam rubber covered with fabric, entered by Eero Saarinen (19101961) and Charles Eames (19071978), both of whom trained as architects (fig. 10.44). Saarinen was the son of the Finnish architect and president of the Cranbrook Academy of Art, Eliel Saarinen, and his wife, Loja (see here); Eames had remained at Cranbrook following a fellowship there as instructor and head of a new department in industrial design. The originality of the Saarinen/Eames collaboration lay in the more contoured three-dimensional molding of the plywood, easily seen in comparison with the plywood seat and back of Aaltos armchair for the Paimio Tuberculosis Sanitarium from 1931 (see fig. 9.64). In other respects, the industrial materials and processes as applied to furniture created an overall impression of lightness (the wooden legs were originally planned to be made from aluminum). ![](images/b188746a5f2bb0e0e73e346d875b82eda61dfad0e728dff533b010680f657b76.jpg) Eero Saarinen and Charles Eames, armchair, molded plywood and foam upholstery with fabric, produced for MoMA’s “Organic Design in Home Furnishings” exhibition, New York, 1940. Organic Design in Home Furnishings hoped to demonstrate the ability of an industrially based design to meet the practical needs of modern domestic living using technology and modern methods of manufacture and to replace the weary forms, materials, and methods of production in much traditional revival furniture. After the bombing of Pearl Harbor by the Japanese, and the United States entry into World War II in December 1941, the emphasis upon usefulness appeared as a proper political and patriotic response to shortages, restrictions, rationing, and the retooling of private industry to meet the demand for goods and materials to outfit and support the war effort. In this climate German émigré Hans Knoll (19141955) manufactured and marketed the furniture of Danish designer Jens Risom (19162016) for the company he had founded (Knoll Associates) in 1938. Designs embodied restrictions to ensure economy in the use of government-controlled raw materials such as timber and took into account shortages in skilled labor. Rather than experimenting with new materials such as plywood, the table and chairs in this dining-room ensemble used solid woods and traditional craft-based construction. French designer Jean Carlu (19001989) visited the United States in 1940 as an employee of the French Information Service and remained there during the Nazi occupation of France in May of that year. He designed his Production poster for the Office of Emergency Management using a clever combination of typography and image for the letter o that serves also as a hex nut, turned by a wrench tightly gripped by a powerful, gloved hand (fig. 10.49). Paul Rand (born Peretz Rosenbaum; 19141996) served as art director and designed covers for the cultural journal Direction between 1938 and 1945. Graphic designers during World War II not only created posters for recruitment, public service, and shaping public opinion, but also designed training manuals in which the efficient and strongly visual communication of information proved especially effective. German-born Will Burtin (19081972) organized the presentation of visual and textual information in the design of instructions for the operation of aircraft gunnery. His approach was neither entirely as artist nor propagandist, but rather as problemsolver and analyst, devising the clearest method for combining words and images for the purposes of facilitating instruction. Burtin took into consideration eye movement as well as the hierarchy and flow of information across two-page spreads using a cut-and-paste approach to composition (fig. 10.51). The design solution flowed from the requirements of the brief, and while not inevitable as the outcome of those requirements, the task of the designer suggested the methods of an engineer rather than the artist or advertiser. According to graphic designer and historian Richard Hollis, Burtins training manual helped to halve the training time for gunners, from twelve to six weeks. While it may not be difficult to distinguish Ruhlmanns Grand Salon from Rodchenkos Soviet Workers Room at the 1925 exhibition of decorative and industrial art in Paris as opposing approaches to modern living, such obvious comparisons do not convey the complex relationship among visions of modernity over the course of these decades, nor the variations, internal shifts, and differences among particular Modernist ideologies and practices and the specific political contexts in which they took shape and developed. In this treatment of Modernism, the intention is to demonstrate that despite the label International Style that emerged with the Museum of Modern Art exhibition of 1932, Modernist initiatives originated from specific local contexts that reveal the various meanings designers attached to the formal visual language they employed. Many designers and movements rejected historicism yet also demonstrated continuity with the past: Art Deco with elements of progressive late nineteenth-century movements such as Art Nouveau, and Modernism with the German Werkbund and its search for more anonymous, practical forms for household goods and furnishings. Certainly the aftermath of World War I lent urgency, a polemical tone, and utopian character to many Modernist projects, based upon an almost militant rejection of existing forms of authority, while the growth of advertising during this time sometimes made commercial exigencies overshadow radical ideologies and determined actual associations between design and industry. Another concern of many Modernist designers and movements was working- class or universal needs rather than luxury living. Still, the role of art in Modernist design varied among its practitioners, and makes any generalization difficult. Nevertheless, socialized housing, minimum requirements for living, Le Corbusiers theory of mechanical selection, and theories of elemental geometric forms underlying the varieties of design practice, all discussed above, informed the activities of Modernist designers, and their often visionary ideas and projects gained broader currency in response to the worldwide economic depression of the 1930s and wartime austerity. Even acknowledging the loose collection of ideas that comprises Modernism, it is important to recognize that the achievement of its utopian vision required more than the design and production of goods and furnishings in a competitive marketplace; behind the Modernist vision was the hope for a complete reorientation of living that required the education of the New Man and New Woman. Early expressions of this Modernist project often have an apocalyptic tone, forecasting the destruction of the old and the emergence of the entirely new. The Russian Revolution and socialist governments in parts of Europe provided political support for several experiments in the design and construction of socialized housing along Modernist lines. Yet at the same time designers such as Josef Frank in Austria and Alvar Aalto in Finland remained advocates of the frank use of industrial materials and modern techniques of production but took into consideration the individual and psychological needs of consumers. Others, such as Mies van der Rohe, demanded high production standards and aesthetic purity that remained exclusive and less applicable to low-cost planning. During the 1930s, Modernism was not a strict set of design principles, nor the result of revolutionary social and political ideology, but an increasingly flexible approach to the advantages and aesthetic possibilities of modern industrial materials and technology, eschewing traditional associations with luxury and ornament, and yet not entirely determined by the criteria of engineering. Less revolutionary and utopian in vision, Art Deco designers reasserted the more traditional liberal belief in an alliance between art and manufacturing and the role of the decorative arts in elevating the taste and improving the quality of life through comfort, convenience, and expanded possibilities for leisure activity. This movement gained momentum from post-World War I reconstruction, with the visual arts viewed as an expression of creative freedom, originality, and national identity and pride. The style originated at the top, in the world of high fashion and hedonism, although designers responded to popular entertainment in the form of jazz as well as to modern dance that crossed class and racial lines, often by exposure to diverse cultural expressions in cosmopolitan centers such as Paris or New York. Mass-circulation magazines, department stores, cinema, and advertising expanded its market and associations with an active and fun-loving lifestyle throughout the 1920s. In the United States modern design flourished in the expanding consumer economy of the 1920s, stimulated by exhibitions stemming from the Paris exhibition of 1925 and the emigration of European designers working independently or under contract to manufacturers. The extension of design to the utilitarian products of mechanized and standardized mass production, such as kitchen appliances or assembly-line automobiles, provided the basis for designers to work for large-scale industries in order to add consumer appeal to such products and stimulate sales, and in tandem with advertising agencies and departments. The phenomenon of Streamlining offers an insight into the complex relationship among competing commercial, engineering-based, and aesthetic interests in the expanding role of design for industry in the 1930s. In exhibitions and through competitions beginning in the early 1930s, the fledgling Museum of Modern Art in New York displayed and advocated for its own version of European Modernism, based at first upon architecture and then expanding to industrial design. MoMA focused its attention upon a particular application of technology to manufactured products, emphasizing aesthetic purity as the result of mechanized production and eschewing original or historical revival decoration. Further justifications for MoMAs design standards included hygiene and an avoidance of overt luxury during the depressed economic conditions of the 1930s. And yet, in addition to models for low-cost housing drawn from the Weissenhof exhibition in Stuttgart in 1927, MoMA also included in its developing canon of Modernism one-off houses for the wealthy such as Le Corbusiers Villa Savoye (19281931) and Viennese émigré Richard Neutras Lovell House (19271929) in southern California, based upon a refined aesthetic sensibility responding to light, open transparent spaces, and smooth mirror-like surfaces whether expanses of glass or polished marble. After World War II and amid economic recovery, MoMAs exhibitions continued to promote its approach to modern design under the name of Good Design, appealing initially to families purchasing homes during the post-war housing boom, to be treated in Part V. ## Global Inspiration IV The Americas This fourth installment of Global Inspiration considers the broader Americas, a vast and exciting area encompassing North, South, and Central American nations, often marginalized as being somehow less central or significant to the history of modern design. In the United States the Spanish-Colonial missions established in California from the later eighteenth century attest to the hybrid Spanish and Indigenous American element in the countrys history, not surprisingly in the American West. The so-called Mission Style used the Indigenous building materials of stucco applied to brick that persisted well into the 1920s in southern California for houses, churches, and municipal buildings. Travel, and perhaps year-round sunshine, drew tourists, artists, and designers to the southwestern United States and to the decorative arts traditions of Indigenous Americans and the monuments of Aztec and Mayan civilization in textiles, pottery, jewelry, and basket-weaving, in a sense not only keeping alive these skills but revitalizing them as well, albeit beholden to tourism and a collectors market. Albert C. Barnes and John D. Rockefeller were among the twentieth-century buyers of Indigenous American artifacts, analyzed and published in catalogs, displayed in exhibitions, and preserved in their collections. More elaborate decorative motifs resulting in original, if fanciful, combinations of Spanish colonial and Indigenous Latin American culture adorned the façades and interiors of theatres and hotels throughout the American West, at times treated abstractly as in the Phoenix Biltmore Hotel in Arizona (1929) or in the exuberant Hispano-Moresque molded decoration of the domed Bellagio Theatre in Los Angeles (1926). ![](images/b8db188bf8c0c2187a6bc6b4e74180bf40b1033dcdcd53accf2cfb8cc5300db8.jpg) GI4.1 Louis Ipsen and Victor F. One has to wonder whether Frederick Hurten Rhead was aware of this living tradition of pottery design during his time in California as a studio potter and whether his tableware name Fiesta suggests a debt to Mexico (see fig. 10.30). Colorful Mexican pottery also contributed to the concept of Mexicanidad that developed after the Mexican Revolution (191020), as seen in the objects, folk art, and colorful interiors of artists Frida Kahlo (19071954) and Diego Riveras (18861957) home (now a museum) in Mexico City and known as the Casa Azul. Kahlo enjoyed wearing clothing based upon traditional Mexican fashion including the square-necked huipil, and Mexican fashions were featured in the October 1937 issue of Vogue as well as in government-sponsored films promoting tourism to the region. In 1938 Argentinian architect-designers Antoni Bonet (19131989), Juan Kurchan (19131975), and Jorge Ferrari-Hardoy (19141977) traveled to Paris as assistants in the studio of Le Corbusier, who at the time was working on a plan for the redesign of the city of Buenos Aires (he also served as a consultant for the Ministry of Education and Health Building in Rio de Janeiro in 1937, part of an extensive urban reform project constructed under Brazilian dictator Getúlio Vargas). Returning to Argentina, they formed the partnership Grupo Austral, producing a lightweight, collapsible wrought-iron-framed chair slung with folded fabric known as theBKF (fig. GI4.3), named for the initials of their last names. The chair consists of two bent and intersecting wrought-iron loops or hairpins that are welded to one another. The quatrefoil-shaped leather seat and back fit round the upper bent corners of the two welded-iron loops. Thin, lightweight, portable, assembled rather than elaborately crafted, the BKF exemplified Modernist concerns with industrial materials and inexpensive assembly. It was manufactured by Artek, the same company that produced the designs of Finnish architect Alvar Aalto (see here) and was marketed and sold in the United States through a New York-based distributor. Two BKF chairs were purchased by the Museum of Modern Art, and after World War II the chair was manufactured by the Knoll Corporation (see here). When inexpensive knock-off versions of the chair appeared on the market, Knoll sued unsuccessfully to protect its exclusive rights to produce the chair, but the courts denied their claim on the grounds that the chair was simply a modified version of a late nineteenth-century wooden collapsible camp chair and was therefore not an original design. In Buenos Aires, in addition to the Grupo Austral, Bauhauseducated graphic designer Grete Stern (19041999) and her husband, photographer Horacio Coppola (19062012), who had moved to the city in 1935, worked in the cosmopolitan city, producing photographs, photomontages, and advertisements that often celebrated modern urban life transformed by electricity at night and the bustling energy of the streets filled with shoppers and outdoor cafés. ![](images/e8755d4372d38c16f87937e912bd26b2a619b1d148007ba53dc268e2fe77bfd5.jpg) Grupo Austral (Antoni Bonet, Juan Kurchan, Jorge Ferrari-Hardoy), BKF folding chair, painted wrought iron, leather, (88.3 × 75 × 75 cm), Buenos Aires, Argentina, 1938. Museum of Modern Art, New York. The scope of design produced in or inspired by the Indigenous and Hispanic Americas during the interwar period was broad and increasingly international. Some designers were drawn to the social promise of European Modernist movements, while others sought to define a national identity based upon the revival of local craft traditions but updated through the introduction of modern industrial materials and production. Both approaches reached broader audiences through marketing, travel, tourism, print, and entertainment. Portuguese-speaking Brazil, and Rio de Janeiro in particular, appears in popular cinema as a free and fun-loving destination for European or North American tourists to escape more proper conventions of social behavior at home. One can observe this stereotypical depiction in the 1933 RKO Pictures film Flying Down to Rio, starring the dancing-act team of Ginger Rogers and Fred Astaire, who show off their skills at a nightclub to the rhythms of a lively tango band dressed in flounce-sleeved shirts and sombreros (fig. GI4.4). There is certainly a hint of Orientalism or cultural bias in the image of Latin America as it is represented in popular entertainment, alluring in its exotic, authentic traditions and pastimes, yet at the same time beholden, even subservient to Western technology and investment. A carefree hedonism appears as well in colorful illustrated Brazilian magazines of the 1920s such as Para Todos or A Maça, often featuring scantily clad young women enjoying the citys night life but dressed in modern European fashion rather than in traditional folk costume.
