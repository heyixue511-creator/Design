After completing her academic training, she worked as a designer at Johansfors glassworks from 1968 to 1972, prior to establishing her own ceramics studio in 1972. From 1981 to 1998, she was one of the principal designers at Skruf glassworks, producing a number of notable designs, not least her Bellman range (1992), which re-popularized the use of small schnapps glasses, and her widely published Mamsel/ carafe (1990). Her glass designs for Skruf possessed a remarkable simplicity of form, which enabled the intrinsic nature of the material to emerge, producing a sense of lightness and fragility. Like that of earlier Scandinavian glassware designers from the 1930s and 1940s, Råman's work has an unpretentious quality, which contrasts strongly with the colourful and at times bravado work of other contemporary Swedish glass designers. Her belief that design should synthesize “simplicity, function and aesthetic values" is demonstrated by her Oil Bottle of 1990, which has a spherical stopper that is slightly, though imperceptibly, flattened so that it will not roll off a table. Råman continually varies her product designs so as to create “family groups” of vases, bottles, bowls and jugs. Since 1999 she has produced art glass for the Orrefors glassworks, which like her earlier designs for Skruf is notable for the purity of its forms. These pieces, however, such as the Tanterella, Skyline and Slowfox vases of 2000, are embellished with simple, non-figurative etched motifs that enhance the inherent qualities of the material. She has also designed ceramics for Gustavsberg, worked as a design consultant to Trussardi in Milan, and advised the Portuguese government on design education. A. Kolehmainen and H. G. Paloheimo in 1910 as a glass manufactory. Initially the glassworks concentrated on the production of domestic glassware, but in 1911 a second factory was established for the production of container glass (bottles and jars). In 1919, the company began the manufacture of blown window glass, and by the 1920s it had become the largest glassware manufacturer in Finland. Around this time it began commissioning domestic product designs from, among others, Tyra Lundgren (1897-1979) and Eva Gyldén (1885-1973). In 1928, the glassworks sponsored a glass competition that was won by Henry Ericsson (1898-1933), who was a close friend of Alvar Aalto (having shared a studio with him). Riihimäki subsequently hired Ericsson, who designed engraved glassware that was shown at the 1929-1930 “Exposición Internacional de Barcelona"and at the 1930 “Exposition Internationale, Coloniale, Maritime et d'Art Flamand” in Antwerp. In 1932, upon Ericsson's insistence the company hiredGunnel Nyman, who remained at the glassworks until 1947, designing both production glass and art glass. Riihimäki sponsored other public competitions in 1933 and 1936, for which it received submissions from, among others, Alvar Aalto and Arttu Brummer (1891-1951), both of whom later designed products for the company. After the factory was modernized in the mid-1930s, the companywas re-christened Riihimäki Lasi Oy in 1937. Two years later, it began the production of crystal glassware and in 1941 merged with the Kauklahti and Ryttylä glassworks. In 1945, Brummer designed his Finlandia vase, which with its broken-ice-like form predicted the more expressive approach to glassware design that flourished in Finland during the post-war years. The following year, the company was joined by Helena Tynell (b. 1918), who similarly explored the expressive and optical qualities ofglass with sculptural asymmetrical forms. In 1949 Nanny Still McKinney also began designing glassware for Riihimäki, which included blown art glass as well as moulded utility glass that distinguished itself by its unusual forms and texturedsurface decoration. Still McKinney remained at Riihimäki until 1976, when the glassworks discontinued its production of blow-moulded glass to concentrate solely on the mass-manufacture of commercial glass, such as bottles and jars. Although it was the largest producer of household glassware in Finland from the 1920s to the 1960s, and was also the first Nordic company to manufacture lightweight bottles in 1983, Riihimäki eventually merged with the Karhula glassworks in 1988 and was finally closed down in 1995.
