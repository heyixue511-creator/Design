# 4 Figurative constructivism and sociological graphics

Benjamin Benus

In August 1929 the Dutch artist Peter Alma and the Czech artist Augustin Tschinkel joined Gerd Arntz as employees of the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum in Wien to assist in designing charts for the atlas Gesellschaft und Wirtschaft. All three artists were affiliated with the international artists' organization known as the Gruppe progressiver Künstler (Group of progressive artists).¹ Together with the group's leading figures, the Cologne painters Franz Wilhelm Seiwert and Heinrich Hoerle, these artists developed a distinctive pictorial language, to which historians have applied the stylistic designation figurative constructivism'.² While Arntz, Alma, and Tschinkel produced their free' work (as they sometimes described their prints, drawings, and paintings of the 192os and 193os) independently of their design work with the Vienna Method of Pictorial Statistics, these two types of production share several key iconographic features. Indeed, some of the most important and frequently recurring Vienna Method pictograms, including those for the basic human figure, the worker-type, and the factory building – as well as those pictograms connected to automobile production and coalmining – were adapted directly from the artists' free work. Yet, the relationship between these two realms of activity has remained obscure. The nature of this relationship will be reassessed here by considering

the circumstances out of which the formal and thematic features of figurative constructivism originated, and by examining the ways in which the free and applied works were presented and discussed together in a variety of publications during the period of collaboration in Vienna.³

As their writings demonstrate, each of the participants in the Vienna collaboration conceived of the relationship between art and design in different terms. Arntz, in his later statements concerning the connection between the Progressive style' and the Vienna Method, always emphasized the distinction between his free and applied work.ª Alma and Tschinkel, by contrast, were far more emphatic in their writings about links between the two areas of production. For Seiwert and Hoerle (who, in any case, plaved no direct role in the design of pictorial statistics), the Vienna project may even be seen to run counter to certain aspects of their artistic programme.⁵ In considering this relationship, then, I do not wish to suggest that pictorial statistics were the culmination of the Progressives' work as printmakers and painters. To the extent that this essay draws parallels between the two types of work, it is with the goal of explaining why the Progressives' prints and drawings had such appeal for Otto Neurath, why the Progressives so actively promoted pictorial statistics in their own publications, and why certain graphic conventions associated with figurative constructivism were so effectively adapted to the design of Vienna Method pictograms.

Among the many pictograms designed by Arntz, Alma, and Tschinkel at the Gesellschafts- und Wirtschaftsmuseum, it is in the form of what Arntz called the standard-figure' that the Vienna Method's debt to the earliest manifestations of figurative constructivism is most evident above all, in the drawings and linocuts produced by Heinrich Hoerle between 1920 and 1923.8 In drawings such as ‘Mann (konstruktive Figur)', as well as in later linocuts like Prothesenkopf' (p. 219 above) and Der Europäer', Hoerle constructed his figures from geometric and mechanical forms – an approach to figuration eventually adopted by other Progressives. In introducing these mechanized beings into his work, Hoerle was responding to what had then become a widespread pictorial practice among artists of the international avant-garde, many of whom had recast the human image in a variety of uncanny guises, including automaton, puppet, mannequin, and tailor's dummy. Hoerle's engagement with this motif beginning in 192o can be explained, in part, by his encounter with the work of Giorgio de Chirico and Carlo Carrà, to which his friend and fellow artist, Max Ernst, had introduced him." In contrast, however, to the classicizing world in which the Italian painters frequently set their figures, Hoerle's mannequin imagery explicitlyaddressed a contemporary phenomenon, namely the plight of wounded veterans in the aftermath of the First World War. (Hoerle had served at the front as a field artillery telephone operator in 1917, and though he suffered no physical injuries himself he was deeply affected by the experience.)1º The dummy-like figure in Der Europäer', with its truncated limbs and mechanical parts, directly references the prosthetic anatomy of wounded war veterans. Indeed, it was the dramatic increase in the visibility of amputees in everyday life after the war – many of them fitted with prosthetic limbs – that in large part accounts for the prevalence of mannequin and automaton imagery among the international avant-garde during these years."

More so than for any of the other artists in his circle, the amputee is a persistent theme in Hoerle's work, though wounded veterans do make occasional appearances in works by Arntz and Alma.1² The amputees, for example, in Arntz's 1927 woodcut, 'Krankenhaus' (Hospital), as well as the truncated figures in his earlier woodcut 'Matrosen' (Sailors, 1925), were intended as a direct reference to the victims of the First World War.¹³ The amputee war veteran reappeared years later in Alma's 1937 oil painting, Oorlog (War), as well as in his anti-war (and anti-fascist) poster design of the previous year, 'De oorlog maakt de man' (War makes the man). In this last example it is noteworthy that Alma alluded to the conventions of the Vienna Method – in the repeated rows of amputeepictograms and the crosses that run along the poster's bottom edge, suggesting the immense scale of war's destruction.¹ª

Deeply connected with the experience of the war and the vision of the amputee's reconstructed body (which had supplied the source material for so many of Hoerle's automatons) was the image of the factory. The replacement of wounded veterans' lost limbs with prosthetic devices designed to perform specific assembly line tasks was a central component of post-war rehabilitation programmes in Germany. 15 Hoerle appears to have been responding to such developments with his 1922 oil painting Fabrikarbeiter (Factory worker), which shows a mechanical worker, limbs replaced by tools and face supplanted with what appears to be a pressure gauge, standing against a background of factory machinery.

The factory image would have had particular significance for artists and audiences in the Rhineland, a region which, over the previous half-century, had emerged as among the densest and most rapidly industrialized on the European continent. For Arntz especially, who descended from a family of local industrialists and for a time had worked in his father's Remscheid factory, the subject had personal significance.16 (See Arntz's oil painting on p. 216 above.) His factory depictions may be distinguished from those of his colleagues, however, in their frequent inclusion of elements taken from popular culture and allusions to fashions and entertainment imported from the United States. Arntz's fascination with the intersection of industry and entertainment is clearly articulated in his 1924 woodcut, 'Amerikanisches' (Things American), where rows of cars and female performers signify these two realms of culture. In this work each motif occupies its own horizontal register: the lower band depicts six female figures – faceless bodies in bathing suits arranged in chorus-line formation – while the row of cars resembling Ford Model-Ts fills the space above. In a particularly jarring contrast, the figure of a hanged man - the victim of a lynching – overlaps and connects the two registers at the left side of the image. The work, as Arntz later explained, was a response to the literature, films, and illustrated magazines then flooding into Germany from the UsA, through which he became familiar with images of both racial violence and bathing beauties'.¹7 Above all, Arntz cited as an inspiration Henry Ford's autobiography My life and work (1922), which had become an instant bestseller in Germany after the publication of the German edition in 1923.18 Through standardized forms and precise, geometric execution, Arntz sought to evoke Ford's rationalized assembly line production process, while his comparison between Model-Ts and showgirls, facilitated by their diagrammatic arrangement in separate registers, suggests a general equivalence between the mechanized character of production and the standardized forms of consumption. In this equation of production and consumption, work and leisure, Arntz's image foreshadows the analysis of Siegfried Kracauer, who later described the synchronized performances of chorus-line dancers and other forms of popular entertainment as 'the aesthetic reflex of the rationality to which the prevailing economic system aspires'.' 19

This fusion of soldier and worker in the mechanized figure of the automaton should not be understood, however, as a condemnation of rationalization per se; rather, such depictions aimed to highlight the perversion of technology under the current political and economic system. Under alternative (i.e. socialist) conditions, the Progressives believed, technological rationalization might be redirected to serve human needs. Thus, beyond a critique of the dehumanizing impact of modern warfare and the mechanized production process, the 'constructivist' figure also emerged as the expression of an ideological position. For Seiwert especially (who, early on, had established himself as the Progressives' principal theoretician) the deployment of 'constructivist' forms in the depiction of factory workers was linked to the political project of council communism. This movement, which emerged in the aftermath of Germany's November Revolution, promoted a system in which local councils, elected from within workplaces and factories, would manage both government and economy, and operate independently of any centralized party or state bureaucracy.2º The grassroots character and participatory political structure of the council system had great appeal for artists like Seiwert, who aimed in his art and his writing to promote a more inclusive, egalitarian, and radically democratic social order.

Council communism was an offshoot of the larger workers' council movement, which enjoyed mass support among the working class throughout Central Europe in the days leading up to and following the end of the First World War, gathering particular strength within the Rhine and Ruhr regions of Germany. Modelled on the Russian soviets that had emerged in the revolutions of 1905 and 1917, German soldiers' and workers' councils quickly spread in the early days of November 1g18 and seized power in cities throughout Germany. In addition to briefly assuming the functions of local governments, soldiers' and workers' councils pressed for a wide range of social reforms. These councils - though at times supported by or connected with various parties on the political left – were, by and large, the spontaneous creations of the working class and had as their primary

goal the introduction of greater democracy and equality in the workplace and in society at large. Fearing that they would carry out a repeat of the Bolshevik revolution, the Social Democrat-led provisional government (which had initially cooperated with and participated in the movement) ultimately took a position against the councils, in some cases suppressing them with military force. The councils, in actuality, were much more moderate

and reformist in their aims than was perceived by either their opponents or their more radical supporters. The council communist ideology to which Seiwert and the other Progressives subscribed, which had as its goal the socialization of the economy and the permanent institutionalization of the councils, was always a minority position within the councils. See Eberhard Kolb, The Weimar Republic (New York: Routledge, 2005), pp. 151–4.

Die Aktion, which was founded in Berlin in 1911 by the critic and publisher Franz Pfemfert, was one of the few avant-garde journals to take an explicit antiwar stance from the very start of the First World War. It was influential for the artistic as well as political development of Seiwert and Hoerle, both of whom had contributed graphics to Die Aktion since 1917. After 1920 Die Aktion became an important advocate for council communism, publishing the manifestos and guidelines of council communist factions such as the Allgemeine Arbeiter Union - Einheitsorganisation (AAU-E; General workers' union – unity organization), of which Seiwert and Arntz were both supporters. While the actual council movement had all but disintegrated by 1923 (and had reached the height of its political relevance in the period immediately following the November Revolution), Die Aktion continued to advance the cause of council communism well into the later part of the decade.

One of the most important channels through which Seiwert articulated his commitment to this movement was Die Aktion, a Berlin-based journal for art, literature, and politics, wherein artists and intellectuals debated the role that art should play in the revolutionary cause.2¹ Between 1922 and 1926 Seiwert's prints appeared on Die Aktion's cover on nine separate occasions, while Hoerle's work was featured on four journal covers during this period – and works by both artists appeared with even greater frequency within the journal's pages. These reproductions were of tremendous importance for the artistic development of other members of the Progressives, who first became acquainted with Seiwert and Hoerle's graphic work, as well as with the tenets of the council movement, through this source.2²

Positioned within the factory gears, these letters reinforce the idea that the workers occupy a strategic position within the production apparatus and remind one of the councils' capacity to jam up the wheels of production through organized strikes.

In this way, his images left the audience to reach an independent conclusion and invited their participation in the completion and fulfillment of the revolutionary project. This conformed to Seiwert's understanding of the revolution, which – rather than a vision dictated to the masses from above, to which they were expected to conform – would have to be a participatory event, created by the masses themselves and organized from the ground up. For Seiwert, the Betriebsorganisationen were emblematic of such grassroots participation: comprised of all workers in a factory and created to serve their interests, these organizations elected their leaders through a democratic process and constituted the basic units forming the workers' councils.

The factory organization also became a recurring subject in prints by Arntz and Tschinkel. Tschinkel's 1928 linocut, ‘BO (Fabrik)', with its grouping of geometrized workers' heads at the lower edge of the image, framed above by factory wheels and transmission belts, reads like an abstract version of Seiwert's woodcut from several years earlier. Bohnen has, once again, interpreted this work in a way that highlights its diagrammatic character: here the two transmission belts form a triangle at the picture's right edge, which encapsulates the initials 'bo' (the placement of which is nearly identical to that in the earlier Seiwert print) and effectively drives a wedge between the workers at the lower part of the image and the factory machinery which subjugates them from above.28

This diagrammatic quality is equally pronounced in another woodcut by Arntz titled 'Räte- und Betrieb Organisation' (Council and factory organization), which appeared on the cover of a 1928 issue of Die proletarische Revolution (Proletarian revolution).29 Here the forms are arranged like a flow chart, suggesting a structure in which workers are organized (literally from the ground up) through local factory organizations, and then connected to one another through larger workers' councils.³º

These later works by Arntz and Tschinkel may be seen, in part, as a synthesis of Hoerle and Seiwert's respective approaches. In their precise execution and geometric approach to figuration, these works draw upon Hoerle's images of mechanized automatons and suggest a similarly ambivalent attitude towards technological modernity. At the level of iconography and pictorial structure they are undoubtedly informed by Seiwert's work, as well as by his revolutionary politics. It should be understood that the Progressives conceived of this pictorial language – the geometrized figuration, the tendency towards diagrammatic compositions, and the typological treatment of subject matter (about
