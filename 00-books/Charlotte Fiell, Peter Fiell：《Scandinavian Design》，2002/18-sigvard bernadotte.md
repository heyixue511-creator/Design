Sigvard Bernadotte, Model no. 1015 silver coffee set with plastic bound handles for Georg Jensen, c. 1953 ![](images/2b403d9043770724143f629ed26a5fb11676441abb6cc2937d00966ba86b4565.jpg) Sigvard Bernadotte, Model no. 9 Bernadotte silver cutlery for Georg Jensen, 1939 # sigvard bernadotte (b. 1907 Stockholm – d.2002 Stockholm, Sweden) ![](images/b8d0f4732f37e38a9f735401e45ed469c28cfe659187c217314d826c9cb09b06.jpg) The son of King Gustav VI Adolf of Sweden Count Sigvard Bernadotte was one of the earliest pioneers of industrial design in Scandinavia. Initially, he studied political science as well as several other subjects at the Faculty of Art, Uppsala University, but it was decided that his artistic talent should be cultivated and so he went on to study drawing and painting under Olle Hjortzberg at the Kungliga Konsthögskolan (Royal University College of Fine Arts) in Stockholm from 1929 to 1931. Bernadotte later completed his studies at the Staatsschule fürAngewandte Kunst (National School of Applied Arts), Munich, in 1931, and following this joined the GeorgJensen workshop. Here he became the first designer in the company to adoptgeometric forms as opposed to the naturalistic Arts & Crafts influenced formsthat were so characteristic of the silversmith's output. His Bernadotte flatware of 1939demonstrates the angularity of form that was typical of his work from this period. He also designed a volumetric metal and Bakelite cocktail shaker during the late 1930s for an English manufacturer. During the 1930s he travelled throughout Europe, Africa and, most importantly for his future career, America, where he visited several leading industrial design consultants including Raymond Loewy (1893–1986), Walter Dorwin Teague (1883–1960), Donald Deskey(1894-1989)and Henry Dreyfuss(1904– 1972). Leaving his position as a director of Georg Jensen, he established a Copenhagen-based industrial design consultancy in 1950 with the Danish designer Acton Bjørn (who had previously worked in collaboration with Douglas andBoeing on the design of aircraft for SAS). Known as Sigvard Bernadotte & Acton Bjørn, Industridesign, Merkantil Grafik, this multi-disciplinary practice's first notable success was a compact metal and plastic adding-machine(1952) for Odhner. The practice was later expanded, with offices being opened in Stockholm and New York. Over their careers together, Bernadotte and Bjørn designed a number of widely celebrated industrial products, includingthe melamine Margrethe stacking bowls for Rosti (1950), which had non-slip rubber bases and highly practical pouring spouts and handgrips, and the compact Privat typewriter (1960) for Facit in “Viking grey”, which reflected the influence of Marcello Nizzoli's(1887–1969)earlier Lettera typewriter for Olivetti (1950). Bernadotte & Bjørn was the first industrial design practice in Denmark and one of the earliest in Sweden, andin the 1950s became the first Scandinavian design consultancy to achieve international recognition. In 1964, Bernadotte went on to independently found the Bernadotte Design Studio, which specialized in heavy industrial design, from forklift trucks to outboard motors.
