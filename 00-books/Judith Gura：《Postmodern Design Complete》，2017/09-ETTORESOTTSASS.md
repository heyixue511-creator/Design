# ETTORE SOTTSASS 1917-2007 AUSTRIAN-BORN, ITALIAN-BRED MEMPHIS FOUNDER, PROLIFIC DESIGNER AND PUBLIC FACE OF POSTMODERNISM. If one designer can be said to have served as the public face of postmodern design, it was Ettore Sottsass (also see under “Living with Postmodern Design"). In his role as the quotable spokesperson of the MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design") collaborative, he was a passionate promoter of the style and his like-minded colleagues. During an almost seven-decade career that began along more conventional lines, he deftly negotiated the barriers between modern industrial design, radical rebellion, and a new generation of architects and designers. Sottsass was best known for his furniture and glass designs, all of which exhibited a consistent love of color, pattern, and provocative deviation from conventional form. Sottsass was born in Innsbruck, Austria, and grew up in Italy, where his family moved when he was twelve years old, in part so that he could follow in his father's footsteps by studying architecture.37 He received his architectural degree from the Politecnico di Torino (i939), served in the army during the Second World War, and worked in his father's practice before moving to Milan in i946, where he opened an architecture and industrial design practice and began writing for Domus. Hired as a design consultant for Olivetti in 1957, Sottsass applied his admiration of pop culture to normally staid consumer goods: he co-designed Italy's first mainframe computer, a sleek black form of which only about forty were made, and drew public notice with the lipstickred Valentine typewriter (1969).38 Frustrated by the constraints of corporate work, Sottsass broadened his range, in 1964 designing a group of monolithic cupboards covered in plastic laminate—a tacky facade that mocked the stodgy form and prefigured his more extreme pieces of the i98os. He began to work in ceramics, his large-scale Totems (ı965) being one result, and was retained as art director for Poltronova\*, designing furniture for them from i966. Sottsass also began to participate in experimental projects with the Radical Design groups: he was part of both Studio Alchimia\* and the Global Tools\* collective. His founding of Memphis in 198o grew out of these early activities, but with Memphis he moved from theoretical concepts to designing actual products for sale. Taking the spotlight on an international stage, Memphis established Postmodernism as the style of the moment. Sottsass drew inspiration wherever he traveled, including from America, where he worked briefly in George Nelson's office and later spent time after traveling to California in 1962 for medical treatment. There, Sottsass was intrigued by Pop art and radicals like the Beat poet Allen Ginsberg. His frequent trips to India were another important influence on his work, reflected in his fondness for brilliant colors and in objects that reference mysticism or have Indian-inspired names, such as the Offerta a Shiva (i964), the Ashoka lamp (1981), and the Tantra collection (i986).39 In 1972, Sottsass participated in "Italy: The New Domestic Landscape" at the Museum of Modern Art in New York, contributing a series of fiberglass microenvironments on casters that could be rearranged as needed by the occupants. Like much of his work, it received considerable attention and critical praise. In Sottsass's furniture, rectilinear or angular silhouettes are accentuated by vivid color and pattern; his glassware uses similarly bright hues to embellish curvilinear forms. Many pieces are implicitly erotic or ceremonial, including the Tantra group and a set of glass objects (produced after his death) inspired by kachina dolls, which he had seen when visiting Nelson years earlier. Working in a greater range of media than his Memphis colleagues, Sottsass was a prolific talent, designing a variety of objects for some one hundred clients,4º including jewelry for ACME and CLETO MUNARI and objects for ALESSI (see all under "Edited Design"). For his design consultancy, Sottsass Associati, he assembled a group of enthusiastic and talented young designers, four of whom— ALDO CIBIC, GEORGE SOWDEN, MATTEO THUN, and MARCO ZANINI (see all under “Design") —would become part of Memphis. The firm took on large projects like the design of stores and showrooms for the clothing company Esprit\*, as well as interiors, exhibitions, and furniture. After leaving Memphis in 1985, Sottsass continued to design with his characteristic enthusiasm. Having created relatively few pieces for Memphis, he introduced a collection of twenty-five assertive objects called Furniture of the Rituals of Life for New York's Blum Helman Warehouse that same year, using luxury materials he had previously avoided: marble, exotic woods, glass, and mosaic. The limited-edition designs included tall cabinets and long tables with names like In Praise of Epicurus and What's the Name of Your Swiss Bank? As serious about the theories behind his architecture as he was committed to their execution, Robert Venturi was the first of his contemporaries to make waves in the calm sea of Modernism. With the publication of Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture in i966, he called attention to the idea that architecture was not a simple matter of following rules, but should offer a depth of meaning and the “messy vitality" of the built environment that had been erased in the sterile glass boxes of the International Style. Dismissing Mies van der Rohe's celebrated phrase "less is more," he proclaimed, "less is a bore," and followed his dictum in his designs. The i972 book Learning From Las Vegas, written with his wife and collaborator Denise Scott Brown, and Steven Izenour, explored urban sprawl, celebrating vernacular architecture and the roadside "decorated shed." Though in later years Venturi and Scott Brown (also see under "Living with Postmodern Design") took exception with the course taken by postmodern architecture, decrying what they labeled PoMo, the concepts outlined in these influential works provided much of the foundation for Postmodernism's embrace of historicism and contextualism. Robert Venturi was born in Philadelphia and attended Princeton University, where he received an undergraduate degree in 1947 and his MArch in i95o. He worked in the offices of Oscar Stonorov and Eero Saarinen and, in i954, was awarded a Prix de Rome. Spending two years at the American Academy in Rome, Venturi met Louis Kahn, and returned home to Philadelphia to work in Kahn's office. He began his own architectural practice in ı958, and joined with John Rauch in 1964 to establish Venturi & Rauch (now Venturi, Scott Brown & Associates). From 1951, Venturi taught architectural theory at the University of Pennsylvania for several years, where he formulated the concepts outlined in his writings. His first architectural commission was the home completed in 1964 for his mother Vanna, in the Philadelphia suburb of Chestnut Hill. His best-known building, it was perhaps the earliest work of postmodern architecture, with a facade that commingles several architectural styles. A huge influence on architectural pedagogy, particularly in the United States, it led schools to re-examine their neglect of historic precedents. It was followed by major projects in what became known as postmodern style that include Guild ![](images/bb02f2fe2a5920f6f6db68c7412141fad1461d6037616620e947c981636f2205.jpg) House (i964), a residence for senior citizens; the classically pedimented Sainsbury Wing of London's National Gallery (ı991); and private residences combining historical and vernacular vocabularies. Venturi's first venture into product design was a collection of nine chairs designed between 1978 and i984 for Knoll\*. The laminated-plywood chairs were formed like silhouettes, with backs shaped in cartoon-like depictions of classic period styles ranging from Queen Ańne to Art Deco. As the silhouettes translate historiç shapes into a modern material, the decoration recalls polychrome ornament. Screenprinted with colorful motifs appropriate to each style, the chairs—particularly the Queen Anne, covered in a “grandmother's tablecloth" pattern—were instant successes with architecture and design aficionados and are among the most recognizable postmodern objects. For ALESSI (see under "Edited Design"), Venturi designed a tea and coffee service that used historical styles as reference, including a Rococo coffee pot, a neoclassical sugar bowl and an Art Nouveau creamer. He also designed a cartoon-like riff on a traditional cuckoo clock, evidence of the tongue-in-cheek attitude that underscores all his work: he is often quoted as calling himself "the Dennis the Menace of architecture," after the mischievous comic character. Denise Scott Brown was born in Zambia, attended the University of the Witwatersrand in South Africa, and graduated from the Architectural Association, London in 1955. She received Master's degrees in architecture and city planning in 196o and i965 respectively from the University of Pennsylvania, where she and Venturi met while they were both teaching. They married in 1967, when Scott Brown was teaching in California, and she relocated to Philadelphia to join his firm, becoming a principal in i969; it was renamed Venturi, Rauch & Scott Brown in i98o. In addition to her architectural work, Scott Brown has taught and lectured at the University of Pennsylvania School of Fine Arts, and at Harvard, Yale, and Princeton universities. Working primarily as a team, the Venturis exemplify both innovative Postmodernism and a productive design partnership. More than that, their work underscores the importance of commitment to an idea, regardless of convention. Having given life to the International Style in i93ı, the Museum of Modern Art gave grounds for its rejection in i975, claiming, “Although the modern movement succeeded in creating individual masterpieces, it has failed disastrously in urban planning."' The international exhibition “The Presence of the Past" at the i98o Venice Biennale was a manifesto for postmodern architecture, drawing on history to paint a picture of warmth, color, and approachability, and reminding architects of what had been lost in Modernism's rejection of historicism.² But postmodernist architecture at its most flamboyant was more an American phenomenon than a European one, encouraged by theorists and journalists looking for new directions. Author Tom Wolfe's From Bauhaus to Our House (i98ı) was a bestselling diatribe against a sterile architectural style foisted on the public by elitist tastemakers.3 There were two approaches to postmodern architecture's mode of looking back: one more overtly (and sometimes cartoonishly) historicist and symbolic, and the other more conservatively replicating classical styles. Postmodern buildings often retained the International Style's rectilinear forms and flat roofs, selectively adding elements from other periods, including geometric shapes, peaked or arched rooflines, and classical ornamentation. Each architect, of course, had his or her own interpretation, in a strategic borrowing that often combined different styles in assemblages ranging from subtly witty to sternly serious.4 Building designs became busier; nostalgia was accepted, even encouraged; and historicism embraced all periods, from classical to neo-Gothic.⁵ The bellwether of postmodern style had been ROBERT VENTURI's (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") house for his mother, built in 1964, though the first to attract broad public notice were MICHAEL GRAVES's (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and “Living with Postmodern Design") Portland Building (i982) and Philip Johnson's\* publicity-generating and critically trashed AT&T Building (i984). Other noteworthy early examples included TERRY FARRELL's TV-am (i982), JAMES STIRLING's Neue Staatsgalerie (1983), and CHARLES MOORE's Piazza d'Italia (ı978). In America, the style flourished during a real estate boom as an antidote to the tedious corporate structures that had proliferated in the i96os and ı97os. Postmodernism offered the ideal means of creating instant brand identity, as witnessed in Michael Graves's Team Disney building (1991), with a frieze of the Seven Dwarfs on the facade. Understandable even to a lay audience, such buildings made the most of Postmodernism's ability to convey multivalent messages and act as corporate signposts. Fillips like Farrell's giant eggcups on the TV-am roof and the faux-classical capitals on JOHN OUTRAM's Pumping Station added a note of humor, as architecture celebrated ornament for its own sake. Even establishment firms adopted postmodern imagery: Kohn Pedersen Fox for the Procter & Gamble headquarters in Cincinnati; César Pelli for the World Trade Center in New York.
