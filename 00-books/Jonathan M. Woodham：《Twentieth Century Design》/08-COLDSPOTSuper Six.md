However, other more strident voices launched more direct attacks on contemporary industrial culture in the United States. Henry Ford, whose assembly-line techniques were increasingly widely applied in factories throughout the industrialized world, was a particular target of those who saw life in the USA as shaped by the spiritual dictatorship of machinery, warfare and magazines and newspapers of large circulation'.11 For example, in Brave New World of 1932 Aldous Huxley attacked wide-spread faith in the concepts of the large-scale and standardization which he saw as major ingredients of what he feared would become a loveless and emotionally arid society, a society in which Fordism was the dominant religion, with Ford replacingGod and Ford's autobiography the Bible. Yet for the consumer or user their forms and decoration were often laden with meaning and associations, whether or not Pevsner in his Enquiry into Industrial Art of 1937 condemned them as part of the 9o per cent of industrial products that he adjudged to be ‘artistically objectionable', whether or not they were designed by a named designer or simply copied from, or variants on, long-standing precedents. With the exception of museums such as the Geffrye Museum in London or site museums such as Ironbridge Gorge or Beamish examples of this kind are rarely collected by museums, let alone located in the context ofordinary' rooms. Such objects are generally seen as artistically deficient rather than culturally significant; documentation concerning their use by consumers is hard to come by; often material concerning their design, production, and distribution has often simply been thrown away. The Art Deco style exerted a significant impact in Britain across many design media and was a highly commercial style, including many items at the cheap, novelty end of the market. Its flat, coloured, geometric patterns could be found in the ‘sunrise' motifs (contemporary symbols of health) on thousands of wooden garden gates, front doors, gable ends, packaging, fabric designs, carpets, tableware, jewellery, and other ornamental products. Its zig-zag, chevron, and other geometric patterns could also be found in typography, posters, and shop-fronts. The Deco vocabulary was widely adopted in both interior and exterior designs for cinemas, particularly those associated with Oscar Deutsch's Odeon chain.
