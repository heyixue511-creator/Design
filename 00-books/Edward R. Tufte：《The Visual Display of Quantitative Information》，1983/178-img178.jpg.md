Lines in data graphics should be thin. One reason eighteenthand nineteenth-century graphics look so good is that they were engraved on copper plates, with a characteristic hair-thin line. The drafting pens of twentieth-century mechanical drawing thickened linework, making it clumsy and unattractive. An effective aesthetic device is the orthogonal intersection of lines of different weights: ![](images/db21d4f7c5abae49749a8f842e1e9a77b30133a50103bb8b084f62b59155f411.jpg) Poster for the exhibition "Mondrian and Neo-Plasticism in America," Yale University Art Gallery, October 18 to December 2, 1979. The original painting was done in 1941 by Diller; see Nancy J. Troy, Mondrian and Neo-Plasticism in America (New Haven, 1979), p. 28.
