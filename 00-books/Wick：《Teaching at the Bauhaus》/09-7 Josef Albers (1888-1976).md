# 7 Josef Albers (1888-1976)

## A New Concept?

Any representation of the preliminary course at the Bauhaus would beincomplete without an examination of Josef Albers's contribution. Under his leadership the fundamental tasks of this propaedeutic course remained intact: removing the shellac of the academic view of art from the Bauhaus initiates, releasing creative energies, confrontation not with art but with the most elementary design problems. With respect to didactics

and methodology, however, in the view of Lux Feininger, sonof LyonelFeininger,Albers changed “the concept of the course itself . . . so drastically... that nothingbut the name remained."1

This assessment is surely accurate to the extent that Albers gave the preliminary course a new, unmistakable profile. It should not be overlooked, however, that he was working in a very specific context of conditions and tradition at the Bauhaus, since he himself hadtaken part in Itten's preliminary course as a student and from 1923 to 1928 he was active in a teaching capacity in the course, alongside Moholy-Nagy, before he wasgivencomplete responsibility for it from 1928on.²Bothexperiences—being taughtby Itten and collaborating with Moholy-Nagy—had, as we will see, consequences for Albers's teaching, so that Lux Feininger's statement, which gives the impression of a radical new beginning or a fundamental change of course, would seem to be in need of qualification.

Werner Spies has outlined what—despite the continuity asserted here, which will be demonstrated below—clearly distinguishes the preliminary course as Albers taught it from that of his predecessors:

[N]o one ever conducted a more comprehensive, systematic preliminary course than he. His teachingwas probably the most universal—it was not, like that of Itten, geared only to the creative individual, and was also not directedprincipally toward the constructivist, like, for instance, that of Moholy-Nagy. BothIttenand Moholy-Nagy had as their ideal the individual achievement, the individual artistry. Albers' teaching encompassed a much wider sphere—a comprehensive visual training. The works done by the students of Albers' preliminary course which are still valuable show

that he did not want them to think and compose primarily in a formal manner but that the important thing was the intensive use and study of a given working material.³

Chapters 5 and 6 will have made clear that these remarks about Itten and Moholy are untenable as stated. Nevertheless, we can agree with Spies in one respect: the concern with materials became the focalpoint of the preliminary course under Albers.

During the Bauhaus years in particular, an interest in exploring the technological and aesthetic possibilities of materials was one of the central aspects of Albers's own artistic work. Because with Albers——as with Itten, Moholy-Nagy, Kandinsky, Klee, and Schlemmer—pedagogical and artistic aspects were directly interconnected, and to some extent even determined each other, it will aid our understanding if we first examine the key aspects of the artist's aesthetic practice-both his artistic oeuvre and his activity as a designer—before movingon to examine the preliminary course.

## The Artist

Albers's development as an artist proceeded relatively slowly. Comparedto the great masters of his generation, hè was a “latecomer,"4 as his international breakthrough came only in the sixties. Together with Victor Vasarely, he has since become known as the father of “op art" or, more accurately, of “perceptual art." To a broad section of the art public he is the painter of squares, the creator of the famous series Homage to the Square, which began in the fifties and continued into the seventies. The Homage to the Square paintings consist of staggered juxtapositions of three or four squares, always in such a way that the inner squares tend toward the bottom (i.e., they are not arrangedaround the geometrical center) so that the lower portion has narrow stripes while the upper zone has larger fields. With this scheme, in which the principles are constant but the dimensions can be varied, the artist found his way “to a brilliant way of demonstrating color phenomenona."5 The square, “a form codified by Malevitch, an intellectual form,"⁶ was used by Albers to depict the all but inexhaustible richness of color relationships and the cross-influence of colors and to sharpen perception of these mutual interactions of colors. Consequently, it is not surprising that his theoretical magnum opus on color theory, written in the UnitedStates, is called Interaction of Color.

We cannot explore here the design problems of the Homage to the Square series. What is interesting for our purposes is the following: (1) Albers worked in series “because there is not just one solution to an aesthetic problem,"7 a principle that was decisive for his work in the preliminary course as well; (2) Albers sought "the least possible compositionalexpenditure, the reduction to the geometrical element,"8 in order to optimize the relation of “effort" and “effect," a principle that theartist communicated to his students at the Bauhaus with the slogan “economy of means."

The paintings of squares serve to characterize Albers's oeuvre only to an extremely limited extent. It is not possible to go into the details to show that it is richer in many respects. Because of their importance, however, we must at least mention two groups of works, Structural Constellations and Double Centric Paintings, which were made in the United States from the forties on in various series such as Biconjugate, Vice Versa, and Variation. The theme of this chapter is not, however, Albers in America, neither as an artist nor as a teacher, but rather Albers at the Bauhaus. Consequently, his teaching activity in America from 1933 on (Black Mountain College, North Carolina; Harvard University; Yale University; and guest lectureships at numerous universities) must be excluded from what follows just as much as his artistic activity after 19339 (on this, see chapter 13).

Born in Bottrop in 1888, he began—like Johannes Itten—as a primary school teacher before deciding to study art. In 1913 he registered at the Königliche Kunstschule (Royal Art School) in Berlin, where he receiveda certificate as an arts educator in 1915. Berlin was significant for him in that he came into contact there with the progressive artistic developments of the day through reqular visits to Paul Cassirer's gallery and the Galerie Gurlitt. In 1916 and 1919 he was active as a teacher in his native city, Bottrop. At the same time he took courses at the School of Arts and Crafts in Essen. There he was a student of the symbolist artist Jan Thorn Prikker, who was known for his stained glass windows (this biographical detail is interesting, as Albers would later, at the Bauhaus, become especially interested in glass as a means for design). Albers's first lithographs and wood- and linocuts were made around this time; they reveal the influence of expressionism and cubism. He kept his expressionism in check, however, and it never had the exalted aspects of, say, the artists of Die Brücke (The Bridge). This restraint is best demonstratedby his self-portraits, which "rather than a strong, soulful aspect [reveal] a more critical glance from out of a safe, introverted sphere."10 The tendency to abstraction is especially marked in the lithographs from this period, which were inspired by cubism in their form and whose rich facets and diaphanous pictorialstructure are reminiscent of Feininger. In 1919 Albers went to the Academy of Art in Munich, where he joined the painting class of the prince of painting at the time, Franz von Stuck, with whom Kandinsky and Klee had studied almost twenty years earlier. It is not surprising that the thirty-year-old remained dissatisfiedwith the instruction in Stuck's course. “Though at the turn of the century it may still have been important to study with this imaginative artist, it seems inappropriate for an artist already thirty, who had seen important cubist paintings in Berlin and who had decided on a simplifying technique: black and white and sober themes."11 So Albers left Munich in the spring, to begin again at the Bauhaus, which had opened just one year before:

All I knew of the Bauhaus was derived from a single sheet, the first manifesto, with one side giving Gropius' new program for reuniting art and the crafts and theother side showing Feininger's woodcut, “The Cathedral." This program gave me the impulse to try out the new idea. After destroying most of my academic studies I began over again in Weimar, beginning with the "Preparatory Course," the introductory course required of all students entering the school.12

1n the preliminary course taught by Johannes Itten—who was Albers's age—Albers acquired a new view of things, the view of “materials not notjced before; the assemblages of discarded materials interested him; the teaching method of allowing students to develop on their own inspired him."13 We will see later that Albers would later introduce elements of Itten's course into his own instruction, though admittedly in modified form.

After completing the preliminary course, it was Albers's intention to transfer to the glass workshop. The council of masters, however, initially placed him in the workshop for wall painting, which was led by Itten and Schlemmer in alternation, a decision to which Albers did not submit:

Since I could not agree, so far as I was concerned that painting walls was a necessary preparation for making glass pictures, I worked without being a member of any workshop.

Equipped with a knapsack and a hammer, I scouted the town dump; the bottles I found there provided me with all the kinds of glassI needed for making my glass paintings.

At the end of my second semester Gropius, much concerned, reminded me several times, as was his duty, that I could not stay on at the Bauhaus if I persisted in ignoring the advice of my teachers to engage first of all in the wall-painting class.

.. . In the required exhibition at the end of my second semester I showed various of my glass-painting studies. They were combinations of bottle shards, arranged in an unusual (unprofessional) way on flattened tin cans and wire screens, etc. I felt that my show would be my swan song at the Bauhaus. . . .

But soon thereafter I received a letter from the Masters' Council informing me first, that I could continue my studies at the Bauhaus and, secondly, asking me to set up a new glass workshop for them. Thus suddenly I got my own workshop and it was not long before I started to get orders for glass windows.14

Albers himself referred to his earliest glass compositions as Shard Paintings. They were inspired by Itten's preliminary course in that they applied the principle of assemblage taught in the course to the area of glass design. It is significant that in these works Albers had already “severed his relationship with the world of objective painting. Besides a new and formalistic occupation with the pieces of glass he had found, there is for the first time the inner debate with the material which was to become so typical of his later teaching activity."15 These early glass works, which, precisely because they were "unprofessional" (Albers) in their craftsmanship, opened up new dimensions of the optic as well as the haptic (glass shards mounted in sheet metal, e.g., Figure; Rhenish Legend; both 1921), were followed around 1922–23 by a series of glass windows that reveal a strictly geometric, formal construction as well as a return to a traditional technique of craftsmanship (and are thus "professional"), namely, lead glazing. Examples include the glass windows in the Sommerfeld House in Berlin-Dahlem that was furnished by the Bauhaus workshops; in the staircase of the Ullstein publishing house in Berlin; and in the entry hall of Dr. Otte's house in Berlin-Zehlendorf.

In comparison to these glass works, which were quite new in their formal severity but nonetheless did not go beyond the usual in terms of their technical production, the so-called singledisk glass pictures (from about 1925 on) repre: sented a definitive innovation. They are glass pictures of relatively small format in which the glass is not a translucent medium but rather an opaque one. These glasspictures were produced from pieces of milky glass flashed with color, usually dark red, dark yellow, or dark blue. "The forms were constructed by means of cutting out stencils for sandblasting,"16 that is to say, the sandblasting removes some of the flashed color and exposes the white underlayer. These single-disk glass pictures evince a tectonically grounded, geometrically simple formal language and a technical precision that leaves nothing of an individual signature. Horizontal and vertical stripes of color rhythmically articulate the surface and evoke associations with architecture (City, 1928) and music (Fugue, 1925). It is clear that the principles evident in these glass pictures—anonymity of the formal language, exclusion of the fortuitous, "activation of the negative elements"17apply to the student works created in Albers's courses as well, which emphasizes once again the extent to which the aesthetic maxims of the Bauhaus teachers determined their pedagogical practices.

The transition from Albers's earlier glass works to the precise geometrical organization of the single-disk glass pictures is generally attributed to the influence of Theo van Doesburg's de Stiil courses in Weimar.18It is surely attributing too much to a single cause, however, to explain, solely on the basis of Doesburg's presence, a transition that was typical of the whole of the Bauhaus after 1923as itmovedfrom thecraftsbased, romantic early phase to the constructive, engineering-oriented phase. It seems more likely that the transition was a complex process determined by a number of factors.

Even at Weimar, the glass workshop—initially under the direction of Johannes Itten, then under Paul Klee in 1922–23—had difficulty asserting its right to exist (Albers was hired as a technical director after completing his apprenticeship in 1923; in 1924 it was subsumed under the workshop for wall painting). After the exodus from Weimar, it was not thought necessary to continue the glass workshop at the Dessau Bauhaus. This was not only because the craft methods typical of glass pictures were geared toward unique objects and thus incompatible with the new ideology of mechanical reproduction, but also because the progressive architecture of Neues Bauen produced no commissions for glass painting. It is interesting to see that Albers created his single-disk glass pictures, with their technical and formal innovations, without any institutionalsupport, somewhat against the official trend, and without any connection to architectural tasks. The strictly rectangular constructions, which seem to paraphrase the principles of Neues Bauen, seen in glass pictures like Interior I and Window (both 1929) were followed by a phase of relaxation of form with the introduction of rounded and diagonal parts, for example, in the sandblasted single-disk glass pictures Glove Lasts or Steps (both 1931). In Steps Albers took as his theme a specific perceptual problem: the confusion of the eye by forms that can be read in many ways, a theme that he would later vary in numerous series in America.

Before discussing Albers as a designer, it is worth looking at the artist's photography, not least because it documents the proverbial versatility that was typical of artists at the Bauhaus.

Like most other members of the Bauhaus, Albers was not a professional photographer (in contrast to Walter Peterhans) but rather he photographed in addition to other activities—usually as a mere sideline and without professional ambitions. The fact that he approached photography as an amateur was no doubt a decided advantage that enabied him to use the medium in a naive or—to use his word—"unprofessional" and yet innovative way. It goes without saying that his photographic work did not stand outside the historical context but rather bore the signature of the age, more precisely, of the so-called Neues Sehen (New vision): boldperspectives, angled horizons, extreme top or close-up views, diagonal compositions, hard shadows, unexpected detail views, and unusual structures. This interest in structures and the effects of surfaces was doubtless awakened in Itten's preliminary course. Naturally one other influence on these photographs leaps out: that of Itten's colleague in the preliminary course, Moholy-Nagy, who revolutionized photography of his day as an experimental designer par excellence and was a lasting influence (see “Aspects of His Artistic Work" in chapter 6).

Albers's photographic works are, in a sense, private photographs, because they were not intended for publication, and also because they often depict peoplefrom theartist'spersonal sphere (e.g., colleagues at the Bauhaus). Nevertheless, it is remarkable to see the artist's approach to the material, which was very unconventional at the time, of arranging individual photographs of the same size, or even different sizes, by subject in ensembles. “The grouping of pictures in this way manifests his interest in sequence, in the repetition of a motif for emphasis. [They] are his most original and unusual contribution to photography. When he combines [several] photographs..., he is always conscious that the effect is made stronger by the aggregation—either through the similarity of the images or through contrast. It results in sequences that are reminiscent of film sequences."19 The modernity of Albers's approach is evident both in the rejection of the individual image and in the serial procedure. It is certainly no coincidence that Robert Rauschenberg, a student of Albers in the United States at Black Mountain College, used precisely this method in his combines.

## The Designer

After this necessarily hurried overview of Albers's work as a fine artist during his years at the Bauhaus, it is worth examining his contribution to applied design. One of Albers's most remarkable qualities was his versatility, which was expressed in the variety of objects he designed.

Alongside his artistic experiments with glass Albers also came into contact with problems of industrial glass production, for example, in designing a fruit bowl and an industrially produced tea glass. Both designs were done around 1924 in the metal workshop led by Moholy-Nagy. Their design was based on a formal severity that is remarkable for the time. For example, the fruit bowl is constructed from simple, basic forms: on three ball feet of wood rests a circular band (nickel silver), and "the round glass plate is also inserted into the wooden balls . . . ; the tea glass is also economical and charming in its construction: the concave center of the glass is surrounded by a metal band that wraps just three quarters of the way around and to which two wood handles are attached. Here a'nonprofessional' invented a minimal form that is functional, economical, and aesthetically satisfying."20

Another focus within Albers's activity as a designer was woodworking. The earliest examples we should mention are the furnishings for the waiting room in the director's office, which were created in the cabinetmaking workshop led by Gropius as master of form in 1923: a newspaper rack, a glass cabinet, a meeting table, and a matching folding chair.21 These pieces of furniture, freed from decorative constructivism à la

The experience with wood that Albers was able to gain during the Weimar period in the cabinetmaking workshop made him seempredestined to take over the leadership of the furniture workshop in Dessau in 1928 when Marcel Breuer stepped down (Albers remained until 1929, to be followed by AlfredArndt). There Albers created the bentwood armchair "Ti 244," designed to be easily disassembled and industriallyproduced, which was particularly well suited to meet Hannes Meyer's emphatic call for practical, inexpensive furniture.

Albers's above-mentioned versatility is also demonstrated by his experimental works in the area of type design. The stencil lettering (1925) he designed was based on the basic geometrical forms of the square, the triangle, and the circle (three-quarter circle)—in keeping with the formal reductionism that was popular at the Bauhaus.
