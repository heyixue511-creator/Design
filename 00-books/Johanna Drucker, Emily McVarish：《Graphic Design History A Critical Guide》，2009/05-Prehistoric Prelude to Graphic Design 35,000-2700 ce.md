Early Writing: Mark-making, Notation Systems, and Scripts 3000-500 BCE The design of proto-writing systems introduced the first stable codes for graphic representation of language. The distinction of mark and interval on a deliberately identified surface is the basis of any graphic communication system. The elements of visual sign systems were defined by fundamental graphic variables: shape, size, orientation, and position. The meaningful organization of signs depended on compositional principles of juxtaposition, sequence, hierarchy, and direction. Pictorial signs were first used to represent concrete things but quickly came to function as a means of encoding ideas as well. Literate culture developed from the capacity to represent language in a visual code agreed upon as a convention by a social group. The effects of writing as a form of social control and power spring from this consensus. 1.1 Marks as signatures, North America, seventeenth century. Signatures in the shape of totem animals attest to the authority of this document. The picture signs are not writing in the proper sense. They stand directly for a person not for language. These marks are "like" writing, but they are not part of a code that can be used to represent or express other statements. Graphic media entered a new age with the coming of writing. By 3000 BCE, new tools for carving, inscribing, and marking were being developed and used on material supports of varying durability, such as clay, stone, papyrus, skin, bone, wax, metal, and wood. But the invention of writing required more than tools. Writing depended on the design of a stable visual code for the representation of language. This connection to language makes writing distinct from other visual signs that stand for ideas or things but not for speech or a linguistic system. Writing extended the principles of graphic organization that had been developed for image and symbol making. The shapes of the first glyphs, letters, and signs connect the earliest human marks to present-day alphabetic and character-based scripts through a continuous legacy. We still use versions of some of the earliest written symbols ever invented. From the outset, these symbols had the effect of changing the power of language by aligning it with the administration of culture. Writing orchestrated economic, political, religious, and other social activities. It expressed laws and social constraints that could be enforced by symbolic power rather than brute force. Whereas language distinguishes humans from other animals, writing introduces significant differences between oral and literate cultures. Oral communication is ephemeral and takes place in real-time circumstances that rely on presence. Writing communicates in the absence of its author. Enduring inscriptions transmit textual records to remote contexts (Fig. 1.1). Mark-making Mark-making is the most basic form of graphic expression and design. Drawing a line in the sand or making a handprint on a wall is a direct sign of individual identity. Such imprints have a curious existence: a mark that is a sign of the self is also always other than the self. 1.2 Rongo Rongo script, Easter Island, date unknown. The Rongo Rongo script of Easter Island has an intriguingiy exuberant rhythmic quality, but it has never been deciphered. Anthropologists now consider that it is probably not a written language but a set of signs used for ritual purposes. The script is not ancient, and scholars have suggested that it was invented by indigenous people exposed to European writing in the eighteenth century. Independent inventions of writing have occurred in many cultures. The graphic properties of this notation make it scriptlike because it contains marks and intervals that are clearly differentiated and nonpictorial elements that are neither physical traces of a hand or tool nor repeated motifs in a decorative pattern. ![](images/eab3e1f56971a4e87e648d2e3350269a03f778fbf135cc0a4422a04aac820922.jpg) ![](images/8f3eca94164d9e7077d49ec97bf63b32cba4c45c826eef3ab5fb705ab098ad57.jpg) Once made, marks exist independently of their makers. A line makes a division, whether in physical space as a boundary or in an intellectual zone to define a category. Differentiating between marked and unmarked things is a conceptual act of enormous significance. We might even suggest that the idea of difference forms the basis of human knowledge, and that the making of a mark is the primary way of inscribing such difference. On this simple principle of differentiation we can structure the complicated oppositions of I/Thou, me/you, this/that, self/other, subject/object, which form our understanding of the world. Marks are not only different from each other but also distinct from the ground on which they are made. ## True writing is a graphic expression of language. This relation 1.3 Painted stones, Mas-d'Azil, France, 10,000 sce. These are some of the earliest remains of systematic markmaking. The regularity of the repeated marks and their careful graphic execution indicate deliberate intention. Though too few in number to represent a language, they may have been conventionalized representations of things. The stick-figure form is referred to as the "hailelujah man" because it appears to be a person with arms raised in jubilation. This sign, like the other simple forms on these rocks, is common to many so-called primitive systems of marks. Even when they develop independently, human scripts often share formal characteristics. This does not suggest that a universal code underlies all writing but rather, pragmatically, that simple lines can be combined in a certain number of fairly predictable ways. Perhaps even more profoundly, in any graphic code, marks are different from the intervals that separate them (Fig. 1.2). Marks and signs alone do not constitute writing. The earliest marked objects that remain from prehistoric periods are the stones from the Mas-d'Azil area in France. They present enough regularity in their repeated use of crosses, waves, and sinewy patterns to suggest a conventional code. But the meaning of these marks relied on a context now lost, and the system to which they belonged seems insufficiently developed to represent a language. These stones may have been signs for objects rather than signs for language. Their designs do not appear to be numerous or varied enough to be a verbal code. This contrast is crucial. True writing is a graphic expression of language. This relation makes writing unique among visual forms of expression and fundamentally different from drawing or painting. But systems on the verge of becoming writing reveal the basic conditions for all letter and notation design: a deliberately identified surface, distinctions between figure and ground, differentiation of mark and interval, and principles of visual organization (sequence, scale, orientation, and juxtaposition) (Fig. 1.3). 1.4 Clay tokens, Mesopotamia, fourth millennium Bce. These regularly shaped objects served as a form of notation for business transactions. Their shapes each corresponded to a different category of agricultural product. The marks on their surfaces were highly schematic and used for detailed accounting. When these tokens were pressed into wet clay, their imprints were part of a graphic system in which relative size, shape, orientation, and groupings were all significant. These tokens demonstrate the emergence of a set of sophisticated graphic principles. The spatial organization of the surface and the elaboration of rules governing the use of visual signs are evidence of graphic refinement. This notation system is still not considered writing because "writing" is reserved for the representation of language, and these tokens are basically cash register receipts. The impressions made by the tokens were precursors to the wedge-shaped signs of cuneiform, the oldest system of actual writing (Fig. 1.4). During the several thousand years in which they were used, the signs on these clay tokens made a critical conceptual leap. At first, they were meant to represent specific things in schematic images. Then they came to function as abstract representations of categories of things or ideas. For instance, from the practice of continually representing four oxen, four sacks of grain, or four containers of oil came the idea that four could be represented by a sign. Thus, the concept of quantity was abstracted from direct, concrete counting. To the contemporary mind, the enormity of such a leap is almost impossible to appreciate. Yet to realize that many categories of thought are similarly abstract provides an insight into the contribution of notation and writing to human culture. The design problem implied in this shift is equally profound. What should a sign look like when it represents an abstract idea ("four-ness") rather than a thing? The transformation of Chinese characters from their pictorial origins to schematic signs happened with relative rapidity. Chinese characters most frequently represent either words or sounds, or some combination of the two. No writing system that required a sign for every word would be practical. The adaptation of characters for Japanese, Korean, and other languages demonstrates the versatility of Chinese script. The word notation refers to signs that stand for categories and ideas in a stable graphic system but fall short of the ability to represent language or speech. The term writing, as we have said, is reserved for graphic forms that specifically express language. The relation of writing to spoken language is not simple-even in a system such as the alphabet in which letter signs are supposed to represent sounds. Many things said in speech cannot be represented in writing, and vice versa. Written and spoken forms are both expressions of language. But they are not necessarily identical to each other. What is the written equivalent of an ironic inflection? A whisper? A seductive tone of voice? Writing has many properties that derive from its graphic nature. What is the spoken equivalent of a hyphen? Or a decorative initial? Or a sans serif face? Or the division of text into two columns on a page?
