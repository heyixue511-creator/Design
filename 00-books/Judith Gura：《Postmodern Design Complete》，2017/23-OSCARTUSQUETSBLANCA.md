# OSCAR TUSQUETS BLANCA b.1941 SPANISH ARTIST, DESIGNER, AND ARCHITECT WHOSE HIGHLY ORIGINAL WORK IS INSPIRED BY BOTH HISTORICISM AND LANDSCAPE. A rare Spanish practitioner of a style largely dominated by Italians, architect Oscar Tusquets Blanca also stands out for the exceptional variety of his pursuits. In a profession of multitaskers, he has performed more tasks than many of his contemporaries, all with considerable success. Avoiding the modern inclination to specialize, he has been quoted as considering himself an architect by training, a designer by adaptation, a painter by vocation, and a writer through the desire to make friends Tusquets Blanca studied art in Barcelona, graduating from the Escuela Tècnica Superior d’Arquitectura in 1965, and with four colleagues (Pep Bonet, Cristian Cirici, Lluís Clotet, and Mireia Riera) formed the design and architectural practice Studio Per. Studio Per's architectural projects included elegant one-family villas as well as larger public buildings. Seven years later, the group organized the company BD Ediciones de Diseño\*, which produces and markets furniture and objects by the founders and other,contemporary designers, as well as reissuing works by Antoni Gaudí and Charles Rennie Mackintosh. In i972 Tusquets Blanca, in collaboration with Clotet, designed one of the earliest postmodern houses in Spain, Casa Regàs, a freeform structure built around a central pool with an irregular plan—what the architects called "harmonious disorder." It was considered postmodern in its bold departure from rectilinear form and machine-for-living functionalism. Studio Per became known for its unconventional approach, in one example producing an urban dwelling that managed to incorporate apartments of several sizes and shapes by devising an unbalanced exterior with vari-sized, misaligned windows and irregular projections. Over the years, Tusquets Blanca has designed furniture and objects that have won awards and museum accolades. When commissioned to design a wing for the Dalí Theatre and Museum in Figueres, Catalonia (i974), he became friends with the artist, whose Surrealist approach appealed to his fanciful instincts. His Dalilips sofa was designed in collaboration with the artist for the Mae West room at the museum, though the design did not go into production until thirty years later, when a new polyethylene molding process enabled the soft texture the designers wanted for the piece. Tusquets Blanca was one of the architects asked to design a building for the Parc de la Villette in Paris, and contributed a gray and white marble pavilion with three closed volumes connected by two greenhouses. Its monumental entrances opened into inviting interiors with wood-beamed ceilings and it is an elegant counterpoint to other structures in the park; unfortunately, it has been poorly maintained. Although not a member of or contributor to MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design"), he was not unnoticed in Italy, and was one of the eleven designers ALESSI (see under “Edited Design") commissioned for the Tea & Coffee Piazza project in i983. In contrast to the bold, geometric lines in many of the other designs, his service is shaped in sensual curves, relating to the forms used in his furniture. Like Frank Lloyd Wright, Le Corbusier, and Mies van der Rohe, James Wines is not a registered architect, though he has been designing or working on buildings for most of his life.4² His highly original and provocative designs have made him internationally known as a leading practitioner and chief interpreter of the movement called Deconstructivism, as well as a creator of intriguing urban spaces that defy categorization. Before practicing architecture, Wines was an artist and sculptor, a predisposition that is reflected in his architecture and environmental projects. He was born in Chicago, and attended Syracuse University School of Art, where he majored in art history and sculpture, graduating in 1956. Winning a Prix de Rome the following year, he traveled to Italy and lived there until 1962, making semi-abstract bronze sculptures These won him an exhibition at the Baltimore Museum of Art in 1958, and his work was recognized again with a Guggenheim Fellowship in i962. As his sculpture became larger and more geometric, Wines sought to create art that could be part of the urban environment instead of simply displayed in galleries. To do this, he founded SITE (Sculpture in the Environment) in i97o, a collaboration of architects, artists, and technicians that describes itself as “an architecture and environmental arts studio."43 The firm has evolved into an international practice whose commissions have included public spaces, environmental artworks, landscape designs, as well as interiors and products. Wines's approach to designing each building has been to view it in a specific cntext—not as an object in the environment, but an object that becomes the environment. He believes that, rather than being separate disciplines, architecture and art are parts of an integrated whole. Of all SITE's work in many countries, the firm's buildings for Best Products are most widely known, and have become legendary archetypes of Deconstructivism. When SITE was commissioned to create new facades for nine undistinguished structures that housed Best's home goods stores, Wines's ingenious solution was to transform the storefronts into witty and provocative threedimensional works of art that mocked their banal, big-box forms. Some appear to bc unfinished, broken, or distorted, such as the crumbling-walled Indeterminate Facade (1975), the broken-corner Notch Building (1979), and the bizarre Peeling Building (i972). Other attention-grabbing devices included a ruined facade faced with a forest of trees, and a broken wall that revealed an ersatz interior display. The designs drew praise from some, derision from others, and media attention everywhere,44 establishing Wines's reputation as an ingenious and provocative thinker. Throughout his career, Wines has taught art, architecture, and environmental design at the School of Visual Arts in New York, at New York University, and Penn State University, and his seven books include Green Architecture (2ooo). Wines has won dozens of art and architecture awards, and his projects for SITE have been featured in retrospective exhibitions in the United States, Europe, and Japan. He sees his approach as an alternate view of Postmodernism and believes that SITE has a place in the movement because of its “fusion of art, architecture and context as a basic philosophical premise." By the early i98os the architectural world was engaged in heated discussions about the pros and cons of the new style, as exemplifed by the early postmodern build ings, with their historicist forms and quirky, classically informed ornament. But for most people, and even many design professionals, Postmodernism could be summed up in a single word: MEMPHIS (see under “Edited Design"). Although Postmodernism's boldest architectural incarnations had drawn attention, the high-profile debut of the Italian collaborative at the 198ı Salone del Mobile in Milan brought a storm of unprecedented media coverage. With its use of inexpensive material, strident colors, and zingy patterns, Memphis designs were nothing like that already on the market, or in anyone's homes. In making expensive furniture and objects from ![](images/663993e711be7740e5622b355afbb1cf9b573133e0b8d17b1985cea1a0ad33ef.jpg) inexpensive materials, Memphis challenged preconceptions about luxury products. And unlike the conceptual designs of the Italian Radicals, its products were for sale. For the first time, furniture and product design were as newsworthy as couture fashion, and sometimes as difficult to put to actual use—the extreme designs were not readily compatible with other styles. But high prices and limited production made Memphis inaccessible to most of the general public, even had they wanted to own it.' Although Memphis was the most high-profile and prolific source of postmodern design, it was by no means the only one. Designers and producers in a few other countries introduced limited-edition experimental designs, though none made much impact in the marketplace. In America, several major architects had designed built-in or custom furniture to furnish the postmodern spaces they had designed, but the new style was inevitably acknowledged by the furniture industry, and adapted for a broader consumer market. Cautiously responding to what they saw as a coming demand for postmodern furniture, some manufacturers took the most obvious course: hiring architects to design it for them. In the vanguard was Knoll\*, which commissioned ROBERT VENTURI (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design' and “Living with Postmodern Design") in i979—even before the Memphis explosion—to design a collec tion of chairs. Introduced in i984, the series of nine pieces was a tongue-in-cheek riff on classical-period styles, with chair backs cut into Queen Anne, Sheraton, Hepplewhite, Chippendale, Empire, Biedermeier, Gothic ![](images/d887f772391f06c15f3f12a21ac63c5456bd53b648c7ab6845b937c03d650bc6.jpg) Revival, Art Nouveau, and Art Deco silhouettes, screened with appropriate colors and occasional ornament. MICHAEL GRAVES (see under “Masters of Postmodern Design" and "Living with Postmodern Design") designed Biedermeier-inspired furniture for Canadian producer Sunar\*, and later a popularly priced collection for Drexel; and Gunlocke commissioned furniture craftsman Wendell Castle\* to create limited-edition designs, while other manufacturers such as Niedermeyer and Thayer Coggin introduced pieces that modified the postmodern look for more conservative American tastes. In short order, much of the industry jumped on the bandwagon. Established Italian producers including Cassina, Driade\*, Giorgetti, and Sawaya & Moroni\* added pieces by high-profile postmodernist architects to their collections. Dubbed neomodern by the media,² they were clearly Memphis-influenced, using primary colors, pattern, and a provocative mix of materials. Design galleries like Néotù and Xo in Paris, Aram in London, and Art et Industrie\* and Max Protetch Gallery\* in New York staged exhibitions of limited-edition or one-off works by artists, designers, and architects, which were linked to Postmodernism primarily in their rejection of conventional forms and materials: few adopted the historicist imagery used by the leading postmodern architects. The new design aesthetic was interpreted somewhat differently across countries: Italian Postmodernism remained cheeky and subversive, American and British translations used classical themes and ornament drawn from historical styles, while the Japanese were more disposed toward futuristic concepts. And some designers, like MARIO BOTTA (see under “Architecture"), SHIRO KURAMATA, and PHILIPPE STARCK, had feet in both camps. In rejecting the strictures of established Modernism, however, both Memphis and the more historicist designs evolved from the same roots. Meanwhile, young designers in London were experimenting with cast-off objects and industrial materials for furniture they assembled by hand, with results such as Ron Arad's Rover chair, made from a discarded automobile seat, and Danny Lane's Etruscan Chair of broken-edged glass, suggesting an archeological ruin. Others engaging in transgressive explorations included Tom Dixon, André Dubreuil, and Marc Newson, whose Lockheed Lounge is postmodern in its references to both the form of a neoclassical chaise longue and the exterior of a i96os aircraft.
