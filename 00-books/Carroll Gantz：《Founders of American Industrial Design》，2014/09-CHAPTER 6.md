# CHAPTER 6 # Recovery, Plastics, and Education The two pioneering train designs connected to the Chicago exposition had a significant and immediate effect on the railroad industry. In November 1934, the Pennsylvania Railroad (PRR) hired Raymond Loewy to refine the Westinghouse design of the GG-1 design for mass production. Loewy had designed a trashcan for Pennsylvania Station in New York in 1932, and at the time had shown the PRR president a futuristic sketch of a streamlined locomotive. The GG-1 was a class of electric locomotives powered by alternating current (AC) overhead wires. Twelve 385 hp traction motors drove the GG-1's 57-inch-diameter driving wheels on six axles. The body, over 76 feet long, was built in two halves joined with a ball-and-socket joint to allow negotiation of sharp curves, and each half had a raised control cab, each facing opposite directions, so it could be driven in forward or reverse. In 1933, PRR had decided to replace its current P5a electric locomotives with ones that were more powerful and more streamlined. PRR asked General Electric and Westinghouse to design prototypes that were capable of 100 mph, a streamlined body shell, and a central control cab. Both companies submitted prototypes in August 1934, and both worked with PRR's Baldwin Locomotive Works as the design progressed. Features from both prototypes were refined and combined to produce the final design. The Westinghouse prototypes were based on six plaster models made by Donald Dohner (mentioned earlier), Westinghouse's director of art in its engineering division, in 1933 when the project began. One of these models, shown in photographs, closely resembles the final design of what became the GG-1 prototype, which essentially was completed by August 1934, three months before Loewy was called in. By all traditions of attribution, Dohner should have been credited with the styling of the GG-1. Loewy was called in for consultation on October 25, 1934, by PRR vice president and heir apparent Martin W. Clement to “enhance the GG-1's esthetics." A fee of \$5,000 was agreed upon, but he had only about a week before the production order was to be placed, so his contributions on the existing prototype were limited to the color scheme, which became Brunswick Green, with five gold pinstripes running along the sides and curving down on the front and rear toward the ground. Loewy also claimed he had recommended a smooth welded body instead of the riveted one used in the prototype, but there is some evidence that the rivets were the result of rushing the completion of the prototype and that they were never intended for use on the production models. One hundred thirty-nine production models would be built between 1934 and 1943, and many remained in service until the 1970s. In any event, Loewy was retained for \$20,000 annually as the primary design consultant of PRR for the rest of the decade. Until his death, he publicly claimed the design of the GG-1 as one of his most important achievements and was exclusively credited with its design in all literature pertaining to the GG-1. Loewy was its ultimate promoter, not Dohner, who was essentially a modest man, who would, within a month after he designed the models, leave Westinghouse to begin his career in industrial design education, for which he is better remembered.¹ ![](images/03bb3e1459a6bfd3841e0d4cf409d7fbd778dd552b8ac4219864c35851f25c98.jpg) Pennsylvania Railroad GG-1 (Classic Trains, Summer 2009. Photograph by Karl Zimmermann). The main reason Donald Dohner never received credit was that after February 1934 he was no longer employed by Westinghouse. In fact he was fired as a result of the Fortune article on industrial design that month. The article, described in detail earlier, revealed the fact that his department at Westinghouse was costing the company \$75,000 per year (nearly \$1.3 million in 2013 dollars), a fact apparently not known by Westinghouse management, which was understandably shocked, and in the middle of a Depression took executive action to cut expenses. ![](images/e6c27aea490ba71a9ca55475f464b90cad80098da45e5f0835f1d91c1a18e8f1.jpg) Alexander Kostellow, ca. 1950s (Industrial Design magazine, October 1954). Ironically, this unfortunate turn of events resulted in a new career for Dohner, and a milestone for the industrial design profession. As you may recall, before joining Westinghouse in 1930, Dohner had taught design as an instructor at the Carnegie Institute of Technology (CIT) starting in 1929. Upon hearing of Dohner's loss of job, his old colleagues at CIT were overjoyed at the opportunity of welcoming back someone who was now a nationally known industrial designer, recently featured in Fortune magazine. Painting and Design Department painting instructor Alexander Kostellow (1896–1954) reportedly shouted to department head Wilfred Readio, "Donald's on the loose! Let's get him! It's [industrial design] the wave of the future! Painting is dead! Start an option [degree] in industrial design!"2 Alexander Jusserand Kostellow was born in Iran (then Persia). He studied in Paris and the University of Berlin and came to the United States in 1916, where he studied painting at the National Academy, the Art Students League of New York, and the Kansas City Art Institute, where he met and married Rowena Reed. Both came to Pittsburgh in 1929 to teach at CIT, he in painting, and she in sculpture. Carnegie was not the first school with interest in industrial design. As mentioned earlier, there were a number of art schools already teaching it in various programs for engineers, and Pratt Institute in Brooklyn had engaged Donald Dohner as a “consultant and lecturer in industrial design." Students, who previously would have studied architecture, were now attracted to industrial design as an alternative, and indeed many did see it as "the wave of the future," as declared by Kostellow. The Chicago Art Institute offered industrial design courses in 1932, the same year the University of Cincinnati initiated an “Art and Industry" option in its School of Applied Arts, and in California, Kem Weber initiated a program in industrial design at the Art Center School in Los Angeles. He would teach there until 1943.3 In 1933 the Detroit Society of Arts and Crafts had made national news by recognizing the automobile as an “art form," and at the Cleveland School (now Institute) of Art (CIA), Viktor Schreckengost (1906–2008) had initiated a pioneer program of industrial design in 1933, which would become well known for automotive design. ![](images/c7c5c6e105bca5512a5bac36bbd962f41f845ab681fda9e16adbcb8afb70c96e.jpg) Schreckengost was born in Sebring, Ohio, to a potter father and had studied design and ceramics at CIA, graduating in 1929. After studying at the Kunstegewerbeschule (Arts and Crafts School) in Austria, he joined the faculty at CIA and worked part time at the Cowan Pottery Studio. There he created his famous Viktor Schreckengost, ca. 1939 (courtesy David Nowacek and Gene Schreckengost). 28-inch-diameter Art Deco “Jazz" punch bowl that had been commissioned anonymously by Eleanor Roosevelt, who later ordered more for the White House and Hyde Park. Schreckengost soon became a consultant to the White Motor Company in Cleveland, and would later create an innovative truck design with the cab over the engine to provide more storage space in the back. ![](images/c457f7c8797cd3bd754987062ca2b465bab69ee92c268cb508266a2ec644ee09.jpg) Robert Lepper, ca. 1940s. At Carnegie in 1933, optional classes in industrial design, based on Dohner's 1930 syllabus, had been offered to students and were in progress as Dohner rejoined the faculty in 1934 and initiated a new degreed program in industrial design at CIT, the first school to do so. Assisting Dohner was Art and Design Department head, Wilfred Radio, and faculty members Kostellow, Russell Hyde, Fred Clayter, and Robert Lepper (1906– 1991). Lepper had been hired to fill in for Dohner's position when he left for Westinghouse in 1930. Lepper was born in Aspinwall, a suburb of Pittsburgh, and graduated with a degree in painting from CIT in 1927. He spent a year or so in Europe soaking up modern art and joined the faculty at CIT in 1930, where he painted "machine art," such as his 1932 Crankshaft, a realistic image of a Lincoln 12-cylinder crankshaft with pistons floating in the air. Because of his interest in mechanical things, he was more or less drafted into the new industrial design degree program in 1934. So in the fall semester of 1934, third-year students were offered an option for a degree in industrial design, along with other third-year options of illustration, interior decoration, or art education. The first class included three women and two males, all to become the first degreed graduates of industrial design in the world. Dohner took them to manufacturing plants, such as Westinghouse, Fostoria Glass, and ALCOA (Aluminum Company of America), to show them how things were made, and then instructed them to design new products based on what they had seen. Elsewhere, modern design continued to be publicized. From November 5, 1934, to January 6, 1935, the Metropolitan Museum of Art mounted an exhibition, Contemporary American Industrial Art, by 20 architectural and industrial designers that featured entire-room arrangements by architects except for a full-scale replica of an industrial design office and studio designed by Raymond Loewy. Architects and museums related all industrial design to architecture because they believed that architects should dominate the new feld, as was the case in Europe. Accordingly, many of the rooms were traditionally credited to name architects, who selected all the decorative furnishings to keep industrial design “in its proper place” under the supervision of architects. However, this time, a number of industrial designers were invited to participate in the event, including Viktor Schreckengost (chinaware), Donald Deskey (an entire dining room), Gilbert Rohde (Herman Miller clock and furniture), Gustav Jensen (rugs, wall treatments), Peter Müller-Munk (silverware), Walter von Nessen (display case and metalware), Lurelle Guild (silverware), Russel Wright (metalware, floor lamps), Alfons Bach (furniture) and Walter Dorwin Teague (on the cooperating committee). Still, the products shown were all from traditiónal art industries, studiously ignoring the many larger artless industry products by industrial designers, such as the elegant gas range Teague had recently designed for the Floyd-Wells Company in Royersford, Pennsylvania, that rivaled Bel Geddes's SGE range in simplicity, whiteness, and modernity. But attempts by architects to supervise and control industrial design were too late. The horse of industrial design was out of the architectural barn. Industrial designers already owned the field of both mechanical and decorative, modern mass-produced consumer products. Streamlined trains were still in the national news at the end of the year. On December 27, 1934, the New York Central Railroad (NYC) exhibited its new steam locomotive at Grand Central Station, with a streamlined shell painted in gunmetal gray and called the Commodore Vanderbilt. Named to honor the original Commodore Vanderbilt locomotive of 1870, it was the first to utilize streamlining by adding a streamlined shell over a steam locomotive, NYC's J-1E 4–6–4 “Hudson” No. 5344. An undergraduate student at Cleveland's Case School of Applied Science, Norman F. Zapf (1911–1974), designed the shell for his 1934 senior thesis, "The Streamlining of a Locomotive." Zapf tested scale models in a wind tunnel and concluded that at typical passenger speeds of 75 mph, streamlining reduced drag by 30 percent and required 350 less horsepower than a nonstreamlined locomotive. Nearly all his suggestions were incorporated into the final Commodore Vanderbilt. The nose form was similar to the "bathtub” design of the Burlington Zephyr. Carl Kantola, an in-house New York Central employee, worked on adapting the design to the required mechanical modifications. A number of sources cite Henry Dreyfuss as being a design consultant for the Commodore Vanderbilt, but his contributions, if any, are unknown. After being exhibited, the Commodore Vanderbilt departed on an exhibition tour of major cities on the NYC system, which was a smashing success, was highly photographed, and was surrounded by crowds wherever it went. After the tour, it was put in service hauling the 20th Century Limited between New York and Chicago, the grandest, most elegant passenger train in America. For the rest of the 1930s, the Commodore Vanderbilt was the most popular Lionel O-gauge model train. Although 1934 was one of the worst years of the ten-year Great Depression, it was the most exciting time for the new profession of industrial design, which became a household word as its leading practitioners became known nationally. Formal professional education in industrial design had begun. Manufacturers scrambled to catch up with the wave of modern design swirling around them by seeking new designs and new designers, hoping to extract themselves from the litany of declining sales reports. New design departments sprouted up in dozens of corporations. National spirits were rising due to the optimism generated by the Chicago Fair and the futuristic, streamlined trains crisscrossing the country. The future seemed to be the light at the end of the tunnel. The fledgling practitioners of industrial design, now that they had established legitimacy in the world of design and in the public arena, were looking to legally protect their innovative, modern, American designs from the wholesale copying that had previously been common practice for European designs. That had been the primary concern of the American Union of Decorative Artists (AUDAC) when it was formed in 1927. Now, in 1933, to address AUDAC's accusations of design piracy, representatives and designers in the furniture industry joined together to found the National Furniture Designers' Council (NFDC) in order to draw up a code for enjoining piracy for the National Recovery Administration (NRA). The purpose of the Roosevelt administration's establishment of the NRA in 1933 was to eliminate “cutthroat competition" by bringing industry, labor, and government together to create “fair practices" and to set prices. Industries were invited to submit codes of fair competition, and the furniture industry did so. But in 1935, the U.S. Supreme Court found that the NRA law was unconstitutional, ruling that it infringed on the separation of powers under the constitution. The NFDC disintegrated, but the seed for a furniture industry design organization had been planted, and in July 1935, Lawrence H. Whiting, director of the American Furniture Mart, called a special meeting of furniture designers, including those from the now defunct NFDC, and engaged Richard Bach, of the Metropolitan Museum, and Alfred Auerbach (1881–1944), design and merchandising consultant as well as editor of Retailing magazine, to serve as speakers for the meeting. The proceedings of the meeting are unknown, but the result, within a year, would be the forming of a furniture design organization, which will be described later. That year, Al Lambach, mentioned earlier, returned to Chicago to work for Merton Gershun at the Chicago Furniture Mart, and also for the Tell City Furniture Company in Tell City, Indiana, where he designed radio and phonograph cabinets for Zenith, Clinton, Traveler, and Emerson. Because home radios of the day were traditionally enclosed in furniture for placement in living rooms, a number of furniture designers in the 1920s and early 1930s were actively designing in this field. Furniture, as one of the traditional art industries, was still in the good graces of museums, such as Bach's Metropolitan Museum, and of course the architects as well, since many famous European architects, and equally famous American architects such as Frank Lloyd Wright, had been designing furniture for generations. A number of pioneer industrial designers had designed furniture too, including Norman Bel Geddes (Simmons Company, 1928); Paul Frankl (Skyscraper furniture, 1925); Gilbert Rohde (for Herman Miller Furniture Company, 1932–1935); John Vassos (RCA radio cabinets and his portable phonograph of 1935); Nathan Horwitt (Beta Chair for the Howell Company, Geneva, Illinois, 1930); Kem Weber (armchair by Lloyd Manufacturing Company of Menominee, Michigan, 1934); Donald Deskey (since 1926); and Russel Wright (Haywood-Wakefield, 1934). But most of those above had designed in the modern styles, not the traditional historic styles of many designers in the furniture industry. Throughout the 1930s, Wright and Walter Von Nessen had designed scores of modern metal accessories for the Chase Brass & Copper Company. In 1935, Wright had renamed his firm from Russel Wright Incorporated to Russel Wright Accessories, and designed a natural maple line of furniture called Modern Living, made by Conant Ball Company for Macy's, which was widely promoted. The popular line would later be called American Modern, produced in bleached wood“blond” maple, and would make Wright famous. He opened a showroom for his business in New York, with the help of businessman Irving Richards, under the name Russel Wright, Inc. In 1934, one of the pioneer furniture designers, Paul Frankl, relocated to California and became an active interior designer, working in a style that would be called “California Modern" at his gallery on Rodeo Drive in Beverly Hills, where celebrities such as Fred Astaire, Katherine Hepburn, Walter Houston, and Alfred Hitchcock became his clients. Other designers were working for celebrities. Around this time, Margaret Bourke-White (1904–1971), the famous Fortune photographer, approached John Vassos to design her studio in the Chrysler Building in New York, as well as the interior of her apartment in Tudor City. He did, and they remained close personal friends until her death. Furniture and furnishings were still the mainstream of the traditional art industries except that now modern design was replacing some of the traditional styles of historical periods. But the new mechanical, mass-produced products of the artless industries remained in artistic limbo as a category yet to be accepted by museums as legitimate art. Museums, including the Museum of Modern Art, and architects still had serious reservations about other mass-produced, mechanical products in which industrial designers were exclusively excelling, such as stoves, refrigerators, sinks, toilets, radios, office machines, etc. Elitists balked at calling these items furniture let alone works of art. To the elite artistic world, these products were commercially embarrassing, far exceeding the profitability of furniture or the decorative arts, and far more financially rewarding to the designers than any architect could expect. Accordingly, many regarded this arena of design as pure commercialism, the enemy of fine art, and were reluctant to embrace such mechanical products on an equal footing with furniture. Nevertheless, evidence of the influence of industrial design was everywhere in the consumer marketplace. Sears, Roebuck & Company had hired Raymond Loewy in 1932 to redesign its Coldspot refrigerator after Henry Dreyfuss had declined the project, due to his similar refrigerator project for General Electric. It was an unwritten code of ethics in early industrial design (and still is) that you didn't accept projects for products that were competitive with your own designs for another client. Upon accepting the job, Loewy described the current Coldspot as an “ill-proportioned vertical shoebox ‘decorated' with a maze of moldings, panels, etc. perched on spindly legs high off the ground."4 Loewy's design was actually done by Clare E. Hodgman, of Loewy's staff, who after the project was completed, was hired by Sears. The design was developed carefully with clay appearance models and came out in 1935—a sleek, white, vertical box with three, vertical, parallel ribs running from top to bottom in the center of its front, accenting the rectangular chrome Coldspot name escutcheon at eye level. Loewy had been inspired by his design of the interior shelf pattern from the radiator of the Hupmobile, on which he was working at the time. Sears paid Loewy \$2,500 for the design, but he had spent nearly three times that in expenses. But sales of the Coldspot in 1935 would double to \$1,388,000, and in five years would increase by fivefold. A highly appreciative Sears paid Loewy \$25,000 for three future annual model changes for the Coldspot, each of which was more aggressively modern than its predecessor. Loewy's reputation was made. He celebrated by relocating his office to a penthouse studio at Fifth Avenue and 47th Street and opening his first office in London, England, the first American designer to go international. Recall that his first design in 1929 was for an English client, Gestetner. He now had 12 clients and was doing very well. Sears hired Robert E. Bourke (1916–1996) in 1935. He was born in Chicago, went to Morgan Park High School in the southwest side, and as a kid was very interested in model airplanes. At age l1, he began to sketch model automobiles. He attended the Chicago Art Institute, cleaning floors and latrines to pay for tuition. He took courses in architecture and engineering at Armour Institute during the Depression and began working at Sears under Clarence Karstadt. His first assignment was to design a manure spreader by the Oliver Farm & Equipment Company. Jack Morgan headed the department. Just like his fellow employee, Clare Hodgman, Bourke would later work for Raymond Loewy. In May 1935, the French oceanliner Normandie, at the time the largest and fastest passenger ship afloat, completed her maiden voyage from Le Havre to New York in four days, three hours, and 14 minutes, winning the traditional Blue Riband (an unofficial accolade given to passenger liners for the highest average speed). The Normandie's luxurious interiors were in the latest Art Deco and Streamline Moderne styles, and the Normandie was quickly heralded as a ship for the rich and famous. ![](images/cd47ca04421bf85589589c7f9b471766263a33ae5f83618909343ed34e484187.jpg) Peter Müller-Munk, ca. 1950s. She was also the inspiration for an elegant, chrome-plated, brass pitcher designed by Peter Müller-Munk (1907–1967), the New York silversmith mentioned earlier. The Normandie Pitcher, shaped like the prow of its namesake, was made by the Revere Copper and Brass Company in Rome, New York, and would be produced until 1941. Müller-Munk was born in Germany, studied as a silversmith at the University of Berlin, and immigrated to the United States in 1926. He worked at Tiffany's in New York as a metalworker from 1926 to 1928, exhibiting at the 1928 Macy's exposition. In 1929, he established his own silver studio and exhibited at the 1929 and 1930 Metropolitan Museum shows. Now Müller-Munk was jumping into the nascent industrial design education field. At Carnegie Tech, in Pittsburgh, upon finishing the first year of the degree program he initiated, Donald Dohner was contacted by his former employer in the Pittsburgh public schools, James Boudreau, who had become dean of Pratt Institute's Art School in 1928. Boudreau wanted Dohner to organize and lead an industrial design program at Pratt, similar to the one he had just started at Carnegie. Having also worked with Boudreau as a lecturer and consultant at Pratt in 1932, Dohner accepted, and Müller-Munk was called in to replace him as head of the program at Carnegie. In the fall, Müller-Munk and Alexander Kostellow would continue Dohner's curriculum and practice of taking students to tour local manufacturing facilities and having them design products based on the production methods they had seen. As a matter of fact, it is this unique accommodation of the client manufacturer's production and economic limitations that distinguished industrial designers from their predecessors, the pure artists and industrial artists. Pioneer industrial designers were developing the unique philosophy of industrial design as a multidisciplinary process that was not only art, but a balance of economics, engineering, materials selection, and human convenience considerations. Their other distinguishing characteristic was their commitment to modern design, rather than the imitation of traditional period design styles, still clung to by many industrial artists, and the most modern style, now known as streamlining, was at the forefront of transportation design. On May 29, 1935, the first of a series of streamlined Hiawatha trains, the Twin Cities Hiawatha, began service from Chicago, through Milwaukee, to the twin cities of Minneapolis and St. Paul in morning and afternoon runs. Operated by the Milwaukee Railroad to compete with the Burlington Twin Cities Zephyr and North Western Railway's Twin Cities 400, the Class A 4–4–2 steam locomotive, built by the American Locomotive Company (ALCO), was designed by ALCO design consultant Otto Kuhler (1884–1977) with a nose form similar to the Burlington Zephyr, but without the front cab windows, the cab being in the rear as with all traditional steam locomotives. The nose was graced with horizontal stainless steel wings with a red engine number at its center. Kuhler also designed the famous Hiawatha “beaver tail" observation car at the rear, and all the interiors down to the napkins and draperies. Kuhler was born near Essen, Germany. At age 19 he was commissioned to illustrate a catalog of steam locomotives. His auto body design for a Mercedes chassis won a gold medal in 1913. He served in the German army in World War I, after which he enrolled in the Academy of Art in Dusseldorf to learn etching. He was employed as a stylist with N.A.G. in Berlin and designed sets for the silent film The Tunnel, then became associate editor of Der Motor magazine. He styled car bodies for Snutzel Pére & Fils in Brussels and a 1914 roadster for Fiat in Italy. Kuhler immigrated to the United States in 1923 and worked as a commercial artist in Pittsburgh. After becoming a citizen in 1928, he opened a studio in Manhattan, promoting the streamlining of railroad equipment and working for the J.G. Brill Company styling trains and trolley cars, including a smooth front-end trolley called the Brill Bullet in 1931. In 1932, the American Locomotive Company (ALCO) commissioned him as a design consultant. In April 1935, Business Week magazine announced that “Depression is a forgotten word in the automobile industry, which is forging ahead in production, retail sales, and expansion of productive capacity in a manner reminiscent of the twenties." At the Ford Motor Company in Detroit, the young styling department established in 1933 under Eugene T. “Bob" Gregorie had been struggling under the dominance of engineers, as the one at Chrysler had been. Ford stylists were subjugated to body engineers, who resented the stylist's role in design, and to Charlie Sorensen, who placed speed of production over beauty and style. Both were under the powerful Henry Ford, who staunchly resisted modernization and change of any type. But like Earl, Gregorie had a corporate patron who understood the growing importance of automotive appearance: Henry's son, Edsel (1893–1943), who was a sophisticated patron of the arts, and who had an excellent eye for design. Edsel was determined to win control of styling for Gregorie's department and assisted Gregorie in battles with engineers and production supervisors. Unlike GM, which had a complex bureaucracy, Ford had only Henry to deal with, and only Edsel to counter him. So Gregorie needed only to convince Edsel of his designs. Edsel had seen the styling disaster of the 1934 Chrysler Airflow by engineers, so when it was time to design the new, low-cost 1936 Lincoln, Edsel made sure Bob Gregorie was in full charge. The early concept for the car was the Briggs Dream Car, designed by John Tjaarda at Briggs for Ford at the 1933 Chicago Century of Progress Exposition, which was a rear-engine car not unlike a sleeker version of Ferdinand Porsche's Kleinauto then under development in Germany, with a front sloping hood that now reminded people of the discredited Chrysler Airflow. Edsel was also concerned and thought the design was "too Teutonic," knowing the German origins of the Kleinauto. Tjaarda was reluctant to change the design, so Edsel called Gregorie in to work with Tjaarda to resolve the dilemma. Gregorie decided to change the front end by relocating the motor to the front and enclosing it in a hood that was gracefully shaped like the inverted prow of a ship with a V-shaped grille. Gregorie's experience in boat design had contributed to an inspiring styling innovation. The fenders and teardrop rear end were retained, but with the ![](images/d8626a91c4660f0586a8ebb13741cd437f68e1680aca45b9f7ae995ec248e4bc.jpg) Lincoln Zephyr, 1936. new prowlike front end, the front fenders seemed to suggest water being sprayed up and to the sides from the prow. The final design would be introduced as the 1936 Lincoln Zephyr, capitalizing on the name of the popular streamlined Burlington Zephyr train. It would be hailed by many as the first commercially successful streamlined car, and its unique front-end design would be imitated by dozens of competitors over the next five years. It was indeed a watershed of automotive design and in fact remains a classic design today. Another transportation marvel was introduced in 1935, the Scarab, engineered by aviation engineer William Bushnell Stout (1880-1956), with the exterior designed by John Tjaarda. Unlike any car of the time, the Scarab was what many call the first minivan, with a large interior compartment with a flexible seating system that could accommodate up to ten passengers. Stout envisioned it as a mobile office. To create the space, he did away with the normal chassis and drive shaft by using a rear engine and a unitized body structure, permitting a low flat-floor interior. The body design was without fenders, presenting a fully streamlined aluminum aircraft-type shell with built-in headlights. Although the Scarab was offered for sale at \$5,000 (a Chrysler Airflow then sold for \$1,345) and up to a hundred a year were to be manufactured, only nine were actually made. The major star at the November 1935 New York Auto Show was the Cord 810 Winchester Sedan, designed by Gordon M. Buehrig (1904-1990) and made by the Auburn-Cord-Duesenberg Automobile Company in Connersville, Indiana. Cord was the brand name, after the owner of all three brands, Erret Lobban Cord, whose philosophy was to build innovative, different cars. The Cord 810 was indeed innovative and different. It featured front-wheel drive, a rear-hinged hood, retractable hidden headlights, absence of a running board, no visible grille, and was offered with a supercharger. It was highly streamlined, with a rounded, boxlike hood featuring a grille of horizontal louvers that wrapped around the lower half of the box, which some dubbed the “cofin." The crowds at the New York show were so dense that visitors had to stand on the bumpers of nearby cars to get a look. Many orders were taken at the show, but the cars would not be ready for delivery until April 1936, due to problems of production. Over 1,000 were sold the first year. In 1936, Buehrig would also design the 1937 Cord 812, which in 1951 would be displayed by the Museum of Modern Art as a work of art. But by 1937, Errett Cord's empire would collapse and all production would cease, another casualty of the Depression. In 1936, Buehrig would go to the Edward G. Budd Company in Philadelphia as chief designer of prototypes. ![](images/dd1f8a2a7bfe9e3f1af785fc0a35adc5bd8908ae4ae4d142cd8918aded19e469.jpg) 1936 Cord by Buehrig. Buehrig was born in Mason City, Illinois, and attended Bradley Polytechnic in Peoria before becoming an apprentice at the Gotfredson Body Company, Detroit, in 1924. He worked for Dietrich, Inc., in 1926, then at Packard, and in 1927 was one of the first employees hired by Harley Earl at the newly established Art and Colour Section at GM. In 1928 he worked for the Stutz Company in Indianapolis, and in 1929 became chief body designer for Auburn-Cord-Duesenberg, where he designed the Duesenberg Model J line of luxury cars. In 1933 he returned to GM, and in 1934 he returned to Auburn-Cord-Duesenberg. Harold Van Doren and his partner, John Gordon Rideout, had developed a growing industrial design business in Toledo, Ohio. In 1933, they had designed a bright green, plastic, skyscraper-style table radio for Air King Products, a large Magnalite (cast aluminum with varying thicknesses, allowing for maximum heat conductivity) teakettle for Wagner Manufacturing Company (designed by Rideout), and plastic stemware for the National Silver Company. In 1934 they designed a Magnalite aluminum covered casserole for Wagner, and a trendsetting sheet-metal gasoline pump for the Wayne Pump Company. In 1935, they designed a lightweight, modern Sentinel Duplex retail scale for Toledo Scale, using the largest plastic molding of the time, and developed an entire line of streamlined children's bicycles, tricycles, scooters, and wagons for the American National Company, including the Skippy-Racer Scooter (1933), the Skippy Sno-Plane Sled and the Skippy Stream Line Racer Wagon (1934), and the Streamline Velocipede and the Pioneer Streamlined Tot Bike (1935). Now even kids could enjoy streamlined transportation! However, in 1935 Rideout left Van Doren to open his own office in Cleveland, Ohio, John Gordon Rideout and Staff, and hired Arthur N. BecVar (1911–2003), who had graduated from Notre Dame University in 1933 with a BFA, received an engineering certificate from Purdue University, and an MFA at the Institute of International Education, Charles University, Prague, Czechoslovakia, in 1933–1934. With Rideout gone, Van Doren renamed his Toledo firm Harold Van Doren & Associates. The reason for Rideout's move was not business, nor problems with Van Doren, but the more romantic result of his relationship with Alice Chalifoux (1908–2008), a concert harpist with the Cleveland Symphony Orchestra and head of the harp department at the Cleveland Institute of Music, whom he married in 1937. ![](images/95d2a908037e07b1bd08611d01b8e75bfe5ffbc2093e7f77f9881afdfddb77ba.jpg) Arthur N. The 1930s saw the development of all kinds of plastics, new synthetic materials with integral coloring that could reduce weight as well as replace many metal parts, thus avoiding the costs of metal machining, finishing, and painting. Before the 1930s, the only plastic used in products was Bakelite, a thermosetting phenol formaldehyde resin developed in 1907 by Belgian-born chemist Leo Baekeland in New York. But it was only available in black, dark brown, or dark maroon, not particularly attractive consumer colors. The new plastics of the 1930s offered a full palette of colors. Typical was the urea formaldehyde called Plaskon used by Van Doren and Rideout in the white Toledo scales, mentioned above. Polystyrene and acrylic, the latter called by trade names Plexiglas or Lucite, had been developed in 1930. Polyethylene was developed in 1933, nylon in 1934 by DuPont, polyester in 1936, and melamine formaldehyde, called Melamine, and Dacron, both in 1938. Plastics transformed the look and feel of consumer products. Not only that, but plastics revolutionized the manufacturing process. New injection molding machines melted a stream of small pellets of raw plastic into semiliquid form and forced it into complex steel molds. It quickly cooled back into solid form, the finished part was ejected, and the machine would begin the next cycle. Hundreds of parts could be produced within hours. Industrial designer Belle Kogan, who at the time was designing clocks in plastics for the Warren Telecron Company, wrote articles in 1935 plastics magazines urging manufacturers to use plastics, which she described as a material with tremendous possibilities ... which no manufacturer, if he be alert and watchful of his competition, can afford to overlook. Radios, clocks, dishes, jewelry—all being developed today—have an enormous significance. Kogan also pointedly advised manufacturers to study the “feminine viewpoint," citing the fact that thirty million women—all potential customers-—constituting practically the entire buying structure of the nation—comprise a force, which cannot, or should not, be disregarded. The tastes of the American woman, her reaction to color and form, are of vital importance to the manufacturer.⁶ In another article, Kogan also advised designers to do their homework: It is the designer's job, therefore, to know materials, to know their qualities, their basic structures, their resistance to wear, and their cost—as well as to know markets and trends—in order to intelligently design for the manufacturer.? Her articles were typical of others being written by industrial designers in trade magazines—stressing practical, technical, and cost-reduction results of design rather than simply artistic beauty. Kogan had an office in New York, producing houseware designs in silver, aluminum, ceramics, glass, wood and cloth for Quaker Silver Company and many other clients. In 1936 she would design a modern double serving dish in silver for Reed & Barton. Regular commercial air transportation was just beginning, inspired by Charles Lindberg's solo flight across the Atlantic in 1927. The China Clipper, a Martin M-130 four-engine flying boat built for Pan American Airways, initiated mail service on November 22, 1935, from San Francisco to Manila. Its interiors, intended for long Pacific trips (59 hours, 48 minute fying time, with overnight stops at Honolulu, Midway, Wake Island and Guam), with sofa-type seating that converted into sleeping berths, lavatories, bar, kitchen, and pantry, were designed by Norman Bel Geddes in 1934. The twin-engine Douglas DC-3 airliner initiated commercial domestic flights shortly thereafter, on December 17, 1935, after refinement with development models DC-1 (1933) and DC-2 (1934). The DC-3 carried 21 passengers, and a transcontinental flight took about 15 hours, with only three refueling stops. People were astounded at the potential for future air travel. Kem Weber was probably inspired to design his famous Airline chair in 1934 by earlier versions of the DC-3. Weber had labored for months to design a chair that could be sold disassembled and readily put together by the consumer, and the Airline chair was the result. Weighing 17 pounds and packed flat in a box, it was to sell for \$24.95. At the same time that industrial design began to flourish, the Roosevelt administration's New Deal was pouring funds into new relief programs to help the unemployed, to provide social entitlement programs for the elderly, and to allow unions to organize. Industrial design, which was now driving industry, would become a potential political target of the newly empowered, organized American labor movement. ## Labor Design and Design Organization With all the publicity and attention focused on the inevitable march of modern technology and design, as well as on the high unemployment levels, strikes and labor disputes of the time, it was perhaps inevitable that industrial design would become involved with government and the intensive labor activism of the Depression era. In April 1935, the Works Progress Administration (WPA) was created by an act of Congress upon the order of President Franklin Roosevelt as part of the Second New Deal. At the time, there were 20 million persons on relief. In order to employ the unemployed, the act provided \$4.9 billion in funds for the construction of roads, bridges, and local public buildings, but it also employed musicians, artists, actors and writers in public works. A number of artist/designers, including some mentioned in this book, took advantage of WPA grants to paint murals in post offices, universities, and other public buildings. In July, the Wagner National Labor Relations Act validated union authority. By August, the Social Security Act provided retirement benefits, the first federal assistance to the elderly. In September, the Hoover Dam (named after Herbert Hoover, president when it was begun in 1931) was dedicated, creating Lake Mead, the world's largest reservoir at the time, with President Roosevelt presiding. The Roosevelt administration quickly renamed it Boulder Dam, but the confusion between names continued. In 1947, the name would be changed back to Hoover Dam. On December 2, 1935, in New York, a frigid and blustery day, several hundred men and women lined up outside the 35th Street offices of the WPA for a chance to enroll in the Design Laboratory, a pioneering school sponsored by the Federal Art Project (FAP), one of the WPA's cultural projects. It was the first institution to offer free, comprehensive education in modernist design. Its brochure claimed it had been “created to supply a hitherto unfulfilled and pressing need in America" for people with skills to devise, package, and promote consumer products that were styled to sell, thereby stimulating economic recovery. Clearly, this noble purpose was inspired by the incredible success of industrial designers in doing precisely that over the previous eight years. By emphasizing “coordination in the study ofesthetics, industrial products, machine fabrication, and merchandizing," the school would "train designers, not specialized craftsmen, by correlating thorough instruction in the general principles of design and the fine arts with shop practice." Two hundred twenty people enrolled that day, more that the school's administration anticipated, and there were still hundreds more on a waiting list The school was the result of political action by the Depression-era avant-garde, the unprecedented public arts bureaucracy of the New Deal, the militant industrial unionism of the Congress of Industrial Organizations (CIO), which had just been formed within the American Federation of Labor (AFL), the United Auto Workers (UAW), and the radical socialist politics of the Popular Front (a broad coalition of different political groupings, often made up of centrists and leftists). The collaboration was an example of how modernism was viewed by some as a means to control cultural production by political action and thus prevent its dominance by business interests and capitalism. The idea of democratizing American culture was attractive to many elites of the time, who resented the new lucrative art form of industrial design being controlled by big business and, by the way, a handful of industrial design celebrities getting rich in the process. The school organizers recruited well-known industrial designers such as Raymond Loewy, Walter Dorwin Teague, Donald Deskey, George Sakier, and Russel Wright to lend legitimacy to an advisory board by the use of their names, and lured furniture designer Gilbert Rohde to serve as director. It is likely that these men were attracted by the idealistic intention of training young designers, as stated in the brochure, and as desperately needed in the field, rather than by the hidden leftist ideology behind it. This ideology was obvious in major study texts required by the curriculum, including The Theory of the Leisure Class, by Thorstein Veblen, published in 1899, which claimed that reform of industrial art had to be through the technology of machines, and highly influential sociologist/historian Lewis Mumford's book, Technics and Civilization (1934). Mumford considered streamlining as mere expressive and “outmoded decoration” and insisted that functionalist esthetics (such as the geometric abstract forms at the Bauhaus) was more appropriate for society, a view shared and promulgated by the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA), which venerated Bauhaus architects, and which would enthusiastically take up the cause of functional esthetics. The recent closure of the leftist Bauhaus by the Nazis as “a Marxist manifestation” generated much political sympathy throughout the liberal and progressive art community, which actively sought to bring exiled Bauhaus refugees to the United States to lead the modern movement they had initiated in Europe. Support of the Bauhaus was seen by some as a gesture of patriotic defiance against Nazism and war. Others saw communism as a similar deterrent to the evils of fascism, since Hitler's government was fervently anti-communist, and communists were fighting incessantly against Hitler and fascism. Despite internal political and philosophical differences of faculty members, the school implemented a provisional three-year design curriculum in August 1936, which promised to “present the ... standards of taste and style ... in the so-called International Style"” (the term invented and promoted by MoMA starting in 1932 and based on the Bauhaus). At the same time, an essay by the Federal Art Project (FAP) historian published in The Magazine of Art in October 1936 denigrated the “handful of super-men who are revolutionizing everything from hairpins to locomotives at a fabulous price," an unmistakable reference to the leading industrial designers. Many students and faculty of the Design Laboratory joined militant organizations of cultural workers, including those where communists, socialists, or Trotskyites (those supporting the political and economic theories of communism advocated by Leon Trotsky and his followers, usually including the principle of worldwide revolution) predominated, and instigated strikes and demonstrations demanding autonomy of the school, calling for a “United Front against War and Fascism," all of which put them at odds with FAP administrators. In December, several students and faculty were arrested for public protests and demonstrations.' Despite the labor unrest and strikes, 1936 was dawning with signs of economic recovery. The main economic indicators had regained the levels of the late 1920s, except for unemployment, still high at 11 percent, though this was much lower than the 25 percent of 1933. Industrial production had recovered significantly, and many businesses were getting back to normal, thus increasing design activity. In February 1936, Raymond Loewy, with employee Carl Otto (b. ca. 1910–ca. 1986 ), hired in 1935, designed the Pennsylvania Railroad's K-4s streamlined shroud for steam locomotive No. 3768, hiding most of the mechanical details. The long-term success of many product designs of the 1930s seems to conflict with the concept described in 1932 by Elmo Calkins of “planned obsolescence," which predicted that annual design changes spur consumer spending. But the longevity of certain product designs can be explained by several reasons. First, while many consumers are eager to obtain newer versions of a product, there are also some who are not and who will keep and use a favored product until it expires mechanically. Second, many new, industrial-designed products, for the first time in the 1930s, acquired a shape, style, and configuration that seemed "right" for its assigned task, establishing what is called a “typeform," that is, a form that seemed to many the ideal for a particular type, or category, of product. Other manufacturing competitors, noting the popular success of a particular brand, would imitate the design, therefore increasing public familiarity and acceptance of the standard typeform until some substantially different, more popular, configuration replaced it as the new typeform for that particular product. The KitchenAid mixer survives because it is regarded as the ideal typeform for this particular category of products. The power of historically successful designs is illustrated by the automotive industry, which has had dozens of different, successive typeforms over the years, but would remain nostalgic about car designs of the 1930s, which produced the first designs that were truly modern. Detroit in the late 1990s and early 2000s would develop a number of so-called retro designs, intending to recall that period, and which were intended to replicate the original typeforms. They included the (new) Volkswagen Beetle (1994), the Plymouth Prowler (1994), Chrysler's PT (Personal Transportation) Cruiser (1999), Buick's 2001 Blackhawk, Ford's 2002 Forty-Nine, and Chevrolet's HHR (Heritage High Roof) in 2006, all in 1930s styles. But in the late 1930s, the problem was too many cars, and not enough roads. Since half of all households now owned an automobile, the U.S. road system was being challenged by an increasing number of cars on narrow, crowded roads. It was an obvious problem addressed by Norman Bel Geddes, on assignment for the J. Walter Thompson advertising agency in 1937. Based on his experience with his scale-model racetrack of the 1920s, and his war game of the early 1930s, he designed a model City of Tomorrow with expressways featured in a national advertising campaign for the Shell Oil Company. The model would be expanded to become the Futurama exhibit for General Motors at the 1939 New York World's Fair, which in turn would inspire the future federal interstate highway system. Jumping rom highway systems to cocktail parties, Bel Geddes also designed a Skyscraper cocktail shaker and a Manhattan serving tray in 1937. The automobile industry was transformed during the Depression. Car production fell from 5.6 million in 1929 to 1.4 million in 1932. Dozens of auto manufacturing brands in 1929 were reduced to 10 in 1935, which included the Big Three of Ford, Chrysler, and General Motors. GM, with five new models since 1930, led the pack and in 1936 was unionized. In 1937, GM's Art and Colour Section would change its name to the Styling Division. Harley Earl had a staff of 200 designers, cranking out annual model changes on the fleet of GM cars. Earl supervised five studios, one for each division (Cadillac, Buick, Oldsmobile, Pontiac, and Chevrolet), as well as 12 special studios. Yet he wanted more designers, and so GM and its Fisher Body Division initiated a talent search by sponsoring and promoting a national, 1:12 scale model (one inch equals one foot) “Dream Car” design competition that awarded college scholarships to the best original student designs for a four-door sedan (later various different body styles were added). It was called the Fisher Body Craftsman's Guild, which would continue for 30 more years, encouraging a new generation of automotive designers, many of whom would end up in Detroit, and not a few in Earl's studios. Actually, the contest was a major addition to an earlier Fisher Body Craftsman's Guild contest that was initiated in 1930, not to innovate designs, but to accurately replicate Fisher plans for a miniature scale model (1:15 to 1:18, where one inch equaled 1.25 to 1.6 feet) of a 19th-century horse-drawn Napoleonic coach, the trademark adopted by Fisher in 1923, and which appeared on all production Fisher bodies made for GM. There were two competitive categories: the Junior Division (ages 12 through 15) and the Senior Division (ages 16 through 19). Winners won prize money for a scholarship to a college of their choice: \$5,000 for each of the top two winners in each category. That was more than double what a man typically earned in a year! The Napoleonic coach contest would continue along with the new model car competition until 1940, so applicants could choose either one to enter, but the Napoleonic contest ended at that time. Students would no longer be interested in historical craftsmanship, but in futuristic cars. In 1935, Amos Northup, automotive designer for the Murray Corporation of America, the independent Detroit auto body manufacturer, had patented his famous “shark-nose” hood design, which generally resembled its namesake. The design would be copied by most manufacturers in the late 1930s, including the 1938 Graham, the 1937 Willys “windswept” design, the 1936 Lincoln Zephyr, and the 1940 Studebaker, Chrysler, Plymouth, and Ford. But in 1937, Amos Northup died at age 48 as a result of slipping on ice and fatally cracking his skull. His designs were a powerful influence as cars in the 1930s were transformed from boxes to the sleek forms that are familiar to us today. Most GM production designs during the 1930s were not futuristic, dramatic or innovative, but followed the relatively conservative annual advance of styling. This changed in 1938, when Earl developed a more radical prototype Buick called only the Y-Job. Like the similarly sounding name iPhone by Apple in 2007 that predicted the future appearance of all handheld computer devices, the Y-Job predicted the shape and style of most postwar cars. Why the name "Y-Job"? Most experimental cars were tagged with an "X," sometimes with a name or number. Just to be different, Earl decided to use the letter “Y," the next letter in the alphabet. The Y-Job was designed by Oldsmobile studio head George Snyder under Earl's direct supervision, with highly innovative styling features of hidden headlights, suitcase fenders, low horizontal grille, flush push-button door handles, electric windows, a poweroperated electric convertible top, and a rounded fuselage body without traditional running boards. Most unusual was the Y-Job's low, long silhouette with a 58-inch (less than five feet) height at the top of the split, angled windshield, A dozen closely-spaced, parallel and horizontal chrome strips (speed lines) adorned the lower half of both front and rear fenders, the chrome bumpers wrapped around the body corners, and a chrome “bombsight" hood ornament, which would become a Buick trademark, was the icing on the cake. The Y-Job was never shown to the public until after the war, but Earl used it as his personal transportation and it attracted a lot of public attention that way. Some of its features would appear in the 1938 Cadillac 60 Special, designed by Bill Mitchell (1912–1988), who had been hired by Earl in 1935. But after the war, the 1950 Buick would look almost like a clone of the 1938 Y-Job. In 1938, Earl became a vice president of GM, and he would rule GM styling for the next 20 years. In 2001, Buick would recreate the Y-Job with modern advancements called the Buick Blackhawk, drawing extensively from the Y-Job and the 1939 Buick. It was entirely handmade, cost \$475,000, and would be featured in the 2003 ilm Bad Boys II. On June 15, 1938, Dreyfuss saw the introduction of his design oflocomotive shrouds for the New York Central Railroad's 20th Century Limited, which was a refinement of his Mercury K-5 design of the previous year. The shrouds hid the assembly of boiler valves, pipes, and operating parts of ten new J-3, 4–6–4 Hudson-type steam locomotives. The shrouds, similar to Loewy's K-4 for the Pennsylvania Railroad, featured bullet-nosed boiler shapes, but with a center fin that resembled ancient warrior helmets. Dreyfuss also designed the cars using blue, gray, and silver, and like the K-5, they were designed with asymmetric furnishings to avoid the tunnel look of most train interiors. Probably the most famous American train, the 20th Century Limited was an express train that operated from 1902 to 1967 between Grand Central Station in New York and LaSalle Street Station in Chicago. The new train by Dreyfuss made the trip in 16 hours. That same year, Dreyfuss also designed Model A and Model B tractors for John Deere, a new client he had acquired in 1937. Raymond Loewy was having a banner year in 1938, the year he became an American citizen. He opened a branch office in South Bend, Indiana, to service his major client, Studebaker. To head the group, he appointed Virgil Exner (1909–1973), formerly with Harley Earl at General Motors, who had joined Loewy in 1937. Exner, born in Ann Arbor, Michigan, studied art at Notre Dame but had to drop out in 1928 due to lack of funds. He took a job as an advertising artist at an agency handling the Studebaker account. He was hired by Earl in 1933, where he became styling chief for Pontiac and designed Pontiac's famous “Silver Streak" hood ornament. ![](images/a57674d97c327a6acd73fd389c7f7d2c8310e9b802c3f29a30a7635828452133.jpg) Pennsylvania Railroad's 20th Century Limited, by Henry Dreyfuss. Loewy also hired Clare Hodgman, who had started his career in the early 1930s with General Motors in the Oldsmobile studio, and who had worked for Loewy on the Sears Coldspot refrigerator project in 1932–1935. The first task of the group was to begin work on the 1939 Studebaker Champion, a low-cost model. They also designed a new Studebaker “Big S” logo and emblem. Loewy had established a personal office in London in 1935, but now in 1938, he staffed it to function on a continuous basis, with Carl Otto (d. 1986) as head, along with Claire E. Hodgman. The new expanded office designed a logo for the British Petroleum (BP) Company, while the New York office designed the main hall for the ocean liner Panama. Hodgman worked on the Hillman Minx and the Rootes Sunbeam Talbot, and also designed conceptual racecars for the British Autocar magazine of March 1938. One of Otto's accounts was Standard Auto (Triumph). In 1938, Brooks Stevens designed the first commercially successful steam iron for the Edmilton Company in Milwaukee, the Steam-O-Matic, which was introduced at Macy's in New York and Marshall Field's in Chicago. Viktor Schreckengost, head of the Cleveland School (now Institute) of Art, began creating stage and costume designs for Cleveland theater productions and continued to produce award-winning ceramic sculptures for Cleveland Museum of Art shows. Kem Weber was designing a line of tubular steel chairs for the Lloyd Manufacturing Company in Menominee, Michigan. In Chicago, Ann Swainson, head of Montgomery Ward's Bureau of Design, designed a plastic radio cabinet, and her staff designed special china called Cascade, which was exhibited at the large-scale exhibition on modern design at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, and which became one of Montgomery Ward's most popular patterns. Swainson had traveled in 1937 to her native Sweden, visiting factories, department stores, and art schools, and was impressed with the Swedish government's part-time training of industrial designers in schools and factories. Elsewhere in the United States, other corporations were hiring and engaging industrial designers as part of their merchandizing strategy, seeking increased sales and modern style. In 1938, a major corporation was now expanding its industrial design activity into all visual aspects of corporate identity. Libbey-Owens-Ford (LOF) was formed in 1931 by the merger of Libbey-Owens Sheet Glass Company and another Toledo company, Edward Ford Plate Glass Company, which pioneered in automotive safety glass. In 1937, H. Creston Doner had been invited by LOF to leave his post with Goodrich in Akron, Ohio, to come to Toledo to direct the department of design there starting in 1938, a position he would hold until 1967. He supervised the architectural and interior design operations, as well as promotional campaigns for color and lighting effects; the design of LOF warehouses, showrooms, offices, and retail stores; and the design of a new corporate image for truck fleets, building signs, business forms, and advertising materials. Meanwhile, in 1935, Libbey Glass Manufacturing Company in Toledo had been acquired by Owens-Illinois Glass Company and became a subsidiary. In 1938, Edwin W. Fuerst was appointed to head the design department at Libbey Glass, while his brother, Dwight Fuerst headed the design area for bottling and packaging at Owens-Illinois. Another industrial designer, Walter Dorwin Teague, also had clients in Toledo in 1938. He had been called in as a consultant to the Owens-Illinois Glass Company, which had acquired the Libbey Glass Company. Teague was to work with two divisions: bottling and packaging, and structural. He designed an Insulis glass construction block in a Circon pattern for the Owens-Illinois Glass Company's structural division, which then asked him to design the division's offices in the Chrysler Building in New York. Additional design organizations began to be formed. In Chicago in July 1938, the Designer's Institute of the American Furniture Mart (DIAFM), founded in 1936 under the auspices of Lawrence H. Whiting, director of the American Furniture Mart, met for the first time since its founding, “at the instigation of Whiting, Alfred Auerbach, and Richard F. Bach of the Metropolitan Museum of Art."1º The principal speaker this time was John Vassos, then head of design at the Radio Corporation of America (RCA) in New York. In his address, Vassos urged that the organization divorce itself from the patronage and support of the Furniture Mart, because it restricted member work to furniture design. In fact, most members were furniture designers, and Vassos himself designed radios, which were often enclosed in wooden furniture. Vassos suggested the organization extend its practices beyond that of furniture design into consumer products, packaging, automotive design or any number of additional design opportunities then available. The group, comprised of 45 cofounding members, agreed with him. Among them were certainly Alfons Bach, Leo Jiranek, Al Lambach, Ernie Swarts, Belle Kogan, and probably, although not confirmed, Lionel C. Algoren (1896–1984), Kem Weber, Donald Deskey, and Gilbert Rohde. They changed the name of the organization on the spot to the American Designers' Institute (ADI) to indicate that it was a national, not just a Chicago organization. Even though Vassos was not currently a member of the group before his presentation, the group elected him the first president of ADI, which would become incorporated in the state of Illinois in 1940. Belle Kogan was elected secretary and treasurer, and she set up meetings, often with speakers, keeping the minutes and other records. Her only help came from her own office staff.
