# Competition and Controversy The Korean War began in June 1950, threatening industry with restrictions of critical materials. Peter Müller-Munk used the threat of austerity to urge manufacturers to reduce excessive decoration of products: The electrical and automotive industries . have reveled in ... superfluous shiny decoration ... to catch the customer's eye and attention.... The effect of this has been deplored by every serious designer.... Chrome and fancy trim used freely without a design need now can be easily taken off.1 In 1950 the New York and Philadelphia design offices of Van Doren, Nowland, & Schladermundt announced they would operate as two independent frms. Van Doren remained in control of his Philadelphia office as Harold Van Doren & Associates, and the New York firm became known as Nowland & Schladermundt. Brooks Stevens designed the Skylark pattern for Formica, the postwar surface of choice for kitchen and bathroom countertop surfaces. Skylark was called the “boomerang” pattern, because of the multicolor, thin line shapes resembled boomerangs, as well as alluding to supersonic airplane wings and modernity. Henry Dreyfuss Associates designed an aluminum outdoor telephone booth for AT&T, which became ubiquitous across the country until cell phones became the norm in the 1990s. C. Stowe Myers was recruited from Teague to join Loewy, Inc., in New York. He would manage Loewy's Chicago office from 1952 to 1954. Arthur BecVar, head of design at General Electric in Bridgeport, Connecticut, relocated in 1950 to Louisville, Kentucky, to become GE's manager of industrial design for major appliances. Donald McFarland would become manager of product planning at GE Bridgeport and would later replace BecVar as head of small appliances. In Chicago, Robert Budlong designed a transverse, two-slot Radiant Control 'T-20 toaster for Sunbeam that lowered bread automatically and raised itself silently when done. It would remain in production until 1995. Also in 1950, Budlong designed, with Sunbeam staff designer Ivar Jepson, the compact Sunbeam Shavemaster electric shaver with a smooth rounded head, the first compact, ergonomic shape held in the palm of the hand, rather than the previous elongated shape held like a hammer. He continued to design radio cabinets for Zenith. Five years after the war, the U.S. economy was back to normal, and businesses were humming along, despite the Korean War, which had reinitiated the draft for single young men. The first generation of postwar industrial designers had graduated and was expanding the field into the smallest of manufacturing companies. Annual salaries for designers started at about \$4,500 (\$43,500 in 2013 dollars). There was no longer a question of the value of industrial design to successful products, but there remained a residual resentment by some engineering managers, who were still in charge of most product development, and who guarded their traditional authority in matters of product design. Proposals by industrial designers, even the most famous ones, were not always accepted. But designers persevered and integrated themselves into sometime hostile environments. Americans were in the mood to spend, manufacturers still had little international competition, and design organizations were expanding and promoting their profession. They were generally good times. By 1951, many Americans were living comfortable middle-class lives, with all the latest appliances, cars, and furnishings. Russel and Mary Wright were designing paper Christmas ornaments, paper table service, table linens, and draperies, among a range of accessories and pottery. They also wrote and published A Guide to Easier Living, which placed them in the position of being the Martha Stewart of their time. The book, in the manner of Stewart, suggested a variety of ways that young families could better organize their homes and make housekeeping easier, such as buying furniture that was easy to clean, how to organize a room for maximum living space, and simple ways to maintain a pleasant and attractive living environment. The American Designers Institute (ADI) changed its name to the Industrial Designers Institute (IDI) in 1951 in order to address the continuing criticism by the Society of Industrial Designers (SID) that “industrial design" was absent in its title, thus accepting members from any specialized product industry, or even one-of-a-kind craftsmen. SID, of course, only accepted designers who designed products in a variety of mass-production industries. This remained as a fundamental difference in the two organizations, as well as their geographical separation of headquarters (ADI in Chicago, SID in New York). The subtle change in title was enough to convince the Chicago Society of Industrial Designers (CSID), founded by Dave Chapman and others in 1938, to merge with IDI. Chapman had been president of SID in 1950, and Russel Wright in 1951. SID had chapters in New York, Chicago, Detroit, and Los Angeles. The competing organizations were growing closer. By 1955, IDI would relocate its headquarters from Chicago to New York, where many of its 200 members resided. In 1951, IDI, possibly inspired by MoMA's “Good Design" exhibitions, initiated the first national industrial design awards program, which recognized the best designs and credited the actual designers by name. This did not sit well with their rival society, SID, where large offices like Loewy, Dreyfuss and Teague never credited anyone but their namesake. But over the next 15 years of these awards programs, most SID members began to credit individual designers, except Dreyfuss, who remained adamant about the issue. No designs from Henry Dreyfuss Associates (HDA) ever won IDI awards, probably because none were submitted. Nevertheless, the awards broke the ice, and many individual designers finally received public design credit for their work, which greatly enhanced their reputations and careers. Dreyfuss appeared on the cover of Forbes magazine in 1951, part of the Society of Industrial Designers' publicity campaign. His firm that year designed an innovative round wall thermostat for Honeywell, the interiors for American Export Lines' SS Independence and SS Constitution ocean liners, and a lavatory sink for Crane. Carl Otto resigned from Raymond Loewy in 1950 to establish his own firm, Carl Otto Design, with offices in New York, Stockholm, and London. His New York office was in the Squib building on Fifth Avenue. He hired Harold Vanderhyde; Jay Doblin (1920–1989), recently director of the night school at the Pratt Institute; Albrecht Graf von Goertz, the equivalent of an Earl (all ex-Loewy men); and Alban Gifford Jackson (b. 1922), a New Zealand boat designer who had just arrived in America in 1949, and who in 2013, at age 91, would receive New Zealand's Order of Merit for his distinguished career in industrial design. Otto and his staff designed the 1951 Schick Model 20 electric shaver in collaboration with Norman Gray, chief engineer at Schick, Inc., reducing the bulk and length of the shaver by rearranging the internal components from the traditional “in-line” configuration to a more compact, wider shape, more easily held. The design won an IDI design award in 1951 and became the standard typeform for shavers thereafter, reaching six million in sales. Otto's firm would also design the 1952 Edison Voicewriter portable recorder (competitor to Dictaphone), which would win an IDI award in 1953. That year, Otto worked on an account in England from the Standard Motor Company to design the Triumph Vanguard. He had hired Tucker Madawick, an automotive design expert with Loewy, to help with clay models and the final design. Otto's international firm would close in 1954, and Madawick would return to the Loewy office as an account executive. Clare Hodgman had also resigned from Loewy in 1951. John Gordon Rideout, one of the founders of the Society of Industrial Designers in 1944, had closed his Chagrin Falls, Ohio, office in 1946 and passed away at age 53 in 1951. Greta Von Nessen designed the Anywhere lamp, which could be used on a table, mounted on the wall, or suspended from the ceiling. Franz Wagner, Richard Latham, and Don Defano, of the Raymond Loewy Corporation, designed the elegant Flight bathroom scale that the Borg-Erikson Corporation would introduce in 1953. It would be selected by Fortune magazine as one of the top 500 designs of all time and would win an IDI national design award in 1954. In Chicago, John Morgan patented a deep fryer. In 1951, Walter Paepcke, head of the Container Corporation of America, initiated the International Design Conference in Aspen (IDCA), which would become an annual event, bringing top designers and philosophers to the lovely natural beauty of the Colorado ski resort. Among the founding members of IDCA was H. Creston Doner, director of the Libby-Owens-Ford design department. The machine design founders were still suffering under their unfortunate “streamlining” reputation. In 1951, Walter Dorwin Teague, in an article promoting American design in Britain's Art & Industry magazine, chided his British colleagues for a certain amount of “ribbing” .. on what they consider our penchant for “streamlining” .. [which has become] a popular metaphor for anything smooth and svelte ... and it is carefully avoided by designers except when it can be used with technical accuracy [as in aerodynamics].2 Of course, while the use of the term was fading as a general criticism of American industrial design in general, the automotive industry continued to utilize streamlining “with technical accuracy." Harley Earl and General Motors (GM) led the automotive design field. In 1951 Earl exhibited a concept convertible called LeSabre, named after the North American F-86 Sabre jet fighter plane used in the Korean War, then still in progress. It featured the first genuine curved, wraparound windshield, which had been in development by Libby-Owens-Ford Glass Company since 1946. Tail fins and a grille simulating a jet plane air intake completed the aircraft theme. LeSabre would set GM style trends for the next ten years, as cars became lower, sportier, and costlier. Few American car designs were included in the Museum of Modern Art's exhibition 8 Automobiles in 1951, MoMA's attempt to subtly criticize the current style trends of Detroit, and to suggest what Detroit should be designing. The eight examples were chosen as historical works of art, MoMA's usual definition of“Good Design." Five of the eight were European; the only U.S. designs were the 1937 Cord, the 1940 Lincoln Continental, and the 1941 wartime military Jeep. Jeep, 1941. In 1951, Loewy wrote and published his book, Never Leave Well Enough Alone, which was a flamboyant, romanticized, and self-promotional autobiography that influenced scores of young men into the profession that seemed to offer the wealth, influence, and celebrity that Loewy enjoyed. As a hobby in 1952, Raymond Loewy established an office in Paris, Compagnie de L'Esthetique Industrielle (CEI, Company of Industrial Esthetics), headed by Harold Barnett. For the next 30 years, the office would design packaging, architecture, and products throughout Europe. By this time, U.S. industrial design students by the hundreds were graduating from about 30 institutions of higher learning with industrial design programs, over 20 new ones since the end of World War II. New firms were being started by recent graduates across the country to provide local, and less expensive, industrial design services than the large, urban offices in Chicago, New York, or the West Coast. Most would not be as fortunate as Loewy, as industrial design salaries and fees were being reduced because demand no longer exceeded supply, as it had in the 1930s and 1940s. In 1952, Hall Cutlery introduced a stainless steel flatware pattern called Highlight, designed by Russel Wright, to match his design of Highlight dinnerware, introduced by Hall in 1951. The dinnerware had won MoMA's Home Furnishing Award. The Highlight flatware was the first modern and contemporary flatware designed in the United States since World War II and would be imitated by many competitors. The United States' 20-year monopoly in industrial design was coming to an end, with many international events devoted to industrial design and designers as part of the development of an international design community. New professional design organizations and international consulting groups were formed in a number of European countries and Japan. Japan established industrial design programs with the assistance of a number of U.S. designers, including Russel Wright, George Nelson, Freda Diamond and Jean Reinecke, who traveled to Japan after 1950 to assist in this process. Germany's Rosenthal China engaged the Raymond Loewy Corporation (RLC) as consultants. In 1954, urged by Richard Latham, head of Loewy's Chicago office, Rosenthal began using international designers for its special division of Studio-Linie products, starting with Coffee Service 2000 by Latham for RLC. These products were distributed internationally in special showrooms called Studio Houses, and over the next 30 years, Rosenthal would employ more than 100 designers from 11 nations for this exclusive line. Industrial design was in the process of becoming an international and global business. Latham in 1955 would team together with Bob Tyler and George Jensen, all previously on Loewy's Chicago design staff, to form the new firm of Latham-Tyler-Jensen, with a staff of 14. In 1954, The Tenth Triennale was held in Milan, Italy, with the theme “Industrial Aesthetics." Peter Müller-Munk, then president of the Society of Industrial Designers (SID), sent exhibits of 40 U.S. products designed by its members for exhibition in Milan, including two Buckminster Fuller geodesic domes, the innovative architectural structures that would make Fuller famous. It was also the tenth anniversary of the founding of SID, and to celebrate its anniversary, SID published Industrial Design in America, a 224-page hardcover book with color illustrations and case histories, publicizing the work ofits members. In addition, because industrial design was becoming a worldwide phenomenon, the name of the organization was changed to the American Society of Industrial Designers (ASID). By 1954, interest in MoMA's annual Good Design exhibitions was slowing down. During that year's winter market, in addition to the Good Design exhibit, the Merchandise Mart staged an exhibition called Good Companions: Today in Tradition, which showed “what the manufacturers of traditional furniture, fabrics and lamps are providing for the homes of today." The reality was that “Good Design," as defined by MoMA, was not popular either with the general public, or with the majority of home furnishing manufacturers, who, unlike MoMA, depended on public acceptance for their livelihood. Even today, traditional (antique) home furnishing styles far outsell so-called modern furnishings by a wide margin. So much for the home furnishings industry, but MoMA still wanted to expand its architectural idea of “Good Design” into what had become the exclusive territory of industrial designers: electromechanical products, major appliances, and automobiles (the latter at its 1951 8 Automobiles show). Accordingly, the jury for the 1955 winter market exhibition of “Good Design” included Arthur BecVar, head of design at General Electric in Louisville, Kentucky, and then president of the American Society of Industrial Designers (ASID). It was MoMA's first token recognition of organized design on any “Good Design" jury. BecVar, Edgar Kaufmann, Jr., and Just Lunning of Georg Jensen's New York store selected 400 products for this final Good Design exhibition, which Interiors magazine noted with surprise included a number of“industrial designs that hardly qualified as home furnishings," such as cameras, telephones, kitchen equipment, and household appliances.3 The historical dividing line between household furnishings (the former art industries) and mechanical equipment (the former artless industries) was gradually being erased, but the Good Design exhibitions were concluded with the resignation of Edgar Kaufmann, Jr., from MoMA in 1955.“Good Design" had run its course. However, in Germany, the legacy of the Bauhaus lived on. In 1953, it was resurrected by the Hochschule für Gestaltung (HfG, Institute for Form), which was founded in Ulm as the "New Bauhaus" and funded by the German government and the American High Commission to foster national economic recovery for Germany. Max Bill (1908–1994), a former Bauhaus student, was named director. The school initially focused on product design, but by 1956 it would abandon this in favor of pure theory. At the same time, Max Braun's sons, Artur and Erwin, were reorganizing the German appliance company Max had founded in 1921. They were inspired by the HfG in Ulm and initiated a corporate design program emulating the Bauhaus tradition, headed by Dieter Rams. Henry Dreyfuss published his book Designing for People in 1955, which encapsulates his personal design philosophy on the title page: “We bear in mind that the object being worked upon is going to be ridden in, looked at, talked into, activated, operated, or in some other way used by people individually or en masse." His book included recollections of his career and illustrations of his most important work. Perhaps most importantly, it introduced “Joe and Josephine," diagrammatic charts of typical anthropometric males and females, in typical standing, sitting and reaching positions, each with appropriate average body dimensions indicated as if on a mechanical drawing, as well as comparable measurements of the largest (97.5) and smallest (2.5) percentiles of people. Joe and Josephine's children were also diagrammed. Dreyfuss had accumulated most of these measurements during World War II, when he measured large cohorts of military personnel in order to design equipment and control panels. The Joe and Josephine charts would be invaluable to the profession as scientific measurement tools. The book established Dreyfuss as a leader in what was at the time called "human factors," later better known as ergonomics: the specialty area of adapting product designs to human sizes and functional physical capabilities. Henry Dreyfuss Associates would later expand and improve the Joe and Josephine measurements into numerous detailed charts that would become essential industrial design tools. The United States became involved in the Vietnam War in 1956 and would see an escalation of that war over the next 20 years, complicated by the Cold War with the Soviet Union that would last for 35 years. These events not only dominated national priorities but also contributed to vast cultural, social, political, and generational changes in the country over this period. Despite this, the industrial design profession advanced and expanded, following the examples and standards set by the machine designers of the 1930s. The Industrial Design Educators' Association (IDEA) was formed in 1957 for industrial design educators, who were denied membership in both the American Society of Industrial Designers (ASID) and the Industrial Designers Institute (IDI) because they were not active commercial design practitioners. The first president of IDEA was Joseph Carriero (1920- 1978), chair of the industrial design department at the Philadelphia Museum School of Art. In London, the International Council of Industrial Designers (ICSID) was formed, comprised of 23 professional and promotional design organizations from 17 countries. Peter Müller-Munk, of the ASID, was elected president of the new international organization, which still exists today. Over many years, ICSID would hold design seminars in many countries to promote the use of industrial design. At a 1954 talk to the local advertising club in Minneapolis, Brooks Stevens had described the mission of industrial design as "planned obsolescence," a phrase that would become almost as controversial in industrial design as "streamlining" had been. By 1956 Stevens was using the phrase frequently and defining it as “instilling in the buyer the desire to own something a little newer, a little better, a little sooner than is necessary."6Actually, to us in the 21st century, this sounds like exactly what happens with the latest and newest version of Apple's iPhone, or any new version of a popular product. Although Stevens admitted to originating the phrase, he did not, and he may not have known it, because he was a student at Cornell at the time when Elmo Calkins, the “Dean of Advertising” partner of Calkins & Holden advertising agency, in his 1932 book, What Consumer Engineering Really Is, wrote that obsoletism is another device for stimulating consumption. The consideration of style is a consideration in buying many things. Clothes go out of style and are replaced long before they wear out. That principle extends to other products—motor cars, bathrooms, radios, foods, refrigerators, furniture.? Calkins went on to say the principle of obsoletism was the solution for solving the Depression because buying things increases prosperity for business, consumers, and the country. In fact, that is precisely what happened. But in Vance Packard's 1957 book, The Hidden Persuaders, Packard (1914–1996) accused industry for “manipulating” consumers and “inducing" desire. He pointed to Stevens' "planned obsolescence," and by implication, all industrial designers, as the leading culprit in this "immoral" practice. In fact, he called Stevens "the crown prince of planned obsolescence." Packard's book reached a wide audience and was hailed by many as a noble cause, because the idea of secretly and deliberately manipulating consumers was considered underhanded in some way. Packard viewed the practice as wasteful, but producing only products that are functionally "necessary" because "old ones” were no longer usable might be morally “frugal, but would be totally unacceptable to modern consumers wanting something newer and better. Consumers have not just functional "needs," but also upward mobility "wants and aspirations." Packard, a reporter and writer, obviously knew little about business, design, or consumers, but it was a time when the public had become suspicious of industry in general, partly because anti-Vietnam War activists referred derisively to the "wartime military-industrial complex. Nevertheless, Packard's book put industrial design on the defensive, and in 1958, Dave Chapman, Walter Dorwin Teague, and Henry Dreyfuss mounted a campaign to publicly address critics. They openly spoke out against what they called "phony obsolescence" that added gimmicks and superfluous changes to products, but they agreed with "planned obsolescence" that resulted in genuine product improvements. Stevens himself argued that the item that is replaced by a new product often finds its way into a lower-priced secondary market and is purchased by someone who otherwise would not have been able to afford a new one. He saw it as essentially recycling of material goods that benefited the lower class at the same time as it produced a healthy economy. The controversy faded in a year or so, but it was an early warning that social issues were becoming more relevant to the public than technological or business issues. The generation of Depression survivors was aging. Many of them had since 1930 been without a decent standard of living and had struggled for five years after 1945 to acquire the material wealth and comfort of the middle class. Now their children, the so-called baby boomers, blessed since birth with all the comforts and security their parents provided, had the luxury of concerning themselves with social, political, and moral issues such as civil rights, clean air, clean water, and environmental concerns. These would soon become mainstream preoccupations with the boomer generation, along with the nuclear age. The first nuclear power plant, in Shippingport, Pennsylvania, went into service in 1957, and in 1958, the first nuclear-powered merchant vessel was launched. In 1958, Walter Dorwin Teague Associates designed the U.S. Atoms for Peace exhibit for the Atomic Energy Commission, held in the Palais des Nations in Geneva, Switzerland, and Brooks Stevens Design Associates designed a futuristic Oscar Meyer Wienermobile, about which he declared, “There's nothing more aerodynamic than a wiener." Machine Designer giant Norman Bel Geddes passed away in 1958 at age 65. Since his office closed in 1947, he had mostly returned to his first love, the theater, by designing stages and theaters, including a television stage for NBC. This shift in priority may have been his tribute to his daughter, who had become a famous stage and screen actress. His autobiography, Miracle in the Evening, which he wrote starting in 1957, would be edited by William Kelly and published posthumously in 1960. It is a wonderfully written story of his early life but is primarily about his personal experiences, with very little about his professional industrial design career, other than a listing of his designs and clients at the end of the book.8 By the time of Bel Geddes's death, his daughter, Barbara Bel Geddes (1922–2005), had become a famous actress in films such as I Remember Mama (1948), for which she won an Academy Award for the Best Supporting Actress, and Broadway productions such as The Moon Is Blue (1951–1953). Before his death, Bel Geddes had the satisfaction of seeing the Federal Interstate Highway System under construction, initiated by President Eisenhower in 1956. It was the full-scale embodiment of Bel Geddes's 1939 miniature Futurama concepts, and at \$25 billion over ten years, it was the largest public works project in American history throughout that time. The archives of Bel Geddes's theater and industrial design papers reside at the Harry Ransom Center at the University of Texas in Austin. The automotive industry was being threatened by foreign competition. From 1955 to 1959, as imports of Volkswagens, Karmann Ghias, Porsches and Mercedes from Germany; Toyotas and Nissans from Japan; Morris Mini Minors, Triumphs and MGs from England; and Saabs from Sweden increased from 1 percent to 10 percent of the U.S. market. Traditional Detroit brand names such as DeSoto disappeared, or merged to form Studebaker-Packard (1954) or American Motors (1954, Hudson/Nash). But Detroit continued with the same styling approach it had followed the previous ten years. In 1957, the first vice president of styling at Chrysler, Virgil Exner, had introduced a new Forward Look line of cars with tail fins even higher than General Motors. There was one exception to this pattern. In 1956, under George Walker, Ford staff, including Gordon Buehrig, chief body designer, had sought to create the elegance of its classic 1940 Lincoln Continental with the new Lincoln Continental Mark II. It had no excessive tail fins, and in fact it seemed to be part of the reason Ford outsold GM for the first time in history in 1957. In 1961, the design would win a national design award from the Industrial Designers Institute. On the other hand, in late 1957, Ford introduced the least successful car design since the 1934 Chrysler Airflow, this time the 1958 Edsel, a new brand name honoring Henry Ford's son, designed by vice president of styling George Walker and his staff after extensive market research. The vertical air intake in the Edsel's front nose, resembling an open-mouth human scream, seemed to be the offending feature, as the public had become more accustomed to horizontal grills and air intakes. The market failure would cost Ford \$350 million and force cancellation of the Edsel brand in 1959. By this time, according to a February 1959 Fortune magazine survey, there were 300 total industrial design organizations, and 180 total members of IDI and ASID combined (a significant decline in membership). One hundred twenty corporations had internal design departments, compared to 180 independent consulting offices. The obvious result was an increase in competition in the market and the lowering of the exorbitant fees of the big-name designers. The day when a Raymond Loewy could get \$50,000 for designing a cigarette package was gone forever. Retainer fees now ranged from \$500 to \$5,000 per month, depending on a firm's reputation. Industrial designers were becoming part of the popular culture. The 1954 film Executive Suite starred William Holden as vice president of design and development Don Walling, who wins the presidency by vowing to replace the shoddy furniture produced by the company with quality products. In the 1959 film North by Northwest, Cary Grant meets a pretty girl, Eve Kendall, played by Eva Marie Saint, who is posing as an industrial designer. AT&T introduced its Princess phone in 1959, designed by Henry Dreyfuss Associates (HDA), with a petite size for teenage girls and an illuminated dial to facilitate bedroom usage. AT&T was the iPhone of the 1950s. HDA also designed the NASA/AT&T Bell Labs Mercury control room for the fledgling space program, interiors for the Lockheed Constellation aircraft, and a Strobonar photoflash for Honeywell. That year, HDA published Measure of Man, a book featuring Joe and Josephine that expanded the applications of ergonomic data to many more design situations and circumstances. Now, the increasingly chilly Cold War with the Soviet Union spawned a new, political market for industrial design. Some would call it propaganda.
