ha s6o Harald Nielsen, Mode/ no. 600B silver teapot for Georg Jensen, 1930 Harald Nielsen, Model no. 15 Pyramid silver cutlery for Georg Jensen, 1926 ![](images/35edeb8024c7344ae18fa3111b1a04c19db05747561d89aa6717fae0fc2efb82.jpg) # harald nielsen anis 10177) The Danish metalware designer Harald Nielsen was the brother-in-law of Georg Jensen and one of his closest colleagues. He began an apprenticeship at the Georg Jensen silversmithy in 1909 and initially worked as a chaser, later gaining the responsibility of translating Georg Jensen and Johan Rohde's rough sketches into highly finished objects. During the inter-war period, Nielsen worked as a silverware designer, producing numerous pieces of hollowware as well as a few flatware patterns for the Georg Jensen firm. Preferring stylized ornament to naturalistic decoration, Nieisen's Art Deco designs were frequently based on guasi-classical forms, yet he imbued them with a sense of modernity. His silver Pyramid cutlery of 1927 demonstrated a remarkable formal simplicity and like the work of Johan Rohde, reflected the silversmithy's shift towards more modern forms. Reputedly, upon seeing the Pyramid flatware for the first time Jensen remarked, "It's neat but it won't sell”, but it nonetheless went on to become one of the firm's best selling designs. Working alongside Jensen and Rohde, Nielsen made a significant contribution to the development of the Georg Jensen style during the 1920s and 1930s with his smooth-surfaced yet softened geometric forms. After the Second World War, Nielsen became the firm's artistic director and as such, was responsible for the introduction of sculptural organic wares created by a younger generation of highly talented designers. During his career, Nielsen executed numerous designs for jewellery and tableware bowls, trays, dishes, candlesticks, coffee pots and tea services – that epitomized the bold forms and sculptural fluidity of the Danish Art Deco style. ![](images/2b04f5adb459035c681c8b4f95bc5a90e5ab6f6b196b8a604ae9bd31e1d3973a.jpg)
