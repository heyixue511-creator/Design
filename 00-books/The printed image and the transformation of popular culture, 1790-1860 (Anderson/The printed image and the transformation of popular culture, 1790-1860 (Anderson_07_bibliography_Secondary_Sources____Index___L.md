In the mid-nineteenth century literacy was by no means universal, and printed imagery, sometimes of a lurid or sensational kind, captured the popular imagination in a way that words alone could not. This illustrated study shows how the widening dissemination of print led to the transformation of popular cultural experience so that by 1840 an essentially modern mass culture had begun to develop. The hallmark of the new culture was its pictorial character. Focusing on four illustrated weekly magazines—the Penny Magazine, Reynolds's Miscellany, the London Fournal, and Cassell's Illustrated FamilyPaper—but examining also penny fiction and broadsides, Patricia Anderson interprets a wide variety of often neglected sources. She shows that although in general people's pictorial experience expanded, diversely enriching ordinary lives, high art remained very largely the preserve of the privileged few.

This book combines modern cultural theory and historical evidence to demonstrate how people of all kinds—especially workers and women— responded to the printed image, helping to shape the increasingly visual culture which was ultimately to lead tò the growth of twentieth-century mass media.

Patricia Anderson is a free-lance writer and historian living in Vancouver, Canada
