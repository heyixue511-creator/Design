All buildings display the narrative of their existence. The language of the building will tell the tale of its life. It will express the culture of those that constructed it, and will speak of the time of its assembly. The style will express the attitudes of those that commissioned it and the capability of the architect. The materials will divulge the ambitions of the owners, the shape will convey the needs of the inhabitants and the condition the attitude of those that cared about it. It will belong to a particular place, time and culture. These clues or cultural references describe the nature of the building; they reveal its identity.

When an architect or designer adapts the building for a new use, they consider whether to embrace these allusions or reject this evidence and intelligence. The building contains many layers of information, which can be enhanced and brought to the fore, or suppressed, hidden, ignored or just forgotten. Rodolfo Machado describes the process of remodelling as interpretation. He considers the already built to be a script or written indication about which the designer will develop an attitude and understanding. It is the information that already exists within the existing building that will provide the impetus for the design of the adaptation. And thus a palimpsest is created, one in which the past, the present and the future all interact.

The KLPAC is situated in a leafy suburb of Kuala Lumpur. The Centre, which is the only independent democratic performing and learning environment in the country, contains two theatres, a fi lm studio, rehearsal spaces and a café. It is housed in a converted railway building, which was constructed during the British colonial era in Malaysia. This facility served the needs of the Federated Malay State Railway from 1906 onwards. The single spanned space is a massive 30 metres wide and 60 metres long. It is a huge English-bonded red-brick arched depot, with a series of arched openings at ground level and circular

windows above. The language of the original building is obviously from this imperial period, in that it directly references the railway sheds of Great Britain.

Malaysia has made a successful disconnection from its imperial past and repositioned itself as a multicultural, multi-ethnic, open country. This attitude led the architects to develop an approach that actively demonstrated this disentanglement, and so the new elements of the Performing Arts Centre contrast strongly with the original building.

A huge steel and glass box has been placed off-centre over the brick shed; this breaks the symmetry of the original building and yet also reinforces the strength of both. The modern components are of the same magnitude as the host building, but the architectural style is obviously much more recent. All materials used for the adaptation were of local Malaysian origin, and they were designed so that a local workforce could construct them. The design is deliberately neutral,

## Introduction

Architects and designers must be very certain that the new function will fi t happily into an existing building if the conversion is to be both convincing and appropriate. However masterful or clever a piece of design is, if it is not suitable for the new users of the space, then it just doesn’t work. The designer needs to be very confi dent that the requirements of the new users will fi t adequately into the remodelled building, and will conscientiously make a great deal of effort to understand what these are. The client may not always fully understand the requisites themselves and sometimes request something that is very similar to their present situation, not fully appreciating the opportunities that are possible. This could be a chance to reorder the manner in which they operate as well as reorder the space. The buildings that we occupy shape us all, and we are constrained by the limitations of the spaces we inhabit, so a new building or interior is an opportunity to pose questions about organisation, relationships, expectations and requirements.

6a Architects were asked to convert a collection of disused buildings in Spitalfields, London into a contemporary arts exhibition centre. The project involved the amalgamation of two former mercers’ houses and a 1970s concrete-framed office building. The buildings had been empty for over a decade because of the difficulty of finding the right sort of organisation that could possibly fit into such an odd assortment of structures and buildings. The spaces are certainly beautiful, but very difficult from a curatorial perspective: the wall panelling and decorative plasterwork make it difficult to show paintings, the cellular plan creates security problems, and the environmental conditions are difficult. However, this was the opportunity for the

arts centre to embrace more contemporary forms of art, to abandon the absolute requirements for white walls and clean box-like spaces, and to occupy a place of character and worth and encourage the art to interact with the space.

The gallery occupies a contained block that fronts two streets with a central courtyard that separates the two distinct buildings. The north-facing building consists of two houses, which date from the 1690s. These were remodelled in the mid-18th century and the façades still retain the ornate Rococo frontage and domestic scale of its use as a silk merchant. The buildings had suffered from neglect and the consequences of a ruinous fire in the 1970s. The restoration undertaken after the blaze was sensitive although it was at this point that the two houses were knocked together on each of their five floors. The south-facing structure – situated on the charmingly named Frying Pan Alley – was a prosaic 1972 concrete-framed office building.

The approach that the architects took was to retain the charm and character of the buildings and then, rather than heavily contrast the new interventions with the old building, they created new elements that strangely looked as if they had always been there, and yet had a contemporary character. The majority of the work to the older properties was based upon the restoration of the interiors; here the architects acknowledged not only the original building, but also the reconstruction work after the fire. There was one exception to this: in the 1920s the linings of one particularly ornate room on the first floor had been somewhat fortuitously dismantled and sold to the Art Institute of Chicago. The panels were returned to the UK in the 1980s, and as part of the project, reinstalled in their original position.

This also linked the ceilings of the entire ground floor with a relatively similar height. All of the services are cleverly pushed to the edge of the plan, thus freeing up the centre for exhibition and display. The upper rooms of the gallery retain their domestic character, while the spaces within the more banal structures offer more anodyne and
