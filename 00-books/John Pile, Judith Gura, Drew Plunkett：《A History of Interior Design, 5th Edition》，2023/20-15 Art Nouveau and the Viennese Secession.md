There are also elements in the furniture, hanging lights, and carpet pattern that anticipate the second phase of his work where the square replaces the sinuous lines of Art Nouveau. John Ruskin had advised those in search of a new architectural language to look at nature for inspiration because, he argued, that was God’s work. A new decorative language did evolve in the last decade of the nineteenth century and the first of the twentieth that drew very obviously on nature and capitalized on the malleability of metals to imitate its forms. The style emerged and flourished in the decadent cities of Western Europe but regional variants arose that shared the spirit, if not the excesses. The most significant of these variants emerged in Vienna in 1897 when a group of artists and designers seceded from the Vienna Academy in protest against the institute’s refusal to show their radical work. They formed the Secessionist group and their collaborations would be important in the early searches for a style appropriate to the fledgling twentieth century. Word of both Art Nouveau and Secessionist work was spread by ever-faster modes of communication. In distant Glasgow, Charles Rennie Mackintosh and Margaret MacDonald Mackintosh and a few of their contemporaries would be inspired by evidence of the work from Brussels, Paris, and Vienna that was published in London’s Studio magazine and reassured that they were not alone in their obsessions. ## ROOTS AND CHARACTERISTICS OF ART NOUVEAU Art Nouveau designers exploited the potential of new production methods to create the languid flowing curves that were characteristic of the style and appeared across the breadth of Art Nouveau creativity, in the graphic arts, fashion, jewelry, ceramics, glassware, silverware, furniture, and interiors. They took inspiration from the most delicate natural forms, flowers, vines, shells, birds’ feathers, and insects’ wings. The movement took its name, rather prosaically, from a Paris store called L’Art Nouveau, which sold artifacts that were characteristic of the style. ## BRITAIN While France is generally regarded as the birthplace of Art Nouveau, it has been argued that the style began with a few individuals who were associated with Britain’s Arts and Crafts movement but whose designs embodied the characteristics of Art Nouveau. In 1882, Arthur Heygate Mackmurdo (1851–1942) created a chair-back carved with writhing floral forms. Sinuous lines also appeared in his metal work, textile designs, and graphic art—in his title page for his book Wren’s City Churches (1883) the suggestion of flames has been compared to the sinuous lines of Art Nouveau (15.2). William Morris and Charles Voysey used stylized, if rather static, natural forms in their wallpapers and textile designs (see Chapter 13). Christopher Dresser (1834–1903) designed metalwork, ceramics, textiles, wallpapers, glassware, and silverware that drew on his knowledge of botany (15.3). Aubrey Beardsley (1872–98) reveled in sensuous curves. ![](images/a7e0a87385c07249f3b8bff9cf215aa1dcb59e4930b1a578a638ea33bd910d17.jpg) Mackmurdo, a devoted supporter of Arts and Crafts principles, was the author of a book urging the preservation of Sir Christopher Wren’s London churches. They are reminiscent of objects produced in Germany by the Bauhaus (see Chapter 17) forty years later. ## Mackintosh Despite these precedents, Art Nouveau’s only significant British appearance was in the northern city of Glasgow where Charles Rennie Mackintosh (1868–1928) and his wife Margaret MacDonald Mackintosh (1864–1933) created their own domestic world in which stylized pink stained-glass roses and heart shapes cut into wooden panels suggest a romantic origin for their work. It is reasonable to assume that his architectural instincts were lightened by her decorative skills. They explored their complementary ideas in two homes they created for themselves with distinct Art Nouveau elements in his furniture and her decoration (15.5). He was invited to design a room for the Eighth Exhibition of the Vienna Secession in 1900 and it allowed him to create a concentrated interpretation of his own brand of Art Nouveau, in particular the delicate free-standing structure around the fireplace, the chair which is similar to those used in his tearooms, and the wall panels which are larger versions of those in the tearooms and may well be by Margaret (15.4). It was an attempt to break from eclectic historicism. France’s principal exponent was Paris-based Hector Guimard (1867–1942). He designed entrances to the Paris Métro subway system that have become monuments to the enthusiasm, even at municipal level, for the style (15.7). The malleability and strength of cast iron, which could be molded to form frames and extravagant cantilevers for glass panels, allowed him to create the extraordinary decorative structures that still sit obtrusively in the stone-built streets of the city. Guimard designed the exteriors, interiors, decorative elements, furniture, and fittings for a number of buildings that offer the most complete interpretations of the movement. The entrance to Castel Béranger (1894–9), a six-story Paris apartment house, is flanked by two stubby, vaguely classical, stone columns around which curl carved stone tendrils (15.8). Writhing lines continue in the vestibule with heavily molded terracotta tiles, the metal bars that retain them and the stenciled ceiling patterns. The interiors of apartments vary according to the taste of their owners but old photographs of Guimard in his studio in the building give a clear indication of his vision for the whole. He built his own four-story house (1909–12) in Paris on an awkward triangular corner site but the twists and turns of Art Nouveau could handle, and even exploit, such awkward geometrics. In contemporary photographs the interiors show non-rectangular rooms with furniture and decorative elements that epitomize high Art Nouveau (15.9). ![](images/02a18b0880ba4e9b8bc028f434f0c5adb62a5caa4b1c17248b2cd4c41be12668.jpg) ## 15.7 Hector Guimard, entrance to Porte Dauphine Station, Paris, c. 1900. People were enthusiastic for artifacts in the style but few could afford to build a house in it, so Art Nouveau had its exponents in other areas of design. As well as furniture, Edouard Colonna (1862–1948) and Eugène Gaillard (1862–1933) designed jewelry. René Lalique (1860– 1945) was best known for his glass but also designed lamps, jewelry, and textiles. He designed his own house in Bloemenwerf, Uccle, near Brussels in 1894–5. He was clearly influenced by British Arts and Crafts and designed his own furniture, fixtures, fittings, and utensils (15.15). He moved from Brussels to Paris and became designer to Samuel (born Siegfried) Bing (1838– 1919) owner of L’Art Nouveau, the store that gave the style its name. He established links with the British designers producing proto-Art Nouveau, then moved to Berlin in 1900 where he influenced the evolution of German Art Nouveau or Jugendstil (Young Style), which he demonstrated in his cigar store of 1899 in Berlin (15.16). In 1902 he moved to Weimar in Germany, to become artis-tic advisor to William Ernest, Grand Duke of Saxe-Weimar-Eisenach. In 1907 he set up the Grand-Ducal School of Arts and Crafts, designed its building, and became its first director. When war broke out in 1914, he was required to leave Germany and he suggested that Walter Gropius succeed him as director. Gropius merged the school with the Weimar Art Academy to form the Bauhaus, the cradle of modernism. Yet it was the Secessionist architects and designers who created the work that was to shape European architecture and design in the next century. ## Olbrich Joseph Olbrich (1867–1908) designed the Secession Building (1897) in Vienna as an exhibition hall and headquarters for the movement. Old photographs of the gallery show a low arched ceiling and walls painted with flowing lines and elongated imagery that are distinctly Art Nouveau (15.18). The tradition of decorative wall painting, often using floral imagery on the rendered exteriors of Middle European buildings transferred easily to Art Nouveau interiors. In their search for a modern style, the Secessionists frequently resorted to geometry—which was at odds with the French and Belgian penchant for flowing tendrils in Art Nouveau. The awkward junctions between diagonal and vertical members, would have been more elegantly achieved in modernist metal. The strong vernacular traditions of Scandinavia rendered it resistant to influences from the rest of Europe and only Finland, in the throes of its Romantic Nationalism movement, showed evidence of a regional expression of Art Nouveau in which Nordic themes dating to the Viking era were combined with vernacular craft traditions. The originality and decorative inclinations of Art Nouveau blended with this revivalist work to produce St. John’s Cathedral in Tampere (1902–7; 15.34) by Lars Sonck (1870–1956). The stained glass, wall painting, and ornamental plasterwork had more affinities with German Jugendstil than the Art Nouveau of France and Belgium. In Helsinki Railroad Station (1906), Eliel Saarinen (1873–1950) displayed a style that was transitional between Jugendstil and an early form of modernism. He would become influential as a promoter of early European modernist theory in the United States. ![](images/94da1c83f968b2fc047c1c31f7648ac40e9d640dcaa8c58203d51346eb29a80c.jpg) Lars Sonck, St. John’s Cathedral, Tampere, Finland, 1902–7. Art Nouveau was welcomed in Finland. Sonck was influenced by the brick churches he saw in Germany, but the flowing curves of the architectural elements, the painted ornamental details, the murals, stained glass, and the hanging lights suggest a distinct Finnish interpretation. ## SPAIN ## Gaudí Antoni Gaudí i Cornet (1852–1926), based in Barcelona, was the leading figure in the Catalan Modernisme movement, the Spanish equivalent of Art Nouveau. It exhibits the same tendency to twist and distort but it has a primitive quality that may be the result of links to, and respect for, Spanish traditions of strong bright colors and murals of broken ceramic tiles. Gaudí’s interiors, with their bulging plaster planes and woodwork studded with small irregularly shaped mirrors, are as busy as those of Guimard or Horta but lack the delicacy of line that makes their work quintessential Art Nouveau. A glance at the plan of his Casa Batlló apartment block demonstrates a persistent need to contort the plans of even the smallest rooms, distortions that are only discernible on a drawing and inconvenient for those using the rooms (15.35 and 15.36). Gaudí’s work may have lacked the delicacy of the great Belgian and French Art Nouveau interiors but it shared their rejection of the right angle. ![](images/149f7133186a7fcfd40c2df6cd437ac616f01f023dc8de6858fe16d6b656b0d4.jpg) ## 15.35 Antoni Gaudí, Casa Batlló, plan of second floor, Barcelona, Spain, 1904–6. 1 Dining room 2 Grand staircase 3 Waiting room with fireplace 4 Salon The building, which stood on a narrow city site, already existed but was reconfigured to Gaudí’s design. The dining room of Casa Batlló has tables and chairs, doors and window frames, paneling, hanging light fixtures, and undulating plaster ceilings in Gaudí’s personal form of Art Nouveau. ## UNITED STATES Art Nouveau in the United States is almost completely confined to the work of two men, Louis Comfort Tiffany (1848–1933) and Louis H. Sullivan (1856–1924). ## Tiffany Tiffany studied painting in the United States and Paris before settling in New York where, at the end of the 1890s, he became interested in the decorative arts. In 1897, he established Louis C. Tiffany & Associated Artists, offering design and workshop production of bespoke pieces and his interiors showed awareness of Arts and Crafts work. In 1885 he reorganized his business as Tiffany Glass Company, concentrating first on stained glass in churches, often with conventionalized pictorial treatment of religious subjects. In domestic and social interiors, his landscape, floral and semi-abstract themes showed similarities to French Art Nouveau (15.37 and 15.38). A window titled Four Seasons, with a panel for each season, was exhibited at the Exposition Universelle in Paris in 1900 and established his international reputation. Bing sold Tiffany’s work in his L’Art Nouveau store in Paris and he began to specialize in the production of vases, bowls, paperweights, and lamps with metal bases, all with colorful floral or abstract motifs. They rivalled the work of the French glassworkers Gallé and Lalique, both of whom had visited Europe in the 1890s when Art Nouveau was emerging as the most radical movement across all design disciplines. Whether either were strongly influenced is debatable. It is as likely that they had absorbed the spirit of their age. ![](images/79eb20689c2f39314a7d46a844cb8b4a06f854418282d19c3b12dbb793906e2a.jpg) Louis Comfort Tiffany, window, Rochroane, Irvington-on-Hudson, New York, 1905 (now in the Corning Museum of Glass, New York). Tiffany’s fame rests primarily on his work in stained glass. He developed techniques for its use in lamps with glass shades and in vases. Windows such as this in a reception room were executed in a pictorial style that related to painting of the period. ![](images/b8f0a95a57969c34719f569a5989b9cd47ff2e838ea65c54fdb52afa4c28d911.jpg) Louis Comfort Tiffany, Tiffany Residence, New York. In his work as a decorator, Tiffany used ornamental elements based on Victorian taste, Arts and Crafts principles, and his own artistic impulses. ## Sullivan The delicate traceries of Louis H. Sullivan’s door furniture for the Guaranty Building, in Buffalo, New York $( 1 8 9 4 - 5 ; 1 5 . 3 9 )$ have been compared to the best of European Art Nouveau. However, chronologically, it is unlikely that he would have been aware of events in Europe and it is more likely that he had taken John Ruskin’s advice and looked to nature for inspiration. Until the emergence of his pupil and colleague, Frank Lloyd Wright, Sullivan was the most influential of the Chicago architects who were setting examples in the design of skyscrapers and his capacity to create exquisitely detailed, usually plant-inspired decoration for cladding cast-iron columns and beams clearly offered a possible language for modern buildings. His decoration, generally executed in unglazed terracotta which allowed fine modeling and batch production, was indisputably modern but was sidelined when the new architectural establishment banished ornament from its vocabulary. ![](images/7acb0451fde3c8838ec0a4480af461b61a95d4e7f0cc641b7ca3d25f22cbf6b9.jpg) ## 15.39 Louis Sullivan, Guaranty Building, Buffalo, New York, 1894–5. The comparison made with Art Nouveau for decorative detail designed by Sullivan for many of his buildings is supported by these doorknobs. His detail was based on natural forms although his writings on architecture emphasized function in a way that pointed to modernism. American Art Nouveau directions had, in the end, no more lasting presence than they had in Europe. Critics and historians in the early twentieth century took to referring to Art Nouveau as a “style that failed,” or to dismissing it as frivolous, tasteless, and even overly decorative. Rediscovery of Art Nouveau began only after World War II, when exhibitions, publications, and fresh study brought it back into its rightful place as an important step in the development of modernism.
