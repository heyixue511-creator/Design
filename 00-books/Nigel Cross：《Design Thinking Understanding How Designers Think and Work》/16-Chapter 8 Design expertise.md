# Design expertise t the beginning of this book, in Chapter 1, I set out some of the ways that the activity of designing can be investigated, and in the intervening chapters I have reported and discussed some of my own studies and related these to the work of other researchers. In the main, my approach has been one of empirical investigation, based on reported experience or observation and analysis. In explaining and relating these various studies, I have tried to build an evidence-based understanding of how designers think and work, and the nature of expert performance in design. In this final chapter I will consider design expertise more generally, its development from novice to expert, and suggest that the developed cognitive skills of design thinking can be regarded as a particular form of intelligence. ## Experts and novices Many of the classic studies of expertise have been based on examples of game-playing (such as chess) or on comparisons of experts versus novices in solving routine problems (e.g. in puzzle solving). These studies are generally examples of solving what are called well-defined problems, whereas designers characteristically deal with ill-defined problems, where there is no definitive, complete formulation of the problem and no single or verifiably correct solution. Some of the standard results from the classic studies of expertise do not match with results from studies of expertise in creative domains such as design. For example, creative experts might reformulate the given task so that it is more problematic – that is deliberately treat it as ill-defined – which is contrary to the assumption that experts will generally solve a problem in the ‘easiest’ way or certainly with more ease than novices. In some ways, therefore, experts in creative fields treat problems as ‘harder’ problems than do novices. We saw examples of that in the discussion of design ability in Chapter 1 and in the case studies of Gordon Murray and Kenneth Grange: examples of designers going beyond the brief or ‘given’ problem. Experts will often see a given problem as more complex than do novices, partly because of their greater experience. In studies of problem-solving, novice behaviour is usually associated with a ‘depth-first’ approach, which means that the novice identifies a problem aspect and immediately begins exploring its solution in depth. In designing, this results in partial sub-solutions that may be difficult to reconcile together into a satisfactory overall solution. The strategies of experts, however, are usually regarded as exhibiting predominantly breadth-first approaches, that is starting with a broad problem exploration and developing partial sub-solutions together in parallel. In designing, this is reflected in expert designers’ considering broader contextual aspects of the design problem before moving on to aspects of more detail. Some depth-first and breadth-first aspects were observed between the behaviour of novice and experienced designers in studies by Saeema Ahmed and her colleagues. They found clear differences between the behaviours of new graduate entrants to the engineering profession and much more experienced design engineers. The novices used ‘trial and error’ techniques of generating and implementing a design modification, evaluating it, then generating another and so on through many iterations. Experienced engineers were observed to make a preliminary evaluation of their tentative decisions before implementing them and making a final evaluation. They used the foresight they had gained from experience to consider whether it seemed worthwhile to move further into the implementation stage of a design decision. However, a more complex view has gradually emerged as more studies are made of expert designer behaviour. We have already seen how designers can exhibit opportunistic behaviour in pursuing interesting conjectures in more depth before returning to a broader strategy. This was contextualized further by Linden Ball and his colleagues, who studied the strategies of both hardware and software designers and suggested that ‘experts will often tend to mix both breadth-first and depth-first solution development strategies . . . the preferred strategy of expert designers is a top-down, breadth-first one, but experts will switch to depth-first design to deal strategically with situations where their knowledge is stretched. Thus, depthfirst design is a response to factors such as problem complexity and design uncertainty.’ This suggests that, as well as spotting promising opportunities for solution generation, even expert designers will run up against unfamiliar problems and will have to make some initial in-depth exploration of partial solution concepts in order to assess their viability. ## From novice to expert We are all familiar with the concepts of the novice learner and the expert performer, and aware that something happens in the development from one to the other. A novice undergoes training and education in their chosen field and then at some later point becomes an expert. The development of expertise usually seems to pass through different phases (Figure 8.1). In all fields, the accumulation of experience is a vital part of the transformation from novice to expert. For some people, the expert level of achievement is where they remain, perhaps with some continued moderate improvement before reaching their peak and beginning their decline. A few manage to go beyond the level of their peers into a further stage of development, reaching outstanding levels of achievement and mastery. At the other end of the scale of maturity, at a young age, all of us are introduced to a variety of human activities, whether it be making music, playing football, drawing pictures or writing stories. We all improve a little, but some, as noted earlier, will begin to practice with a dedication – and probably a joy or even obsession – that sets them apart. ![](images/9020afa44a534732f656677f41b33ff999333a1d77b43373afc4c64586cd1b9f.jpg) FIGURE 8.1 Progressive stages in the development of expertise. Moving from one level of expertise to another is not necessarily a steady progression. It is not simply a matter of knowing more and learning to work more quickly or smoothly. The changes in level involve learning to work in different ways. This shift to working in a different way can develop almost unnoticed by the learner but can mean shifts in level of attention that we all experience in learning a skill, as the fundamental practices become performed less consciously and flow more easily. There has been much more of a history of studies on understanding expertise and expert performance in fields and contexts other than design, including chess, music, science and sports. From these studies, there is a general view that expertise can only be developed over time as a person matures. Usually, there comes a point when a peak of performance is reached and then a seemingly inevitable decline begins. This performance peak will be reached at different ages in different domains: for physical sports, it may be around the age of middle-twenties, whereas in cognitive activities it may not be until much later in life; in the sciences, people seem to produce their best work in their thirties, whilst in the arts it may be in their forties. Some outstanding individuals seem to defy the general pattern of development-peak-decline and continue producing great work well into later years. A general aspect that seems agreed from studies of expertise is that it requires a minimum period of practice and sustained involvement before performance reaches a recognized expert level of achievement – at least ten years from first involvement. This is a matter not simply of experience or exposure to the field of endeavour but of dedicated application. One of the key factors in the acquisition of expertise is believed to be deliberate, sustained, guided practice. The psychologist Anders Ericsson, who is an expert in the study of expertise, has suggested that ‘The attained level of performance of many types of experts, such as musicians, chess players and athletes, is closely related to their accumulated amount of deliberate practice’. Usually, a young person may display a certain aptitude or interest, and parents or teachers then encourage and guide their development. But without the dedicated application of the individual, levels of performance will remain modest. Again, according to Ericsson, Superior expert performance is primarily acquired . .  . Many thousands of hours of deliberate practice and training are necessary to reach the highest levels of performance . . . Most international masters emphasise the role of motivation, concentration, and the willingness to work hard on improving performance . . . The masters seem to consider inborn capacities and innate talent as relatively unimportant. So in all fields it seems that the key to the development of expertise is practice, practice, practice. ## Development of expertise As we have seen, many studies of expert designers suggest that from initial scoping of the given problem they move on to early solution conjectures and use these conjectures as a way of exploring and defining the problem and solution together. This solution-focused nature of designing was first noted by Bryan Lawson in comparisons he made of the problem-solving behaviours of scientists and architects. Lawson set the same problem-solving exercise for students of science and architecture and found that senior students could be distinguished as adopting predominantly either problem-focused or solution-focused strategies. The science students attempted to discover the underlying structure, or rules of the problem in order to solve it (problem-focused), whereas the architects made solution attempts and then sought to improve them (solution-focused). These differences were noticeable amongst senior students but not amongst first-years, so it appeared that the architects had learned their solution-focused strategy during their education as an effective strategy for resolving the kinds of problems that they had been set. They had learned to think like designers. Education in design has long-established practices that are assumed to help the progression from novice to expert, although in general they are not based on evaluated evidence. There is still rather limited understanding of the differences between novice and expert performance in design, and how to help students move from one to the other, but there has been some progress. For example, Henri Christiaans found, from protocol studies of industrial design students, that junior students did not seek a lot of information on the project they were given, and tended to ‘solve a simple problem’, being unaware of a lot of potential criteria, difficulties and possibilities. These are characteristics of a novice. Amongst senior students he found that they could be divided into two types. Although they all gathered more project information than junior students, for one type the activity of gathering information was too dominant, and sometimes just a substitute activity for actually moving on to design work. The more successful type, in terms of the quality of their solutions, gathered rather less information but considered it more carefully and gave the impression of consciously building up a broader view of the problem. Very similar conclusions were reached by Cynthia Atman and her colleagues, who found from protocol studies of engineering students that, amongst first-year students ‘those who spent a large proportion of their time defining the problem did not produce quality designs’. As with the industrial design students, it seemed that the novice engineering students just solved simple interpretations of the problem or became stuck in problem definition and did not progress satisfactorily into further stages of the design process. However, amongst senior students Atman did find that attention to initial ‘problem scoping’ (i.e. ‘adequately setting up the problem before analysis begins’, including gathering a larger amount and wider range of problem-related information) did result in better designs. In general, developing greater expertise means developing a broader and more complex understanding of what has to be achieved, and this seems to be the case in design: more experience leads to more sophisticated problem scoping and defining. ## Developing design thinking Cynthia Atman has led a significant programme of studies into the nature of design expertise and its development through education. She and her colleagues focused on recording and assessing the design processes of engineering students, through a series of laboratory-type protocol studies of design projects in different years of study. They also made complementary studies of the processes of experienced professional designers working on the same projects as were set for the students. They recorded the sequences of different activities that were employed by the designer throughout a design process, from problem definition to idea generation, through modelling and evaluating proposals, to communication of a final design. For each designer, the pattern of activities was portrayed in standardized timeline plots of their individual design processes. These timeline plots help to visualize and convey the process of designing. Figure 8.2 shows example design process timeline plots for students and expert designers tackling a playground design project. Each entry along the horizontal left to right timeline indicates the designer’s focus of attention across the rows of different types of activity. A significant difference that can be observed between the patterns of activities is that experienced designers, as they progress through a design project, transition back and forth across a broad range of activities throughout the whole process. There is a general overall progression from problem definition and gathering data to deciding on and communicating a final proposal, with the majority of the designer’s attention being given to modelling ideas for solutions, but their attention is also constant across all the different activities, with frequent switching between them. Atman called this a ‘cascade pattern’ that is typical of experienced designers. It shows a ‘cascade’ of the designer’s attention, generally from top-left to lower-right in the charts, with periods of consistent attention to certain activities but with a continual breadth of attention across all the various activities of a design process. So the evidence from Atman’s extensive studies in the development of expertise is that students do develop along a path towards expert behaviours, growing in their capacity to identify and integrate relevant factors, and to perform more complex design processes, and that development continues with more experience. Atman and her colleagues have fed their observations and analyses of design process behaviours back into education, to both students and tutors, and found that the timelines evidence encourages a greater awareness of design expertise and its development, and has a positive educational effect. ## Expertise in teamwork Atman and her colleagues also found the rich, cascade process pattern occurring in team design as well as in individual designers. In analysing the activity of the experienced design team working on a digital pen project that was introduced in Chapter 2 to illustrate analogical thinking, she and her colleagues generated a timeline plot covering the joint discussion of all seven team members. Courtesy of Cynthia Atman. # Experience What we have seen throughout this book is that designing is a form of skilled behaviour. It has also become clear that, as with other skills, successful behaviours are something that can be learned. The performance of a skilled practitioner, whether musician, footballer or designer, appears to flow seamlessly, adapting their performance to the circumstances without faltering; but the learning of any skill relies on controlled practice and the development of underlying technique. It is not simply a matter of possessing ‘talent’ but comes as the result of a dedicated application to and practice in a chosen field, and the accumulation of experience. Peter Lloyd and Peter Scott found from protocol studies of architects and engineering designers that, for both, their approach appeared to be related to the amount and type of their previous experience. They found that more experienced designers used more of a conjecturing, solution-focused approach, more of what they called ‘generative’ (productive or abductive) reasoning, in contrast to the deductive reasoning employed more by less-experienced designers. They concluded that, in particular, ‘It is specific experience of the problem type that enables designers to adopt a conjectural approach to designing, that of framing or perceiving design problems in terms of relevant solutions’. Clearly, part of the development of expertise lies in the accumulation of experience. Something that distinguishes experts from novices is that the experts have been exposed to a large number of examples of the problems and solutions that occur in their domain. It is a matter not just of copying from examples but of learning from them. A key competency of an expert is the mental ability to stand back from the specifics of the accumulated knowledge of precedents and form more abstract conceptualizations pertinent to their domain of expertise. Experts are believed to be able to store and access information in larger ‘chunks’ than novices and to recognize underlying principles, rather than focusing on the surface features of problems. This kind of behaviour classically occurs in chess playing, where chess masters are able to survey chess board positions and recognize patterns, and strategies for resolving them, whereas novices cannot see beyond the next one or two moves. Bryan Lawson has drawn parallels between expertise in chess and design, and suggested that design masters also recognize patterns in problem situations and draw upon knowledge of precedents that they have abstracted into solution principles, or ‘schemata’ as Lawson calls them. A typical design schema for an architect might be a way of organizing internal space, such as around an atrium, or for an industrial designer might be grouping together different functional parts of a product. Lawson also suggested that, like chess masters, design masters have repertoires of ‘gambits’, or ways of proceeding, of entering into and opening up the problem situation. A typical gambit might be like that of Kenneth Grange (Chapter 3), starting a new project by ‘trying to sort out just the functionality, just the handling of it, and by-and-large out of that comes a direction’. In order to develop skilled expertise it seems that a novice needs lots and lots of practice, guided by good teachers. The novice designer also needs exposure to good examples of expert work in the domain, and needs to learn to perceive and retain these examples in terms of their underlying schemata or organizing principles. Like learning a language, it is a matter of immersion and internalizing different levels of understanding and achievement. Just as design problems are ‘ill-defined’, so expert designers appear to be ‘ill-behaved’ problem solvers. Rather than the conventional behaviours of solvers of well-defined problems, designers are solution-focused, not problem-focused. This change of focus appears to be a feature of design expertise that develops with education and experience in designing. In particular, experience in a specific type of design domain enables designers to move quickly to formulating a problem frame and proposing a solution conjecture. Successful, experienced designers are repeatedly found to be proactive in problem framing, actively imposing a view of the problem and directing the search for solution conjectures. To cope with the uncertainty of dealing with ill-defined problems, a designer has to have the self-confidence to define, redefine, challenge and change the problem as given, in collaboration with the clients and stakeholders and in the light of solutions that emerge in the very process of designing. People who prefer the certainty of structured, well-defined problems are unlikely to appreciate the delight of being a designer. ## Design intelligence What I have attempted to show throughout this book is that design ability is a multi-faceted skill. More than that, I have tried to show that there are particular, designerly ways of thinking and working that identify designing as equivalent with other highly regarded forms of cognitive skill. In fact, it seems possible to make a reasonable claim that design ability is a form of natural intelligence, one of the kind that the psychologist Howard Gardner identified. Gardner’s view is that there is not just one form of intelligence (as conventionally identified in forms of ‘intelligence tests’), but several, relatively autonomous human intellectual competencies, including linguistic, musical, mathematical, bodily and spatial intelligences. It seems reasonable that we might add design intelligence to those. The understanding of design expertise suggests that a designer has a high level of intelligence used to plan, review, reflect on, adapt and, above all, create novel solutions. We have seen many aspects of this design intelligence in this book. For example, we have seen that good designers have a way of thinking that involves operating across different levels of detail in a project, from high-level systemic goals to low-level physical principles. Rather than solving merely ‘the problem as given’ they apply their intelligence to the wider context and suggest imaginative, apposite solutions that resolve conflicts and uncertainties. They have cognitive skills of problem framing, of gathering and structuring problem data and creating coherent patterns from the data that indicate ways of resolving the issues and suggest possible solution concepts. Design intelligence involves an intense, reflective interaction with models and representations of problems and solutions, and an ability to shift easily and rapidly between concrete representations and abstract thought, between doing and thinking. Good designers also apply constructive thinking not only in their individual work but also in collaboration in teamwork. ## The designing brain The nature of design intelligence becomes particularly and tragically highlighted in rare cases where it is impaired through neurological damage to the brain, such as through a stroke. One of these cases was reported by cognitive scientists Vinod Goel and Jordan Grafman, who studied an architect who had had a seizure, associated with a tumour in his right prefrontal cortex, a region at the front of the brain that is associated with high-level cognitive functions. Before his attack, this person had practised successfully as an architect but was unable to do so afterwards. In an experiment, Goel and Grafman compared his post-seizure design ability with that of a ‘control’ participant, another architect with similar education and design experience, on being given a relatively simple task of re-designing a laboratory space. The sequences of design sketches that the two architects produced are shown in Figures 8.6 and 8.7. Each began by making a survey drawing of the existing laboratory and its furniture. The healthy control participant then produced a coherent series of sketches, beginning with abstracted considerations of circulation and organization, then developing proposals and refining the preferred one. The neurological patient produced three separate, basic and incomplete proposals, finishing with a ‘final proposal’ that was still inadequate and incomplete. The differences in the thinking processes of the two people become clear in graphs of the amount of time each devoted to different cognitive activities, as revealed by their ‘think aloud’ comments made during the experiments. These are shown in Figure 8.8. The control participant focused initially on problem structuring, with periodical returns to that throughout the process. He then moved on to preliminary design and to refinement and detailing. His graph clearly shows a complex but controlled pattern of activities, with overlap and rapid transitions between activities. So in this unhappy case we can see exposed some of the considerable complexity that there is in normal design thinking, and evidence that the human brain has high-level cognitive functions that control or process activities that are essential aspects of design ability and that contribute to design thinking as a form of intelligence. Neurological studies of brain activity have identified specific areas of the brain as being active during design thinking. Katerina Alexiou and her colleagues conducted such a study using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) of the brains of volunteers who performed two similar tasks that were presented in different ways: one corresponding to a well-defined problem-solving task and the other corresponding to a typically ill-defined design task. The tasks were to plan a simple domestic room layout using a given set of furniture. In the problem-solving version, the task was given as: Arrange the furniture so that: the bed is in the corner of the room, with its head to the west – the wardrobe is next to the door and next to the bed – the desk is under the window. In the designing version, it was given as: Arrange the furniture so that: the room is functional – the room is comfortable – it has at least a bed, a wardrobe and a desk. The problem-solving version is constrained and a correct solution can be formulated; the designing version is open-ended and qualitative and can have many satisfactory solutions. The results indicated that designing and problem-solving each involve cognitive functions associated with distinct brain networks and that designing draws on a more extensive network of brain areas in the right prefrontal cortex. The prefrontal cortex is involved in complex cognitive behaviours including planning and decision making. ![](images/8e61677a49f5b99187629e88d886ba0fb073834eb03327a4aba2d68062a4ac04.jpg) ![](images/1194155217b2b84b6024b2a38f177a25c8a2151b9c0fca292d177721fe1e1ce7.jpg) FIGURE 8.8 The pattern of design activities as recorded in the thinkaloud comments of (a) the architect control participant and (b) the patient. The two hemispheres of the brain, right and left, have different cognitive specialisms. The neural networks specific to designing appear to lie more within the right rather than the left hemisphere of the brain. Neuroscience studies tend to confirm that the right hemisphere is more specialized in spatial and constructional tasks, in aesthetic perception and emotions. The left hemisphere is more specialized in language abilities and verbal reasoning. Damage to the left hemisphere often results in the loss of some speech functions, whereas damage to the right hemisphere, as we have seen, can result, amongst other things, in the loss of design ability. A view of design thinking as a form of intelligence does not necessarily mean that some people ‘have it’ and some people do not. Design ability is something that everyone has, to some extent, because it is embedded in our brains as a natural cognitive function. Although everyone can design, like other forms of intelligence and ability design intelligence may be possessed, or may be manifested in performance, at higher levels by some people than by others. And like other forms of intelligence and ability, design intelligence is not simply a given ‘talent’ or ‘gift’ but can be trained and developed. Otherwise, what would be the point of having design schools? ## Summary Results from studies of expertise in creative domains such as design do not always match with results from standard studies of expertise; expert designers exhibit different strategies from conventional problem-solving. The development of expertise happens over time as a person learns and matures, and usually passes through different levels of attainment. Moving from one level to another is not necessarily a steady progression: the changes in level involve internalizing different ways of working. Developing greater expertise generally means developing a broader and more complex understanding of goals, which is related to the amount and type of previous experience. Typically, experienced designers traverse across a broad range of activities throughout the whole design process. Achieving skilled patterns of design behaviour can be assisted through education and guided practice. An expert designer can be regarded as having a high level of a particular design intelligence. As with other cognitive abilities, aspects of design cognition are located in specific areas of the brain and can be impaired through neurological damage to the brain. ## Sources Full citations are given in the References.
