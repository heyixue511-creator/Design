# 5 CONTEXT

Contextualisation is central to design anthropology work. Put simply, anthropologists provide contexts, and the acts of contextualisation are meaningful operations which make things into design.

In design teams, often the role of the anthropologist is to provide information about the context. They advocate on behalf of the contextualised lives of users, mediating between designers and users. Context is also an idea which gives anthropologists credibility, legitimacy, and value when compared with other professionals. Ethnographic methods are often understood as being basically about context. This has been the case ever since the 1920s moment when Malinowski brought anthropology off the veranda of colonial houses, where interviews were traditionally conducted, and took himself to the places where peoples’ lives were actually being lived (Jarvie 1964).

An anthropologist working with design should, therefore, develop an idea of context. This is not straightforward. On the one hand, context can be considered having fixity. Through reembedding themselves, the ethnographer experiences an uncompromising new kind of reality – to eat, interact, sleep and survive – that necessitates adaptation to the context. It feels real. But on the other hand, context can also be seen as something created elsewhere, a set of para-fictions constructed by anthropologists in their offices, around the apparent truths of their observations. Context can be a knowledge operation, a device anthropologists use to think with. As a mode of analysis or meaning creation, context has little to do with places, but rather is by definition about abstraction and analysis.

The appropriate definition of context is ultimately something which you decide, according to your work process, how you see it having value, your outputs, who you collaborate with and of course your fieldwork. It only appears to be a ‘given’, it is an emergent relation between anthropologist and field site. In the previous chapter, we saw how design anthropology work can be seen as a kind of transformational conceptual work using the material outputs of ethnography. Knowledge about peoples’ lives based on observation is literally reshaped and recombined in studio environments to develop design concepts. The deliberate movement of material knowledge, between field sites and professional sites, is a key way of knowing in design projects. In this chapter, I examine the idea of ethnographic information as ‘context’, the ‘stuff’ of context and the ways of knowing which characterise contextualisations and recontextualisations. I argue that contextualisation is a particular way of working with knowledge, thought, and meaning, which is appropriate for design anthropology because contextualisation and conceptualisation are mutual, material activities. However, the tendency in academic anthropology has always been to deconstruct context, such that design anthropology projects call for a new ‘stronger’ or ‘more real’ theory of context, as can be supplied by material culture approaches.

## The spade work of design ethnography

A fundamental skill in design anthropology is building ethnographic information and then presenting it in a studio, lab or office. Leinbach (2002) presents an example of how this may work. He was employed by an automotive design company to find out how mobile homes (RVs in American English) were being driven, used and lived-in in campsites across the United States. The work involved travelling across the United States through trailer parks and campsites, talking with mobile home owners, discovering and experiencing what life was like in camper vans and how people used them. Back at the company, they presented the lives and concerns of the people they had worked with and revealed how many of the presumptions of the vehicle designers were not well-founded. The designers had worked to give their customers liberty, creating vehicles capable of long periods away from anyone else. Yet owners did not particularly use the huge fuel tanks the vans were equipped with. They did not head off for huge distances but hopped between a few sites close to one another, spending time in each one. The big fuel tanks took up space and weighed the vehicles down, but most drivers would rather use that space and weight in the vehicles for when they were stationary and camping, rather than when driving. So the lifestyle vision of the RV owner was very different from the designers’ imagination of it.

In these kinds of projects, it is very important how a corporate presentation is done, and some researchers find ways to ‘perform’ the role of anthropologist, becoming the person who has been ‘out there’, explored, discovered other sites, and returned to bring the information back. Design ethnography is embodied (Wakeford 2006). For the audience, it may seem as if the anthropologist is bringing back facts, which can be simply redeployed like scientific truths, yet most anthropologists are aware that what they are doing is evoking contexts and sites. What a designer may hear is ‘vans should have smaller fuel tanks because drivers don’t necessarily want or need big tanks’. But the way this information is conveyed, and the way it sticks in the mind of the audience, is through summoning up a lifestyle and environment unlike that of the design studio. Facts must be conveyed in such a way that they make sense and have meaning. RV drivers do not consciously ‘want’ smaller fuel tanks; it is not knowledge at the forefront of their minds, they are simply living their lives. The fuel tanks exist not as simple cause–effect designs or consumables, but as significant things in association with practices, beliefs, aspirations, places and other social phenomena. One of the anthropological points learned was that ‘mobility’ or ‘freedom’ are variable and contingent phenomena in RV culture.

Often in design anthropology, the work in the studio ‘conjures up’ other contexts, the sites of use, to help a design project discover the dimensions of its own context, in other words what it can and cannot achieve. In their work ‘writing on walls’, Nafus and Andersen (2009) undertook an ethnography of a street in Brighton, United Kingdom for Intel Labs. Back in the design offices, they created a patchworked mural of many interlinking snapshots of parts of the street. This big photo collage then came to be the backdrop for presenting the ethnography to computing engineers. Because they had manifested their site in this way, the presentation sessions more easily became discussion sessions. The collage acquired written comments and post-it notes, as different audiences engaged with it. It was rolled up and transported to different sites and audiences, where it acquired more comments and post-its. The collage became a manylayered artefact and multiple conversations. At its root, it was very clearly a representation of a ‘site’ (or context), but because of the way it grew, the post-its of the offices also by implication began to evoke the corporation as also a site and enable engineers to think about their own environments as meaningful contexts. This was possible because of the capacity of the informational artefact to be a scaffolding of associations and relationships.

These two examples illustrate the ‘stuff’ of context which design ethnographers pursue. The ‘stuff’ can represent experiences, workflows, practices, identities, relationships, perspectives, meanings, journeys, users, places, beliefs, values, rationalities, histories, localised histories, narratives, resources, and any number of other research foci. Design anthropologists create artefacts which directly represent, illustrate and organise these phenomena (Figure 5.1).

Design ethnography is the spade-work of design anthropology, and in a world where everfaster productivity of goods and services leads to success or failure, we need more design ethnographers capable of producing quantities of ethnographic stuff. The work can be shorter and more intense than traditional ethnography. Design ethnographers become used to heading off to a site, any site, be it a company, a home, a street or a community, and working to produce data (this kind of spade-work was pioneered at Dundee University by their Design Ethnography Masters programme; see also Holzblatt and Beyer 2016; Holzblatt 2004). Rather than attempting to hang out and fit in for months or years, the design anthropologist often has a short period of time to make use of. Given a day in one city street, for example, it is important to emerge not only with a personal understanding of the street, or field notes, but with data and material resources which will be good to ‘think with’ collaboratively after the fieldwork. Design ethnographers work long hours taking photos, recording conversations, videoing events, and producing texts. This amassing of instances could be very many simple observations, or it could be a few more profound explorations. The information has the feeling of possessing breadth and depth, in other words, it has some quantity and quality. It is not just observing and asking questions or remembering, but production of stuff which has its origin in the context itself, and which will maintain that association over time. At subsequent phases of the work process, there should be information present which retains its roots in an actual site. This will mean that the later studio work will, ideally, not seem exclusive to the people in the studio, but be aware of how the designers’ ideas touch other peoples’ thoughts. A successful evocation of context is ‘thought-rich’ and more meaningful stuff, an evocation of thinking actors connected by design.

## Towards a strong theory of context

In these examples, we can see that many design professions consider context to be central to design anthropology. Anthropologists are often paid to present and report first-hand research data (stuff), when everybody is complicit in the knowledge that the skill of the anthropologist or ethnographer has at least equal value. Nova (2016: 117), writing from a designer’s point of view, describes the work of design ethnographers in this way: ‘Their goal was actually not to ‘do ethnography’, nor to offer a ‘truthful account of reality’. Instead, their purpose here was to frame the design work by taking people and context into account’.

Since the 1920s, different anthropologists have discussed contextualisation as a mode of explaining culture and society. This has created a potentially tricky situation for contemporary design anthropologists, a divided viewpoint on one of their key tools. On the one hand, context often refers to meaningfulness in the world of people ‘out there’. On the other hand, it is an explanatory tool which anthropologists use to think with. These are perspectival points of view. Professionals in design workplaces might see communities as creating meaningfulness around goods and services, while the design professionals do the active thinking. Communities of people in field sites might see the situation as exactly the opposite, that industry provides goods and services but the active intentional thinking is done by the people using them. Design anthropology works across these two divides, between how the world is understood by vernacular and professional design, and between meaning and thinking. There is a sense of a design self and design other, similar to the ‘us and them’ points of view on culture and creativity which we looked at in Chapter 2 (Wagner 1981).

Through the 20th century, the broad thrust of debates about context was towards treating context as a set of knowledge operations, specifically contextualisation, decontextualisation, and recontextualisation. In design anthropology, we are now witnessing a swing back towards a need to take context more seriously as a kind of social reality, convergent with being a mode of accounting for observations. I suggest that what design anthropologists need to develop is a ‘strong’ theory of context.

Back in 1959, Beattie asked the question ‘how do social anthropologists go about explaining the different kinds of data they deal with?’ (Beattie 2006 [1959]: 150). He was working in a different era of anthropology, when anthropologists saw themselves as producers of general laws about social and cultural structures. Beattie set out four modes of explanation, which can still be identified in many pieces of research: by antecedent (historical), by the ‘interconnectedness of things’, by ends or purposes (teleological), and by general principles or laws. Most social anthropologists, he argued, use a combination of the first three modes to explain a feature of culture. Contextualisation is here about the interconnectedness of different phenomena at a particular place and particular time. Some people refer to this approach as ‘holism’, and it is epitomised by the idea of the ‘ethnographic case study’. Disparate phenomena such as religion, identity, mobile phones and water are actively observed to coincide. Contextualisation also takes into account specific histories, aspirations and motivations which attach values to things. Things have a social purpose in relation to each other.

Contextualisation renders the world as if seen through a cultural lens, in which what is strange can be comprehended, and the familiar can seem strange. This enables cross-cultural comparison. For example, Meigs (1976) studied male pregnancy in a community in Papua New Guinea. This does not mean that men have children. It means that in a context where the body is understood in terms of the flows of certain kinds of fluids and substances, and pregnancy is understood as a certain condition of the body, men who eat the wrong foods or are exposed to the wrong fluids can be pregnant. The anthropologist observed this. In addition, this particular notion of the body makes sense with regard to many aspects of life, one’s lifecycle, foods, forms of work and activity, and the sense of the surrounding environment. Studies such as this enable a questioning attitude to multiple ideas of what ‘pregnancy’ is in other places. These sorts of cultural thinking exercises are used by anthropologists to make sense of new designs and technologies in different communities and places, exploring their cultural range.

Contextual anthropology also has other implications. A contextual observer tries to explain things less by social beliefs and more by social action. Social phenomena appear as contemporaneous, and every community seems up-to-date, with diverse global human experiences considered being important at the same level (Jarvie 1964). Contextual anthropology completely dispensed with the 19th-century idea that one could explain culture through evolution. It, therefore, leaves open the question of how context can account for the idea of progress, which lies at the heart of design, if research depends on ethnography alone (and we shall return to that theme in Chapter 7).

This quintessentially anthropological work of contextualisation proved especially useful in 20th-century modernist anthropology, approximately from the 1920s to 1980s, and particularly for anthropologists in Europe and the United States (Huen 2009; Strathern 1987). Strathern (1987), however, emphasised that context should rather be seen in terms of how researchers must create texts and representations to convince readers and audiences of their arguments. She displaced contextuality with textuality. Context for her was not so much the mass of observations produced, as a tool-like mode by which the triangular relationships of informants, anthropologist, and audiences can be negotiated. The anthropologist requires their audiences and readers to buy into the notion that there are other contexts beyond their own, other realities and modes of living and understanding, and that there are many contexts in the world. Having achieved this conviction, we can begin to deal with the implications of our own limited cultural horizons.

Critical considerations of the inherent paradoxes and contradictions of the notion of context continued through the 1990s and 2000s. Contextualisation tends toward polarity, between extreme relativism and extreme individualism, where everybody sees the world from their own point of view (Dilley 1999; Scharfstein 1989). Ethnographers can select certain observations from many possibilities, in a subjective fashion, risking a Hobson’s Choice between ‘an infinity of variables and arbitrary reductionism’ (Hervey 1999: 95). Contextualisation can also involve a movement from meaningfulness to function. What at first is a form of observed data comes to be a mode of not only explanation but justification, and phenomena are represented as worthwhile, and as normal (Holy 1999). One can, therefore, only usefully deploy contexts with some kind of mode of evaluation, patterning, or prior framework of assessment. So in design anthropology, even though ethnography is open to discovering new ideas and experiences, the prior purposes of the design work come to be an important comparative reference point.

Following these anthropologists’ critiques, which acted to deconstruct context as a thing, and contextualisation as an act, in recent years, there has been a move to specify more directly what contextualisation involves. Contextualisation particularly involves three idioms (Huen 2009). It can involve accounting for observations by linking the part to the whole (mereological contextualisation), where context is the environment or surroundings. It can involve justifying the worth of something, in a more utilitarian paradigm (logical contextualisation). Lastly, it can be a question of working with perspectives or points of view (mereographical contextualisation). Many thinkers move between these three frameworks, and indeed contextualisation is a potentially endless chain:

What acts as a context from one standpoint can be subsequently contextualised by other contexts. Since there are always some contexts which are regarded as significant for some purposes, from some perspectives, there is always room for a further gain of information or knowledge.

(Huen 2009: 152)

As this kind of work develops, understanding human lives and experiences involves constantly focussing on particular elements, and in doing so continually producing backgrounded cultural frames through which one interpolates such elements, a chain of coalescence, focussing and framing. Wright (2010) argues that when anthropologists ‘contextualise’ what they see, they can appreciate the power of experiences less. Working with very aggressive ritual performances, using film ethnography, he shows that if the film explains early what is going on, the force of the ritual can be lost. Life is lived with unknowns and is lived ‘from the inside’, with very real implications for people. It is not necessarily a puzzle to be unpacked. In a similar vein, Jarvie (1987: 273) asked the question in reaction to Strathern’s idea of context as textual, ‘what has happened to truth?’.

In design anthropology, contextualisation, decontextualisation and recontextualisation constitute work processes of coalescence and focussing. This produces relational, situated knowledge. When we summon up or describe contexts, the elements are ‘compatible’ but not necessarily ‘comparable’ (Huen 2009: 159), and these elements can mutually act as contexts, or frames, for one another. In the figure of the cyborg, for example (Haraway 1991), one might consider the organic body as a context for technology, or technology as a context for the body; but as the discrete figure of a cyborg entity emerges into focus and cultural consciousness, the cyborg becomes itself the context in its association of disparate elements.

Design anthropology, much more than other areas of anthropology, needs a strong theory of context. While it is useful to reflect on the range of ways in which a social researcher can contextualise and recontextualise, a design team works to produce meaningful contexts (Appadurai 2013; see Chapter 2). Rather like Wright’s argument for making film ethnographies which are less explained, design is actually lived. People are designed for and with, not only observed for design elsewhere. There is a direct relationship between the capacity of a design ethnographer to produce research material which will be ‘good to think with’ or ‘thought rich’, and the increasing sense that we live in a designed world where there is an expectation for things to be considered or ‘well thought’ (see Chapter 1).

When a design ethnographer works to produce the ‘stuff’ of context, anthropologists are increasingly arguing that they are also conveying particular forms of meaningfulness and thought. Presenting ethnography involves selecting certain elements which make sense in relation to others, not wholly randomly, and that association also conveys acts of conscious thought appropriate to the context. An action makes sense in relation to another element not only in the mind of the researcher but also as an association in the minds of people who live and work in the fieldsite. Hence, ethnographic material which we describe as ‘rich’ manages to evoke a mutual project where informants are also contextualising and, hence, are engaging themselves not only in making meanings but in making sense of their lives. We can see more of how this works by exploring two examples, contextual computing and designing for remembering, before examining how we might develop a ‘stronger’ theory of context.

## Context and contextual computing

As we saw in Chapter 1, design anthropology exists partly because of the project of ubiquitous computing. One of the key threads in that work was contextual computing, meaning embedded information processing, so that computing designers and engineers required anthropologists who could deliver data about contexts and about peoples’ lives beyond workplaces. In 2001, Dourish argued that computing is a social issue because of the changing idea of context, and that computing does not happen outside contexts, but ‘in context’:

Context can mean many things; it might be the tasks that the system is being used to perform, the reasons for which the tasks are being carried out, the settings within which the work is being conducted, or other factors that surround the user and the system. The context, though, is as much social as technical… Computation is part of a richer fabric of relationships between people, institutions, and practices that sociology can help us explore.

(Dourish 2001: 56)

The need to reorient questions from technical ones to social ones, such that engineers and designers had to begin to ask social and cultural questions, meant a paradigm shift in perspective. Computing was increasingly conceptualised not as a set of devices and infrastructures which have sensors to inform them about contexts (context-aware computing) but as technologies which constitute the social context (contextual computing). In parallel, this also meant a reconception of the role of designers and engineers, who like the technologies had now to be considered a part of the social fabric, not working ‘on’ it:

Another critical aspect of context that is often overlooked is the interaction between the designer and the user through the system

(Dourish 2001: 56)

Context became not so much a thing as a ‘problem’ for systems design, sometimes an existing ‘thing’ (a positivist approach), but more often a mode of experience (phenomenological), or a mode of ethnographic critique. Dourish (2004) argued that context should be viewed not as a representational problem (how we design the interfaces) but as an interactional problem. One should think about the ways in which interaction occurs between the designed system, and the people using it, as a part of a process.

This fundamental shift in orientation has in recent years been highlighted by the displacement of the trope of contextual computing by the trope of artificial intelligence (AI). AI has moved from a technical idea, or problem, to a social debate about the nature of intelligence, of humanity, and society. Bryson (2019 drawing on Winston 1984) reminds us that one current in the definition of intelligence has always been awareness of being in a context:

‘Intelligence then requires:

the capacity to perceive contexts for action

the capacity to act

the capacity to associate contexts to actions

(Bryson 2019: 3)

Intelligence in this framework is not limited to humans. Synthetic intelligence, however, is evidently not a question of artefacts which think, but ‘superintelligence’ (Bryson 2019: 3) located at a systemic level. In Bryson’s view, it is possible, with forethought, to retain a sense of humanity within such new social and cultural contexts, but humanity will not be, and indeed never has been, distinguished by the quality of intelligence alone. Instances, such as this, instantiate the idea of a shift in the nature of thinking—from a situation where people and computers think ‘about’ contexts to a situation where we inhabit a thought-infused world.

The recent development of thinking around contextual computing then illustrates questions, on the one hand, of the nature of humanity and, on the other, of the nature of dwelling. Information processing which was once the prerogative of certain devices on a human scale, at a given moment, becomes ubiquitous and a property of the lived environment. This inversion, by which the human perspective shifts from the person looking ‘in’ to looking ‘out’ at surroundings, is not exclusive to IT developments but rather is common to the development of many designed things. Concept pieces created by designers routinely become normal, everyday and ubiquitous in design:

the goal is not to articulate the implications for design that follow from some understandings of the social but rather to examine and explore the implications for cultural embedding that are already inherent in design.

(Dourish and Bell 2011: 189–190)

## The framing of remembering

The audiophotography project undertaken at HP Labs (see Drazin and Frohlich 2007) illustrates the ways in which contextualisation, as a form of associative meaning, and design elide. Audiophotography referred in the 2000s to the various ways in which sound might be joined with a still image (Frohlich 2004), something which is now commonplace with social media, but was then an experimental possibility arising from new technologies. Remembering is a social activity. By this, I mean that it is actually only rarely about one individual looking at photos or listening to recordings and reminiscing about what they have experienced. More often, it means showing and sharing things with selected other people, as well as organising and filing and storing material memories. This means that remembering is a kind of constant ‘sense-making’ in life, working through relationships, what is important, values, and identities. It is not dependent on one specific medium, but changing memory technologies. Designing for remembering is, therefore, not only about improving specific technologies but also responding to a broad spectrum of messy possibilities, within which the people who are to benefit from the design are themselves constantly evaluating, comparing, reacting and acting with very particular intentions within their personal lives.

HP Labs at the time was profiting from the printing of photographs using inkjet printers, but the company was also developing a range of new technologies for imaging, sound and digital storage. These new technical possibilities were being pioneered by different engineers spread across different research groups and sites. It was a period of intense interest in remembering as a popular business, as well as openness as regards technical possibility. Still images could be viewed on different platforms, including paper, paper-like digital films, screens, PCs, televisions; the sounds of memory included contextual recorded sound, narration, and music; and the storage base might be, for example, a PC, paper, cloud storage, forms of printed memory (where the printed substrate stores the data) and various memory discs, to mention simply the commonly known examples. Underlying a lot of this was the assumption among many engineers that better memory was about better quality content in media, so a higher resolution image, for example, could help people remember better. In fact, a lot of the best mementoes are of poor quality, decaying and out of date. Many families use crumbling artefacts as foundations on which to tell rich stories to one another, but the things are like essential backdrops. Sometimes, in this project, the assumption that high-tech must mean ‘better’ was the elephant in the room.

Before I joined the project as an ethnographer, the socially engaged exploratory work had drawn on the notion of ‘cultural probes’ (Gaver et al 1999). A range of people, largely family acquaintances and contacts of HP staff, had been given disposable cameras and audio recorders and asked to produce audiophotographs. They had created a mass of highly imaginative material. A cold day by the seaside had been transformed into an exciting surfing competition by the simple addition of an improbably enthusiastic commentary. Marching bands which were very small in an image were transformed into an all-absorbing presence once their resonant ‘big sound’ was added. Those audiophotographs had demonstrated tremendous imagination, social performance, and the technical possibilities of ‘what if people had a camera with a microphone?’. They were, however, driven by the assumption that people are driven solely by aesthetic creativity and playfulness, with little sense of contextual motivation, audience, or value within peoples’ lives. We aimed to build a different, more anthropological approach to complement that earlier work.

For the ethnography, we worked with much more specific groups with a pre-existing interest in the material culture: a hobby group who made memory-albums and scrapbooks, and the longstanding local Recorded Music Society. The Recorded Music Society had monthly meetings where a speaker would present a programme of recordings. So one group collated images, the other music. After meeting people in these groups, I managed to network on to meet with some friends and relatives, people with whom they shared memories and media. The research was in three stages for each person I met. First, we simply talked about photographic and music collections in the home, focussing on the stories of the artefacts, images and tracks. That meeting might take half a day or an entire day and built a sense of how they kept memory media and the practices and motivations of remembering and sharing memories. Often, I did not have to raise the topic of remembering, it simply arose. Once I had got a sense of these media, we created audiophoto albums from their collections, usually one using music and another using their personal narration. The third stage came later at HP, where we mocked up their albums as different kinds of audiophotographic products on a range of formats. We invited informants in who knew one another to show one another their albums and discuss and evaluate what these

various formats might mean for them.

The important thing here is the contextualisation. Some design anthropologists believe that creating new things is the difficult part of the work, but the value lies in contextualisation. In this example, working to contextualise happened in many ways and was a characteristically anthropological way of proceeding. Context consisted in participation, working in sites, gathering material data, in motivations, relationships and in the connection of lab-based and home-based designing. Spending extensive time hanging out with people in their homes was the foundation and took the longest time. It built an idea of relationships and motivations. We worked hard to gather material stuff, both ethnographic research data, and also peoples’ own existing materials. These ‘found designs’ were artefacts which embodied narratives and existing purposes. There were reasons to preserve and show them, which people experienced as embodied in the artefacts: for example, particular photos had a sense of obliging people to frame, share or keep them. To put this another way, material memory artefacts are tangential in time; they have a sense of an appropriate future and a sense of social direction – think of how certain images are right to share with specific people after an event, or to store in particular places.

Context also characterised the later stages. When we user-tested technologies, we invited groups who knew one another, when standard practice for other disciplines (e.g., psychology) is that a discussion group should involve people who have never met, so they have to explain themselves more to the group. We also introduced HP Labs into the context of peoples’ memories and sharing: people had shared memories with us, and so in inviting them to the Labs, we were trying to create an exchange relation.

What did contextualising achieve? We made audiophotographs which made sense to people. An American woman who would make duplicate memory-album pages, one to keep and one for her mother in America, made her audiophoto album with her mother in mind. She narrated a ‘bedtime’ audiophoto album for her young daughter, with whom she was in the habit of sharing memories too. One wonderful but lonely gentleman, with whom I danced around the living room to his music, would reminisce about his late wife. He decided to create an audiophoto album for her, about trips they had made. In this way our project’s audiophotos, all utterly different, explored not so much different aesthetic creations, but a spectrum of very real social practices of remembering corresponding with relationships and intentions.

In giving people some licence to shape the remit of audiophotographic project within the context of their own lives, we hoped to reveal the potentialities of these technologies outside laboratories. In doing so we challenged, for better or worse, the laboratory hope that memory technologies are superior because of technical excellence. We had access to extraordinary cutting-edge technology. However, most of those aimed at excellent content of image and sound (higher resolutions, using minute amounts of memory, with excellent audio and visual replay), when the best modes of remembering in homes were about the framing and presentation of artefacts. Ageing, grainy images or scratched recordings could embody personal histories and experiences, but these technically sub-standard artefacts appeared ‘messy’ to an engineer or designer. This ‘messiness’ also applied in a social sense, since all of our informants were unique and few fitted the expectation of a stereotypical consumer of company products. The albummaking hobby group contained a lot of women with children, many of them religious, with links to evangelical churches with roots in America. The recorded music society, founded decades before, consisted of retired people who used cassette tapes and vinyl LPs, although it was during the age of the CD. Our research challenged the corporate imagination of a normative consumer (who may or may not exist) with real people with unique lives, and yet this messiness provided the social richness. Religious belief clearly sustained some peoples’ motivations to preserve family memory, and remembering was at the heart of some retired lifestyles. In addition, by networking, we built an understanding of social difference within families and relationships. So we could, for example, contrast a grandfather’s work selecting music compilations from his life’s collection of LPs, with his grandson’s mixing MP3 tracks bought off the web. We could think about the difference between a woman who avidly scrapbooked printed photographs of every family event and her husband, the computer engineer, who used digital photography for work purposes. Their contrasting motivations, practices, audiences and memory artefacts set out a tapestry. Practices which a marketing executive might have seen as separate market segments were brought into articulation as mutual parts of a social economy of memories.

When we asked people to create audiophotos, those new artefacts were important resources for cultural interpretation. The audiophoto albums were in many cases actual constitutions of the relationships. While the earlier research had aimed at fictions, a ‘what if you could?’ situation, our ethnographic project worked to actually create appropriate albums. So the audiophotos consisted in our informants’ actual negotiations of what might be appropriate for this kind of purpose, and their form and aesthetics were our informants’ acts of social thinking within their own lives, as far as we could manage. One way of thinking about relationships is to consider what people think they share materially (blood, alcohol, conversation, laundry and space) and in that sense these audiophoto albums, as well as being abstractions of a concept, constituted relationships.

One woman we worked with, Jill, had had a very difficult time giving birth to her twin sons. The pregnancy had been at risk at a late stage, and while lying in hospital trying to keep calm, she used to listen to choral music composed by John Tavener. This was music she had sung herself in her choir, and it calmed her. When she chose a picture of her young sons, she twinned it with that music, music which was not just a tune but was a physical sensation passing through her. The music had helped her sons to live. As with most of our informants, she had never previously joined images to music, so the creation of this media, in which the music provided context for the image, and vice-versa, was a physical rethinking of what that relationship of mother and child entailed sharing. She was in the habit of collating family photos for her sons, giving them their own photo album on a low shelf, and working on computer images with them too, so much of her memory work at the time was directly for her sons. In an instance such as this, it was not so much a question of asking what mothers make for children, as if motherhood is a given, but rather an exploration through design of the changing meaning of motherhood, and what mothers do, within one particular family.

The apparent ‘messiness’ of family life was, counter-intuitively, an entry point into understanding social ordering, not as an overarching ideology, but as the persistent moral evaluation of what sort of remembering would be appropriate whenever one encounters memoryrelated media. By working with people of a certain generation, and then networking to grandchildren, one does not just understand what motivates people of a certain age, one begins to understand generation and age as ordering and valuing categories in society: what it means for an older person to album memories of a younger person, or vice-versa.

Such explorations of social fissures and connections often emphasise context above content. The range of ways of framing and treating printed photographs illustrates this. We realised that people devoted a lot of time to how they treated and worked with photographs and music. In fact, they spent more time working with images and music than producing them. They arranged, framed, organised, looked at, stored, unstored, forgot, rediscovered, sent, received, displayed and hid their memories. Only occasionally would photos or music be disposed of or destroyed. A loose sense of how families organised and ‘framed’ (or contextualised) their photographs helped us gain some insights. We found people keeping big archives of loose, disorganised photos in shoe boxes and (increasingly at that time) on computers, a kind of raw ‘stuff’ of memory, a resource of individual moments in time to draw upon. We saw other photos pinned on notice boards, on fridge doors or on display areas in hallways and around front doors – ‘Rogues’ Gallery’ as one informant put it. Those would be loose, informal displays of images of friends which the family had been sent, treated very casually, dog-eared and pin-pricked, and might change every few months. Rogues’ galleries gave old friends a presence in everyday life, remembered daily. They also reminded people to occasionally send a photo back: we remember them, they remember us. Other kinds of framing included albums of experiences and events. Albums preserved and fixed memories in perpetuity and worked to preserve the physical and technical integrity of photographs. Arrangements of images in albums evoked emotions and sensations, recalling not just the event but the joy, the excitement, the pain or the shock. Pressed flowers in albums can evoke scents, and other objects such as bus tickets are textured, and make memory tactile so that the body becomes involved. Sofas and photo albums are mutual remembering technologies for some families, working in ways that laptops have never achieved. Lastly, we also observed framed photographs, in which family members and relatives were presented. As a rule, a framed photograph signifies a kinship relationship (through blood, marriage or godparenthood). The kin relation is in the frame, not the image, and indeed some kinds of family photographs demand frames. Unlike in albums or rogues gallery images, once it is framed, a photograph becomes a temporally transcendent or long-term artefact. The portrait, sometimes taken in a studio with no visible backdrop, evokes a person across their lifetime, the essence of who they are irrespective of the temporal moment in their life when the image was produced. If in an album, one can feel a sense of nostalgia and ageing, and one witnesses people getting older, this sense is not always there – strangely – in portrait photographs.

So the material contextualisation of photographs, and some music, operated a scheme of ‘good intentions’ in these families. The framings signified a moral stance, a sense of responsibility and exchange in wider relationships, an aspiration, an expectation of how the social world might change or not change. The messy social contexts were not loose and unstructured but expressed a sense of aspiration and possibility to help guide the direction of professional design. By explaining the future temporalities of material framing, we could work to bring HP Labs’ ‘designs for the future’ into elision with peoples’ own designs and aspirations, which were often not only about new possibilities, but existing binding commitments and dedicated desires to be good people.

## Contextualisation and theories of meaning

Both of these examples illustrate the mutuality of contextualisation and conceptualisation in design anthropology, and how both can be thought about as constituted in material things. Social purposes, the imagination of new ways of living, a commitment to relationships and intentionalities about the future, are manifested in how things are framed by other things.

Both examples are illustrations of a key moment when design anthropological thinking comes to the fore, which is the moment when a design concept re-enters a site. Traditional anthropology comprises in two phases of contextualisation. First, there is participatory fieldwork, during which the ethnographer experiences the cultural context as an enveloping and very real environment. During that phase, the ethnographer seeks cultural explanation and meaning from the people around them, who are also part of the context and often submits to their accounts. The second phase is when the ethnographer returns to a university or company, at which point the researcher’s accounts and the disciplinary archive of theory becomes the framework by which to account for the observations. In effect, the ethnography is contextualised as a part of anthropological thinking. In design anthropology, there is a third phase, which is when design concepts and designed things move back into field sites. As we have seen in previous chapters, that moment is often the measure of design, when one finds what ‘fits’. This third recontextualisation illustrates a form of cultural meaningfulness which epitomises design but has been relatively neglected in anthropology, meaningfulness by association. We can understand this by looking at a brief history of ‘meaning’ in anthropology.

Over a hundred years ago, Frazer (1890) suggested two twin theories of ‘magical thinking’ which have constituted pillars of anthropologists’ ideas of meaningfulness ever since. Frazer called them ‘sympathetic’ and ‘contagious’ magic. Sympathetic magic is mostly based upon principles of similarity, such that something which is ‘like’ something else can produce effects upon it from a distance. One might work to magically purify blood by working with red beet juice or use a simulacrum of a person to produce effects upon them. Contagious magic by contrast works by principles of contact. One might, then, magically work upon a person using a piece of their clothing or a hair. While these kinds of magical thinking and working overlap, Frazer observed how beneficial magic is more often sympathetic, while malicious magical work and witchcraft frequently work with the principles of contagion.

Later anthropologists realised that these principles not only applied to magic but also to meaning generally. Notions of meaning through similarity have developed into a much more complicated consideration of meaning through metaphor, indices and categorisation (especially in structuralism). Ideas about contagion and contact meanwhile have developed into considerations of context. In general, anthropology has favoured metaphorical meanings, at the expense of contextual ones. This is significant for design, which works much better with contextualisation, unlike fields such as art and marketing, which work more with metaphor.

The most singular move away from contextual meaning happened with the growth of semiotics, structuralism and structural Marxism, in which meaningfulness is epitomised by the sign. Signs, comprising a compounded entity of signified and signified (originally by Peirce [1991], although structuralism drew more on Saussure’s, slightly less rich, ideas), may take different forms. An iconic sign is perhaps the most easy, because it involves signification by resemblance. A portrait photograph may stand for a person simply because it is understood to look like them. This reading involves skill and learning, because photographs in many ways are wholly unlike persons. Alternatively, a sign can be an index, which is understood as a causal relation. A photograph has been taken by a photographer, who lined up, framed and captured the image at a particular moment. The photograph indexically suggests the photographer and indeed the moment as causes, which have resulted in the image. Lastly, a sign can be a symbol signifying other kinds of qualities or things. A rose may symbolise love. These various forms of metaphorical meaningfulness can be very powerful critical tools, because they help people to begin to perceive how many different people all seem to implicitly share understandings, in other word that there is some kind of emergent pattern (Turner 1970) or embedded structure (Levi-Strauss 1970) to culture.

The nature of metaphorical meaning is a topic which deserves a lot more attention, but in this instance, my main concern is to show why it is not always appropriate in design anthropology work. Different forms of metaphorical meaning, as a rule, operate through absence. It is often crucial that the meaningful relations stretch across distance and time, so a sign is read as indicating an invisible quality or an object or person elsewhere. An index is often read as the intentional action of somebody who was present in the past. This necessary absence is both an advantage and disadvantage. It opens up the possibility of inferring structures, qualities, identities and persons. Signs mean that a person can function in the world without having to perceive and experience everything at once, but nonetheless with certainty. I am able to move through the world, along streets, through gardens, and across countryside, and even in the absence of people, I am able to read the intentions and presence of humanity into my surroundings. The world I experience in all its diversity can become, culturally speaking, as useful as a road sign is to a driver. In order to reliably interpret this situation, as an anthropologist, I should really base my arguments about meaning on the accounts of my informants in some form. Even if for me as an ethnographer a ‘rose’ seems to suggest loving emotions, if I am a decent ethnographer, I will base that argument on my encounters and experiences with other people.

When anthropologists use metaphor, it can be very creative because the absent referents open up a wide space for imagination and inference. In many instances, the absent referent is actually a human. Humans tend to metaphorically read the world for signs of other humans (which in much of the world means members of our species, but in some traditions, such as shamanistic ones, can extend beyond one species). Some theories which depend upon metaphor make indexicality practically synonymous with humanity (Gell 1998). These indexed humans, which in design can mean figures such as the ‘user’, can, however, appear as relatively unchanging, fixed and culturally passive. Metaphorical meaningfulness in short has a tendency to create a ‘them’, more than an ‘us’. Design is more interested in the ‘us’, in the production of a subject position which is collaborative and very much present within the production of culture.

Contextual meaning happens not by building categories and signs but by the association of incomparable, but often interdependent, things which are experienced as co-present. This meaningfulness happens through three key operations – experiencing, focussing and framing. As Bourdieu (1977) observed, humans have an extraordinary capacity for experiencing. We are in one sense similar to experience-recording machines. When we do or experience something, a sense of it remains with us and we recognise, usually unconsciously, associations. Sounds, flavours, colours and shapes go together, so we can associate a kind of wallpaper with certain kinds of music, or the height of a room with a colour. We have a sense of discrimination about infinitesimal aspects of the material world.

Alongside of this capacity for recording our impressions of the material world, we also have the capacity to focus. Our attention is drawn to one element of the world, while the rest becomes background. Through our eyes, at any one time, we focus on one thing, while other elements of the world fade into the background. When we listen, we have a similar ability to separate out particular voices or tunes, relegating other sounds to background noise. It is difficult to tell whether focussing is undertaken by our sense organs or by our brains. A camera, which has no consciousness, is capable like us of focussing on one thing, while everything else in the image is out of focus, but in other instances, it is our conscious mind which undertakes the operation of focussing, by thinking consciously and intently about one element above all else. Barthes (1981) wrote of the different ways in which we focus, in his analysis of the studium and punctum of images. The studium conveys how we study a photograph for the underlying sense of genre and the photographer’s intention, but sometimes one detail can leap out at the viewer personally, like an impact which only they may see and experience, the punctum. In both ways, and in all of the human senses, human experience of the world is not a flat and even field, but very much like a fish-eye lens.

Focussing involves framing. What in effect occurs is that we are able to focus on certain elements of a moment, rendering all other elements as an associated background, which of necessity becomes an assembly of associations. When we then focus on something else, what we first focussed on becomes background. In effect, different things are forever being foregrounded and backgrounded, and at each moment, there is a sense of gathering together of elements into a frame. This is the experience of continual context formation, a framing which is also an assemblage. This is why it is so difficult to appreciate contextual meaning perhaps – contexts are always backgrounded by our own activity, a seeming by-product, and yet also crucial to how we form a lasting cultural impression of how the world should be.

Associative relationships are often not binary but collective. Whereas a metaphor has two poles, a dualistic relationship, associations often happen through assemblages of any number of things. There is the potential for scalarity in contextual meaningfulness, where one can conceptually ‘zoom into’ parts of ethnographic information, or zoom out to consider it as a whole. In design work, there is a playful element to this context-formation. The focus of design work, the thing created, implies also inevitably the formation of a context in the background, out of focus and yet of which we are aware. Contexts here may not only refer to past experience, even if that is a key reference point, they are also spaces of possibility.

A great deal of design indulges itself with constant zooming in, focussing on elements. The overall message conveyed, however, is about the context of associations. As things move to foreground and background, associations are created which convey a potentially revolutionary new way of living, a lifestyle of connected elements which together amount to significant progress. As we have seen, from early days, design has aspired to new ways of living, and in recent years, to new ways of existing and being human.

## The design effect: people as contexts

The realisation that design anthropology draws attention back to a broad but relatively neglected current of interpretation and cultural mechanics helps to show one of the ways it is a distinctive form of work. The work contrasts with marketing (which depends on signs and branding), and with the study of technology and art. The cultural connections which design-related work conjures are not binaristic X-means-Y, but evocations of frames from dissimilar, actually existing elements. Much of the literature about context in anthropology has amounted to deconstructing it as an over-essentialised concept. In doing so, we have perhaps moved away from the possibility of appreciating the relativistic, perceptual and subjective meaning-making capacities which contextualisation and recontextualisation offer for anthropologists to use in their work.

The implication is that design as a field creates a certain kind of relationality in the world, and that it also can create people as themselves contexts for design. We can see this with reference again to the legacy of the Bauhaus. In the late 1920s, Bauhaus designers were commissioned to implement some of their vision by building the Törten estate, where workers in Dessau’s factories might live. The estate’s terraced houses used modernist design principles, but in a much more cost-effective and non-luxurious fashion than the more prominent Meisterhauser which had appeared in the publicity films. They were nonetheless a part of the proffered design revolution in ways of living. Among the images on display for people taking the tour of the estate are pictures of family life during the early days in Törten. One of the sites open to design aficionados is the Haus Anton, which once belonged to the Anton family. In order to bring sites like this back to life, Bauhaus historians and curators have searched out family photographs. In one image (see Figure 5.4), a woman is stretched out sunbathing on a stark, white terrace. In the background, below, extends the long strip of garden belonging to the house, which was designed by the architects to be large enough for growing vegetables. The image suggests that the design of the estate is not only about a physical form but also about a certain kind of lifestyle. In the context of the design history archive, the physical forms are lent a sense of purpose, are used and find their significance alongside persons who live with, or through, the designs. The woman in this photograph is Helene Hedwig Anton. Design historians at the Bauhaus have worked to give her a voice, interview her and her family about their memories and experiences. Nonetheless, ever since this personal family photograph was donated, it has become a photograph of the architecture of the Törten estate, an image of how people and design interrelate. It takes effort to re-establish the identity of Helene because she is in some sense out of focus to visitors who have come to see the buildings not the people.

The initial impression is that the estate is the context in which Helene lives. Design constitutes a routinised, predictable habitat. However, in fact it is her who provides the context for the designed environment. She animates the surroundings and is the focus of the image in the same way that, in an image from a fashion catwalk, a model is the focus of an image. In most fashion shows, people are called models because their bodies provide the frames for the clothes. Likewise, in the Törten image, there are several inversions happening. The person becomes the context for the architecture, and the periphery of the image becomes the focus more than the centre. It is as if the image of Helene is performing the same function as a picture frame, except that she is located centrally. This is not to say that her image is redundant, however. Rather, her image is crucial for the constitution of the surrounding buildings as designed, she is their motivation, because her way of living justifies its conception. Without her, these would just be shapes.

The fitness of people to design presents us with a design effect, a paradigm of meaning and perception, which is unlike many other kinds of materiality. The figure of the person as context is inextricably associated with their surroundings, while at another level maintaining their sense of distinction as subjects. Designed things do not seem as discrete as many art objects or technologies, rather they seem very much a part of how somebody acts themselves at any particular moment. What one might call the fuzziness of people in designed environments consists not because they are stereotypes (design users), but because as a cultural process design renders them constantly in focus and out of focus, with designs and people as mutual contexts for one another in an unending cultural recontextualisation.

In some ways, the notion of what a person is is here being shaped by the designs. The sense of what a person does, how they live, and what their purposes and thoughts constitute is infectiously communicated by the designed material world around them. Normally, we think of meaningfulness as something which humans give to objects, through thoughts, intentionalities and modes of signification. Often, materials things comprise extensions of persons. In instances of design, such as this Bauhaus image, however, the object world is in some ways predominant, and un-like persons.

This effect, by which people can be contexts (see Huen 2009), is paradoxical in which it both privileges people but changes them. As contexts, people and their lives are privileged in that they are explanatory of design, a slightly different relation than being the intention behind design. At the same time, there is a process of designing, whereby a situation makes sense in and of itself, rather than evoking a special but absent kind of imagined humanity. The sign (signifier-signified) collapses into the context. When design works well, it cannot be easily perceived, thought about and ‘made sense of’, because it fits. Rather than making sense through the work of thinking, it is now common sense and requires no external referents.

The principle of contagious meaningfulness which governs design culture also has a second fascinating implication for how we understand people, which is that people come to appear as assemblages. Just as a focus on a person renders their surroundings as diverse associated things, a focus on designed things renders people as an apparent assemblage of associated elements, partible and socially dissectible in their many elements. The ongoing processes by which people can become contexts for objects and vice-versa is part of what drives the contagious nature of design, forever experienced as if it is a personalised framework for oneself, and one is a personalised framework for it. The associative contagion of design culture does not then transmit only through indexical means but by networks, nexuses or effects. Those are models which work by metaphor. Instead, the prime means of transmission of design might be better envisaged as an oscillation of centres and peripheries, pluralistic things framing people and vice-versa.

If in design people and things can share some of the same characteristics – notably style – how are they then distinct? As we have seen, at the heart of the materiality of design is maintaining an absolute boundary between a thinking person and an unthinking object, making a separation between the performance of tasks and the intention. This is possible because, as we began this chapter with, design work privileges contexts, and it also privileges people and their lives insofar as they also can be contexts. People are placed in an explanatory position as regards the nonhuman material world, an instantiation of and identification with the idea of the social – the deusex-machina. Persons through design become associative and, like contexts, infinitely individual as well as infinitely reproducible.

## Consequences

We began this chapter with some examples of design anthropologists who research contexts, gather material data which manifests those contexts and present contexts to design audiences. The notion of ‘context’ is a deliberately fuzzy one, not something which it is possible to define in absolute terms, but rather a concept which is a thinking tool for design anthropologists, and which is often selected and adapted according to the job in hand. Because of this, design anthropologists, in particular, need a ‘strong’ theory of context, one which does not simply deconstruct it, but which acknowledges the cultural operation of contexts and contextualisation in the world.

In the first chapter, I argued that design anthropology has become especially important in recognition of a ‘designed world’ in which it is important that different elements have been thought through, and well-thought. Some traditions in anthropology and social science have tended to place this burden of making sense upon individuals in their own environments, such as in phenomenology or through the notion of habitus. In the contemporary world, the burden of sense-making through design is distributed across many people and institutions. The work of anthropology and design are mutual parts of this exercise, contextualising, decontextualising and re-contextualising concepts for products and services. Contexts explicate concepts, and contextualisation forms a mode of meaning which works mutually with the mode of thinking which conceptualisation represents.

## Summary

This chapter has argued that the idea of context is about the heuristics of design anthropology, a way of knowing which emerges within specific projects. Traditionally, anthropology has emphasised other ways of knowing, and so design anthropology needs stronger theories of context, capable of appreciating how through design it makes a very real difference to peoples’ lives.

Context is crucial in design anthropology. The hard work of ‘providing context’ is often the reason given for anthropologists being involved in design work.

Contexts can be thought about as something actually existing ‘out there’; as a material quantity of social data; or in terms of an intellectual act of contextualisation, undertaken especially by anthropological specialists. (This echoes the varied stances of design anthropology to ethnographic knowledge, as we saw in Chapter 4.)

Understood as data, contexts can take different forms, including physical sites, material culture, motivations, relationships and responsibilities, workflows, meanings, experiences or local histories.

All of these phenomena concern the relation of the general to the particular. Context can be described not only as the operation of one particular aspect of social life (such as the specific motivation a person has for an action) as the convergence of many phenomena to create particularity.

Understood as an intellectual act, contextualisation comprises in an act of explication by means of framing. In design anthropology, collective acts of sense-making can involve

many different people across many sites.

Such acts can be compared with what Suchman (2011) calls the relocation of ethnographic knowledge, by which ethnographic representations in different places and institutions produce critical friction and self-reflection.

Context is also one of the defining characteristics of whether things are designed, according to the idea that design comprises the infrastructures which help designed things in their search for appropriate contexts.

Contextualisation and recontextualisation are ways by which design creates meaningfulness in society. Products and services are understood as being ‘for’ certain kinds of people, situations, places and moments. The processes by which, in design, there is an alternation of being the focus and being the frame can help us understand how design becomes contagious in society and creates constant new associations and related tensions.

Processes of recontextualisation in design are important in understanding how design draws attention to human users, creating people at times as the special focus of design, and at other times as its anonymous background.

Design anthropology is premised on the idea that better design emerges from human diversity (see Chapter 1). This means that appreciating other people, places and situations is seen as more productive than a design monoculture where the designer and person designed for are similar to one another. The premise is important to recognise because where there is significant cultural difference, there is also the potential for value in exchange, and the convergent potential for exploitation. The exploration of value appreciates both a sense of intrinsic identity among people who share similar orientations and priorities and also appreciates the fissures of difference where value transformations are mobilised between people who recognise value in others’ situations.

Design anthropology distinguishes itself from traditional anthropology by the particular ways it is value-infused. While of course anthropologists generally think of themselves as putting human values at the heart of their work, design anthropology confronts questions of values very directly and acts on them. It is in this sense a profoundly moral activity, involving appreciation of personal and community values while working within organisational value frameworks. Some design anthropology projects and traditions are profit-oriented, while others are communitarian or based on informing state policies. Within design, it has been argued that an orientation to helping people at a grass roots level in the work of building their own values is the fundamental project of 21st century design (Buchanan 1998). Hence identifying and supporting social values is a common definition of contemporary design work. Value is one of the elements which design anthropology work provides to designers and through which it informs them. Designers need social research to show how their designs and innovations can produce value, and what their ‘value model’ (e.g. business, state-based or third sector) might be. Social researchers, in general, meanwhile need an awareness of design anthropology to make their work relevant beyond universities but remain ethically and culturally appropriate.

However, as with context, value is not a pre-defined term but emerges as a heuristic device within a controversial space of problematisation. In most projects, the specification of value and values emerges in terms of what the design anthropologist is being asked for by somebody else, their informants, their collaborators or their clients. That is why it would be counterproductive to provide a singular definition at the outset, because it would negate the dialogical question-andanswer space of value. This chapter is really about exploring the variety of ways in which value can emerge in a design anthropology work. It starts with a deeper examination of the Irish Rural Transport work at Intel Digital Health Group, which we visited in Chapters 1 and 4. Questions about values were at the heart of that project, and we set out some of the range of moments of observation which could be interpreted as value. The chapter then looks at some anthropological theories of value and sets out some contrasting examples of the ‘stances’ a project can take to address value-infused topics. Returning to the rural transport, the chapter then looks at an example of values arising in ethnography.

## Rural transport projects in Ireland

The rural transport is a network of projects across Ireland which helps to bring people who are living in isolated areas into towns, to social facilities, and to social events. For example, a typical route might be a minibus on a Thursday, which is often the day that pensions are delivered to the post offices. These minibuses circulate around the lanes door-to-door to different houses and farms. They bring elderly residents into local towns to collect their pensions, do some shopping, perhaps visit the doctor if they need to, and meet friends. Later in the day, the minibus picks up the same group and ferries them back to their respective homes.

The core aim of Intel’s ethnographic work was clear. The research group existed to understand and develop the hugely expanding market for products which assist with independent living across the world. As the global population ages, more and more people are living in their own homes for extended periods of retirement, creating a huge future need for support for living independently, which cross-cuts public and private sectors, and a convergent market for goods and services. The project was a relatively focussed exploration of certain dimensions of this tendency, looking at the cultural particularities of how living alone, mobility and communication could articulate in the context of rural Ireland. Through understanding this, the team might build a skill base to inform the role of IT in the domain of global ageing, mobility and communication. So the business model was both medium term and long term, both conceptualisations of design spaces for products and services, and a long-term knowledge base.

The ethnographic work was deliberately designed to learn about a variety of value and business models. While rural transport schemes can be found in many countries; in Ireland, their historical diversity has been striking. It sometimes seems as if each county in Ireland has its own way of organising a project. Originally, going back to the 1990s, there were a couple of social projects which ran minibus schemes in their local area. In 1998, the government recognised the extraordinary benefit that this kind of work could give to elderly people in the countryside and provided funding to extend it nationally. In the different counties, people and organisations put in proposals for setting up and running rural transport schemes in their area, and it seemed every project was a bit different. From the late 2000s, there was some integration, and various changes of policy and name (from the 1998 RTI Rural Transport Initiative, to the RTP Rural Transport Programme, to the 2012 RTN Rural Transport Network), but a very local flavour has persisted. At the time of the research, in 2007, a review was underway of the various kinds of projects, and so the design ethnography work was happening in a climate where the balancing of monetary budgets and social benefits was very visible. A few examples will illustrate something of the diversity.

County Kerry and County Meath were projects which saw what they were doing as very much a community service. In these counties, social entrepreneurs developed events and services for local people from the 1990s, and especially with older, more isolated people in the farming communities. So the priority of the organisers was developing events such as active ageing clubs, evening classes or dances, often based in community centres and church halls. The rural transport busses then were seen by the local organisers as an infrastructure to help people get to these events. In these counties, networks of routes stretched out from centres devoted to helping people live better and more enjoyable lives. These are extensive: by 2018 in Kerry there were over 140,000 passengers and 170 weekly routes. As ethnographers, in Kerry and Meath, we encountered dynamic and charismatic local people who set out clear visions for their counties and what the rural transport could do. At the same time, the projects organised their plans very differently. Local Link Kerry owned none of its busses, using subcontractors, while Meath Flexibus owned eight of its own minibuses during our work.

In the South East of Ireland, the Kilkenny project Dial-a-Ride was established a little differently. Founded as a private company, but with many of the same socially aware objectives, Dial-a-Ride invested in booking software developed specially for the purposes of booking doorto-door routes. The IT system was capable of adapting a bus route to be more efficient according to who booked. The organisation was oriented much more towards catering for individual passengers, within a defined distance either side of a particular route. Many Kilkenny routes would bring passengers into the small towns scattered across the region, rather than community centres. It, therefore, envisaged not so much a defined community group, as a set of passengers who might have very individual aims and were deciding and planning their day for themselves.

Other counties and projects had different arrangements again. Sligo CLASP built a service from an original foundation of events at a particular community centre, as well as on a meals-onwheels service which delivered dinners to people who needed home assistance. In parallel with the busses, it developed a network of volunteer car drivers, some of whom were regular commuters into Sligo town. That roster of about 20 drivers enabled older people in the area with medical conditions to request trips individually for clinics in Sligo General hospital (although relatively isolated, many country residents had no cars, either because they only drove tractors, or were widowed). South Westmeath meanwhile had barely any infrastructure, simply one person in an office and a volunteer steering group. In that county, the scheme contracted out routes to a range of drivers with their own minibuses. Some routes ran into town centres and some to health centres. One route collected people at a village community centre and brought them into town to a shopping centre. So each route was developed with a carefully considered logic as to its benefit, but there were several other kinds of logic.

So when our team spent time in these projects, there was a wide difference across the various social and financial arrangements and intentions. There was variety of geographies, many ways of booking, different presumptions about how and why people were involved and different ways of working with money and accounting. Sometimes, the bus driver might be a permanent employee, sometimes a contractor who owned the bus and sometimes a charity volunteer. Some busses ran to community events, some ran away from them.

The amazing point about all of these projects was the clear emergence of recognisable patterns and social phenomena. Spending an initial phase of a week in each project, we three ethnographers were all profoundly impressed by the wonderful impact these projects had. Many people reported how the busses had transformed their lives for better (Drazin 2018). It was almost unequivocal. We were very much placed in a position of being the learners, not the teachers.

The way this benefit happened was also similar, a set of routines familiar across the country. In every project, we found ourselves joining in with what were basically community-events-onwheels. A coterie of familiar faces would join a bus once a week and the volume of the conversation and the atmosphere would swell as the bus travelled. These were more rides than routes. Often, certain passengers would take responsibility for knowing who was coming and would sit behind the driver and direct them. The groups of people would have a strong sense of ownership, leadership and responsibility over the routes and the busses. In some places, groups clubbed together financially to pay for extra trips at weekends on the bus. Some felt like passengers, but most felt a part of the project and would begin to volunteer and arrange things or set up parallel social events around the busses.

## Value spaces and proposals

When as design anthropologists we think about value, a project often begins with the expectation that there may be a single point of value, a USP (unique selling point). However, in practice it is more likely that you encounter a broad system of values than a single valuable quality. In this instance, there was the hope of working out which specific operating model worked best, how and why. Value systems can be conceived in several ways but are more often about transformations in values and schemas of evaluation than in exchanges of valuable things. For example, in the rural transport, we can see monetised infrastructures supporting highly moral relationships and modes of giving and sharing, and the interlinking of different value schema is more important than simple funding levels or volunteering per se. The project illustrates something of the diversity of ways in which theories of value and ways of thinking about value emerge in design anthropology, and I will illustrate this with reference to requirements, capital, rationalities, spheres of value and the idea of making value explicit.

The appreciation of a social and systemic way of thinking about value, as in the rural transport project, is different from the work of the designer who makes or creates value. A common question asked of design anthropologists is ‘what did you make?’ Although in this project we did produce a range of proposed services and platforms, a more substantial output concerned understanding a problematic systemic space of value.

A linear approach to engineering design would take the approach of exploring what a particular group may need, defining the design requirements more specifically and creating a response. That approach results in many good products and services. However, the risks are that the design responses are very often small scale, that sometimes they are simply what one might expect anyway, and that they either simply manifest what people themselves already know they need, or else do not take account of what people think is valuable. They can be of the order of, for example, deciding to work with isolated people and proposing more ways of socialising, events which they may not attend, or working with people with limited mobility and designing a bus, which they may or may not use.

A systemic approach instead tries to appreciate a complicated territory of value-creation and explore how it is people recognise value in their own lives, and how their own work and relationships may manifest value. It is often larger scale with longer term ambitions and a different relationship to the field site. Building genuine social, technical and commercial progress means recognising how the people involved in the rural transport already have not only established solutions and proposals for their difficulties but also clearly do have long-standing issues and problems worth addressing in innovative ways. In this project, we can see that everybody with whom we worked, everybody we met, is themselves engaged in creating value, and that many of them are doing it in different ways, indeed in ways which seem to clash.

The Rural Transport work then is partly about understanding the range of ways that different people involved create, think about and exchange aspects of life which they recognise as valuable. The project had to recognise that ‘value’ is not the preserve of the analysts but is a term used by many people as a shorthand for culture and social life itself. Value is a lingua franca for talking about making a living, brand values, community values, corporate values, personal values, household values and in fact just about anything important to anybody. One way of thinking about design anthropology is as the work of making explicit and conscious how important aspects of life are valuable, through creating direct links with authoritative organisations and corporations who might be able to engage with them. Making value explicit and conscious necessarily means creating the conditions for participation, contestation and for negotiation. The prime space of participation and contestation is evidently between corporate and community senses of value.

Let me set out something of the variety of the clashing ways in which value, values and acts of value manifest themselves in the context of the rural transport projects. Specifically, I want to set out approaches based on ideas of capital, on rationalities and on spheres of value.

## Value, work, and capital

‘That woman’, said a volunteer member of a rural transport county committee, as we were presenting ethnographic videos to the central organisation. ‘That woman’, he repeated, and he told the story of her jam business. She had begun to produce jams and other farm produce to sell in the local town and had used the rural transport bus to transport boxes of her produce. The business grew, one box became two and she was beginning to use up the space on the bus usually used for other passengers’ shopping bags. Eventually, they had to have a word with her, pointing out she could not use the service for free transport for her own business. After that, she arranged alternative ways to move her boxes around, but she continued to use the service on a Thursday on her own behalf just to get into town and was a key and lively member of the community of passengers. For a time, the bus had enabled her to save money and was possibly one of the elements in getting her little enterprise off the ground financially, bending the rules though it was.

Very few passengers would ever have the gall to use the rural transport service in that fashion, but it does illustrate the first, most simple, but very valid approach to value. That is, there is value in efficiency and the calculation of monetary profit. As we have seen, some of the transport projects and routes very explicitly thought of their passengers as undertaking functional, costeffective journeys with a singular purpose. For example, a route from Ballinacarrigy to Mullingar was populated mainly by people in the village without cars, who would do some shopping and then return. They did not seem to know one another and few were elderly. Some of the key observations made by our work drew on this kind of purposeful and calculating logic, for example, how much space a passenger might need for their shopping, or the practical difficulties of getting from their house into the bus and the different ways used to negotiate that short journey for fragile passengers (wheelchairs, walking frames, steps, wooden boxes and so forth). All of these examples represent the basic notion that value lies in efficiency and ‘making the most of’ space, time, labour and money with a particular aim in mind. This is often called ‘use value’. However, most of the work did not correspond to that way of thinking about value at all.

‘Is she coming?’, ‘is herself coming?’ was a common refrain on most rural transport busses. The passengers were rarely perceived as ‘going’ with the bus, rather they were seen as ‘coming’ on the bus. This illustrated a sense of community building. On most routes, a few people came on the bus who actually had nothing to do at the other end. The fact of people getting on a bus who do not have anywhere to go shows community building as a form of value building. Some people probably joined simply because they could: the bus provides a foundation for liberty. The verbal reason often given was to ‘support’ the rural transport. By joining the route, the idea is that you build up the social relationships and connections into something which may endure. One informant compared the busses to the Irish word meitheal, which describes the way that traditionally neighbours in the countryside would work together to bring in the harvest. At its simplest, meitheal could be translated simply as community teamwork, but in practice the Irish word conveys a range of different kinds of specific village practices and common understandings, framed as a local way of doing things, ‘our’ way of doing things.

This understanding of value has a clear connection with various concepts of social capital. There are various interpretations of social capital, including Putnam (2001), Coleman (1988) and (in a slightly different way) Bourdieu (2010 [1984]). All of these interpretations point out how social relationships and belonging can be thought about as repositories of value. The implications are that these relationships can be seen as a resource for getting things done and convergently can be seen as a mode of storage of value, the motivating factor, and end point of social action (Putnam might favour social capital as a means, Bourdieu more as an end). All sorts of cultural phenomena, hobbies, consumption or social gatherings, are not only about creating meaning but also about building enduring qualities such as trust. They are also seen as potentially socially generative in themselves. Concomitant to this, social capital reflects how value is not only seen to reside in objects and money, but in people, bodies, skills, tastes, practices and conversations.

These widely debated theories of social capital undoubtedly reflect what many design anthropologists observe in their work, but many designers have found it difficult to make use of them in practical ways. Most social capital work depends upon large-scale quantitative work, where an aggregation of hundreds or thousands of people can give you a sense of how a community draws on its collectivity to make a difference. For this reason, it is often more useful in the development of policies than material designs (especially Coleman’s and Putnam’s interpretations of social capital). Coleman and Putnam also emphasise its role in constituting and cohering social relationships. The more capital, in effect, the better. Bourdieu’s version is more contested, depending on social debates and divisions about taste.

When we look at the bigger canvas of how design anthropology works, we are reminded of the roots of the term ‘capital’ in Marxist analyses of how value operates in society. Design work in itself requires a degree of financial capital, because it often does not show an immediate profit, so it often necessitates a larger company, a Government body, university or charity. Most design anthropologists are by definition building connections between communities and capitalised capitalist structures.

Marx’s original critical insights into value as capital, by contrast with some arguments about social capital, emphasise the role of labour as a source of real value, and the potential role of relationships in exploiting that labour. Marx’s notion of fetishism argues that when people begin to perceive value as residing in objects and money, it becomes possible to persuade them to undertake work in exchange for goods and money, and the potential for exploitation arises. Deploying Marx’s critical insights into capitalism does not necessarily lead to the Marxist solutions to society’s problems which he argued for. Many ardent capitalists use Marx’s ideas. What his work draws attention to is the ways that capital is not only about social cohesion and trust but also about inequality and exclusion. The concept of value as capital begins to elide notions of belonging with notions of property, such that there are risks in design work of not appreciating but co-opting others’ work.

In County Sligo, one of the volunteer drivers told me a story of how she stopped in town, parked her car and headed into a shop. When she came out, somebody was sitting in the passenger seat of her car. It was one of the customers whom in the past she had volunteered to drive into the hospital. He recognised her car and wanted a lift back home. He simply thought the volunteer cars were there to ferry him and other people using the rural transport around, like a free taxi service, and did not have a notion that she would have her own life, nor that she was actually a volunteer who would drive people voluntarily.

In examples such as that, there are many ways in which the rural transport illustrates the possibility of misunderstanding of other peoples’ work, and the ways in which fissures and divisions of belonging and ownership emerge around labour. There is the serious possibility of design outputs deliberately obscuring human work, and being seen as valuable in themselves. The rural transport projects have partly grown organically, from grass roots social entrepreneurs, volunteers and community initiatives. There are literally centuries of traditions of neighbours looking after one another, including by helping with mobility. Policy initiatives (and design) have been catalysts more than originators. But it is increasingly common globally for ridesharing services to be seen as a platform, or an ‘app’, rather than being seen as comprising the people who actually do the driving and transporting, and who by doing so build relationships in the community.

There are, therefore, a range of approaches to value which emphasise notions of capital and work: including emphasising efficiency, capital acquisition and labour. All of these notions tend to locate value within how social life is structured. Given this emphasis on structure, these understandings also carry with them an appreciation of the possibility of humans being alienated, peripheral to the processes of capital and of designing.

## Value and rationalities

A board member from one of the rural transport projects, let us call him Dáithí, explained to me how he was once delivering a lamb when he took a call from the national office. He literally had his arm up a sheep and found the conversation about the transport project a little hard to follow. He wanted to make the point that the economics of the projects can look very different from different places, offices, jobs and roles. He characterised his own viewpoint as a farmer’s. A farmer, he argued, views the transport routes in a similar way to livestock, as an investment. The farmer does not necessarily go and secure funding for more animals from the bank and then go and purchase them. Rather, they secure good animals at the mart (i.e. the livestock market), and having secured the exclusive option to purchase, they can then go to the bank and explain that they have found good livestock, a good investment and then proceed with the financing. Farming involves cultivation of assets, livestock and crops which reproduce and grow, such that the trading and exchanges are managed around that potential for growth. By contrast, he made the point, businesspeople and civil servants, working with money from customers and from taxpayers, work with different models.

Now, some of what Dáithí reports sounds very similar to what we previously learned about building social capital. But the important point is that he presents a self-aware rationality about value. His own mode of accounting for value matters. We encountered a range of people like him who each had a clear vision or model of what kind of value they were about.

When we interpret value and values through the forms of rationality, we are moving away from a history of ideas going back to Marx, towards a Weberian tradition. Weber had an emergent and historical viewpoint on culture, and considered that there can be many perceptions of value, but that a key distinction can be made between intrinsic and extrinsic value. Intrinsic is where something is seen to have value in and of itself, whereas extrinsic describes how the value resides elsewhere and can be in the future. This means that different kinds of work are thought about differently, using different rationalities. For example, Sennett (2008) argues that a lot of craft work is undertaken because it is fulfilling in itself. The act of crafting objects can mean reproducing similar objects over and over, but it maintains the skill. Craft is felt in the body; it produces a feeling which itself comes to be sought after and can also create a separate mental space which can be seen as therapeutic. Its value is interpreted as intrinsic and that equates to a ‘subjective rationality’ (wertrationalitet).

Other kinds of work might employ a different rationality, emphasising ends rather than means and aiming to produce value in future, elsewhere, and within the grander scheme of things. That is ‘objective rationality’ (zweckrationalitet). When one is thinking of things objectively, one is also giving up on the idea that you yourself are the exclusive judge of whether it is valuable or not. Weber argued that objective rationality helped explain the origins of capitalism in 16th century Holland. It accounts for how it was that so many trading families would trade, and trade, and build up money in the bank, but not spend it. Through deferral and denial, these wealthy families were actually building up a sense of moral worth which would eventually be rewarded in heaven. This analysis of how early capitalist economies connected with religion seems rather optimistic in the contemporary age of 21st century banks (Graeber 2011). Nonetheless, objective rationality can be seen in the lives of many people, such as the many people living and working away from ‘home’ across the globe, who save up with a future comfortable life in mind.

Weber’s two-fold analysis tries to emphasise kinds of collective social-economic logics based on how people behave. He argued that most people, in Europe at least, will describe their thinking in terms of a contrast between making a monetary profit, or working for moral and communal ends. That is the binary division which people experience in their lives. But in fact, subjective and objective ways of thinking, which merge and cross-cut the domains of calculation and morality, are a closer model of what people do in society.

Contrasting rationalities of value creation are very important for design anthropology, because they help us to reflect on how value is fundamental to motivation. This is about the question of why people do things at all. Although in general in the world, we think that the key problem is the tension between the profiteering ways of thinking and the ones based on social beliefs, for Weber that is an artificial conflict which can never be resolved, and it is more true to talk about the differences between the means and the ends, the subjective and objective ways of thinking about work and things. In this paradigm, value is framed in temporal terms, ranging from experiencing success now, to the possibility of eternal deferment. That is very different from the exclusively acquisition- and resource-based ideas of capital. In this sense, we can try to access points of view on appropriate change and progress (as I will discuss in the following chapters). There is a definite need for design work to reflect on peoples’ own notions of the value of their own actions.

## Scales and spheres of value

In the various Irish rural transport routes, as we have seen, there is a given reason for a route and a journey. Given that it is a subsidised service, supported by taxpayers’ money, it is important that there is regulation of what is permitted and what is not, and an accountability consequent on that countability. Projects are simply not free to set up routes as they wish. Drivers have to follow a known route and cannot diverge a long way from it. Each route has a rationale corresponding with the destination: to main streets for shopping, to community centres for active ageing groups, to sports centres or medical centres for health. That rationale then forms a part of the justification for the money spent supporting the route. However, sitting behind the drivers are often bunches of exuberant retired pensioners with nothing to lose. Their joking is constant and they have seen it all. On occasion, the groups may persuade the driver to make detours if people have other things to do. So alternative destinations might include out-of-town LIDL or ALDI, where shopping is cheaper; or hospitals or health centres, likewise often on the edge of towns. When new events happen, it gives a joyful sense of liberty and sometimes subversion.

Some interpretations of value emphasise on-the-spot choices, and comparisons, as people experience the world, rather than the structures of Marx or the rationalities of Weber. In a world of ongoing experience, value here is to be found in the constant transformations between different kinds of values which people exercise, and which constantly create a sense of different spheres of value. These perspectives draw ultimately on the work of Georg Simmel (among others), developed especially by Appadurai (1986) and Kopytoff (1986). Simmel tied value closely to exchange, and pointed out how as well as acquisition, exchange involves sacrifice, which is perhaps more important in the creation of value itself. In a modern, metropolitan society, it is not so much labour and work which creates value, as that people are prepared to relinquish one thing for another. The world of consumption evoked by Simmel feels as if it is resisting our possession and use and consequently results in a problematic condition of desire. Appadurai (1986) suggests that this condition of a world charged with potential sacrifices can be interpreted or modelled as a set of spheres or hierarchies of values. If we think about the lives of material things, they are surrounded by constant choices, possibilities, uses, disposals, and other moments of human experience. The accumulation of such serendipitous moments around the ongoing life of things evokes all sorts of human values. So, for example, a spoon may be found in a shop, among hundreds of other identical spoons, and bought for money. It may become part of family life, at the centre of key family events, helping to build exclusive family relationships, but used for certain specific exchanges with specific people and building their identities. On occasion, spoons become even more valuable. There are spoons housed in glass cases in museums, a part of national heritage, only loaned out to other countries for occasional exhibitions. I am giving a casual example, but all objects are potentially like this spoon. Material things may pass through many value-based states, transformations, and spheres. The consequence is a constant evaluation of social relations against appropriate goods, services and exchanges: I may offer tea or coffee to visitors who come to discuss work, but not a meal; for friends we may sit in the garden with cake and for family it graduates to a meal. These social and moralised hierarchies of exchange do not just reflect relationships, they constitute and create them, within fine discriminations of consumed goods, services and material qualities.

In Westmeath, I was invited to eat dinner at the home of the father of some friends, and he told me about one of the houses down the lane. A year before, the man who lived there unfortunately passed away, and it was because of the rural transport bus that his death was discovered. He was a regular passenger on the bus but did not emerge at the door as usual, and so of course the driver got out and checked. This story is not uncommon, and one of my colleagues repeated it from the county where she worked. Some people may think of this story as indicating a chance event, the coincidence of the bus with a tragic death. That impression would be based on the perception of two separate cultural domains or spheres: a world of formal, professional activity where relatively anonymous bus drivers fulfil their jobs, and alongside it, the world of the village, intensely human, moralised and transcendent, within which houses and lands are exchanged, and people develop relationships through births, marriages and deaths. Busses and deaths do not seem to mix, and things and people likewise. However, these are parts of the same moralised economy, comprising human exchanges and interactions, where the lives of people and goods and services are cross-cutting, and while they are not the same, both experiences value transformations. In the countryside, much social life comprises in regularised and routinised interactions, such as the church on Sunday, the Gaelic Sports on Saturday and the pub on a Friday. Longer term engagements happen monthly, such as livestock marts, and other engagements are annual. Funerals are one of the most important gatherings, celebrating somebody’s life and attended by hundreds of people. In many parts of Ireland, the deaths in the county are announced on the radio at midday, and many people tune in to the announcements every day to keep in touch. All of these routines and rhythms comprise a lively overlapping network of news, mutual support and ways of creating active social persons. If somebody does not appear as per usual in the expected events, others make note. The rural transport busses become one of the elements of this routinised social life, the fabric of meetings, interactions and exchanges which constitute the moral economy, not necessarily a wholly separate phenomenon from gatherings such as funerals, and exchanges of houses and lands.

As the busses travel across the countryside, the importance of land constantly emerges. Many of the passengers are ‘looking after the land’. They may not be farming it directly any more, but they ensure their family farm lands are cared for, grazed, mown and so forth. On the rural transport rides, these people constantly survey the passing fields, their near-neighbours’ lands and they see and know the evidence of care, tenancy, rebuilding and property, by which the local people live their lives in parallel with parcels of land. One could not have invented a better type of technology for this type of activity than a minibus.

There is here a putative hierarchy of values, based on long-term, medium-term, and short-term exchanges, interactions, things and relationships. If interpretations of capital emphasise labour and acquisition, and rationalities emphasise behaviours and their justifications, exchange-based ideas of value emphasise experience, choices and comparison. Exchangers are often travellers. Simmel’s (2011) extensive work on the culture of money describes money as the objectification of the means of comparison of things, a kind of pure symbolic relation. Because of money, a connection and comparison can be made between incommensurable and distant things, a family holiday in St Petersburg and a sheep in Peru, or any other two phenomena. Whereas in other approaches, money is a repository of value, for Simmel it is the objectified mode of relation, which then produces value, making money one of the potentially most moralised artefacts (Singh 2013; Zelizer 2010). People are constantly making comparisons and decisions and in doing so are relinquishing other possibilities. These constant comparisons help people to build their senses and hierarchies of values.

## The diversity of value

The exploration of values is then one of the fundamental activities of contemporary design, and the work of design is often understood as supporting people in their work of creating value within a new economy (Foster 2007). One of the results of this has been the notion of valuesensitive design as an approach (Friedman and Hendry 2019), as well as the very different and utilitarian value-driven design engineering. In practice, this means identifying through research what aspects of life may be important to people, and assisting them in building those aspects, whether as physical experiences or as more abstracted conceptions of social significance. Some designers may work with broad schemas of value (Buchanan 1998, for example, talks about things being ‘good’, ‘just’, ‘useful’ or ‘pleasurable’), but other projects may identify specific ethnographic themes, such as land, family or care and redefine these as values.

What a lot of value-sensitive work does, even though it has proved beneficial for promoting anthropological research, is take values as already explicit and manifest in themselves. As the previous discussion has made clear, not only do very particular themes emerge through a process of ‘ethnographic evaluation’, but the reasons why they can be seen as valuable is linked to interpretation. So a phenomenon such as ‘land’ or ‘family’ can be seen as valuable for several different and potentially contrasting reasons. Potentially, ‘values’ is simply shorthand for ‘culture’, unless considered more carefully.

If we work with paradigms of thinking about utility, or about capital, the implication is that we consider value to largely reside in social structure and is not necessarily something of which people are directly conscious. Many things become valuable in the senses of being stores for value or resources for work. If, second, we emphasise value rationalities, we have a much more temporally linear understanding of what value is. Human action can be seen as long term or short term, and the specific way in which somebody presents the sense of what is happening is crucial to understanding value. Behaviour is judged against imagined futures and pasts. If, third, we shift the emphasis towards exchange, there is more of a sense of value as it resides in comparativity. Rather than a linear sense of future-oriented action, exchange tends to emphasise that people are

constantly acting with a sense of value residing in how one thing may be related to another.   
Experience, choice and singular events come to the fore.

So what can we say about the value of a theory of value? Value theory lends the appearance of unity to disparate inquiries; and it adds significance at the expense of specificity. There are many value theories, so you have to pick and choose. Their value is relative to your question. So, rather than oppose Marx to Marshall or to anyone else who you think has a rival value theory, perhaps the best strategy… is to find out what each of these theories is particularly good for. This is likely to be less than their protagonists claim for them, but a selection could still be useful for your project.

(Hart 2011: 13)

In design ethnography, it is important to be open to certain key themes, including work, property, exchange and the accounts people give of what is valuable. The questions and interpretations which may emerge include the rationalisations of work, inequalities, spheres of exchange and mutual transformations between monetised and moralised domains.

## Stances on values

So far, in this chapter, we have established two key points about values and diversity. First, there are many different kinds of observed moments and phenomena in ethnographic work, which can all be interpreted in terms of value and values. Second, there are different ways to produce an interpretive framework about value. We can now look at other examples of design work within this territory of culture and value: MIT’s ‘one laptop per child’ project, the story of the practical relationship between design and crafts and the Set Fashion Free project.

By definition, designed things articulate different sites and contexts, and very global and very local economies. As a field and set of practices, design has the intrinsic capacity to facilitate transformations from the anonymous generalised sphere of industrially produced services and goods, through the deeply personal and humanised worlds of people and families. To mention ‘design’ is, therefore, to explicitly locate a moral economy measured in money, and to bring into articulation spheres of value. These designerly acts of ‘making value conscious’ generally occur across unequal and different partners. On the one hand, we have the worlds of informants who are less powerful but often in a situation of some freedom of expression, within moral constraints, and on the other the powerful world of corporations and organisations, within which people work in varying degrees of unfreedom to fulfil design ends. The inequality is often reflected in the kinds of imagination of values on either side. Sometimes, informants welcome corporate representatives into their own homes and lives in the belief that they are introducing them to new value systems; at other times, there may be the belief that everybody shares global, human understandings of value and exchange. Various mutual understandings and misunderstandings are possible.

## The \$100 laptop

In 2005, the MIT MediaLab promoted widely its support for ‘one laptop per child’, to provide every child in developing countries with a computer. This would be possible to do by driving down the cost of manufacture, using new and cheaper technologies. At an early stage, then, the project was promoted as the “\$100 laptop” project, a conception of design work as not creating the most high-performance laptop but as innovation through creating an accessible, rugged and usable device (see Papanek 1971). The project could draw on different narratives at different moments and for different audiences. To design audiences and scientific audiences, mainly in the United States, the work was promoted as innovation. In Thailand, Egypt, Brazil and other countries, where the laptop was to be mainly used, the project was also promoted as a social and development initiative (although of course child poverty is also relatively widespread across the world, including the United States).

The project designed, built, mass-manufactured and distributed a laptop depending on early flash memory, capable of networking with similar devices or with internet resources. The work is still running 14 years later and has had a number of successes, but it has not resulted in what one might call a contagious spread across the world of this kind of laptop. From an early stage, it was criticised from a number of angles, and especially the ‘\$100’ strapline. Why, some people asked, do we get simple laptops worth only \$100, and not the same computers which most Americans have? Far from providing ‘value’, some interpreted the devices as creating a two-tier system of value in computing, with the implication that people across the world also live in a two-tier global society. In a similar vein, some argued against the notion of providing computing through small commoditised devices. The real deficiency, some said, in the countries was a lack of public infrastructure, such that an informational infrastructure was argued for. From a different angle, others criticised the simple notion of providing laptops and computers to children who might need more basic commodities, especially clean water, food, solid homes and school buildings.

Hence, the one laptop per child project subscribed at an early stage to a particular notion of values, incorporating an idea of local values (the possession of one affordable but effective possession, individually), alongside a global systemic understanding (in which Egypt, Brazil and Thailand occupy comparable spaces) and a conception of value in the United States, as a form of creative innovation and intellectual property. Value, not technical capacities, was where it was critiqued and had to defend itself. Its funding was radically cut in 2008, but the company continued to make its laptops. By 2013, it was producing relatively cheap tablet computers for children which were sold across the United States, and its products were no longer presented as socially minded, but rather a part of the low-cost end of global computing products generally. By that stage, smartphones and mobile IT infrastructures had spread quickly and astoundingly across large parts of Africa, Asia and South America. The computing power of smartphones of different kinds were giving families access to information on the web, and enabling all sorts of useful work in business, banking, health and social work. Through smartphones, access to computing has famously outstripped access to clean water in countries such as India. The informational revolution then was proceeding less through education, but other areas of life. The One Laptop Per Child machines occupied a small part of a media and computing landscape globally.

The one laptop per child project (the name itself betrays its quantitative rationale), therefore, contained many exciting, progressive and socially oriented elements. Yet, it was initially mistargetted. One reason was simply the singularity of its perspective, and the perception of its origination as a technically driven project at MIT. Change was not perceived as originating within Thailand, Egypt and Brazil. While socially aware, the project was not socially led and did not begin with research into peoples’ lives and priorities. It presented a combination of convergent understandings of value, in a fashion which gives the impression that they all can work harmonically together, when in fact there is a clash of understandings and perspectives.

## Revaluing crafts

If the \$100 laptop is an example of a technically led project, socially but not contextually informed, a very different kind of example is the relationship between design and crafts. This is evidently a very wide-ranging and long-standing field of inquiry, which I can only summarise here. Yet it is one of the most significant fields in which design anthropologists work, and a lot of their work has been about acts of mutual re-evaluation. Unlike the \$100 laptop, which initially took the approach of ‘designing for’ communities, most of the engagements between crafts and design see themselves as trying to work with craft, learn from craft, and maximise the potential of craft practitioners and their products.

Not only has the idea of craft and handmade objects developed in parallel with design, but an interest in local traditional production has been a key part of the history of anthropological work in design. One of the landmark moments in advocating a more politically aware design, led by local contexts and values, was the Ahmedabad Declaration on Industrial Design for Development (Balaram 2009), following the 1979 Design for Development Congress. That ground-breaking conference (Clarke 2016) showcased, advanced and debated some of the design politics and principles championed by India’s National Institute of Design. Founded in postindependence India, the Institute’s wider social and economic role was especially informed by the India report by the American designers Charles and Ray Eames, while its educational model was inspired by Bauhaus ideas. The India Report had called for ‘a sober investigation into those values and qualities that Indians hold important to a good life’ (Balaram 2009: 54), seeing design praxis as not oriented to a market, so much as interwoven into life in India. One of the primary roles of design in India was seen as supporting cottage industry. So rather than design products for manufacture in factories, many designers in India work alongside, and learn from, communities of people with a tradition of working with particular materials or goods and design goods appropriately to support these communities. Hence, if we look at the engagement of design and crafts in India (and there are parallel narratives from other countries), we can see the importance of an orientation towards craft communities in shifting the centre of gravity of global design away from Europe and the United States, and in doing so shifting it towards a valuesensitised practice.

Craft cannot be defined with absolute precision. Although it is presented and encountered in life with unambiguous clarity and with the force of authenticity, on reflection, it frequently intersects with design. It has been pointed out that some define crafts as a set of particular kinds of handmade objects, work processes and identities which came to be recognised as distinctive in Europe in the wake of mechanisation during the industrial revolution (Greenhalgh 1997). Others emphasise that craft is better seen as a process, not certain kinds of goods (Adamson 2018). Anthropologists have argued that craft can be understood as a particular, embodied way of knowing (Marchand 2011, 2016), and as politically engaged social critique (Wilkinson-Weber and Ory DeNicola 2016). Herzfeld (2003) deploys the ‘spheres of value’ idea, to position it as a particular sphere within global hierarchies of exchange. Some people distinguish craft from design, while others argue they are both examples of unfolding creative traditions, and that many examples of what people call crafts are really forms of design appropriate to particular cultural contexts.

While interpreted differently, craft traditions do seem to have some common qualities, their convergent strengths and weaknesses. Craft practices and traditions can be silencing. Many skilled makers do not communicate modes of making in words, but do it through experience and embodied knowledge. When crafted goods are sold, often the maker is anonymous, with a persistent idea of the ‘unknown craftsman’ (Martin 2016; Yanagi and Leach 2013). Craft activities are characterised by work which is often routinised and repetitive but also careful and precise and capable of producing novelty and excellence from routinised and familiar ways of working. As objects, crafted goods are often expected to have a cultural self-sufficiency, to be intrinsically beautiful and functional, in themselves. They do not need to evoke in the mind of the user their maker, or a particular role, they can be appreciated and valued in themselves.

One of the pre-eminent issues which design anthropology is often called upon to answer, then, is how to support, invigorate, revive and enliven crafts within communities. Design anthropologists all across the world are working within moments of encounter between different creative traditions in which one side emerges as more like design and the other as more like crafts. The core approach here is how to create value in craft work using design. This means finding ways to design craft products, support ways of working by hand, support their marketing and sale, create new institutional arrangements for craftspeople and facilitate new encounters and connections across the globe.

There is a wide range of ways by which anthropologists have interpolated into this relationship between crafts and design, depending on the particular conceptualisation of problems which they wish to address. Wilkinson-Weber and Ory DeNicola (2016) focus on the specific issue of explicitness. Crafts, they argue, constitute fundamental human processes and actions through which people are able to ‘tell themselves’ (Wilkinson-Weber and Ory DeNicola, 2016: 1), that is negotiate and establish the relation of people, places and time. This is not, however, a fluid and frictionless activity, and both anthropologists and designers can come to the fore in this work of ‘telling’. It is not useful for them to define craft as a phenomenon in itself, rather it is a ‘claim’ within a contested territory of like claims, which advances cultural work on behalf of certain people and places. Many others would agree with this notion of telling, but there are very different positions on the nature of the political landscape it happens within.

As we have seen, many designers undertake participant observation work with craft communities, in order to develop new approaches. For example, Boradkar (forthcoming) works with the copper-making Tambat community in central India, and with a local design school whose students and staff develop new products. The Tambat community traditionally live close to one another in certain districts, with the copper workshop below the family’s living space, and the businesses often pass from one generation to another. This is, typically for many parts of South Asia, not only a job but an identity and way of life. Yet the kinds of artefact which the Tambat is called upon to make changes regularly and some of the common household and kitchen artefacts in the local area which used to be copper are no longer called for. At the same time, however, there has been a call globally for the kind of aesthetic embodied in coppertone artefacts. The rosy warmth of the dimpled reflective surface of copper bowls and cups fits a certain kind of artisan-inspired interior design aesthetic. In this environment, the design school can play a clear role in imagining designs for new kinds of copper vessel and promoting them. The relationship, however, is not one way. Design also benefits. Ory DeNicola and DeNicola (2012) characterise the mutual connection as ‘rescue and redemption’. While designers in India can complement themselves on rescuing craft communities, craft can think of itself as redeeming design. Craft provides a potentially alienating, anti-local and unrooted professional practice with authenticity, and connection to place, to history, and to the ongoing lineages of creative practitioners. Balaram (2011, Chapter 9) advocates further, beyond designers doing research in rural areas, to training people from local communities with design skills (the ‘barefoot designer’) and make them design producers and educators.

Other anthropologists characterise crafts as not so much collaborative but potentially subversive and very much human-focussed. McKay (McKay and Perez 2017; McKay et al 2015)

has worked for some years with Igorot communities, both in the Philippines and in the United Kingdom, and has focussed especially on artefacts made from plastic. These include woven baskets, in forms which used to be made with rattan leaves, and plastic jewellery, in forms which were once made using Venetian glass beads. This is a community which commonly encounters itself positioned as a minority, seen as Igorot within the Philippines, or seen as Filipino within the United Kingdom. At times, people wrestle with a rather burdensome stereotype which would represent Igorot women and men as very traditional, even living in the past, rather like craft traditions generally. In response to such over-determined past-oriented ethnic and gender stereotypes, McKay consistently emphasises the way that the celebration of handmade plastic artefacts is subversive. In publishing, co-publishing and co-curating exhibitions of crafted plastic artefacts, McKay as anthropologist works within this subversive space.

Some anthropologists understand the work of ‘explicitness’ as one in which most craft traditions are, in essence, forms of design insofar as they are ways of rethinking, reproducing and innovating in the material world and ways of living. For Tunstall (2013), design anthropologists should very clearly build an understanding of local values, as one way of rehabilitating minority and oppressed practices as alternative modes of designing. This is the parallel work of decolonising and diversifying global design.

Hence, different anthropologists and ethnographers work within the space of design and crafts in different ways. Some see design and craft are conceived of as essentially different sets of skills and groups of people, and because of this possessing a potentially collaborative relationship. Others see a fundamental similarity between them and diagnose a largely colonising and subjugating relationship in which design is the problem, and re-formulating design in new ways is the solution.

This project recognised the self-colonising implications of the geopolitics of design and crafts, which can strike at the heart of communities in whichever part of the globe they inhabit. This is to say that the perception of fashion as existing in certain urban centres (e.g. Paris, Milan, London, New York and Tokyo), and the correlating perception of many parts of Africa (and elsewhere) as rich in crafts, but not designs, is actually a cipher for self-perception. Thinking with geography and locality stands for ways of thinking about people and it whitens fashion and design work.

Young people participating in the project learned about fabrics and fashion. Trips included to the sources of global fabrics across London, which is a city where shops and traders sell large quantities of fabrics from parts of Africa, South Asia and elsewhere. Teams examined the presentation of fashion and fabrics in authoritative institutions such as the British Museum and the Victoria and Albert, and the history of the presentation of materials, skills and fashion. They also received training in business skills, project planning and budget management in order to present their collections.

The project had several aims, and the feedback showed how it achieved them with many participants:

An alternative sense of identity than that portrayed in the media; through positive images of themselves… An improved knowledge of culture and heritage… New possibilities for further study or career routes… More confidence when seeking employment; through the experience of having a defined job role and developing skills in team work, planning and event management, creative thinking, garment construction… Increased financial management skills resulting in better life skills.

(From a 2011 promotional leaflet)

Evidently, all of these effects are ambitious, and many are not physical, material outputs, but about economic and social roles. Interestingly, the claims for value in the project are about what difference it makes to people, and what they learn, not the creation of fashion artefacts. Nonetheless, there is a material foundation to the work, and that concerns the textiles themselves which, frankly, look and feel glorious. The project identifies a specific material manifestation – West African textiles – which are themselves a silent, everyday manifestation of cultural presumptions, and it re-imagines them. In the communities where Set Fashion Free worked, especially Southwark and Lambeth, these textiles are familiar to many people and can be seen to represent the sorts of clothes their parents may wear, especially their mothers, who have moved to London from West Africa, among other places. Hence, these common materials carry with them a certain immovability and authority and convey a very concretised association with West Africa. The fabrics are not fixed in their colours, patterns and textures, there is a great deal of variety and experimentation. The sense of cultural fixity lies elsewhere.

We believe… in a future where every young person is able to fully participate in society grounded in a strong sense of who they are… that understanding your place in the world is vital to achieving happiness and success in life. … that textile heritage is more than a fashion commodity. It documents our history, telling us stories of who we are and where we come from.

The project is not so much a design project as a piece of curatorial work, which brings together certain skills, objects and projects in a carefully considered way so that they all begin to make sense, and more importantly make better sense. It recognises the relational nature of the problem it wishes to address, and the balance of objective and subjective elements: not a problem with people, things or places per se, but a problem in the interrelationships. There is a clear decolonising agenda at work, and a very potent sense of ethnicised inequality and politics. But the project also uses fashion and design to achieve a less colonised world. By working with textiles specifically, using fashion education, it aims to achieve subjective re-evaluations of peoples’ own self-worth. It does this through working with a geopolitical cultural space-time, a kind of personal expansion. The imagination of futures is expanded by learning about pasts, and a sense of more profound and secure London roots is established through learning about another continent. There is here, then, a material rehabilitation of the person within the space of the city, achieved through a re-imagination of textiles.

## Material outputs in design anthropology work

These examples make clear that design anthropology does not usually produce valuable artefacts directly, but constructive and critical reflections on value. The kinds of value outputs which may be informed by the work include products and services which are useful, things which fit into a simple model of supply and demand, more streamlined production processes, brands or innovations whose value is as intellectual property. These kinds of output each depends on a fairly direct and simple notion of value in themselves, such as use value, market exchange value, monetary profit, symbolic significance and legal ownership. But to inform those designs, the anthropologist works with ethnography to develop things such as themes, conceptualisations, provotypes, prototypes, narratives, value models and contested perspectives on value.

We saw in Chapter 4 Suchman’s proposal that design anthropologists produce critical ethnographic accounts for design companies and studios which produce a sense of friction which can stimulate and guide design work. According to this interpretation, what anthropologists develop are interpretations of value which will act as a critical counterpoint to corporate, bureaucratic ways of understanding value. Most organisations use formal, calculating and quantitative rationales. Even qualities and abstract meanings are commonly ascribed numerical values in many institutions. In a frictional interpretation of design anthropological work, those value rationales actively work against developing new notions, and the anthropologist must engage with those corporate notions while presenting very different versions of value.

A more extreme, confrontational version of that account comes from Tunstall, advocating a decolonising agenda:

Projects should use design processes and artifacts to work with groups to shift hegemonic value systems that are detrimental to the holistic well-being of vulnerable groups, dominant groups, and their extended environments.

(Tunstall 2013: 245)

For her, a typical design anthropologist attempts to constitute, manifest or model the value system of their informants. So sketches, artistic productions and models can be considered as direct manifestations of these subjugated and alternative value systems. Tunstall here is highlighting how many design professionals and corporations do not factor their own value systems into the equation as a part of the work. A lot of design work proceeds because of a certain set of values, often purportedly very humanitarian and culturally open, and yet never questions these. Multinational corporations can become imprisoned by having a fixed set of values, which come to define them as much as guide them; so too local communities, who when over-defined by social research can be represented simplistically as relics, their ways of living and motivations inevitably emerging from past roots. Design anthropology projects are important in being able to shift monolithic, unyielding and potentially, therefore, fragile sets of ideas and build the necessary cultural flexibility for developing value.

A more sustained and developed commentary on the anthropology of value has been developed by Graeber (2002). His analysis is wide-ranging, ‘value as something social, aesthetic, economic and linguistic’ (Hart 2011: 2). One of the most useful reflections is his putative suggestion for thinking about value as

The way people represent the importance of their action to themselves.

(Graeber 2002: 45)

This admirably succinct hypothesis is informed by a long discussion of many of the ideas about values which I discussed earlier, from Marx and Weber to Saussure and Strathern. The implication is that ultimately, it is peoples’ own accounts of value which matter most, although people are complex and their desires are subject to social norms and practicalities (‘Values are ideas about what they ought to want’ Graeber 2002: 3). Work (action) is fundamental to value, but that work is not invariably exploited because of the structures of capitalism and comparable economic systems. Graeber’s observation also is significant in which it recognises that the specific details, forms and materials present in an ethnographic site matter for value. Effective representation of value means building on the observation of existing material realities, so that design makes sense beyond the design studio.

These various observations illustrate how the design anthropology of value is not as simple as producing an analysis of value as a set of ‘social facts’, but a process of mutual articulation, emergence and making explicit of what is important. The design anthropologist produces material which works across the fissures and boundaries of the diversity which is intrinsic to a sense of value.

## The use of provotypes in the rural transport project

We will now return briefly to the Irish rural transport Project, before concluding. I have repeatedly asserted that design anthropology does not directly produce design outputs, and yet, in spite of this, some readers will have read my descriptions of the work with an increasing sense of frustration, asking ‘but what did thus work actually produce?’ or ‘what did they create?

The closest answer is that we produced ways to continue exploring and critiquing value in cultural ways. In the studio, we worked on a range of design concepts for services and platforms which might support rural transport initiatives and their passengers. Four of these were developed into simulations of services which we could show on a laptop or iPad. We brought these four concepts, which belong to Intel, back to the field, and asked passengers and staff what they thought. Because these things were, among other things, a way to deepen our understanding of the very real social and cultural issues people were negotiating, we called them ‘provotypes’.

The obvious kind of service would have been some kind of ride-sharing app, by which somebody can simply order a ride, but none of our concepts were functional in that sense. Apart from anything else, the software functionality to order a ride or taxi already existed. Our concepts all suited certain social situations and contexts, which might need some explaining. For example, one concept envisaged somebody setting up a social event, and inviting people, with the transportation as a potential functionality within that invitation. Another concept was more about communication and organising different social networks. We used visual elements which echoed what had emerged as significant in the research. For example, two of our concepts were based around maps, because of the importance of the land and the landscape in the research as a principle of social organisation.

When we showed our concepts to passengers, we began to build a much better sense of what kind of system of values they occupied, through talking about where, how and with whom these concepts might work. So, for example, a touchscreen might be imagined located in somebody’s home or on a bus for driver and passengers to use, or in a health centre or community centre, or with a relative living nearby. Depending on where an interface is located, and who has access to it, one gains a sense of what sort of set of exchanges and interactions is understood to be appropriate. Do these kinds of material artefact fit into the private sphere of family where people are all related? Do they belong among peers, for example, among a community of retired friends of the same age, but not related? Do they articulate the relations of people with formally employed professionals such as nurses or drivers? These developments were accompanied by a much more stark and direct expression of the issues which design might address. ‘Our village is dead’ commented two passengers in Sligo, in stark contrast to our earlier experiences on the busses. So when we talked about the concepts, we began to gain new understandings of the potential for value, and the kinds of exchange systems which these artefacts would occupy, and the value transformations and exchanges they might facilitate. There were particular ways in which older modes of socialising, institutions and sources of trust had been declining, but at the same time, the potential for building social groups around alternative institutions and relationships was opening up.

In these ways, the work of re-contextualising the design concepts was not exactly ‘user testing’ for functionality, although there was an element of that. More importantly, it was a continuation of the work of exploring value in a systemic fashion. It had an important element of critique in it, challenging the corporate conceptions of value and values, and undermining the notion that either the communities or the company had fixed value systems. Anthropological and ethnographic exploration, learning, interpretation and critique does not end with the first phase of ethnography, and the de-contextualisation of ethnographic observations; it rather continues with re-contextualisation in later phases of the work, which is equally anthropological.

## Consequences

The thrust of this chapter has been to build an understanding of the implications of seeing value and values as a heuristic device, a tool for thinking with. This is to say that value is neither something which is provided by innovation, intrinsically contained within new technologies, goods, and services nor is value only a model of how a community or social life works, as in an anthropology ‘of’ value (Graeber 2002). Design anthropologists also work ‘with’ value in a fluid field, across many interdependent groups. Successful products, companies and the livelihoods of skilled people who design depend upon researchers delivering to them a well-informed and realistic sense of what may work and how. Most design anthropology works across multiple interdependent fields of economic practice and thinking, for example, public service, private profit and household economies. To consider value as a heuristic device is to emphasise its role in communicating across this connected diversity, across social fissures and bridges and making potential value explicit and manifest. This is because design comprises a part of a moral economy (Hann 2010: 190; Thompson 1971), in which the notion of the market forms one element, but embedded social values are more motivating and encompassing.

It is very important to stress the risks and the high stakes. Designing can be precarious and disruptive, but that does not mean we should not do it. Every anthropologist learns of historical examples where new goods have played a part in social upheaval and cultural decline because new goods change value systems and established hierarchies. The once wealthy economies of the Native American North West Coast are one example. With an influx of new goods in the 19th century, wealthy local people at first responded with immense ceremonies of ludicrously opulent giving and destruction. People would demonstrate their worth in immense events, potlatches, competitively giving and destroying huge quantities of wonderfully made artefacts. Such events came at a time of crisis, and also the deliberate spreading of disease using infected goods. Ultimately, many communities suffered catastrophic collapse. While traditions lived on (art, livelihoods, stories, music, dances and memories), with fewer people, these civilisations were not the same. It is clear that new goods, radically changing exchange systems and changing hierarchies during which peoples’ own value was challenged played a significant part.

A more familiar example may be the nature of the introduction of domestic technologies during the 20th century, such as washing machines, into most European and American homes. Such goods were advertised as ‘labour saving’ and ‘liberating’ for women. They addressed issues of labour but did not address the nature of gendered domestic exchanges, and the relative value of domestic work. It was a subsequent generation in the 1960s who began to establish a sense of gender equality. New domestic technologies do not only change the nature of work, but how people understand systems of value. At the time of writing, every parent whose children have an iPhone will be familiar with how those artefacts are not only about individual function, but social exchanges and evaluations within household economies and across communities.

To work in design is to be involved in a perpetual age of discovery, in which discovery of new horizons of knowledge and mutual discovery between people happen at the same time. In this situation, the material outputs of design anthropology, convergently social research and design, are manifestations both of the value of work and also constitute relationships and exchanges. Put simply, a thing can be considered a relationship. Value and values are human-centred, not about abstract infrastructures, institutions or tools. Hart et al. (2010: 6; see also Wakeford 2004) call this ‘the Human Economy’: ‘the object of an economy was always the reproduction of human life and beyond that the preservation of everything that sustains life’. One of the particular aspects to pay attention to in this situation is the mutuality of work-oriented and exchangeoriented approaches. In the former, people rationalise their actions over time, pasts and futures, and material things help with this constant evaluation of means and ends, of action against ideals. In the latter, things are constantly manifesting comparisons, of one thing against another thing, to create an unfolding sense of spheres of value, of ersatz and incommensurable domains.

The good news is that on this shifting territory of different scales and forms of value, design anthropology works constantly to celebrate the relevance to real people. Value is friction-filled and contested, but these moments of tension may also be the significant ones within which people take the responsibility for cultural work upon themselves. In the previous chapter, we discussed the ‘centre and periphery’ qualities of design action, by which people come not only to be the objects of design within designed contexts but also to be contexts themselves for the world, elevating persons as particular and special within a largely ersatz material world. Successful designed things then are often “taken on” by people, who may assume the mantle of responsibility implicit within design.

## Summary

This chapter has argued that conceptions of value are crucial in design anthropology, because it is both an intrinsically moral and intrinsically financial domain. This means that practitioners are not only involved in the production of valuable things, on a small scale, but in producing understandings of value-infused social life on a much broader and more diverse canvas.

Values can be considered in terms of profit or benefit; as different kinds of rationalities for action; as appropriate comparison between different things or as processual transformations around a thing. It is important, however, to understand value not as a single thing, but as a systemic interpretation of how different but important phenomena fit together.

The value of things in design culture can be understood in terms of the ways that people represent the value of their own work to themselves (following Graeber 2002).

For some, design anthropology work specifically develops manifestations of the values and value systems of its informant communities, in order to confront institutions with critical alternative ways of conceiving of the value of their work.

Each design anthropologist should consider the particular transformations of value which are occurring around design concepts. Design is not only about proving useful and meaningful to individual people but also about developing relationships and exchanges in communities. The designs arising from anthropological work can become socially generative and build value more widely.

A design anthropologist should ask what sets of values characterise the community with whom they are working, and what values characterise the professional environments with whom they are collaborating. It is important to consider their own role between these conceptions of value, for example, as a mediator, a critic or a provider of friction.

The design anthropologist should also consider the value of their own social research work. Is it based upon, for example, their skills, upon the formula for the process of work or upon the material outputs of the research?

Working with values also concerns asking why and how would different parties in a project work with an anthropologist? How is ethnographic work going to circulate, be given or exchanged? What appropriate material forms might the work take, and have people been informed about the circulation of value?

## 7 FUTURES

The third heuristic device which should concern every design anthropologist, one which is even more problematic, is the concept of the future, which implicitly concerns the concept of change. Fundamental to design anthropology is the work of bringing how professional design sees change into elision with how the communities which are to benefit from design see change. It is not only important to effect different ways of living, but also how it happens.

The work of thinking about futures is fundamentally paradoxical and problematic in a number of ways, and this chapter explores some of the difficulties and stances. The chapter eventually argues three key points: first, design anthropologists can think about cultures of futures through material culture and objectification, since, in general, people work with their ideas of time through material objects. Second, a sense of progress in design is intrinsically connected to contested political relationships with social institutions such that the journeys of design researchers between design studios and other sites are acts of thinking about change. Hence the research products which travel between these sites manifest the experience and perception of what progress is. Third, the implication of a material culture approach is that imagining futures and remembering pasts are mutual activities, in fact very similar. In most cases, greater certainty about futures is achieved through using material culture to re-frame ideas about pasts. This gives life a sense of trajectory: where a person is going is often expressed through where they are coming from. The implications of those three points will be pursued in more depth in the chapter following this one.

Futures and change are, however, treacherous intellectual territory. It is important to remember that almost all design work is premised upon effecting change, so to appreciate the full range of conceptions and ways of working with change and futures, you would have to explore every piece of work on design per se. Designers are experts in this field, and anthropologists are still catching up. A diversity of ways of thinking about change are also as diverse as ways of thinking about time such that different researchers are actually talking about entirely different phenomena. Everything has temporal qualities (Adam 2004). We may talk of change, futures, progress, histories, narrative, experience, models, creativity and practice, and all of these terms are vehicles for talking about temporality in cultural ways.

However, in spite of this relativism, I suggest there is such a thing as progress. Design is premised upon the idea that we can make the world a better place, and help people, but that this is not inevitable. Most design anthropologists share this optimism, but in doing so, they make their own job very difficult. Where there is progress, there is also the possibility of regression, and what is good for one person or group may be bad for another. The idea of progress also runs counter to the fundamental ideas of the equality of human cultures and respect for others, which underlie both anthropology and most design work. Progress therefore implies a degree of inequality, either across time or between different social groups (Suchman 2011). Because of this, progress as an aspiration can disrupt ethnographic work, which requires respectful participation in other peoples’ lives, and listening to how their lives make sense as they are. Design anthropologists are called upon to evaluate situations and make judgements both about informants’ lives and about design colleagues’ work. They are expected to be diagnosticians. Much like a doctor who considers whether to prescribe to treat symptoms, or causes, of health problems, in design anthropology, one is expected to make useful judgements about what design is able to do, and indeed who is responsible for causes, effects and symptoms.

The subject of change, and of how design relates to temporality, can become unnervingly philosophical. But it does not have to be. We can spend endless moments inventing thought experiments about cause and effect, whether designers or users or designed things result in change, about guns and soldiers, bricks and windows, feet and footballs. When it comes down to it, the best design anthropological approach is that there are two options when a project feels uncertain: either one must draw on experience to make a decision, and call it professional judgement, or else, more advisedly, one should go out to a relevant field site and ask somebody. You don’t know whether a meeting technology was intended to make a decision or just build social cohesion? – go and ask the people who were in the meeting what they think. You don’t know what progress is? Ask somebody. You will not get an answer which is some kind of universal truth, but you will get relevant and informative perspectives. It is important that the evidential and research basis for decisions is clear. It is also important to acknowledge and think about our mutual ignorance about futures. Intellectual humility in the face of this work can help to actively drive innovative thinking.

At root, the key issue is bringing into elision how designers and people in their own lives locate themselves in relation to temporality, change, historicity and futures. The design anthropologist should ask such questions as:

Where do people imagine themselves to be in their future lives?

Do people see change as an exception to normal life, or normal life as involving perpetual change?

How do people think change happens? Are there specific things in their environment which indicate how they may envisage the unfolding of change? What are the means, routines, places and actions by which people engage with change, control change, or prevent change?

Are there specific temporal horizons of fragmentation and change in this piece of design anthropology work? Is this social microcosm a ‘revolutionary’ system of change and development, or a more gradual one?

What is ‘significant’ change? How does change have meaningfulness for people?

## The emergence of futuring

In the Summer of 2019, the company WGSN (a ‘trend forecasting’ consultancy) declared that the colour of 2020 would be ‘neo-mint’. This colour synthesised a lot of what they believed the important trends would be for 2020:

neo mint is a gender-neutral colour with ‘an oxygenating, fresh tone that aligns science and technology with nature’.

(Dezeen website https://www.dezeen.com/2018/05/11/neo-mint-colour-2020-wgsn-trends-

For the consultancy, this colour does not only express a particular fashion in colour choices and designs for 2020’s products, interiors and clothing. The colour is also a vehicle for social commentary, and for what kinds of cultural principles may be important in 2020. Those sociocultural observations are based on the interpretation of research of different kinds.

By contrast with WGSN, we can consider the colour company Pantone, who have not at the time of writing made their 2020 prediction, but who predicted ‘Living Coral’ for 2019:

An animating and life-affirming coral hue with a golden undertone that energizes and enlivens with a softer edge… In reaction to the onslaught of digital technology and social media increasingly embedding into daily life, we are seeking authentic and immersive experiences that enable connection and intimacy.

(Pantone website https://www.pantone.com/color-intelligence/color-of-the-year/color-ofthe-year-2019; accessed August 2019)

Pantone, unlike WGSN, is a company which specialises in consultancy about colour. Whereas WGSN’s clients may begin with thinking about changing markets and society, and exploring colour is one possible route to take, Pantone’s clients start and end with colour. So the business model differs, but both of them explore futures and find it possible to do so through colours. These are examples of how choosing a particular material quality, object or substance is commonly nowadays used as a vehicle into ‘futuring’. In rendering these colours as futures, they become not only colours but also thought experiments into social conditions, desires and experiences. Material things exist as design concepts, at an interface of spaces of possibility and of solutions, as temporally ambiguous phenomena. They are, in a sense, both social questions and social answers.

An increasing concern with the future frames, supports and constrains contemporary design anthropology, as we saw in Chapter 1. The industry of futures and prediction, once a peripheral and largely instinctive arm of market research, is now mainstream and based on defined research methodologies (Cunningham 2017). Some futures consultants and consultancies undertake observational research, seeking out material forms and styles which may be indicative of ‘trends’. Most trends work is as much about observation and skill, involving the art of weaving the connections between different phenomena to perceive something greater. They may travel to key global design shows, for example in Milan or New York, while others work with online images and social media, and others use data and statistics.

Other consultancies do not seek out futures in observation of the world so much as through workshopping in the studio. Sketching, envisaging, modelling and imagining objects and things are all ways used to access possible futures which arise from the existing knowledge and expertise of people in companies and designers. Futures can emerge from both ‘out there’, and from within. Good futures consultancies are very precise about the kinds of work they undertake, they judge its quality, and are very precise about what one can expect from it or not: for example, next year’s futures and the futures of 50 years’ time should be dealt with using different approaches. Some pieces of work amount to predictions while others might amount to anticipations.

The point here is that material things and substances are very often deployed as manifestations of futures and often work better than words because objects can evoke not what a future will be exactly, but rather what it will be ‘like’.

In this situation, ‘Design is always future-making’ (Yelavich 2014: 12). Work, which might previously have been understood as making, crafting, responding to consumer demand and so forth, is now increasingly understood in temporal terms as futuring. In this sense, design is reflecting wider societal and economic concerns. Environmentalism and sustainability, a debtbased monetary system and a concern with investment risk above production output, all reflect how the sources of wealth draw on certainty about the future as a limited resource. As we explored in Chapter 1, human culture is itself increasingly understood as creativity and innovation, with vernacular design as an extension of the modern culture concept.

A great deal of design literature has recently focussed on ‘futures’, in many different ways (Appadurai 2013; Bryant and Knight 2019; Ehn et al 2014; Fischer 2009; Halse et al 2010; Julier and Kimbell 2016; Rosenberg and Harding 2005; Salazar et al 2017; Yelavich and Adams 2014). Prominent in this field is the work of Sarah Pink (Salazar, Pink, Irving and Sjoberg 2017; Akama and Pink 2018; Ardévol et al 2016). Much of this work aspires to move beyond the previous oppositional binaristic understanding of design as either innovation or participation. By thinking about ‘futures’, design anthropologists aspire to develop terminologies which are capable of outlining the spaces, opportunities and social contexts of design and can also begin to envisage design inputs, responses and solutions.

The relationship between fashion and time is not necessarily smooth or coherent but can be asymptotic and critical. In these perspectives, design and temporality can be seen as coproductive, and design can re-open the dialogue between memory, futurity, and newness, rather than serving as a mere mirror of commodified duration.

(Appadurai 2013: 9)

The fact is, as some of this literature points out, futures are not exclusive to design and do not happen only in design studios. An ethnographic example will illustrate.

Peg was a woman with whom Kyle Kilbourn worked at Intel Digital Health Group. This research formed a part of the ‘Material Culture Ireland’ project, in which we worked with a range of retired households in Ireland, with a particular focus on the material culture of homes, and reenvisioning it to support independent living. Peg’s kitchen was moved wholesale by her family from a house in Dublin to a similar house in Cork, when she moved to be closer to them. Her family recognised that as she got older, she needed consistency in the material layout of her home. Cupboards, chairs and things in the material world were props for Peg, often literally, since as she walked around the house, she would lean on sideboards and furniture. When she made tea, she worked through the same physical stages in her kitchen, one after the other. When Kyle interrupted her in the middle of boiling the kettle, she turned to answer him, and then she went back to making the tea; but in order to do so, she turned her body around in imitation of the tea-making process, to the teapot, the teabags and the kettle, until she arrived at the kettle-boiling stage. Her possessions and home could be seen as a large embodied memory, an extension of her and her way of living which would persist over time.

What struck our research team when it came to her envisaging of her future was the TV in her bedroom, which was not connected and did not yet work (Figure 7.1). She referred to this as her ‘walking stick’. While she did not elaborate on this, we understood this to mean that she was anticipating, without verbalising it, that she might in future have to spend long periods of time in bed and unable to go downstairs. The television indicated an anticipation but also had a sense of support for her. If she had to spend weeks or longer bed-bound, the TV would be something to keep her occupied.

![](images/409befb19fa23a496ff73b9ccbfcfd1a43ed016641f92d0e4e3fcc304cf8a8a9.jpg)  
FIGURE 7.1Peg’s “walking stick”. Photo by Kyle Kilbourn.

For people like Peg, redesigning physical environments can be actually dangerous. Even proposing a physical move, or anticipating a physical accident or decline, can be detrimental. By exploring the stories around objects, however, one finds that futures are being manifested, even when not verbalised. Objects such as Peg’s walking stick evidently show us how material things are ways of working with temporality and have the capacity to move across it. Her TV is entirely experimental on her part, non-functional, and broaches a hypothetical future, not a definitive thought or significance.

In ethnographic studies of homes, interesting temporalities are often manifested around certain things, especially those which are understood as ‘technological’. On the one hand, there are often media-based objects, screens, televisions, speakers or other things which simply do not look as if they belong alongside everything else, ‘black boxes’, literally and conceptually (Grint and Woolgar 1997). A lot of technological design produces black boxes which perform certain functions, but whose smooth and impenetrable surfaces and blinking lights make no concessions to the fabric of domesticity, to wooden furniture, soft carpets, the sharp fairy lights of Christmas trees or bikes in the hall. Domestic life happens around such sleek technological objects, a life of which they are now the centre since the IT revolution, but while they facilitate what people do, they rarely participate in it. Other technological objects in homes are what one might describe as ‘fossils’. In drawers, attics and sheds, one can find boxes of old remote controls, mobile phones, charging cables and devices which frankly nobody can remember any more what they do or used to do. One may ask what the difference is between different kinds of black box, littering homes, and the most obvious answer is not content but time. Their multiplication around the home then is not only indicative of what people are doing, but of how they conceptualise, define and manifest the time frame of the home within which they live. Not all objects are actually a part of what is happening now, and some indeed appear as if they are a part of something happening at another time. Ethnographic experience and observation reveals social worlds which are very temporally messy and uneven, with multiple things existing in multiple temporal frameworks which are potentially cultural resources.

Whether it happens in homes, design studios or other places, a lot of the material culture of futures amounts to ‘presencing absence’ (Buchli and Lucas 2000). Objects are used to symbolically manifest futures, and everybody is doing this, it does not only happen in design studios. Luhmann (1982) calls these ‘present futures’, as distinct from more abstract ‘future presents’. In its strongest form, this kind of time-transcending materiality constitutes a form of magical thinking, which is to say that through indexically indicating a particular future, it will somehow be more likely to be brought to happen.

The chapter continues by exploring various examples of manifesting futures, and, more importantly, the problems and difficulties around that work, in four stages. We look first at some moments when designers have conjured up visions of better futures, and at critical futures. We then examine the difficulties involved in undertaking ethnographies of cultures of temporality, and culminate with examining the idea of the alterity of futures, suggesting that when futures seem ubiquitous, people are constantly and problematically encountering them and confronting them.

## Visions of a better society

Some design, and some design anthropology, sees its temporal work as the presentation of new social futures to the world. In the 1920s, Bauhaus designers presented a vision to the world of modern ways of living. Across the contemporary world, many of the material things around us convey echoes of their 1920s designs, from doorknobs to windows to how rooms are arranged in a house. As we discussed in Chapter 5, these many individual material elements were presented as a part of a context, a totality which would present a modern lifestyle, ‘how we live now’. At the forefront of many Bauhaus designers’ minds was the idea of human products and artefacts as a ‘total work of art’ (Gesamtkunstwerk). That invention of a vision, a lifestyle and a context was itself as revolutionary as the products. Design from this moment was not only the work of responding to the desires of consumers in a market, nor to the capacities of factories with specialised production lines, but also envisaging and proposing futures, and doing so with clarity using images and media on a human scale.

Design anthropology produces many such social and cultural visions, among its various outputs: storyboards of how designed things might work in practice, narratives, videos, diagrams and models. These outputs explore both the design spaces and the design concepts convergently. They are themselves created with a human audience in mind, because the human scale is fantastically important in design, probably more than in fields such as engineering or art. Even if an issue is complex and too large for one person to experience, for example an infrastructure, then design will often work to render it in a form which one person can take in and envisage personally.

The Bauhaus demonstrates two simple ways of conceptualising how design articulates with change. Either technical innovation in laboratories and studios produces change, in a cause-andeffect way, or else, designers work towards a social vision by producing innovations.

The implicit difficulties of thinking about a vision-driven approach to change can be seen much more recently in the history of ubiquitous computing, as concept and as experience (Dourish and Bell 2011; see Chapter 5). Ubiquitous computing might have started as an evocation of a possible future, one which might be either a prediction of how life ‘out there’ beyond the design studio would change or a proposal for how technology was going to change life ‘out there’. Yet now, when most people have ubiquitous access to computing power, through smartphones and Wi-Fi, it is unclear if this change came about because of the vision, or would have happened anyway without the vision. Ubiquity did not happen in the way people expected, not from the computing industry so much as the telecommunications industry, from the people who used to provide homes with the plastic phones which plugged into the wall and who began to adopt silicon processors into their products.

It is also evident that design anthropology has been complicit in making informational ubiquity a reality, because the vision compelled computer scientists to find out about life as it is lived in many places. It was not always clear where to go and do research, because it was not clear exactly ‘where’ was meant by ‘ubiquity’, and it hence necessitated a research journey to find out about ‘elsewhere’s. Anthropology as a discipline was ideally suited for this journey to the sociocultural periphery. While the vision of ubiquity added to the corpus of ethnographic work, and some very good anthropological commentaries, the vision itself did not emerge from anthropology or observation. Most anthropologists were placed in a subcultural position, challenging, critiquing and unpacking the proffered laboratory-based imaginations of social life. Retrospectively, much of the social research work of ubiquity was directed at the detection and unearthing of needs, desires and capacities for the technologies; more than in conceptually exploring what social life with informational ubiquity looks like. So the first major problem with vision-led change is that, while it gets technical work done, it is self-fulfilling, and the actual complicated route by which the change happens can be unexpected.

For both Bauhaus-style modernism, and ubiquitous computing, the second problem is that they simply do not take account of how people relate to change itself. Cultures of change are diverse and contested, not linear. Barry (2001) observes that a culture of technology is one which privileges change rather than stasis and then ascribes change to technology and technological invention. Since stasis is problematic in certain cultures, and since design is seen as a key cause of change, design then comes to be a privileged, overarching value system which people aspire to and by which they live their lives. Rather than being defined and observable, change manifests as progress in different ways according to how contextually and culturally it is evaluated.

What actually happens very quickly when designers present grand visions for new ways of living is that they can quickly become old, because they are often cultures or lifestyles imagined as frozen, more than imaginations of cultures of change. What can result, as I shall discuss later, is a sense of living in the ruins of past designs (Navaro-Yashin 2009). The Bauhaus sites of Germany, in Dessau, Weimar and Berlin have become in some ways pilgrimage sites. The visions of futures and of endless revolution are now heritage and are carefully preserved and maintained. These 1920s heritage sites, maintaining past futures, are the focus of community pride and value and mobilise a sense of identity. Similar observations can be made about many technological innovations, and many homes are littered with the fossilisations of what were once visions of future living but have become defunct. Dreams age. The inevitable connection between the experience of ruination and the experience of innovation is not necessarily a major problem. In fact, it is a resource, as I will discuss later. But it does present complications for how design anthropology thinks about futures and change.

The third problem with considering the project of design as presenting a vision for change is political. When work tries to achieve change through innovation-and-impact models, it is based on pre-conceived temporal structures and supports the status quo, rather than rethinking temporality. So change may happen, but some kinds of inequality can become more entrenched. Capitalism can be considered as a temporal order, a political structure of dominance based on the control of time (Cross 1993; Harvey 1989, Mintz 1985), as well as the more common conceptions which privilege freedom of economic action, or control of production. Graeber (2018), for example, points out how many capitalist enterprises effectively try to control people by occupying their time, more than giving them productive work to do. These observations can be translated directly into examples of the design of ‘engagement’ in social media sites. While some designers may conceptualise a site as aiming to increase social engagements and conversations between people, institutionally ‘engagement’ can mean keeping people on the site for as long as possible. In this way, many capitalist institutions re-frame social meaningfulness and utility as the occupation of peoples’ time.

When visions of the future become mistaken with ways of controlling time, in this fashion, there is a risk of ossification. Verdery (1996), in her analysis of why Soviet socialism failed in 1989, suggests that ultimately it ‘ran out of time’. She sees the various socialist regimes as, essentially, subsets of capitalism, largely basing their power on being able to control the organisation of time, underpinned by the claim that socialism offered superior progress and brighter futures. Ultimately, however, this resulted in an ossified political economy in which people spent long hours in factories producing poorly designed and unusable goods. Criticism of society and culture was nearly impossible when the most powerful institution, the state, claimed to offer the best future such that ironically the worship of social and economic progress became detrimental to most beneficial material change.

Design companies and institutions are of course not the same as the communist regimes of 20th-century Europe, but the aim is to highlight the political problems of futures when many capitalist institutions base their authority on the control of temporality. There is a risk of inflexibility, and ultimately failure, when too much faith is placed on simply having the authority to broadcast and push out one’s own vision of the future. Postcolonial thinking about design commonly frames global design as colonising, and maintaining global inequality through its claims to be closer to ‘the future’ than others. This is why Bhagat (2016) criticises people who have embarked on design histories ‘of’ Africa, which consigns an entire continent to a singular historical timeline. He advances instead pluralist histories of designing ‘in’ Africa, by many contrasting practitioners and for many reasons.

## Critical futures

There are therefore profound difficulties with seeing futures through a paradigm of innovation alone. But there are alternatives.

The Center for Genomic Gastronomy uses artistic methods to explore the science, futures and cultures of food. Its events, installations and exhibitions aim to actually confront people with future possibilities (see Howells 2014; <genomicgastronomy.com>). Every event is different, but they all deploy food as a material vehicle for experimental thinking. For example, the centre organises tailored meals in which each course may explore a particular set of possible food futures. In one meal, alternative choices for one course included: a salad of genetically engineered tomatoes, one based on seaweed from the West of Ireland, or a quinoa recipe from the Andes. The point is that each of these is a food which at some time has been suggested as a future superfood which can be a part of resolving humanity’s food issues, but each one on different grounds. Genetically modified tomatoes represent the idea that new technologies and scientific innovation will provide the answers. Scientific methodologies and work processes, this course suggests, can provide a food, which will contain any particular nutrition we require, and help to maximise crop yields. Quinoa, by contrast, is a food with a long human tradition of cultivation in the Andes. It contains a wide range of nutrients and proteins because, although it looks like a grain crop, it is actually a kind of vegetable. Quinoa, therefore, is something which anthropologists might bring to the attention of global culture. It comes with its own recipes, aesthetics, cookware, modes of cultivation, labour processes and cultural histories, and it is attractive because of this sense of having an established and well-known place within human society already. The third alternative, seaweed, also has a history of being eaten by people, especially in Japan and Ireland, but its main attraction is that it forms an established part of the global ecosystem. There is a vast quantity of seaweeds growing across the world, supporting rich ecosystems with no human input whatsoever. Although much of it is potentially edible, it is generally under-exploited, with many cultures looking elsewhere for food. When the Center for Genomic Gastronomy offers these three alternative dishes, they are offering three alternative paradigms and agendas for considering design futures: technological, cultural and environmental. In the meal, one can feel, taste and experience what those projects might mean.

The underlying philosophies behind this kind of critical design work go back to the mid-20th century, which connect it to certain assumptions about how social progress happens. In the 1930s, when Karl Popper was studying scientific knowledge, he argued strongly that the scientific method does not depend on proving particular outcomes, but rather on disproving them. A common understanding at the time was that scientists did experiments in order to prove something works, and hence make progress a reality. However, Popper argued that no matter how many times something works or happens, it can never be proved with 100% certainty. The false premise was explained later by Bertrand Russell who gave the example of chickens in a farmyard. Every day, the chickens are fed at the same time, and they come to expect being fed as a predictable certainty and flock to the farmer. And yet a day comes when the farmer instead kills them to eat. In a similar way, scientific method does not progress by proving that something is a fact because it will happen in the same way when you repeat it tomorrow and the following days; rather, it progresses by hypothesising possible explanations, and then trying to disprove them, and then come up with better ones. Progress happens when we try to show that the sun may not rise tomorrow. Extending this philosophy of science to the domain of society and politics, Popper (2002 [1945]) argued that beneficial change happens in societies where it is possible to challenge and disprove political and social ideas. In other words, an open and democratic society in which there is a tolerance of difference and of different opinions will be better at designing. Fundamental to this argument is that nobody actually knows with certainty what makes for a better future, rather futures are always hypothetical, experimental and emergent from dialogues and critiques.

It was in the 1990s that a profound sea change happened in design work, to put speculative and socially critical work at the heart of design practice. Designers throughout the 20th century had always of course thought about the future, but had generally focussed on ways to improve and innovate in the objects and devices they worked with. A classic example of a 20th century approach is Dieter Rams’ principles for ‘good design’, ten striking and inspiring insights into judging the design element in products. Because of the influence of people like Popper, future societies and cultures were often seen as open, blue skies of possibility which could not be predicted with much confidence; and designers drew invariably on contemporary research for concrete examples of the contexts where designs would be located. From the turn of the century, Anthony Dunne and Fiona Raby’s work had a major impact, although their studio practice had been established since 1994, and other designers were also producing parallel work.

Their exhibitions and installations of ‘design noir’ (Dunne and Raby 2001), even though they presented very plausible design ideas, were not recommendations, but dystopian. They worked to imagine problems and difficulties alongside advantages, and in doing so produced sensations and imaginations of the future contexts of design in which neither the ‘good’ nor the ‘bad’ was necessarily clear, but which instead seemed disquieting. The social imaginations of design noir often seem very realistic in some dimensions, and yet in others profoundly unfamiliar and even distasteful.

For example, Elio Caccavale’s 2003 Utility Pets sets out a scenario in which people will own pets which are biologically and genetically suitable to cultivate and grow body parts suitable for transplantation into their owners. This is a social vision which is based on the scientific work of the time, in which scientists were beginning to be able to do this in the laboratory. The best known example was the Vacanti mouse, on the back of which an ear-shaped structure of cartilage was grown which could in theory have been used to replace an actual ear. Caccavale considered how this technology might work in practice and proposed that families might own domestic pigs as pets and grow organs and organ-like materials based on their own family genetics and DNA. Realistically, transplants have a strong likelihood of rejection unless there is a close match, so personalised cultivation may make sense, and pigs are seen as a species physiologically close to human in terms of the material constitution of their muscle and skin. Caccavale set out some of the implications of this vision, how people might keep and live with these utility pets, how they might maintain their pets’ physical and genetic healthiness, since they would be a cultivation of their own future body tissues, and so forth. The ideas were displayed in a typical designerly form, with display panels suitable for an exhibition, explanatory posters and prototype artefacts.

Through the 2000s, these dystopian visions developed into broader frameworks of critical

design (Dunne 2008) and speculative futures (Dunne and Raby 2013).

This corpus of work takes a very different stance on futures. Rather than undertaking the work of outlining the future as a subject, it frames futures as a way of knowing, a vehicle or space for asking ‘what if…?’ This stance is intrinsically connected to the politics of an open society, one which debates and argues and is socially capable of producing environments of optimism and pessimism in equal measure. The possibility of progress itself depends on this capacity for argumentation and social critique, the ability to voice what others may find distasteful and to speak against those who would see humanity and design as inevitable improvement:

It is hard to say what today’s dreams are; some say that they have been downgraded to hopes – hope that we will not allow ourselves to become extinct, hope that we can feed the starving, hope that there will be room for us on this tiny planet. There are no more visions. We don’t know how to fix the planet and ensure our survival. We are just hopeful.

(Dunne and Raby 2013: 1)

Speculative futuring and critical design might be called social science. Most practitioners have never studied anthropology, but their work is directed at social commentary and interpretation. Their primary interest is in people, humanity and human culture, and often only secondarily in developing new products and services. The core of the work, however, is not social research in the field, but studio work. Ethnography can be involved, but the majority of speculative futuring is inspired by technical developments. In the main, projects conceptualise social contexts as places which are faced with the problem of how to accommodate hypothetical innovations. By contrast, design anthropology more typically involves the opposite process, by which professional design environments are forced to accommodate the priorities, motivations and futures as envisaged by people in their own lives. Suchman (2011) refers to this type of critique as ‘friction’. For her, social critique and interpretation should ideally be based first and foremost on social observation.

In this regard, the physical and spatial vehicles of the various forms of critical design are crucial. The more artistic end of the spectrum, as represented by practitioners such as Dunne and Raby, works by exhibiting to audiences in galleries. The point of engagement with an audience then happens in a carefully considered environment. The advantage of this is that galleries and exhibitionary spaces act to create spaces of consideration and interpretation, which some might call ‘third spaces’. There is a defined and designated threshold beyond which we know that we have entered a zone of conceptualisation, which legitimises speculation and alternative thinking. Yet there are many ways of framing this sort of work and finding audiences.

In work at Aarhus University, the SPIRE Research network (2008–13) explored a range of participatory innovation approaches and considered frameworks for both research and design engagements. SPIRE explored, among other things, the use of performative methods to help imagine and define design spaces and concepts. Following a phase of ethnographic observation and participation, designers might develop social scenarios which imitated design inputs and changes. Key informants might then act out such simulations within their workplaces and homes, sometimes using objects which were simulations or prototypes. For example, when exploring battery recycling methods, a team might use a cardboard box in a shop, to help the shop owner envisage and act through the process, build an idea of its potential within the context and business model of the shop. The project employed a theatre group for the performances, who worked alongside of six different disciplinary teams.

When we think about the three examples I have given (the Center for Genomic Gastronomy,

Utility Pets and the use of performances by SPIRE), we can see that there are many different ways of developing critical and conceptual design. All of these, to return to the key theme of this chapter, illustrate acts of manifesting possible futures by using particular designerly objects. In each case, we can see that the point of engagement brings together social critique with a sense of a temporal gradient, those two elements being convergent in design. The notion and experience of the meal, the exhibitionary space and the notion of performance, each of these is a device intended to do the work of design thinking, giving licence in different ways for people engaging with them to think and experience outside of the immediate present. Conceptual designers pay a great deal of attention to the material qualities involved, which are ways of managing the friction involved. An appropriate metaphor might be the use of gears in a car, where you need to find the appropriate gear in order to achieve a sense of traction between how people understand design and the potential of futured objects. The Center for Genomic Gastronomy must manage to set out flavours, tastes and sensations which will work in just the right uneasy fashion. Gallery-based critical design work meanwhile tends to use an artistic sensory language of form, light and colour, communicating a unified aesthetic appropriate for a gallery audience who are placed in the position of viewers and thinkers. By contrast, the use of prototypes and simulations in usercentred design performances often tends to be much more everyday. Cardboard boxes, polystyrene shapes and rough everyday things invite participants to make use of them. In contextual design performances, participants should feel they can actively possess these things and establish the possibility of ownership. Dawdy (2016) writes about the moments when sensorial qualities evoke temporal frames as ‘patina’, the particularities of ageing surfaces, and conceptual design’s many devices for engagement are patinas in this sense, managing both social and temporal engagements.

## Their time

So far, I have argued in this chapter that there has been a relatively recent turn to ‘futures’, not exclusively in design but as a general condition in society, and that one of the key ways in which people work with temporality and futures is through material objects. Among the stances which design anthropology might take to futures are the direct approach of proffering a vision of a better social future or using futuring as a mode of social and cultural critique by opening up an alternative space for debate. Both of these stances have advantages and problems. The third stance, more traditionally anthropological, and also problematic, is that design anthropologists work to ‘ground’ both visions of change and critical commentaries in observations of contemporary life. As we have just seen, although critical design has given licence to anthropological voices, a lot of the work is not founded in direct social observation but technical possibility. What design anthropologists can provide is a sense of the infinitely varied cultures of temporality which exist among the people and communities where design finds its raison d’etre, a sense of ‘their time’.

A brief diversion will illustrate the importance of appreciating cultures of temporality and futuring. In his autobiography, the poet Lemn Sissay reflects on a moment when, as a child who lived at the time in various foster and care homes, he went on a trip to the theatre:

Memories in care are slippery because there’s no one to recall them as the years pass. In a few months I would be in a different home with a different set of people who had no idea of this moment. How could it matter if no one recalls it? Given that staff don’t take photographs, it was impossible to take something away as a memory. This is how you become invisible. It is the underlying unkindness that you don’t matter enough. This is how you quietly deplete the sense of self-worth deep inside a child’s psyche.

(Sissay 2019)

This recollection contrasts radically with the people we met in Chapter 5, during my HP Labs work on memory and remembering. Our informants there worked intensively to remember, and by remembering to manifest their good intentions for the future of their relationships. In most of the homes we entered, one person (usually a woman) took on the role of being the ‘architect’ of family memory. These women photographed, documented, albumed, printed, framed, sent, narrated and performed a hundred other forms of work with visual and sound media to maintain the structures of remembering. Sissay is reflecting on the way that there are not only such things as media memory, and photographs, but there is also a social infrastructure which organises a sense of temporality for the future, appreciating and manifesting each person as they will exist in future. While it might be too much to say that time has structure, time is given form by culture, not only content.

It is contingent upon design anthropologists to work to convey the forms of temporality in peoples’ lives, and by doing so to attempt a mutually inclusive articulation with the temporal structures of professional design (Prendiville 2015). Design needs to be included in everyday life, and vice versa. In this way, as well as predicting and critiquing, design anthropologists may provide an understanding of the temporal conditions for genuine success.

Anthropological approaches to time are long-standing, but, in recent years, have been increasingly concerned with how temporality can also be about inequality (e.g. Bear 2016; Gell 1992; Munn 1992). If we look back to the mid-20th century, anthropology had a reputation for under-representing peoples’ capacity to change and develop. Levi-Strauss’ (1970: 24) description of mythology and associated traditions was as a ‘machine suppressing time’. If modern anthropologists were to see their role, like Levi-Strauss, as an exercise in describing how culture structures social life, then they would by definition be opposed to design’s work. Because of this intellectual history, anthropology used to stand accused of participating in the exploitation of various global communities, by representing them as cultures which do not privilege progress, history and change (Wolf 1982; Auge 1998; Fabian 1983). In recent years, there has been more attention paid also to how anthropology understands its own time. Bear (2016) points out how, dating back to classical Greek traditions, anthropologists consider techniques (techne), authoritative knowledges (episteme) and ethics (phronesis) as different kinds of temporality. Today, design anthropology works directly in the wake of these critiques, thinking about culture and society as concepts at the heart of change.

Ladner (2012) considers, for example, the success of the television recording system TiVo, when compared with the idea of WebTV. In the late 1990s–2000s, a range of rival visions of how people might watch television competed with one another, in the form of products and services. After the launch in the late 1990s of the set-top box TiVo, the practice of recording television programmes on to a hard drive became a domestic standard in many countries by 2010, after which various forms of cloud-based television systems began to work in parallel with it. By contrast, WebTV envisaged televisions as Internet devices, through which one might perform multiple web-based activities, including watching TV or purchasing products. Ladner argues that a key reason why TiVo beat WebTV hands-down during this decade was that it fundamentally appreciated peoples’ own temporal rhythms. Traditional TV offered a timescape in which you had to suit the broadcaster’s schedule and work within that – even using a video VHS recorder meant having to programme it to record at a certain time. TiVo liberated people from that schedule in certain ways while retaining a sense of structure. It maintained television as a separate domain, where one devotes time to entertainment and to watching, unlike WebTV, which intermixed multiple functions in one device and hence did not actually respect peoples’ ability to not only control but also to structure their timescapes. ‘Successful products do not disturb an existing timescape, but rather fit within it’ (Ladner 2012: 32).

Ladner is arguing that an understanding of temporality is crucial to developing successful products and services, and that ethnography is one of the best methodological frameworks to do this. While digitised ‘big data’, with its ubiquitous timestamps and scientific measurements of time, can produce a profusion of apparent ‘facts’ about how practices are situated in time (Prendiville 2015), it cannot provide you with an understanding of the ‘timescape’ according to people themselves. Timescapes can be understood as subjective frameworks and understandings of time (Adam 1990). With regard to engagements with a product or service, a timescape has three particular dimensions: the time of use (when activity happens and for how long), the timing of use (how the time synchronises with other activities) and the tempo (the sense of rhythm and pace). Unless we understand the relativism of timescapes, happening in and around certain contexts and things, we will fall into the trap of ‘big data’, in which the measurement of time is unidimensional. ‘Temporal confusion is perception that “all time is equal”, when it clearly is not’ (Ladner 2012: 35). I will return to this point about the equality of time in the next chapter.

Design anthropologists are often called upon to produce timescapes. Product and service designers, and especially those who are involved in engineering and technology, hope that a social researcher will provide them with a ‘model’ of time as it exists for ‘their’ users. One can, for example, focus on ‘routines’ and the patterning of practices. One can consider the material culture through which temporality is organised and controlled, such as clocks and timepieces. One can develop mappings of different timescales with informants or examine pasts and futures. One can examine the language through which time is talked of, packaged, negotiated, disputed and controlled. These models of time are very relativistic and amount to specific creations for particular purposes. When you consider that a ‘user group’ could mean ‘homes in France’, a company, people who play a computer game, or ‘Japanese teenagers’, it shows just how specific to the work a timescape or model can be.

Many anthropologists access cultures of temporality through metaphor, which can mean words or material things (Tilley 2000). Temporalities can be progressive and linear, in which moments follow on from one another. Others can be circular and repetitive, following the rhythms of annual or daily routines, and in those conceptions or representations we witness ‘returning’ to the same moment. There are potentially infinite ways in which these kinds of representations can be manifested or set out in ways people find easier to think about time and make temporal connections between people and things. For example, the connection between generations of families can be represented through the growth and ruination of homes; or the mode of propagation of key crops, such as rice or manioc, can become a central organising metaphor. Time may in different cultures be represented through trees, with roots, or leaves, presenting pasts or futures. Historicity unfolds in different ways. In much of Europe, people in the present are understood as being at the ‘forefront’ of history, with ancestors ‘behind’ us; but, in some cultural contexts, the inverse applies, and people in the present are conceived as following on from a long line of precedent ancestors. Such metaphorical forms and understandings present us with extraordinary variety and intellectual creativity around time.

Designed things, because they are explicitly temporal and future-oriented, are particularly inclined to become recognised as organising metaphors by which people are able to think about their time. By taking this culturally relativistic stance towards them, a design anthropologist can avoid either thinking that new designs directly cause changes in, or simply fit into, pre-existing timescapes. Designed things should be, to paraphrase Levi-Strauss, ‘good for thinking about time’.

A further example of the importance of temporality can be illustrated with respect to the Paper++ project (Luff et al 2007). Paper++ was a research project I worked on in the early 2000s funded by the European Union’s FP4 and headed by a range of chief investigators at King’s College London, HP Labs, Arjowiggins and ETH Zurich. The broad purpose of the work was to explore a range of possibilities for paper-like digital interfaces. In other words, rather than use digital technologies to develop ‘paperless’ workplaces, this project envisaged alternative ways of materialising digital information, on paper-like surfaces. In terms of social research, what was necessary at the time was to understand work processes, routines, purposes, contexts and uses, and to connect these with a range of possible technologies which might be called ‘digital paper’. While the idea of ‘digital paper’ may sound concise and simple, it actually conceals a thousand possibilities: screens, plastics, fabrics and papers; moving or static information; artefacts which store memory or link to it in the cloud and use traditional pens and pencils, or require wholly new ways of writing.

The Paper++ team at HP Labs (including Abi Sellen, Guy Adams and Rachel Murphy) produced a simulation of an educational product, using (with permission) coursework from an Open University course. I was responsible for finding out how students might learn using these materials, either using traditional media or digital paper media. The analogue coursework comprised a book, a VHS video cassette and a DVD of interactive media for a computer. The prototype digital paper translated the identical learning materials into a paper interface using Anoto technology, and an early tablet touchscreen computer made by Microsoft (this was before the launch of the more successful iPad brand). The technical affordances of the digital paper were, for the time, highly innovative. Using a special pen, a student could touch an icon printed in their course booklet, and trigger a video on the screen, and graphs in the booklet linked to interactive versions on-screen in which you could play around more with the data. These technologies envisaged very new modes of interacting with computers, at a time when most interaction was through the keyboard and mouse, with a few touchscreens, but nobody was engaging with computers directly through printed texts.

I recruited a number of science students to test these out, some of whom had the analogue materials and the others the new ones. In this project, the budget and time did not stretch to doing any kind of extended participatory work in peoples’ own lives, it was exclusively lab based. In that sense, it was neither anthropological nor ethnographic, but it was user testing based on ideas from HCI and psychology, undertaken by an anthropologist. We used the approaches appropriate for the job. The students would come into HP Labs in order to try the materials out, and I would video their sessions and interview them about the experience. As an anthropologist, I would also ask about how they normally worked and studied, what materials they normally used, what lay behind their interest and try to get as much information as I could about the context of their learning and what sorts of people they were.

This small piece of work produced a lot of information about time, and different elements informed technical, psychological, designerly and social dimensions. Among other things, I would characterise it as an exploration of the meeting of timescapes: the personal timescapes of individual students, within the overarching timescape of the laboratory (what Suchman 2011 might call ‘company time’). The students had a range of different ways of approaching their learning exercise. They might read a text, touch on the icon on the paper next to it, view the graphic on the screen, read back and touch again to run the graph. Or, they might read the booklet beginning to end, then watch the relevant parts of the video cassette all in one and then do the exercises. Because we were using video to record this, and the video was also a reference point for the subsequent interviews, every observation had a timecode. The data then intrinsically recorded how much time was spent on different actions or phases, practices became temporal moments, and the act of learning itself appeared as a sequence in time, with some attention given to the order of what happened before or after what else. However, just as Ladner found, it was not the scientifically recorded time which was most insightful. One of the most obvious findings was that the amount of time spent on a particular medium or piece of information (e.g. a text, a graphic, a section of video) is unrelated to which pieces of information a student thinks is most important. Often, a sense of learning and understanding happens in flashes or moments, not in blocks of time. The sense of time passing experienced by a person doing a learning task is often experienced in the form of stages, moments or significant moments of movement from one stage to another, while an observer, by contrast, observes the stretches of time, which can be measured in units of time like seconds or minutes. That point – learning does not translate into amounts of time – is one which is obvious to anybody who thinks about study. Yet it is an observation which can be forgotten nonetheless by people who have to work hard to get products working in a technical sense.

Amazingly, although at the time I found our digital paper to be technically extraordinary and groundbreaking, something which did not yet exist in peoples’ homes, it was nothing special for the students. They simply used it and accepted its innovations as ordinary. As an artefact, it made sense to them.

Importantly, the newer digital paper helped students to organise their study. This kind of technology was not one in which people would use digital online resources to ‘explore’ the topic in some kind of blue-skies way. Rather, the paper document provided a fairly solid and reassuring process by which students knew when they had progressed through the exercise and were able to synthesise and digest the key elements fairly quickly, moving back and forth through the paper pages but always using it as a touchstone of how much they had done. While they might not simply move through it beginning to end, they were always able to locate what they were doing within the sequence proposed by the original authors of the textbook. By contrast, students with the analogue materials had the liberty of choosing paper or video or computer and used them in multiple ways and orders, but had to work to create their own connections between the materials and information. The digital paper also resonated well with peoples’ backgrounds. Some study materials envisage that students are people who devote large blocks of time to study, in an organised environment, and that they have themselves decided that they want to study. The people with whom we worked were, however, not exactly like this. Recently out of school, they talked not about the work of studying, but of what kinds of subjects they liked or did not like. Even though they studied science, this was not their dedicated project in life, and they might nonetheless prefer literature or art, so this study exercise did not define them as people. Their study practices were likewise unpredictable. While a few were organised, working at desks for a couple of hours at a time, many seemed to fit study into other aspects of life, however, they might. One woman lived with her parents, and would study at the top of the stairs, sitting on a step and spreading her books and writing about on the upstairs landing. Some had jobs, and would study in intervals of an hour or two around fixed hours of employment and fixed classes. So personal timescapes and routines might emphasise personal preference, or be set by the university, by paid employers or by family commitments. A great deal of this was about what it means ‘to be a student’, not simply what it means ‘to study’.

The Paper++ project shows us how a designed thing, in this case a simulation or prototype, can become the central touchstone of understanding when different timescapes meet. Design culture by definition means a situation where understandings of temporality are changing, and where the envisaging of futures is ‘frictional’ (Suchman 2011). Student life and learning work have, for the past 20–30 years, grappled with the implications of ubiquitous information and distributed student lifestyles which juggle many different locations and projects. Within this situation, a physical artefact can help negotiate a personal time frame of learning through the different contexts and informational sources.

## The problematic alterity of futures

In this chapter, I have been arguing that there are particular ways in which design anthropology should work with concepts of futures and of change. In general, an anthropologist should argue against the idea that futures emerge only from studio work. They should use ethnographic work to redress the power balance by stressing how futures are ubiquitous, in the sense that they do not originate ‘in’ design, laboratory, and corporate environments. Change and futures happen without design. Yet design is a great catalyst, and the most important way of thinking about change is in the cultural dialogues between professional and everyday environments, which is much more likely to result in enduring change. Material culture is often the primary vehicle to access cultures of futures and change, because material things manifest a temporally uneven field open for people to work with and think about time. Things and materials such as buildings, spectacles, post-its, meals and a million other things provide some imperfect means for humans and their actions to connect across time. Diverse material cultures connect across long- and short-term periods of time, both across pasts and futures.

The work of Deleuze (1994 [1968]) has been fundamental to dismantling modernist notions of time as linear, revealing the variability of temporality and exposing the various modes of human orientation to the future. Deleuze’s work on this topic is itself in some ways opaque, and his interpreters are more clear (Williams 2011; Baross 2011; Voss 2013). The fragmented present in this interpretation comprises in, among other things, a prehistory of the future, more than a distinct moment per se. In anthropology, the work of Wallman (1992) first took futures seriously in anthropology, pointing out that the future emerges at certain key moments in social life and human interactions, and that the notion of the future does certain kinds of cultural work. Harding and Rosenberg (2005) then showed how this notion of futures as culturally ubiquitous could be explored best through an attention to material culture, something which generations of philosophers have reflected upon: ‘Time… thickens, takes on flesh, becomes artistically visible; likewise, space becomes charged and responsive to the movements of time, plot, and history (Bakhtin 1981: 84).

To conclude the chapter, I want to return to the example of the Bauhaus. What it shows is that, rather than thinking about ‘made’ futures, manifested in the products of studios and labs, anthropologists can think about ‘found’ futures using the concept of alterity. In other words, futures are unexpectedly discovered and confronted as problematic, material-temporal inconsistencies in the world ‘out there’, for ethnographers to interpret and use to create critical dialogues.

In 2015, the Bauhaus Dessau Foundation hosted the exhibition ‘On the Art of Housekeeping in the Twenty-First Century’. The exhibition celebrated the reopening of the Meisterhauser (Masters’ Houses), which in the 1920s had acted as showhouses for Bauhaus designs, by revisiting some of the questions which those 1920s designers had been asking. As design anthropologists, myself, Robert Knowles, Anais Bloch and Isabel Bredenbröker (see Drazin et al 2016; Bredenbröker et al 2016) developed an exhibit based on ethnographic observations in Dessau to explore the theme of cleaning. This work then was not aimed at designing a product, service or architecture, it was design anthropology because it aimed to think through some of the cultural issues of designing with future households in mind.

As we saw earlier, the name of Dessau is synonymous with the birth of modernist design. The 1927 film ‘How We Live Now’, which presented and advertised the products, aesthetics and ways of living as future ways of living, was filmed in the Meisterhauser, which have now been declared UNESCO monuments for world heritage. In some senses, the future started here. Dessau today is, however, a city facing decline, in spite of its heritage and vibrant architecture school. Since the reunification of Germany after 1989, Dessau’s larger factories closed and there has been a huge exodus of the younger generation. Parts of the city, once vibrant, seem empty and hollowed out. It is also a tourist attraction for people interested in design and architecture. For example, there are regular tours of the Törten housing estate, designed by Walter Gropius. Small groups of tourists wander along the quiet streets, looking at terraces of square houses with roses in the front gardens, while residents wash their cars. The tourists look at window frames and front doors which in form could be anywhere on earth, but which existed here for the first time. Public buildings across the world, as well as windows, doorways, handles, light switches, chairs, plumbing pipes and other accoutrements, in every country on earth are echoes of the things found at these sites in Dessau. At the time they were made, however, they seemed extraordinarily new, unlike anything that had come before (although in fact there had been forerunners). The light concrete and glass surfaces of these buildings appeared in the 1920s as illuminated visions. The lasting global dominance of these designs testifies to Dessau’s past success.

Our work focussed on the sites of the exhibition, the Bauhaus buildings and the Meisterhauser themselves. In separate visits, we spent days in these sites, working out what was going on routinely, as well as interviewing different people interested in them. What our work focussed on was acts of cleaning in these heritage sites, and we attempted to illustrate through artistic means the ways in which everybody who engaged with the material forms of the Bauhaus buildings was constantly being confronted with designerly dilemmas about futures. The cleaning of the Bauhaus sites was undertaken daily, very carefully and with the agenda of preserving and curating the valuable heritage of the place. This was work which people in Dessau undertook with a sense of pride and care. The cleaners talked about the amounts of labour, of water, of cleaning chemicals which they used on 1920s floors and walls. They talked about the details and the care required. They talked about the sense of judgement with which they had to approach dirt, spillages and surfaces. As heritage buildings, some of the sites are actively crumbling, and one must evaluate whether the dust you can see is something which should be removed or preserved.

The issues faced by the cleaners are paralleled by the people working in, and working on, the Bauhaus sites. The interesting thing about the Bauhaus is that it is both design heritage and a site of design practice, so there are staff working within a protected heritage monument. This means that one cannot install things like modern cables and Wi-Fi without special clearance from heritage curators. It is forbidden to stick things on walls, because one could actively remove the surface with Blu-Tack or other adhesives. So people working ‘in’ Bauhaus sites have to balance their roles as workers or curators. This also applies to the professional design historians working ‘on’ Bauhaus material heritage. In the storage warehouses and collections, there are stacks of the 1920s period doors, radiators, drainpipes, old floor coverings, loose bricks and fragments of mortar. With each object or substance, the curators of the collections juggle the possibilities of preservation against destruction by evaluating whether something like a rusted pipe would benefit from cleaning, or preservation, dirt and all, or should be simply called dirt and thrown out. In this environment, the line between global heritage of eternal value for all humanity, and valueless rubbish, can be very thin.

The most interesting aspect of these issues is that they are not only experienced by the design and curation professionals employed by the Bauhaus institutions but also reflect major concerns across the city. The material treatment of Bauhaus ‘stuff’ is of deep and lasting concern to everybody in Dessau and beyond. Citizens from across the city participate by contributing the original fixtures from their homes, many of which were constructed in the 1920s period. Many people in the city do not throw things out so much as contribute to the archive. The feeling of tension between nostalgia and alternative futures is not specific to Dessau but reported in different ways across the post-socialist region: Øye (2007: 112), writing with affection about concrete buildings in Eastern Germany, characterises this as a ‘feeling for gray’.

More importantly, at a policy level, the imagined future of Dessau has at times been directly linked to questions of what to do with the material architecture of the Bauhaus building itself and the Meisterhauser. Through the late 1990s and into the 2000s, there were major debates, ongoing for many years, about what was to happen on the Dessau sites. Among the competing visions for how to reinvigorate this city, two stood out. The Bauhaus could be reopened as a design school or centre of design practice. Its name would attract major designers, and the work could directly feed into German manufacturing industry, hypothetically bringing some much-needed economic reinvigoration to the area. Alternatively, the second vision would restore and preserve the sites as monuments to the 1920s glory days. They would not attract practising designers, but nostalgic design tourists. And yet, as preserved sites, the Bauhaus buildings would act as a symbolic inspiration for a brighter future, by reflecting what can be achieved. The debate between these two visions of the future, in practice, was often acted out through specific debates about what to do with the physical sites, the Bauhaus building and the Meisterhauser. Alternative future possibilities were negotiated through an exploration of the material culture of the past, and that kind of negotiation was reflected at every level of society in different ways, from daily cleaners, through designers, curators, municipal policymakers, national ministries and right up to the global level of UNESCO departments.

In our proposed exhibition, we tried to engage a design audience in these issues. We broadcast the wet sounds of cleaning in one of the basement rooms so that standing in the empty space, people might begin to appreciate how the concrete surfaces around them had effectively been produced in their current form by hundreds of acts of repeated careful cleaning. That installation tried to make the everyday labour of heritage visible and present cleaning as potentially the work of heritage production and value production. We had recorded the wet, sloppy mopping and wiping and spraying sounds which were unmistakeable. Every listener could identify with the sounds of cleaning and re-enact them if they wished.

We also collected dirt from around the Bauhaus sites and, in the second room, installed a mock ‘auction’ of Bauhaus dirt. The premise behind this auction was that one might produce a sense of authenticity and value by buying and scattering Bauhaus dirt in your own home. Small posters and advertisements explained the value of genuine, certified Bauhaus schmutz. We wished to put visitors to the exhibit in a position where they were forced into considering what they perceived as valuable heritage, and what they perceived as zero-value dirt.

Dessau, while an extraordinary and lovely city in many ways, is also very ordinary, a city which embodies an aspiration to be the new ‘normal’. It provides a very good example of a place where engaging with futures means a complicated constant confrontation with the relics of every successive generation’s own vision of the future. The archaeology of futures in Dessau bridges many historical periods and sets of political aspiration, from the medieval period around the cobbled central square, through the 19th-century imperial period districts, the modernist 1920s, the fascist 1930s, the communist period and the post-socialist. The patchworked architectures manifest each new generation’s new kind of social optimism and aspiration, for civilisation, for technical development, for freedom, for equality or for any number of other futures. Futures in Dessau involve what I call a ‘problematic alterity’. Futures are not so much made from ‘within the imaginations of designers, as discovered, confronted and negotiated around material objects and materials.

‘Alterity’ here means an encounter with extreme cultural otherness, as we saw in the ‘Introduction’. It is a concept which is fundamental not only to anthropological analysis but also to anthropological knowledge production. Ethnography involves an encounter with cultural difference. The longest standing use of notions of alterity and otherness has examined ethnicity and identity. Anthropologists have observed how many communities develop ideas which are founded upon the notion of being different from specific other people (see Chua and Mathur 2018). In this instance, alterity refers to people. Taussig (1991) extended notions of alterity to refer to material things, envisaging a strongly politicised global field of identity in which commodities and material resources create connections between radically unequal groups in the world. Alterity and otherness in recent years have come to refer not only to observable social moments, like encounters between people, or people and things, but also to more profound moments of knowledge creation. A situation of alterity recognises the coexistence of alternative social truths or worlds (Treanor 2005; Leistle 2016). When cultural worlds collide, there is the experience that one cannot really hope to appreciate exactly how somebody else understands the world. One only knows that it is different. Studies of alterity explore a range of often difficult and problematic cultural relationships: disgust, exoticism and desire. Alterity is provocative, confrontational, and often seems to produce a situation which needs to be somehow resolved.

Alterity can be understood to refer to not only different cultural truths in the present, but to different kinds of temporality. Knauft (2002) examines multiple forms of modernity as the key manifestation of alterity in the world today. When it comes to futuring, what notions of otherness can teach us is how change is not linear and unitary. Neither past, nor present, nor future should be considered as moments which make sense in and of themselves, they are all heterogeneous. So understanding the relation of design to change does not mean simply designing as a mode of originating new concepts and things. Rather, designing works with multiple radically ‘other’ time frames and generally pre-existent futures.

A great deal of design work still sees itself as existing in a time frame which originated in the 1920s. Modernism postulated a focus on a contemporary moment of human existence and lifestyle, and by focussing on the contemporary, it created a sense of ‘future’ and of ‘past’, as different from the present and from one another. To put this another way, modernist thinking emphasised the way in which time is a prime mode of cultural meaning because it becomes an axis of similarity between two things. Because modernism emphasised ‘the moment’, rather than broader, looser conceptions of temporality, moments then come to be a point of connection. This time frame then affects what we see as ‘progress’, ‘innovation’, ‘newness’ and so forth. Where time can be measured and pinned down, acting as a consistent and factual background against which designed things can emerge, our capacity to recognise what is innovative, unique or different happens against a presumption that the future is different from the present. These issues will be the focus of the following chapter.

## Summary

One of the most important things a design anthropologist can deliver is a better understanding of appropriate change. This chapter has argued that futures often seem discovered, not made. Material culture is how people think about and work with futures. However, materialised futures can appear as if they are alien, belong to different people, and unnervingly ubiquitous. This is what characterises what I have called the ‘problematic alterity’ of futures.

The idea that the future is unknown is not unique to design. All humans live with no absolute knowledge of what tomorrow may bring, and for personal and social life to happen at all, it is necessary to assume that tomorrow is largely known.

In recent years, there has been a distinct politico-economic ‘turn to the future’, in which many dimensions of life borrow against future possibility and certainty. One of design’s key projects is ‘refuturing’ in a situation of ‘defuturing’ (Fry 1999, 2008). Anthropology is one of the disciplines which companies and governments turn to inform them in this situation.

The underlying presumption of design anthropology that a project can affect the lives of informants, or people like them, is methodologically problematic. Change and futures need very careful handling in order to be ethically appropriate. Hence design anthropological work is not as simple an exercise as developing an innovation or ‘making a change’ (for the better). It is rather an embedded, informed way of interpreting and working with time.

Perspectives on change are especially infused with senses of value. As explored in the previous chapter, conceptions of value are strongly related to how people rationalise their work and actions with respect to futures. A good design is often not only usable, but fits with peoples’ ideas of what is socially generative or creative.

Design anthropologists have a range of possible approaches to temporality. One is to search for manifest futures, possibilities, plans, desires and needs, for example expressed in material things. The second approach is to produce a broader model or interpretation of informants’ understandings of temporality in the field site. What are the important routines, social rhythms? Do informants aspire to a sense of sudden change in their lives, and to what purpose? What are the tensions, contradictions and frictions which exist within the field site? The third approach is a frictional one, which develops these understandings as a way in which to challenge and critique the sense of temporality within corporate and institutional design cultures.
