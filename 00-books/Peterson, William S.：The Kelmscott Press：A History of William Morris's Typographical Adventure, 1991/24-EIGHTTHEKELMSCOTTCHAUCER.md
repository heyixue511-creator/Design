One of the earliest references to the book (in the Athenaeum, 22 October 1892) struck the note of admiration bordering on adoration that recurs in nearly all future allusions to it: ‘Lovers of Chaucer on both sides of the Atlantic will rejoice to hear that [Edward Burne-Jones] has made very great progress with a series of designs, fifty or sixty in all, which are to be cut in wood under his own superintendence, and intended to illustrate the “Canterbury Tales" and the other poems of Chaucer. These designs promise to be charmingly graceful and beautiful in execution. Indeed, we are confident that since, precisely a hundred years ago, Flaxman . .. designed for the first Countess Spencer the famous "Illustrations to the Tragedies of AEschylus," poet and artist have never been better fitted to each other. The text to which these designs are adapted will be collated with the best manuscripts and carefully edited by Mr. W. Morris and Mr. F. S. Ellis. The typography will be worthy of the occasion, and the volume a stately quarto [sic].' In 1892 The Works of Geoffrey Chaucer was still in embryo, but as its birth neared there was widespread gossip about the astonishing folio in production at the Kelmscott Press. A New York periodical, the Book Buyer (18 November 1895), published a facsimile of the first page of The Canterbury Tales and declared that the Chaucer will be without question the grandest book of the century'; in 1895 some sheets of the Chaucer were shown at the Arts and Crafts Exhibition in Dublin, prompting the Studio (January 1896) to describe the book, not yet published, as holding the place of honour among the other productions of this press'. Morris's associates did not hesitate to bestow superlatives upon it. 'It's the finest book ever printed and it's more than likely there'll never be such another,' Burne-Jones said. Ellis argued that ‘for typography, ornament and illustrations combined', the Chaucer was the grandest book that has issued from the press since the invention of typography'.' Many outside the Morris circle have repeated this extravagant language: a reviewer in the Library (1896), for example, described it as 'the finest book ever printed', and the Athenaeum (3 October 1896) called the Chaucer the crowning achievement of the Kelmscott Press'. Even in the second half of the twentieth century there is still a tendency to abandon all rhetorical restraint when discussing the Kelmscott Chaucer, as evidenced by this passage from an American library exhibition catalogue of 1976: ‘This is one of the two greatest ikons of the bibliographic faith, the other being the Gutenberg Bible. As the cross of the Christian community proclains by its very presence the gospel of Christ, so the Kelmscott Chaucer on vellum stands surrogate for all that was important in the book world before it was completed in 1 896. . . . While there have been many litanies of praise heaped upon William Morris, and upon the Kelmscott Chaucer on vellum in particular, none of them have been adequate. Not until one has had the opportunity to go through a copy does the full majesty of the Kelmscott accomplishment appear. . . . The ultimate limitations of words are reached when one tries to describe the Kelmscott Chaucer on vellum." And so on for several more breathless paragraphs. Naturally such ecstatic approbation invites a cynical response, and there have always been some readers willing to argue that the Kelmscott Chaucer is a ponderous, overdecorated non-book which threatens the eyesight and physical safety of anyone foolhardy enough to hold it on his lap (a vellum copy in the pigskin binding weighs more than thirteen pounds). We have seen that the Chaucer is far from being a typical Kelmscott volune, but it is certainly the most celebrated and frequently photographed, and it represents, in a sense, Morris's tendencies in book-design and ornamentation carried to their logical conclusion; thus, rightly or wrongly, the Chaucer has become a litmus test which measures one's response to Morris's work as a printer. ## That the most ambitious, richly ornamented book of the Kelmscott Press proved to be an edition of Chaucer came as no surprise to any of Morris's admirers, for he and Burne-Jones had counted Chaucer as one of their great discoveries at Oxford, and throughout his life Morris's poetry was constantly being compared by critics with that of his medieval predecessor.3 Though Morris himself protested occasionally against the injustice of the analogy, he had only himself to blame for his identification in the public mind with Chaucer: he had, after all, addressed Chaucer as ‘my Master' in both Book XVII of The Life and Death of Jason and the Envoi' to The Earthly Paradise, and the latter poem was plainly patterned after The Canterbury Tales. Not only did he feel ill-equipped to supply such an introduction, but Morris also objected to Ward's plan to print merely a small selection from Chaucer's works.⁴ Behind the Kelmscott Chaucer lies a long lineage of Chaucer manuscripts and printed editions, many of them illustrated, with which Morris, as à collector and antiquarian, was well acquainted.s The practice of wedding picture with Chaucer's text extends back to the earliest manuscripts, the most celebrated of which is the fifteenthcentury Ellesmere manuscript of The Canterbury Tales (now in the Huntington Library) containing twenty-three portraits of the individual pilgrims, each mounted and placed in the margin near the decorated initial at the beginning of his tale. The tales themselves are not illustrated; this convention, which may seem rather odd to us today, was observed by all illustrators of Chaucer until the late-eighteenth century, and only in the Kelmscott edition is there at last a serious attempt to represent the action of the pilgrim's stories beyond a few simple tableaux. Burne-Jones's departure from this iconographic pattern is so complete, in fact, that he shows us none of the pilgrims except Chaucer himself (and he is not mounted), nor does he draw the pilgrims in procession or at table in the Tabard Inn, two other conventions of Chaucer's early illustrators. The portrait of Chaucer in the Ellesmere manuscript is one of only two presumed to be reliable likenesses; the other is known as the Hoccleve portrait, also found in an early manuscript. A third likeness of Chaucer which has captured the imagination of later generations is an early fifteenth-centry painting of Čhaucer reading his poems to the royal court - a theme that recurs in a mid-Victorian Pre-Raphaelite representation by Ford Madox Brown. It is remarkable that, even though these images of Chaucer had been frequently engraved and were familiar to Victorian readers, Burne-Jones scarcely alludes to them in his illustrations for the Kelmscott Chaucer. His portrait of Chaucer does show a general indebtedness to both the Hoccleve and Ellesmere likenesses; there are broad similarities in pose and costume, the outstretched hand, even the forked beard. Yet Burne-Jones's Chaucer is more gaunt and solitary, more inward-looking (he is reading a book), and more visionary. One notices that by the end of The Canterbury Tales he has aged perceptibly, and by the last page of the volume he looks quite decrepit. John Urry's edition (1721) brings together, for the first time, all the mounted pilgrims and displays them at the moment of their departure from the Tabard. This edition appears to have been a financial and critical failure: readers disliked Urry's textual emendations and — as a symptom of the growing nostalgia for all things medieval – they regretted the abandonment of Gothic for Roman type. (One of the sponsors of the book, Christ Church, Oxford, was able to dispose of its remaining copies only by forcing every undergraduate to buy one when he entered the college.) In Bell's edition there is also an engraving by Thomas Stothard of a small group of pilgrims on horseback, and early in the nineteenth century both Stothard and Blake produced large-scale engravings — not intended as book illustrations — of Chaucer's pilgrimns leaving the Tabard. What is most noticeable about all the eighteenth-century editions of Chaucer is how abruptly they depart from the medieval spirit of the earlier blackletter versions. To read The Canterbury Tales in, for instance, Urry's Roman type, with neo-classical initials and ornaments and an extraordinarily anachronistic Squire, is a very different experience from that of studying a typical sixteenth-century page in Gothic type, illustrated with a woodcut of a pilgrim who is lineally descended from Caxton and the manuscripts before Caxton. Bell's fourteenvolume edition of Chaucer has been praised for the excellence of its typography by Stanley Morison, and it deserves our attention also as the first attempt to illustrate the stories of The Canterbury Tales; but however admirable Bell's printing may be, one must recognize that it was precisely this eighteenth-century rendering of Chaucer — made over into the image of an Augustan gentleman ofimpeccable manners — that Morris attempted to obliterate at the Kelmscott Press. Morris's aim was to recapture, without direct copying, the simplicity and vitality of fifteenth- and sixteenth-century editions of Chaucer that were closer in time and spirit to Chaucer's own age. Morris believed that only by peeling off the Renaissance and neo-classical layers of cultural interpretation could he recover something like the Chaucer of the Middle Ages, and this involved both a careful restoration of Chaucer's text (including his spelling) and a return to a more medieval style of typography and ornamentation. The Kelmscott Chaucer is in fact the splendid culmination of the Victorian rediscovery of Chaucer and of early English literature in general. The person who was largely responsible for this revival was Morris's friend F. J. Furnivall, who founded, among others, the Early English Text Society (1864), the Ballad Society (1868), the New Shakspere Society (1873), and the Wyclif Society (1882). Furnivall created the Chaucer Society in 1868 in order (according to its prospectus) to do honour to Chaucer, and to let the lovers and students of him see how far the best unprinted manuscripts of his works differ from the printed texts'. The Chaucer Society was never a large organization — Furnivall frequently complained of its meagre membership in the annual reports, and in 19oo Ellis observed sarcastically that by then only sixteen private individuals belonged, about one in every two million and a quarter of the population of the British Isles" — and it was disbanded shortly after Furnivall's death in 191o. Nevertheless, during the four decades ofits existence the Society laid the foundations of modern Chaucer scholarship by publishing early manuscript versions of Chaucer's works and other primary materials, mostly edited by Furnivall himself, a man of unflagging energy. For one of these publications, a Chaucerian manuscript in the Cambridge University Library, W. H. Hooper, who was to engrave Burne-Jones's designs at the Kelmscott Press, made facsimile engravings of the original illustrations.7 The most important achievement of the Society, issued in instalments over several years from 1868 onwards, was Furnivall's parallel-text edition of the six Chaucer manuscripts judged to be the most trustworthy. Like Morris, Furnivall believed passionately in the importance of digging down through the centuries of accumulated debris in order to find Chaucer's actual words rather than the glosses and conjectural emendations of later editors; like Morris, too, he took a relatively uncomplicated view of Chaucer, emphasizing what he called the most genial and humourful healthysouled man that England had ever seen'.8 Morris, of course, never remoulded Chaucer into a Muscular Christian as Furnivall did, but both men tended to ignore Chaucer the subtle Italianate poet, sometimes introspective and melancholy, seeing him instead as a cheerful realist whose value to modern readers lay chiefly in his portrayal of medieval life and manners. During the 186os the University Presses of both Oxford and Cambridge were laying plans for scholarly editions of Chaucer, and, although Furnivall hoped that the former might print his six-text version, Oxford decided instead to publish a single text based on a collation of all the authoritative manuscripts, to be edited by the Revd Walter W. Skeat, Professor of Anglo-Saxon at Cambridge. Skeat was by no means an ideal editor: following the example of Furnivall (who was in many respects his mentor),º Skeat constructed a text based on intuitive, eclectic principles and drawn exclusively from Furnivall's six-text edition. Both Ellis and Burne-Jones commented on the donnish humourlessness of Skeat's notes. Every student of Chaucer must be grateful for the labour and learning bestowed on his works by Professor Skeat, but to be a parson without any sense of humour is not an advantage for an editor of Chaucer,’ Ellis remarked wryly. Burne-Jones said to Sebastian Evans, Skeat is a bore, I must say. He never edits a poem without saying it's dull and unimportant and long-winded. How does he know and what business is that of his — if it's unimportant what does he edit it for? He is himself always long-winded.° Yet Skeat's monumental six-volume edition of Chaucer — published under Oxford's Clarendon Press imprint in 1894, with a supplementary volume issued in 1897 - seemed a godsend to Ellis and Morris, for it was incomparably superior to all previous editions and relieved Ellis of the heavy burden of constructing his own text for the Kelmscott version. It was a staggering accomplishment, and it took its toll on Morris's health. By the spring of 1896 Burne-Jones was terrified that Morris might die before he could complete the engraved title-page: the Chaucer title page is done at last,' Burne-Jones commented on 28 March. ‘So that's all safe now, and there's nothing but the printing’ (Rooke diary). Printing of the first sheet of the Chaucer started on 8 August 1894: BEGAN PRINTING THE CHAUCER,’Cockerell wrote in his diary in uncharacteristic capitals. Morris also recognized the momentousness of the occasion: we printed the Ist side of the Chaucer on vellum; successfully, I think, after some trouble,’ he reported to Jenny.2 Towards the end of 1894 Morris acquired another Albion press — this one very large and reinforced with steel bands in order to withstand the additional pressure of printing his large borders - and rented Sunderland Cottage, 21 Upper Mall, to hasten the progress of the book. ‘Morris is very busy,'Charles Fairfax Murray wrote in November. ‘He is just setting up a new press to print the Chaucer faster, which he calculated would [take] him otherwise 2 years to print."6 During the spring and summer of 1895 the book fell several months further behind schedule when yellow stains began to appear on some of the sheets already printed (see Chapter Four). But Walker was undoubtedly correct in his assertion that Burne-Jones illustrated those stories most abundantly which appealed to his own taste. It was Chaucer the poet of courtly love and sophisticated teller of French and Italianate tales who most interested Burne-Jones (not the sturdy medieval bard that Morris found in The Canterbury Tales), and it was accordingly the stories of the Knight, the Clerk of Oxenford, and the Franklin which attracted him; the tales of bawdy realism he passed over in silence. I have abstained from decorating certain of the Canterbury Tales,' he wrote to Swinburne.. . . Morris has been urgent with me that I should by no means exclude these stories from our scheme of adornment - especially he had hopes of my treatment of the Miller's Tale, but he ever had more robust and daring parts than I could assume.'33 When his studio assistant congratulated him for ignoring Chaucer's 'base stories', Burne-Jones replied, 'I'd like to pretend Chaucer didn't do them. Besides, pictures to them would have spoiled the book. You don't want funny pictures either. Pictures are too good to be funny. Literature's good enough for that.'⁴ In private Burne-Jones did have a taste for ribald comedy: his notebooks and letters are filled with lively caricatures, mainly of emaciated, terrified men threatened by grotesquely obese women. But his public art was reserved for high seriousness, which means that the Chaucer is populated with the usual mysterious human figures — willowy and graceful, intense but expressionless - that are so familiar from his paintings. It is certainly not true, as one recent commentator has claimed, that Burne-Jones ruined’ the Kelmscott Chaucer by this partisan approach to illustrating it,³ yet it would be fair to say that his wood-engravings, by ignoring Chaucer's humour and irony, convey a very incomplete interpretation of the poetry. Because the older English woodcut illustrations of Chaucer held no interest for him, Burne-Jones was compelled to turn to other visual sources. We are told that he and Morris regularly pored over illuminated manuscripts at both the Bodleian Library and the British Museum, and an exhaustive study of the manuscripts he could have seen there would probably turn up a few miniatures from which he borrowed or adapted some details in his Chaucer illustrations. There is (to choose a more remote example) a miniature in a manuscript of Boccaccio's Teseida which is remarkably similar to the wood-engraving which appears on page 9 of the Chaucer to illustrate the Knight's Tale: it is not clear whether or how he saw this particular manuscript (which is now in the national library of Austria), but probably the miniature represents an iconographic tradition which Burne-Jones encountered in other manuscripts as well.³⁶ He was also able to draw upon his own earlier work. In 1857 Burne-Jones painted scenes from the Prioress's tale on a wardrobe as a wedding gift to the Morrises, of which the left panel is almost identical to the illustration on page 6o of the Kelmscott Chaucer. (Another version of this scene appears in the last work Burne-Jones completed, a water-colour exhibited at the New Gallery in 1898.) On the right panel of the wardrobe Chaucer himself is portrayed, looking very much like the Ellesmere miniature and not yet as gaunt and melancholy as he was to become in 1896. Chaucer also reappears in a series of cartoons Burne-Jones did for Ruskin in 1864 to illustrate The Legend of Goode Wimmen; Ruskin intended that the girls at Winnington Hall should embroider a set of hangings under Georgiana Burne-Jones's supervision, but the scheme came to nought, and a water-colour of 1871 — on which the Kelmscott illustration on page 416 is based – was the only tangible result of the aborted project. Burne-Jones insisted that he was trying to see Chaucer's poetry afresh, not merely through the eyes of earlier illustrators, and this often led to a peculiar sort of literalism in his interpretation of the text: the example often noticed by readers of the Chaucer is the extraordinary image of the House ‘made of twigges' as a large wicker basket in The Hous of Fame. Such an approach was not always easy for the illustrator. Burne-Jones began the Chaucer illustrations in late 1892 or very early 1893, usually working on the designs on Sunday when Morris came to his studio to talk and read aloud. As the task drew to a close, he was able to look back on it with satisfaction: ‘And I have been calculating that . . . the time I have given to the Chaucer work in the past two years and a half is exactly to an hour the time I should have spent in visits from Saturday till Monday at "houses," if I had been amiable and sociable — for I haven't let it invade the week's work, but have designed only on Sunday with very little exception -. . . . I have fourteen more designs to make and then my share is done, and I may permit myself the luxury of one smile. I have always been happy over it: it has never tired me but refreshed me always.' ,42 Yet this mellow retrospective mood fails to convey the genuine anxiety both he and Morris felt at earlier stages in the book's history. Many of the illustrations had to be redone several times. Burne-Jones said their rule was that in illustrating a story we must always choose the essential incidents',43 but their radically differing conceptions of Chaucer's poetry must have led to some very lively discussions between him and Morris; moreover, the elaborate procedure by which Robert Catterson-Smith translated’ Burne-Jones's delicate pencil strokes into thick, bold lines suitable for engraving (see Chapter Five) was time-consuming and expensive. There were moments when it seemed that the book would never be done. Burne-Jones completed the last designs at the end of December 1895 and wrote one of his delightfully whimsical letters about them: 'I have just finished my Chaucer work and in May I hope the book will see the light. I hope sincerely it will be all the age does not want — I have omitted nothing I could think of to obstruct the onward march of the world. The designs are carved in wood . . . the lines as thick as I could get then. I have done all I can to impede progress — you will always bear me witness that I have not faltered — and that having put my hand to the plough I invariably look back.'44 Burne-Jones received £soo for his Chaucer designs; the full cost of the book was $\begin{array} { r } { \oint _ { \scriptstyle \mathbf { \nu } } 7 , 2 { \mathbf { I } } 7 ~ { \mathrm { ~ I ~ I ~ } } s . } \end{array}$ , an enormous investment (extending over four years) that was a source of worry to Morris, who occasionally used the word ‘panic’ in his diary to describe his response to the multiple problems encountered in its production.
