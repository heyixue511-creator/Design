The Eames House was assembled from standard materials intended for the construction of industrial sheds. The High tech style was born. It integrated the interior with the garden and bathed the interior with natural light. The Eameses catholic collection of artifacts personalized the space, suggesting that the empty shell of a building benefits from the humanizing influence of its users. After World War II, the priority among Europeans was to rebuild acceptable lifestyles and they had the example of the United States as their target. They did not necessarily wish to mimic it but to equal it—and those European countries that were not part of the Communist bloc would achieve broad parity with it and each other. Britain, linked to the United States as wartime allies and via a common language was most prone to absorb and recycle its popular culture. But twenty years after the war, suddenly, unaccountably, with its pop music and its street fashion, Britain would redefine popular culture in Europe and the United States. ## UNITED STATES In The Life and Times of the Thunderbolt Kid (2006) Bill Bryson, an American travel writer living in Britain, wrote, “I can’t imagine there was ever a more gratifying time or place to be alive than America in the 1950s. No country had ever known such prosperity.” The United States had rallied economically during World War II. In the Great Depression of the 1930s, unemployment had peaked at 24.9 percent. In 1943, after the country entered the war, it was 1.9 percent. World War II was, like World War I, a social and aesthetic watershed but this time aesthetics were not shaped by radicals in Paris but by American suburbanites who inhabited a world of new oil-based plastics. Prefabrication techniques, evolved to speed up wartime production, were not adapted for social housing as in Britain, but to build houses for sale. An East Coast developer, Levitt & Sons, bought and built on enormous tracts of land within commuting distance of cities and major towns. Returning service members could buy homes without paying a deposit thanks to government aid; their monthly repayments were no more than the rent for a city-center walk-up apartment with no outdoor space. William Levitt (1907–94) described the company as a manufacturer rather than a builder with teams completing a house every 16 minutes on average. Each house came with a built-in television, a hi-fi set, and two trees in the yard, included in the price. ## Residential Design Suburban houses were never going to win critical approval and a quasi-folk song, “Little Boxes” (1964), complained that “they all look just the same.” But they did not stay “just the same” for long. Proud owners quickly began to customize. The rich called in designers and decorators but suburbanites did it themselves. The houses’ open-plan living room, dining room, and kitchen were really one room punctuated by pieces of built-in furniture. Colors were bright and patterns bold. They made prewar Art Deco seem restrained. Natural and artificial materials were mixed, splayed legs supported tables with plastic laminated tops and chairs with gaudy upholstery. No one wanted machines to live in, they wanted houses that were enjoyable to live in. They were not interested in philosophical debates about aesthetics—they knew what they liked and there was a consensus that explained how citizens preferred their interiors and, by extension, the public interiors they shared. Architectural style had been under consideration. In 1939 the New York World’s Fair (see here), with World of Tomorrow as its theme, had featured a Town of Tomorrow. New consumer and industrial products such as television, air-conditioning, and color film were introduced to the public at the fair. Architects contributing to the exhibits were told that their work should be modern but not modernistic—it should incorporate the advantages of technological advance and did not need to be deployed in a particular style, the exterior and/or interior could be deployed in any style, new or old. ## An Ideal Home In 1940 Daniel S. Defenbacher (1906–86), an architect by training and an arts administrator at the Walker Arts Center in Minneapolis, initiated the Idea House project to introduce Minnesotans to the positives and pleasures of modern living. The house designed by local architects, husbandand-wife team Malcolm (1913–2003) and Miriam Lein, and constructed in the grounds of the center. Local suppliers and retailers provided materials and furniture. It was open plan with sliding screens offering options for privacy. It had an electric stove, refrigerator, dishwasher, and washing machine. Surfaces were easy to clean. To guide the 20,000 Minnesotans who visited the house, Defenbacher suggested, “As a consumer every man uses art . . . his composition is his environment.” He insisted that Idea House was not a universal answer but a prompt to give visitors an idea of what they might achieve and that they should make their own decisions. He was identifying interior design as a folk art (21.2). ![](images/32fe2d2c0def7375e6e41079caab5d8bb30533cf607f190e5dfc26f434dc9729.jpg) Malcolm and Miriam Lein, Idea House I, Walker Art Center, Minneapolis, 1941. A worthy first attempt to introduce Minnesotans to the advantages of modern design using locally available materials and furniture. In 1947 the project was repeated but with a better budget. Idea House II, designed by William Friedman and Hilde Reiss (1909–2002) with Malcolm Lein, was again built in the grounds of the center. It was decorated with a more extensive collection of fabrics, wallpapers, and paint colors. National manufacturers were interested in the project. On-site interior designers offered curious visitors explanations and strategic advice but insisted that final decisions about layout and decoration should be left to householders. The principal room was flexible, designated for relaxing, eating, working, and entertainment, while storage units divided activity areas. A chimney breast of unplastered concrete blocks provided a focal and pivotal point in the manner of a Prairie house (see here). Furniture was supplied by Knoll and included chairs by Charles and Ray Eames, lighting by German-born designer Walter von Nessen, and storage walls by George Nelson (21.3). Defenbacher may have hoped that visitors would appreciate the quality of the superior furniture and fittings in Idea House II, but he continued to insist that, ultimately, decisions about domestic interiors should be made by those who would live in them. 21.3 ![](images/875f33814fce84cc576bba0cd74d35156b91f50c5cba704c380835a0e7ac1fb9.jpg) William Friedman, Hilde Reiss and Malcolm Lein, Idea House II, Walker Art Center, Minneapolis, 1947. The better-funded second Idea House, with improved furniture and finishes offered a more radical impression of what the new post-war home might be. Daniel S. Defenbacher, for all his insistence that householders should make their own decisions about their interiors, was gently pushing modernistic options. Defenbacher’s insinuation that popular taste deserved to have its say bothered some. In 1949 Philip Johnson and the Museum of Modern Art in New York appropriated Defenbacher’s idea and had Marcel Breuer design a two-bedroom house for the museum’s sculpture garden. A number of Breuer’s designs were cloned on the East Coast. Defenbacher had refused to give people plans of the Idea House since they were bespoke, but the Museum of Modern Art stipulated that Breuer’s design should be replicated precisely and the interior offered limited options for personal expression (21.4). ![](images/232c357d40a1a30ddc1035e4181535966f901aa76af8553adba336d9226531d0.jpg) Marcel Breuer, The House in the Museum Garden, Museum of Modern Art, New York, 1949. An American step away from the strict minimalism of European modernism of the 1930s. The furnishings, the fireplace, and the floor patterns are closer to the tastes of the American suburb. The design community, unsettled by rampaging suburbia, felt obliged to bring the public to heel. The Museum of Modern Art led the crusade, organizing “Good Design” exhibitions from 1950 to 1955 and, in Los Angeles, Art and Architecture magazine commissioned and built, as exemplars of acceptable modernism, designs for model suburban homes. In 1949 it invited Charles (1907–78) and Ray Eames (1912–88) to design the eighth house in the series and the Eameses decided to live in their design. It was constructed, with a speed that rivaled Levitt’s, from steel and aluminum products intended for building industrial sheds and its empty shell suggested an eccentrically glazed warehouse, but the Eameses filled it with plants, rugs, scatter cushions, and knick-knacks. They demonstrated that modernity benefited from—and was perhaps complemented by—the decorative objects that modernists previously condemned as ornament, and so the Eames House followed the Maison de Verre in Paris (see here) on to the list of interiors that modernists revered. Both buildings depend on the incongruous juxtaposition of the soft and the intimate against the hard and tectonic (21.1). Another house in the Art and Architecture series, by Pierre Koenig (see here), built in 1958, was a hybridization of the Eameses’ concept and the Farnsworth House: a black steel single-story structure with white corrugated panels for walls and ceilings, a single space punctuated by a utilities block. Photographs show it sparsely furnished, a show house with no evidence of a bonafide owner’s idiosyncratic taste. A house constructed in 1965–6 in Ventura County by Buff, Hensman & Associates had finishes and furniture that belonged to the same band of taste as the Koenig House. Both were fine and of their time but, unlike the Eames House broke no new ground. The Eameses, who had married in 1941, were multidisciplinary designers shaped by the Scandinavian ideas of the Finn, Eliel Saarinen (see here), who taught them at the Cranbrook Academy of Art in Bloomfield Hills, Michigan, where they developed an understanding of the structural and shape-making potential of bent plywood. Their lounger, with its fat leather cushions held in place by the upturned edges of walnut-veneered plywood, and its matching ottoman have become architects’ comfy chair of choice. The extensive range of chairs created by the Eameses has become popular, striking a chord with twenty-first century designers and householders who have grown to appreciate them. Their DSR side chair with its plastic seat on wood or metal legs has become ubiquitous. The plastic seat, with or without arms, is simple to manufacture and, unlike the chairs of Europe’s modernist pioneers, it is affordable. Eliel’s son, Eero Saarinen (1910–61), was also educated at Cranford and knew the Eameses. In 1939 he collaborated with Charles to design a chair to be used as auditorium seating for Kleinhans Music Hall in Buffalo, New York. Formed from a single piece of plywood for seat and back, it appears to have been influenced by Alvar Aalto’s Paimio armchair (see here). Eero became a partner in Eliel’s architectural practice and in 1942 they completed the First Christian Church (also known as the Tabernacle Church) in Columbus, Indiana (21.5). Its white walls and simple timber furniture, with a minimal crucifix slightly offset on the floating wall demonstrate that modernism may have a dimension that transcends practicalities. Eero addressed the problem of creating modernist interiors with something more than the logic of function by becoming an Expressionist, that is creating architecture that expresses a conceptual idea that is not generated by rational analysis of a building’s function. In his TWA Flight Center airport terminal at Idlewild Airport (now John F. Kennedy International Airport) in New York (1956–62), the undersides of its soaring roof planes became soaring ceiling planes and are said to be symbolic of flight (21.6). His 630-feet (190-m) tall stainless steel Gateway Arch in St. Louis (1963) is an emphatically expressionist gesture. ![](images/b0bbf289840cd273d0c3d547df71996bbc2f9148f1f7ab39962cec91c9239a21.jpg) Eliel and Eero Saarinen, First Christian (Tabernacle) Church, Columbus, Indiana, 1942. Considered the first American church of modern design. It is an expression of faith that modernism could create a spiritual and meditative environment without imagery and symbols, apart from the cross. ![](images/257420a032544c78fd8827e7f9e447c5c1d89adf7e186310038499dc4c2f808b.jpg) Eero Saarinen, TWA Terminal, John F. Kennedy International Airport, New York, 1956–62. Saarinen exploited the structural capacity of reinforced concrete to create free-form curves, which are said to be a metaphor for flight. They characterize the building’s exterior and interior in a baroque modernism. ## Furnishings The development of modern interior design in the 1930s and 1940s was held back by the lack of suitable furniture for commercial and residential contexts. The Artek company had found some outlets for pieces by Alvar and Aino Aalto and Thonet but, in 1937 Hans Knoll (1914–55) arrived in New York from Germany and set up his eponymous furniture company. He was not a designer but understood the furniture needed for the modernist interior. He joined forces with Danish designer Jens Risom (1916–2016), who arrived in the United States in 1939 bringing with him Scandinavian ideas and methods. Risom designed wooden-framed tables and chairs with wooden backs and seats of ribbed webbing. When the United States entered World War II in 1941, Knoll’s designs were suitable for military facilities and could be made in small workshops. The 650 chair of 1941, with its tapered legs and wide-webbing seat and back, is still in production (21.7). ![](images/862e089d2485f02dd305c84036b421a16561a93ed3e8ad85bd9297415d428925.jpg) Jens Risom, Knoll 650 low lounge chair, 1941. A perfect example of how the legacy of Scandinavian craft could be redeployed in the cause of modern furniture making. In 1943 Florence Knoll (née Schust; 1917–2019) joined Knoll to deal with interior-design projects. Her career was a triumph of luck and determination. An orphan, she had attended Kingswood School for Girls in Michigan, which is part of the Cranbrook Educational Community, where her interest in architecture and interiors was encouraged. From 1936–7 she worked on furniture with Eero Saarinen and Charles Eames and met Alvar Aalto, who recommended the Architectural Association in London as a place to study. She went there, from 1938 until the outbreak of war in 1939, then became an unpaid apprentice for Walter Gropius and Marcel Breuer, and studied under Ludwig Mies van der Rohe in Chicago. She later used those connections to persuade Mies and Breuer to have their designs produced by Knoll. Eero Saarinen also contributed pieces, including his womb chair of 1948 and his Tulip chairs and tables of 1955–6 (21.8). She designed pieces herself to fill gaps in the range (21.9). In 1946 she married Hans Knoll and began to build the company’s interior-design service. It became a favored consultancy for modernist architects. She added a textile division in 1949, led by the Hungarian-born Eszter Haraszty (1920–94), who added stronger colors to the sober, Mies-influenced interiors. ![](images/fbfef29a441d296ae9cc6a6d64679e3f948fe8aa967e2b78f9b9dfcf1c6f4ee6.jpg) 21.8 Eero Saarinen, Tulip range for Herman Miller Furniture Company, mid 1950s. As design director of Knoll Associates, Knoll was key in maintaining the company’s commitment to manufacturing modernistic products and producing pieces herself to cover gaps in its range. The Herman Miller Furniture Company was Knoll’s rival. When Gilbert Rohde (see here) died, in 1944, his place as lead designer was taken by George Nelson (1908–86), who designed a complete range of furniture that was launched in 1946. This table was a curious organic addition to the Knoll range. Its free-form base, formed with two identical wooden sections, supported a biomorphic clear-glass top. It sat incongruously among the straight lines and right angles of the rest of the collection. In 1952 Nelson recruited Alexander Girard (1907–93) as head of the textile division. Girard had trained as an architect in London and Rome. His first collection of textiles with squares, circles, triangles, and stripes worked well with the aesthetic of Nelson’s team and contributed significantly to its influence on post-war interiors. His collection of folk art inspired his more colorful and densely patterned fabrics and he collaborated with a Mexican textile mill to develop what he called “mexidots” and “mexistripes,” which became popular for upholstery and wall panels (21.13). In 1971 he developed forty fabric panels to enliven offices which, under the influence of pragmatic space planners, were becoming bereft of visual stimuli. ![](images/59bc3995a424c99667a1cbb0858f897e2bd413fcac7f8e9b24738a80e06bec59.jpg) ## 21.13 Alexander Girard, striped textile, 1954. Girard was influenced by the bright colors he found in his collection of folk art from Arizona, Mexico, South America, and India. In 1965 Girard worked for Braniff International Airways on “The End of the Plain Plane” project, designing a range of items, from check-in desks to sugar sachets, and the blue, beige, ocher, orange, turquoise, and lemon palette from which a distinctive livery was created for each airplane in the Braniff fleet. In 1960 he designed everything from floor and wall tiles to tableware and menus for a Latin American restaurant in Manhattan, all on themes prompted by his knowledge of South American folk art. For The Compound restaurant in Santa Fe (1967), he created eighty different sun motifs as wall decoration. In 1964 he assembled a mural 180 feet (55 m) long and 8 feet (2.5 m) high from found objects, for the John Deere tractor company headquarters designed by Eero Saarinen in Moline, Illinois. Hungarian-born interior designer Paul Laszlo (1900–93) was another Jew forced to abandon a successful practice in Europe and leave for the United States in 1936. Laszlo was based in Beverly Hills, California, creating glamorous homes before moving on to retail and designing individual stores, department stores, and shopping malls. He designed a wide range of building types including offices, banks, and casinos but he kept his practice small to control its output. He wanted time to refine detail and built a network of makers and suppliers who met his standards. He favored wealthy clients who shared his creative ambitions, believing that interaction with a supportive client drove ideas. His work was restrained and relied for its quirky elegance on fine craft processes. He expressed approval for the modernism of his fellow Jewish exile in California, the Austrian architect Richard Neutra (see here) but favored decorative devices. The austere elegance of his furniture suggests fin-de-siècle Vienna and his generous upholstery recognizes the need for comfort. Laszlo attracted bizarre projects. In 1955 he designed an underground bomb shelter for the owner of the Hertz car-rental company. In 1956 he designed a speculative bombproof underground town, Atomville, for an article in the magazine Popular Mechanics, where houses had rooftop (or ground level) helipads for the helicopters expected to replace automobiles. ## INSIGHTS ## Modernism and Social Environments In the late 1940s, as high-rise social housing demonstrated the grim reality of Le Corbusier’s theories, Architectural Review expressed doubts about modernism’s ability to create successful social environments. To test this, in 1949, it launched a competition to design the interior of a public house (bar), something progressive without being inhuman and popular without being silly.1 The judges’ report spoke about the importance of atmosphere, colors, intimacy of scale, and decoration. In 1958 the magazine suggested that architecture and interior design existed in different time frames: The architect’s mind geared to generalized functions that change slowly, or never, is unlikely to be athletic enough to provide an appropriately ephemeral interior solution.2 It advocated that interior designers should have training similar to that of architects but did not suggest that they also needed quite different skills. 1. Architectural Review, vol. 106, issue 633, 1949, p. 265; 2. Ibid., vol. 123, issue 735, 1958, p. 227 ## EUROPE After the war, Europe’s priority was rebuilding, literally and metaphorically. Some countries escaped lightly, such as France which had been defeated quickly, Finland which had limited strategic significance, and Sweden which had asserted its neutrality. Others had not: Italy had been a war zone as the Allies fought their way to Germany, and Germany had been a battlefield as American, British, and Russian forces converged on Berlin. Consequently, images of American interiors and American lives were viewed enviously in post-war Europe. The United States had its military hard power but its soft power was perhaps even more effective. Its lifestyle was celebrated in films. Its popular music became pervasive and young Europeans envied and aped the lifestyles of their American contemporaries. ## Britain Throughout the war, Britain had been on the front line. From September 7, 1940 German aircraft bombed London for fifty-seven successive days before moving on to more far-flung British cities. Raw materials were scarce, food and clothing were rationed—the country’s war loans from the United States would not be paid off until 2006. In 1945 Britain elected a socialist government on a mandate to create an egalitarian utopia. New schools and hospitals offered citizens their first encounters with the modernist interior, which was hard-edged but brightly optimistic. By the end of the war, a quarter of a million houses had been destroyed and two million badly damaged by enemy bombing; two and a quarter million people were homeless. In 1944 manufacturing had already began on small, prefabricated asbestos cement bungalows with a living room, two bedrooms, a bathroom, and a kitchen. For those who had shared overcrowded, insanitary pre-war houses the “prefabs” were wonderful self-contained family domains with hot running water. Apartments in high-rises, which were ostensibly cheaper and faster to build than traditional row houses, seemed like permanent solutions to housing shortages. Britons might dream of Americans’ suburbs, of new houses on their own plots, of bright interiors packed with plastic laminates and electric gadgets but the social housing provided for victims of wartime bombing and pre-war slums was created by modernist architects for the approval of other modernist architects. ## Festival of Britain In 1943, when wartime victory seemed likely, Britain began planning an event to mark the centenary of the Great Exhibition of 1851 (see here). The government considered that an optimistic view of its future would boost national morale. Sir Hugh Casson (1910–99), who understood that exhibitions should entertain and exhilarate, was appointed director of architecture. He employed the promising young architects and designers who would go on to produce the better interiors of the 1950s. It was quirky and colorful and had something American and Scandinavian about it. It photographed well and images of bright laminates, fabrics, and wallpapers with abstract patterns appeared regularly in homemaking magazines. The feature wall—usually that containing the fireplace and decorated more vibrantly than the other walls in a room—was introduced and suggested that the convention of four matching walls in a room was obsolete. Light-hued woods and the splayed legs of tables and chairs replaced dark brown stolidity—cacti replaced aspidistras. The interior designers who, in 1980s Britain, would change the creative ambitions of their fellow professionals and the expectations of those who would use their buildings, grew up in such environments. ![](images/4ce8494093d2add0c33a7f6e207dc2cd124b3b93ad8651b26bde57e1fca624a6.jpg) Contemporary style, Britain, 1953. Plastics, bright colors, whimsical shapes, and shiny finishes became popular and common in the years after the Festival of Britain. Britain had its star designers in the married couple Robin Day (1915–2010) and Lucienne Day (1917–2010). He designed furniture with molded plywoods and plastics seats perched on chromed or matt black legs (21.17). Her textiles were brightly colored with imagery derived from plants (21.18) and scientific symbols—it was a time in which science was delivering benefits in the form of plastics and an existential threat in the form of atomic bombs. Throughout their long careers they shared a long work table in a series of beautiful homes. They featured frequently in newspapers and magazines, they were glamorous, and made design glamorous. ![](images/0c496db5e42f5dd23107a8ff9a655c90c1cab9c4a572bdf786f72c9c52dba6ed.jpg) Robin Day, chair 675, 1952. Calyx, produced for the Festival of Britain, is Day’s best-known design and, like much of her work, drew on plant forms. It and many more of her fabrics remain in production. Contemporary style made inroads into the public arena. Italian immigrants introduced coffee shops into London’s racy Soho area as alternatives to traditional bars and tearooms, and the model spread to provincial towns and cities (21.20). After their enforced insular wartime existence people wanted to feel cosmopolitan. Most coffee shops were put together expediently by their proprietors who concentrated on creating atmosphere. Architects designed a few. In 1955 Marghanita Laski, a British novelist and radio panellist, was asked to comment on the coffee-shop phenomenon in The Architectural Review magazine. She praised them as “somewhere to go outside home that isn’t hell to look at” and listed their distinguishing features as “wooden vertical slats . . . fleshy green plants, taper-legged, wicker-seated backless stools . . . thick glass coffee cups on the bare organic tabletops.” She did not mention the exotic hissing and spluttering of the espresso machines. ![](images/829cda759a820aedf89df86c7406123f22884140c3a08f77783c55db8a605273.jpg) John (1925–92) and Sylvia Reid (1924–2022), coffee shop, Marylebone, London, 1955. An interior that incorporates many characteristics of a coffee shop: an undulating ceiling, a patterned floor, vertical hardwood wall cladding, a plastic laminate counter front, Arne Jacobsen chairs (see here), Scandinavian light fittings, and a coffee machine. Underpinning Contemporary’s aesthetic there was, just discernible, the influence of Scandinavian design. Sweden had remained neutral through-out the war, leaning toward Germany when it seemed likely to win and toward the Allies when the pendulum swung toward them. Precarious neutrality allowed its factories to carry on producing modernistic pieces shaped by the values absorbed from traditional craftwork. Denmark spent most of the war under German Occupation but escaped aerial bombing and, after the war, quickly made its contribution to the Scandinavian canon. Finland was threatened by Germany and the U.S.S.R., but was fortunate not to be on the immediate agenda of either and opened state-sponsored showrooms in London in 1953 and 1958—photographs show plywood furniture, colored-glass light shades, and bright textiles. Contemporary was undoubtedly modernistic. Its shapes and colors were prompted by new plastics and production techniques (21.21). It conformed to the modernist principle of “truth to materials” but its output was too flippant for those who considered modernity no laughing matter. The Festival of Britain introduced the British public to a new Contemporary aesthetic and this store incorporates contemporary fittings, the mirror-stand, the chair, the light shades, and the carpet. Following his success with the Festival of Britain, Casson was asked to establish an architectural course at London’s Royal College of Art in 1952. The Royal Institute of British Architects, which controlled architectural education and professional registration, refused permission so the college settled for a postgraduate interior-design course with a long-term strategy of converting it to architecture. This conversion would take another thirty years but, over that time, the course evolved an architectural bias that gave its alumni a creative breadth that would eventually filter down to undergraduate courses. ## Italy Italian designers trained in architecture schools but saw themselves as creative polymaths. In the 1950s they were excited by the possibilities of plastic and the opportunities it offered to break with tradition. Anna Castelli Ferrieri (1918–2006), the first woman to graduate in architecture from Milan Polytechnic, was a pioneer of designing with plastics from the 1950s to the 1980s. Her husband, the chemical engineer Giulio Castelli (1920–2006), formed the Kartell furniture company and led the field in plastic-manufacturing technology. When his two partners left the business, Ferrieri reluctantly agreed to become Kartell’s inhouse designer and commissioner of consultant designers, on whose behalf she liaised with the production team. Her own designs, with strong geometric forms and bright colors, established the company’s reputation. In 1964, in collaboration with Ignazio Garbella, she designed a round, glass-reinforced polyester pedestal table, in 1968 a plastic chair made from a single mold and the Multi-Box range of square stackable, injection-molded ABS plastic storage units. In 1968 she refined Multi-Box to make the Componibili circular stacking modular storage units, which meant that doors can slide and hinges are eliminated (21.24). Cesare “Joe” Colombo (1930–71) studied architecture at Milan Polytechnic, then became an Abstract Expressionist painter. He switched to design when he inherited his family’s electricalgoods company in 1959 and was prompted to investigate new materials and production techniques. He had the 1960s impulse to rethink the familiar and worked for innovative Italian manufacturers such as Alessi, Flexform, and Kartell. Its interiors and furnishings were broadly modernist with white walls, light-hued wooden floors, highly crafted wooden furniture, and elegant, witty light fittings. They demonstrated how hard, modernist lines might be made amenable and their work struck a chord with designers trying to find their own post-war world. Arne Jacobsen (1902–71) was Denmark’s recognized design doyen. While still a student, he won a silver medal at the International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts in Paris in 1925, where he was excited by Le Corbusier’s Pavillon de l’Esprit Nouveau. He then traveled to Germany to see the work of Mies and Gropius. In 1929 he collaborated with a fellow Danish architect, Flemming Lassen (1902–84), to win a House of the Future competition in Denmark and their design was built at the Architects Building and Housing Forum in Copenhagen. It had a spiral plan, glass and concrete walls, and a flat roof that would act as a helipad. It was considered disturbingly radical. After 1934 he worked closely with the Fritz Hansen manufacturing company for his furniture designs and with the Louis Poulsen company for his lighting. When Denmark was annexed by Germany in 1940, Jacobsen, a Jew, was obliged to retreat to neutral Sweden but, when he returned after the war in 1945, his practice grew quickly thanks to a national program of rebuilding. His commitment to Gesamtkunstwerk—the total work of art—led him to design the furniture and fittings in his most important projects. For his SAS Royal Hotel in Copenhagen (1956–60), he designed all the furniture, which included his Egg and Swan chairs. The former has a high back that wraps around a sitter’s head, the latter a low back with separate armrests and back, which when viewed side on, suggest a swan, both have shape-hugging upholstery and rotate on low steel cruciform bases (21.26). Jacobsen also created light fittings, and miscellaneous objects. For St. Catherine’s College, a new college in Oxford, England, he designed all the buildings, the furniture, light fittings, cutlery, and the garden, where he even specified the breed of fish for the pond. He was influenced by the experiments of Charles and Ray Eames with plywood and in 1952 created Ant, which is a light, stackable chair with seat and back molded from a single length of plywood that rests on chromed legs (21.29). Like the Swan chair, it bears some resemblance to the creature that gave it its name. Hans Wegner (1914–2007) was a designer of chairs. From the age of fourteen to seventeen he was apprenticed to a cabinetmaker and, after compulsory army service, attended the School of Arts and Crafts in Copenhagen to refine his skills. He designed almost exclusively in wood, augmented by traditional materials including cane and paper cord. His work is characterized by steam-bent, tapering timber frames with exquisitely executed interlocking joints. With its simple wooden frame, the generous sweep of the bent top rail of its backrest, and its woven seat it has become a popular dining chair. Verner Panton (1926–98) was Danish and an exponent of colors and plastics. In 1964, the Beatles established Britain’s pop hegemony in the United States and beyond. British soft power supplanted that of the United States. Swinging London in the Swinging Sixties became the epicenter of a changing world and the epicenter of Swinging London was Carnaby Street, a street of cheap clothes shops aimed at the young and fashion-obsessed. Carnaby Street was the invention of John Stephen (1934–2004), who recognized untapped opportunities to sell fashionable clothes to young men and opened His Clothes in Carnaby Street, painting the store’s façade bright yellow and blasting pop music into the street. Over the next ten years, he opened fifteen shops on the street, all trading under different names, to suggest independent businesses catering for niche tastes. His strategy of ageappropriate staff selling cheap fashion became, and continues to be, the formula for fashion retailing. With Stephens’s model in mind, independent boutique owners like the coffee-shop owners of the 1950s were happy to design their own stores, it was part of the romance of ownership. Interiors were dark and spotlights shone on bulging racks of clothes. Counters disappeared and customers wandered at will. Increased prosperity allowed Britons to travel in Europe. They returned impressed with its apparent sophistication, renaming fashion stores “boutiques” and dance halls “discotheques.” Boutiques spread from inner London to the provinces. Management responsibilities diluted the glamor of ownership and bankruptcies were frequent but a few, cleverly conceived in the right locations, prospered. In 1966 Granny Takes A Trip opened at the less fashionable end of King’s Road, far from Quant’s Bazaar. Its façade was devoted to a sequence of graphic images that had nothing to do with the clothes it sold. A permanent feature of the façade was the exhortation “One should either be a work of art or wear a work of art.” Boutique owners liked to think they were doing more than selling clothes. The interior was practical with marbled wallpaper, a beaded curtain at the entrance, and a lace curtain that offered rudimentary privacy to the changing room. The façade made its reputation and it was featured in “London: The Swinging City” edition of Time magazine. Biba, the most celebrated of the boutiques, owned and designed by Barbara Hulanicki (b. 1938), opened in 1964 on the very edge of fashionable London and grew in a succession of small stores, each in a better location and each under-lit, with dark walls and dark potted palms. The influences of Art Nouveau and Art Deco were modest but clear—both styles were fashionable but the latter was in the ascendency. (Four years later Bevis Hillier would write Art Deco and give the style its name.) Biba’s final upgrade was to the shell of a former department store in which each floor was devoted to a product genre: women’s clothing was augmented by floors for men, children, food, and household goods. It was an embryonic lifestyle store and continued its black-painted, underlit Art Deco theme but introduced bright Pop Art displays of ironic/iconic merchandise—cans of baked beans were the most incongruous. The shop’s appeal had been to young women who wanted clothes, not beans. Theatricality took precedence over selling. The romance of the small, overcrowded interior, where customers competed to buy from tantalizingly limited stock, declined and it closed in 1976. Hulanicki then began a second career as an interior decorator. When they recognized the economic significance of the youth market, established retailers moved into fashion retailing. Maurice Broughton Associates, an established design company, created Dress Circle, a boutique and coffee shop on the top floor of Harrods, one of the most prestigious London department stores. A dedicated elevator by-passed the traditional departments below. Dress Circle was finished to a much more sophisticated level than the boutiques of the King’s Road and Carnaby Street and photographs of the coffee shop show the bulbous plastic curves and seamless coved junctions of floors, walls, and ceilings that were the fashionable details of the time (21.32). ![](images/6e00b7ac779976e030b7cb366b5b1e1f144d80c91db0d61bf5e3007e2e0c593a.jpg) Maurice Broughton Associates, Dress Circle, Harrods, London, 1968. A haven for the young and fashionable in the dowagers’ department store, it was the epitome of late 1960s design, plastics, rounded corners, and soft upholstered shapes. Garnett Cloughley Blakemore was an architectural practice adept at creating fantasy, and not ashamed to allude to palm trees, coconuts and bamboo in the hotels and bars it designed. It also designed a number of King’s Road boutiques, including Stop The Shop, in which a slowly revolving circular platform took up the width of its frontage. The practice had pioneered the rotational mechanism for a restaurant on top of the Post Office Tower, which was, at 620 feet (189 m), then the tallest building in London. South Molton Street in Mayfair, London was a fashion node distant from Carnaby Street and the King’s Road, more expensive and closer to haute couture. In 1968 Rex Wilkinson (b. 1947), Roger Calvert (b. 1948) and Piers Gough (b. 1946) completed a boutique for the knitwear designer Mary Farrin, which provided a model for less glamorous shops on less fashionable streets into the 1970s. Transparent and opaque glass panels were set in quadrant-cornered, glass-reinforcedplastic frames, colored to suggest brass. The interior was tiny, 20 × 11 feet (6 × 3.5 m) but stretched by a mirrored back wall. The geometry of the fenestration was repeated in color blocks on walls and ceiling (21.31). ![](images/a2b060f4220ed3c0b7e3201c01cc5b07af3769b96e31859140ea4d34ee028f39.jpg) Rex Wilkinson, Roger Calvert and Piers Gough, Mary Farrin boutique, London, 1968. In 1968, Wilkinson, Calvert and Gough’s second South Molton Street boutique, Knitwit, seemed to anticipate postmodernism with its sky-blue walls, colored floor tiles, ziggurat display shelves, and palm-tree lights. In the 1970s as fashion retailing continued to grow it became more serious as it passed from the control of idealists to business managers. Boutiques reverted to being stores. Stephen Marks (b. 1946), a tactically astute retailer, opened the first store in his French Connection chain in 1972. He understood the importance of the interior: “To me it’s all quite simple—you can’t get people to go in if they don’t like the look of the store from the outside.” The importance in Britain of innovative interior design to attract newly affluent young customers across all commercial sectors led to the exponential growth, particularly in the 1970s, of interior design as a profession that was increasingly distinct from architecture. A number of large practices emerged of which the most successful was established by Rodney Fitch (1938– 2014), who famously declared “the purpose of life is shopping.” He remembered that, in drab post-war Britain the glamour of upmarket store windows persuaded him to become a designer and he enrolled at London’s Central School of Art and Design to study theater, television, interior and furniture design. He empathized with William Morris’s explanation that the purpose of design was to give hope. He proposed that retail was “the only true democracy in the world”— people voted with their money and therefore cared about where they were spending it. His declared ambition was “to deliver to ordinary people better places to shop,” better places to cast their votes on the world they wanted to inhabit. In 1963 he was recruited to Terence Conran’s Design Group and by 1968 was its managing director. When Conran sold Habitat and his design consultancy, Fitch bought the consultancy. In 1972 he established Fitch & Company Design Consultants and created a template for multidisciplinary retail-design practice. He saw design as a visceral or emotive activity. He encouraged his staff to “read the signs of the street, not remain in [their] insular creative cocoon.” Most of the designers who established London’s leading design practices worked for him early in their careers. He trusted visceral reaction before rational analysis. Fitch’s clients were retail chains, department stores, and grocery supermarkets. He was particularly proud of his work for the Topshop fashion chain that had branches in towns and cities across Britain—because it was popular with young women, a demographic that shared his enthusiasm for shopping. ## The Modern Home Zeev Aram (1931–2021), was a Romanian Jewish wartime refugee whose family had migrated to Palestine at the outbreak of World War II. He grew up there but settled in London in 1957. In 1964, across the street from Quant’s Bazaar, he opened a small furniture store and design consultancy, selling “modern classics” by Le Corbusier and Mies and introducing design enthusiasts to a then unknown Eileen Gray (see here). In the same year, 1964, Sir Terence Conran (1931–2020) opened his first Habitat furniture store and introduced a wider public to modern design, made more palatable by being sold alongside the vernacular household objects of Mediterranean peasantry. The store’s décor of white-painted brick walls, reddish-brown, quarry-tiled floor, and batteries of spotlights was a statement of stylistic intent, and a means to seduce and induct customers into the Habitat lifestyle. The Habitat mail-order catalog carried Conran’s message across the nation (21.34). Elegant photographs introduced readers to visions of high-ceilinged, white-walled Victorian rooms with extant moldings and fireplaces filled with Habitat furniture counterpointed by pieces from thrift stores. Habitat branches began to appear in towns and cities throughout Britain. Early on, he recognized the impact that information technology would have in the workplace and understood that existing office stock was not ready for the digital upheaval on the horizon. He rejected space planners’ mechanistic layouts of bleak cubicles, and praised Germany, the Netherlands, and Scandinavia for pioneering Bürolandschaft (office landscaping) informal layouts subdivided by strategically placed plants with an emphasis on democratic control of the working environment. He encouraged designers and clients to collaborate in evolving designs. In his essay “An Escape from Banality” (1983) he attacked the mundane interiors produced for commercial clients and asked, “When was the last time anyone wanted a suspended ceiling because it moved the heart?” He praised architects, such as the Victorian practitioner Richard Norman Shaw (see here), who designed “rooms that looked like rooms,” and Louis Sullivan and Frank Lloyd Wright for creating humane, multistoried, multioccupancy office buildings. He accused Mies of being “totally uninterested in what went on inside his buildings.” He identified four factors that would transform office design: digital information technology, social change with workers given more control over their environments, the need to conserve energy which would change the shape and section of buildings, and the rise of “facilities managers” who, he hoped, would thwart architects’ diktats. In 1973 Centraal Beheer, a Dutch insurance company, employed Herman Hertzberger (b. 1932) a leading Dutch architect to design an office building in Apeldoorn in the Netherlands that seemed to meet Duffy’s criteria (21.38). It offered an empty matrix, multilayered both vertically and horizontally, and was intended as a solution to the impersonality of both open-plan and cellular office floors. Workers would be responsible for fine-tuning their own working environment. The concrete frame was infilled with concrete block walls to heights appropriate to the function behind them. Complex plans and sections created distinctive spaces, often looking into internal voids, and workers personalized them with plants, photographs and artifacts. It was a generous solution. Far exceeding standard office-sector space allocation, the experiment paid off as productivity improved by 25 per cent. Ten years later, employees were no longer happy with blockwork walls and concrete columns as backgrounds for the material they might chose to express themselves and, indeed, not all felt the need to express themselves. Communal areas were neglected. Employees had come to expect a good working environment without having to do it for themselves. It is the perennial problem with interiors: one is necessarily intimate with one’s surroundings and, with time, they may lose their charms. ![](images/50c9a31663d966453fb09d6964f5f4cc48374a9c72025eafe92e63aea160dfd6.jpg) ## 21.38 Herman Hertzberger, Centraal Beheer, Apeldoorn, the Netherlands, 1973. His role as head of interior design at the Royal College of Art had caused him to consider the demarcation between interiors and architecture, and his seventy-five pages for Inscape were the most significant writings on the subject up to that time and, possibly, since. He wrote about the connection between interior design and decoration and how functionalist machismo caused architects to shy away from the subjectivity needed to design interiors. He argued that change of use in buildings meant that interiors need no longer take their stylistic cues from the exterior.
