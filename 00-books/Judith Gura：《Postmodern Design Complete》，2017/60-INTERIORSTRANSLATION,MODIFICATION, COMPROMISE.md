# INTERIORS: TRANSLATION, MODIFICATION, COMPROMISE Postmodernism did not translate painlessly into interiors. Its design vocabulary was formulated for architecture, and its execution in furniture resulted in pieces so extravagant as to preclude the use of several of them in a single room, unless that room had an explicitly postmodern aesthetic. There was in any case a time lag between the creation of architectural environments and the availability of furniture to fill them. Most of the earliest postmodern interiors were illustrated in design publications without decoration or furniture, acknowledging that conventional furnishings were altogether inappropriate. It took a while for manufacturers to respond. Architects were the first to show interest in designing postmodern interiors; first because they were already thinking about, or working in, the new aesthetic, and second because interiors could offer them opportunities for commissions in a new area, an idea that was especially welcome in a decade of economic recession. Some architectural elements (such as columns) translated comfortably into interiors; others (pediments or elaborate arches, for example) somewhat awkwardly. In many cases, however, a focus on contextualism led to the use of narrative, in the form of murals and photomurals that told their own stories. The most striking postmodern interiors were in public rather than private spaces. In its banner years, showrooms, restaurants, retail stores, even theaters sprouted the columns, arcades, and pediments of the style's most familiar architectural elements, executed in a bright pastel palette that promised relief from mostly white International Style walls. Some of the most notable examples were designed by marquee-name architects like MICHAEL GRAVES (also see under "Masters of Postmodern Design"), ARATA ISOZAKI (see under "Architecture"), HANS HOLLEIN (see under “Architecture"), who created environments that made even conventionally modern furniture look up-to-the-minute. Commercial spaces like showrooms and stores took little risk in being fashion-forward, since they were likely to be redecorated within as little as a year. Other locations required design-savvy clients, or corporations that welcomed the opportunity to commission projects that would serve as branding statements. For a firm like DISNEY (see under "Edited Design"), the exuberant new style was the ideal garb in which to dress its Graves-designed Swan and Dolphin hotels, as well as most of their corporate facilities. Despite wanting to project an up-to-date image, however, few other major companies were willing to go so far out on the postmodern limb.
