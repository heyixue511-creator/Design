# 6 László Moholy-Nagy (1895-1946)

## A Born Educator

In the previous chapter, we saw that Johannes Itten remained distant from—not to say critical of— the goal of an integration of art and technology Gropius had postulated and that his view of art ultimately remained indebted to tradition. Despite the modernity of his pictorial language, he seldom ventured beyond the boundaries of easelpainting. Moholy-Nagy, by contrast, was progressive and avant-garde in that his interests as an artist were entirely focused on exploring new artistic media, on bringing“art's conception of it-

self" in line "with the media of mechanical reproduction,"1 and on providing a new place for art in an advanced industrial society. For Moholy-Nagy the question of the social relevance of aesthetic production was one of central importance; for Itten, by contrast, artistic activity was primarily an individual problem.

In the spring of 1923, László Moholy-Nagy was called by Walter Gropius to the Staatliches Bauhaus in Weimar. Although at the time he had only been active as an artist for a few years, his success as an experimental designer was remarkable; and although he had had no systematic experience as a teacher, Gropius must have recognized intuitively that he was dealing with a natural pedagogical talent, a "born educator."² There is sufficient evidence to suggest that the question of pedagogical qualification played a particular role in Gropius's appointments. For example, we know that he consideredTheo van Doesburg too dogmatic to be a teacher and that he would not consider appointing Archipenko “because he assumed he lacked pedagogical interests."3 Although in putting together the teaching staff Gropius had at first frequently sought out advice—his wife, Alma Mahler-Gropius suggested Johannes Itten; Itten, in turn, promoted the appointments of Klee, Schlemmer, and Muche4—Moholy seems to have been Gropius's personal discovery. Gropius was clearly not disappointed, as can be seen from the fact that Moholy's teaching career at the Bauhaus lasted five years, and Gropius later wrote that Moholy's teaching decisively influenced developments at the school.⁵

It is not possible here to even begin to do justice to Moholy-Nagy's universal personality. The

theme of this book compels us to limit ourselves to Moholy's Bauhaus years and specifically to his activity as a teacher. As a consequence, we can neither treat his work in the years between 1928 and his death in 1946, including his activity in Chicago at the New Bauhaus, the School of Design, and the Institute of Design (see chapter 13) nor explore in detail. his oeuvre as a painter, typographer, sculptor, photographer, stage designer, industrial designer, and theoretician of design.6 Picking out particular aspects—in this case, the pedagogical one—from a complex overall context may seem especially irresponsible in Moholy's case, as he himself always insisted on totality and polemicized against the "segmented human being."7 The fact that Moholy-Nagy as pedagogue is the focus here should not be allowed to obscure the artist, designer, and thinker, especially as there is no question that Moholy's activity as a teacher was deeply affected by his own aesthetic practice. In what follows, then, we must first provide a limited overview of this aesthetic practice.

## Aspects of His Artistic Work

László Moholy-Nagy, born in Hungary in 1895, was self-taught as a teacher and artist. It seems it was precisely this lack of formal training that enabled him to go beyond artistic boundaries and expand the traditional concept of art. Free of any academic fixations that might have hindered innovation, the specific features of his constitution—which his first wife, Lucia Moholy, identified as confidence in his intuitions and an ability to transform quickly—enabled him in an astonishingly brief period to find his way into the ranks of the postwar avant-garde.

Moholy's artistic development began with expressive watercolor and chalk drawings that were made in 1917 in response to horrible experiences in the war, employing an impenetrable thicket of lines to depict the barbed-wire landscapes of the battlefields and dying soldiers. The use of the line as the basic artistic element seems to be highly emotionally charged in these works, that is to say, it does not serve primarily to represent an object but rather "communicates agitation andvibration."8Moholy's decision after thè war to give up the legal studies he had begun earlier9 was accompanied by profound internal conflicts. His propensity for social concerns led him to ask self-critically in 1919 whether, in a time of great social revolutions and difficult problems for everyday life, he was at all justified to "claim the privilege of art for myself"10 and become a painter. In the end, his decision in favor of art was firm: “I know now that if I unfold my best talents in the way suited best to them—if I try to grasp the meaning of this, my life, sincerely and thoroughly—then I'm doing right in becoming a painter. It is my gift to project my vitality, my building power, through light, color, form. I can give life as a painter"11 (my emphasis—R.W.). In 1917, suffering from delirium caused by fever while recovering from a war injury, Moholy wrote his poem "Light Visions"12the earliest evidence of his view of light as the principle of existence and a means for design—and in the passage just cited the phenomenon of light is once again the theme, a theme that would stay with Moholy-Nagy throughout his life.

After the war, Moholy-Nagy established contact with the artists of the Ma group in Budapest (ma is Hungarian for "today"), a group of artists inclined to the avant-garde and to social revolution with cubist and expressionist leanings. The collapse of Béla Kun's brief soviet republic in Hungary made Moholy a political émigré in 1919. With Vienna as a way station, he arrived in Berlin in 1920, then the center of the latest artistic movements. Moholy-Nagy had already confronted cubism in his work in Hungary, and his painting in Berlin found strong stimulus for its maturation in his encounter with Dada and with works by artists from the Eastern European avant-garde, like Malevitch, Rodchenko, El Lissitzky, Tatlin, Kandinsky, and others. After a brief period in which he produced paintings, drawings, and graphic works that are essentially objective (despite the strong influence of constructive and abstract design principles) and that reveal the enthusiasm for technology common at the time (bridge construction, railway signals, gear mechanisms, and so on), Moholy was quickly able to learn the “language of geometry”13 and to develop a form of constructivism with a very personal element. In 1922 he justified his decision in favor of constructivism as his basic conception as follows:

Reality is the measure of human thinking. It is the means by which we orient ourselves in the Universe. . .. And this reality of our century is technology: the invention, construction, and maintenance of machines. To be a user of machines is to be of the spirit of this century. It has replaced the transcendental spiritualism of past eras. Everyone is equal before the machine. . . . There is no tradition in technology, no class-consciousness. Everybody can be the machine's master, or its slave. This is the root of Socialism. . . . This is our century: technology, machine, Social: ism. .. . Art crystallizes the emotions of an age: art is mirror and voice. The art of our time has to be fundamental, precise, all-inclusive. It is the art of Constructivism. Constructivism is neither proletarian nor capitalistic. . . . It expresses the pure form of nature—the direct color, the spatial rhythm, the equilibrium of form. It is not confined to picture-frame andpedestal. It expands into industry andarchitecture, into objects and relationships. Constructivism is the socialism of vision.14

The contrast to Itten's metaphysical and individualistic tendencies could not be more sharply expressed than it is here. Moholy sought to resolve his affirmation of the constructive aspect, of the machine, and of new technology not only in formal terms but also by experimenting with new techniques and materials.

Among the most spectacular experiments in which Moholy made very early use of the possibilities of modern technology are the so-called

Telephone Pictures. As the artist himself expressed it, they sought to

demonstrate that a complete mechanization of techniques poses no threat to the essential creative power. . . . Painting by hand may retain historical significance; sooner or later, however, it will lose its exclusivity. . . . In 1922 I ordered by telephone fivepictures ofporcelain enamel from a sign company. I had the company's color scheme in front of me andI sketched my pictures on graph paper. On the other end of the line the director of the company had the same squared paper before him. One of the pictures was delivered in three different sizes, so that I could study the fine differences in color relations that result from enlargement and reduction.15

Moholy, the artist's first wife and his closest collaborator during the early twenties,16 has attempted to strip away the embellishment of legends that has surroundedthe creation of these Telephone Pictures; in her book Marginal Notes she indicates that Moholy, satisfied with the result of industrially produced enamel pictures, cried out: “I could even have ordered them by phone!" She also notes that in a commentary on these pictures the artist merely made the hypothetical suggestion that “Imight even have done it over the telephone."17 In light of this, it would not seem quite so certain that, as is generally asserted in art historical literature, Moholy actually used the telephone to communicate aesthetic instructions coded in units of millimeters to the company manufacturing the pictures. In any case, the idea of ordering pictures by telephone can be found several years earlier in an essay in the Dada-Almanach that Richard Huelsenbeck published in 1920: "The good painter can be recognized by the fact that he orders his pictures from the carpenter by phone."18 Whether or not the telephone actually playeda role in ordering enamel pictures, it must be considered a pioneering act that Moholy proved by example that art in the industrial age could consist of an anonymous machine process of high precision and exist independently of the personal intervention of the artist's hand; thus the act of artistic creation should be seen in the intellectual aspect and not in the manual one.

One important field of experimentation for Moholy-Nagy was photography; his wife, Lucia, a trained photographer, put him on the track of its unsuspected aesthetic possibilities. Apparently without any knowledge of the experiments of Christian Schad (schadograms) and Man Ray (rayograms), Moholy began in 1922 to explore photography without a camera.19 The result was the invention of the photogram.2º The early photograms correspond closely in form to the artist's constructivist approach to design, and they come across as translations of his pictorialproblems from painting into photography. By contrast, the later photograms, which are much freer in form, show a genuiné interest in actively modulating space by means of nonobjective light forms.²1 The separation of conceptual work and its material execution that was typical for the Telephone Pictures is also, in somewhat modified form, relevant to the photograms. In his subtle analysis of Moholy-Nagy's photographic oeuvre, Andreas Haus explicitly alluded to this aspect: “He [Moholy] sought to eliminate completely all material work . . . and transposed the process of 'design' into the intellectual disposition of 'preparation. In doing so he was performing as an artist the ,equivalent of the separation of manual and intellectual labor found in modern technological production. Whereas until then the artist was still the bearer of the ideology of ’identical' and thus unalienated work, Moholy enthusiastically turned over to mechanical means the process of actually shapingthematerial and insteadmerely designed its 'application.' In the photogram, finally, it is light that does the work."22 In this respect Moholy adopted an attitude that is modern in the sense that it is clearly distinct from the back ward-looking utopias of the arts and crafts of the nineteenth century and, in their wake, of the founding manifesto of the Bauhaus. Moholy saw an opportunity to overcome alienated work not by reconstructing a lost unity of the art and the crafts but rather by “implementing means of production using artistically 'productive' design and thus relieving it of its heteronomy."23 This was very compatible with Gropius'spostulate of a new unity of art and technology (1923), so that Otto Stelzer was right to remark that the Gropius era did not really begin with the founding of the Bauhaus in 1919 but with Moholy's appointment in 192324 (which might suggest that the early years of the Bauhaus could be called the Itten era).

We return from this brief digression to Moholy-Nagy's use of photography. In addition to his experiments in the area of abstract photography without a camera (photograms with objective forms like heads, hands, and so on, are rare), we should mention a second variation: the camera photographs,2⁵ which stood out by their unusual framing and camera angles (close-ups, bird's-eye andworm's-eye views, diagonal compositions) and became models for the development of the so-called Neues Sehen (New vision)26 in the photography of the twenties. They are not realistic in the sense of striving for an objective portrayal of reality but rather are an example of "using and testing Constructivist theories of perception and composition as applied to reality. This results . .. in quite novel aesthetic insights which could not have been attained either through photography or through painting by themselves."27 Many of Moholy's photographs are characterized by the sort of strange states of floating and weightlessness that are also found—though in different form—in the photographic works of other members of the Bauhaus (Walter Peterhans, for example, but also some of the Bauhaus students who did photography) and can be interpreted as the expression of a desire typical of the time to escape “beyond the order of gravitational forces" and “perspectival spatial structure"28 of the certitude of earth. Jeannot Simmen has describedfloatingas the definitive sign of modernism. There is sufficient proof of this: Lissitzky's dream for the future of overcoming the foundations, Malevitch's "separation from earthly points of orientation in an omnidimensional, cosmic perspective,"29 and Kandinsky's liberation from the material and the substitution of the pictorial center with the homelessness of the “anywhere." In the same way the gravitational relationships in Moholy's photography using a camera are often indeterminate; a clear orientation with respect to up and down is no longer possible; all directions are of equal value. This is confirmed by Moholy-Nagy's comments on his photograph In the Sand, which shows a womanon the beach photographed from above: “Formerly regarded as distortion, today a startling experience! An invitation to re-evaluate our way of seeing. This picture can be turned round. It always produces new vistas."30 This means "recognizing the four corners of the image as the only system of orientation. Top is no longer below here; gravity is suspended; one looks for a handhold on the edges."31

A third facet of Moholy's occupation with the medium of photography consists of the photo montages he himself called photo sculptures. In these he succeeded in combining a constructivist asceticism of form with a Dadaistic pictorial humor. Moholy's photo sculptures differ from Dadaistic photo montages, which are often overburdened with individual elements, in their sparing use of photographic citations as well as in their clarity and transparence of pictorial organization. The photo sculpture makes it possible to

use photography to communicate discoveries andtrains of thought that couldnot be achieved to the same extent by other means. Visual and mental aspects are accessible in a moment, must be accessible in a moment, if the effect is to be achieved. For that reason, a balanced composition of the mental and the optical is an especially important component here. The visual structure of these photo sculptures is not, however, composition in the old sense, not a solution of form and harmony for its own sake, but composition formedin pursuit of the goal that has been seen: theformationof ideas.32

The phenomenon of light in its various forms plays an important role in Moholy's artistic oeuvre—not just in photography but also in painting and sculpture, so that the artist once referred to himself as a “Lichtner (manipulator of light)."33

In the paintings of his second, constructivist phase (from 1923–24 on) Moholy-Nagy's interest in the phenomenon of light took the shape of exhibiting geometrical forms in transparent penetration. Inspired by traditional glaze painting, in which—starting with a bright chalk or plaster ground and an imprimatura that is usually greenish—the forms are worked out using many translucent layers of glaze, Moholy got the idea that "easel painting should be understood as a detour in the path of painting with direct light. At one time pigment was recognized as e place for storing up light, but later this original idea was forgotten."34 So the artist took up this ancient method of transparent penetration as the design principle for his constructivist paintings. In the first issue of bauhaus journal in 1926, in an article titled "Geradlinigkeit des Geistes: Umwege der Technik" (The straightness of the mind: Detours of technique), he formulated his experiments in the phrase “The immanent mind seeks: light, light! The detour of technique finds: pigment." And later: "All light design right up to the present has been following this detour of the tracks of Western painting, even though there have been direct paths to light's magic spell ever since the first magic lantern, the first camera obscura:playing with projectingandreflecting, with colorful, flooding light, fluid, material floating, transparent fall of glowing colors, vibration of space with shimmering emulsion of light. . . .35

It is only logical, then, that Moholy increasingly concentrated during the twenties on the possibilities of direct light design, not only in the photographs and photograms mentioned above but also in his sculptures. For example, from 1922 on, he began to produce three-dimensional compositionsof wood, glass, andmirror-polished metals. They were static sculptures “in which the movement of the viewer reveals the first stage of a free play of light in space that is produced by the mirrorlike, reflecting material,"36 sculptures whose form was inspired in part by the constructions that could be viewed in Berlin in the First Russian Art Exhibition of 1922.

Moving beyond these static sculptures, in a constant processof seekingandfindingfrom 1922 to 1930 that was marked by fruitful collaboration with technicians and engineers (including AEG, among others), Moholy-Nagy created the famous kinetic Light Prop, better known as the Light-Space Modulator. He clearly he thought more about the possibilities for concrete application of the things he derived from its example (from stage design to lighted advertisements) than the possibilities of presenting it in a museum.

It seems appropriate to pause here for a moment and reflect on Moholy's activities as an artist of light. For some time now it has been fashionable to go against the grain of the history of high modernism, as it were, by searching for the hidden spiritual, metaphysical, even occult and esoteric sources on which many of the supposedly so rational artistic concepts of the early twentieth century fed.40 Veit Loers views the whole oeuvre of Moholy-Nagy's light art against this sort of spiritual, "cosmic-gnostic background,"41 and indeed it is possible to find in the artist's writings indications of cosmic inclinations, nor should his early contacts with theosophy and Mazdaism be ignored (in this he was surprisingly close to Johannes Itten). Nevertheless, it is still difficult to answer the question of the spiritual content and metaphysical quality of Moholy's experiments with light. For if, on the one hand, in Molderings's view, the artist's photograms "represent self-referential structures,"42 they cannot, on the other, simultaneously be expressions of a "spiritual and transcendental metaphysicsof light."43 As quoted above, as early as 1922, Moholy himself had declared the “transcendental spiritualism of previous times"44 to be obsolete. At the conclusion of this section we should mention briefly Moholy-Nagy as typographer. The principles of the New Typography of the twenties, which Moholy showed to advantage in the Bauhausbücher series that he edited together with Gropius (clarity and legibility, a preference for sans serif typefaces, asymmetrical arrangements of type, emphasis marked by underlining) cannot be discussed in detail here. Moholy himself commented on several theoretical aspects of the new type design in his essays “Die Neue Typographie" (The New Typography; 1923) and "ZeitgemäßeTypographie"(Contemporary typography; 1926).46Although Moholy's typographic works often have touches that seem somewhat forced (this is especially true of the layout ofKlee's Pädagogisches Skizzenbuch[Pedagogical sketchbook]), andalthough eventhen the decorative character of the underlining for emphasis was attacked,47 the artist nevertheless succeeded in giving the fourteen volumes of Bauhausbücher a relatively unified appearance that influenced“two generations of progressive typographers."48

Shortly after Gropius resigned as director of the Bauhaus in 1928, Moholy too left the staff of the school. In Berlin he began a successful career as a stage designer, typographer, and designer of industrial fairs. Under the political pressure of fascism he felt compelled to emigrate for a second time. In 1934 he went to Holland, then in 1935 to England, and finally in 1937 to the United States of America, where he established the New Bauhaus in Chicago. Financial difficulties soon led to the closing of the successor institute to the Bauhaus, but in 1938 in Chicago he opened his own School of Design, which was later granted university status as the Institute of Design and, even after Moholy-Nagy's death in 1946, was responsible for spreading the ideas of the Bauhaus throughout the United States. In addition, Moholy's (posthumous)book Vision in Motion,49 which was reprinted many times, also contributed to the broad reception of the ideas of this Hungarian universal artist in the United States (see chapter 13).

## An Outline of a Theory of Art

Moholy's ability to quickly respond to new stimuli and to transform them productively was true not only of his artistic work but also of his pedagogical practice and of the formulation of his own theory of art. Whereas the previous section emphasized design problems and thus could only suggest theoretical aspects in passing, now we will sketch Moholy's theory, a clever amalgam of varied intellectual approaches.

Literary and theoretical reflection was for Moholy (as it was for Mondrian, van Doesburg,

Malevitch, Lissitzky, Rodchenko, and Kandinsky) not just a supplementary commentary but rather an integral part of his activity as an artist, to the extent that Andreas Haus is inclined to assume that Moholy's significance and fame in the awareness of the art public is based more on his theoretical work than on his artistic oeuvre itself.50

In contrast to metaphysical determinations of the function of art, an individualistic understanding of the artist's role, and an idealistic conception of the creative act (views that were held to a great extent by Itten, Kandinsky, Klee, and to some extent even Schlemmer), and despite the spiritual orientation discussed in the previous section, Moholy adopted a standpoint that was decidedly focused on society, from which perspective he hoped that "we, the creators of our own time, should go to work with up-to-date means."51 Consequently, his artistic practice was ultimately not concerned with the search for a new spirituality (Itten) or the "pure and eternally artistic" (Kandinsky), not with the design of a transcendental counterpart to existing reality (Klee) or the foundation of an ideal image of humanity (Schlemmer), but rather with productively coping with the technical and urban world of the industrial age by exploiting the new media like photography andfilm that are“calculated to convey news authentically."52 A first suggestion of such a position can be found in the "Manifesto on Elemental Art" of October 1921, which was signed by Moholy along with Raoul Hausmann, Hans Arp, and Ivan Puni:

We love bold invention, a renewal in art. Art is the product of all the forces of an epoch. We live in the present. Hence we demand the products of our epoch, we demand an art that can only originate from us, which did not exist before us and will not exist after us—that is not like changing fashion but the result of understanding that art is something new and does not stop at the products of the past. We stand for elemental art. Art is elemental because it does not philosophize, because it builds up itsproducts from elements of its own. To yield to the elements of creativity means to be an artist. The elements of art cannot be determined by the artist alone. They do not originate in his individual and arbitrary will; the individual is not a separate unit and the artist is only an exponent of forces which make manifest the elements of the world.53

This manifesto is interesting for two reasons. First, because it contains an unrestricted affirmation of the present and a sharp rejection of the sort of individualistic ideology that sees the artist as an individual who stands above society, as it were, and as the designer of timeless realities that point beyond the earthly toward the cosmic (see the chapter's 8 and 9). With certain nuances of difference, this standpoint was typicalfor all the socially and politically engaged artists of the early twenties—from Theo van Doesburg to El Lissitzky and Hans Richter. Raoul Hausmann, whose ideas had a lasting influence on Moholy's thought, had expressed himself along similar lines a good half year before this manifesto, in February 1921: "let's grasp the present at every second. . . . We want to be new, bold, and more than American, we want to be entirely disrespectful. . . . The beauty of our daily life is determined by mannequins, the toupee art of hairdressers, the precision of technical construction!"54

We need not emphasize here again that the fascination with "technical construction" played a role in Moholy-Nagy's entire oeuvre. Instead, we must examine more closely the aspect of the elemental, which is central to the "Manifesto on Elemental Art" and also played a significant role at the Bauhaus. In this context, we should point to the empiriocriticism that was founded by Richard Avenarius (1843–96) and worked out by Ernst Mach (1838-1916) in particular and then developed as empiriomonism by Alexander Bogdanov (1873-1928), a student of Avenarius. The central thesis of empiriocriticism—a positivist philosophical movement, influential in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, that was hostile to metaphysics and aimed at achieving pure experience—was that there is no objective reality that exists independently of consciousness and sensation, but rather that reality consists of sensations of the elements of the real that are manifested as optic, haptic, acoustic, and other sense data. ("Sensation" here—-as in Hölzel—is used in the physiological sense as a stimulus to perception and is not-as in Itten—synonymous with “feeling.") According to Mach, matter is not the primary given but rather "colors, sounds, spaces, and times are for us the last elements whose given context we still need to explore."55 With the help of the subjective epistemological modelof empiriocriticism-—according to which reality is constructed in the subject itself by means of the formation of complexes of sensation—it should be possible for the individual to seem to regain dominance over an external (social) reality that has become all but unfathomable and uncontrollable and thus to overcome alienation.56

One interestingaspect ofempiriocriticism that sheds light on Moholy's educational program is the view that it is the task of art to train human sense organs to increase the sensory faculties—or, more precisely, the ability of the subject to constitute reality from sensory elements. This functionalizes aesthetic enjoyment by attributing to it the effect of building up and strengthening the ego. In this view, art is neither an end in itself nor a means of perception but rather an instrument to "strengthen and project the personality of the ego into its surroundings";57 this is a biological perspective that is found for the first time in Nietzsche. An explicit aspect of this functional conception of art is the idea of educating people to find aesthetic enjoyment even in their modern, technological, and urban surroundings, the "chemist's scale," the "machine as such," the "belching smokestack,"58 and it is clear that this aesthetic ideology found deep resonance at the Bauhaus.

In the empiriomonism of Bogdanov, the central figure of the Soviet Proletkult movement, the epistemology of empiriocriticism is applied to society: "Social life is consciously psychological in all its manifestations. .. . Sociality is inseparable from consciousness. Social being and social consciousness are . . . identical. ... Every material process of production is always a procedure in the consciousness of its observer.. . . Epistemologically speaking, then, the external process of production is not the prius but rather the subject or subjects are."59 After the October Revolution, Bogdanov gave this theory, which in fact sharply contrasts with that of Marx, a new, more Marxian profile as part of his general theory of organization ("tectology"). In this new theory, art organizes the human being as a unity of consciousness and the psychophysical; and artistic work is one aspect (alongside the political and economic aspects) of the organization, the structuring, of the new socialist society. This understanding of artistic work as socially productive work was the basis for the art theory of the Russian productivists, whose ideas Moholy-Nagy encountered in 1922 in the Hungarian exile magazine Egység. Productivism emphasized the necessity of placing artistic productivity, which is unconnected to society and often bogged down in formal and aesthetic aspects, under obligation to the ideology of socialism. That meant transferring it from an abstract and transcendental plane to the plane of social reality60 or, as Osip Brik put it, from the nonutilitarian laboratories to the laboratories of real life. This certainly does not mean that aesthetic production could be reduced to a utilitarianaspect like the design of functional objects, for what is meant is another utility, namely, that of a deliberate organization of human consciousness with an eye to developing socialist relations of production and society. This was to be achieved by means of a"transformation of the material elements of industrial culture into volumes, planes, color, space, and light,"61 that is, by visualizing the new social meaning of material production.

Though it may be possible to imagine this concept of a transformation of the material elements of industrial culture into elemental artistic categories like plane, color, space, and light, the question is what all this has to do concretely with Moholy-Nagy, with his theory and pedagogy of art.

There can be no doubt that the principle of a comprehensive training of the senses of the sort found in the empiriocritical theory of elements of sensation and later adopted by Moholy—after being interpreted and modified by Raoul Hausmann,62 among others—played a special role in his educational program. At the same time, however, it must be emphasized that for Moholy this educational goal was not purely subjectively determined, as it is in empiriocriticism, but is rather founded in society, much as it was for the politically engaged avant-garde in Russia.

In this context it is important to recall that the Hungarian exiles who emigrated to Austria after the fall of the Hungarian soviet republic and were associated with the exile magazines Ma and Egység were among the first “in Central Europe to come into contact with the new Russian avantgarde."63 Moholy—who had been close to the Ma group even in Hungary and who was the Berlin correspondent for the magazine Ma, under the direction of Lajos Kassák—actively participated in the theoretical discussions of the day regarding questions of the political and social goals of constructivism. Such questions were discussed, as they were everywhere in those days, at the International Congress cf Progressive Artists in May 1922 in Düsseldorf. Theo van Doesburg, El Lissitzky, and Hans Richter declared in a joint statement at that congress:

We define the progressive artist as one who denies the hegemony of the subjective in art and struggles against it, and whose works are not structured according to lyrical whimsy but on the principle of the new design according to systematic organization of means to achieve an expression that can be universally understood. . ..

Art, just as much as science and technology, is a method for organizing life in general.

We observe that art today is ceasing to be a dream that opposes itself to the reality of the world, that it is ceasing to be a means to discover cosmic secrets. Art is a universal and genuine expression of the creative energy that is organizing the progress of humanity, that is to say, it is a tool of the general process of work.

In order to translate it into reality, we must struggle, and for this struggle we must be organized. . . .64

With this statement van Doesburg, Lissitzky, and Richter took a decisive stand against the tendencies to individualism that were becoming evident at the congress (production of traditional panel paintings, traditional art exhibitions), which they saw as an impediment to the formation of a planned union of progressive (i.e., socially constructive) artists.

Together with Ludwig Kassák, Ernst Kállai, and others, Moholy-Nagy aligned himself with this critique of principles in a statement "from the artists of the activist magazine Ma." The statement criticized

the emphasis on the special interests of the “individual artists"; by contrast, we observe life from the social standpoint of humanity, aware that man as an individual is an atom that combines with others to form society. Consequently, his creativity must always be the statement of one person equipped with collective forces. . . . For that reason . . . , [we] want to proffer everything to encourage the active cooperation of all creative spirits who believe in the collective society of the future as the only possible basis for the complete development of our creative life.

The first task in the realm of artistic work is to show that art has renounced any significance as a means of expressing subjective, psychological experiences. . . .65

In a phase of historical revolution, in which the existing capitalist society (apparently) does not need the artist but in which the new, socialist society does not yet exist,66Moholy believed that the decisive task of the designer was gradually to "build an organized social order, or even better, to promote the development of a more human human being"67 with the goal of helping to overcome the "modern system of production" characterized by “drudgery, mad rushing, a lack of social plan, the extortion of profit."68Unlike many a socialist romantic of the nineteenth century, Moholy was not thinking of eliminating the system of production of industrial society but rather of humanizing it. The fundamental affirmation of the machine, of technology, of industry and its possibilities derives not only from his own artistic practice (the use of advanced technological means such as photography or film) but also from countless theoretical statements that reveal an unmistakable affinity with the Russian productivists: "We need the machine. Without any romanticism. . . ."69 Or:

[T]echnical progress is a factor of life which develops organically. . . . Notwithstanding its manifold distortion by profit interests, the struggle for mere accumulation and the like, we can no longer think of life without such progress. It is an indispensable factor in raising the standard of life. It could level out screaming differences [my emphasis—R.W.] and, bridging time and space, help to prepare understanding between people. The possibilities of the machine . . . has necessarily led to a mass production which has its own significance, and the task of the machine—satisfaction of mass requirements—will in the future be held more and more singly and clearly in mind. .. . The solution lies accordingly not in working against technical advance, but— if correctly understood from the social standpoint—in exploiting it. Through technique man can be freed, if he finally realizes—for what purpose.70

Thus the goal was to take the human being who has been deformed and denatured by the capitalist system andreturn him to his“biological bases," to help him recognize his "organic functions," and to make him aware that he himself and not profit or the mechanical product—is the one who stands as the psychophysical unity at the center of production.71

Moholy's goals were liberation from the alien control of the capitalist system—that is, overcoming alienated work and experiencing work instead as an “expression of organic forces"72 and as creative self-realization—and the renewal of the self along the lines of the original "total human being" (an unmistakable reflection of Marxist thought).73 These goals seem to have derived their strength not from a politically oriented theory of socialism but rather from a diffuse social humanism interspersed with touches of organological theory. Thus Moholy also asserted "that the class struggle is, in the last analysis, not about capital, nor the means of production, but in actuality it concerns the right of the individual to a satisfying occupation, work that meets the inner needs, a normal way of life and a real release of human powers."74

Correspondingly, despite his insight into the fundamental class antagonisms of capitalist society, Moholy did not seek out the possibility of a violent revolutionary struggle that could transform the political, economic, and social macrostructures from outside, as it were, but rather hoped for a revolution from inside. Thus, in keeping with the idea of the individual as an atom that combines with others to form society (as expressed in the Ma statement), he believed the desired changes could be achieved by starting with the individual and working to develop his “organic, biologically determined functions" (especially sense perception) as well as “the creative energies found in [human] nature."75 Art—namely, as we have seen, elemental art, whichfor Moholy is the constructivist art that requires entirely new ways of seeing—represents a decisive instrument in this process, an instrument that significantly increases the sensibility of the human being, guarantees for the individual a creative and actively shaping position in the face of the forces of a highly developed technology, and immunizes it against the mechanism of  "purely material evaluation of his vitality."76 (lt is astonishing to see the ways in which Moholy's ideas correspond on central points with Marcuse's social philosophy of freedom, both in the diagnosis of capitalist society and its corresponding therapy. In Marcuse's Essay on Liberation of 1969 in particular, there are astonishing parallels to Moholy's theory of art.)77 For Moholy art is an "indirect means of education that sharpens human senses and protects them against all sorts of surprise attacks, and does so with an intuitive certainty. ... For example, film (and the montage principle in general) is a kind of training in lightning-fast observation of simultaneous existences on all levels of creativity. . . ."78Moholy's proximity to the ideas of empiriocriticism (as applied to society) can be felt as clearly in this passage as it can in the pithy formula "Constructivism is the socialism of vision."79

In the view of Andreas Haus, Moholy saw his "lifelong task" in "reconfiguring 'technology' from being an alien force to an arsenal of ever new playful possibilities for self-realization. He was 'productive' in the sense that he wanted to optimize not the pure 'function' but the 'lived experience' of the ego in its technical surroundings, and he also became concretely 'subversive' in an undramatic way by reinterpreting all the forms produced by the technological and industrial world away from their economic use by others toward their individual and vital use values. Moholy's instruction in the New Vision carries the seed of an instruction to revolt against the alienated bourgeois world."80 This is surely the horizon along which Moholy developed his preliminary course at the Bauhaus. We should not overlook, however, that at the same time Moholy risked being caught “in the snares of his functional pragmatism,"81 which can be seen in the artist's activity as a master of form in the metal workshop.

## Master of Form in the Metal Workshop

The metal workshop of the Bauhaus under the direction of Itten (see “Between Art and Design" in chapter 5)andSchlemmer producedcraftsmanship of quality, but it was hardly something that pointed the way to the future, even if it did experiment with geometrical form elements in a decorative sense. From 1923 on, by contrast, Moholy-Nagy successfully accomplishedthe switch to industrial design that was typical of the Bauhaus at this stage in its development. This development had been introduced shortly before Moholy took over the metal workshop, when the Bauhaus student Carl J. Jucker designed an extensible electric wall lamp, as Moholy described it:

withdevices for pushing andpulling, heavy strips and rods of iron and brass, looking more like a dinosaur than a functionalobject. But even this was a great victory, for it meant a new beginning. After this we developed lighting fixtures introducing such useful ideas as: the closefitting ceiling cap; combinations of opaque and frosted glass in simple forms technically determined by the action of light; securing the globe to the metal chassis; the use of aluminum, particularly for reflectors, etc. All of these were adopted for industrialproduction.... The metal workshop also handled other problems of industrial design: utensils and household appliances.

The function of the metal workshop was a special one, involving simultaneously education and production. We therefore selected for young apprentices problems from which the use of materials, tools and machinery could be learnedandwhich were at the same time of practical use. During those days there was so conspicuous a lack of simple and functional objects for daily use that even the young apprentices were able to produce models for industrial production (ash trays, tea holders, etc.) which industry bought and for which royalties were paid.82

Although Moholy apparently did not himself design any prototypes for industry⁸3 but was active only as a teacher—apart from his own autonomous metal sculptures—his historical contribution lies in having forcefully moved the originalproduction style based on craft methods toward standardization and the design of models for industrial mass production, which also established an example and set the standard for the other teaching workshops of the Bauhaus. The metal workshop under Moholy was the first one at the Bauhaus in which the“fundamentals of Bauhaus production" that Gropius proposed could be realized in full:

An object is defined by its nature. In order, then, to design it to function correctly . . . , one must first of all study its nature; for it must serve its purpose perfectly, that is, it must fulfill its function usefully, be durable, economical, and “beautiful."

This research into the nature of objects leads to the conclusion that by resolute consideration of modern production methods, constructions, and materials, forms will evolve that are often unusual and surprising, since they deviate from the conventional. .. .

The creation of standard types for all practical commodities of everyday use is a social necessity. On the whole, the necessities of life are the same for the majority of people. . . . The machine . . . is an effective device, which . . . can free the individual from working manually for the satisfaction of his daily needs and can provide him with mass-produced products that are cheaper and better than manufacturedby hand. ... The Bauhaus workshops are essentially laboratories in which prototypes of products suitable for mass production and typical of our time are carefully developed and constantly improved... .

The crafts of the past have changed, and future crafts will be merged in a new productive unity in which they will carry out the experimental work for industrial production. Speculative experiments in laboratory workshops will yield models and prototypes for productive implementation in factories.84

Moholy fundamentally agreed with these theses proposed by the Bauhaus director, as is clear not only from their shared basic convictions with respect to the social function of industrial design but also manifests itself quite concretely in numerous designs andprototypes for industry (for example, by MarianneBrandt, Wilhelm Wagenfeld, Carl J. Jucker, Gyula Pap), some of which have long since become classics of design. Wulf Herzogenrath reported "that, after long preparation andnegotiations, in early 1928 Moholysigned aproduction agreement with the Leipzig lightingmanufacturers Körting & Mathiesen for a variety of lamps that were designed in the metal workshop and then developed by technicians from the firm for inexpensive mass production. ... Even by 1932, the Leipzig firm had sold (under the brand name Kandem) 50,000 of the lamps designed under Moholy's tenure, and the income from licensing that the Bauhaus received was significantly increased."85

There is no question that from 1923 on Moholy had an enormous stabilizing effect on the Bauhaus: not only through his consistent transformation of Bauhaus principles into the practice of the designer but also and perhaps especially through his pedagogical activities in the preliminary course after Itten's departure.

## Moholy-Nagy as an Arts Educator

Even today, Moholy's importance as an arts educator has been inadequately appreciated—at least in German-speaking countries.86 It has been very much overshadowed by the extensive reception of the pedagogical approaches of Itten and, to some extent, Albers—in part, perhaps, because these artists, both of whom outlived Moholy by several decades, could participate directly in the postwar discussions of arts education. For example, the two standard works of Johannes Itten, Kunst der Farbe and Mein Vorkurs am Bauhaus were first publishedin 1961 and 1963, respectively (and translated into English as The Art of Color in 1961 and Design and Form in 1964), and have been reprinted several times. Itten's theory of color has been translated into thirteen languages, and with more than 500,000 copies in print has long since become a “sleeper hit."87 Josef Albers's The Interaction of Color—the summa of the experiences of an artist who was closely associated with the Bauhaus during the decisive period of his life—was published first in the United States in 1963 and then in Germany in 1970. These books have doubtless had an eminent influence on arts education and even more so on daily teaching practices in schools, and it seems they continue to do so. Moholy-Nagy's two main theoretical works, by contrast—Malerei, Photographie, Film (1925; 2d ed., 1927; translated as Painting, Photography, Film in 1969) und Von Material zu Architektur (1929; translated as The New Vision in 1932 and in a revised and expanded edition in 1938)—had been published more than thirty years earlier but were long since out of print. The facsimile reprints of these books, which appearedin 1967 and 1968, respectively, in the series Neue Bauhausbücher, had no lasting influence on pedagogy in the arts. This is all the more astonishing because in the arts pedagogy of the years around 1970, with the establishment of the concept of “visual communication," a strong impetus towarda critique and transformation of society was felt, and there was a call to extend arts education beyond the limited scope of art by including all visual media. In contrast to visual communication's one-sided commitment to ideologicalcritique and thus aprimarily cognitive approach to visual media, the 1970s increasingly introduced new formulations of the goal into the discussions of the fieldinfluenced by Marcuse, among others—that can be summed up in the phrase “emancipationist sensory nature." It is not difficult to see that elements of this more recent discussion appeared some fifty years earlier in Moholy's art theory and in his practice as an artist and teacher, though admittedly with different emphases and in another historical context. Still, Moholy—for whom art was not merely something individual but always eminently social as well, for whom art represented an opportunity to approach life actively and to change existing circumstances—was barely acknowledged by the socalled critical arts pedagogy. This may be because, as a master at the Bauhaus and a constructivist artist, he was unthinkingly identified with an affirmative functionalism and an apolitical formalism. This approach ignores illuminating statements like the following: “This so-called 'apolitical' approach to art is a deception. Politics is not intended here in its sense of political parties but as a path to realizing ideas of the welfare of the general population. . . . Art can apply pressure just as energetically to the sociobiological solution of problems as social revolutionaries can to political initiatives. The difficulty is that, because of inadequate education, few people are sensitive enough to perceive the true meaning of art, no matter whether it is modern or ancient. . . ."88 This articulates one of Moholy's central goals for education, that of educating the sensibility of the students, though this should certainly not be misunderstood in a subjective or private sense. In his book Von Material zu Architektur—which was originally to be called Von Kunst zu Leben (From art to life), and which not only gives concrete information about Moholy's basic teachings at the Bauhaus but also contains his pedagogical credo, as it were—he examined this set of problems more closely in the section titled “Erziehungsfragen" (Questions of education). Moholy saw the need for aesthetic education not in increasing humanity's sensitivity to art but rather, more generally, in "leading it to a richer and more active enjoyment of the modern world."89

In Moholy's view, this goal can only be achieved if the approach to education in a society that is organized to a large degree according to the division of labor and divided according to function in a way that produces a "segmented human being"90 is not "carried so far that the individual becomes stunted. ... The segmented human being must again be based in the central human being who grows organically within a community: strong, open, happy, like a child. Without this organic security the richest differentiations of specialized study . .. are mere quantitative acquisitions, bringing no intensification of life, no widening of its scope. ... The future needs the whole man."91

This call for the "whole man" was in no way original. It coincided with the goal that determined the actions of, for example, Itten and other progressive art educators of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. One decisive nuance, however, was that for Moholy totality meant permeation of all spheres of life with a deliberate introduction of modern technology.

Although he saw the increasing technology and mechanization of the modern system of production as the main cause behind the creation of the alienated"segmented human being," he did not form part of that great choir of cultural pessimists who would have been happiest if they could have banned technology and the machine entirely from their surroundings. Moholy emphatically included technology in his theoretical conception and his educational program, as we saw in the previous section on his artistic work. This had nothing to do with an unreflected and uncritical affirmation of a dubious doctrine of progress but rather took place against the background of a humane approach that was expressed pointedly in the phrase "Not the product, but man, is the end in view."92 And Moholy stated further: "[T]echnical progress should never be the goal, but always the means"93 to assure the premises for an optimal, complete realization of the self in a free society. This implies not only the elimination of that which Marcuse denounced as "additional oppression"—that is, as the "subordination of human beings to the apparatus"94(in order to share in the “exploitative abundance," it is necessary for people to work more and harder than is actually necessary to satisfy their material needs)—but also the strengthening of sensory functions, the exercise of inventiveness, and the cultivation of the creative in order to be able to develop in the world while taking an active role in shaping it. In his pedagogical reflections, Moholy started art from a premise that anticipated Beuys's dictum "Everyone is an artist" by almost half a century:

Everyone is talented. Every healthy man has a deep capacity for bringing to development the creative energies found in his nature. . . .

Everyone is equipped by nature to receive and assimilate sensory experiences. Everyone is sensitive to tones and colors, has sure touch and space reactions, etc. This means that by nature everyone is able to participate in all the pleasures of sensory experiences, that any healthy man can also become a musician, painter, sculptor, architect, just as when he speaks, he is "a speaker." That is, he can give form to his reactions in any material (which is not, however, synonymous with “art".. .).95

This is the anthropological foundation on which Moholy built his philosophy of education. In his pedagogical efforts to overcome the segmented human being, Moholy referred not only to the tradition sketched in chapter 5 reaching from Pestalozzi and Fröbel by way of Montessori up to Lichtwark and Cizek and the whole of reform pedagogy but also and especially to the experiments in music pedagogy made by Heinrich Jacoby (1889–1964), to which Moholy expressly alluded in various places.96 In the work of Jacoby, who worked from 1913 on in the school Jaques Dalcroze founded in Hellerau and who founded a "Iaboratory for observing the sociological and psychological conditions of creative, cultural activities" in Berlin in 1925,97 we can find formulations of the ideas that Moholy presented that correspond even down to the choice of words: "Everyone with healthy sense organs is, by virtue of being human, capable of expressing himself in music. That is not as paradoxical as it might sound to many people at first. For music is a very universal means of human expression. ... Naturally, in this context we are not referring to ’art music'. .. . We are dealing with an elementary area of expression, like language, gestures, or pictorial representation."98

Moholy felt the opportunities for realizing a pedagogical concept basedon anopen-ended concept of talent—that is, on the fundamental possibility of any individual to develop his or her creativity—had been recognized at the Bauhaus in that it did not place "'subjects' at the head of its curriculum, but man, in his natural readiness to grasp the whole of life."99According to Moholy, the first year at the Bauhaus was “directed towardthe development and enrichment of feeling, sensation and thought—especially for those young people, who in consequence of the usual education, have brought with them asterile hoard of text-book information."100 And even the specialized training in one of the Bauhaus workshops should keep an eye on the goal of the "total human being," the human being "who, if he but works from his biological center, when faced with all the material things of life can again take his position with instinctive sureness, who does not allow himself to be intimidated by industry, the rush tempo, external evidences of an often misunderstood 'machine culture.'"101

## Exercises for the Haptic and Optic Senses

Recognizing that in highly developed industrial societies primary experiences are increasingly supplemented or even replaced by secondary experience from an expanding media reality, Moholy concluded that education must "fall back upon the most primitive sources" in order to achieve “the whole of life."102 As part of this exploitation of“primitive sources," Moholy foresaw a specialplace for the “experience with the material" by means of "primitive tactile exercises." Instead of the“school of seeing"—to borrow a term from Max Burchartz103—Moholy stands for the training of the sense of touch, which has drastically degenerated in modern man. Moholy is concerned with “a grasp of materials through actual experience ... such as is never attained through book knowledge in the usual school exercises and the traditional courses of instruction."104

The idea behind this goal seems to have been prepared by Raoul Hausmann, who—as we have seen—was responsible for the “Manifesto on Elemental Art," and whose eminent influence on Moholy is often overlooked. In his manifestolike essay “PRÉsentismus gegen den Puffkeismus der teutschen Seele" (PREsentism against the puffkeism of the German soul), Hausmann raised two challenges in early 1921 that Moholy-Nagy. would attempt to realize as an artist and teacher: (1) “And so we want eliminate the fixed stare at one thing, because our expanded view has become well rounded and complete thanks to science, because historically we have absorbed all of the optical possibilities into our way of seeing and now we have progressed in optics to the basic phenomena of light [my emphasis—R.W.]. We love light andits movement!!. . . We call for electric, scientific painting!!!"; (2)“[W]e callfor the extension and overcoming of all our senses. We want to explode all boundaries that have existed until now!!!... We call for haptism, just as we call for odorism!!Let us expand the haptic and give it a scientific basis beyond all the arbitrariness that has existed thus far!! Haptic art will expand the human being! . . . We want to set about refining our most important sense; long live the haptic emanation!! ... [H]aptism is the means to differentiate the modern life feeling!"105 Whereas the first of Hausmann's challenges had its effects above all inMoholy-Nagy's own artistic practice (photography, Light Prop), the postulate of haptism (which had also been advocated under the name tactilism by the leader of the Italian futurists, Tommaso Marinetti) had pedagogical and practical significance for the basic instruction that Moholy gave at the Bauhaus. Thus the preliminary course he taught from 1923 to 1928 (together with Albers)regularly began with tactile exercises that continued a tradition started by Itten but with a methodology that differed from that of his predecessor's preliminary course in one important point. Whereas in Itten's course the materials that were to be explored tactilely were freely composed, so that these material montages could have a distinctly picturesque appeal, the combinations of materials producedin Moholy's course were distinguished by a systematic rigor.

For example, in order to exercise the haptic faculties, Moholy-Nagy had tactile tables, wheels, andribbons made on which materials were arranged according to fixed, defined criteria, usually in the form of two-line scales that could bè held at the same time, for example, from smooth to rough or from sharp to dull. The materials on these tactile tables were "thus transformed immediately into 'abstract' qualities and their relations." That means that the point was ultimately not to recognize or experience the material but, more generally, to "condition the senses to focus on facticity of the haptic. The real pedagogical effort consists of translating these haptically stabilized sensory values into optical ways of perceiving. This translation is somewhat reminiscent of teaching a blind person to orient himself in space."106 In fact, the training of the senses in Moholy's preliminary course beganwith the students identifying various materials by touch while blindfolded.

![](images/caa0ee09ebf1a5f1bcf77f21b2e14077ebe70c2191361296365fbd33d18d98d0.jpg)

The training of the sense of touch in the preliminary course, as we have just suggested, was not an end in itself, nor did it serve to teach a new form of artistic expression (as Marinetti's tactilism did);107 rather, it was seen as a pedagogically necessary preliminary stage for the process of developing the optical sense of perception that would follow. To the latter end, students were told to transform the material montages they had made themselves into drawings and/or paintings, with the goal of producing a representation of reality that was as authentic—that is, as true to nature—as possible. This served two goals: it sharpened optical sense perception and provided practice in fundamental techniques of representation.108

The exercises for training the tactile and optical senses do not appear to have been fundamentally different from those Itten gave in his preliminary course. There are, however, two significant differences: first, in the new formal language, whichwas now indebted to constructivism; second, Itten's tactile exercises always retained sensualist, emotional, andexperiential qualities, whereas Moholy sought to objectify what was subjectively felt (here "feeling" means the registering of a physiological stimulus) by means of "tactile diagrams" and in this way made accessible to an intersubjective reconstruction. Such a practice not only clearly corresponds to the tendencies toward systematization and rationalization of the design process that were clearly recognizable at the Bauhaus from about 1923 on but can also be seen as a logical consequence of Raoul Hausmann's theses that were discussed above.

For Moholy, a rational approach to design began with language, and so he made an effort to establish terminology to distinguish between the appearance of various materials and to define these terms precisely. His goal was to establish linguistic rules that were as unambiguous as possible for terms like“structure” [Struktur], “texture" [Textur], and“surface aspect" [Faktur], which had been used inconsistently.109

Moholy defined structure as the "unalterable manner in which the material is built up," that is, the inner makeup of the material.

Moholy called texture the "organically resulting outward surface"; he also spoke here of “epidermis, organic texture.'

Surface aspect—the German word Faktur (derived from the Latin facere, “to make") was, according to Moholy, “both the manner and the appearance of the surface, the sensorily perceptible result (the effect) of the working process, which shows itself in any treatment of material, as in the upper surface of material which has undergone change through external factors (epidermis, artificial)."

It is confusing here that Moholy asserted that this external effect "may be due either to elemental cause (influences of nature) or to mechanical causes, as by machine treatment and the like."110 This equation of natural influence (like the surface aspect of wood eaten by bark beetles, the surface of waves on the sea caused by the wind) and human intervention (for example, the effect of hammer blows on a sheet of metal) was clearly made possible for Moholy through the medium of photography, which can transform the different surfaces—whether made by hand or by machine or even by natural effects—"into the same apparent form"111 according to the point of viewor the light relationships. Photography— and Moholy used examples from photography to make his terminology clear—thus proves to be a medium capable of great abstraction, leveling out the specific features of things but also emphasizing thedifference between natural processes and human work. Thus “Moholy also manages to postulate light as the most general equivalent of allphenomena of surface aspect and thus to 'sublimate' the material appearance of things in an extreme form."112

A fourth property of materials, one which Moholy closely associated with the category of surface aspect and in fact subsumed to it, is the "regular, rhythmical, or else irregular, massing."

## Studies of Spatial Experience

Alongside the exercises to train the haptic and optic senses, the exercises in three-dimensional construction represented another focus of Moholy's "general system of elements."117

Whereas the three-dimensional assemblages of found objects that were created under Itten are often marked by a farcical charm and humorous witticisms, the exercises in construction under Moholy were directed at the systematic study of problems of bodies and space and were intended to lead to constructive solutions. Consequently, these studies in wood, sheet metal, glass, wire, and string "served primarily to develop visual perception and thinking regarding construction, static and dynamic factors, balance and space" and "carried fundamental significance for later practice in all fields of design."118

In his programmatic work on pedagogy, The New Vision, Moholy distinguished among "five stages of development of sculpture from the standpoint of treatment of the material," which he arranged under the guiding principle of "from static treatment to movement."

The five stages are:

1. blocked-out

2. modelled (hollowed out)

3. perforated (bored through)

4. equipoised

5. kinetic (moving)119

Moholy gave examples of this development from the history of art as well as illustrations from the avant-garde of the time—ranging from the prehistorical idol by way of sculptures by Leonardo, Carpeaux, Rodin, and Brancusi to Picasso and

Naum Gabo, to name just a few—and he supplemented these visual samples with concisely stated commentaries. He also cited examples to support his five-stage model that did not derive from the art world, for example, the Caaba in Mecca (blocked-out), the lattice structure of a radio tower (perforated), or the light diagram of an illuminated carousel turning at night (kinetic). Hannah Steckel has remarked that with this book Moholy demonstrated not only a “comprehensive authority in the visual sector of art" but also an “astonishing knowledge of the areas of nonartistic visual experience of his day."120

A characteristic feature in the development from the block to the kinetic is a decrease in volume, or to put it another way, the reduction of mass. This was a problem that was given particular attention at the Bauhaus: we need only recall, for example, Gropius's transparent architecture or the seemingly dematerialized furniture of MarcelBreuer. Consequently, in his preliminary course Moholy didnot stop with “blocked-out" or “hollowed-out" sculpture (stages 1 and 2) but above all had his students pursue studies that corresponded to more historically advanced stages in the development of sculpture (stages 3 to 5). This resulted in constructions of wood, metal, glass, andplastic that, in their drastically reduced use of materials, clearly dispensedwith anything blocked-out and often want as far as total transparency or defined their physical presence entirely by means of a linear framework. Such exercises in spatial experience would have been inconceivable without the example of the spatial constructions of the Russian constructivists (the Stenberg brothers, Rodchenko, and others) and of course Moholy's own experimental sculptures. At the same time, they clearly reflected a continuing trend toward the economy of means that can be traced at the Bauhaus from the midtwenties on. This was an aspect that was of central importance in the pedagogical practice of Albers in particular. In describing the sculptural studies produced in the preliminary course, Moholy remarked on the problem of economy of materials: "The artist of yesterday concerned himself but little as to exact calculations of balance in his work. Considerations of a few pounds, or even a hundred-weight more or less, did not enter into the older sculp-'ture at all. In the Bauhaus, the students were taught to have strict regard for these components and each gram saved—the effect remaining the.same—often represented a little victory of ingenuity."121

In his preliminary course, Moholydevoted much space to studies of equilibrium, the point of which was to demonstrate how unstable and often fragile constructions composed very sparely from the simplest elements were optically or actually held in balance, either because the constructions were placed on one or more struts or because they hung in space, seemingly free-floating, on barely visible wires. This demonstrates yet again the extent to which problems from the artist's own aesthetic practice——like dematerialization, weightlessness, and the overcoming of gravitational forces (see the section on Moholy's artistic oeuvre)—became part of the instruction. (The problem of weightlessness was dealt with extensively at the Bauhaus by Paul Klee as well, in his Bildnerische Formlehre [translated as The Thinking Eye]; on this, see chapter 9.)

These sorts of "design studies" served two pedagogical goals. First, they performed the task of providing students with insights, by means of their own experimental activity, into elementary categories of visual aesthetics like scale and proportion, static and dynamic, tension and contrast; second, they were intended to pass on to the students a fundamental understanding of the properties and behaviors of various materials (specific gravity, elasticity, strength, and so on).122

In parallel with his own artistic experiments in the area of kinetic sculpture (Light Prop), Moholy also had his students experiment with the fifth stage of sculptural development. This resulted in mobile sculptures that extended the experiences gained in the context of the equilibrium studies and provided new insights into the problems of bodies and space—where space is understood as the “position relation of bodies."123 That this did not exclude questions of practical utility is demonstrated admirably by Franz Ehrlich's sketch of a mechanically adjustable display window or his model for a mechanical display window sculpture, both from 1928.124

## Historical Assessment

The systematic and constructive character of the material and equilibrium studies done in Moholy's course might seem to suggest the idea that we are dealing with a teaching system that rests exclusively on a rational basis. This would seem, moreover, to form a contrast to the clearly recognizable tendencies to an aesthetic and pedagogical irrationalism in Itten's course. However, where the goalis an omnidirectionaldevelopment of the “total human being" and a comprehensive development of his“organic functions," educational approaches to sensory refinement and rational approaches to design problems are not sufficient in and of themselves. So Moholy-Nagy alwayspointed to the significance of the nonrational, of the intuitive in the creative process. This was based on the recognition that

There are naturally countless cases in which the exact calculation of all the functional elements cannot as yet be made—in many cases, perhaps can never be made. In every creative work there is a sphere in which a certain freedom is left to the artist.

The creative problem enters at the point where this freedom begins, where the visible function no longer determines, or at least not wholly, the form. In such cases an instinctive sureness of perception is required, and this is nothing more than the end result of complicated processes, going on in the subconscious, but in the last analysis determined biologically.125

Although this quotation seems to have an undertone of regret that the design process cannot (or cannot yet) be totally rationalized, Moholy-Nagy spoke out resolutely against any formulaic application of “esthetic rules," like those derived from theories of proportion, for example, and emphatically emphasized that “Formulas can never be the basis of creation." He came to the conclusion: "creation needs intuition, on the one hand, and

In contrast to the UnitedStates, where Moholy-Nagyhadwide-ranging resonance as a teacher,129 the reception of his contribution to aesthetic education in Germany can be described as at best fragmentary. Arts pedagogy in schools, whichall too often turns into quasi-theological terminology and diffuse postulates about creativity, has thus far reactedwith silent reserve. For work education, Bodo Wessels has pointed to the importance of Moholy's exercises withmaterials and also taken over his conceptualapparatus of "structure," "texture," and"surface aspect."130 Among the teachers in the Bauhaus tradition, we shouldmention Oskar Holweck's rigorously rationalconception, which explicitly refers to Moholy-Nagy.131 Even so, the reception of Moholy, both in manualarts training and in Holweck, remains superficial in that it has ignored the artist's ambition for a comprehensive approach to education that reached into all aspects of life and sought to change humanity and its sociocultural relationships; insteadit has focusedentirely on the methods andstrategies for obtainingbasic qualifications for designandconcentratedon elementary experiences with materials.

Only a few art historians hereandthere have pointed to the profound significanceof Moholy's systematic approach to developing the senses andrecognized the importance of his pedagogical concept, which is based on the utopia of a new human being in a humane society132 though even they have failed to have a lasting influence on the professional discourse with the established pedagogy of art and design.
