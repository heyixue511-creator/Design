## Introduction

Artists, designers and architects who work with the material of existing context strive to reveal the hidden spirit of a place. Through a process of exposing and exploiting the memories of a situation, and in contrast to the amnesia of much contemporary production, they seek to interpret these meanings and construct an additional layer of consequence that will bestow new value on the place. This approach, which is based upon a perceptive and discriminating reading of place, produces both dynamic and appropriate results. The interior architect, designer and artist all have the opportunity to refl ect upon the contingency, usefulness and emotional resonance of particular places through the examination and appropriation of the existing situation.

The use and reuse of an architectural site creates a direct connection not just with the present, but also with the past. It is a strategy that establishes an explicit relationship with context, not just of the building and its immediate surroundings, but also with the society that constructed it. The reading of a building or site can uncover a layered and stratifi ed narrative. The understanding of the inherent qualities and conditions of building or site can provide clues to the redesign of the place. It is through a thorough knowledge and understanding of the existing condition that the architect or designer can uncover the meaning within a place. This knowledge can be used to activate, liberate and instigate a new future for the situation. And so the architect or designer who is to modify, transform or change the building to accommodate a new use has to adhere not just to the agenda of the new users, but also the intentions of the original building. This act of modifi cation is part of the evolution of the building, it as another layer in the archaeology of the site.

To look closely at a particular situation can cause the familiar to become unfamiliar; equally, somewhere unknown can become recognisable. The initial purpose of this analysis will remove any preconceptions and alleviate any assumptions, so that the qualities of any built environment are not immediately coloured by supposition or prejudice. This will allow comparisons, juxtapositions and correlations to be made. The architect or designer may look at the urban grain, buildings and spaces, history, topography, geology, culture, food, evolution, geography, typologies, climate, population, fl ora, architecture, antiarchitecture, key fi gures, activities, growth and decline, narrative, stratifi cation, spatial development, occupation and defi nition, the meaning of space and probably many other things as well. All of these can inform the adaptation of the site.

Carlo Scarpa is generally considered to be the pre-eminent exponent of the art of reuse. He practised in the second half of the 20th century, mainly in the Veneto area of Italy. His work can be said to epitomise, in microcosm, the very character and nature of that particular area. The Olivetti Showroom in Venice (see page 2) was constructed in the late 1950s. The narrow single space fronts onto the magnifi cent St Mark’s Square and is situated next to a long dark alleyway. Venice has a direct relationship with water, and Scarpa exploited this to dramatic effect within the shop. He conceived the interior of the store as a continuum of the exterior space. The elements of the interior were lifted above a distinct datum line, to both physically and visually account for the regular ingress of the acqua alta or the high tide. The display counters and the storage cupboards are raised by about 400mm above the tiled fl oor, the pattern of which is slightly irregular. This suggests that it is continually moving, and thus the water from the square is both welcome and catered for. The stairs to the mezzanine slide into the main body of the shop, as if offering a step on to dry land. The fi rst fl oor balcony surrounds the shop interior, and this allows a view back onto the watery fl oor, and through the plate glass window into the square. The idea of movement is very much part of the manifesto of the Modernist movement, so this places Scarpa in both Venice and the 20th century. The interior demonstrates a relationship between a specifi c place and its surroundings, and relates the complex story or narrative of the place.

## Structure

This chapter will contain fi ve sections. The fi rst four will reinforce the argument that was developed in the fi rst volume, while the fi fth introduces the sustainable advantages of adaptation.

History and Function will concentrate upon the idea of recollection and expectation that is implicitly contained within any building and how this can be harnessed and interpreted within the adaptation of the structure. Context and Environment argues that the situation in which a building is located has a defi nite infl uence upon the character of the existing and by association the remodelled structure. These may be things immediately surrounding the original structure, and embrace the threedimensional construction of the building itself, but also include things further away and dependent upon the infl uence of climate and the vernacular. The Form and Structure section discusses the manner in which a building was constructed and the infl uence that this will have upon the redesign. Rhythm, confi guration, materials and detail are all important elements that contribute to the new and the old. The need for the designer to be completely aware of the needs of the users of the remodelled building is discussed within the Proposed Function section. Those who will occupy the new spaces may not be completely aware of the best method of arrangement or current thinking as they are likely to be blinded by the presentism of their existing organisation.

Rereadings volume 2 introduces a new section: Sustainable Adaptation. It can be argued that to reuse a building is an incredibly environmentally friendly act – after all, the structure and the infrastructure are in place, consequently saving energy and materials. The building itself may also play an important role in the collective memory of the area, therefore its adaptation and reuse strengthens the livelihood and general wellbeing of the community. The concept of the sustainable interior can be further enhanced if the designer constructs a building that can be used in a sustainable manner, in that the users or inhabitants are encouraged to act in an environmentally friendly manner while they inhabit the place. This can involve simple measures from windows that open, heating and cooling that can be manually controlled, to specifi cally placed meeting points within the interior.

To remodel a building in a sympathetic and successful manner, the designer needs to be able to anticipate the needs of the new users and combine this with a thorough knowledge of the memory and the circumstance of the existing.

## Form and Structure

## Introduction

The form of the building and the manner in which it was once occupied has a direct connection with the character of the remodelled structure. It is obvious that this infl uence can affect the manner in which the architect or designer approaches the design problem. To actively embrace the qualities of the existing will inevitably create a sympathetic and appropriate response.

The width of a room can be dictated by the size of the beam that supports the fl oor or ceiling above. Until relatively recently, trees were the construction material that dictated the organisation of a building. The beam in most buildings would have originally been made of timber and depending upon which type of wood is used, there is generally a comfortable size of about three to fi ve metres, with an optimum of up to 12 metres. Of course the advent of steel and concrete meant that must larger spans could be bridged, but the beam does get fatter the greater the width to be crossed. The rhythm of rooms can create distinctive buildings and interiors. The typical townhouses of Northern Europe have an unmistakable rhythm to the structure. The repetition of the façades of these terraced houses is unambiguous and can either be extremely plain and simple, or highly decorated. The façades of the great buildings that face many market places in Flanders are incredibly fi ne; they are almost completely perforated as all of the structural integrity is held within the cross walls and the timber beams.

Figure 1.5 The Dining Room at Trinity College, Dublin by de Blacam and Meagher. The new insertion is timeless and robust, something which refl ects the forceful nature of the original building.

The interior of the Dining Hall at Trinity College in Dublin was severely damaged by fi re in 1984. The exterior of the rather neat classical building built in 1760 by Hugh Darley survived relatively unscathed, and much of the panelled refectory was saved, but the atrium needed to be

completely rebuilt. De Blacam and Meagher conducted the restoration work and also made two notable interventions. The fi rst was the rather extraordinary decision to construct a full-sized replica of Adolf Loos’s Kartner Bar in the former Senior Common Room above the Dining Hall. The second was to insert a somewhat timeless structure to support and organise the atrium. This elegant three storey timber installation is composed of an exposed frame, which supports a series of long screens. The sequence of balconies provides access to the different levels, while the long timber shutters can be closed to allow for privacy and acoustic control. The rhythm of the structure has a direct relationship with the size of the timber beams, which are necessarily large to accommodate the great span and the vast weight that they have to carry. The simplicity of the structure is complemented by the robustness of the elongated shutters, which are smooth on the atrium side and show the frame and diagonal bracing on the balcony side. The effect of the tall timber structure and randomly open shutters is quite dramatic, and somewhat reminiscent of Shakespearian performance spaces. The structural rhythm of the interior appears to be completely appropriate to the simple classical language of the building, especially the open ceiling of the atrium.

The form of the remodelled building can be predicated upon the precise form of the original building. The designer can choose to adhere exactly to the parameters of the existing and quite exactly fi t the new interior into the existing space. The artist Rachel Whiteread takes this approach to quite an extreme: she has completed a series of pieces of work where she fi lls a building or interior with sprayed concrete then removes the walls of the original structure. This method makes solid the space of the room, it makes hard what was air, makes the negative positive, and while doing so exposes the patina of age and of wear. A relatively small project in Vienna is the Judenplatz Holocaust Memorial, also known as the Nameless Library.

The monument is an inverted library constructed from concrete, and it appears as if it is the casting of the insides of a small book-lined room. It is a single space that has been made solid, the dimensions are derived from those found in a room surrounding the square. A large installation can be constructed to sit quite comfortably within an existing space such as an internal atrium or courtyard. Balance needs to be achieved between the magnitude and scale of the new element and the size of the open space that it is to sit within. Too large and the equilibrium is upset and the space appears to be cramped and too compressed to meet the needs of the users.

Figure 1.7 The vivid staircase is deliberately placed off-centre, thus releasing the rest of the space in the hall for other activities.

If the installation is too small it is overwhelmed and can appear to be inconsequential. The position of the new element also needs to be considered,

inevitably there will be space left over and surrounding it. If the sizeable installation is simply placed in the centre of the room, then there is little hierarchy within the adjacent area. If the object is positioned off-centre, this leaves different sizes of horizontal space to accommodate a variety of distinct uses.

Vertical positioning is also something that needs to be considered especially if the installation is to be occupied. If the users can access the top level of the installation, they can develop a completely different relationship with the upper areas of the

existing building than they would otherwise have from ground level.

The Why Factory is a three storey bright orange structure positioned in the covered courtyard of the School of Architecture at the Delft University of Technology. Local architects MVRDV designed it to house the independent research unit which was actually founded by the practice. Studios, meeting rooms and research facilities are carefully hidden within the orange ziggurat, while the large steps up the exterior of the structure allow the whole space to be transformed into an auditorium. The steps, which act as seats, are also used for presentations, exhibitions, and even graduation ceremonies. The central location and distinct orange colour means the installation has become a landmark within the complex of buildings, and as such also functions as a beacon or way-fi nding device.

The buildings, which were organised into the traditional prison panopticon, were built in 1855 on the recommendation of the then inspector general of prisons, Édouard Ducpétiaux. A hundred prisoners could be accommodated in back-to-back cells. These were spread out in four wings around a central octagonal base, which housed the observation post. The labyrinthine nature of the organisation of the buildings ensured that the prison itself had the quality of existing very much as a separate world behind the great containing wall, as an independent city within the actual city of Hasselt. The solid brick prison wall, which was part of the city’s collective memory, was a symbol of exclusion. The form of the original building dictated the organisation of the new elements; it informed the position of the large spaces and the smaller ones. The existing composition was uncompromisingly precise and the architects were able to use this structure as a guide to the placement of the new elements of the faculty of law. The building became a small, bright and open town, with several entrances and exits, squares, streets, courtyards and an unexpected roof garden for which the prison wall acts as no more than a parapet. The individual cells were preserved as study rooms. Particular marks decorate the walls, the ghosts of the lines by which prisoners counted out the almost innumerable days of their sentences, thus preserving the memory of the former function. Glass rather than solid doors were installed in these intimate spaces. The interstitial spaces, the gaps between the rooms and the walls, the places where surreptitious activity may or may not have once taken place, have been preserved. These intermediate spaces are for conversation, for collaboration and for romantic collusion. By utilising the form and structure of the existing, a radical transformation of the buildings has been completed without losing the memory of the previous use, but also without invoking the true ghastliness of the experience.

A substantial building may not require significant remodelling if the structure is still intact and the new function will fi t relatively easily into the existing container of the walls. The adaptation may be an ingenious collection of quite modest and unpretentious moves that use the structural integrity of the existing to support the new elements.

Malcolm Fraser Architects adapted three derelict mews buildings and a Victorian lawyers’ offi ce in Edinburgh into light and spacious open-plan offi ces by adding a series of new metal roofs. The original Craigleith stone walls of the buildings, from which much of Edinburgh was constructed, had great inherent strength. These were

cleaned and new large paned windows were inserted into the existing openings; the deep plan of the new interior was provided with plenty of natural light and ventilation.

However, the fl oor-to-ceiling heights of the original mews cottages were woefully inadequate for 21st century use. Contemporary offi ce space needs provision for under-fl oor cabling, controlled lighting and suffi cient ceiling height to ensure that the open space of the deep plan is comfortable. The required space was achieved fl oating a new roof over all of the structures. This served to tie them together, thus creating a single integrated entity which not only facilitated the offi ce

space but also modestly signalled the change of use. The new roof was constructed from grey zinc, perfectly refl ecting the predominant colours of the area. The vertical circulation was placed in a new glazed structure that was attached to the rear of the building. This timber and steel staircase tied the different levels together. A small and contained courtyard garden, accessed by a stone staircase, completed the project.

This is a simple project that unassumingly uses the natural qualities of the buildings and the area to facilitate the needs of new users.

Buildings are often taciturn; they can be secretive, reticent and unforthcoming. They can be solid and impenetrable and it can be diffi cult to understand the nature of the structure. The interior can be awkward too; walls can be much greater than simple partitions.

The organisational method can mean that, by virtue of a circulation system that discourages interaction, spaces that are immediately adjacent to each other are hidden, inaccessible, and

uncommunicative. Gordon Matta-Clark questioned the relationship between the spaces in a building when, with just a chainsaw, he cut holes into the body of the structure.

The Offi ce for Subversive Architecture (OSA), who operate somewhere between art and architecture, also produce massive scale installations using complete buildings. The consistent aim of OSA has been to develop untraditional approaches to reinterpretation of the architecture in the city. Their projects deliberately cross boundaries between art and architecture, varying from minimal or moveable installations to the construction of actual buildings and urban or spatial strategies.

One such project is Discharge/Recharge, which was part of the 2013 Urban Lights Ruhr Festival. This installation, which was completed with artist Christoph Rodatz, was an architectural performance of light and sound that focused upon a tower block in the centre of the city of Bergkamen.

The building, which was simultaneously a landmark and an eyesore, had been unoccupied for 15 years and was due to be demolished at the end of the project. The tower was painted black to ensure the total absorption of light, an act which was also symbolic of the obliteration of its life. At nightfall it became the screen for a laser projection performance.

This exhibition depicted the narrative of the building’s structure. It showed where the great elements of construction were hidden behind the façades, the hollow threedimensional nature controlled by the surrounding walls, how the leaves of the fl oor plates lay one above another, the position of the stairs that linked the individual apartments together, where the rubbish shoots were positioned, and ultimately it simulated the process of destruction. A radio station positioned on the top of the tower simultaneously broadcast interviews with former inhabitants; it told their stories of occupation.

The evocative and dramatic project made visible the previously concealed structural nature of the building, showing the complex arrangements of elements and it exposed the system that organised the occupants. It revealed the story of the tower block.

## History and Function

## Introduction

An existing building or particular situation can contain an overwhelming history. The condition and character of the place can have an important position within the chronology of a particular culture, or make a signifi cant individual contribution to the artistic development. The place may be of great importance to the collective memory of a population, it could be an exceptionally fi ne example of a specifi c type of precedent, or it may just be wonderful.

There are a number of approaches that the architect or designer can take when presented with the problem of how to develop, enlarge, update or re-equip such a place. Without resorting to pastiche, the two most common are to deliberately contrast the new additions or to act in a manner that completes the situation. In the mid-20th century, Carlo Scarpa perfected the technique of deliberately discriminating between the old and the new. This he achieved by using a modern language combined with the careful separation, through the use of a shadow gap, of the original parts of the building from the new elements of facilitation.

A more contemporary approach is for the remodelling work to act in a manner that completes the building. The designer may choose to use a present-day language, but instead of generating contrast or distinction, they will create elements that complement the original, that appear to balance the old and new, that deliberately work directly with the existing environment in a satisfactory and symbiotic manner. This was the approach that Peter Zumthor took to the remodelling of the former St Columba Church in Cologne when it was converted into the Kolumba Museum (see pages 7–9). The new building unites the fragments of

the site. It rises from the remaining pieces of the Gothic church, as well as the ruins from the Roman and Medieval periods, and integrates these into a new threedimensional structure for the museum. This building vaguely refl ects the form of the original building, it invokes the memory of the place, respects the history and spiritual value of the site; it completes the ruins.

The concept of completing a building or interior rather than deliberately contrasting with it, can be used on a much smaller and more intimate scale. This is the process that the architects Caruso St John took to the design of the new exhibition galleries, interpretation gallery, entrance facilities and shop at the Sir John Soane’s Museum in London. The house that Sir John Soane designed for his family and his offi ce, is regarded by many as the most signifi cant domestic scale building ever constructed. It was adapted from a number of town houses over about 30 years in Lincoln’s Inn Fields. Soane bought Number 12 in 1792, then Number 13 in 1807, and eventually acquired Number 14 as well in 1824. He constructed a new façade on the fi rst building and ingeniously remodelled the interior to contain his vast assortment of architectural memorabilia. This collection of models, casts and drawings, plus the paintings, statues, books, and even an Egyptian sarcophagus, were left with the house as an inspiration to the nation.

Caruso St John regarded the project as an exercise that expanded the narrative of the whole house. The language they use is utterly contemporary, and yet strangely also has the quality of looking as if it has always been there. The lightness of Soane’s approach is refl ected in the design of the furniture, cabinets and linings that inhabit the restored rooms of the museum; they contain the intelligence and grace of the original fi ttings.

The new elements are built to intimately inhabit their exact position; each situation was considered and an individual solution reached. For example, the display cabinets which fl ank the fi replace in the front gallery are positioned tightly against the structure, as Soane would have directed. However, they are not symmetrical, which is something that he may not have completely approved of; one is pushed simply against the inside wall of the room, while the other curves slightly away from the light as it collides with the window. The materials were also exactly considered and the architects again made a radical decision: to use mahogany and Corian for the openable cabinets. The weight of the glass meant that a strong and fl exible material was needed, and so the contemporary and the past collide in a simple and sympathetic manner.

## Preston Bus Station

Location Preston, UK

Date 1969

Organisation Gate 81

Designer Building Design Partnership

![](images/f17a8e448f36340eb468afb4af34ca5adf144ee1012245f5c40a0bb90cd07e10.jpg)  
Figure 1.18 The Bus Station is constructed from a series of curving horizontal raw concrete plates suspended over a huge concourse.

Preston is a small provincial city in the north west of England; it has evolved into an awkward mixture of the traditional and the modern, with large civic buildings that are testament to the previous wealth of the city, next to unattractive and dirty Modernist structures. In the middle of this miscellany of different styles is the ‘marvellously Brutal Bus Station, which was constructed in 1969 by BDP Architects. It is an extraordinary building. It was opened in the year of the fi rst moon landing and exhibits that same sense of confi dence and optimism. It is an incredibly long and elegant building and the architectural language of the building can be described as Brutal, in that it is constructed from raw concrete. The building contains a series of car parks situated on extended fl oor plates with upturned curving parapets, which appear to fl oat over the double-height space of the public concourse; the interior of which still contains the original rubber fl ooring, timber benches and white-tiled walls. The concept was to emulate an airport; even the different bus stops were referred to as gates.

The building was under threat for many years, but in late 2012, the City Council proposed its immediate demolition and replacement with a surface car park. They considered that the prohibitively high conservation costs combined with the prime location of the site meant that the destruction of the building could act as a prime factor in the regeneration of the quite depressed city. This provocative act galvanised the various groups that were already campaigning to save the building. Gate 81, one of the foremost of these “urban activist” groups, curated a collection of events based in and around the Bus Station. There had been a considerable amount of negativity surrounding the future of the building and Gate 81 intended to bring some optimism to the situation. The aims of the group were deliberately

non-antagonistic: that is, provocative but not confrontational. It was not about demands, demonstration and protest; rather, Gate 81 wanted to celebrate and appreciate the building. Explicit within the manifesto was the intention to ‘enjoy the building while we still can’, and with reference to the airport-style agenda of the original architects: ‘Preston Bus Station has 80 Gates, we’d like to keep it this way.’<sup>3</sup>

The fi rst act of Gate 81 was to empower people with knowledge. The complete archive of the original architects, BDP, had been lost in a fi re at their storage warehouse in the 1980s, so there was no drawn record of the Bus Station. Gate 81 commissioned a set of downloadable drawings that were made freely available to everyone. The fi rst physical project was a midnight poetry reading, ‘Journey to the End of the World’. This was staged on the night of the equinox, and used the interior of the Bus Station as a continuous stage. As those reciting their poetry moved around the building, so the audience followed them, using the very character of the spaces to emphasise the emotion of the performance. The most ambitious project was the workshop or HacLab, which was held on a Saturday in May. It was deliberately highly accessible and open to anyone and everyone. The day centred upon an open workshop, which was interspersed with a series of lectures and performances. The workshop was well attended; some people actively joined in the search for ideas for the future of the building, some noted their memories of the place on specially printed postcards, while others just listened. Alternative activities occurred during the day. ‘Four Lost Buildings’ was a curated walk around the city, deliberately highlighting a number of great Preston buildings that had already been demolished – or were about to be. Gate 81 also curated three exhibitions, all of which were designed to open at the workshop.

A café provided locally sourced food, and a group of schoolkids set up a stall selling screen-printed tee shirts with their own images of the building. Other projects included visits to local primary schools to discuss the importance of the cultural legacy of a place, a one-week project entitled ‘Preston Interota’ at the Antwerp Winter School and a formal design charrette. The fi nal project was a procession; not an aggressive march, demonstration or protest making demands and ultimatums, but more like a cavalcade or cortège. It was again an opportunity to celebrate, recognise and enjoy the building.

The campaign was successful, and the Bus Station was granted a Grade II Listing. This means that any redevelopment of the city has to consider the presence of a huge Modernist masterpiece at the heart of any proposal. The campaign was presented with the government sponsored Heritage Alliance award. They specifi cally cited the Save the Preston Bus Station battle as a ‘phenomenal example of people power’ and a fi ne example of how it is possible to save a piece of heritage through public pressure.

Milk bars usually had a long high counter where customers could sit, chairs and tables to linger at, and would normally contain a jukebox and occasionally a pinball machine. It was one of the fi rst places that teenagers could visit by themselves; it was a bright, clean and unthreatening place. The milk bar has assumed a special place in the collective memory, one which accentuates the happy carefree time of the teddy boys and girls, before the ubiquity of television and rampant consumerism. It is not uncommon for elements of a building to be removed from one location and reused in another. An ornate doorway or fi replace is a valuable commodity, while whole screens, elaborate windows, even timber panelling can and have been transported to a new place. Distinct parts of a building can be used and reused. They can be exploited and manipulated; an elaborate artefact may enhance a somewhat dowdy building and confer a status that may not have previously existed. The element may lose its former meaning as the act of translation occurs when it is

transported from one environment to another. Spolia, which derives its name from the collection of the spoils of war, is the practice of the winners of a battle plundering and then displaying the valuable goods of the losing side. It is also an expression used to describe a collagelike approach to reconfi guring buildings. It relies on the materials that are to hand, the ease of their reuse, on contingency, availability and ease of supply.

This act of transportation can be likened to the approach taken by the bricoleur.

# Context and Environment

## Introduction

The relationship between the interior and its wider location has often been seen as somewhat ambiguous, and yet, it is possible to describe some situations – that is rooms, spaces or buildings – as encapsulating, in miniature, the characteristic qualities or features of a much wider situation. The interior space has an obvious and direct relationship with the building that it occupies and with the people who use it, but it can also have a connection with the area in which it is located. Much has been written and discussed about the manner in which we inhabit space and about the interior’s relationship with its immediate context. But little attention has been paid to the association between interior space and its more extensive context or environment, that is, the room as a microcosm of the city or region.

The designer can work with the identity of a place to create interior space that is appropriate to its location. There are a great many examples of such spaces that are so particular to a specific location, that not only could they not have been constructed anywhere else, but also it would be impossible to transfer them elsewhere. Carlo Scarpa’s interventions could not have existed anywhere other than Veneto, Giancarlo de Carlo’s work is determinedly fixed in Urbino and Tadao Ando’s buildings are firmly rooted in Osaka. Other contemporary designers have been able to interpret the meaning within different places: Jørn Utzon, David Chipperfield, Steven Holl; even Rem Koolhaas is now claiming affiliation (Venice Biennale Exhibition 2011). Examples of interior design projects that have a strong affiliation with their context include the Viennese shop interiors of Hans Hollein, the bar interiors of Ben Kelly and the retail units of Future Systems.

This method of working closely with the given environment can be referred to as Contextualism. It is an approach that advocates a careful reading of the situation before making any alterations to it. It is almost 30 years since Kenneth Frampton wrote about Critical Regionalism, Colin Rowe and Fred Koetter composed Collage City and Aldo Rossi recorded The Architecture of the City. Although the movement, which emerged as a reaction to Modernism, is a generation old, it is now more relevant than ever. The architect or designer will propose changes that do not destroy the nature or character of an area, but actually contribute to that built environment. The designer must also create interior space that is totally relevant to the time in which it is constructed, design that uses contemporary technology and is suitable for the needs of today. This means not resorting to pastiche, but designing interiors that are visually and operationally applicable to the present day.

The architects Lone and Søren Asmusson have converted the barn which was next to their farmhouse in Farum, about 15 miles to the north of Copenhagen, into a music studio. At first sight it looks pretty much like the other buildings in the complex: single storey with whitewashed walls and what appears to be a thatched roof. However, although the walls of the studio are standard, the slightly elevated roof is made from black aluminium. The approach that the architects took was to design a perfect replica of the building that could slip very carefully into the void of the original barn.

This highly insulated box has openings that align exactly with the original openings, the reveals of which are lined with laminated timber. The interior walls are painted white to reflect the sunlight and this is reinforced with an enormous mirror that covers the

entire slanting ceiling of the north half of the open space.

The building is referred to as the Posehuset, which means “bag house”. This is a direct reference to the somewhat eccentric previous owner of the property who had a penchant for collecting plastic bags. The studio has the quality of looking as if it has always been there, while at the same time being completely new. It contains the character of the region, but also belongs to the 21st century.

The intimate relationship between an interior and its immediate context can be observed in Florian Beigel’s design for the

Half Moon Theatre. The auditorium was a collection of enclosed spaces that were formed in an unused and neglected gap on the busy Mile End Road in London. The distance between the actors and the audience was consciously reduced to encourage deliberate interaction. Thus, the open and connected interior spaces were conceived of as a continuum of the street, as if the journey from the outside world was almost accidental and the audience members had unintentionally wandered into the drama.

The context that a building, interior or situation occupies can inspire the nature of the adaptation or design. The natural and artifi cial elements that constitute the site can provide the impetus for the remodelling of the environment.

Figure 1.27 The swimming pools are squeezed between the massive infrastructure of the road system and the energy of the Atlantic Ocean.

Through careful study, combined with intuitive reaction, the designer can create a new environment that appears to be more than an extension of the context: it actually completes it. The situation is so inspiring that any interventions seem to be born of that environment, to have evolved within

that place, rather than having been designed and placed there.

This is exactly how the swimming pools by Álvaro Siza on the Portuguese coast just to the north of Porto appear. They are situated on the Leça de Palmeira beaches, on the northern coastline of Matosinhos, crushed next to the rocks between a duel carriageway and the Atlantic Ocean. The industrial port can be observed across the sands.

Siza embraced this situation by integrating the pools with the rocky coastline. The

Leça Swimming Pool complex consists of changing rooms, a café and two swimming pools, one for adults and one for children. The building is sunk below the level of the road; this immediately creates a disconnection between the pools and the infrastructure of the city. The visitor enters the complex via a smooth concrete ramp which is parallel to the road, and as the path slopes downwards, so the disengagement with the city begins. A concrete barrier, just a shade lighter than the natural stone, reinforces this detachment. The visitor passes through the quite brutal changing rooms made of the same coloured concrete, and is then encouraged to turn towards the sea.

This is the fi rst real engagement with the magnifi cence of the water, and the experience is intensifi ed by the delayed gratifi cation. The two pools merge with the rocks and they appear to be of a similar magnitude. The children’s pool, which is situated towards the land, is bound on one edge by a curving concrete wall and the other by the rocks, whereas the adult pool is positioned further out towards the sea. Three edges are made from concrete, while the other is the natural formation of the rocky coastline. The water is refreshed daily as the tide fl ows in and out of this natural location. The orthogonal concrete interventions do not seem out of place in this tranquil and organic setting. There is a clear view towards the Port of Leixoes, one of the most important cargo ports in Portugal, which handles around 14 million tonnes of goods and 65,000 passengers each year. The shared industrial language creates a strong relationship between the pools and the quays and harbours. Thus the Leça Swimming Pools appear to have the quality of emerging from the nature of the area and the culture and industry that has developed there.

The Half Moon Theatre Company was a radical and innovative group that developed creative and alternative ideas to the established order of theatrical productions. They challenged the conventional nature of the auditorium, and questioned the space between the theatre and the street, the cast and the audience. The theatre company developed progressive productions that drew on the most direct forms of theatre of the past, such as the Italian commedia dell’arte, and the Elizabethan theatres with galleried courtyards such as Shakespeare’s Globe. The guiding principle was to break down the barriers between the actors and the audience and encourage both to be seen by each other.

Beigel and Christou proposed a design for the new theatre that exploited this concept; they developed an auditorium space that resembled a typical street scene. Thus the Half Moon Theatre was basically a spatial progression starting from the pavement of the Mile End Road in the East End of London, through a gateway into an open-air scenic space or public courtyard. The journey continued through a steel curtain and into the main performance area, which again both fi guratively and metaphorically resembled a scenic street. From here, large doors led into a small garden and the Young People’s Theatre pavilion. This was a dense and layered series of spaces, some inside and some outside; each was distinct but held

together by their proximity and the manner in which people moved between and occupied the journey.

The street scene was so particular and strong because it was actually a series of pure spaces within the awkward shape of a once derelict area. This was achieved with the careful placement of new three storey block walls containing rectangular openings.These acted as linings, defi ning the exact dimensions of the rooms and also importantly providing acoustic control. This complex surface geometry to the sides of the auditorium effectively scattered the sound to ensure good acoustic diffusion. The new high ceiling also created a problem of late-refl ected sound, and an absorbent sky, that is fairfaced woodwool slabs above what appeared to be a fl oating perforated metal ceiling, provided the acoustic control. The pattern on the fl oor, which was a slightly different angle to the geometry of the space, reinforced the dynamism of the journey, and encouraged movement within the platonic solidness of the auditorium. Beigel and Christou created not a building, but the space between buildings. The theatre responded to the context by extending the street into the heart of the space, encouraging a dynamic relationship between the urban elements of the immediate surroundings and the intimate nature of the theatre.

José Guerrero was an Abstract Expressionist painter; he created big vibrantly coloured images that suggested massive landscapes, primitive architecture or possibly even atmospheric events. He spent his early years in Granada before emigrating to the USA, where he hung out with many prominent members of the American avant-garde including Willem de Kooning and Mark Rothko. Guerrero returned to Granada towards the end of the Franco regime when a more relaxed and tolerant period had emerged.

Antonio Jiménez Torrecillas created a museum dedicated to the work of the painter in a 19th century house in the centre of Granada. The approach that the architect took was to exploit the highly intimate relationship that the building has with its constricted context, while providing space and light for the explosive nature of the paintings. The building is accessed through the narrow and contained streets of the old city. These lanes and passageways evolved in this location because they provide shelter from the worst of the summer sunshine and protection from the cold winter winds. The nature of this urban landscape means that there is no opportunity for the whole building to be viewed from the front; the prospect is always oblique, from the side, without access to the complete picture. It is impossible to see the complete façade.

The building itself is a fairly traditional three storey townhouse of this period, brick with arched windows. The architect’s fi rst intervention was to position a new marble plane over the ground level façade; this mimics the original frontage, but also signals the change of use. The original interior was stripped away, and replaced with a very simple organisational device: Torrecillas placed an unadorned freestanding folded wall into the space. This acted to push the services, such as the elements of circulation and the offi ces and the facilities to the edge, thus allowing the centre of each fl oor to be dedicated to exhibition. As the visitor negotiates the journey through the building, they are presented with glimpses of the views from the existing windows. These are constrained and engaged by the context, as the walls of the cathedral opposite are just a few metres away across the street. As the vertical promenade continues, so the level of natural light increases and the intimate relationship with the cathedral is amplifi ed.

The fi nal and most dramatic intervention is the placement of a new storey on the top of the existing building. This is completely contained and has just one very large window, which provides a spectacular vision of the cathedral wall. The ornamental cornice, bathed in sunlight is just an arm’s length away. The view is treated with the same reverence as the pictures, with a carefully positioned bench encouraging the visitor to contemplate the magnifi cence of the situation. Torrecillas has provided the Jose Guerrero Museum with a building that echoes the excitement of the work of the great painter with a statement of equal remarkable intent.
