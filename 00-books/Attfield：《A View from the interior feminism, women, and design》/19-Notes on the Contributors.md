# Notes on the Contributors

Juliet Ash teaches the history and theory of fashion design at Ravensbourne College of Design and Communication. She is coeditor with Lee Wright of Components of Dress: Design Marketing and Image Making in the Fashion Industry, Routledge & Kegan Paul/Comedia, London, 1988.

Christine Boydell studied design history at Manchester Polytechnic and completed a postgraduate diploma in the history of art and design at Birmingham Polytechnic. She is writing an M.Phil/Ph.D. on Marion Dorn. She teaches design history at Lancashire Polytechnic, Preston.

Jos Boys is a researcher and journalist specialising in issues concerning women and the built environment. She was a founder member of Matrix, feminist architects, and was also involved in setting up the Women's Design Service, a resource centre on design issues affecting women. She teaches architecture part-time at the Polytechnic of North London, where she is researching a Ph.D. on the history of the ideal' home.

Cheryl Buckley teaches design history at Newcastle Polytechnic and is writing her doctoral thesis onWomen Pottery Designers in Britain, 1914–1945' at the University of East Anglia. In 1983 she co-organised the first national conference on Women in Design with Lynne Walker. She has published various articles on women's relationship to design written from a feminist perspective. She is currently writing a book on women pottery designers, 1860–1955, to be published by The Women's Press.

Anthea Callen is a lecturer in history of art at the University of Warwick. She has written a number of books, catalogues and articles as well as her pioneering Angel in the Studio: Women in the Arts and Crafts Movement 1870–1914, 1979. Her doctoral thesis for the Courtauld Institute of Art, University of London has been published in part as Techniques of the Impressionists, 1982. She is currently completing a book on Degas and Modern Life Figure Painting for Yale University Press.

Zöe Munby studied design at Manchester Polytechnic and went on to do research into the history of Lancashire textiles. She works as a tutor-organiser with West Mercia District of the Workers' Educational Association and is contributing an essay to a forthcoming book on imperialism and design.

Angela Partington is writing a Ph.D. on working-class women and consumerism in the 1950s at the Centre for Contemporary Cultural Studies in Birmingham. She lectures at Bath College of Higher Education and at St Martin's School of Art.

Alison Ravetz studied English and Archaeology at Cambridge and London. Being a housewife and mother led her into the history of housing and planning and she published Model Estate (1974), Remaking Cities: Contradictions of the Recent Urban Environmient (1980), and The Government of Space: Town Planning in Modern Society (1986). She has a daughter and two sons and lives in Leeds.

Meg Sweet is archivist of the Archive of Art and Design of the Victoria and Albert Museum.

Lynne Walker studied the history of art and architecture as an undergraduate in the USA and completed her Ph.D. on the history of the Arts and Crafts Movement at the University of London. She taught the history of architecture and design at Newcastle Polytechnic and now works in the British Architectural Library at the Royal Institute of British Architects. She also edited the catalogue to the exhibition Women Architects: Their Work, 1984.

Suzette Worden studied fine-art at Portsmouth Polytechnic and went on to Brighton Polytechnic, where in 1980 she completed her Ph.D. thesis on Furniture for the Living Room: 1919–39’, which looked at the relationship between social use and developments within the furniture industry. She now teaches the history of design at Brighton Polytechnic. in 1982 she organised an exhibition on Brighton's electrical pioneers.

Lee Wright was awarded an MA in design histcry from Middlesex Polytechnic in 1983. She lectures in fashion history and theory at Winchester School of Art and Ravensbourne College of Design and Communication. She is co-editor with Juliet Ash of Components of Dress: Design Marketing and Image Making in the Fashion Industry.

What does design tell us about our society, and women's place in it?

A View from the Interior examines design history from a feminist perspective. It does more than add women's names to the textbooks. It exposes the socially constructed nature of design history, and the assumptions of male designers and architects about women's tastes, women's place and the way women live. And it illuminates women's relationship to design both as designers and those designed for.

Women have gained a foothold in pottery and textile design, and predominate in the ‘naturally suitable' field of domestic design. Why are they still a minority in the areas of architecture and industrial design? What part does design education play in this exclusion?

Absorbing for the general reader, this book is also indispensable to the student.

Judy Attfield is a researcher and lecturer in History of Design at Brighton Polytechnic and a member of the Design History Society. Pat Kirkham is Principal Lecturer in History of Art and Design at Leicester Polytechnic.
