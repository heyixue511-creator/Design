Expansion and Taste (1801– 1865) D uring the early nineteenth century, technical change and manufacturing<sub>continued to expand and accelerate in a political and economic climate</sub> generally favorable to international trade, free markets, competition, and colonial exploitation. From a European, and more generally Western, perspective, such expansion was often viewed as evidence of economic and social progress. Yet as the international exhibitions (beginning with the Great Exhibition at Crystal Palace in London in 1851) attest, the growth of manufacturing – from raw materials, to labor, to finished products – was global, interdependent, and varied. Moreover, a host of new concerns accompanied increased production and commerce. Merchants and manufacturers competed in the growing market for goods and furnishings, offering an array of styles to appeal to varied tastes; but critics expressed fears that as craft production in workshops yielded to divided labor in factories the quality of manufactured goods was compromised, raising questions about the ways in which technology was straining the traditional relationship between the fine and applied arts as well as its effect upon the lives of workers. Observers at the time also expressed concern about the effects of expansion upon “taste,” echoing “the habits of fashion” and reinforcing the need for standards noted earlier by Percier and Fontaine as well as Thomas Hope (see here). The contemporary discourse surrounding “taste” was not simply a question of aesthetic choice but touched directly upon ethics, politics, and threats to the existing social order, placing design at the center of culture and the meaning of progress. A number of artists, as well as politicians, clamored for reform, initiating efforts to train designers, regulate (if not control) the practice of design, and educate consumers. Government-sponsored schools, museums, and prescriptions for designers as well as consumers for the proper role of decoration in the applied arts were part of an attempt (an uphill battle) to promote common, unifying principles for an increasingly diverse consuming public. Competing attitudes toward expanding production, trade, and consumption culminated in the aforementioned Great Exhibition (also known as the Crystal Palace exhibition) held in London in 1851, the first of numerous international exhibitions that took place in the major urban and industrial capitals of Europe and the United States throughout the later nineteenth century. These hugely popular expositions sought to stimulate trade, showcase production technology, foster competition, create markets for manufacturers, promote the construction of national identities, and entertain as well as educate the public. The diversity of goods and styles at the World’s Fairs attracted the attention of visitors and observers, some of whom marveled at the display of productivity and abundance, and others who advocated for common, unifying principles amidst the dizzying variety on view. Less questioned at the time was the Eurocentric bias that justified colonial expansion and power abroad in the name of progress. Among the nation states and nascent colonial empires of Europe as well as the United States during this period, combined political and economic interests focused public attention upon West Asia, territories under the control of the vast Islamic Ottoman Empire with its capital in the Turkish city of Constantinople (modern-day Istanbul), but encompassing Serbia, Greece, Iran, Iraq, Egypt, and Tunisia. The arts of the Ottoman Empire included highly developed traditions of ceramic production, woven carpets and textiles, carved furniture, and distinctive styles of dress (see here). Visitors who flocked in increasing numbers to the popular World’s Fairs admired displays of West Asian goods. Attitudes toward the design and culture of West Asia and North Africa varied: reformers such as Owen Jones believed that the Islamic art of these regions (often referred to as “Moorish,” a term now considered by many to be derogatory) exemplified universal principles of the design of decorative patterns; early photographers recorded views of ancient and modern sites, while painters, some of whom had visited the regions, produced landscapes as well as scenes of life there that combined curiosity with stereotypes emphasizing sensuality, violence, and hedonism, justifying imperialist attitudes toward the regions and reinforcing an attitude known now as “Orientalism.” Attention to the decorative arts of West Asia and North Africa acknowledges their prominent role in nineteenth-century design history while also revealing the cultura attitudes that relegated these vast regions to the margins of that history, ignoring their economic importance and rich internal history. ## Chapter 3 # Growing Pains: Expanding Industry in the Early Nineteenth Century A Culture of Industry and Progress in the West Following the War of 1812, Britain enjoyed peaceful relations with the United States, while the Peace of Vienna, forged in the wake of Napoleons defeat at Waterloo in 1815, inaugurated a period of reduced political hostility in Europe that stimulated trade and the growth of markets for manufactured goods. Eli Whitneys (17651825) invention of the cotton gin (1793) serves as an example of the many technical advances that also occurred at the time. The cotton gin mechanically removed seeds from balls of raw cotton, a task previously performed painstakingly by hand, and this greatly increased the cultivation of raw cotton in the southern United States. Whitneys invention, coupled with the introduction of steam power for the process of spinning in British factories from the mid-1780s, reduced prices for woven textiles and made cotton fabric (until this time usually imported from manufactories in India) available to middle-class buyers. Farmers maintained lower prices for raw cotton through the use of slave labor on plantations in the southern United States, while manufacturers introduced mechanical methods of pattern printing on cloth and, by the mid-century, the sewing machine for stitching garments and trim. In turn, these technologies contributed to expanding the market for womens fashion and ready-to-wear clothing, reaching a wider public through printed fashion plates that illustrated ideals of feminine beauty and propriety in dress in popular magazines such as Godey’s Lady’s Book. These dynamic, global, and interdependent developments in the cotton industry involved changes in technology and an elastic market. They also relied on labor (including institutionalized slavery), producing fears for the human consequences of unfettered economic expansion that continue to the present day, whether for the displacement of human workers by robots or the exploitation of outsourced labor in poorer countries around the globe. Industrial expansion included the acquisition of colonial territories by European nations, with efforts to access their raw materials, products, markets, and labor, one part of which (the Islamic world) will be the subject of this sections Global Inspiration. Advances in printing technology, such as the introduction of steam-powered cylinder presses, lithography and chromolithography, papermaking machines, and continuousfeed paper, helped to create a better-informed and literate public. This was especially evident in the widespread proliferation of popular illustrated weekly broadsides and journals. Continuous printing processes were also introduced for patterned or scenic wallpapers, using longer lengths of paper and making paper wall-coverings and the cultivation of design in the home an attainable luxury for the middle class, while woodengraved illustrations in catalogs and weekly magazines provided examples to attract the attention of consumers. Railroad construction, first in Britain and then throughout Europe, the United States, Japan, and imperial colonies, stimulated commerce, as the speed and ease of transportation of raw materials and commodities increased. The railroad also contributed to the ease of personal travel and the emergence of a tourism industry that accelerated in the twentieth century. Finally, it is worthwhile to note the connection between the history of design and manufacturing in the first two-thirds of the nineteenth century and the beginnings of the advertising industry and retail shopping described in literature (for example, Émile Zolas Au bonheur des dames, or The Ladies’ Paradise, published in 1883) familiar to todays consumers (the first advertising agency, Volney Palmer, in Philadelphia, dates to the early 1840s, and the first modern department store, the Bon Marché in Paris, opened in the 1860s). Advertising in posters, in the pages of magazines and newspapers, along with fixed prices, street lamps, and lighted displays behind expanses of plate glass all defined the modern character of urban life, increasing opportunities for social interaction, making shopping a middleclass leisure activity. Writers, painters, and illustrators of the period document displays of goods in store windows, attractive salesgirls behind counters, and the use of signs, handbills, and sandwich boards to entice shoppers. ## New Materials and Processes The range of products and processes that emerged during the first half of the nineteenth century is overwhelming, but a sampling may suffice to represent the expanding scope of manufacturing activity. As an example George Elkington of Birmingham, England (18011865), developed and patented in 1840 the process known as silver electroplating. Electroplating involved a chemical reaction created by electricity that attached a thin coating of silver to inexpensive base metals such as nickel to produce a wide variety of metal wares for use and display in the home, as well as for a variety of other products from coins to public sculpture (today the same process is used to protect metal automobile parts with a coating of chromium). Electroplating quickly competed with pewter, eclipsed a mid-eighteenth-century method of plating copper between thin layers of silver, and made silverware and silver tea services popular as symbols of comfort and leisure in many middle-class homes. Elkingtons first clients were institutional, including hotels, clubs, passenger ships, railroad companies, and restaurants who purchased large quantities of his wares to lend an air of luxury to the experience of travel or dining. The illustration from Elkingtons c. 1855 catalog of designs (fig. 3.1) features an array of muffineers, mustard pots, and salt cellars with a wide range of styles and prices that rise with the amount of engraving or chasing requiring additional design of dies for stamping or skilled labor; while Elkingtons catalogs offered customers graduated pricing, the company emphasized luxury associated with high levels of skill linked with decoration as well as elaborate services with specialized containers and utensils. One plate illustrates egg frames, a butter cooler in the Gothic style, tea caddies, and tea bells, the latter with putti-shaped handles. To market his process, Elkington exhibited unique specimens as presentation pieces at Worlds Fairs beginning with the Crystal Palace in London in 1851 and again in 1862 (see here), where they earned medals and prizes, and were praised by observers as examples of the beneficial relationship between art and manufacturing, demonstrating that technological invention need not threaten high standards of design and skill. Indeed, the designers hired by Elkington were the highest-paid of the companys many employees, which by the 1880s boasted more than 1,000 workers. ![](images/cc67e9b216dc3a34380997822b4724fbb3ace4f53cf863dfb9188c02312f5456.jpg) George Elkington, designs for electroplated silver muffineers, mustard pots, and salt cellar from the catalog: Elkington, Mason & Co. patentees of the electro plate, manufacturing silversmiths, bronzists. &c. by Elkington, Mason & Co., c. 1855. A slightly later innovation in metallurgy was the discovery and first applications of aluminum, dating to around 1855. The early use of the new material was for jewelry, which is not surprising since its cost was comparable to that of silver or gold. Only when electrolytic processes for extracting aluminum more cheaply were introduced industrially toward the end of the nineteenth century did applications expand to include casting for architecture, furniture, and other products (see here and fig. 6.39). Manufacturing expansion also included new materials for furniture such as cast iron and bentwood for chairs and benches. In 1830, German-born cabinet-maker Michael Thonet (17961871) used steam, glue, and wooden molds to bend beechwood rods into curved shapes that were then assembled to make chairs, hatstands, and supports for tables. His bentwood process eliminated the need for carving and other costly handwork, and came to the attention of Prince Klemens von Metternich (17731859), advisor to the Austrian emperor, who commissioned a large order of furniture from Thonet for the emperors palace at Liechtenstein in the early 1840s. An earlier Thonet chair, illustrated in figure 3.2, is lightweight, meeting the need for occasional seating for different events, and includes a woven cane seat and curvilinear shapes with decorative back and stretchers in a contemporary restrained Neoclassical style. Like George Elkington, Thonet catered to institutional clients, obtained patents to protect his invention, and manufactured folding chairs for easy storage as well as fixed seating for theaters and other modern, public performance spaces. That such grandiosity was a tendency in the furnishings of this period may be inferred from a passage in Stendhals (Marie-Henri Beyle, 17831842) novel Le Rouge et Le Noir (The Red and the Black), published in 1830. Toward the end of the novel, Mathilde, a member of a privileged and well-bred aristocratic family, is pleasantly surprised by the furnishings in a parlor while she waits for the Bishop of Bescançon, and contrasts their refinement and delicate luxury with the vulgar magnificence one finds today in Paris in the best houses. Such magnificence may be seen in the apartments designed for Napoleon III (r. 1852 1870) in Paris, an extension of the Louvre and designed under the direction of architect Hector Martin Lefuel (18101880) between 1856 and 1861. Carved, gilded, and utilizing the new technique of tufted upholstery in red silk and damask throughout, the furniture and setting embody a mid-nineteenth-century ideal of luxury conveyed through richness and abundance of material as well as elaborate carving and innovative construction. In terms of extravagance, little compares with the eclectic fantasy created for the Prince of Wales (later King George IV, 17621830) at the Royal Pavilion in the seaside resort town of Brighton, built between 1815 and 1817 as a summer residence designed by John Nash (17521835). The Pavilions ten onion-shaped domes and minarets were based upon published engravings of Indian Mughal architecture. The grand banqueting room (fig. 3.6) features an elaborate gas chandelier constructed of mirrored glass, gems, and tinted glass lotus flowers. As Prince Regent and later king, George IV was Englands most lavish patron of the arts at the time. The Pavilion was a highly original design whose opulence recalls earlier royal palaces that projected authority and distinction, as well as stimulating production in the national interest. The references to the arts of China and India suggest the continued popular appeal of chinoiserie as well as Britains imperial aspirations. Yet royal patronage, within the economic and social context for design during this period, did not guarantee the widespread adoption and commercial success of an official style. The Royal Pavilion was admired by some observers as playful or even magical in its effect, but other visitors considered it indulgent and pretentious, material evidence of the Princes reputation for wasteful spending and philandering. The challenges that commercial expansion, fashion, and a free press posed to traditional forms of authority worked in multiple directions in the early nineteenth century: markets developed dynamically, and taste was difficult to dictate: design retained its link to politics and power, but the relationship was more complex, unpredictable, and volatile than in the Age of Absolutism. ![](images/08f26ae2863bd65de761b5b7bc03ae62649c2d1d4abe89bd3fb465eaed37ab26.jpg) John Nash, Royal Pavilion at Brighton, banqueting room, 1815–1823. ## Beyond the Printed Page In the eighteenth century, the printing industry was geared primarily to book publication for a limited readership. Newspapers, broadsides, and other ephemeral material, such as Benjamin Franklins Poor Richard’s Almanac (see fig. 2.12), are examples of more popular kinds of printed literature existing at the fringes of the traditional printing industry. During the early nineteenth century, printers and typographers greatly expanded the range of their production to meet the needs of an increasingly literate public that read daily and weekly broadsides, and to compete for attention in advertising. Larger type sizes, new fonts, wood rather than metal type, and the introduction of lithography to create or transfer images for illustrations exemplify the invention that transpired during this time. Such changes affected both the printed word and the printed image, laying the groundwork for new professions in the printing industry, including illustrators, cartoonists, and eventually designers who could creatively combine type and image to fulfill new purposes and reach new audiences in the advertising industry, business communication, childrens literature, and popular journalism. Printers in urban centers, and particularly in the commercially active city of London, designed a variety of new display typefaces and a wide range of sizes for signs, handbills, and newspaper advertisements in an effort to grab the attention and interest of consumers or to influence public opinion. Casting large letters in metal proved expensive and unwieldy, so typographers cut type in the less durable medium of wood. By the second half of the nineteenth century, wood type was cut with the help of templates and routing machines that cut grooves more quickly and precisely than handheld knives and chisels. Wood type solved the needs of expanding trade in a climate of free enterprise and competition. Printers seized the opportunity to print ephemeral materials to attract a buying public, inventing an array of new letterforms designed to communicate with immediacy and expression. If the terminology historians of the printing industry use today seems arcane, there is good reason: much of this specialized vocabulary has not changed since the early nineteenth century. For example, new faces developed in the early nineteenth century were generally called fat, and are characterized by very large letters with proportionally thicker heavy strokes, often contrasted with thin strokes. The extreme contrast between thick and thin strokes recalls the new and rational later eighteenth-century Modern faces (see here), but at an enlarged scale and with less refinement than that of the printed book. Fat faces were often accompanied by simplified bracketed serifs that looked triangular and tended to make letters appear dense and tightly spaced (fig. 3.7). The term Egyptian is also often used to refer to fat faces. One type of Egyptian face uses slab serifs, heavy letter endings that anchor the letters to an insistent horizontal base line and reduce the space between them, strengthening the immediate recognition of each word (fig. 3.8). Designers in the twentieth century returned to such slab serif lettering for visual impact in developing corporate logos (see figs. 11.76 and 11.77). Curiously, another new face linked to the growing demand for advertising eliminated serifs altogether and often used strokes of uniform thickness, also permitting the very close spacing of letters and giving a strong sense of overall density. Known at the time as a form of Egyptian type or sometimes as Grotesque or Grotesk, these letters (fig. 3.10) were also (quite transparently) called sans serif. Sans serif letterforms were an article of faith for proponents of the New Typography (Neue Typographie) in the 1920s in Germany and elsewhere in Europe (see here); in the post-World War II period, many multi-national corporations favored sans serif faces as the basis for their logos and identity programs, taking advantage of the close spacing and similarity among letterforms to create a strong sense of visual unity (the Mobil logo is one example, see fig. 13.32). Printing technology also developed rapidly in the early nineteenth century, primarily as a result of the increased circulation of newspapers. Efforts began with larger and easier to operate presses, often made of cast iron rather than wooden parts, then with the use of steam to provide mechanical power. Steam-powered presses, first introduced by a German engineer working in London named Friedrich Koenig (17741833), operated on a principle derived from copper-plate printing, where the type forme moved horizontally on a bed, first under an inking roller and then under a cylindrical press. These steam-powered presses were installed in 1814 at the London newspaper offices of The Times. Later versions printed simultaneously on both sides of a sheet of paper. Print historian Michael Twyman, who has traced these developments and their impact, relates that circulation of The Times from the late eighteenth century to 1830 grew from 1,500 copies per day to 11,000. The next major development, occurring simultaneously in Britain and in the United States, was the use of continuous rolls rather than individually cut sheets of paper to further speed mechanical production. Continuous-feed presses required transferring the flat type formes to a curved surface, accomplished by using papier-mâché to make curved impressions that were then used to create cylindrical metal formes (the process is known as stereotyping). By 1850, circulation for The Times had reached 38,000 copies daily. Lithography (literally stone writing) was the name given to a planographic printing process developed in Germany by Alois Senefelder (17711834) in the early nineteenth century. Based on the chemical repellence of oil and water, a design was drawn with greasy ink or crayons on a flat limestone surface (the best limestone was known to come from Bavaria). The stone surface was then coated with water, which was absorbed by the stone only in those areas not covered with crayon. An oily printing ink was next applied to the surface of the stone, which adhered only to the drawing, being repelled by the wet parts of the surface. The stone was then covered with a sheet of paper and placed beneath a press to produce a printed impression. Lithography has been called the most significant development in nineteenth-century printing, although its progress in the early nineteenth century was hampered first by the slow progress of the technology during the Napoleonic Wars and second by the lack of machine presses to speed up printing until after 1850. Known for its versatility and the relative ease of the process in comparison with intaglio and relief methods of printing, lithography could be used for reproducing both illustrations as well as letter forms, and thus became a popular medium for printing early advertising posters as well as magazine covers and illustrations. Its commercial success was ensured as well by the use of color inks and the development of a complicated but effective process called chromolithography, patented in 1839 and used increasingly after mid-century for a wide range of printed materials such as book illustration, journal covers, and advertising posters. ## Wallpaper and Fabric Printing The technology of continuous printing contributed to the growth of other industries in the nineteenth century, particularly the manufacture of printed wallpapers. Though printing by hand from engraved wooden blocks continued, the introduction of patterns engraved on metal cylinders fed continuously by rolls of paper decreased costs and dramatically increased production that reached a much broader market for wall coverings by the early 1840s. Even before the advent of rotary printing and steampowered pressing machines, wallpaper manufacturers had produced a wide range of styles. A typical selection available to clientele ranged from scenic papers involving several blocks carefully registered and separately inked to produce the effect of painted frescoes, to papers that reproduced repeated vignettes of village life or chinoiserie, to imitated textures such as marble to flocked papers produced from pulverized silk or wool attached to glue-coated patterns. Also popular were geometric patterns or themes derived from nature or historicizing decorative motifs. In Stendhals novel Le Rouge et Le Noir (The Red and the Black, from 1830 and mentioned above, see here), the narrator comments on the social pretensions of wealthy calico printers in the French town of Verrières, an indication of the prosperity enjoyed by a new class of industrial manufacturer. The manufacture of machine-printed cotton was simultaneous with the introduction of more automated methods for the weaving of silk. The center of this latter development remained the French city of Lyon, which had supplied the fashionable capital of Paris and other European cities with woven silk since the early eighteenth century, when the industry employed more than 20,000 silk workers (see here). The French Revolution disrupted the industry, but production resumed after the Peace of Vienna (1815) and the introduction of a new type of hand-operated loom that facilitated the weaving of silk threads by guiding them through a series of cards that were punched to create patterns. Although it was usually named the Jacquard loom for one of its innovators, Joseph Marie Jacquard (17521834), several individuals were responsible for equally significant contributions to this ingenious process. The developments in the silk and cotton industries during the first half of the nineteenth century expanded the market for womens clothing. Evidence of the importance of fashion during this time appears in the work of portrait painters who depicted with painstaking detail the wardrobes of their wealthy subjects, and the more numerous and stereotypical hand-colored woodblock and engraved illustrations of contemporary dress known as fashion plates. These plates appeared as features in monthly subscription magazines (sometimes as few as two or four at the beginning of each issue) with commentaries on fashion trends along with advice on matters relating to public appearance and polite social behavior. With the revival of Lyon as a center for silk manufacturing, and the restoration of the monarchy after 1815, France continued to set the tone for fashion and for printed fashion plates. Fashion plates acknowledge the role of a more public social life that stimulated fashion in the early nineteenth century, a consciousness not only of ones dress but also its relation to proper social behavior for women (as noted above, Percier and Fontaine had cited the increased social interaction in urban settings as a cause for deviations from more stable standards of good taste; see here). In the fashion plate (fig. 3.14) from German-born publisher Rudolph Ackermanns Repository of the Arts (1827), accessories and props such as chairs or benches imply a narrative or story. They reveal foremost a feeling for activities associated with family and leisure (rather than work or active physical exercise), such as reading, playing an instrument (after the 1840s the most popular instrument was the piano), walking in the park, attending dances, or spending time with friends and children, all presented with idealized young women in relaxed, informal poses. Until the 1820s, the preference in womens fashion was for high-waisted long dresses (a legacy of the classically inspired Empire style), enlivened and personalized by accessories such as satin ribbons or velvet borders and elaborate coiffures. During the second third of the century, fuller skirts and patterned cloth, printed or woven, become more common, and pastimes illustrated in the fashion plates grew to include attendance at modern spectator sports such as horse races or concerts. The popularity of these plates demonstrates that dress was a way in which the habits of the wealthy were communicated to an increasing number of those with sufficient means to aspire to such practices, and who might use the plates to plan their own wardrobes. Through marketing, merchandising, and less costly methods for the color reproduction of illustrations, the second half of the nineteenth century brought such habits into an even broader and increasingly competitive commercial realm. Designers, as well as publishers such as Ackermann, navigated a new and dynamic commercial territory that brought together the social aspirations of a wider range of consumers, the business practices of manufacturers, competition, tradition, and novelty. The sense of material and social progress may be gleaned from the text of An Encyclopedia of Cottage, Farm, and Villa Architecture and Furniture (1833) by English designer and author J. C. Loudon (17831843), who remarked that beauty as well as comfort were concerns of the cottager (not just the wealthy); that is, design was a means to improve the human condition  the arts were a form of knowledge once limited to the wealthy, now capable of being shared with the many. Loudon acknowledged the coexistence of a variety of styles, from the Grecian as seen in the work of Thomas Hope (see here), to the Gothic, the Tudor, and the Italian; all styles signified progress, a standard of living and quality of life beyond subsistence, a civilizing achievement (albeit Eurocentric, see here) now made possible for a broader public by the spread of knowledge, by technology, by the efficiency and skill of increasingly rationalized labor, and the creative invention of designers. ![](images/38ec7855cec0fb95f6c183269c3812c1cf79f79012518c054d0995c38998ff4d.jpg) ## 3.14 Fashion plate, hand-colored engraving, $9 \% \times 5 ~ 1 \%$ in (24 × 14.6 cm), from Repository of the Arts, Rudolph Ackermann, 1827. One of the key elements, according to design historian John Heskett, was the mass production of interchangeable parts in the manufacture of firearms in the early nineteenth century, a process that required precision and careful oversight in order to ensure precise uniformity and eliminate time-consuming handwork. At the outset the high capital costs of experiments with machine-made parts were underwritten through government contracts to manufacturers for large quantities of uniform goods. The main advantages to this method were the ease of repair and replacement of parts, and the vast savings in the cost of skilled labor, especially in the time-consuming tasks of filing and fitting parts to each product. The principle upon which this productivity was based, once again, was the division of labor; the virtues of a rational approach to production were praised by British economist Adam Smith (17231790), whose description of a pin factory extols the virtues of dividing tasks and links such developments to productivity and general material progress: One man draws out the wire, another straightens it, a third cuts it, a fourth points it, a fifth grinds it at the top for receiving the head ... and the important business of making a pin is, in this manner, divided into about eighteen distinct operations, which in some manufactories are performed by distinct hands ... This separation too is carried furthest in those countries which enjoy the highest degree of industry and improvement; what is the work of one in a rude state of society being generally that of several in an improved one. In the United States the application of Smiths principles to specialized sequences of machine operations designed to fabricate increasingly complicated products with precise interchangeable parts may be seen in a number of goods, from rifles manufactured by Simeon North and later by Samuel Colt in Hartford, Connecticut, in the 1840s (under contract from the American government), to the clock mechanisms made by Eli Terry at around the same time, and also to the manufacture of the sewing machine in the 1850s by Isaac M. Singer in New York and other companies for factory rather than domestic use. The success of this approach, which became known as the American System of Manufacture, should also be viewed in light of the absence of guilds or longstanding craft traditions, whose working methods and continued emphasis upon skill could not easily be adapted to the strict uniformity imposed by Singer and others. Historian David Hounshell remind us, however, that the transition to the American System was slow and uneven, and that even successful companies such as Singer continued to employ workers to finish individual parts until demand forced greater attention to ensuring precise uniformity through increased capital investment in special-function machines. For the most part, Europeans praised the utilitarian approach of these early American manufacturers for its efficiency and practicality, often described abroad as uniquely American values. But some critics labeled the American System artless, lacking those outward signs of beauty and skill that linked manufactured products with individual identity, social progress, and culture. And, as historian Jeffrey L. Meikle has observed, the self-sufficiency and practicality praised by many observers of American society was both a generalization and also somewhat of a myth  decoration and democratic variety came to characterize the expanding American market for domestic ceramic and glassware and demonstrate middle-class material aspirations in a fluid, heterogeneous society. While the production side of American design emphasized rationalized unskilled labor, standardization, and the substitution of human labor with machines, the consumption side turned to fashion and a parade of eclectic, quickly changing patterns and methods of decoration. As an example we might cite the shelf clocks manufactured by Eli Terry in Connecticut beginning in 1816 that used wooden rather than brass gears and required winding every 30 hours as opposed to every eight days. Many of Terrys wooden clock cases were designed by Chauncey Jerome (1793 1868) in a restrained Neoclassical style featuring columns to each side, a scrolled pediment above (fig. 3.15) and a hand-painted scene inside of the glass panel that protected the painted dial. But such a standard approach soon expanded to include a variety of styles with painted and carved decoration. Ornament was not a requirement of the manufacturing process but seemed to be an essential component of the marketing side of the American System. Philadelphia Museum of Art. In addition to the American System of Manufacture, writing the history of modern design in the United States today also demands a consideration of race. The standard of living enjoyed by the wealthy and increasingly available to the middle class in the nineteenth century through the acquisition of designed goods was generally less available, if not legally denied, to the nations Black and Indigenous populations, especially in the American South. Institutional slavery and deeply embedded racial prejudice severely limited the circulation of such goods to the enslaved, to the poor, and to the marginalized to whom freedom of movement and opportunity for economic advancement were denied. Yet even under the conditions of slavery, Black Americans produced compelling objects. David Drake, an enslaved man in South Carolina, signed his large hand-thrown glazed pots with the name Dave, and also inscribed bits of text and sometimes poetry on the surface of these largely utilitarian wares, even though literacy was often forbidden in enslaved people. The history of design under such conditions has yet to be written, and is but one instance of white privilege that has made little room for Black experience. In the wake of Emancipation, the path toward equality was slow, and the Jim Crow laws reinforced segregation and deepened stereotypes and caricatures that persist to this day. As noted in Chapter 7 (see here), the mass-circulation merchandise catalogs featuring an array of products published by the Sears Roebuck and Montgomery Ward companies in the later nineteenth century were essentially colorblind: unlike retail stores that refused to serve Black shoppers, catalogs allowed Black consumers access to design, contributing to the expanded market for designed goods. Craftsmen and labor organizations, especially in Britain, often viewed mechanization as a threat to jobs and to their status and independence as artisans. They lobbied for restricted use of machines by manufacturers, and such restrictions eventually allowed American goods to compete for the British market. By the second half of the nineteenth century, design, eclectic historicism, and printed advertising had become regular features of manufacturing in the United States, as techniques of mechanized mass production were adapted to supply variety and choice in stimulating consumer demand for goods and furnishings. With improved transportation, patents to protect invention, and reduced tariffs, products such as soap, biscuits, condiments, and breakfast cereal (for example Quaker Oats) became national brands with recognizable logos and packaging. Changing styles were often viewed as a barometer of improvement and ingenuity while at the same time product standardization communicated reliability and consistency  just as new frontier settlements, reaping machines, and railways were transforming nature by making it more productive and civilized, manufacturers were transforming raw materials into finished consumer goods, augmenting their usefulness, availability, and consumer appeal. # Chapter 4 # Design, Society, and Standards ## Early Design Reform Cities were the focus of an expanding industrial and commercial economy throughout the nineteenth century. The scope of industrial development was increasingly global, culminating at mid-century with the first of numerous Worlds Fairs (Londons 1851 official name was the Great Exhibition of the Works of Industry of All Nations, see here) that brought together the goods, raw materials, and markets of Europe, North America, Asia, Africa, and South America, promoting a Eurocentric world view identifying Western technology with material and cultural progress. The growth of urban populations throughout the nations of Europe presented challenges to governments in terms of housing, sanitation, unemployment, safety, and conflicts between classes and levels of economic opportunity. The years 1830 and 1848 saw outbreaks of social unrest in Europe with working-class rebellion and the threat of socialist revolution leading to armed conflict and its suppression through stronger central authority. A portion of the emerging class of industrialists, merchants, and professionals during the first half of the nineteenth century took an interest in the living and labor conditions of factory workers and artisans whose numbers swelled in the cities of Europe and whose labor was essential to owners profits. Along with industrial wealth came a desire, even a sense of moral obligation, to address economic inequality and the ills of rapid urbanization, even if the motivation toward reform was often tied to maintaining a hierarchical social order within a more commercial, complex, and diverse society. Reform efforts included an active role for the arts in promoting progressive social values (for instance, the establishment of government-sponsored museums of art such as the National Gallery, London, in 1824), as well as a focus upon the role of design in manufacturing as a means of increasing the appeal, enjoyment, and civilizing value of useful goods. While some reformers presented moral arguments supporting the Gothic style as the model of beauty and nostalgia for a shared faith and unified, harmonious society, others took more modest initiatives to cultivate a broader shared taste in the public at large through education and the founding of collections whose displays would develop discriminating judgment. Design reform aimed to establish standards for taste in the public interest that combined aesthetics, politics, and ethics, part of a broad response to the changing social dynamics that accompanied industrial and commercial expansion toward the middle of the nineteenth century. Self-interest merged with groupinterest, as reformers argued that manufactures were a source of wealth, prestige, and economic and social progress in a competitive, international free-market economy. While reformers often employed polemical rhetoric for effect, their efforts seldom achieved the results they intended, took account of the increasingly heterogeneous market for manufactured products, or fully understood the commercial forces (generally criticized as the effects of fashion) that operated across the broad spectrum of desire for such goods. For this reason, until the 1860s design reform was mostly limited to the luxury market and to those manufacturers that catered to that clientele, whether private or public. From the 1860s until the end of the nineteenth century, design reform initiatives expanded to include consumer guides, published as books or as installments in periodicals, often directed toward women, who bore the responsibility for creating a proper home and family environment. Both before and after 1860, reform efforts further invested the applied arts with cultural meanings, attributing to them the ability to shape values and behavior, establishing a basis for shared standards and a canon of modern design that continues to influence our understanding of its history today. ## Industry and Its Discontents Not all observers welcomed the expansion of production and the emergence of a consumer society with the same enthusiasm as economist Adam Smith. In the early nineteenth century, Britain experienced a staggering growth of industrial manufacturing, particularly in the production of textiles in steam-powered mills, in cast iron, and in the construction of railroad engines, rolling stock, and track, accompanied by an increasing pressure to mine coal as a source of fuel. The large-scale production of commodities accelerated the dynamic relationship between production and consumption created by rationalized labor, the introduction of mechanization, and marketing to an elastic market for manufactured goods. Free trade, competition, and overproduction combined to make large-scale manufacturing a volatile and uncertain business, often with decidedly negative consequences for workers. Even as emerging urban industrial areas in Britain such as Manchester, Birmingham, and Liverpool gained a legislative voice in Parliament, and as the industrialists who were chosen to represent their interests lobbied for free trade and the further expansion of markets for their products, there was ample cause for concern about the negative human consequences during these early stages of industrialization. The demand for labor in factory towns resulted in problems of crowding, inadequate sanitation, disease, and increasing alcoholism. Workers remained socially and politically marginalized, with little if any electoral power and with few choices but to suffer low wages, poverty, dangerous working conditions, and long hours, the last enabled through the new technology of gas lighting that extended evening working hours. Working-class discontent led to uprisings and revolts between 1810 and 1830, followed by the beginnings of workers organizations (trade unions) and the practice of organizing strikes to protest against low wages. The circumstances of factory life also led to some legislative reforms such as child labor laws and the first experiments in socialism in Great Britain and the United States by manufacturer-turned-reformer Robert Owen (17711858). In Owens New Harmony community in Indiana, workers lives were organized along a collective and egalitarian rather than an individual and hierarchical basis with communal responsibility for education and child-rearing. Poverty and working-class exploitation were themes for a number of writers, including Scottish-born Thomas Carlyle (17951881), who lamented the extreme contrast between the prosperity of the upper and middle classes and the poverty of the working class, pleading for reform: The condition of England ... is justly regarded as one of the most ominous, and withal one of the strangest, ever seen in this world. England is full of wealth, of multifarious produce, supply for human want in every kind; yet England is dying of inanition. With unabated bounty the land of England blooms and grows; waving with yellow harvests; thick-studded with workshops, industrial implements, with fifteen millions of workers, understood to be the strongest, the cunningest and the willingest our earth ever had; these men are here; the work they have done, the fruit they have realised is here, abundant, exuberant on every hand of us: and behold, some baleful fiat as of Enchantment has gone forth saying, touch it not, ye workers, ye master-workers, ye master-idlers; none of you can touch it, no man of you shall be the better for it; this is enchanted fruit! On the poor workers such fiat fall first, in its rudest shape; but on the rich master-idlers nor any richest or highest man escape, but all are like to be brought low with it, and made poor enough, in the money sense or in a far fataler one. In response to the demands of an expanding working class in Britain, and fearing that unrest might lead to revolution, Parliament undertook efforts to implement standardized codes for the due process of law, access to broad-based educational opportunities, the founding of public museums and parks, restrictions to employers exploitation of child labor, and reformation of the nations prison system. Just as attempts were made to regulate working conditions, so too governments undertook legislation to standardize and regulate aspects of industrial production, seen, for instance, in the adoption of standardized widths for the laying of railroad track in England. With many privately owned and competing companies, such shared standards designated a role for central government, regulating untrammeled industrial expansion by ensuring compatibility in connecting one railroad line to another. Such efforts remind us that the history of design extends beyond private middle- and upper-class consumption of household goods and furnishings associated with individual creators; it also involves decisions in the broader public interest undertaken by political process and consensus, whether for safety or compatibility, whether railroad track or (in the early twentieth century) battery sizes, or receptacles for electrical current and elsewhere in the utility industries. Whether stirred by the fiery rhetoric of Carlyle or by other progressive calls for social justice, many reformers, whether artists, architects, industrialists, or politicians, were motivated by a sense of social responsibility and a desire to address social inequality and improve the lives of working people. As noted above, such efforts may be viewed as a genuine concern for improving the quality of life and serving the public interest, or as measures to exert social control, promoting values imposed from above in order to ensure compliance and prevent disruptive protest or revolution. Understanding the context of early design reform involves reconciling these conflicting views and motivations, and recognizing that reform embodied complex attitudes aimed at balancing the desire for shared values with a recognition and appreciation for the democratic, spontaneous impulses of an emerging popular culture and managing the speed and direction of change. In Britain, design reform was directed toward establishing common ground through taste. In most cases, the study of the past was the best guide to designs present and future, but this approach, usually referred to as historicism in art, did not mean slavish imitation of particular examples, but rather a starting point that might inform the manufacture of products using new materials and production methods. In short, history and modernity were compatible rather than antithetical. Architect and writer Matthew Digby Wyatt (18201877), welcomed technology in the form of new materials and processes to which guidelines might be applied, especially in relation to metals and casting; others, such as A. W. N. Pugin (18121852), called attention to the moral and ethical implications of style, ignoring the varied preferences of consumers. Still others, such as Parliament member Sir Henry Cole (18081882), lobbied for schools of design, the creation of a museum devoted to the decorative arts, and public exhibitions to display national and international contemporary design to inform manufacturers and educate consumers. In all cases, however, reformers believed in the existence of universal fixed standards for design, in the erosion of those standards due to a combination of industrialization and commercialism, and the need to establish and implement them in the best economic, social, and moral interests of an ever-expanding consuming public. Thus design reform was in this basic sense political, and many reformers understood that in order to be effective it was necessary to be persuasive. They used the latest technology in the printing industry to publish illustrations of designs that demonstrated their standards in books and journals in order to familiarize an expanding middle-class readership with the practice of good design, and staged exhibitions that promoted their point of view. ## Reform and the Gothic Revival One of the strongest advocates for standards or principles of design was A. W. N. (Augustus Welby Northmore) Pugin. Pugin, an architect, designer, and author who collaborated with Sir Charles Barry on the design and interior decoration for Londons Houses of Parliament (1837 ff.), contrasted the eclectic tastes in contemporary English architecture and design with the stylistic unity of its medieval and specifically Gothic past. For Pugin the consistency of the latter expressed moral truths as well as aesthetic harmony. Pugins conversion to Roman Catholicism in 1835 and his enthusiasm for the beauty and truth of the Gothic style led him to a passionate investigation of and appreciation for the style. He criticized the indiscriminate and superficial application of any type of carved, printed, and painted decoration to a wide variety of furnishings and utilitarian goods, which he felt was motivated by commercial greed and self-interest. The equation of decoration with excess was not entirely new, but the focus upon the Gothic style as the purest expression of the appropriate relationship between construction and decoration, and the urgent pleas for reform in Pugins writings, are characteristic of a polemic tone and an ethical undercurrent in the nineteenth-century design reform movement. Pugins principles of design stemmed from his understanding of Gothic architecture (recall that architecture was also the basis for Percier and Fontaine in recommending ancient Greece and Rome as models for interior design, see here). He believed that painting and sculpture (whether decorative or figural) were essentially embellishments of architecture and as such should be subordinated to, rather than independent from, the structure they decorate. Pugin argued that this rational, subordinating principle of organization is best exemplified in the Gothic style. Looking at the early-thirteenthcentury north transept portal of Chartres Cathedral, for example (fig. 4.1), one can observe that the jamb statues maintain their character as columns supporting a porch, other carved figures do not project beyond their containing niches, and virtually all individual elements are part of a larger unit that is in turn related to the tripartite division of the doorway. As suggested by art historian Margaret Belcher, within such a framework a unity is imposed upon all of the arts that might also serve metaphorically for an ideal society in which all individuals are unified by a single faith and live in harmony  individual difference yields to an ideal unity. In addition, Pugin advised respect for the characteristics of materials and argued, for instance, that carved materials, such as wood, should not look as if they are molded (nor should gutta percha masquerade as carved wood), and that while it is natural for artists and artisans to transform the materials they work with, stone should always retain in some measure its essential stony character, and should not pretend to be something else. Such truth to material remained a tenet of design reform well into the twentieth century, whether exploring the malleability of reinforced concrete or the tensile strength of steel to create the curtain walls of steel-and-glass office towers. Disrespect for the true nature of materials not only violated the example of Gothic architecture and design but was also dishonest. The arbitrary use of pointed arches and quatrefoils for the base of a lamp or the back of a chaise was for Pugin a superficial appropriation of details, which revealed a lack of understanding of both the stylistic and symbolic unity of the Gothic style. Other grievances against contemporary design included the use of scenic or illusionistic patterns for wallpapers or carpets since the modeling of forms might be inconsistent with the effects of natural light, and such views interfered with the structural function of walls and floors. He registered some of his objections in a satirical drawing of monstrosities where the Gothic style is used to construct a collection of elaborately decorated doorstops, lamps, and other objects that misappropriate the styles principles. His disdain for the modeled forms and detail in examples such as the Gothic wallpaper design illustrated in figure 3.12 was registered in the following comments: What are commonly termed Gothic pattern papers for hanging on walls, where a wretched caricature of a pointed building is repeated from skirting to cornice, door over pinnacle and pinnacle over door. His remarks are condescending in both social and aesthetic terms, as when he comments that a particular misguided style of decoration was a great favourite with hotel and tavern keepers. ![](images/4b35ab675b328349e4c8ff1d00220ec48b527d4f5e02e528ad78fed59e966b5d.jpg) North transept portal, Chartres Cathedral, France, c. 1210–1220. In 1841, Pugin enumerated his principles derived from the Gothic style in True Principles of Pointed or Christian Architecture, illustrated with engravings based on the authors drawings. In this book he noted that there should be no features about a building which are not necessary for convenience, construction, or propriety, and all ornament should consist of enrichment of the essential construction of the building. He also published books of illustrations intended as models for the design of furniture, metalwork, ceramic plates and tiles, and ecclesiastical garments. A number of firms manufactured his designs, the moral as well as aesthetic dimension of which can be seen in a plate manufactured by Minton in 1849 whose border is inscribed with the admonishment Waste Not Want Not in bold Gothic letters. Elaborated in many forms by other contemporary designers and authors, Pugins true principles became the basis for many nineteenth-century approaches to design standards that advocate the harmony between utility and beauty. Art criticism for Pugin was inseparable from social criticism, and a disregard for design principles as he defined them was also a deviation both from beauty and moral principles. Eclecticism and lack of discrimination were signs of disorder and decay, while disregarding the proper use of materials was deceitful as well as ignorant of enduring standards. On the positive side, the understanding and practice of fixed principles were socially as well as aesthetically desirable, contributing to the well-being of society, instilling common values during a time of uncertainty and change brought about by industrialization and rising materialism. Wedded to a discriminating historicism and looking to the past as a guide to the future, the moral dimension of Pugins views lent urgency to his writings. His views were based on a firm belief in the ability of the arts to shape behavior, and were shared by a number of artists, tastemakers, manufacturers, and politicians during the Victorian age. Many of Pugins designs for patterns and interior furnishings were inspired by fourteenth- and fifteenth-century examples he studied and recorded in England and on visits to France. Motifs such as the quatrefoil or fleur-de-lys are common, as are other plant or lobed forms that share the same characteristics  repetition or symmetry as a basis for composition, crisp contours, and flat shapes. A lengthy government report of 18356, prepared by Parliaments Select Committee on Arts and Manufactures under the auspices of the Board of Trade, noted a decline in demand for British goods abroad and an increase in foreign imports at home (a negative trade balance, in contemporary economic terms). Drawing was an essential component of this education, based upon the belief that the noble and didactic aims of the fine arts would also elevate the general standards of public taste in manufactured goods. The Select Committee also advocated specific recommendations for such standards, based, not surprisingly, upon a balance between utility and beauty, and the use of decorative patterns derived from the study of botanical forms and select examples from the past as well as from other cultures including India and West Asia. Among the most prominent advocates for design education was Sir Henry Cole (18081882), a civil servant who believed that an alliance between fine art and manufactures would promote public taste as well as restore Britains competitive edge in the expanding global market for manufactured goods. Coles attitudes were strongly influenced by the House Report of 18356. Under the pseudonym Felix Summerly, Cole edited a series of illustrated travel books and storybooks intended for children (known as the Home Treasury) that were intended to improve the quality of childrens literature available in the market. Throughout his long career in public service, he argued that public museums should serve the interests of the working class and be the antidote to brutality and vice, providing models for responsible behavior and social harmony. Turning to design, the harmony of utility and beauty in the decorative arts also served as a metaphor for the socially cohesive virtues of moderation and restraint, and government-sponsored design education provided practical training for artisans as a necessary component in the expanding production of a wide range of domestic products. As a model business venture, Cole furnished designs (both his own and those of his artist friends) for tea sets and other manufactured goods, founding Summerly Art Manufactures in 1847 as a model for manufacturers. Richard Redgrave (18041888) was a painter of frequently moralizing subjects who contributed to a number of Coles initiatives, providing illustrations for the Home Treasury and designs for Summerly Art Manufactures. Glass allows for the tentative exploration of three-dimensional space, since it is naturally transparent and thus permits such effects while remaining true to its material. Neither Pugin nor Cole condemned the use of unskilled and increasingly divided labor or the use of new technologies to increase the efficiency of production. Rather, they were interested in articulating, practicing, and disseminating common standards of taste in design. If machines or specialization (sometimes taking the form of piecework, that is, the hiring out or subcontracting of particular parts of a product that are later assembled and sold) could increase efficiency and lower cost, it was only natural that manufacturers employ such means to their advantage. While concerned with the welfare of working people, the reform efforts of Cole and his group applied more to finished products than to the labor practices by which they were manufactured. Unlike Pugins insistence upon the morality and unity of the Gothic style, however, Cole and his group advocated a broader range of styles in their approach to reform, and direct studies from nature formed part of their program to educate designers for industry. Cole recognized that his ideas would never gain acceptance without lobbying for education and attempting to shape public opinion. He helped to sponsor a periodical entitled The Art Journal (184951) that published illustrations for designers and manufacturers to follow, and also played a role in determining the curricula of Britains government-run schools of design, focusing upon drawing as a mainstay of training rather than the development of workshop skills. Following the Crystal Palace exhibition of 1851 (see below), Cole realized his longstanding ambition to establish the South Kensington (later Victoria and Albert) Museum as a collection devoted to the decorative arts and the improvement of public taste (his portrait, in mosaic, is still found in a stairwell in the older part of the building located on Exhibition Road). In the 1860s the South Kensington Museum installed gas lighting to permit working people to view the collections until ten oclock in the evening three nights a week. ## The Great Exhibition of 1851 The Great Exhibition of the Works of Industry of All Nations held in London at Hyde Park in 1851 was a watershed for design at mid-century; this first of many Worlds Fairs emphatically confirmed the reality of a heterogeneous middle-class consumer culture in Britain, Europe, their imperial territories, and North America, that linked commodities with technological progress, abundance, social transformation, competition, and the forging of national identities. Never before had the decorative and industrial arts been the focus of such widespread international attention, crossing class boundaries and stirring the popular imagination. Supported by royal consort Prince Albert (1819 1861) and organized by Henry Cole and his group of reformers, the Industry of All Nations or Great Exhibition of 1851 expanded upon earlier national exhibitions of industry in France (see here) and was the model for numerous international exhibitions in the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries in Europe, Australia, and the United States. The exhibition was originally conceived as a national fair, but in the process of planning became international, drawing 14,000 producers, half of whom were from outside Britain. It was housed in a temporary structure (fig. 4.5) designed by architect Joseph Paxton (18031865), attracting great interest by its frank use of cast and wrought iron, breathtaking expanses of glass, and the complete absence of traditional building materials such as stone and load-bearing walls. Relying upon uniform prefabricated parts, the Exhibition Hall was built in the short span of eight months, a necessity resulting from a lack of agreement among organizers in earlier planning efforts. Although Paxtons choice of materials and method of construction had previously been used in utilitarian structures such as railroad stations and greenhouses, the Crystal Palace, as it was dubbed at the time, with its colorful painted ribs of the arches of the nave, high vaults, rhythmic arcades, and cast structural elements, aspired to rival if not to surpass the great architectural landmarks of the past, sounding a note of modernity, progress, and confidence in the aesthetic possibilities of industrialized technology. ![](images/95417ed2a610a60292d2e60c47706736d6d9341bb38e0844a6f6235eb5fe5819.jpg) Joseph Paxton, Crystal Palace, north water tower and transept, London, built 1851, photographed by Francis Frith. Although the goods on display were not for sale, the display of myriad products in close proximity to the spectator linked the exhibited objects to the promise of abundance, unlimited availability of goods for the middle class, and the fulfillment of dreams and desires in the near future. Historian Thomas Richards placed the exhibition in the context of nineteenth-century theatrical spectacle, suggesting the transformation of goods as they entered a vast space of association. Such magical, quasi-religious reactions to the Crystal Palace and its exhibition were not uncommon. Queen Victoria herself remarked that when standing inside the structure she was filled with devotion. By the early 1860s, enterprising investors and merchants had translated the magical associations of such vast spaces into a new kind of shopping experience with the first department store, the Bon Marché in Paris in 1869, followed shortly after in the United States with Wanamakers in Philadelphia, Macys in New York, and then in Britain with Londons Selfridges. These large, multi-storied businesses co-existed with boutiques and other smaller, specialized retail establishments. Department stores introduced uniform pricing (rather than bargaining with merchants), seasonal sales, and a large and diverse inventory of ready-made goods and fabrics. The combination of optimism and the embrace of materialism as the measure of economic and social progress seen in the broad public reaction to the Crystal Palace is mirrored in the confident social aspirations of characters in contemporary fiction on both sides of the English Channel. In Honoré de Balzacs (17991850) novel César Birotteau (1837), the protagonist comes to Paris as a poor provincial with no formal education or connections, rising through hard work and prudent investment to become a respected and successful Parisian perfume merchant. Upon being awarded the prestigious medal of the Legion of Honor for his support of the monarchy, César undertakes the expansion and decoration of his Paris apartment with the help of an aspiring architect in order to host a ball celebrating his financial success and social achievement. While subsequent investments and speculations bring disaster to César and demonstrate the unpredictability of both fortune and friendships based upon it, the novel portrays self-improvement and social advancement as realistic middle-class aspirations in this nineteenth-century novel. The spirit of progress, expansion, and nationalism associated with manufactured goods at the first Worlds Fairs defines the iconography of the Albert Memorial in Kensington Gardens (fig. 4.6), built between 1864 and 1876 and dedicated to the Prince Consort, who died in 1861 and who had strongly supported the Crystal Palace exhibition. The seated Albert, holding a copy of the exhibition catalog (rather than a traditional symbol of authority), peers out over the four corners of the earth, each represented by a beast and symbolized by an allegorical figural group referring to the continents of Asia, Africa, Europe, and America. Corresponding to the four continents are four categories of productive human endeavor: manufactures, agriculture, engineering, and commerce. The manufactures group includes the bearded Herculeslike figure of a blacksmith representing labor and a seated figure holding a plate and urn as examples of human-made products. A standing female figure holds an hourglass and beside her sits a beehive, both referring to time-saving production efficiency. The symbolism of this orderly vision of the world seems clear: Britain stands at the intersection of heaven (note the cross at the top of the baldachin) and earth: natures riches under Gods watch are increased by the creativity and ingenuity of the human race, an orderly vision of prosperity and peace. It is not surprising that the markedly Anglocentric vision of the Albert Memorial coincides with aggressive imperial expansion that provided raw materials to an expanding industrial empire under British and other European nations control. ![](images/c7403b01e83e21d45e4ad2c60aad4152c7b168330b491a0fb9946d2f0cc6b1a9.jpg) Popular reaction to the Great Exhibition may also be gauged from the proliferation of printed materials in the form of illustrated catalogs, books of illustrations featuring masterpieces from the exhibition or views of its varied displays, and smaller-scale ephemera in the form of sheet music, souvenir plates, even Christmas cards celebrating the event and printed using the recently developed process of chromolithography. While the Great Exhibition celebrated productivity and progress in manufacturing, the reform-minded organizers themselves were disappointed with the results, for there was little evidence that public taste had been improved or that standards of design had been widely adopted by British manufacturers. The cover of Coles Art Journal devoted to the exhibition illustrates the didactic aspirations of the organizers (fig. 4.7). An allegorical figure of Peace holding a dove stands in front of a globe flanked by two kneeling figures  a designer and a craftsman. To her right, a well-groomed gentleman, clearly designated as the designer, holds a drawing of a chalice and is surrounded by books and a painters palette. These attributes  in combination with his groomed, stylish hair and long-sleeved tunic, both of which are impediments to physical labor  associate the designer with the fine arts, their inclusion in the liberal arts, and their place in design education at the London School of Design. In contrast, the craftsman kneeling opposite him, and whose hand he clasps, has cropped hair and wears a workers smock with rolled-up sleeves, and is surrounded by the tools of his trade, including an anvil and some finished products. This somewhat simplistic characterization of the relationship between design and production suggests that the healthy (if subservient) collaboration between craftsman and artist produces useful goods that contribute to prosperity and peace. ![](images/64e4d834cf5954c8663a0c8824f4b96ae922bfac7966778856eb8381d3f53330.jpg) LOSDON: PUBLISHED POR THE PROPRIRTORS, IY GEORGE VIRTUE Catalog of the Great Exhibition of the Works of Industry of All Nations, front cover, published in The Art Journal, London, 1851. Ralph Wornum, Keeper of the Queens Collection of Pictures, won a prize for an essay devoted to the goods on display at the exhibition, published in the Crystal Palace Illustrated Catalogue (the same book that Prince Albert holds on his lap in the Albert Memorial). Wornum embraced the tenets of design reform, namely the harmony of utility and beauty, and criticized the use of naturalistic flowers and plants for the decoration of carpets and other floor coverings as a violation of fitness to purpose. He also disapproved of the whims of fashion and argued that principles of good taste provided a reliable and permanent foundation for excellence in design. Wornum described an immense Renaissance Revival sideboard exhibited by the French firm of Fourdinois (fig. 4.8) as if it was a work of sculpture, commenting upon its appropriate use of imagery (the subjects include allegories of the continents of Europe, Africa, Asia, and America, foods associated with them, and four hunting hounds, all relating to a dining room setting) as well as its expert carving and the ability of modern work to rival the masterpieces of earlier periods. Such an iconographic analysis might well have appeared too elevated for the general public to grasp or even care about. Moreover, it ignored the motivations of many middle-class consumers, who looked to commodities not for instruction or narrative meaning, but rather as symbols of status and luxury. At the same time, Wornum lamented the preponderance of deluxe furniture at the expense of less costly and more practical examples of good taste directed toward shaping the needs and improving the taste, and lives, of average middle-class consumers. Critics often focused their attention upon the more ornate objects of carving in wood, cut glass, silver, and other materials on display. Size and decorative richness naturally drew attention in the vast interior space of the Crystal Palace, and ornament was often seen as evidence of art that added value to objects of use. In addition, ornate work often won medals and prizes that contributed to manufacturers reputations. ![](images/f7a5acbadb97969f0bc0c358e3cce770d87c40e34c2e32965879fedaabf5f5b6.jpg) Henri-Auguste Fourdinois, sideboard, manufactured in Paris. Wood engraving from Dickinsons’ comprehensive pictures of the Great Exhibition of 1851. Many if not most of the critics of the Great Exhibition were architects and fine artists. For instance, German architect and theorist Gottfried Semper (18031879), who lived for a time in London and taught at the government-sponsored School of Design there, was dismayed by the uneven quality of goods on display at the Great Exhibition, comparing it to the biblical Tower of Babel (Genesis 11: 19). Richard Redgrave arrived at much the same conclusion, writing that the absence of any fixed principle in ornamental design is most apparent in the Exhibition. Semper, however, was optimistic that the study of the exhibitions best objects might form the basis of standards that considered the rational use of new materials and techniques along with function and the appropriate use of decoration; the public reaction to Coles Marlborough House exhibition (see below) suggested otherwise. Owen Jones (18091874), an architect and designer who also was an organizer of the exhibition, was responsible for the color scheme of the buildings interior, and an advocate of design reform. Following the opening of the Great Exhibition, he wrote that the majority of goods on display demonstrated novelty without beauty, beauty without intelligence, and all work without faith. One can understand his disappointment, for Jones had published examples of decorative patterns based upon drawings from the fourteenth-century Nasrid Alhambra palace in Granada, Spain. The plates of Joness Alhambra volume were intended not only as illustrations of a famed site, but also as didactic models for designers and manufacturers. In spite of his reaction to the Great Exhibition, Jones continued his reform efforts, both as a designer and as the author of The Grammar of Ornament (1856), the most extensive dictionary of design standards at that time. The Grammar of Ornament contained 100 color plates (using the chromolithographic process, see page 69) of patterns based upon historic Western and non-Western examples, in media ranging from carpets to ceramic tiles, from relief sculptures to illuminated manuscripts, selected to illustrate universal principles of design. Jones advocated a broad historicism based upon principles (32 in all) printed at the end of the volume. The principles are reminiscent of those established by Pugin from the study of Gothic architecture, but extend to a wider variety of examples both geographically and chronologically. Joness principles include: Construction should be decorated. Decoration should never be purposely constructed; Flowers or other natural objects should not be used as ornaments, but conventional representations founded upon them sufficiently suggestive to convey the intended image to the mind, without destroying the unity of the object they are employed to decorate. Universally obeyed in the best periods of Art, equally violated when Art declines. The Grammar of Ornament went through numerous printings in the late nineteenth century, and its approach to ornament and color was adopted for the decoration of numerous buildings and interiors during this period. In addition to advocating the use of models from other periods and cultures, as in one of three plates devoted to Moorish decoration (fig. 4.9), Jones included the possibility of constructing original patterns based upon the study of nature tempered by the rules he enumerated and as taught in the British Schools of Design (see also the Global Inspiration II, here). British designer Christopher Dresser (18341904) contributed original plates to the Grammar featuring ornamental shapes based upon leaf and floral motifs in a style that simplified organic forms into flattened linear contours (see fig. 6.1 and Chapter 6, here). Dresser was a student at the London School of Design and was hired by manufacturers to create original designs for workshop production. During his career, along with other contemporary artist- and architect-designers, Dresser attempted to expand the reform standards by embracing greater originality and freedom in pattern design, and greater invention in borrowing from the design of other cultures, including Japan, that attracted his interest during the 1860s and prompted his visit there in 1876 as a representative of the South Kensington (renamed Victoria and Albert) Museum. ![](images/fd4df073d46a67bf1db4a297f3f3351294409773c0ea8ea2a29839bd61bb9ac9.jpg) Owen Jones, Arabian, no. 4, plate 34, color lithograph, 13 ${ ^ 1 } / _ { 4 } \times 8 ~ { ^ 7 } / _ { 8 }$ in (33.7 × 22.5 cm), from The Grammar of Ornament, London, 1856. Private collection. Public reaction to early design reform often varied with the views of experts such as Wornum, Semper, or Jones. For his efforts to establish standards for taste through exhibitions, publications, and education, Sir Henry Cole was much in the public eye, and was frequently a target for ridicule. In the novel Hard Times, published in serial form in 1854, Charles Dickens (18121870) poked fun at government efforts to dictate taste. A commissioner (a thinly veiled portrait of Cole) visits a government-run school and asks the students whether they would use a carpet bearing representations of flowers. A young girl, sent to the school by a well-meaning benefactor, replies that indeed she would, because she is very fond of flowers. But the commissioner sternly asks whether in that case she would put tables and chairs upon them and have people walking over them with heavy boots. It wouldnt hurt them, sir  they wouldnt crush and wither if you please, sir. They would be the pictures of what was very pretty and pleasant, I would fancy. But the commissioner replies, Ay, ay, ay. But you mustn’t fancy [italics added]. While the motives behind reform were perhaps well-intentioned in a paternalistic sense, Dickens is here defending the democratic freedom of choice as inherently more fundamental than parameters of good taste, whether or not they result in excess or sentimentality. Effectively, Dickens used the example of a child to connect the absence of good taste with innocence rather than vulgarity or dishonesty. In 1852 Cole established a Museum of Manufactures (precursor to the Victoria and Albert Museum, officially opened in South Kensington in 1857) at Marlborough House in London. There he put on view a comparison between objects that conformed to reform principles with a selection of those that violated those principles, dubbed the Chamber of Horrors. The latter ranged from illusionistic patterns on wallpapers (see, for instance, fig. 3.13) to objects whose excessive ornamentation interfered with their practicality; whether out of curiosity or delight, the majority of visitors spent their time viewing the examples of false rather than true principles. Although elevating the taste of the public remained part of the political mission of design reformers, the polemical tone and moralizing arguments of Pugin are less pronounced in Joness Grammar. Later, reform efforts generally assumed the friendlier tone of advice books by tastemakers such as Charles Eastlake (18361906). Eastlakes Hints on Household Taste in Furniture, Upholstery, and Other Details was published in 1868 and was popular in both the United Kingdom and the United States. Directed toward a mostly female readership who were expected to be responsible for creating a restful home environment, Eastlake wrote: We require no small amount of art and instruction and experience to see why direct imitation of natural objects is wrong in ornamental design. The quasi-fidelity with which the forms of a rose, or a bunch of ribbons, or a ruined castle, can be produced on carpets, crockery and wallpapers will always possess a certain kind of charm for the uneducated eye, just as the mimicry of natural sounds in music, from the rolling of thunder to the crackling of poultry, will always delight the vulgar ear. Both are ingenious and amusing but neither lie within the legitimate province of art. A comparison between one of Eastlakes wallpaper designs (fig. 4.10), manufactured by Jeffery & Company and appearing in Hints, and the printed or woven designs at the Great Exhibition (fig. 4.11) conforms to design reform principles. Eastlake criticized the lack of discrimination in matters of design, but his prose focuses less upon the moral dimensions of design than upon the cultivation of taste as a vehicle for selfimprovement as well as the reinforcement of middle-class cultural attitudes that identified the home as the sphere for womens activities. Turberville Smith, London. ## Images for All The expansion of design in manufactured goods and the creation of a consumer economy was accompanied and stimulated by advances in print technology, particularly in the reproduction of images for illustrations in books, magazines, and newspapers as well as advertising. A competitive free press, coupled with printing technologies and the gradual elimination of taxes on paper and printed materials, provide the technical and political context for the success of illustrated weekly journals aimed at an emerging mass market, including working people, that reached a circulation in Britain of over 200,000 copies by the middle of the nineteenth century. One of the earliest examples of this type of affordable illustrated journal is The Penny Magazine (beginning in 1832), published by Charles Knight (17911873), who began his career as a craftsman and printer. Knight is known for developing an economical method for producing color images from multiple engraved woodblocks. The Penny Magazine contained wood-engraved reproductions of works of art intended to provide examples of personal and social virtue. Knights venture was endorsed by the Society for the Distribution of Useful Knowledge, which supported the role of the arts as a civilizing force in Victorian society. The Penny Magazine was joined by other weekly journals, often called miscellanies, that included illustrations of examples of the decorative arts. While a paintings narrative might provide edifying models of behavior, the useful arts also could convey, through form and decoration, an appropriate balance between utility and beauty that elevated ordinary goods into artistic ones. In addition to Knights civilizing content, miscellanies also featured illustrations accompanying popular serialized and sensationalized fiction dealing with crime, intrigue, sex, and the occult. Thus illustrations and text that encouraged self-improvement, restraint, temperance, and social harmony coexisted with the broader commercial appeal of aberrant behavior, escapism, and political radicalism  a rich and diverse combination of conformity and non-conformity better known as popular culture. Aside from lithography, printers developed other methods such as wood engraving to satisfy a growing demand for illustrations for reproduction in books, magazines, advertisements, and newspapers during the first half of the nineteenth century. Owing to the efforts of printer Thomas Bewick of Newcastle-upon-Tyne in northern England (17531828), the technique of producing woodblocks by cutting with engraving tools against rather than with the wood grain resulted in greater precision and a more subtle tonal range in the rendering of texture and three-dimensional form. Prior to the invention of the half-tone photomechanical process, wood engravers worked directly from blocks traced from photographs and drawings. Fine detail was also achieved beginning in the 1820s using a process that created steel-engraved plates, replacing previously used copper plates. The surface of steel plates was more durable than copper  greater numbers of prints could be run on a steel plate with minimal loss of quality. Steel plates also offered more precision when printing large quantities of thin lines to suggest minute gradations of tone in relief. The method, developed in the United States by Jacob Perkins (17661849), was particularly effective for secure forms of printing such as postage stamps and paper currency, where complexity increased the difficulty of counterfeiting. English printers such as Charles Whittingham, Joseph Cundall, and Henry Shaw were knowledgeable in all phases of print technology, and combined a respect for craft with the benefits of new techniques of mechanical reproduction. Whittingham collaborated with publisher Thomas Pickering at the Chiswick Press in that London suburb to publish a number of titles, and was respected for maintaining the highest standards of typography, illustration, layout, and binding (though generally on cardboard rather than more expensive leather). Whittingham and other book printers rejected modern typefaces with their stronger contrast of thick and thin strokes (more common in the nineteenth century for larger display types; see fig. 3.11) and revived more traditional faces derived from William Caslon. Many printers took an avid interest in the expanding market for childrens illustrated books, printed from wood engravings in both black-andwhite as well as color. The firm of Robson, Levey & Franklyn published James Burns Nursery Rhymes, Tales and Jingles in 1844, using Caslon types, rustic frames sometimes hung with vines, and wood-engraved illustrations (fig. 4.12). The asymmetrical yet balanced relationship between framed text and illustration provided both consistency and variety from poem to poem, suggesting a desire to create designs with a clear hierarchy of information that could be easily grasped by young readers. Other contemporary publishers were more lavish in the use and variety of decoration and typefaces. This is most evident in the title pages of contemporary fiction that often appeared in serialized form. George Routledge (18121888) of London published a series of novels known as the Railway Library, presumably meant to be read while traveling and a forerunner of portable paperback fiction (and todays smart phones, tablets, Kindles, and other digital readers) that, along with the days headlines, continues to occupy commuters on suburban trains and airlines. The cost of most illustrated books was generally beyond the means of working people. But the appeal of stories told with pictures, before the widespread emergence of photomechanical reproduction and the advent of film (and later of television) was strong, satisfied through less expensive illustrated broadsides and weekly journals such as The Penny Magazine. ![](images/b73682005ad2478c27bf57edb4e4cc15829cc62d5f188f6a73049b658350073f.jpg) 4.12 James Burns, drawings printed from woodblocks, 6 × 8 in (15.2 × 20.8 cm), Nursery Rhymes, Tales and Jingles, 1844. Images in popular weekly journals and broadsheets were printed in black-and-white. But concurrently with these strictly tonal processes, a significant development in color printing, known as chromolithography, was also emerging. Patented in the 1830s by Godefroy Engelmann (17881839) in France, the new technology quickly spread to Britain, Germany, and the United States. Chromolithography produced images in color using a series of stones with flat, polished surfaces, each inked with a different color and printed in succession using exact registration of the paper. The process, as perfected in England by William Savage (17701843), was used initially for the accurate color reproduction of paintings for expensive, high-quality prints and illustrated books beginning in the 1840s. A number of early books with illustrations produced by chromolithography were facsimile editions of manuscripts such as the ninth-century Hiberno-Saxon Gospel book known as the Book of Kells. Others were private devotional books meant to rival their exquisite medieval forebears, such as H. Noel Humphreys chromolithographed plates for The Miracles of Our Lord, published in 1848 (fig. 4.13). In the late nineteenth century, the chromolithographic process was adopted for the color printing of popular greeting cards and souvenirs. Harper’s Weekly reported on contemporary domestic and international events and used wood engraving to reproduce drawings by a number of artists who served as reporters, recording events based upon first-hand (eyewitness) experience. Artist Winslow Homer (18361910), for instance, supported himself early in his career by documenting battles of the American Civil War for Harper’s Weekly. Later his paintings and those of other fine artists were reproduced as wood engravings, often covering an entire page or even a double-page spread in the weekly magazine. Another illustrator for Harper’s was Thomas Nast (18401902), a gifted draftsman whose family had moved to New York from Germany in 1846. As a young man, Nast traveled at the magazines expense to Europe to cover a prizefight in Britain, as well as reporting on Giuseppe Garibaldi (18071882), who led military campaigns in the movement to create the nation of modern Italy in 18601861; Nast was also sent to report first-hand on events during the American Civil War. Both during and after the Civil War, Nast increasingly sought to shape public opinion through illustrations aimed at a broad readership. In addition to documenting actual events, he illustrated generalized themes relating to the Civil War that had tremendous popular appeal. While he certainly had precursors who used caricature as a tool of political change (George IV, patron of the Royal Pavilion at Brighton, was a frequent target of satire; see here), and may have borrowed from them, the combination of clearly recognizable images of contemporary political figures and an inexpensive vehicle for distribution gave new power to the role of images in mobilizing public opinion. Public officials feared Nasts pen and were well aware of his ability to influence opinion against their untrammeled greed and corruption. The most celebrated of his campaigns, between 1869 and 1871, was directed against William Boss Tweed, a New York State senator and Sachem (leader) of New York Citys Democratic Party with its headquarters in Tammany Hall. With both Harper’s Weekly and the New York Times supporting the reform of city government, Tweed and his so-called Ring fared poorly in the November 1871 elections, and were later indicted on charges of fraud, bribery, and corruption; in time the reign of the Ring came to an end. Their efforts were in part political, in that the reformers attempted to claim as universal a rather narrow set of criteria and felt a responsibility to educate, and hence convince manufacturers and a growing consumer public to accept their views. The effort was epistemological as well, grounded in a belief that laws underlie all spheres of human endeavor, from mathematics and the physical sciences to architecture, extending as well to the application of ornament to a wide variety of furniture and household goods. Such views ignored the reality of free trade, technological change, innovation, and a free enterprise economy, in which the harmony of utility and beauty was only one among many motivations for consumers to purchase clothing and manufactured commodities. Indeed, the tastes of new classes of consumers might be dictated by a different set of criteria, in particular their relation to individual identity and social aspirations. The reform agenda also did not sufficiently recognize the sheer excitement and flow of imagination stemming from experiments with new processes and materials, and the promise they held for personal expression in the form of material possessions. It has been argued that those who lamented the spectacle of eclecticism, decorative variety, and ostentation among the middle class were seeking to perpetuate not only traditional tastes but also a conservative social system based upon the well-meaning but exclusive preferences of a small, dominant elite. While design reform and the harmony between utility and beauty formed a cornerstone of the narrative of a canonical modern movement in design as described by art historians such as Nikolaus Pevsner, a more recent appreciation for eclecticism, popular culture, and the broader cultural values and commercial underpinnings of retail shopping and global markets as revealed in Worlds Fairs, suggests that recognizing and embracing competing points of view is necessary for a cultural appreciation of design history and of modern society in the nineteenth century and beyond. And while issues of taste are essential to such a design historical narrative, reforming taste played less of a role under certain conditions, for instance in the design of industrial equipment such as the first Singer sewing machines of the early 1850s, or the Model T Ford in the early twentieth century (see here). The search for standards or common ground seems in many ways a natural response during any period of rapid change and the consequent erosion of traditional values and authority amid rapid industrial and commercial expansion, and this certainly applies to the history of modern design. Whether they are standards for decoration, utility, performance, or environmental or social responsibility, the desire for common ground continues as part of the political, social, aesthetic, and technical dimensions of design. ## Conclusion So if one seeks to understand it [society], one must realize that it embodies at one and the same time everything that it has been, is, and will be in future; it is an accumulation over the long term of permanent features and successive inflections. So wrote French historian Fernand Braudel (19021985) in his three-volume study that examines the social and economic order in Europe roughly from the fifteenth to the eighteenth centuries. Braudels words and his reference to permanent features of society sound a note of continuity that might be applied as well to the history of modern design: both what it had been and much of what it was to become are embedded in the complex interplay between changes in the technology of production and the social dynamics of consumption in the eighteenth and early to mid-nineteenth centuries. Beyond Braudel, we might add that a history of modern design should consider a world view beyond the Eurocentric formulation found in the Albert Memorial, one that allows as much as possible an appreciation of the interconnectedness of the design enterprise and that permits diverse cultures to speak for themselves rather than through a Western lens. Perhaps a central issue to emerge during the first half of the nineteenth century in the industrializing West is the apparent conflict between technology, commercialization, and democratization on the one hand, all of which signify change and a state of flux, and the search for standards on the other, the desire to regulate change through the application of principles that might stand beyond the whims of fashion, the quest for profits, and the speed of innovation in industry. It is in this context that early design reform may be analyzed. In the nineteenth century, exchange between the Arab-Islamic world and Western Europe expanded greatly, with Western producers and merchants negotiating trade agreements with their Arab-Islamic counterparts for raw materials, minerals, and the import and export of manufactured products. Turkey, with Istanbul the capital of a weakened Ottoman Empire, as well as the semi-autonomous states of Tunisia, Persia, and Egypt, invested in modernizing reforms (known as Tanzimat) that included education, infrastructure, industrial technology, aspects of dress, and urbanization that created closer (and often dependent) political and economic relations with the West, while at the same time preserving distinctive features of their Arab-Islamic heritage, including religion, traditional aspects of dress, diet, and the vestiges of a nomadic heritage. However, it must be remembered that in the nineteenth century, the level and type of interest was different because European countries colonized many of the majority-Muslim countries in the Mediterranean basin, plus India, and what is now Pakistan, Myanmar, and Bangladesh, as well as large parts of sub-Saharan Africa. They controlled these regions militarily, politically, and commercially, so had a very different kind of interest in them than they had ever had before. First, the French painters (for example, Delacroix and Gérome) who visited Tunisia, Algeria, Egypt, and Morocco returned home and made paintings in especially intense colors. Second, the European (particularly the British) fascination with Islamic pattern design began. What caught the eye of design reformers in England, especially, was the conventionalized, often aniconistic patterns from these regions of the world. They fitted with the theories nineteenth-century Europeans were promoting about the superiority of conventionalized medieval flat patterns to hyperrealistic modern ones, and their praise of imperfect skilled handicraft over alienating machine manufacture. The combination of praise and criticism, envy and condescension, is characteristic of European attitudes toward cultural difference in the middle and later nineteenth century. Though Europeans greatly admired the works made by people from these regions, they at the same time scorned the people who made the work as backward and barbaric, in need of civilizing and modernizing. Even as Europeans said they were trying to civilize and modernize (i.e., assimilate) the people of this region, they were simultaneously co-opting their traditional aesthetics into their own (European) design playbook (quite literally, in the case of Owen Joness Grammar of Ornament). Nineteenthcentury design reformers, especially, thought adopting aniconistic Islamic ornamental design/flat patterns would improve or reinvigorate Western flat pattern design that had become over-civilized, enfeebled, and degenerate. Womens clothes became brighter in the late 1800s after the invention of mauve and aniline dyes, and European patterns got more abstract, at least at the high end of the market where the reformers had some sway, culminating in the later Japanese-inspired patterns of the Arts and Crafts movement. Many scholars note the unequal power relationship between Western European nations and West Asia and North Africa, pointing to trade agreements favorable to the West, along with increasing imperial expansion and colonization, beginning with the French occupation of Algeria in the 1830s, followed by Tunisia becoming a French protectorate in the early 1880s, and British control of Egypt. Increasingly this controlling viewpoint or gaze found expression at Worlds Fairs, with observers praising the products of West Asia and North Africa as precious but also technologically backward, indicative of a less highly developed stage of civilization than the more rational and disciplined Western nations. Displays tended to support a Western bias that combined a romantic attraction to an authentic, pre-industrial culture with a sense of technological and moral superiority. Countries such as Tunisia sought to project an identity that communicated compatibility between Arab-Islamic tradition and modernizing reforms based upon European models for education, urban infrastructure (with rulers often hiring Western architects to design buildings and residences), and dress (the short conical felt fez hat was part of the Tanzimat reforms mentioned above, here), but the reception of such efforts by European visitors was not always positive. Although on the one hand admired as models of skill and respect for universal principles of decoration, on the other hand, the work of these craftsmen was also prone to demeaning cultural stereotypes, the result of a controlling, Eurocentric gaze that reduced cultural difference to a set of hierarchical ethnic tropes that included a pre-modern nomadic way of life, duplicity in commercial practice, a penchant for violence, tyranny, the subordination of women, and an unbridled appetite for sex. Such attitudes became more prevalent as the Worlds Fairs began to include separate national pavilions in the later nineteenth century (rather than courts within a single, large structure), seen, for instance, in the Tunisian pavilion at the 1876 Centennial Exhibition in Philadelphia, illustrated in a weekly periodical that featured a staged Turkish Café with slouching musicians and an exotic dancer. At the 1867 Exposition Universelle in Paris, a life-size diorama depicted in gruesome detail a pair of lions attacking a Bedouin riding a camel, another example of Orientalism. The diorama was purchased by the American Museum of Natural History in New York, displayed again in Philadelphia in 1876 for the Centennial Worlds Fair, and now resides in a large glass case in the entry foyer to the Carnegie Museum in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania.
