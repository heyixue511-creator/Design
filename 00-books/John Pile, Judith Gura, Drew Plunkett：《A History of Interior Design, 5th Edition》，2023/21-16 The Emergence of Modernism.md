Although the little basement room of this bar has no applied decoration, it is intense with a mix of marble, onyx, mahogany, brass, frosted glass, and mirrors, all on a green and white tiled floor. Art Nouveau was an attempt to find an architectural language that did not revive past styles. It was extremely decorative, briefly fashionable, and faded away within twenty years. In the United States, and Chicago in particular, commercial forces were driving technological innovation and a confident national identity encouraged the idea of a unique American architectural style. When Louis Sullivan created ornament, he did not draw on classical precedents but on nature—and American nature, not European nature. Frank Lloyd Wright would also look for his inspiration to the American landscape in the shape of the prairies of the Midwest. ## SULLIVAN Louis H. Sullivan (1856–1924) has been described as the first modern architect but his persistent commitment to decoration separates him from twentieth-century European modernists for whom the elimination of ornament became an article of faith. He was born in Boston where he worked briefly with Frank Furness before moving to Chicago in 1873, when the city was in the midst of a building boom following the Great Fire of 1871. There, he worked with William Le Baron Jenney (1832–1907), who had built the city’s first steel-framed building, but Jenney’s practice may have been too prosaic and he decamped to Paris. After a year at the Ecole des Beaux-Arts he returned to Chicago and, in the office of Joseph S. Johnston & John Edelman, designed stencils for a decorative frieze in the Chicago Avenue Church (now Moody Church, c. 1875) that established his reputation as a designer of decorative motifs. In 1879 German-trained Dankmar Adler (1844–1900) hired him and, a year later, took him into partnership. Adler dealt with technical matters and finding commissions, Sullivan led on design. His first significant work was the Chicago Auditorium Building (1886–90; 16.2), an opera house hidden in the center of a hotel and office complex. His principal contribution was the interiors of lobbies, public areas in the hotel, and the auditorium, which demonstrated his ability as both a spatial tactician and designer of ornament. The ceiling of the auditorium was a series of great arches concealing electric partitions that could be lowered to reduce the size of the 4,200- seat auditorium. The hotel dining room was at roof level overlooking Lake Michigan, under an arched ceiling with skylights. Its walls and ceilings were edged with Sullivan’s decorative friezes. ![](images/89dedf489ba6ee3ff2a130ac705cca4e18a6219410962b9fc7b5f012cd93508c.jpg) 16.2 Louis Sullivan, Auditorium Building, Chicago, 1886–90. With Dankmar Adler, Sullivan designed the building that combined offices, a hotel, and the opera house that gave the building its name. Sullivan designed many of the interior spaces, of which the auditorium is the most spectacular. It was unlike any older theater or concert hall in its decoration: the ceiling arches with their painted detail, the proscenium design, and the organ grills brought light, color, and texture. He became a virtuoso in cladding the raw steel structures of the new commercial buildings, such as the Bayard-Condict Building in New York (16.3 and 16.4) or the Carson Pirie department store in Chicago (16.5) in strips and panels of terracotta moldings, all inspired by natural forms. Thus it was significant when in 1892, in his essay “Ornament in Architecture” for The Engineering Magazine,” he suggested a moratorium on the use of ornament until an appropriate decorative language could be evolved. He said, “It would be greatly for our aesthetic good if we were to refrain entirely from the use of ornament.” It was a rhetorical statement, a plea not for the abandonment of ornament but for reflection on an appropriate form for the new urban contexts. He offered another homily in 1896 when he said that “form ever follows function and this is the [natural] law,” giving modernists a mantra, although for them function referred to practical efficiency and ignored that one of a building’s traditional functions was to give pleasure and denied the human instinct to decorate that had been prodigiously manifested in Victorian homes. ![](images/abd46386c1501680d2df95218d6c7e3173e43fe333c80220c449bd4288e9d116.jpg) The building is on 65 Bleecker Street between Broadway and Lafayette Street. Its steel frame is overlaid with small scale moldings that give the façade a soft texture at a distance. Detail of the intricate molding above the main entrance bears no resemblance to any historical precedent. It is modern, but satisfies the popular enthusiasm for ornament and becomes visually richer as the viewer comes closer. In 1914 Sullivan’s Merchants’ National Bank, in Grinnell Iowa, was in essence a plain brick cuboid with a large, modestly decorated side window and cornice but his greatest offence to modernist eyes was his full-blooded embellishment of the monumental entrance door. There is no precedent for the decorative language he used but the pure geometry of the major elements and the leaves and twigs of secondary elements offered a comprehensible decorative language, free from familiar Neoclassicism and Neo-Gothic heritages (16.6). Within only a few years, ornament in any form would be declared a crime against modernity. ![](images/084134ffc18730df7fd4f98be0b0a28ef4f5e2ab26d70ca21c75c8daa46fba40.jpg) Louis Sullivan, Merchants’ National Bank, Grinnell, Iowa, 1914. A modest brick cuboid building with strategically placed decorative detail. The isolation of decorative elements in an otherwise blank façade is a break from tradition but the over-scaling of the entrance is traditional. Modern buildings seldom signal their entry point so dramatically. ## WRIGHT Early in his career, Frank Lloyd Wright (1867–1959) was employed in the offices of Adler and Sullivan, from 1887 to 1893, and he worked closely with Sullivan, who recognized the value of his contributions. Wright later acknowledged Sullivan as a significant influence: the pronouncement that form should follow function freed Wright from the conventions of Beaux-Arts eclecticism and Sullivan’s nature-inspired ornament encouraged his inclination toward “organic” forms. They had a fractious relationship however. Wright wanted creative independence, and broke his contract by working for his own clients outside office hours and later resigned. Once independent, Wright was prolific, completing more than four hundred projects over an eighty-year career that can be divided into two phases. The first phase extended until c. 1920. The second phase began after 1930. He set up practice in 1893 in the Chicagoan suburb of Oak Park, designing his own house (1889) and an adjacent studio. He received commissions from successful businessmen who, with good private and public transport, could now live in desirable suburbs. The houses Wright designed for them were categorized as Prairie houses by critics and academics, who presumed that the long, low buildings were influenced by the flat plains of the Midwest. They were also influenced by the British Arts and Crafts movement but Wright’s intellectual insularity and response to the landscape in which he built produced dwellings in what has been described as the first uniquely American architectural style. The Winslow House (1893; 16.7) in River Forest, Illinois has a symmetrical elevation but a strong horizontal emphasis, particularly in a low-pitched hipped roof that extends significantly beyond external walls and the horizontal bands of different external finishes. Internally, rooms were clustered around a central fireplace and this became Wright’s standard planning strategy. The entrance hall has an arcaded alcove with seats on either side of a fireplace. The dining room on the other side of the chimney extends into a semi-circular conservatory. Ornamental detail, which includes stained-glass inserts in some windows, suggests Sullivan’s influence but shows signs of moving toward Wright’s own more abstract geometric motifs. ![](images/4aed79295bf7cbce193487a81b67336cc29b93c9cf8c4705d4953909d56f00f2.jpg) Frank Lloyd Wright, Winslow House, River Forest, Illinois, 1893. In his early work, Wright often used details that hint at architectural tradition and the influence of his association with Louis Sullivan. Off the entrance hall, this small loggia holds a fireplace flanked by built-in seating. The Hickox House (1900) in Kankakee, Illinois retains symmetry only in its interconnecting living, dining, and music rooms. It has the overhanging gabled roof, long horizontal bands of windows, and low walls extending into the garden that characterize Prairie houses. The street façade is entirely asymmetrical. Its white plastered wall surfaces divided by strips of wood have a Japanese flavor, which may reflect Wright’s interest in Japanese prints, which he shared with the Aesthetic movement in Britain. In 1907 Wright designed a house surrounded by gardens, a pool, and service buildings for Avery Coonley in Riverside, Illinois, another Chicago suburb (16.8). The plan is developed on a modular square grid, a device Wright often used to unify elements. Squares are the unifying theme for decorative tiling and plaster patterns on exterior walls, and for interior details such as patterned woven rugs and stained-glass window inserts. A drawing of the interior, which Wright made for the Ladies’ Home Journal in 1901, shows interconnecting living areas, extensive built-in joinery, and bespoke furniture. Ceilings reflect the profile of the roofs, wooden strips suggest the patterns of structural members, and decorative ceiling panels conceal light sources. As in most of Wright’s interiors, warmth and color were provided by natural materials. In 1954, in his book The Natural House, he wrote, “A plain flat surface cut to shape for its own sake, however large or plain the shape, is, the moment it is sophisticatedly so cut, no less ornament than egg and dart.” He was in agreement with those who would see in the natural colors and textures of building materials all the decoration they needed. He differed in that he was prepared to call it decoration. ![](images/0fca801e63598bc56f7709f6bd1d33dea12e40d0e5f15f7ba7419b1a3032fc00.jpg) 16.8 Frank Lloyd Wright, Coonley House, Riverside, Illinois, 1907. By the time this house was designed, Wright had established his own modern style as is evident in this drawing, reproduced in magazines and books in the Netherlands and Germany. The ceiling pattern reflects the roof structure but is strongly decorative. Geometry underpins the design of stained glass and the bespoke rug. The furniture is designed by Wright. Wright’s greatest Prairie house is considered to be the Robie House for Frederick C. Robie in south Chicago (1908–10). It has long, cantilevered roof overhangs with long, low horizontal windows, and long brick walls that stretch into the garden. On plan, two rectangles “slide” past one another. The southwest block holds the principal living areas above a playroom and a billiard room. The entrance hall is at the top of a central stair and leads to a living room to the west and a dining room to the east (16.9). The rooms flow together, separated only by a central fireplace and the open stairway behind it. On the south side, a range of twelve doors with stained-glass panels open to a balcony that runs the length of the building. At the west end, a triangular, glazed bay window is flanked by a pair of glazed doors with stained-glass inserts. Decorative wooden bands across ceiling surfaces and lighting fixtures unify the interior. Wright designed furniture, rugs, and textiles. His high-backed dining chairs were intended to give a sense of enclosure for diners sitting around the table in the open space of the dining area. He only supervised the early stages of construction for the Robie House because he closed his studio in 1909 amid a scandal over his affair with Mamah Cheney, the wife of a client for whom he had designed a Prairie house in 1903. ![](images/0aceaf4cad9b034f4b8f1a630afd2e1b08e2f75c186a6deb4823376a7e3a51a2.jpg) 16.9 Frank Lloyd Wright, dining area, Robie House, Chicago, 1908–10. The high-backed chairs and the lamp structures that rise above tabletop level to give diners a sense of intimacy were designed by Wright, although it is likely that he collaborated with George Mann Niedecken on the design. The built-in cabinet work, the stained-glass windows, and ceiling detail are typical of Wright’s work at the time. Wright’s growing reputation brought him a number of non-residential commissions, including a six-story office building for the Larkin Company in Buffalo, New York (1904; 16.10). The company sold soap by mail order and Wright designed its offices around a central atrium, 76- feet (23-m) high, crowned with inscriptions in praise of honest labor. The well-lit central space protected workers from the pollution of surrounding railroad yards. At the base of the five-story atrium there were desks set at right angles to the long axis, and each desk could accommodate six members of staff. Parapets of cream brick on each of the upper floors were set back by one brick width—9 inches (23 cm)—from the face of the structural brick piers that thus rose uninterrupted for the full height of the atrium until crowned by discreet Wrightian molded detail. Wright designed every element of the interior, including clusters of lights on the ends of each desk in the atrium, and introduced various technical innovations: glass doors, air conditioning, radiant heating, built-in desks for storage, and toilet bowls cantilevered to aid cleaning. Wright described it as “a genuine expression of power directly applied to purpose, in the same sense that the ocean liner, the plane or the car is so.” European modernists would also consistently express their admiration for those examples of technological modernity. The Larkin building demonstrated how to strip away decorative excess while still creating a place that inspired awe and wonder but, at its unveiling, critics regretted the lack of Beaux-Arts embellishments. In 1905 Wright designed Oak Park Unity Temple, a Unitarian Universalist church. The site 16.11 bordered a busy road, so he located the church entrance on the opposite side and turned once more to the introspective Larkin formula of blank exterior walls and a glazed ceiling. It was his first use of reinforced concrete into which he sparingly cast decorative motifs internally (16.12). Projecting balconies, a ceiling with a grid of square skylights, bands of wood on white walls, pendant light fittings, and geometrically patterned, stained-glass windows created an abstractly complex space suggestive of directions that were to surface in Europe a few years later (16.11). The minister, Rodney Johonnot, had asked for a building embodying “unity, truth, beauty, simplicity, freedom and reason,” words that might have been borrowed by fledgling European modernists for their cause. Wright pronounced that Unity Temple represented the first expression of an entirely new architecture. ![](images/462cc42b0ba36e79d33e353f74cde8bc127665deb0c3aa685c62645144b9ec57.jpg) Frank Lloyd Wright, interior, Oak Park Unity Temple, Chicago, 1905. The decoration cast into Unity Temple’s reinforced-concrete walls takes no obvious inspiration from classical or Gothic precedents but has some resemblance to the abstract geometric patterns in the stained glass of Wright’s Prairie houses. In 1909 Wright left his wife and children to go to Europe, ostensibly to oversee the publication of one hundred lithographs of his work by the German publisher Ernst Wasmuth Verlag but also to rendezvous discreetly with Mamah Cheney. The book was to have a significant influence on the evolution of European modernism. Wright’s most valued assistant and a renowned architectural illustrator, Marion Mahony (1871–1961), made more than half the drawings but, when Wright left for Europe, Mahony declined to take over the practice and he offered it to Hermann V. von Holst (1874–1955), a prominent Chicagoan architect, stipulating that Mahony should have control over the design of ongoing projects and George Mann Niedecken (1878–1945), who had worked on the interiors of three Prairie houses, should deal with interiors. Niedecken was a successful designer of furniture, stained glass, lighting, tapestries, and murals in his own right but chairs for the Gilmore House of 1908 were collaborations with Wright and his table lamp for the Edward P. Irving house, which he designed and which belongs to the Prairie school, treats wooden elements and stained glass in a Wrightian fashion (16.13 and 16.14). In 1909 the Wasmuth portfolio of his Prairie house drawings galvanized Europe’s architectural radicals. It is easy to see how the horizontal emphasis in the houses offered a subversive alternative to the verticality of classical and Gothic traditions. Wright was pleased to accept his role in influencing European modernists but refused to acknowledge that he had learned anything from them and the buildings he designed later in his career show nothing to suggest he did. ## CHANGE IN EUROPE In Britain, Queen Victoria died in 1901, after ruling for sixty-three years. She was succeeded by her sixty-year-old son Edward VII and, like her, he gave his name to an era, but one remembered, not for rectitude, but for moral laxity and conspicuous consumption. The British Empire continued to generate enormous wealth but the country was losing its place as the dominant world power to the United States, which, 125 years after breaking loose from that empire, was colonizing its own vast, western hinterlands and absorbing the immigrants who were deserting Europe for the opportunities it offered. Americans, old and new, seemed to have an entrepreneurial gene that allowed them to understand the potential of European innovations and find a way to make them practical and profitable. In England, Sir Humphry Davy (1778–1829) developed the first practical electric arc light in 1806 but it was Thomas Edison (1847–1931), from Ohio, who created the incandescent filament light bulb in 1879 that eliminated the gloom of the Victorian interior and changed how it would be used and decorated. At the end of the 1870s, the unstoppable Edison took out patents on the recording machines and gramophones that replaced the piano as the source of domestic entertainment, and the telephone that changed the practices of business and social intercourse. In France, the Lumière brothers, Auguste (1862–1954) and Louis (1864–1948), along with George Méliès (1861–1938) pioneered cinema but it was Edison who, in 1891, filed patents for the filmmaking equipment that allowed escapees from Eastern European ghettoes to play major roles in the transformation of popular mass entertainment and replace vaudeville with cinema. In Germany, Karl Benz (1844–1929) developed the first operational automobile but Henry Ford (1863–1947), from Michigan, pioneered the production-line techniques that democratized automobile ownership and established the template for a second industrial revolution. The Wright brothers, Orville (1871–1948) and Wilber (1867–1912), made their first short motorpowered air flight in 1903. The airplane, the automobile, and the possibilities they suggested are cited as inspiration for the seismic artistic upheavals. The Futurists, a group of Italian poets and painters, declared in their manifesto of 1909 an intention to destroy everything old. The world was awash with “modernity,” even warfare in World War I would be described as “modern.” The arts appropriated the word. In 1930, the English novelist E. F. Benson (1867–1940) reflecting on change at the beginning of the twentieth century, wrote of “such manifestations of scientific marvels as no other short period can point to, motorcars and moving pictures, telephones and electric lighting, X-rays and other ultra-spectrum potencies.” He was not alone. In the first decades of the century, it seemed to a few radicals that industrialization and new technologies had delivered change that deserved its own architectural style. New and old building types had been shaped and reshaped by necessity. In 1899 the French engineer Auguste Choisy (1841–1909) published L’Histoire de l’Architecture (The History of Architecture), in which he argued that significant changes in architectural styles had been the result of engineering innovation, citing classicism’s post-andbeam construction, and Gothic’s flying buttresses. Nineteenth-century engineers like him had created the iron and glass roofs of railroad termini and raised the skyscrapers. Practical changes were accepted for the practical benefits they brought, but the architectural, and popular, consensus was that they should be clad in familiar decorative skins. Nineteenthcentury innovation was challenging the capacity of accepted architectural styles to deal with new building forms and the architects, whose job it was to create façades for cast-iron structures, appeared to be intimidated by the task and looked backward to find ways, however incongruous, to dress the new. Even the nineteenth-century radicals who had disapproved of Gothic Revival’s excesses sought their solution in another revival, the neo-vernaculars of Arts and Crafts. Fin-desiècle aesthetes had struck blows, and sardonic poses, against Victorian orthodoxies but the rebels of the new century chose to be serious, to destroy not just revivalisms but the aesthetic instincts to decorate that had prevailed since caves were painted. ## The Radicals ## Duchamp In 1917 the French painter, sculptor, and chess player Marcel Duchamp (1877–1968), aka R. Mutt, displayed a urinal in an art gallery and introduced the idea of the readymade object as sculpture— a precedent and validation for interior designers who would learn to complement new and extant elements when empty utilitarian buildings became prized for their intrinsic qualities. This became important with the advent of postmodernism in the 1970s when the found object and the recycled material provided a way to evoke ersatz character within bland building shells. ## Loos Adolf Franz Karl Viktor Maria Loos (1870–1933) had strong opinions on decoration. He was an Austrian and Czechoslovak architect and designer, a theorist, and a journalist. He enjoyed being provocative and, among other things, declared his admiration for English tailoring and American plumbing. His entry for a competition to design offices for the Chicago Tribune newspaper in 1922 confirms his mischievous inclination. He proposed a tower in the form of a Doric column that rose twenty-four stories from twelve stories of a ziggurat-topped cube (16.15). It looked absurd but little more than the winning Gothic Revival entry by John Mead Howells (1868–1959) and Raymond Hood (1881–1934)—a tower that fused the verticality of American commercial architecture with Gothic flourishes, under a buttressed crown inspired by that on the thirteenthcentury cathedral in the French city of Rouen. That crown would have a significant influence on the topping out of the American skyscraper over the next two decades. Adolf Loos, competition entry for design of the Chicago Tribune Tower, 1925. ![](images/d97641e71519058be6090cbd15fb6532d9084573711be4e5ea1e9e2dc126a7b9.jpg) Loos’s design for a tower is supposedly a visual pun on the idea of a newspaper column. Loos visited the United States between 1893 and 1896 and would have been aware of Sullivan’s suggestion that ornamenting buildings should be temporarily suspended. In his essay “Architecture” of 1910 he suggested that “only a very small part of architecture belongs to the realm of art: the tomb and the monument.” Everything else, from factories to railroad stations to shops and restaurants he pronounced to be mere buildings and conceded that they could pander to the untutored taste for decoration. In 1910, in a lecture in Vienna, he suggested that civilized people had no need for ornamental superficialities and explained, perhaps facetiously, that tattoos were the self-inflicted, self-loathing ornament of the criminal underclass. In 1913 the text of the lecture was published, as Ornement et Crime (Ornament and Crime) in the French magazine Les Cahiers d’Aujourd’hui. To anglophones “ornement et crime” sounds like “ornement est crime” (ornament is crime) as if ornament was being criminalized but, in 1913, few agreed with Loos. The black granite façade of the Knize store is bulky and polished but without decorative detail. When constructed it stood out on a street of banks and luxury stores with conventional wooden framed windows. Like Hoffmann and Wright, Loos proposed that the inherent beauty of favored building materials was superior to applied ornament. In 1908 he tested the theory in the dark basement of the American Bar in Kärntner Street in Vienna—it has also become known as Kärntner Bar (16.1). Materials included marble, onyx, mahogany, and brass above a green and white checkerboard floor. Light was filtered through frosted-glass tabletops and the kaleidoscopic mélange was bounced back and forth to infinity between mirrored walls, one above the bar and one on the opposite wall. Had Loos applied ornament it is doubtful if it would be noticed in the visual cacophony. He treated his residential interiors similarly, using veneers of wood grains and marble veins. His other contribution to the domestic interior was his raumplan, in which areas were subdivided by changes of level, and walls—when unavoidable—were cut away and pierced to give glimpses from one area to another. The strategy was most effectively demonstrated in the Villa Müller (1930) and was, perhaps, another idea he had picked up in the United States where he encountered the areas of Wright’s Prairie houses separated only by fireplaces and stairwells (16.18). ![](images/dcc96f9e85c11a36b4c836ceb860c545a915e736e83fc5e2acf5c02768f8a4c1.jpg) 16.18 Adolf Loos, Villa Müller, Prague, 1930. An example of Loos’s raumplan, in which areas are decided by changes in level and walls are opened up to give views between rooms. Having dispensed with applied decoration, he relied on the grain of marble to decorate his surfaces. Stripped of decoration, Loos’s houses were raw, with flat wall surfaces pierced by windows that were free of any decorative moldings. The exception was an unsolicited and unbuilt project of 1928 for the celebrated African American entertainer Josephine Baker, for whom he proposed façades of narrow horizontal black and white marble stripes, which are presumed to have symbolized her integration into white society. ## WAR AND REVOLUTION World War I (1914–8) and the Russian Revolution (1917–23) seemed to energize architectural revolutionaries. They poured out manifestoes and demanded the rejection of social and cultural norms on the presumption that traditional architectural languages had legitimized and celebrated the social and political structures, precipitating World War I. Adopting the cause of detoxifying architectural culture, they proposed to eliminate its familiar forms and motifs. In doing so, they ignored the enshrined principles and decorative devices that made buildings aesthetically enjoyable and sometimes uplifting. An evolutionary rather than a revolutionary process might have produced a popular modernity but avant-gardists were disinclined to compromise and keen to outrage those beyond their circle. The removal, at the end of the war, of incumbent political and cultural dinosaurs left the way clear for political and cultural totalitarians. It was a time of belligerent, hyperbolic sloganeering. In their manifesto of 1909 Italian Futurists had rhapsodized about the “red sword of the sun slashing for the first time through the shadows of a thousand years.” In 1918 the Dutch radicals of the De Stijl movement called on “all those who believe in the reform of art and culture to annihilate these obstacles of development that prevent further development.” Le Corbusier began his 1923 polemic Vers Une Architecture (Toward an Architecture) with “A great epoch has begun. There exists a new spirit.” (The title of the English-language translation was “Toward a New Architecture.” The original title suggested something portentous and systemic, the translation suggested a change of style.) Most of the venom was directed against decoration, the essential component of interior design, which was abruptly deemed synonymous with degeneracy and reaction. ## Le Corbusier World War I granted Futurists their wish for powerful machines and mass destruction. Charles-Edouard Jeanneret, known as Le Corbusier (1887–1965), was a Swiss architect working in France and retreated to his neutral homeland at the outbreak of war in 1914. There, excited by airplanes, automobiles, ocean liners, and grain silos, he formulated the theories that became modernist dogma. In 1915, in collaboration with the engineer Max Dubois, he revealed his design for Domino House, which was essentially a structural diagram showing reinforced-concrete floor slabs supported on reinforced-concrete columns that eliminated the need for load-bearing walls. With that structural system there was no reason why floor areas could not be compartmentalized and any elevational treatment, including historical pastiches, could not be applied to the exterior. However, the Domino House diagram suggested empty spaces and transparent elevations (16.19). ![](images/d8685192b4df8cf15764d647441cc51dd5cdc401102e691ba1909021683de6cd.jpg) Le Corbusier in collaboration with Max Dubois, Domino House, 1915. A polemical drawing illustrating how reinforced-concrete columns, which eliminated the need for load-bearing externa walls, could support reinforced-concrete floors eliminating the need for structural internal walls. In 1917 Le Corbusier collaborated with the painter Amédée Ozenfant (1886–1966) to write Après le Cubisme (After Cubism), in which they accused Cubism of having become decorative. They advocated a form of Cubist abstract painting they called Purism, in which color was considered secondary to form—although it could be used to enhance it. An important Purist concept was the “objet-type” (object-type), the archetypal object. They cited examples such as the Bordeaux wine bottle and the violin as objects that had evolved to perform tasks efficiently and, consequently, seemed incapable of being improved. Le Corbusier would claim that such perfectly evolved objects were all that was needed to make a perfect interior. ## DE STIJL ## Van Doesburg In the Netherlands, Theo van Doesburg (1883–1931) founded De Stijl (The Style) magazine in 1917 and exhorted its readers to destroy everything that stood in the way of progress. Like-minded artistic iconoclasts came together to form the De Stijl movement, among them the painter Piet Mondrian (1871–1944) and the sculptor Jean (Hans) Arp (1887–1966), who were both advocates of pure abstraction. They eliminated the figurative forms in their work and developed a theory called Neoplasticism, in a number of manifestoes. It asserted the superiority of geometric form and color (black and the primaries) over all naturalistic and subjective values in art. Their insistence on the importance of straight lines, right angles, and a limited color range relieved them of the obligation to consider more subtle solutions. The greatest De Stijl interior was van Doesburg’s Café l’Aubette in Strasbourg, France (1926– 8; 16.20), an amalgam of bar, dancehall, and movie theater that sat behind an eighteenth-century Baroque façade but unwaveringly conformed to De Stijl’s principles. Van Doesburg “exploded” a De Stijl painting by translating it into three dimensions. Booths lined two sides of a dance floor. A white film screen hung in front of a wall of red and gray rectangles angled at 45 degrees. These were “folded” at the junction of wall and ceiling to generate horizontal triangles, squares, and rectangles across the ceiling. Walls and ceiling were visually deconstructed. There is little evidence of modernism’s sober hallmarks. ![](images/45355c4da416c7f07f9e3a02906a47cb3d0d12b212563cf03a4f2e9389d81187.jpg) ## 16.20 Theo van Doesburg, movie theater/dancehall, Café l’Aubette, Strasbourg, France, 1926–8. In this entertainment center, with bars, ballrooms and movie theater, van Doesburg used De Stijl abstract geometric forms to generate an unsettlingly modern interior. In the movie theater/dance hall seen here, films were projected onto the central screen while patrons occupied booths or danced on the central dance floor. Van Doesburg worked with Jean Arp and his wife, the Swiss artist and designer Sophie Taeuber-Arp (1889–1943), on designing the abstract elements, which the public disliked when it was opened. ## Rietveld The best-known De Stijl work was produced by Gerrit Rietveld (1888–1964), the son of a joiner who became his father’s apprentice at the age of eleven. He studied at night school and worked as a draftsman for a Utrecht jeweler, then in 1917 set up his own furniture workshop and designed his red–blue chair (16.21). Its flat, unupholstered planes so blatantly ignored the essentials of comfort that it was, and continues to be, revered by modernists. Rietveld kept the design simple because he hoped the chair would be mass produced. In 1918 he set up his own factory and, having become familiar with De Stijl principles, painted the back red and the seat blue, with black structural members sporting yellow ends. Continuous production has satisfied the demand, principally among architects and designers, to own this icon. It is said to be reasonably comfortable. ![](images/741eb2ddadc91c7523fa85c1bb4ab222c73f6565b985c83299a02bbd107a212e.jpg) ## 16.21 Gerrit Rietveld, red–blue chair, 1917. Two sheets of plywood, held in position by the black frame, serve as seat and back in this chair. Connections between the members are made by hidden metal pins so that the elements are visually independent and painted in three primary colors plus black. Rietveld’s major work was a small suburban house in Utrecht (16.22) for pharmacist Truus Schröder-Schräder (1889–1985). Until the death of her husband in 1923, she had lived in a large apartment but, intrigued by De Stijl principles, had collaborated with Rietveld to create her own stark room in the otherwise conventionally furnished interior. After she was widowed, she commissioned Rietveld to design a house for her and her three children. She knew what she wanted, collaborated with Rietveld, and has been credited as its joint designer. Theirs was a harmonious partnership, they became lovers and he lived in the house until his death. 16.22 ![](images/b01c4d003b052eb3ccd168c1284cd9cac34f05788b78eb7c9a87cb0ad677d768.jpg) Gerrit Rietveld with Truus Schröder-Schräder, Schröder House, Utrecht, The Netherlands, 1924. This colorful, fragmented object sits provocatively on the end of a conventional brick terrace. Perhaps only a furniture designer and a pharmacist could have created such a deviation from architectural tradition. The house sits, a colorful abstraction, on the end of a conventional nineteenth-century brick row but embodies the De Stijl manifesto and clearly spurns the past. It is scrupulously orthogonal on plan and section but projecting elements never quite align, suggesting a building in a state of flux. The mass is eroded further by balconies and projecting eaves. The façades are broken up into what appear to be free-floating rectangles painted in shades of light grays; color is provided by vertical and horizontal red and yellow strips that are window frames and balcony supports. The lower floor is conventionally subdivided into bedrooms. The upper living area can be subdivided by sliding panels (16.23). The base colors for walls and ceiling are white and gray; they are broken by black lines of framing and rectangles of primary colors that define areas when the panels slide back. The house is without precedent or obvious imitators. It was an early warning of rumblings in architecture and design. 16.23 ![](images/c2d0442f72bed9df49eaa4ae6d0486021d67be82467ebcbc911eceaf0e550b32.jpg) Gerrit Rietveld with Truus Schröder-Schräder, Schröder House, Utrecht, The Netherlands, 1924. Sliding panels can screen off individual rooms, or open up to make one space, as shown. The color scheme of white, black, red, and blue is undiluted De Stijl. Rietveld went on to produce more conventional modern architecture that is good but solidly in the mainstream. In 1934 he produced another extraordinary chair, made again from flat wooden panels, assembled in the shape of the letter “Z” with an added back panel. It appears fragile but rivals the red–blue chair’s status as an example of modernism. It has generally held that Wright’s Prairie houses, as revealed in the Wasmuth volumes, influenced the development of modernist design theory and it is easy to see how the low profiles and oversailing roofs, prompted by the flat plains of the American Midwest, struck a chord with the Dutch who had claimed their own flat lands from the sea, but De Stijl had little in common with the definitive brand of modernist architecture that would evolve in France and Germany in the 1920s. The Dutch variant would have appeared a little frivolous and its principles too open to individual interpretation. French and German modernism had rules and restrictions that left little room for input from interior designers.
