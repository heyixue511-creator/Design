Disgusted with the long-running civil war known asOnin-no-ran(1467-1477), Yoshimasa transferred the role of Shogun to his son and pursued a quiet life at the east end of Kyoto, cultivating his taste for calligraphy and the tea ceremony. One of the country's greatest wars to date, Onin-no-ran marked a turning point in Japan's history. During the decade of hostilities, many of the cultural assets that had been accumulated in Kyoto burned, and taking root in the “eastern" culture that Yoshimasa began to develop, Japanese culture started anew, with innovation in all aspects of its conventional traditions.
