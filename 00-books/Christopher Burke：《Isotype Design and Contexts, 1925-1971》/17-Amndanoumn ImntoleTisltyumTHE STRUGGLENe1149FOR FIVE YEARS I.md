Although Ivanitskii is credited as overseeing the creation of the charts in this album, only four of them featured his filmstrip method; the rest showed an approach that was closer to the Vienna Method. In chart no. 37, ‘Growth in public food service in the ussR' (see opposite), the modularity of the table pictograms is compromised by the use of perspective and the attempt to contain the plate pictograms (which have a separate value) on them; but such a complex configuration was not used elsewhere in the album. Grey guide-pictures appear in the majority of the charts, and they are generally separated from the statistical pictograms according to Vienna Method principles, although some effort was still concentrated on their illustrative qualities. Ivanitskii explained that ‘in the chart “Oil production in the UssR", oil is represented by a pictogram of an oil storage tank, but an additional drawing portraying oil wells points to the subject of the chart, namely extraction rather than the transportation of oil or the consumption of oil'.4 The purpose of the guide-picture, as Ivanitskii outlined it, was to help the viewer understand the subject matter of a chart, or divide the chart into sections. The Vienna Museum's guideline notes stated that the guide-picture cannot be standardized in the same way as the pictogram' but thatonly as much is shown in a guide-picture as is absolutely necessary to the statement in hand'.⁵º Yet many of the guide-pictures in Profsoiuzy SSSR / The struggle for five years in four drew attention away from the actual data in an attempt to stress the success of socialism rather than simply indicate subject matter. In chart no. 44, 'Growth in capacity of city nurseries', not only is the baby pictogram executed in some detail, but the guide-picture presents an idealized image of a modern, Soviet city nursery with children fed and cared for by attentive staff. In chart no.58, ‘Total circulation of trade union publications', the artist has attempted a narrative guide-picture: the worker submits his manuscript, it is printed, and then made available to other trade union members.

To some extent, the charts in Profsoiuzy SSSR / The struggle for five years in four may reflect wider tensions in the visual arts of the ussR during the early 193os.⁵¹ Throughout their time in Moscow, the Vienna Museum staff seemed to have been aware that their position was not very secure. A shift towards celebrating individual, identifiable Soviet heroes in art and literature was felt by those at Izostat almost from the start. Marie Neurath recalled: 'While we were still there, a separate group inside the office made a different kind of symbolism, men with ears and eyes, according to “Soviet Realism"'.⁵² Arntz felt the rejection of the Russian avant-garde artists even more keenly as he attempted to engage with them and their work. He recalled that he attended an exhibition of work by Vladimir Maiakovskii but found himself alone in the gallery, and 'when I explained in the [Izostat] Institute how I liked it, they preferred not to speak of him: he was already out of favour'.⁵³ Nevertheless Arntz seems to have enjoyed his first few months in Russia in 1932 and he socialized with Russian colleagues (they spoke a mixture of German and French); he visited Leningrad with some of them for the 'white nights' in June.⁵4 He found it satisfying that the Izostat staff were willing to learn and follow his artistic direction:

Some pictograms created at Izostat are recorded in a version of the picture dictionary' that appears to have been compiled by Arntz or one of his Viennese colleagues during their time in Moscow. It is a particularly valuable source, as the proliferation of certain themes indicates the focus of Izostat work on the industrial and agricultural goals of the First Five-Year Plan. It contains numerous variations of the tractor pictogram, and guide-pictures of factories and industrial landscapes, as well as of the Kremlin, Lenin's Mausoleum, and the Triumphal Arch. The Moscow‘picture dictionary' also indicates that Izostat staff recognized the need to use visual markers of national identity in order to engage viewers with their charts. A number of human pictograms have features that could identify them as Russian: for example, no. 59 shows a student walking to school in a fur cap with ear-flaps. However, most human pictograms that have a recognizable Russian' identity are associated with pre-revolutionary Russia. No.1661 (opposite, above left) is presumably meant to represent a Russian peasant, with a beard and wearing traditional bast shoes fixed by means of cloth wrapped around the leg (clearly shown through the careful design).

Arntz soon established control over the design of Izostat pictograms, as is evident from another set of charts published in 1932, 15 let Oktiabria (15 years since the October Revolution). Here, simple pictograms arranged according to Vienna Method principles superseded the discrepancies and eccentricities evident in some previous examples. This boxed set of twenty charts celebrated Soviet economic and social achievements since the 1917 October Revolution, particularly under the First Five-Year Plan. The charts were printed on poor-quality paper and mounted onto card, probably intended for display in a schoolroom or workers' club; they were accompanied by instructions giving the order in which they should be arranged. A number of the charts were modified versions of some first published in Izvestiia, but unlike the newspaper charts they could employ colour as a visual indicator. Red, long associated with revolution and socialism, was the most widely used colour in Izostat charts for any outcome achieved since 1917. So, in the chart about coal production, the amount of coal mined in 1913 is represented by a blue wagon pictogram whereas those for the later years are coloured in red.

Red was also used in charts to differentiate the ussR from capitalist countries, which were shown in blue or black; and to differentiate urban from rural, which was shown in green (as in the chart about school attendance). 'The food situation was still difficult and there was a severe lack of goods. It was awkward to live in a hotel as a privileged foreigner and to have better access to food and consumables than my Russian colleagues.'º The situation for those living outside Moscow was much worse, as Arntz encountered in the summer of that year. For his outstanding work at Izostat – he later described himself as the Institute's first Udarnik (shock-worker) – Arntz and his family were offered a month's holiday at the Black Sea resort Sochi. This was the destination of choice for privileged members of Soviet society and Party officials, and its luxury hotel provided Arntz with an uncomfortable contrast to what he saw during the 52-hour train journey through Ukraine:

... the homeless children travelling along under the wagons, the starving farmers, and again and again the throngs of begging children at the stations. ... Much could be reported about sacrifice on all sides, caused by collectivization. The sight of the swathes of shelter-seeking exiles at the stations could in itself have provided material for such reports. One really had to close one's eyes and only read the victory announcements' in the newspapers in order to live in peace with this, even in Moscow.

Arntz also observed the persecution of individuals for alleged disloyalty to the Communist cause: 'From close quarters I witnessed purification" in business, with self-accusations during business assemblies, and I had good interpreters at my disposal in these. That was a chapter of Soviet life in itself.' He experienced the growing atmosphere of secrecy and caution: whereas in 1932 he had enjoyed mixing with his Russian co-workers, in 1933 he seldom met them outside of the Institute because they had to be extremely careful about their associations; therefore his contact was restricted to a small circle of foreigners.60

Arntz made his last visit to Moscow between the middle of October and the beginning of December 1934, after having left Vienna to settle in The Hague with Neurath and Reidemeister. He remembered that Izostat was inundated with work for the Second Five-Year Plan: 'enormous amounts of propaganda were designed, exhibitions organized and plans explained'.61 One example is the English-language publication, The Second Five-Year Plan in construction (1934). The charts in this book are evidence of the tremendous effect that the Vienna Method and Arntz's influence had exerted on Soviet pictorial statistics since 1931. However, a chart such as Creches' also demonstrates why the work of the Izostat Institute was, by the end of 1934, regarded as contrary to official cultural policy. Arntz explained:

She reported on the situation at Izostat when writing to thank him:

Mind you the work here has become engaging again to some extent, due to things being most tolerable in my department; hardly anyone interferes. But also, apart from that, the Vienna Method' is really the guiding principle, above all concerning the formal side of things, since, despite many thoughtless trials with naturalism, nothing clever has resulted – QED. She added that an in-house facility for lithographic printing had been established and that, while a strict adherence to the precision in execution that was learned from Vienna' was maintained, there were many sins' committed in the new work.

Perhaps the most notable publication to be issued during Izostat's latter years was the English-language publication USSR: an album illustrating the state organisation and national economy of the USSR, which was one of a series of books produced by the Soviet authorities for the 1939 New York World's Fair.6 El Lissitzky oversaw the design of this album which contained a mixture of pictorial statistics, wood engravings, and photographs of notable Soviet figures. The pictorial statistics here remain close in spirit to the Vienna Method, while departing from it in certain details.
