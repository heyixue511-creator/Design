# St Peter’s Seminary

Location Scotland

Original date 1966

Designer Gillespie, Kidd & Coia

![](images/ddf93748ce12436ab10dc057d1cd446deac4c5e1b3371078f652d2915c652cfc.jpg)

Figure 1.38 The great dining room of the seminary commands a romantic and brooding presence.

Most buildings have resilience, in that they are suffi ciently robust to allow a remodelling to occur. An uncomplicated building may have a number of distinct lives; it may undergo many quite simple transformations. An obvious instance of this is the 18th- and 19th century warehouse, of which there are innumerable examples all over the western world. Many which have outgrown their initial use have been converted into galleries, apartments, shops or offi ces. However, a building that is designed so specifi cally to accommodate the needs of the users, that is so exactly measured to the rhythm of their requirements, can be incredibly diffi cult to adapt. Some buildings are unambiguous

and precisely designed around the functions and the activities that take place within them, meaning that the character of the space is extremely distinct. To make the building anywhere near approaching habitable, almost impossible concessions would have to be made by the new occupiers of the building.

One such building is St Peter’s Seminary. It was designed by the Glasgow practice Gillespie, Kidd & Coia, and was consecrated in 1966 as a training college for Roman Catholic priests. It is a powerfully modern cast concrete or béton brut building set within the wild countryside of southern Scotland. It contains the

traditional elements of a seminary: chapel, refectory and cells, all of which are organised around an open court. In the time since construction, the manner in which priests are educated has changed – rather being cloistered away in isolated communities, they are encouraged to gain experience of real life and enter into small parishes. In 1980, the Catholic Church closed St Peter’s down and the building was used as a drug rehabilitation centre for a few years. However, since 1987 the building has lain empty and has been allowed to become derelict. The building is very exact: it responded so completely to the needs of the users, so acutely that it has proven impossible to convert it into anything else. There have been a number of proposals to convert the building, including plans for a hotel and for domestic accommodation, but the inconvenience of the site, the cost of the proposals, and the very specifi city of the building itself defeated these.

The neglected building has started to be used as a canvas by artists: graffi ti adorns the walls and pop videos have been fi lmed in the dirt and detritus of the ruin. This Brutalist masterpiece is also a place of pilgrimage for architects, who stare in wonder and understanding at the exposed structure. The ruin is a place of romance. It allows the visitor to engage their imagination and connect with the past and the future. A ruin could be regarded as an acknowledgement of the force of history, of nature, climate and culture. It is a sign of a superseded society, a monument to a distant time of different priorities and values. The preservation of a building in a ruinous state recognises this. Perhaps sometimes the best approach to an obsolete building is not to do anything, to let it crumble and decay, to let it be.

# Dornbusch Church

Location Frankfurt, Germany

Original date 1962

Remodelled 2005

Designer Meixner Schlüter Wendt

![](images/0ce5b40df3beb17b007f27a1a9500c09f98a002ce584221a733b07bfb92bccb1.jpg)

Figure 1.40 The great relief on the south side of the church serves as a reminder of the areas of the building that have been taken away.

It is the responsibility of the architect or designer to acquire a highly specifi c knowledge of the needs of the client and the users of the remodelled building. This knowledge allows the designer to tailor the design of the adaptation to their exact requirements. The designer can propose a strategy that the client may not have considered; they can suggest a method of organisation that the users of the building had not previously thought of. The designer has the ability to garner a strategic view of the problem, which the client or the user may not be able to see.

The Dornbusch Church is situated in a residential area in the north of Frankfurt. The original building was constructed in the 1960s, at a time when fi re stations and churches seemed to look very similar: a great hall of suffi cient size to hold several engines or a large congregation, fl anked by a tower that was either used to dry the hoses or support the bells.

This particular tall white bell tower became symbolic of the progressive attitude of the area, while the congregation of over 12,000 were housed in an enormous glazed block.

Figure 1.41 Diagram created by the architects to show the reorganisation of the buildings and spaces.

Figure 1.42 A large exterior space for recreation has been created between the tower and the church. The position of the pews and other furniture has been marked on the ground to provide an amusing pattern to play within.

However, by the beginning of the 21st century, the building was in poor condition and the congregation had dwindled to less than a third of its original size. A proposal was made for the complete demolition of the building with the construction of a new much smaller prayer room to replace it.

The architects Meixner Schlüter Wendt felt that this was not the right approach and after intensive consultation with the community, undertook a series of planning studies from which they were able to develop an overview of the project. They proposed a solution that was far more advantageous to the congregation, the people of the town and to the immediate urban area. This alternative scheme involved just the partial demolition of the building, and the remodelling of the remaining structure to create a considerably more clever and complex solution than just a simple prayer room. Thus the immediate settlement contains what could be regarded as an archetypical church with a landmark tower, a churchyard or public square, and a community hub.

The nave of the church was substantially reduced; half of the building was simply taken away. This act of subtraction concentrated the internal organisation of the building. A highly coloured stained glass window, which had once been on the periphery of the space, has become the focus of the reordered church. An elaborate wall that appears to be carved in relief now fi lls the raw edge of the building – this provides both stability and also articulates the narrative of the change. The sculptural surface seems to almost echo the elements that have been removed from the building, thus acknowledging the passing of time and the changing of attitudes.

![](images/fcb77320973ff395d60c3316c3a155dd69a55f6c727e2a1b24f8af5655064709.jpg)

Other changes to the building were fairly minimal, basic conservation and decoration. However, on the outside of the building the remodelling is very obvious. The demolition created a great separation between the church and the tower. This exterior space is marked with the memory of the original nave, the layout of which is painted onto the grey asphalt, thus creating a surface that is obviously reminiscent of the lost building, but strangely also looks somewhat like a playing fi eld. The public space is approachable, it encourages people to use it and yet is under the protection of the church – an evocative memento, perhaps, of the place that the church once held within western society.

# The Cement Factory / La Fabrica

Location Sant Just Desvern, Barcelona, Spain

Former function Cement works

New function

Family home and studio

Remodelled 1975

Designer Ricardo Bofi ll

![](images/99fd51279904abfcc21cf9f69c78ae8154a6913f1df639d3fc6a89456752ba8d.jpg)

The Modernist adage that form follows function does not generally apply to building reuse projects. The form of the building already exists. The spaces are defi ned: the walls are in position, the roof is in place and the relationship between these elements and the immediate context has long been established.

Figure 1.43 The mature foliage reinforces the character of the ruinous building.

Of course it is possible to change all of this. Walls can be demolished, new elements constructed and fresh relationships established – but the essence of the building will still exist. The character of the place will still be present. This is the charm of the remodelled structure. It retains the character of the original programme and inhabitants, and combines this with the needs of the new users. Adaptation is always a delicious compromise between the two.

The vast complex of a disused cement factory on the outskirts of Barcelona was, in the mid-1970s, converted into an offi ce and a home for the architect Ricardo Bofi ll. This extraordinary and romantic project was the vision of the young architect, who had not only grown up in the construction industry, but had come of age in a country just emerging from the oppressive years of the Franco regime. It was an extraordinary time in Barcelona, a city with a vivid and progressive attitude that had nurtured such artists as Gaudí, Picasso, Miró and Dalí, but in that critical post-war period also saw the rise of Brutalism. So the sight of the almost surreal complex of concrete structures, did not daunt Bofi ll, but apparently actually fi lled him with a ridiculous kind of magical hope.

![](images/00b064e81c48a75cde19beb7ac31fe5c792afc420e03f8fa68f3bffc6abcd192.jpg)  
Figure 1.44 Plan of the cement works showing how the big spaces are quite modestly occupied.

The uninhibited cement works was immediately adjacent to the site where the architect was constructing the Walden Seven housing complex at about the same time. This uncompromising multi-level building-city is candidly monumental: it contains 18 towers, 446 apartments, bars, shops and 2 swimming pools. It actually overshadows the factory and this contrast of scale between the two structures reinforces the sculptural quality of both.

The cement factory had been abandoned and was partially in ruins, and so the adaptation process began with further demolition. This removed much of the detritus and additions that had accumulated over the years since the original construction. This defi ned a series of distinct spaces, which were little more than cleaned, thus the memory of the structure’s former use, the industrial aesthetic and the spatial quality is preserved in the raw concrete walls. Small additions, such as new walls to complete spaces, openings to allow for light and access, and vast amounts of greenery completed the project. It then came to the problem of how to occupy this vast edifi ce; distinct spaces had evolved from the process, each had a particular and defi nite quality. The occupiers considered the nature of these new volumes while also contemplating the activities that would happen within them. Thus a symbiotic solution was reached; one which accentuated the conditions and character of the building while ensuring that the users completed tasks in the most sympathetic surroundings. So, for example, the original factory hall was transformed into the conference and exhibition room, and with reference to the ceiling height of over 10 metres, it is called ‘La Catedral’. # Rucksack House

Location Leipzig, Germany

Date 2004

Designer Stefan Eberstadt

![](images/eb0c3df2c8bda1d16159173e7764149f83155d23af68a3048d71fffa81520410.jpg)  
Figure 1.46 Extra space is created within a cramped apartment by carefully suspending a new room from the exterior walls.

The lack of usable space within any property can be a problem for the inhabitants. There are a number of quite straightforward solutions to this: an addition to the side, or a new level above, but if the space cannot be extended in this straightforward manner – if it is for example, an apartment, then other more extreme ideas have to be considered.

Stefan Eberstadt developed the Rucksack House in the mid 2000s. It is a very basic but clever method of extending a fi nitely contained dwelling by basically attaching a new room to the outside of the building. This space simply hangs from the exterior walls in a very similar manner, as the name suggests, to the way in which anyone would wear a rucksack. The Rucksack box is compact, just nine square metres of fl oor-space and fairly lightweight. It is suspended from steel cables that are anchored to the roof or to the façade of the existing building. The room is made from a welded steel cage, the interior of which is lined with a light birch veneer. The exterior is clad exterior grade plywood with an absorbent resin surface. The window

openings are fi lled with a lightweight acrylic glass. The concept is that the box is owned by the occupier of the home, and so when they move on, so does their extra room, thus that extra space is always theirs. The Rucksack House was fi rst constructed in Leipzig in 2004, then in Cologne in 2005 and then moved to Bamberg in 2011.
