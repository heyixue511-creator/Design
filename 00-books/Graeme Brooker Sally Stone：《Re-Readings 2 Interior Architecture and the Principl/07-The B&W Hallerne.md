# The B&W Hallerne

Location   
Copenhangen,   
Denmark

Former function Warehouse

Proposed function Student Halls of Residence/Venue

Designer Urban Power

The post-industrial western society has the common problem of what to do with the obsolete landscapes and the buildings of production. The reduction in the amount of heavy industry in most of Europe and the USA has created an abundance of sheds, warehouses, empty factories and other detritus of the industrial era.

The character and identity of each individual industrial development was predicated upon the technical principles necessary for the optimum output and effi ciency, and so each is different as each contains a direct connection to both the land and the process of production. Industrialisation affected both economic growth and the society that it was built upon. It physically shaped the landscape and townscape, and also infl uenced social structures and social relationships.

The rapid construction of buildings, chimneys and infrastructure meant that the industrial landscape differed greatly from the pre-industrial one. This is one of the most important themes of the history of the last two centuries and de-industrialisation is its reverse. The de-industrialisation process has not only affected people but also the landscape of the urban society. These post-industrial landscapes contain historical and cultural heritage and there is a need for them to have a sustainable and viable future. Traditional heavy industry has been replaced with the service and the educational industries; the later especially can be seen as an investment in human

capital and an important contributor to economic development.

Urban Power have proposed a project that preserves the industrial traces of the harbour front in Copenhagen, while introducing a mixed-use project that facilitates the needs of a contemporary population. The B&W Hallerne on Refshaleøen Island has proved to be a great venue for large-scale concerts and other massive public events – the Eurovision Song Contest was held there in 2014. However, the isolation of the buildings is a problem. Urban Power have proposed a hybrid solution to this by

suggesting that a student hall of residence could be packed into the walls of the structure. Over 2,000 single-room homes could be crammed into the façades, while the interior space could still hold concerts, football games, and support other activities. Each of the students would have their own privileged view of the dramatised space. As well as possibly solving Copenhagen’s problem of a lack of student residences, this might ensure that the island is less isolated by bringing a bright and vibrant population to the area. It could act as a catalyst for future developments in the area and perhaps provide a viable future for the structures.
